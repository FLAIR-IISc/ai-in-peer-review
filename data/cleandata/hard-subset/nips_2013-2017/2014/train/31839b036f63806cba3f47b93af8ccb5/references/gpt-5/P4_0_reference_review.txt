Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops graphical conditions and constructive procedures for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data subject to missingness, using Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) as the formal representation. Building on Mohan et al. (NeurIPS 2013), the authors:

- Generalize the ordered factorization approach to include missingness indicators R in the factorization (Definition 2 and Theorem 1), enabling recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms influence each other (edges among R nodes).
- Provide a new recovery formula for the joint distribution in semi-Markovian models when no admissible sequence exists, via partitions of R and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)) (Theorem 2) and a corollary for the case without latent confounding between R and substantive variables.
- Give necessary graphical conditions for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2).
- Show that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when associated probabilistic queries are not, including cases where Y is not d-separated from its missingness mechanism Ry (Theorem 4 and Example 3, leveraging d*-separation and do-calculus).
- Derive necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering joint distributions and causal effects under simple attrition (|Vm|=1) (Theorems 5 and 6), correcting claims in Garcia (2013).

This work situates within graphical causal inference (Shpitser and Pearl, 2006; Pearl, 2009), ADMG Markov properties (Richardson, 2003), and the missing data literature (Rubin, 1976; Little and Rubin, 2002). It extends NeurIPS 2013 results on m-graphs by broadening recoverability conditions and adding non-recoverability criteria and causal recoverability under MNAR.

Strengths
- Technical contributions broaden the class of m-graphs for which recovery is possible, notably through (i) general ordered factorization including R nodes, and (ii) a constructive formula based on R partitions and Markov blankets.
- Non-recoverability criteria fill a documented gap, providing necessary conditions that help practitioners recognize when estimation is impossible under stated assumptions.
- Demonstrations that causal queries can be recoverable despite inseparability of Y and Ry are conceptually important and practically relevant (Example 3; Theorem 4).
- Attrition results provide clean necessary-and-sufficient graphical tests and simple estimands, with clear implications for practice.
- The work appropriately connects to and extends prior NeurIPS contributions and causal identifiability theory (do-calculus, d*-separation).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Empirical evaluation is absent. The paper would benefit from synthetic and real-data case studies quantifying finite-sample performance, robustness to graph misspecification, and comparisons with ML/EM, IPW, and MI under MAR/MNAR.
- Algorithmic aspects are under-specified. While Theorems provide conditions and closed-form estimands, the paper does not detail procedures (and complexity) to (a) search for admissible sequences, (b) compute R partitions and Mb(R(i)) in general ADMGs, or (c) verify Theorem 4’s identification sub-conditions in practice.
- Clarity issues and notational inconsistencies (e.g., initial definition “V = V ∪ U ∪ V* ∪ R”; repeated overloading of V; reliance on figures without sufficient in-text description) hinder readability. Minimality in Definition 2 is crucial; a clearer algorithmic prescription would help.
- Assumptions such as positivity are stated but treatment of violations is brief (footnote). Guidance on diagnostics (e.g., from Mohan and Pearl, 2014) and remedies would improve applicability.
- Scope of causal recoverability beyond |Vm|=1 is only partially addressed (Theorem 4 conditions are specialized); broader characterizations would further strengthen significance.

Quality
- The results are well-motivated and appear technically sound, consistent with established graphical criteria (d-separation, inducing paths) and causal identifiability tools (do-calculus). Proofs are in the appendix; in-text proof sketches for central theorems (e.g., Theorems 2, 4, 6) would increase confidence. The provided examples correctly illustrate when sequential factorization fails and how the generalized approach succeeds.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable but could be improved by: fixing notation; adding pseudo-code for recovery procedures; explicitly defining all graph operations (e.g., Gw, GX,W1) in the main text; and expanding explanations for key concepts (R partitions, Mb(R(i))) with self-contained examples. Explicit statements of assumptions (e.g., R not parents of V∪U) should be reiterated where used.

Originality
- The extension of ordered factorization to include R and the non-recoverability criteria are original within the m-graph framework. Demonstrating causal recoverability despite non-separability of Y and Ry is a notable conceptual advance. The attrition results (necessary and sufficient conditions) are novel and correct prior claims in the literature.

Significance
- Missing data is ubiquitous; graphical recoverability criteria and constructive estimands under MNAR have high potential impact. The non-recoverability conditions are particularly useful for avoiding futile estimation attempts. The attrition results address a common applied scenario. Broader adoption would be facilitated by algorithms and empirical validations.

Arguments for acceptance
- Advances state of the art on recoverability under MNAR with precise graphical conditions and constructive estimands.
- Provides needed non-recoverability criteria.
- Clarifies when causal effects can be recovered even when probabilities cannot, with correct use of do-calculus.
- Offers practically relevant, necessary-and-sufficient conditions for attrition.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lacks empirical evaluation and practical algorithms; reproducibility and practical utility are not demonstrated experimentally.
- Some clarity/notation issues and reliance on figures reduce accessibility.
- The strongest causal results are limited to special cases (e.g., |Vm|=1), leaving open broader generalizations.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add algorithms (with complexity) for: finding admissible sequences, constructing R partitions and Mb(R(i)), and verifying Theorem 4’s identificability sub-conditions; provide pseudocode or flowcharts.
- Include simulation studies comparing proposed estimands against EM/ML, IPW, MI under controlled MAR/MNAR, and a real-data attrition case.
- Clarify notation and ensure self-contained definitions; add proof sketches for main theorems.
- Discuss robustness to m-graph misspecification and positivity violations, and connect to available testing procedures (Mohan and Pearl, 2014).
- Explore extensions of Theorem 4 beyond single partially observed variable, or clearly delimit the scope.

Overall, the paper makes substantive theoretical contributions that extend prior NeurIPS work on m-graphs. Strengthening clarity and adding empirical/algorithmic components would considerably enhance its impact.
