Summary
The paper advances the graphical treatment of missing data via Missingness Graphs (m-graphs). It extends earlier work (Mohan et al., 2013) in four directions: (i) a refined sequential factorization theorem that allows ordered factorizations to include missingness indicators R, thereby handling interacting missingness mechanisms; (ii) a new recoverability result (Theorem 2) for joint distributions in semi-Markovian models when no admissible sequence exists, using partitions of R and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)); (iii) graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2); and (iv) conditions and procedures to recover causal queries P(y|do(x)) even when the corresponding probabilistic relations are not recoverable, including the important “simple attrition” case with necessary and sufficient conditions (Theorems 5–6). The paper positions m-graphs as a unifying language to diagnose when queries are recoverable from incomplete data and to construct estimators that condition on R as needed.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds directly on Mohan, Pearl, and Tian (NIPS 2013) by broadening their ordered factorization approach to include R variables and by tackling cases with R–R interactions. It also leverages do-calculus, identifiability results (Shpitser and Pearl, 2006), dormant independencies and d*-separation (Shpitser and Pearl, 2008), and classical MAR/MCAR paradigms (Rubin, 1976). The attrition analysis connects to sample selection/selection diagrams and epidemiologic approaches (Robins et al., Van der Laan & Robins). To my knowledge, the non-recoverability criteria for P(V) and P(Y|X) in general semi-Markovian m-graphs and the necessary-and-sufficient conditions for causal effects under simple attrition are novel and useful additions.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and scope: The paper cleanly separates probabilistic and causal recoverability and shows that the latter can be feasible even when the former is not, a message that is often misunderstood in practice.
- Generalization of ordered factorizations: Allowing R into admissible sequences (with minimality) is a natural and powerful extension that unlocks important cases (Example 1).
- New graphical tools beyond sufficiency: The non-recoverability criteria help users recognize when assumptions are insufficient; these complement recovery results and are practically valuable.
- Attrition results: The necessary-and-sufficient conditions for both joint (Theorem 5) and causal (Theorem 6) recoverability in the |Vm|=1 setting are sharp, easy to check, and correct some misconceptions in the applied literature.
- Solid grounding in causal inference: The do-calculus-based recovery (Theorem 4) illustrates how causal structure enables identification even when Y and Ry are inseparable, and it correctly enforces identifiability in the substantive model as a prerequisite.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Clarity and notation: The paper is heavy with notation and introduces several non-standard constructions (general ordered factorization, R partitions, Mb(R(i))) without sufficient intuition or algorithmic guidance. The conditions in Theorem 1 (especially item (2) for Yi = Rz and the role of “minimality”) would benefit from a clearer, constructive procedure to build admissible sequences. Likewise, computing Rpart and Mb(R(i)) in Theorem 2 deserves an explicit algorithm and complexity discussion.
- Assumptions and scope: Early on, the paper assumes “R variables may not be parents of variables in V ∪ U.” This is reasonable (missingness cannot cause the data) but should be foregrounded as a modeling restriction. Several results (e.g., Theorem 4) condition on R′y; practical implications (selection bias, positivity, and when such conditioning preserves identifiability) should be discussed more explicitly.
- Tightness and completeness: Theorem 3 provides necessary conditions for recoverability of P(V), but the reader is left uncertain about their tightness. Are there additional necessary conditions, or are these nearly complete? For conditional distributions, Corollary 2 provides sufficient non-recoverability conditions; how far are they from necessary conditions in general semi-Markovian models?
- Relation to earlier necessary/sufficient results: Theorem 2 partially overlaps with conditions stated later (Theorem 5) and with necessary conditions in Theorem 3. The hierarchy of assumptions and implications could be organized more clearly (e.g., a figure or table showing where each theorem applies and how they relate to Mohan et al., 2013).
- Empirical validation: The paper is entirely theoretical. While acceptable at NIPS for a theory paper, at least one synthetic study comparing the proposed recoverability-based estimands to graph-blind methods (ML under MAR, IPW, MI) would greatly improve accessibility and demonstrate finite-sample behavior and robustness to misspecification of G.
- Exposition and references: There are multiple typos, inconsistent notation (e.g., V vs Vo/Vm/V*), and several references are poorly formatted. The paper would benefit from a careful edit.

Specific comments and questions
- Theorem 1: Provide an algorithm to search for admissible sequences (including R) with guarantees: if one exists, will the procedure find it? What is the complexity in graph size?
- Theorem 2: The condition “no element in Vm is either a neighbor of its missingness mechanism or connected … by a collider path” is restrictive. Can you provide examples where Theorem 2 succeeds but prior results (e.g., Mohan et al., 2013) fail, beyond the brief mention? A worked example would help.
- Theorem 4: The identification steps conditioning on R′y appear to rely on dormant independencies and do-calculus. Please add a self-contained derivation for the example in Figure 3 and clarify when P(Y|do(W1), do(X), Z, R′y) is identifiable given that R may be affected by substantive variables.
- Attrition: The “inducing path” criterion in Theorem 6 is subtle. Including a small catalog of patterns (neighbor/collider/inducing path) showing when P(Y|X) is recoverable would help practitioners.
- Positivity: The paper assumes strict positivity over complete cases. Can any of your results be extended (as hinted) to cases without positivity? Guidance would be useful.

Pros for acceptance
- Advances state of the art on recoverability under MNAR with clean graphical criteria.
- Clarifies the separation between probabilistic and causal recoverability with constructive estimands.
- Provides sharp results for a practically important special case (attrition).

Cons for acceptance
- Dense and sometimes opaque exposition; limited guidance for applying theorems in practice.
- No empirical demonstrations; unclear robustness to model misspecification.
- Some redundancy and organizational issues; references need cleanup.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The technical contributions appear sound and novel, with potential impact for causal inference and missing data in ML. I recommend acceptance if the authors improve clarity (especially algorithms to construct admissible sequences and partitions), add illustrative worked examples (and ideally a small simulation), and polish the writing and references.
