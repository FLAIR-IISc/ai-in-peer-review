- Extends Mohan et al. (2013) m-graph framework to handle interacting missingness mechanisms and recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries.  
- Main contributions: refined sequential factorization including R variables to recover when missingness mechanisms influence each other.  
- Main contributions: new general recoverability result (Theorem 2) using partitions of R variables and Markov blankets via collider paths for semi‑Markovian models.  
- Main contributions: graphical non‑recoverability criteria for joint distributions (Theorem 3) and conditional distributions (Corollary 2).  
- Main contributions: conditions under which causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even if corresponding probabilistic relations are not (Theorem 4).  
- Main contributions: necessary and sufficient probabilistic and causal recoverability conditions for simple attrition (|Vm|=1) that refine prior claims (Theorems 5–6).  
- Builds on Mohan et al. (NIPS’13), Pearl’s do‑calculus, and ADMG/latent variable Markov properties (Richardson 2003).  
- Provides graphical, assumption‑explicit criteria that go beyond MAR and complements classical missing‑data methods (ML/EM, IPW/DR, MI).  
- Theoretical results are well stated with constructive estimands in several examples, and necessity/sufficiency for |Vm|=1 is valuable.  
- Extension of sequential factorization to include R variables is carefully motivated, emphasizing minimal conditioning for admissibility.  
- Non‑recoverability criteria fill a gap and give necessary conditions for joint recoverability and sufficient conditions for conditional non‑recoverability.  
- Causal recoverability result using d*‑separation and dormant independences is technically interesting and constructive.  
- Several conditions and definitions are subtle and hard to parse; more intuition, diagrams, or algorithms are needed for practitioners.  
- Theorem 2’s assumptions (no neighbor/collider path between a variable and its own missingness) are strong and need clarification on practical prevalence.  
- No empirical or simulation study to demonstrate usability, robustness to graph misspecification, or finite‑sample behavior.  
- Modeling assumptions are restrictive (semi‑Markovian DAGs, no edges R→V/U) and should be justified or their scope discussed.  
- Paper is generally well organized and examples help, but notation is dense and occasionally inconsistent or unclear.  
- Key modeling assumption (R variables not parents of V∪U) should be stated crisply and shorthands defined consistently.  
- Theorems would benefit from “how to apply” checklists and running examples beside statements.  
- References are poorly formatted/incomplete and minor typos/formatting issues detract from readability.  
- Novelty: handling interacting R variables, explicit non‑recoverability criteria, and causal recoverability despite inseparable Y–Ry paths are original contributions.  
- Significance: advances theory of MNAR recoverability and is likely to influence future methods, though short‑term adoption may be limited without tooling/empirics.  
- Pros: resolves open cases, provides negative guidance via non‑recoverability, and gives surprising causal recoverability results.  
- Cons: dense presentation, no empirical validation or complexity analysis, and some strong assumptions limit scope.  
- Suggested improvements: provide procedural pseudo‑code for Theorem 2, clarify R→V/U restrictions, add simulation comparing standard methods, and discuss robustness to m‑graph misspecification.  
- Overall assessment: technically strong and original with important theoretical contributions; recommend acceptance contingent on improved exposition, examples, and reference fixes.
