This paper presents an extension to missingness graphs (m-graphs) for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from incomplete data. While the topic is undeniably important, and the authors build upon existing work in a logical manner, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and inconsistencies that, cumulatively, detract from its overall quality. My assessment leans towards rejection, despite acknowledging the potential significance of the core ideas.

The introduction is adequate but verbose. The phrase “all branches of experimental science are plagued by missing data” is a cliché and adds little substance. The organization, as stated, is followed, but the sections themselves often lack internal coherence.

Section 2, introducing m-graphs and recoverability, is generally sound but contains several minor issues. The notation, while consistent with prior work, is dense and potentially confusing for readers unfamiliar with the field. The definition of V* and R, though formally correct, feels unnecessarily complex and could benefit from a more intuitive explanation. Crucially, equation (1) lacks sufficient explanation regarding the distribution of v*i. Is it deterministic? The text implies this, but should explicitly state it. The definition of “strictly positive” is referenced but not directly stated, requiring a lookup of Mohan et al. [2013]. 

Section 3, focused on sequential factorization, is where the paper begins to stumble noticeably. The refinement of the theorem from Mohan et al. [2013] is presented without a clear justification of *why* the original theorem was insufficient. The introduction of “admissible sequence” is sound, but the examples (specifically Example 1) are convoluted and difficult to follow. The justification for each step in the factorization feels rushed. The claim that minimality is essential is repeated but not rigorously motivated. Why doesn’t a non-minimal factorization work? The authors state it doesn’t, but provide insufficient explanation.

Section 4, addressing cases where sequential factorization fails, introduces Theorem 2. The notation R(part) and Mb(R(i)) is again dense and lacks intuitive explanation. The conditions outlined in the theorem appear complex, and it’s unclear how practical application would proceed. The example referencing Figure 2(b) relies heavily on the reader’s ability to visually parse the graph and connect it to the theorem’s conditions.

Section 5, detailing non-recoverability criteria, feels underdeveloped. Theorem 3 offers necessary conditions, but they are relatively weak and don't provide strong guidance for practitioners. Corollary 2 is similarly limited in its practical value.

Section 6, discussing causal queries, represents the most novel contribution of the paper. The demonstration that P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when Y and Ry are inseparable is intriguing. However, the explanation surrounding Example 3 is once again overly complex. The repeated references to "do-calculus rules" without explicitly stating them inline hinders readability. The justification for each step in the derivation is insufficient. Theorem 4 adds further complexity without substantial benefit, and relies on definitions (d*-separation, inducing path) that require referencing external work.

Section 7, applying the results to attrition, feels tacked on and lacks depth. The discussion of simple attrition is brief and doesn’t fully leverage the theoretical developments presented earlier. Theorems 5 and 6 offer little beyond restating previous findings in the context of attrition.

Section 8, related work, is reasonably comprehensive but lacks critical analysis of how the presented work differs from and builds upon existing approaches.

Section 9, the conclusion, summarizes the work adequately.

Throughout the paper, there’s a pervasive lack of clarity in the presentation of mathematical derivations. Steps are often skipped or glossed over, making it difficult to follow the logic. The reliance on external references for basic definitions (e.g., strictly positive, d*-separation) is a recurring issue. The figures are small and lack clear labeling, making them difficult to interpret. The writing style is often dense and academic, hindering readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles an important problem in statistical inference.
*   The extension of sequential factorization offers a theoretical refinement.
*   The demonstration of causal query recoverability under certain missingness conditions is novel.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper is plagued by numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies.
*   The presentation is often dense, convoluted, and difficult to follow.
*   The mathematical derivations lack sufficient detail and justification.
*   The examples are overly complex and poorly explained.
*   The practical implications of the theoretical results are unclear.
*   The writing quality is subpar, with frequent reliance on jargon and external references.

**Overall Assessment:** Reject. While the core ideas have merit, the numerous flaws in presentation, clarity, and rigor significantly detract from the paper's overall quality. A substantial revision addressing these issues would be necessary for reconsideration. The paper feels more like a working draft than a finished, publishable manuscript.
