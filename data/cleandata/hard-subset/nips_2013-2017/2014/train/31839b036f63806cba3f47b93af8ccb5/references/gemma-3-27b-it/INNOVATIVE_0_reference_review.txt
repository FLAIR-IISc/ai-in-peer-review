This paper tackles the important and notoriously difficult problem of recovering causal and probabilistic queries from datasets with missing data. The core idea – extending and formalizing the ‘Missingness Graphs’ (m-graphs) framework – is genuinely novel and represents a bold attempt to provide a comprehensive graphical framework for reasoning about recoverability. The authors successfully extend prior work, offering refinements to sequential factorization and developing algorithms for cases where that factorization fails. The key strength lies in demonstrating that causal effects *can* be recovered even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not, a counterintuitive but highly valuable result. The application to attrition problems is a concrete and relevant illustration.

However, while conceptually strong, the paper suffers from a lack of extensive empirical validation. The examples are helpful for illustration but do not provide strong evidence of the practical utility of the proposed algorithms or the applicability of the new theorems to real-world datasets beyond simple scenarios. The exposition, while generally clear, occasionally feels dense and relies heavily on previously published work (Mohan et al., 2013) without fully self-contained explanations of complex concepts.  Corollary 1, for instance, jumps directly into assumptions without sufficient motivation or intuitive explanation.  The connections to existing work on missing data (e.g., multiple imputation, inverse probability weighting) are mentioned but not deeply engaged with – a more thorough comparative analysis would strengthen the contribution.  The introduction of several new definitions (general ordered factorization, Rpart, Mb(R(i))) adds to the density without being fully justified by the practical benefits they deliver.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The extension of m-graphs and the demonstration of recoverable causal effects under non-separability are genuinely novel contributions.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a formal, graph-theoretical framework with clear definitions and theorems.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition problems highlights the potential practical impact.
* **Bold Approach:** The authors tackle a fundamentally difficult problem and propose a comprehensive solution.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The lack of substantial experimental evaluation limits confidence in the practical applicability of the methods.
* **Density of Presentation:** The paper is occasionally dense and relies heavily on previous work, hindering accessibility.
* **Insufficient Comparison to Existing Methods:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth comparison to established techniques for handling missing data.
* **Overabundance of Definitions:** Several new definitions are introduced without sufficient justification.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a significant new theoretical framework for reasoning about missing data.
* The result on recoverable causal effects is surprising and potentially very impactful.
* The work represents a bold and ambitious attempt to address a challenging problem.
* The theoretical foundations appear solid and well-reasoned.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of extensive empirical validation raises concerns about practical applicability.
* The density of the presentation may limit its accessibility to a wider audience.
* The comparison to existing methods is insufficient.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its flaws, I lean towards **acceptance**. The novelty and theoretical depth of this work are compelling. While the lack of extensive empirical validation is a concern, I believe the significance of the theoretical contributions outweighs this weakness. This paper represents a genuine advance in our understanding of missing data and its impact on causal inference. I encourage the authors to prioritize a more thorough empirical evaluation and improve the clarity of the presentation in a future iteration of this work. Addressing the comparison to existing methods would also be highly beneficial.



**Recommendation:** Accept (with encouragement to address the weaknesses in future work).
