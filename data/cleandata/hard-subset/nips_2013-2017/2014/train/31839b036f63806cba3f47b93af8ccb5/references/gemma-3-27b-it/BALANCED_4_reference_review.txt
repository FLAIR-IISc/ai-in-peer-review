This paper investigates the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing data, utilizing and extending the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced in prior work. The authors build upon their previous work (Mohan et al. [2013], Mohan & Pearl [2014]) to present general conditions for recoverability, focusing particularly on scenarios where causal effects can be identified even when the underlying probabilistic relationships are not. They refine the sequential factorization theorem and introduce new criteria for both recoverability and non-recoverability, ultimately applying their findings to the common problem of attrition.

**Strengths:**

* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper demonstrates a strong theoretical grounding, building logically upon existing work in causal inference and graphical models. The extensions to the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) and the introduction of Theorem 2 to handle more complex missingness patterns are valuable contributions.
* **Novelty in Causal Query Recovery:** The finding that causal queries P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when Y is not d-separable from its missingness mechanism (Ry) is a significant and interesting result. This addresses a limitation of traditional approaches and has practical implications.
* **Comprehensive Exploration:** The paper systematically explores conditions for both recoverability and non-recoverability, providing a more complete picture of the limitations and possibilities of the proposed framework. The theorems and corollaries offer specific, testable criteria.
* **Application to Attrition:** The application of the results to the problem of attrition adds practical relevance and demonstrates the potential impact of the research.  The discussion of simple attrition cases is well-articulated.
* **Detailed Proofs:** The inclusion of detailed proofs in the appendix is commendable and allows for thorough scrutiny of the theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Notational Burden & Clarity:** While the notation is consistent with the prior work, it's dense and can be challenging to follow, especially for readers unfamiliar with the m-graph framework.  The frequent use of shorthand (Vo, Vm, Rw, etc.) adds to this complexity.  Some examples could be more thoroughly explained.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is almost entirely theoretical. While the examples are illustrative, the absence of any empirical validation (simulations or real-world data analysis) weakens the argument for the practical utility of the proposed methods. How well do these theorems translate into practice when dealing with messy real-world datasets?
* **Overreliance on Prior Work:** The paper heavily relies on citations to Mohan et al. [2013] and Mohan & Pearl [2014]. While building on prior work is expected, a more thorough discussion of related literature outside of this specific line of research (e.g., multiple imputation, inverse probability weighting) could provide a broader context.  The related work section feels somewhat limited given the vast literature on missing data.
* **Theorem 3's Practicality:** The non-recoverability criteria in Theorem 3 seem somewhat restrictive.  It might be beneficial to discuss the prevalence of these conditions in real-world datasets.
* **Minor Typographical Issues:** There are a few minor typographical errors (e.g., "semi-markovian" occasionally written as "semimarkovian") that could be addressed.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental problem in statistical inference (handling missing data) with a rigorous and theoretically sound approach.
* The results on recovering causal queries, even with non-separable missingness, are novel and potentially impactful.
* The framework of m-graphs provides a valuable tool for explicitly modeling and reasoning about the missingness process.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation limits the assessment of the practical utility of the proposed methods.
* The high level of theoretical complexity and dense notation might hinder accessibility for a broader audience.
* The related work section could benefit from a more comprehensive overview of existing techniques for handling missing data.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically strong paper that presents valuable theoretical contributions to the field of causal inference with missing data. The novelty of the findings regarding causal query recovery is particularly compelling. However, the lack of empirical validation and the high level of theoretical complexity are significant weaknesses.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should prioritize including empirical validation (e.g., simulations or a small-scale real-world application) to demonstrate the practical relevance of their results. They should also consider improving the clarity of the presentation, perhaps by providing more intuitive explanations of the theorems and examples, and by expanding the related work section. Addressing these concerns would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
