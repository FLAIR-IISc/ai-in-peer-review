- Refines the sequential factorization theorem to handle interacting missingness mechanisms (Section 3).  
- Develops general algorithms to recover joint distributions when sequential factorization fails (Section 4).  
- Introduces graphical criteria for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions (Section 5).  
- Shows causal queries P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when related probabilistic relations are not recoverable (Theorem 4, Figure 3; Section 6).  
- Provides necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for causal effect recovery in attrition/longitudinal studies (Section 7).  
- Strong theoretical depth and rigor with well-defined theorems and proofs, extending prior m-graph results.  
- Novel and important insight that causal effects may be recoverable despite non-recoverable probabilistic relations.  
- Comprehensive coverage of both probabilistic and causal recoverability across varied missing-data scenarios.  
- Clear, well-organized exposition with precise definitions, consistent notation, and illustrative examples.  
- High practical relevance to fields with common missing data/attrition (epidemiology, social sciences, clinical trials).  
- Lacks empirical validation on synthetic or real datasets to demonstrate practical applicability.  
- The m-graph framework is complex, potentially limiting accessibility for practitioners.  
- Insufficient detailed comparison with existing missing-data methods (e.g., multiple imputation, IPW).
