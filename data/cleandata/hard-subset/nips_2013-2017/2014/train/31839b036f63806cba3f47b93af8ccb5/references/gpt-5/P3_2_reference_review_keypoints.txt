- Main contribution: Develops graphical conditions for recovering probabilistic (joint/conditional) and causal queries under MNAR using m-graphs, extending Mohan, Pearl & Tian (2013).

- Key technical advances: (i) extends ordered/sequential factorization to include missingness indicators R with a minimality condition (Theorem 1).

- Key technical advances: (ii) provides a general joint-recovery result for semi‑Markovian models via partitions of R and Markov blankets reachable by collider paths (Theorem 2).

- Key technical advances: (iii) gives necessary non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2).

- Key technical advances: (iv) shows causal effects can be recoverable even when associated probabilistic quantities are not, using dormant independence and do-calculus (Theorem 4).

- Key technical advances: (v) fully characterizes “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1) with necessary and sufficient conditions for P(V) and P(y|do(x)) (Theorems 5–6).

- Relation to prior work: Positions between MAR/MCAR literature and latent-confounding identification, complementing and extending prior m-graph recoverability results.

- Technical soundness: Theoretical results are plausible, well-motivated, and appear to generalize earlier sufficient conditions; examples demonstrate key points (e.g., causal recoverability when P(y|x) is not).

- Proofs and intuition: Proofs are deferred to the appendix; some statements (notably Theorem 1 minimality and Theorem 4’s construction of Z/W1) need more intuition, sketches, or constructive algorithms.

- Empirical evaluation: No experiments or simulations provided; small-scale synthetic studies would help demonstrate finite-sample behavior and estimator robustness.

- Clarity and notation: Paper is dense and notation-heavy with inconsistencies (e.g., W vs W1, d*-separation subscripts); induced graphs GX, GX,W1 and some conditioning notation need clarification and standardization.

- Modeling assumption: The central assumption that R variables are not parents of V∪U should be highlighted and its practical implications (e.g., measurement not altering true values) discussed.

- Figures and examples: Figures must fully specify graphs; Example 3 and attrition examples need explicit graph descriptions to make recovery steps reproducible.

- References and formatting: Reference list contains formatting errors and incomplete fields that must be fixed.

- Originality: The minimality-augmented ordered factorization, partition/Markov-blanket theorem, and formal non-recoverability criteria are novel contributions relative to prior m‑graph work.

- Insight: The demonstration that causal effects can be recoverable despite inseparability of Y and Ry is a significant and non-intuitive contribution.

- Practical significance: Broadens MNAR problems where consistent estimators can be derived; provides explicit estimand formulas suggesting implementable procedures, especially relevant to attrition in longitudinal studies.

- Impact amplification: Would benefit from an algorithmic decision procedure (given an m-graph and query, decide recoverability and construct estimand) and complexity analysis for searching admissible sequences/partitions.

- Pros for acceptance: clear theoretical advances over prior m-graph work; first formal non-recoverability criteria; novel causal recoverability insights; sharp attrition conditions.

- Cons for acceptance: presentation and notation issues hinder accessibility; lack of experiments and implementation; some results restricted to |Vm|=1; assumptions (positivity, R not parenting V/U) and sensitivity not fully discussed.

- Suggestions: add a high-level decision procedure/pseudocode, include synthetic experiments comparing estimators, clarify Theorem 1 with graphical intuition and counterexample, give constructive guidance for Theorem 4 (finding Z, W1), and fix notation/figures/references.

- Recommendation: Weak accept.
