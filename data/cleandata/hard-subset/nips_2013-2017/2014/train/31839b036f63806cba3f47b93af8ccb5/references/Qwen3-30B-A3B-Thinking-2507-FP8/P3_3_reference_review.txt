

# Peer Review: "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

## Summary
This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to develop formal conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors establish that causal queries like P(y|do(x)) can sometimes be recovered even when the underlying probabilistic relations (e.g., P(y,x)) are not recoverable—a significant theoretical insight. They introduce new graphical criteria for recoverability of joint/conditional distributions, provide necessary/sufficient conditions for causal effect recovery under attrition, and demonstrate how their framework rectifies previous misconceptions in the literature.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Soundness & Novelty:** The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution by rigorously distinguishing between probabilistic and causal recoverability. The key insight—that causal effects may be recoverable despite inseparability between Y and Ry (Theorem 4, Example 3)—challenges conventional wisdom and fills a critical gap in missing data literature. This distinction is particularly valuable for causal inference practitioners.

**Formal Framework:** The extension of the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) to handle interacting missingness mechanisms (Figure 2a) is a significant technical advance. The paper's formalization of "admissible sequences" with minimality constraints (Definition 2) addresses a subtle but important limitation in prior work.

**Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition problems (Section 7) is well-motivated and addresses a pervasive issue in longitudinal studies. Theorems 5 and 6 provide clear, testable graphical conditions for causal effect recovery that rectify prior errors in Garcia (2013), demonstrating the framework's practical utility.

**Comprehensive Related Work:** The paper thoroughly contextualizes its contributions relative to missing data literature (Section 8), correctly identifying gaps in existing approaches (e.g., unexplored interactions among missingness mechanisms).

## Weaknesses

**Limited Practical Validation:** While the theoretical development is strong, the paper lacks empirical demonstration of the proposed conditions in real-world settings. The absence of simulations or case studies to validate the theoretical claims weakens the practical significance. For instance, it would be valuable to show how often the conditions in Theorem 4 are satisfied in common missing data scenarios.

**Assumption of Known m-Graph:** The framework assumes the m-graph (i.e., the missingness mechanism) is known—a significant limitation. The paper doesn't address how to learn the m-graph from data, which is a major practical hurdle. This is especially problematic given the authors' own work on "testability of models with missing data" (Mohan and Pearl, 2014), which suggests this is a non-trivial problem.

**Overstatement of Novelty:** The paper's claim of "extending the results of Mohan et al. (2013)" is accurate, but the novelty is somewhat incremental. The core ideas of m-graphs and recoverability were established in prior work, and the paper's main contribution is refining and extending these concepts. The paper could better emphasize the precise theoretical advances over Mohan et al. (2013).

**Algorithmic Gaps:** While the paper provides theoretical conditions for recoverability, it doesn't offer concrete algorithms for implementing the recovery procedures in practice. For example, Section 4 describes "general algorithms" but doesn't detail how to compute the required conditional probabilities from data.

## Critical Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of missing data mechanisms. The core insight—that causal queries can bypass probabilistic recoverability constraints—has profound implications for causal inference with missing data. The paper's formalism is precise, and the examples effectively illustrate the key concepts.

However, the paper's significance is somewhat limited by its purely theoretical nature without empirical validation. While NIPS welcomes theoretical contributions, the absence of any demonstration of how these conditions apply in practice (beyond the illustrative examples) weakens the impact. The assumption that the m-graph is known is also a significant practical constraint that the authors don't address.

## Recommendation

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution that advances the field of missing data analysis. The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability is particularly important and well-justified. To strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. Adding a brief empirical validation (e.g., simulation study showing how the conditions apply in practice)
2. Discussing how the m-graph might be learned from data (even if briefly)
3. Clarifying the precise theoretical advances over Mohan et al. (2013) in the introduction

The paper is technically sound, well-structured, and addresses an important problem. With the minor revisions noted, it would be a strong contribution to NIPS. The theoretical insights alone make this work significant for researchers working at the intersection of causal inference and missing data.
