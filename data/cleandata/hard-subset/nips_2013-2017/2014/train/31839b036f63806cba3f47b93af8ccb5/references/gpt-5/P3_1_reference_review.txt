Summary and relation to prior work
The paper advances the graphical treatment of missing data using missingness graphs (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors: (i) generalize ordered factorizations to include missingness indicators R and provide a refined sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) to recover probabilistic queries when missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) provide a new recoverability result for joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists, based on partitions of R and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)) defined via collider paths (Theorem 2) and a corollary for models without latent parents of R; (iii) introduce necessary conditions for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2); (iv) show that identifiable causal queries P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when associated probabilistic queries are not, leveraging do-calculus and d*-separation (Theorem 4), with special attention to attrition/monotone missingness cases, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for |Vm|=1 for both joint distributions (Theorem 5) and causal effects (Theorem 6). Proofs are in the appendix; examples illustrate each result.

This work fits within a line of research showing that graphical structure can diagnose recoverability under MNAR mechanisms beyond MAR/MCAR, complementing IPW/ML/MI approaches (Rubin; Robins & Rotnitzky; van der Laan & Robins). It also connects to ADMG/latent projection theory (Richardson; Shpitser & Pearl).

Quality
- The theoretical contributions appear sound and nontrivial. Extending ordered factorizations to include R and enforcing minimal conditioning sets is a useful correction/strengthening of prior work. Theorem 2’s constructive estimand P(V)=P(V,R=0)∏i P(R(i)=0|Mb(R(i)),R Mb(R(i))=0) is intuitive and operational when its conditions hold.
- The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) are valuable: practitioners need to know when no amount of clever modeling will help.
- The causal recoverability results are insightful: Example 3 and Theorem 4 clearly demonstrate that causal effects can be estimable even when Y and Ry are inseparable in the probabilistic sense, via dormant independences and appropriate conditioning on Ry=0.
- The attrition results (Theorems 5–6) provide clean necessary and sufficient conditions in an important special case and correct misconceptions in the applied literature.

However:
- Many core results are stated without proof sketches; given the novelty, short intuition for each theorem would help the NIPS audience assess correctness.
- Several conditions are intricate (e.g., collider paths vs. inducing paths; the minimality constraint in Theorem 1; the role of W in Theorem 4). More formal definitions in the main text and small worked examples would improve reliability.
- The paper lacks empirical demonstrations (synthetic or real) validating the estimands’ performance under finite samples and verifying failures when conditions are violated.

Clarity
- The manuscript is generally well organized, with a clear progressive structure. Nonetheless:
  - Notation is heavy and occasionally ambiguous. For Theorem 1, the clause “Z ∉ Xi and Xr∩RXm=∅ and Rz ⟂ RXi|Xi if Yi=Rz” is hard to parse. Please define Xr, RXm, and Rz consistently and earlier.
  - The definition of manifest compatibility is terse; an illustrative table mapping V, V*, R would help.
  - The definition of collider path differs slightly across sections (Theorem 2 vs. Theorem 3); ensure consistency (colliders only vs. substantive colliders).
  - The reliance on figures (2–4) is high; ensure they are fully annotated (latent bidirected edges, districts) and include at least one example where Theorem 1 fails but Theorem 2 succeeds.
  - References formatting needs attention.

Originality
- The general ordered factorization including R and the admissibility/minimality criterion is a novel and important refinement.
- Theorem 2 generalizes prior sufficient conditions to broader semi-Markovian models with interacting R’s using partition/Markov blanket ideas; to my knowledge, this is new.
- The non-recoverability criteria and the explicit demonstration of causal recoverability when Y and Ry are inseparable are also original contributions.
- The necessary and sufficient characterizations for |Vm|=1 provide a complete answer for a common scenario (attrition).

Significance
- Missing data under MNAR is pervasive in ML (recommender systems, healthcare, surveys). Graphical recoverability criteria are actionable, model-agnostic tools that can inform whether to proceed with IPW/EM/MI, redesign data collection, or perform sensitivity analysis.
- The results advance the theory of recoverability and are likely to influence both causal inference and practical ML with missingness.

Questions for the authors
1. Algorithmics: Is there an efficient procedure to search for admissible sequences (Theorem 1)? Greedy construction based on minimal d-separating sets? Complexity?
2. Scope of Theorem 2: How does it relate to completeness? Combined with Theorem 1, do these cover all cases without latent R–V confounding, or are there known graphs where P(V) is recoverable but neither theorem applies?
3. Theorem 4 assumptions: The statement mentions |Vm|=1; can it be extended to multiple partially observed variables? How does interference between multiple missingness indicators affect the do-calculus steps?
4. Positivity: Beyond “strictly positive over complete cases,” do any results require additional support/overlap (e.g., positivity of P(R=0|…))? Please state explicitly.
5. Practical guidance: Can you provide a decision flowchart for practitioners to decide recoverability of P(V), P(Y|X), and P(y|do(x)) given an m-graph?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add brief proof sketches or intuition for each theorem in the main text.
- Include at least one synthetic experiment demonstrating recovery vs. failure and finite-sample behavior of the proposed estimands.
- Provide pseudocode for: (i) searching admissible sequences; (ii) constructing R partitions and Mb(R(i)).
- Discuss what to do when non-recoverable (bounds/sensitivity analysis), and relate to partial identification literature.
- Improve notational consistency and reference formatting; add missing relevant references on MNAR identification and selection models.

Pros
- Solid theoretical advances; clear separation of recoverability vs. identifiability.
- New non-recoverability criteria.
- Complete characterizations for attrition (|Vm|=1).
- Relevance to widely occurring MNAR settings.

Cons
- No empirical validation.
- Dense notation; some definitions unclear in the main text.
- Limited discussion of algorithmic complexity and completeness of the proposed procedures.

Overall evaluation
A strong theoretical paper that extends the state of the art on recoverability with missing data via graphical models. With improved clarity and at least minimal empirical illustration, it would make a valuable contribution to NeurIPS. Pending these revisions, I lean toward acceptance.
