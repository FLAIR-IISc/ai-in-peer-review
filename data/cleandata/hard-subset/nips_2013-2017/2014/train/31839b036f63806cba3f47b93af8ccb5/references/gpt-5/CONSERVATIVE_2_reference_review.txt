Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values using missingness graphs (m-graphs) as introduced by Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013). The authors: (i) generalize the ordered-factorization approach to include missingness indicators R in the factorization and give a sufficient condition (Theorem 1) that extends prior results to cases where missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) provide a more general recoverability result for joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists (Theorem 2) based on partitions of R and Markov blankets defined via collider paths in semi-Markovian (ADMG) models, plus a corollary under no latent parents between V and R; (iii) introduce graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2); (iv) show that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when the corresponding probabilistic relations are not (Theorem 4), leveraging do-calculus and dormant independencies; and (v) apply the framework to “simple attrition,” providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering joint distributions (Theorem 5) and causal effects (Theorem 6) when one variable is partially observed. Proofs are in the appendix; worked examples illustrate the use of the criteria.

This work builds directly on Mohan et al. (2013) and Mohan & Pearl (2014), and connects to identification via do-calculus (Shpitser & Pearl 2006), dormant independence (Shpitser & Pearl 2008), and ADMG Markov properties (Richardson 2003). It diverges from common MAR/MCAR-based approaches and from ML/EM/MI/IPW methods by explicitly modeling the missingness mechanism and reasoning graphically under MNAR.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and perennial problem: inference under MNAR where standard MAR-based methods fail.
- The extension of ordered factorization to include R, and the general recoverability condition in Theorem 2, broaden the applicability beyond the earlier NIPS paper, including interacting missingness mechanisms.
- Providing explicit non-recoverability criteria is valuable for practitioners to recognize when recovery is impossible under stated assumptions.
- The insight that certain causal queries can be recovered even when Y and Ry are inseparable is interesting and aligns with modern identification results (do-calculus/dormant independencies).
- The attrition case offers crisp necessary-and-sufficient conditions and simple estimands, potentially useful for longitudinal studies.

Weaknesses
- Validation: The paper offers no empirical validation (synthetic or real), no finite-sample evaluation, and no implementation. Given the practical motivation, at least simulated studies demonstrating recoverability vs non-recoverability and comparison to MAR/EM/IPW baselines would substantiate usefulness.
- Algorithmic clarity: While Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 are presented as “general algorithms,” the paper does not provide concrete procedures with pseudo-code, completeness guarantees, or complexity analysis for constructing admissible sequences, computing R partitions and Mb sets, or checking the stated conditions automatically on a graph.
- Scope limitations: Several conditions (e.g., Theorem 2 excludes any Vm that is a neighbor of or collider-connected to its own R; Theorem 5/6 are limited to |Vm|=1) seem strong. It is unclear how often realistic MNAR settings fall into the covered classes versus excluded by Theorem 3.
- Novelty vs. consolidation: The causal recovery in presence of Y–Ry inseparability appears to rely on known do-calculus/dormant independence machinery. The contribution here reads more as an application within m-graphs rather than a fundamentally new identification result.
- Assumption sensitivity: The approach presumes a correct m-graph, yet practical procedures for diagnosing misspecification are only briefly referenced. No robustness or sensitivity analysis is provided; recoverability conclusions can be brittle to small graph misspecifications.
- Exposition/clarity: The notation is heavy and sometimes confusing (e.g., Rw vs Wr; R′x vs Rx=0), and the minimality requirement in Definition 2 is key but quickly motivated. Some figures are essential for understanding the examples; make sure statements (e.g., Example 3) are reproducible without figures. References are inconsistently formatted.

Quality
The theoretical claims are plausible and build on established graphical identification machinery, but rigor is hard to assess without reading the appendix. The main concerns are lack of empirical support and lack of concrete, automatable procedures and guarantees for the “algorithms.” For a NeurIPS audience, more than purely theoretical statements are expected, especially for practically motivated problems like missing data.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-structured, but the presentation would benefit from:
- An explicit algorithmic section: input (m-graph, query), outputs (recoverable? estimand), pseudo-code for Theorem 1/2 workflows.
- A small catalog of canonical patterns illustrating when each theorem applies/fails, with step-by-step derivations and failure modes.
- Clearer notation and a table of symbols; unify R′x notation and avoid overloading.
- Proof sketches for the main theorems in the main text to aid intuition.

Originality
Moderate. The work extends Mohan et al. (2013) in a natural way and packages known causal identification tools within the m-graph setting. The non-recoverability criteria and attrition characterizations are useful but not a radical departure from existing theory.

Significance
Potentially significant for researchers handling MNAR in causal inference and graphical models. However, without empirical validation, software, or automated procedures, practical impact is uncertain.

Pros
- Addresses MNAR with explicit mechanisms and gives both positive and negative criteria.
- Extends previous NIPS work to interacting missingness.
- Clear, useful results for simple attrition and some causal queries.

Cons
- No experiments or simulations; no comparison to standard methods.
- Lacks concrete algorithms and complexity/automation discussion.
- Strong conditions restrict applicability; robustness not addressed.
- Exposition/notation issues and reference formatting.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide an explicit algorithm (and complexity) to decide recoverability and construct estimands under Theorem 2, given an ADMG?
- How robust are the estimands to modest m-graph misspecification? Any sensitivity analysis?
- Can you include synthetic experiments demonstrating consistency and finite-sample behavior, and comparisons to EM/MI/IPW under MNAR?
- Are Theorem 3’s non-recoverability conditions tight? Any characterization gaps?
- Can Theorem 6 be extended beyond |Vm|=1, or are there known obstacles?

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical advances are incremental but worthwhile; however, the paper lacks empirical validation, automated procedures, and clarity needed for NeurIPS. Strengthening with concrete algorithms, simulations, and improved exposition could make this suitable.
