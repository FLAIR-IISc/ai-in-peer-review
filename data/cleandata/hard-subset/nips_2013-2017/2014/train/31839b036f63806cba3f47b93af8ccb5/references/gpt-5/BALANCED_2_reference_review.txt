Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends the m-graph framework of Mohan, Pearl, and Tian (NIPS 2013) to provide new graphical conditions for when probabilistic and causal queries are recoverable from data with missingness. Key contributions are:

- A generalization of the ordered factorization approach to include R-nodes (missingness indicators), yielding a new sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1). This enables recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms interact (edges between R’s), which were not handled by the earlier ordered factorization.

- New constructive recoverability results for P(V) in models where no admissible sequence exists (Theorem 2, Corollary 1), based on collider paths, partitions of R, and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)).

- New non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2), giving necessary graphical conditions that preclude recovery—complementing the prior focus on sufficient conditions.

- Results showing that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when the associated probabilistic targets (e.g., P(y|x), P(x,y)) are not recoverable due to inseparability between Y and Ry (Theorem 4), leveraging d*-separation and dormant independence (Shpitser & Pearl 2008).

- Necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering joint distributions and causal effects in “simple attrition” with a single partially observed variable (Theorems 5–6), correcting claims in Garcia (2013).

The work builds squarely on the m-graph formalism (Mohan et al. 2013) and do-calculus (Pearl 2009; Shpitser & Pearl 2006), adds principled non-recoverability criteria, and clarifies when causal effects can be recovered even under MNAR. It relates to MAR/MCAR-based ML/EM, IPW, and MI literatures by providing model-aware graphical conditions beyond MAR, and connects to testability results (Mohan & Pearl 2014).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The statements appear consistent with known graphical-separation/identification ideas and with the m-graph semantics. The necessity in Theorem 3 matches assumptions used in Theorem 2, and the attrition results (Theorems 5–6) are clean and plausible. The worked examples are helpful. Proofs are relegated to the appendix; brief proof sketches in the main text would improve confidence.

- Completeness: The paper provides constructive expressions where recoverable (e.g., factorization formulas in Theorems 1–2, Eq. 5), but algorithmic details (how to find admissible sequences, construct R partitions and Mb sets, and check conditions efficiently) are not fully specified. No empirical validation (synthetic or real) is provided.

- Care in evaluation: Claims are carefully scoped (semi-Markovian models; positivity over complete cases; known m-graphs). The paper notes non-positivity extensions exist, but details are thin.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear but notation is dense. Some notational choices could be made more consistent and defined earlier (e.g., GX vs GX; primed notation X′ for Rx=0; the precise construction and uniqueness of R partitions; how Mb is computed in mixed graphs with bidirected edges).

- The presentation would benefit from (i) an algorithmic summary with pseudocode for building admissible sequences and for applying Theorem 2 (including complexity), (ii) a running example that is revisited across the theorems, and (iii) a short practical recipe for the simple attrition case.

- Reference formatting is inconsistent and should be cleaned (years, venues, typos).

Originality
- The general ordered factorization including R-nodes and the resulting Theorem 1 seem like a meaningful extension of the 2013 NIPS result to interacting missingness mechanisms.

- The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3; Corollary 2) are valuable and, to my knowledge, not previously formalized in this way for m-graphs.

- Showing recoverability of causal effects despite inseparability between Y and Ry (Theorem 4) is conceptually important; it leverages known tools (d*-separation, do-calculus) but tailors them to missingness and clarifies a gap in the literature.

- The necessary and sufficient results for simple attrition (Theorems 5–6) look new and practically relevant.

Significance
- The results advance the state of the art in graphical handling of MNAR beyond MAR/MCAR heuristics, and provide both positive and negative conditions that can guide analysts. The attrition results and the message that causal effects may be recoverable when probabilistic queries are not are valuable takeaways.

- Broader impact will depend on usability: tool support for verifying conditions and estimating the derived formulas, and empirical evidence of robustness and finite-sample performance. As written, the contributions are primarily theoretical.

Strengths
- Clear, principled extensions of the m-graph framework.
- Both recoverability and non-recoverability criteria; constructive formulas.
- Novel insights about causal recoverability under inseparability.
- Clean necessary and sufficient conditions for simple attrition.

Weaknesses
- No empirical validation or case studies; no comparisons to practical baselines (EM/ML, IPW, MI, modern MNAR estimators).
- Algorithmic aspects are under-specified (construction of admissible sequences; procedures for R partitioning and Mb computation; complexity).
- Assumes the correct m-graph is known; limited discussion/sensitivity to misspecification, learnability, and testability beyond a brief citation.
- Some notation and reference formatting issues reduce readability.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide a concrete algorithm (with complexity) to:
   - decide existence of an admissible sequence and construct one if it exists, and
   - construct Rpart and Mb(R(i)) for Theorem 2?
2. How sensitive are the recovery formulas to mild violations (e.g., small positivity violations or slight mis-specification of the m-graph)? Any guidance on sensitivity analysis?
3. Are your results readily implemented for continuous variables and high-dimensional settings? Any regularization considerations?
4. For Theorem 4, can you give additional examples illustrating the conditions and how to verify d*-separation operationally?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add pseudocode and a decision workflow that integrates Theorems 1–3, 5–6 (input: m-graph and target; output: recovered formula or “non-recoverable”).
- Provide at least a synthetic study and one real-world attrition example comparing your estimands to EM/IPW/MI under MNAR and misspecified MAR.
- Expand on testability and diagnostics (building on Mohan & Pearl 2014) to help practitioners check assumptions with incomplete data.
- Clarify notation and definitions (primed notation, GX/GX, collider-path vs inducing-path distinctions). Clean references.

Decision
Weak accept. The paper presents solid theoretical contributions that extend prior NIPS work, adds useful non-recoverability criteria, and clarifies an important and somewhat counterintuitive point about causal recoverability under MNAR. The main weaknesses are the lack of algorithmic clarity and empirical validation. Addressing these would significantly enhance the paper’s impact.
