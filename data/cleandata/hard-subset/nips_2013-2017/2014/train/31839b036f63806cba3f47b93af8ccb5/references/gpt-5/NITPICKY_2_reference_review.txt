Summary
The paper studies identification/recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from incomplete data using missingness graphs (m-graphs). It extends Mohan et al. (AISTATS’13) in four directions: (i) a generalized sequential factorization that admits R-nodes in the factorization order (Theorem 1), enabling recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) a new recoverability formula for P(V) when no admissible sequence exists, based on partitions of R and Markov blankets via collider paths (Theorem 2) and a corollary under no latent confounding between V and R; (iii) graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2); and (iv) recoverability of certain causal queries P(y|do(x)) even when Y is inseparable from Ry (Theorem 4), with an application to attrition. For “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1), the paper gives necessary-and-sufficient conditions for recovering P(V) (Theorem 5) and P(y|do(x)) (Theorem 6).

Relation to prior work
The paper builds directly on Mohan et al. (NIPS/AISTATS 2013) on m-graphs and recoverability, and uses standard causal identification tools (Shpitser & Pearl 2006; Pearl 2009; Shpitser & Pearl 2008). The attrition application relates to Garcia (2013) and to epidemiologic treatments of missingness/selection. The non-recoverability criteria and the extension to include R-nodes in the factorization order seem to be the key claimed novelties.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and persistent issue: estimation under MNAR via explicit graphical assumptions.
- Clear conceptual contribution: allowing R-nodes in ordered factorizations is natural and potentially useful; Example 1 shows the necessity of minimality.
- Non-recoverability criteria are valuable for users to recognize impossibility.
- The demonstration that some causal effects are recoverable even when P(Y|X) is not is conceptually important.
- Necessary-and-sufficient results for |Vm|=1 (Theorems 5–6) are crisp and practically relevant (attrition).

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness/precision
- Notation is inconsistent and sometimes self-contradictory. Early: “Let G(V,E) be the causal DAG where V = V ∪ U ∪ V∗ ∪ R.” V is used both as “observable nodes” and as “all nodes,” which is impossible. You later use Vo, Vm as a partition of V, but also write “V is partitioned into Vo and Vm” after redefining V. Please separate node universe N = V ∪ U ∪ R (and V∗ as proxies) from V (substantive), with consistent symbols throughout.
- GX and GX “represent graphs formed by removing from G all edges leaving and entering X, respectively.” The two symbols are identical; likely G\X vs G_X or G¯X/G_X were intended.
- Several facts use Rxi, Ryi with set vs. element ambiguity (e.g., Theorem 1 conditions “Yi ⟂ (Ryi, Rxi) | Xi \ {Ryi, Rxi}”): define Rxi precisely (set of mechanisms for Xi?).
- Assumptions about edges from R are unclear. You state “R variables may not be parents of variables in V ∪ U,” but later rely on edges among R and allow V→R. Please formalize the class of m-graphs considered and ensure all theorems’ conditions align.
- Theorem 2’s precondition “no element in Vm is either a neighbor of its missingness mechanism or connected to its missingness mechanism by a collider path” looks stronger than Theorem 3’s necessary conditions (which require “all intermediate nodes are both colliders and substantive variables”). Clarify the exact path class excluded in Thm 2 and reconcile with Thm 3; as written, Thm 2 seems to assume (a superset of) Thm 3’s necessary conditions, diminishing its generality.
- R partitions R(1),…,R(N): Existence/uniqueness not addressed. If multiple valid partitions exist, does the formula for P(V) depend on the choice? What happens when R-nodes are connected by both non-collider and collider paths via mixed nodes? Please formalize.
- Markov blanket Mb(R(i)) in ADMGs: definition references Richardson (2003), but the blanket used includes nodes connected by collider paths; provide precise definition and justify identifiability of the factors used in the product formula.
- Theorem 4 (d*-separation) statement/notation deviates from Shpitser–Pearl (2008). The condition “Y ⟂w Ry | Z” and use of W1=W\X are hard to parse. Please restate with standard do-calculus/ID notation and clearly identify identifiability requirements (P(W1,Z|do(X)) and P(Y|do(W1),do(X),Z,Ry=0)).
- Example 3 relies on do-calculus equalities (eqns 3–4) without showing the relevant separation in manipulated graphs. Provide the graph conditions that certify these steps.
- The claims “algorithm” and “general algorithms” are not supported by explicit procedures or complexity analysis. The paper offers characterizations/estimands rather than algorithms.

Clarity/presentation
- Figures 1–4 are referenced but not present; this makes several results hard to assess.
- Numerous typos and formatting issues impede readability: unmatched parentheses, inconsistent capitalization of variables, LaTeX residues (e.g., “< −− >”, “∑ Ry”, “.1 Recovering…”), missing spaces, dangling footnote marker “1”, and raw BibTeX placeholders in references (e.g., “?, \Q2013\E”).
- Several definitions overload symbols (V, V∗, Vm, Vo) and later use shorthand Rx, Rw, etc., without consistently reintroducing them; e.g., Rw vs. Wr clarification is awkward and easy to misread.
- Corollary 1 mixes conditions (no latent confounding between V and R; and Y not a parent of Ry) that should be lifted to the top as a model class assumption. Also, Pasub(R(i)) vs. Mb(R(i)) interplay is not explained.

Scope/evaluation
- No empirical demonstrations, synthetic case studies, or implementation. Even a small suite of synthetic graphs illustrating where each theorem applies/fails, with simulation verifying recoverability/non-recoverability, would improve confidence.
- The novelty relative to Mohan et al. (2013) needs clearer positioning: precisely which classes of graphs were not covered before, and concrete examples where the new theorems succeed and old ones fail (beyond brief mentions).

Questions for authors
1) Provide precise, non-ambiguous notation for node sets and manipulated graphs. Can you supply a concise “notation glossary”?
2) Reconcile Theorem 2 vs. Theorem 3 path conditions. Are they logically consistent across ADMGs?
3) Is the partition Rpart unique? If not, prove that the resulting estimand is invariant.
4) For Theorem 4, can you connect your condition directly to ID/d-ID outputs and show an explicit identification derivation on the example graph?
5) Can you include the missing figures and an appendix with full proofs? Several claims are not verifiable as is.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with nontrivial new insights.
- Presents useful negative criteria and simple necessary-and-sufficient conditions for |Vm|=1.
- Highlights that causal effects can be recoverable when certain probabilistic quantities are not.

Cons for acceptance
- Notational inconsistencies and numerous presentation errors substantially impede verification.
- Missing figures/proofs; “algorithmic” claims are not instantiated.
- Some theorem statements are ambiguous or appear stronger than necessary without discussion.
- References are malformed; related work positioning could be tighter.
- No empirical illustrations.

Recommendation
Reject. While the core ideas are promising and likely contain publishable advances, the paper in its current form suffers from substantial clarity, notation, and formatting problems that prevent careful assessment and reuse. I encourage the authors to (i) fix notation and model class assumptions, (ii) include figures and full proofs, (iii) provide explicit algorithms/estimands with correctness proofs, and (iv) add illustrative examples or simulations. The work could be strong after a thorough revision.
