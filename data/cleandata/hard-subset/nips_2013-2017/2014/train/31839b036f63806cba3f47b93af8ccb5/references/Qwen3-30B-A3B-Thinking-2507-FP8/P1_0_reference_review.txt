

# NIPS Review: "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

## Summary
This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to establish formal conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors introduce a general framework for determining when joint/conditional distributions and causal effects (P(y|do(x))) can be consistently estimated from incomplete datasets. Their key theoretical contribution is demonstrating that causal effects may be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations are not, specifically when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable. The paper also provides novel graphical criteria for non-recoverability and applies these results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.

The work builds upon Mohan and Pearl's foundational missingness graph framework (2014) and Pearl's causal inference framework (2009), extending them to handle complex missingness mechanisms where missingness processes interact with each other. Unlike prior work that primarily focused on recoverability of probabilistic queries, this paper establishes the important distinction between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries.

## Strengths
The paper makes several significant theoretical contributions. First, Theorem 1 generalizes the sequential factorization approach to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact with each other (e.g., longitudinal studies), extending Mohan et al.'s (2013) work. Second, Theorem 4 provides a novel insight that causal queries may be recoverable even when Y and Ry are not d-separable—a finding that contradicts conventional wisdom in missing data literature. Third, the non-recoverability criteria in Theorems 3 and Corollary 2 fill an important gap in the literature by formally identifying when recovery is impossible.

The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear definitions, well-chosen examples (e.g., Example 1 and Example 3), and a logical progression from probabilistic to causal queries. The application to attrition problems (Section 7) is particularly timely and relevant for longitudinal studies, and Theorems 5 and 6 correct prevailing misconceptions in the literature (e.g., Garcia, 2013).

## Weaknesses
While theoretically strong, the paper lacks empirical validation. The authors establish rigorous theoretical conditions but do not demonstrate how these conditions would perform on real datasets or compare with existing missing data methods. For a NIPS paper, some empirical validation would strengthen the significance of the theoretical contributions.

The notation could be slightly improved for consistency (e.g., the distinction between R variables and Ry). While the paper provides detailed proofs in the appendix, some of the more complex theorems (like Theorem 4) could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the conditions hold.

The paper also doesn't thoroughly discuss how their methods would scale to high-dimensional data or large graphs, which is an important consideration for practical applications in modern machine learning.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a formal framework for distinguishing recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries
- The non-recoverability criteria fill a critical gap in the literature
- The results have direct implications for causal inference in missing data settings, which is a major problem in medical and social sciences
- The paper builds upon and extends a strong foundation of prior work (Mohan et al., Pearl)

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Lack of empirical validation limits the practical significance of the theoretical results
- The paper doesn't address scalability concerns for large-scale applications
- Some complex theorems could be better motivated with additional intuition

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the field of missing data analysis and causal inference. The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries is particularly novel and important. While the absence of empirical validation is a limitation, the theoretical depth and clarity of the paper make it a strong candidate for NIPS, especially given the conference's focus on theoretical advances in machine learning.

The paper advances the state of the art by providing formal graphical criteria for recovery that go beyond the standard MAR/MCAR assumptions. The results correct misconceptions in the literature (e.g., regarding attrition) and provide a unified framework for understanding missing data mechanisms. For a theoretical paper, the lack of empirical results is less problematic than for an applied paper, and the rigorous theoretical foundation is compelling.

The paper is well-written, well-organized, and builds effectively upon prior work. While some aspects of the notation could be clarified, the core contributions are substantial and clearly presented. The significance of this work lies in its fundamental theoretical insights, which will guide future research in handling missing data in causal inference settings.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS as a strong theoretical contribution that will influence future work on missing data problems in causal inference. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in statistical learning with rigorous theoretical analysis.
