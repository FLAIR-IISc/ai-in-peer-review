This paper presents a thorough investigation into recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing data, building upon the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. (2013). The authors extend previous work by providing more general conditions for recoverability, particularly focusing on scenarios where probabilistic relations are non-recoverable but causal effects *are* recoverable, even when Y is not d-separable from its missingness mechanism. The paper meticulously explores conditions for both recoverability and non-recoverability, and applies these findings to the common problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is firmly grounded in causal inference theory, leveraging concepts like d-separation, do-calculus, and sequential factorization. The extension of the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) is a significant contribution, broadening its applicability to more complex missingness scenarios.
* **Novelty in Causal Recoverability:** The demonstration that causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic ones are not (Section 6) is a noteworthy result, offering a potentially valuable tool for practitioners facing MNAR data. The theorems and definitions relating to this (e.g., Definitions 3 & 4, Theorem 4) are clearly articulated.
* **Comprehensive Treatment of Attrition:** The application of the developed theory to the attrition problem (Section 7) provides concrete examples and insights into recovering causal effects in this frequently encountered setting.  The comparison with existing literature (Garcia, 2013) highlights the paper's contribution.
* **Rigorous Mathematical Treatment:** The paper is replete with theorems, definitions, and proofs, demonstrating a high level of mathematical rigor. The appendix likely contains detailed proofs, which is essential for validating the theoretical claims.
* **Clear Organization:** The paper follows a logical structure, building from the foundations of m-graphs and recoverability to more advanced topics like non-recoverability and causal inference.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Accessibility:** While theoretically strong, the paper is dense and requires a solid background in causal inference and graphical models. The extensive use of notation and theorems, while rigorous, may be daunting for readers less familiar with the field.  A more intuitive explanation of key concepts alongside the formal definitions would improve accessibility.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper primarily focuses on theoretical results.  While examples are provided, a more extensive empirical evaluation—perhaps through simulations or application to real-world datasets—would strengthen the practical relevance and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
* **Assumptions:** The theorems often rely on specific conditions (e.g., specific graph structures, minimality in factorization). The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the sensitivity of the results to violations of these assumptions and guidance on how to assess their validity in practice.
* **Relationship to Existing Imputation/Weighting Methods:** The paper positions its work as complementary to existing missing data techniques. However, a more detailed comparison of the proposed approach with methods like multiple imputation, inverse probability weighting, and full information maximum likelihood would be valuable.  Specifically, how does this m-graph approach offer advantages over these well-established methods?
* **Corollary 2's practical application:** While mathematically sound, applying Corollary 2 to determine non-recoverability in complex graphs might be challenging. Providing practical guidelines or algorithmic support for identifying the conditions described would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental problem in statistical inference—dealing with missing data—and provides novel theoretical insights.
* The extension of recoverability results to causal queries is a significant advancement, potentially enabling more robust inference in challenging MNAR scenarios.
* The rigorous mathematical treatment and clear organization demonstrate a high level of scientific quality.
* The paper builds upon and extends the existing literature on m-graphs and missing data.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The paper’s density and mathematical rigor may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
* The lack of empirical validation weakens the demonstration of practical impact.
* The assumptions underlying the theorems require careful consideration, and their sensitivity is not fully explored.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference and missing data. While its density and lack of empirical evaluation are weaknesses, the novelty of the results, the rigorous mathematical treatment, and the clear organization outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.

**Suggested Revisions:**

* **Improve Accessibility:** Add intuitive explanations alongside the formal definitions and theorems. Consider including more illustrative examples.
* **Add Empirical Evaluation:** Include simulations or application to real-world datasets to demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods.
* **Discuss Assumption Sensitivity:** Thoroughly discuss the sensitivity of the results to violations of the assumptions.
* **Compare with Existing Methods:** Provide a more detailed comparison with existing missing data techniques (imputation, weighting, ML) and highlight the advantages of the m-graph approach.
* **Guidance for Corollary 2:**  Provide practical guidance for applying Corollary 2 to determine non-recoverability in complex graphs.
