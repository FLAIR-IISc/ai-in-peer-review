This paper addresses the critical problem of missing data, specifically focusing on graphical models and recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries. The authors extend previous work by presenting conditions for recovery even when standard identifiability assumptions are violated, a potentially significant contribution. However, despite the promising core ideas, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet pervasive, flaws in presentation, notation, and rigor. While the technical content *appears* sound, the lack of meticulousness undermines confidence in the correctness and impact of the results.

The introduction is adequate, but lacks specific motivation for *why* extending Mohan et al. [2013] is necessary beyond a general statement about attrition. The claim about detecting modeling assumption violations is intriguing but not elaborated upon. 

Section 2 introduces missingness graphs, but the notation is needlessly complex and inconsistent. For example, the definition of *V* as *V ∪ U ∪ V∗ ∪ R* is confusing; typically, *U* denotes unobserved confounders, and *V* represents the observed variables. The introduction of *V∗* as a proxy seems oddly specific and not immediately clear why it necessitates a separate symbol. The shorthand notations (e.g., *Wr*, *Wo*, *Wm*) are helpful, but not consistently applied throughout the paper. The definition of compatibility between manifest and underlying distributions (equation 1) is awkwardly presented and could be significantly clarified.

Section 3 refines the sequential factorization theorem. While the extension to include *R* variables is a logical step, the presentation lacks sufficient explanation of *why* the original theorem doesn't apply to longitudinal studies with interactions between missingness mechanisms. The minimality requirement in Definition 2 is crucial but not adequately emphasized. The example provided is helpful, but overly verbose and could be streamlined. The reiteration that neglecting minimality leads to inadmissible factorizations feels unnecessarily didactic.

Section 4 introduces algorithms for recovery when sequential factorization fails. The reference to Theorem 2 is vague; the reader is left to hunt for the details in the appendix. The definitions of collider path, *Rpart*, and *Mb(R(i))* are presented inline without sufficient context. The example in Figure 2 (b) is crucial but not clearly explained in terms of how *Rpart* and *Mb(R(i))* are determined.

Section 5 provides non-recoverability criteria, which is a valuable addition. However, Corollary 2 seems weakly stated and could be strengthened.

Section 6 discusses the recoverability of causal queries. The claim that causal effects can be recovered even when *Y* and *Ry* are not d-separable is the most significant result, but the presentation is convoluted. Example 3 is helpful, but the chain of reasoning leading to the recovery of *P(y|do(x))* is dense and requires careful parsing. The invocation of “Rule-2” and “Rule-3” of do-calculus without clear explanation feels abrupt.

Section 7 applies the results to attrition. The discussion is largely a repetition of the previous sections, with limited new insight. The statement that Garcia [2013] is incorrect is bold but requires more rigorous justification. 

Section 8 contains a superficial overview of related work. Important citations are missing (e.g., works on multiple imputation, inverse probability weighting, and shared-parameter models for missing data).

The writing style is generally repetitive and lacks conciseness. Minor grammatical errors and typos are scattered throughout the paper (e.g., “Gw, and” in Theorem 2). The formatting is inconsistent (e.g., spacing around equations). The proofs in the appendix were not reviewed in detail due to the time constraint, but given the issues in the main text, I suspect similar issues exist there.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Potential to advance the field of missing data analysis.
*   Theoretically sound (though requires careful verification).
*   Addresses an important and challenging problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Numerous minor flaws in presentation, notation, and rigor.
*   Lack of clarity and conciseness.
*   Inconsistent formatting and grammatical errors.
*   Superficial literature review.
*   The benefit of the theoretical results isn't convincingly demonstrated with practical examples beyond simple scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

Despite the potentially significant contributions of this work, the sheer number of minor flaws significantly detracts from its overall quality. While the ideas are promising, the lack of meticulousness undermines confidence in the correctness of the results and makes the paper difficult to read and understand. A substantial revision addressing the issues outlined above is required before this paper could be considered for publication at NIPS. I recommend rejection in its current form.
