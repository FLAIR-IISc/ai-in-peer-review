## Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

**Summary:**

This paper extends previous work on missing data recovery using missingness graphs (m-graphs). It refines the sequential factorization theorem, presents algorithms for cases where this theorem fails, introduces criteria for non-recoverability, and crucially, demonstrates that causal queries (P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relationships are not, even when Y is not d-separable from its missingness mechanism. The authors apply these results specifically to the problem of attrition.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** Handling missing data is a fundamental challenge in observational and experimental studies, and this work addresses it with a rigorous theoretical framework.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper builds upon established concepts from causal inference (do-calculus, graphical models) and provides novel theoretical results regarding recoverability conditions.  The extension of sequential factorization (Theorem 1) is a significant contribution, particularly for complex missingness structures.
* **Novelty of Causal Query Recovery:** The demonstration that causal effects can be recovered even when probabilistic relationships are non-recoverable is a key and surprising result. This is a strong differentiator from existing work.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition provides a concrete example of the utility of the developed theory.
* **Thoroughness:** The paper is comprehensive, covering both recoverability *and* non-recoverability conditions, and provides detailed proofs in the appendix.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Notational Overhead:** The paper relies heavily on notation, which, while precise, can be challenging for readers unfamiliar with the specific framework of m-graphs.  A more gradual introduction of the notation, perhaps with a running example, might improve accessibility.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the theoretical results are strong, the paper lacks empirical validation.  Simulations or application to real-world datasets would significantly strengthen the claims. It’s unclear how robust the conditions for recoverability are in practice.
* **Complexity:** The theorems and corollaries are dense and require careful study. While this isn’t necessarily a flaw, it increases the barrier to entry for readers.  More intuitive explanations of the core ideas could be beneficial.
* **Related Work Could Be Expanded:** While the authors mention related work, a more extensive discussion of recent advances in missing data imputation techniques (e.g., multiple imputation, deep learning-based methods) would be helpful to contextualize the contribution.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs supporting its claims. The extension of previous work is well-motivated and logically presented. It is a complete piece of work *in terms of theoretical development*, but lacks the empirical validation typical of NIPS papers. The authors are careful to discuss limitations and conditions under which the results hold.
* **Clarity:** The paper is clearly *structured*, but the density of notation and the complexity of the theorems make it challenging to follow.  Improvements in accessibility could be made.
* **Originality:**  The work is highly original, especially the result regarding causal query recoverability.  It presents a novel application of m-graphs and extends existing theory in a significant way. The authors adequately reference related work.
* **Significance:**  The results are potentially significant.  The ability to recover causal effects from data with complex missingness patterns could have a substantial impact on fields such as epidemiology, social science, and clinical trials. However, the practical impact hinges on the robustness and applicability of the derived conditions, which are currently unclear without empirical evaluation.



**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and novel results.
* Important problem with broad applicability.
* Clear advancement of the state-of-the-art in missing data recovery, particularly for causal inference.
* Thorough and rigorous treatment of the topic.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of empirical validation.
* High notational complexity and challenging readability.
* Limited discussion of competing approaches.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of missing data and causal inference. The novelty of the results, particularly concerning causal query recovery, is compelling. However, the lack of empirical evaluation is a significant concern.  

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors adding some empirical validation to their claims.  This could be in the form of simulations demonstrating the applicability of their theorems, or an application to a publicly available dataset with missing data.  Additionally, improvements to the clarity and accessibility of the paper, such as a more gradual introduction of notation and more intuitive explanations of the core concepts, would be beneficial.  Addressing the points in the "Weaknesses" section would elevate this paper from a strong theoretical contribution to a truly impactful work.
