

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in data analysis: recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. The authors extend the work of Mohan et al. (2013) by introducing Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to formally represent missingness mechanisms and derive conditions for recoverability of various queries. The paper makes two key theoretical contributions: (1) general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (joint and conditional distributions), and (2) the surprising result that causal queries P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are inseparable (a condition that prevents recovery of probabilistic queries).

The paper builds on previous NIPS work by Mohan et al. (2013) and Mohan and Pearl (2014), extending their framework to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact with each other (e.g., in longitudinal studies). It also addresses a gap in the literature by providing formal non-recoverability criteria, which previous work lacked.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical results supported by proofs in the appendix. The authors carefully distinguish between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries, and provide clear examples (e.g., Example 3) to illustrate their key insight about causal queries. The paper acknowledges limitations, particularly the need for assumptions about the missingness process. However, the lack of empirical validation (only theoretical results are provided) is a notable limitation that weakens the practical significance.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and generally well-written. The notation is consistent, and the theorems are logically presented. The examples effectively illustrate the theoretical concepts. However, some sections (particularly Theorem 2 and its corollary) contain complex notation (e.g., Rpart, Mb(R(i)), Pasub(R(i))) that would benefit from additional explanation or a more intuitive description. The connection between the theoretical results and practical implementation could be strengthened.

**Originality:** This work significantly extends the prior literature by demonstrating that causal queries can be recovered even when probabilistic queries cannot. This is a novel and important insight that challenges the prevailing understanding of missing data limitations. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work (e.g., Garcia, 2013), particularly in addressing the interaction between multiple missingness mechanisms. The application to attrition problems in longitudinal studies provides concrete, practical value.

**Significance:** The problem of missing data is pervasive across scientific domains, making this work highly relevant. The paper provides a unified graphical framework for addressing missing data, with the causal query results being particularly significant as they overcome a known limitation of previous approaches. The results have potential applications in epidemiology, social sciences, and machine learning (e.g., recommender systems with missing data).

**Strengths:**
1. The key insight that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y and Ry are inseparable is novel and significant.
2. The paper provides formal non-recoverability criteria, filling an important gap in the literature.
3. The theoretical framework is comprehensive and builds logically on previous work.
4. The application to attrition problems demonstrates practical relevance.

**Weaknesses:**
1. Lack of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess practical utility.
2. Some theoretical results (Theorem 2) are complex and could benefit from more intuitive explanation.
3. The connection between the theoretical framework and real-world implementation is underdeveloped.
4. The paper doesn't sufficiently contrast with recent work on causal inference with missing data (e.g., by Shpitser and Pearl, 2008).

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of missing data analysis, with the causal query results being particularly noteworthy. The work is well-executed, technically sound, and addresses an important problem.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Add a short empirical section demonstrating the application of the framework to a small synthetic dataset.
2. Simplify the presentation of Theorem 2 and its corollary by providing a more intuitive explanation of the key concepts (Rpart, Mb(R(i))).
3. Strengthen the discussion of practical implementation by describing how one might apply the theorems to real data.
4. Expand the related work section to better contrast with recent causal inference work on missing data.
5. Clarify the relationship between the "d*-separation" concept (Definition 4) and standard d-separation.

The paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of the limitations of the approach (e.g., how sensitive the results are to assumption violations) and a clearer statement of the practical conditions under which the proposed methods would be applicable. Despite these limitations, the theoretical contribution is substantial and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
