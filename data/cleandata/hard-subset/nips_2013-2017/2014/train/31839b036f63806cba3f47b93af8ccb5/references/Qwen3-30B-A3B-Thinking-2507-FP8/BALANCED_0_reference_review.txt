

# Review: Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data

## Summary
This paper extends Mohan et al. (2013) by developing a formal graphical framework ("Missingness Graphs" or m-graphs) to analyze recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors present theoretical conditions under which joint/conditional distributions can be recovered, demonstrate that causal queries P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable, and apply these results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies. The work builds on Mohan et al.'s foundational m-graph framework while addressing gaps in the literature regarding non-recoverability criteria and causal query recoverability.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound theoretical contributions with rigorous proofs (provided in the appendix). The authors carefully distinguish between recoverability conditions for probabilistic versus causal queries, which is a significant conceptual advance. The distinction between "separability" requirements for probabilistic queries versus causal queries is particularly well-motivated and theoretically rich. The paper properly acknowledges the limitations of previous work (Mohan et al. 2013) and demonstrates how their extensions address these gaps.

**Originality:** The paper makes several novel contributions: (1) General conditions for recovering probabilistic queries beyond sequential factorization; (2) First formal non-recoverability criteria for joint/conditional distributions; (3) The key insight that causal effects may be recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not (Theorem 4); (4) Necessary and sufficient conditions for causal effect recovery in attrition problems (Theorem 6). These contributions significantly extend Mohan et al. (2013) and fill important gaps in the missing data literature.

**Significance:** Missing data is a pervasive problem across scientific disciplines, and this work provides a theoretically grounded framework for determining when consistent estimates are possible. The insight that causal effects may be recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not challenges prevailing assumptions in the literature (e.g., Garcia 2013) and has important practical implications for fields like clinical trials and social sciences. The application to attrition problems demonstrates concrete relevance to longitudinal studies.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written with precise definitions. The examples (particularly Example 1 and 3) effectively illustrate the theoretical concepts. The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability is clearly articulated. The notation is consistent and well-motivated.

## Weaknesses

**Lack of empirical validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical with no empirical demonstrations of the proposed methods. While theoretical contributions are valuable, NIPS generally expects papers to include at least some experimental validation of the proposed approach. Without empirical results, it's difficult to assess how well these conditions perform in practice or how they compare to existing methods.

**Limited discussion of computational aspects:** The paper presents theoretical conditions but doesn't address computational complexity or practical implementation challenges. For a framework to be useful in real-world applications, these considerations would be important to discuss.

**Underdeveloped connection to existing missing data methods:** While the related work section covers relevant literature, it could better position this work relative to established missing data methods (e.g., multiple imputation, inverse probability weighting). A brief discussion of how this graphical framework complements or differs from these approaches would strengthen the paper's significance.

**Conceptual accessibility:** Some key concepts (like d*-separation) are introduced without sufficient intuition. A few more plain-language explanations or analogies would help readers unfamiliar with causal inference.

## Recommendation

This paper makes significant theoretical contributions to the field of missing data analysis, particularly regarding the recoverability of causal queries. The core insights are novel, well-supported, and address important gaps in the literature. The work has clear significance for researchers dealing with missing data in causal inference settings.

However, the purely theoretical nature of the paper without empirical validation represents a notable weakness for NIPS, which values both theoretical and empirical contributions. The absence of experimental results limits the paper's impact and makes it difficult to assess practical utility.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include a small empirical validation section demonstrating the proposed conditions on synthetic or real-world datasets (even if preliminary).
2. Discuss computational complexity and practical implementation considerations.
3. Better contextualize the approach relative to existing missing data methods (multiple imputation, inverse probability weighting).
4. Add a few more intuitive explanations for key concepts like d*-separation.
5. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the framework might handle practical challenges like model misspecification.

## Conclusion

This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances the understanding of missing data mechanisms and recoverability conditions. The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability is particularly valuable and represents a significant conceptual advance. While the purely theoretical nature of the paper is a drawback for NIPS, the quality of the theoretical work and the importance of the problem area make it a noteworthy contribution. With empirical validation and some additional practical considerations, this would be a compelling NIPS submission. As it stands, the paper's theoretical soundness and novelty make it a borderline candidate for acceptance, but the lack of empirical results tips the balance toward rejection.
