This paper presents a theoretical framework based on "missingness graphs" (m-graphs) to determine the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. The work extends the results of Mohan et al. [2013] by generalizing a sequential factorization theorem, introducing new criteria for recoverability in cases where factorization fails, and establishing novel graphical conditions for non-recoverability. A central and interesting claim is that causal queries, such as P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries, like P(y,x), are not. The authors apply these theoretical findings to the specific problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

This work builds upon a line of research that uses graphical models to formalize the assumptions underlying missing data mechanisms, moving beyond the traditional MAR/MCAR/MNAR taxonomy. The aim is to provide a principled way to assess whether a query of interest can be consistently estimated before attempting estimation.

### Strengths

*   **Theoretical Contributions:** The paper presents several new theoretical results that advance the graphical modeling approach to missing data. Theorem 3, which provides graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, is a particularly valuable contribution, as it helps practitioners identify situations where consistent estimation is impossible under the given model assumptions. The distinction drawn between the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries in Section 6 is insightful and highlights a key advantage of the causal inference framework.
*   **Methodological Rigor:** The paper is formally sound. The definitions are stated precisely, and the theorems appear to be logical extensions of the underlying graphical framework. The use of small, illustrative examples helps to clarify the application of the proposed theorems.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins with necessary background, progressively introduces more complex theorems for recoverability, addresses non-recoverability, and then moves to the specific case of causal queries and attrition. This makes the paper's dense theoretical content relatively easy to follow.

### Weaknesses

The primary weaknesses of this paper stem from its purely theoretical nature and its disconnect from established practice and empirical validation.

*   **Complete Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper contains no experiments, simulations, or applications to real data. While the theoretical results are interesting, their practical utility remains entirely speculative. For a venue like NIPS, the absence of any empirical evidence is a major shortcoming. How would an estimator derived from these recoverability criteria perform in finite samples? How does it compare to standard, well-understood methods like Multiple Imputation (MI), Inverse Probability Weighting (IPW), or Expectation-Maximization (EM) under various missing data scenarios? Without such a comparison, it is impossible to judge whether this formal framework offers any real-world advantage over established methods. The claims are not benchmarked against any existing approach.
*   **The M-Graph Oracle Assumption:** The entire framework rests on the strong, and often unrealistic, assumption that the practitioner can specify the complete and correct m-graph. This graph encodes detailed causal assumptions about both the data-generating process and the missingness mechanism. In practice, these mechanisms are rarely known with certainty. The paper does not discuss the sensitivity of its results to model misspecification. The binary nature of graphical recoverability (either possible or not) can be brittle; a single misspecified edge could change the conclusion entirely, whereas established statistical methods often exhibit more graceful degradation.
*   **Limited Practical Guidance:** The paper successfully answers the question of *if* a query is recoverable, but provides little guidance on *how* to perform the estimation in practice, beyond the factorization formulas. It does not discuss the development of practical estimators, their statistical properties (e.g., variance, efficiency), or computational considerations. This limits the paper's impact for practitioners who need to actually analyze incomplete datasets.
*   **Niche Framework:** The m-graph formalism, while elegant, is not widely used in the machine learning or statistics communities that regularly deal with missing data. The paper does little to connect its graphical conditions to the more conventional language of conditional independence assumptions used to justify methods like MI or EM. This insularity makes the work less accessible and its contributions harder to integrate with the vast existing literature on missing data.

### Summary and Recommendation

This paper presents a solid piece of theoretical work that extends a formal graphical framework for reasoning about missing data. Its key strengths are its theoretical novelty and methodological rigor.

However, the weaknesses are significant. The complete absence of empirical validation or comparison to established baseline methods makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the contributions. The work exists in a theoretical vacuum, relying on the strong assumption that the true causal graph of the missingness process is known a priori. While identifying conditions for recoverability is an important first step, it is not sufficient for a contribution to be considered complete or impactful for the NIPS community, which generally values a clear connection between theory and practice. The ideas presented are innovative, but in their current unproven state, it is difficult to endorse them.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides novel, theoretically sound graphical criteria for both recoverability and non-recoverability of queries from missing data.
2.  The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability is an important and well-articulated insight.
3.  The paper is clearly written and methodologically rigorous within its chosen framework.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  No experimental or simulation results to demonstrate the practical utility or performance of the proposed methods.
2.  No comparison against established, widely used methods for handling missing data (e.g., MI, IPW).
3.  Relies on the strong, untestable assumption that the true m-graph is known, without addressing model misspecification.
4.  The contribution is an incremental development within a niche theoretical framework, with unclear connections to mainstream practice.

Given my preference for well-validated and methodologically established approaches, I lean towards recommending **rejection**. The work is an interesting theoretical exercise, but it does not meet the standard of providing extensive evidence for its claims. The authors should be strongly encouraged to validate their theoretical framework with simulation studies and comparisons to standard methods to demonstrate a tangible benefit to researchers and practitioners.
