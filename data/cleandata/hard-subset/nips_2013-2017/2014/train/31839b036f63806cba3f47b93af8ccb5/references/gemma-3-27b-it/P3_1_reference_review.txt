## Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant and valuable extension to the framework of missingness graphs, offering refined theoretical conditions for recovering both probabilistic and, crucially, causal queries in the presence of missing data. The work builds logically on prior research (Mohan et al. 2013, Pearl & Mohan 2013) and addresses a fundamental problem in observational studies. While the paper is dense and requires careful reading, it provides a rigorous and insightful contribution to the field. I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions to improve clarity and accessibility.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper extends the concept of ‘Missingness Graphs’ (m-graphs) to provide sufficient conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries when data is missing. The core contribution lies in refining the sequential factorization theorem, handling more complex missingness patterns (where missingness mechanisms influence each other), and demonstrating that causal effects (specifically, P(y|do(x))) can be recovered even when the underlying probabilistic relationships (like P(y,x)) are not. A key result is that causal recoverability can occur *without* requiring separability between a variable and its missingness mechanism – a stark contrast to traditional MAR assumptions. The paper situates itself well within the existing literature on missing data, acknowledging work on deletion methods, maximum likelihood estimation, inverse probability weighting, and the growing role of graphical models in addressing this challenge. The review of related work is comprehensive and accurately reflects the state of the art.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is mathematically sound, with clearly stated theorems and proofs (provided in the appendix). The extension of sequential factorization (Theorem 1) and the non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) are valuable contributions.
* **Causal Focus:** The demonstration of recoverable causal effects even under non-separability is a particularly strong point. This significantly expands the applicability of m-graphs to real-world scenarios where MAR assumptions may not hold.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition (Section 7) highlights the practical implications of the work and demonstrates its potential for addressing a common challenge in longitudinal studies. The examples provided (Figures 1, 2, 3, and 4) are helpful in illustrating the concepts and theorems.
* **Completeness:** The paper is relatively complete, offering both sufficient conditions for recovery *and* criteria for identifying non-recoverability. This provides a valuable tool for practitioners.
* **Clear Notation:** Despite the complexity of the subject, the notation is generally consistent and well-defined.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Accessibility:** The paper is *very* dense and technically demanding.  The initial sections, introducing the m-graph framework, are particularly challenging for readers unfamiliar with the existing literature. More intuitive explanations and motivating examples early on would significantly improve readability.
* **Example Complexity:** While the examples are helpful, some (e.g., Example 1) are quite complex and require close attention to follow. Simplifying these examples, or adding more step-by-step walkthroughs, could improve understanding.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the paper focuses on theoretical results, a small-scale empirical demonstration (even simulated data) to illustrate the recoverability in practice would strengthen the impact of the work.
* **Corollary Overload:** The numerous corollaries, while logically derived, sometimes feel dense and contribute to the overall difficulty of the paper.  Consolidating some of these, or prioritizing the most impactful ones, might improve clarity.
* **Do-Calculus Familiarity:** The discussion of causal recovery (Section 6) relies heavily on familiarity with do-calculus. While this is reasonable for the NIPS audience, briefly explaining the relevant rules (like Rule-2 and Rule-3) within the text would be beneficial.

**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 2.1 (Recoverability):** Consider adding a brief illustrative example of a non-recoverable query to complement the definition of recoverability.
* **Definition 4 (d∗-separation):** This definition is relatively advanced. A short parenthetical explanation of its relevance to causal inference would be helpful.
* **Section 7 (Attrition):** While the discussion of simple attrition is useful, consider briefly mentioning the limitations of this scenario and potential avenues for extending the framework to more complex attrition mechanisms.
* **Theorem 6:** Clarify what type of data is required to test the conditions in this theorem. Is it sufficient to observe a complete dataset, or is it necessary to have information about the missing data mechanism?



**5. Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Significant theoretical extension to m-graphs with strong implications for causal inference.
* Addresses a crucial problem in observational studies – missing data.
* Rigorous mathematical framework with clear theorems and proofs.
* Novel result demonstrating recoverable causal effects under non-separability.

**Con:**

* High technical barrier to entry due to density and complexity.
* Limited empirical validation.
* Potential for improvement in clarity and accessibility.




**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should focus on improving the clarity and accessibility of the paper, particularly in the early sections. Adding a small empirical demonstration and simplifying some of the examples would further strengthen the contribution. Despite its density, this paper presents a significant and valuable addition to the literature on missing data and causal inference, and is well-suited for the NIPS audience.
