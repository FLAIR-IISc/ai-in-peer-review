This paper tackles the important and notoriously difficult problem of causal inference with missing data, framed through the lens of missingness graphs. The core idea – extending recoverability conditions beyond probabilistic queries to causal effects, even when the underlying probabilistic relationships are non-recoverable – is genuinely novel and represents a potentially significant advance. The authors build upon their previous work (Mohan et al., 2013) and demonstrate scenarios where causal queries *can* be identified despite missing data challenges that would traditionally preclude estimation. The theoretical results, while dense, appear well-developed, and the examples help illustrate the key concepts. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The central finding—causal effect recoverability even with non-recoverable probabilistic relationships—is a significant contribution. It challenges conventional wisdom and opens new avenues for causal inference in practical settings.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a rigorous mathematical framework for analyzing recoverability, building on existing work in missingness graphs and causal inference. Theorems 1-6 provide clear conditions for identifying recoverable queries.
* **Clear Focus:** The paper maintains a tight focus on the specific problem of recoverability, and the mathematical development supports this goal effectively.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition (a common real-world problem) enhances the paper’s relevance and potential impact.
* **Extension of Prior Work:** The authors successfully extend existing sequential factorization theorems to handle more complex missing data patterns.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Accessibility:** The mathematical notation and terminology are dense. While the examples help, a reader unfamiliar with missingness graphs and the “do-calculus” will likely struggle to grasp the key ideas. The initial sections, defining the framework, could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper is heavily theoretical. While the examples are illustrative, there's a notable lack of empirical evaluation. Demonstrating the practical applicability of these theorems on real or simulated datasets would substantially strengthen the work. It’s unclear how sensitive the results are to model misspecification.
* **Assumptions:** The theorems rely on several assumptions about the m-graph structure (e.g., no latent confounders between variables and their missingness mechanisms). The robustness of the results to violations of these assumptions is not discussed.
* **Corollary 2's strength**: Corollary 2 is stated as a non-recoverability criteria. But theorem 3 seems like a more general statement and could potentially be used to derive corollary 2. Therefore, it might be redundant. 



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core idea is genuinely novel and has the potential to significantly impact the field of causal inference.
* The theoretical development is rigorous and well-supported.
* The paper addresses an important practical problem (missing data) with a novel approach.
* Even with the limitations, the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation is a significant weakness.
* The high level of mathematical formality may limit the paper’s accessibility and impact.
* The strong reliance on assumptions requires further investigation of robustness.
* Minor issues in clarity and potentially redundant proofs.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a novel and theoretically strong contribution to the field of causal inference with missing data. While the lack of empirical validation and the density of the mathematical notation are weaknesses, the originality and potential significance of the findings outweigh these concerns. I lean strongly towards acceptance. The authors have identified a critical gap in the literature and propose a promising framework for addressing it. I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the accessibility concerns by adding more intuitive explanations and potentially including a small-scale empirical demonstration to validate the theoretical results. While a thorough empirical study is desirable, even a limited simulation study would bolster the paper’s credibility.
