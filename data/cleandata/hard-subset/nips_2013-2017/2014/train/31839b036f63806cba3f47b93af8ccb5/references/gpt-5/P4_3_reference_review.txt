Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data subject to missingness, using missingness graphs (m-graphs) that encode both substantive variables and missingness indicators. Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors:

- Generalize ordered factorizations to include missingness indicators R, yielding a strengthened sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) that applies when missingness mechanisms influence each other (e.g., R-to-R edges). This enables recovery of joint/conditional distributions in settings where previous sufficient conditions do not apply.

- Provide a new recovery result (Theorem 2) for semi-Markovian models when no admissible sequence exists, via a decomposition based on collider-path structure and partitions of R, with an associated Markov blanket Mb(R(i)). A corollary handles the subclass with no latent confounding between R and substantive variables.

- Introduce necessary graphical conditions for non-recoverability of joint distributions (Theorem 3) and of conditionals (Corollary 2), offering formal obstruction criteria that were largely missing in the literature.

- Show that certain identifiable causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when corresponding probabilistic quantities (e.g., P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are not, including cases where Y is inseparable from its missingness mechanism (Theorem 4). The argument leverages do-calculus, d*-separation, and dormant independencies.

- Specialize to “simple attrition” (one partially observed outcome), proving necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for recoverability of the joint (Theorem 5) and causal effects (Theorem 6), correcting some claims in prior work (e.g., Garcia 2013).

The work relates to foundational identification results (Shpitser & Pearl 2006) by assuming identifiability in the substantive model and focusing on recoverability under missingness. It connects to MAR/MCAR notions (Rubin 1976), emphasizing when graph-blind methods suffice. Relative to Mohan et al. (2013), the paper broadens sufficient conditions (via general ordered factorizations including R), treats cases with interacting missingness mechanisms, supplies explicit non-recoverability conditions, and gives necessary-and-sufficient criteria in the attrition case.

Strengths
- Technical contributions: The extended sequential factorization (Theorem 1) is a clear and useful generalization; Theorem 2 provides a recovery formula beyond admissible sequences in semi-Markovian settings; Theorem 3 and Corollary 2 supply crisp obstruction criteria; Theorems 5–6 give necessary and sufficient conditions for a practically important special case (attrition), including explicit estimands.

- Conceptual impact: Demonstrating recoverability of causal effects when Y and Ry are inseparable is insightful, clarifying differences between probabilistic and causal recoverability.

- Rigor and grounding: Results are grounded in standard graphical concepts (d-separation, ADMG properties), do-calculus, and positivity assumptions; proofs are said to be in the appendix.

- Relevance: Missing data is pervasive; m-graphs provide a principled way to articulate assumptions and decide recoverability without defaulting to MAR.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and presentation: The exposition is dense, with heavy notation and several new sets (e.g., Rpart, Mb(R(i))) introduced quickly. The procedure for constructing admissible sequences and partitions is not given as an explicit algorithm. Some definitions (e.g., Theorem 4’s conditions and the search for Z, W) are hard to operationalize for practitioners. References have formatting issues, and figures are central but not fully explained in text.

- Practical guidance: While sections mention “algorithms,” the paper mainly presents theorems and examples; it lacks concrete pseudo-code, computational complexity, or a decision procedure that integrates the proposed criteria into a unified tool. There are no empirical demonstrations (simulations or case studies) to illustrate finite-sample behavior, robustness to misspecification of the m-graph, or performance relative to MAR-based baselines.

- Assumptions and scope: The approach requires a correct m-graph and positivity over complete cases; sensitivity to deviations is not explored. The assumption that R variables are not parents of V or U is adopted but its implications and limitations could be discussed more explicitly.

Quality
- Technical soundness appears strong: results align with established causal inference theory and ADMG properties. Non-recoverability criteria and attrition characterizations are well-motivated. However, the operational steps to find separating sets and verify d*-separation (Theorem 4) could be better specified, ideally with an algorithmic checker.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized, but notation-heavy. Minimality in Definition 2 is important and well-motivated via Example 1. Still, more step-by-step guidance, a summary flowchart for deciding recoverability (probabilistic vs. causal), and clearer figure descriptions would improve readability. Reference formatting needs fixing; some typos remain.

Originality
- The contributions beyond Mohan et al. (2013) are meaningful: inclusion of R in ordered factorizations, general recovery without admissible sequences, explicit non-recoverability criteria, and necessary-and-sufficient results for attrition. The causal-recoverability-despite-inseparability result is notable.

Significance
- The results advance theory for MNAR problems and can influence practice by clarifying when and how causal effects remain recoverable. The attrition results and non-recoverability criteria are likely useful to researchers designing analyses with missing outcomes. Wider practical impact would benefit from algorithmic tooling and empirical demonstrations.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide an explicit algorithm (with pseudo-code) to:
  - Search for admissible sequences (Theorem 1).
  - Construct R partitions and Mb(R(i)) and apply Theorem 2.
  - Check Theorem 4 conditions (d*-separation, dormant independencies) and construct Z, W automatically.
  - Produce an overall decision graph for recoverability.

- Add simulated case studies illustrating:
  - Recovery in finite samples vs. MAR approaches.
  - Sensitivity to graph misspecification and violations of positivity.

- Clarify assumptions (e.g., R not parenting V/U) and discuss when they may fail.

- Improve notation summary and figure captions; fix references.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid and novel theoretical advances extending prior NIPS work.
- Provides both positive (recoverability) and negative (non-recoverability) criteria.
- Clarifies an important gap: causal effects can be recoverable when probabilistic queries are not.
- Necessary-and-sufficient characterization for attrition is practically valuable.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited practical guidance: no end-to-end algorithmic framework or empirical evaluation.
- Some conditions (e.g., Theorem 4) are hard to verify in practice without tooling.
- Exposition and references need polishing.

Overall, this is a technically strong theoretical paper that extends the state of the art on missing data with graphical models. With improved operationalization and empirical illustration, its impact would increase substantially.
