

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper extends the Missingness Graph (m-graph) framework introduced by Mohan et al. (2013) and Mohan and Pearl (2014) to develop general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors build upon previous work by providing new theoretical results that characterize when joint/conditional distributions and causal effects (P(y|do(x))) can be recovered. The key theoretical contribution is demonstrating that causal queries may be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations are not, particularly when Y and its missingness mechanism (Ry) are not d-separable.

The paper makes significant theoretical contributions through several new theorems. Theorem 1 generalizes the sequential factorization approach to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact with one another (e.g., longitudinal studies with edges between R variables). Theorem 2 provides necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering joint distributions in a broader class of problems than previously addressed. Theorem 3 establishes novel non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions. Most notably, Theorem 4 demonstrates that causal queries can be recoverable even when Y and Ry are not separable (as shown in Example 3), which represents a key theoretical insight beyond previous work.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical development. The authors provide formal definitions, theorems, and proofs (referenced to the appendix), establishing clear mathematical conditions for recoverability. The paper carefully distinguishes between recoverable and non-recoverable cases through Theorem 3 and Corollary 2, demonstrating a balanced assessment of the work's limitations. The examples (Example 1, Example 2, Example 3) effectively illustrate the theoretical results. The paper addresses a well-defined problem in missing data analysis with comprehensive mathematical treatment.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors build logically from definitions to increasingly complex results. The use of formal notation is consistent and appropriate for the domain. The examples provide concrete illustrations of abstract concepts. The paper adequately informs readers about the problem and solutions, though the technical depth may challenge readers without strong background in graphical models and causal inference. The appendix contains proofs for all theoretical results, supporting reproducibility.

**Originality**: The paper makes significant theoretical contributions that advance beyond Mohan et al. (2013) and Mohan and Pearl (2014). The key novelty is the demonstration that causal queries can be recovered even when probabilistic queries are not, which is a substantial theoretical insight. The paper provides new graphical criteria for recoverability that are not present in previous work. The related work section (Section 8) adequately positions the paper within the broader literature on missing data, highlighting how this work extends beyond previous approaches that focus primarily on probabilistic queries or assume MAR/MCAR mechanisms.

**Significance**: Missing data is a pervasive problem across scientific disciplines, making this work highly relevant. The paper advances the state of the art by providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of both probabilistic and causal queries under MNAR mechanisms. The application to attrition problems (Section 7) demonstrates practical utility. The theoretical insights about causal queries being recoverable when probabilistic ones are not represent a significant conceptual advance that could change how researchers approach missing data problems in causal inference. The paper provides a formal framework that could guide future methodological development in missing data analysis.

**Strengths**: 
- Provides necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of multiple query types
- Demonstrates the key theoretical insight that causal queries can be recoverable when probabilistic queries are not
- Extends the m-graph framework to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact
- Comprehensive treatment of both recoverable and non-recoverable cases
- Clear examples illustrating the theoretical results

**Weaknesses**:
- Limited empirical validation on real-world datasets (the paper focuses on theoretical results without demonstrating application to real data)
- The paper could more explicitly contrast its theoretical results with recent machine learning approaches to missing data (e.g., deep learning methods for missing data imputation)
- The technical depth may limit accessibility for practitioners without strong background in causal inference

**Conclusion**: This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of missing data analysis by extending the m-graph framework to handle causal queries under MNAR mechanisms. The key insight that causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not represents a significant conceptual advance. The paper is technically sound, well-structured, and makes clear contributions to the literature. While the lack of empirical validation on real datasets is a limitation, the theoretical rigor and novelty of the results justify acceptance. The work provides a valuable formal framework for understanding and addressing missing data problems in causal inference, which has significant potential for practical application in experimental science. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it advances the theoretical foundations of a fundamental problem in statistical learning.
