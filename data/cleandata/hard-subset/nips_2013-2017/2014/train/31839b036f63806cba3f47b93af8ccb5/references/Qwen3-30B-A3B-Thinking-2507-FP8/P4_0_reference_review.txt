

This paper extends the Missingness Graph (m-graph) framework introduced by Mohan et al. (2013) to provide comprehensive graphical conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors address a fundamental challenge in empirical research where missing data can bias outcomes if not properly handled. Building on prior work that established m-graphs as a formal representation for missingness processes, the paper makes three key theoretical contributions: (1) general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (joint and conditional distributions) when sequential factorization fails; (2) novel results showing causal queries P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separated (a condition that prevents recovery of probabilistic queries); and (3) application to attrition problems with necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for causal effect recovery.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through precise definitions, formal theorems, and rigorous proofs provided in the appendix. The sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) generalizes prior work by handling cases where missingness mechanisms interact, while Theorem 2 provides a necessary and sufficient condition for recovering joint distributions in semi-Markovian models. The most significant theoretical contribution is the demonstration (Theorem 4 and Example 3) that causal queries can be recovered even when Y and Ry are connected by an inducing path, which represents a fundamental distinction from probabilistic query recovery. The application to attrition (Section 7) rectifies previous misconceptions in the literature (e.g., Garcia 2013) by providing necessary and sufficient conditions for causal effect recovery.

The paper is well-organized with clear exposition of concepts, including helpful examples (e.g., Example 1) that illustrate the necessity of minimality in factorization. The notation is consistent and carefully defined, making the theoretical developments accessible to readers familiar with graphical models. The related work section (Section 8) provides appropriate context, distinguishing the paper's contributions from prior methods like listwise deletion, multiple imputation, and existing causal approaches to missing data.

The paper's originality is substantial. While Mohan et al. (2013) established m-graphs and recoverability for probabilistic queries, this paper makes three key advances: (1) extending recoverability conditions to handle interacting missingness mechanisms; (2) establishing the surprising result that causal queries can be recovered without d-separation between Y and Ry; and (3) providing necessary and sufficient conditions for attrition problems. The paper thoroughly contextualizes its contributions against previous NIPS work (Mohan et al. 2013) and related literature, demonstrating clear differentiation from prior contributions.

The significance of this work is high. Missing data is a pervasive issue across scientific disciplines, and the paper provides theoretically grounded guidance for when and how to recover queries from missing data. The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic vs. causal queries has important practical implications for causal inference in fields like epidemiology and social sciences where attrition is common. The paper's results on attrition (Theorems 5-6) directly address a widespread practical challenge, correcting previous misconceptions in the literature.

Strengths: The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution by resolving a key limitation in missing data handling—showing causal queries can be recovered without the separability condition required for probabilistic queries. The necessary and sufficient conditions provided are valuable for practitioners. The paper's structure effectively builds from foundational concepts to more advanced results.

Weaknesses: While the theoretical results are strong, the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of how these conditions might be implemented in practice (e.g., how to estimate the required conditional probabilities from data). The paper could also more explicitly contrast its approach with alternative methods like multiple imputation, though this may be beyond the scope of a theoretical contribution.

The paper is clearly within NIPS scope, addressing a fundamental problem in machine learning and statistics with graphical models. It advances the field by providing rigorous theoretical conditions for recoverability that were previously lacking, particularly for causal queries. The results significantly extend the m-graph framework while maintaining theoretical rigor.

Pro: Clear theoretical advances in missing data handling with practical implications for causal inference; novel distinction between recoverability of probabilistic vs. causal queries; comprehensive conditions for various missing data mechanisms including attrition.

Con: Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges; could better contrast with alternative missing data methods.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that addresses an important problem with clear significance for the field. The paper makes substantial advances over previous work by Mohan et al. (2013) and provides valuable theoretical insights that will inform future research on missing data. The results are technically sound, well-justified, and significantly advance the state of the art in graphical models for missing data.
