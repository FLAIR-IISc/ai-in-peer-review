Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values using Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) as a causal representation of the missingness process (building on Mohan et al., NIPS 2013). The main contributions are:

- Extending ordered factorization to include missingness indicators R, yielding a general ordered factorization (Definition 2) and a strengthened sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1). This enables recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms interact (edges among R nodes), which were not covered by the earlier theorem in Mohan et al. 2013.

- Developing recovery methods when no admissible sequence exists. Theorem 2 provides a sufficient condition for joint recovery in semi-Markovian models by partitioning R into components not connected via neighbors or collider paths and using Markov blankets Mb(R(i)). A corollary handles the case without latent confounding between R and substantive variables.

- Introducing graphical non-recoverability criteria. Theorem 3 gives necessary conditions for the joint distribution to be recoverable (no adjacency or collider-only paths from X to Rx), and Corollary 2 provides necessary conditions for non-recoverability of P(Y|X).

- Showing that causal effects can be recoverable even when associated probabilistic queries are not. Using do-calculus and dormant independencies (d*-separation), Theorem 4 gives conditions (for |Vm|=1) under which P(y|do(x)) is recoverable despite inseparability of Y and Ry via an inducing path.

- Applying the above to attrition (one partially observed outcome). Theorem 5 gives necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering P(V) with |Vm|=1, and Theorem 6 provides necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering P(y|do(x)) (with an explicit estimand) under simple attrition. This corrects prior claims (e.g., Garcia 2013) by showing non-recoverability can occur even when X is not an ancestor of Ry, due to collider paths.

These results build directly on and extend Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), connect to identifiability via do-calculus (Shpitser & Pearl 2006; Pearl 2009), and complement classical MAR/MCAR results (Rubin 1976; Little & Rubin 2002) by giving graphical criteria and constructive estimands under MNAR.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The statements rely on standard causal graphical tools: d-separation, do-calculus, inducing paths, and semi-Markovian models. The constructive estimands (e.g., in Example 1 and Eq. 5) are consistent with the m-graph semantics (use of proxy variables V* and R=0 conditioning).

- Theorem 1’s emphasis on minimal conditioning sets is well-motivated; the example clarifies that chain-rule factorization can fail where minimality-based factorization succeeds.

- Theorem 2 provides a clear sufficient condition using R-partitions and Markov blankets defined via neighbors or collider paths; the resulting product formula is plausible and aligns with selection-model style factorizations.

- The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) offer necessary conditions that are easy to check and align with intuition: direct dependence between a variable and its missingness or certain collider-only connections preclude recovery.

- The causal section is careful to separate identifiability from recoverability and to leverage do-calculus to bypass non-recoverability of probabilistic factors. Theorem 4 (|Vm|=1) is supported by the d*-separation framework.

- Proofs are deferred to the appendix. Within the main text, derivations are correct at a high level; full verification depends on the appendix.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized, moving from probabilistic recovery to non-recoverability, then causal recovery, and finally an application to attrition.

- Notation is dense. Some symbols appear without prior clear definition or with ambiguous overloads (e.g., Xr vs Rx; RXm; Yi conditions in Theorem 1 item (2)). A consolidated notation table and consistent use (e.g., always Rx for the missingness indicator of X) would improve readability. Define all shorthand (Wr, Wo, Wm, Rw) once and reuse systematically.

- Several examples aid understanding; however, providing at least one fully worked numerical example would help readers reproduce estimands in practice.

- Reference formatting contains numerous errors and inconsistencies (author repetitions, placeholders like “? \Q2013\E”), which should be corrected.

Originality
- The extension of ordered factorizations to include R variables and the resulting broader recoverability via Theorem 1 appear novel relative to Mohan et al. 2013.

- The sufficient recovery conditions in semi-Markovian models without admissible sequences (Theorem 2), and the explicit necessary graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) fill gaps not treated formally in prior work.

- The demonstration that causal queries can be recoverable despite inseparability of Y and Ry, with precise conditions (Theorem 4), is a notable insight leveraging dormant independencies; the necessary and sufficient results for simple attrition (Theorems 5–6) further sharpen the state of the art.

Significance
- Missing data is pervasive in ML and causality; providing constructive estimands and graphical diagnostics under MNAR is impactful.

- The non-recoverability criteria and attrition results are likely to be adopted in practice and theory, and the causal/probabilistic contrast clarifies when do-calculus helps.

- Practical tooling (algorithms to search for admissible sequences, to compute R-partitions/Mb, and to check conditions) would further enhance impact; the paper gestures toward “general algorithms” but does not detail complexity or automation.

Strengths
- Clear expansion of recoverability conditions beyond prior work, with constructive estimands.
- Useful necessary non-recoverability criteria.
- Insightful causal results showing recovery beyond probabilistic separability.
- Application to attrition with necessary and sufficient conditions.

Weaknesses
- Heavy and occasionally inconsistent notation hinders accessibility; some conditions in Theorem 1 (item (2)) are opaque without the appendix.
- Limited discussion of algorithmic procedures, computational complexity, and automation for detecting conditions and constructing estimands.
- No empirical or synthetic validation to illustrate finite-sample behavior or robustness.
- Several results (e.g., Theorem 4) are restricted to |Vm|=1; the multi-variable missingness causal case remains open.
- Reference list formatting and completeness need fixing.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide a concise algorithmic summary: given an m-graph, how to automatically (i) find admissible sequences, (ii) compute R-partitions and Mb(R(i)), and (iii) detect the non-recoverability conditions. Discuss complexity.
- Clarify notation in Theorem 1 (define Xr, RXm, RXi, and the role of Z /∈ Xi). Consider a notation table.
- Extend discussion on how to select Z and W in Theorem 4 in an automated way via do-calculus or existing identifiability algorithms.
- Add a small synthetic example with numbers to illustrate the estimators derived.
- Correct and standardize references.

Arguments for acceptance
- Advances theory of recoverability with novel, practically checkable graphical conditions and constructive estimands.
- Clarifies an important conceptual point: causal effects can be recoverable when joint/conditional distributions are not.
- Provides necessary and sufficient results for a common practical scenario (attrition).

Arguments against acceptance
- Clarity and notation issues impede accessibility.
- Lack of algorithmic detail and empirical illustration.
- Some main causal results limited to single partially observed variable.

Overall, the paper presents substantial theoretical contributions that extend prior NIPS work on m-graphs and recoverability. With improved clarity, reference fixes, and some algorithmic guidance, it would be a strong contribution to the NeurIPS community.
