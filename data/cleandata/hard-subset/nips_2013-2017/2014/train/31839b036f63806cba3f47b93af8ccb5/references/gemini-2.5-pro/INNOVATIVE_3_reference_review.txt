### Review

**Summary:**
This paper extends the recently proposed framework of "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) to provide a more comprehensive graphical theory for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values. The work builds directly on Mohan et al. [2013], which introduced the m-graph formalism. The authors present several new theoretical results. First, they generalize the sequential factorization theorem to handle cases where missingness mechanisms can influence each other. Second, they provide a new theorem for recovering joint distributions in cases where factorization fails, based on the concepts of collider paths and Markov blankets. Third, and perhaps most importantly, they introduce the first formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, allowing researchers to identify when a query is impossible to estimate given the model assumptions. Finally, they investigate the recoverability of causal queries, showing—in a compelling and counter-intuitive result—that a causal effect P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic quantities like P(y,x) are not. The theory is applied to the practical problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** The most striking contribution of this paper is the demonstration that causal queries can be recoverable even when the joint distribution is not (Section 6). This is a bold, non-obvious, and powerful idea. It decouples the problem of causal inference from that of full distributional recovery in the context of missing data. By showing that `do-calculus` can be leveraged to navigate around missingness-induced confounding (as in Example 3), the paper opens up a genuinely new direction of inquiry. This insight alone makes the paper a significant contribution, as it suggests that many causal questions, previously thought lost to "Missing Not At Random" (MNAR) processes, might in fact be answerable.

2.  **Introduction of Non-Recoverability Criteria:** The development of graphical criteria for *non-recoverability* (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) is a crucial and often overlooked aspect of identifiability research. Providing sufficient conditions for recovery is useful, but providing necessary conditions that allow a researcher to definitively conclude that recovery is impossible is arguably just as valuable. It prevents wasted effort on futile estimation attempts and clarifies the precise structural assumptions needed for recovery. This is a novel and highly practical contribution to the field.

3.  **Rigorous Generalization of an Important Framework:** The paper successfully generalizes the m-graph framework to a wider and more realistic class of problems. Theorem 1 (extending sequential factorization) and Theorem 2 (providing an alternative recovery method) substantially increase the scope of the m-graph approach, particularly for models with interacting missingness mechanisms, which are common in real-world scenarios like longitudinal studies.

4.  **Clarity and Grounding:** Despite the theoretical density of the material, the authors make excellent use of minimal, clear examples (e.g., Figures 2, 3, and 4) to illustrate each key theorem and concept. The application of the framework to the problem of attrition (Section 7) effectively grounds the abstract theory in a concrete and widely recognized challenge, demonstrating the practical relevance of the results. The correction of prior claims in the attrition literature (Garcia [2013]) further underscores the paper's significance.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong and self-contained, the absence of any simulation studies or application to a real dataset is a notable omission. Simulations could have been used to verify that the proposed recovery algorithms indeed produce consistent estimates and to explore their finite-sample performance. While the persona I adopt prioritizes novel theory, the paper would be undeniably stronger and have broader impact if it included even a simple empirical demonstration of its core ideas.

2.  **Accessibility:** The paper assumes a high level of familiarity with advanced topics in graphical models and causality, including d-separation, do-calculus, inducing paths, and Markov blankets. This makes the work somewhat inaccessible to a broader machine learning audience that may not be steeped in the Pearlian causal inference literature. While this is not a flaw in the work itself, it may limit its immediate audience at a conference like NIPS.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a highly novel and significant idea: the separation of causal recoverability from probabilistic recoverability. This is a foundational insight.
*   It provides the first formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability in this context, a major practical and theoretical contribution.
*   It rigorously extends an important emerging framework for reasoning about missing data in a principled manner, moving beyond standard MAR/MCAR assumptions.
*   The work is technically sound, and the theoretical claims appear well-supported by the proofs provided in the appendix.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The work is purely theoretical, with no experiments or simulations to validate the proposed estimation strategies.
*   The contributions are extensions of a very recent paper (Mohan et al., 2013) by the same authors, which could be perceived by some as incremental. However, the conceptual leap in Section 6 and the introduction of non-recoverability criteria are significant enough to stand as a novel contribution.

**Recommendation:**
I strongly recommend accepting this paper. While the lack of empirical results is a limitation, it is a minor one in the face of the paper's bold and highly novel theoretical contributions. The core idea—that causal effects can be salvaged even when the joint distribution is lost to a complex missingness process—is a fundamental insight that could change how researchers approach problems with missing data. The paper advances a principled, graphical approach to a ubiquitous and difficult problem. This is exactly the kind of conceptually ambitious work that NIPS should champion, as it has the potential to open up new research avenues and provide other researchers with powerful new tools for thought.
