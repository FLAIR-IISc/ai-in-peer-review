This paper extends the framework of "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs), introduced in previous work by the authors, to establish novel theoretical conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values. The primary contributions include: (1) a generalization of the sequential factorization theorem to handle dependencies between missingness mechanisms; (2) a new theorem for recovering joint distributions in cases where sequential factorization is not possible, based on graphical properties like collider paths; (3) novel graphical criteria that preclude the recoverability of certain queries, a useful negative result; and (4) an important analysis showing that causal queries may be recoverable even when the underlying joint or conditional distributions are not. The authors apply these findings to the problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

### Strengths

*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper is theoretically rigorous, providing formal definitions and theorems with proofs supplied in the appendix. The work builds logically on a clear line of research in graphical models for causality and missing data.
*   **Novel Contributions:** The paper presents several novel ideas. The formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) are a valuable contribution, as they help delineate the boundaries of what is possible with this framework. Furthermore, the decoupling of recoverability for causal versus probabilistic queries (Section 6) is an insightful result that leverages deep concepts from the causal inference literature (e.g., do-calculus, d*-separation) and applies them to the missing data context.
*   **Conceptual Clarity:** The m-graph formalism provides a clear language for encoding assumptions about the missing data process. This can be a useful tool for researchers to reason about and communicate their modeling assumptions, moving beyond the often-imprecise MAR/MNAR categories.

### Weaknesses

The primary weaknesses of this paper stem from its purely theoretical nature and its isolation from established practice in handling missing data.

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper contains no experimental results. While the theoretical contributions are interesting, their practical relevance is unsubstantiated. There is no evaluation on synthetic data to verify that the proposed recovery algorithms produce consistent estimates, nor is there any application to a real-world dataset to demonstrate their utility. Established methods for missing data, such as Multiple Imputation (MI) or Expectation-Maximization (EM) for Maximum Likelihood estimation, have been tested and refined over decades. A new theoretical framework should be accompanied by compelling evidence of its practical value, especially in finite-sample regimes where asymptotic consistency may not be sufficient.
*   **Strong and Potentially Unrealistic Assumptions:** The entire framework is predicated on the availability of a correctly specified m-graph. This assumes that the practitioner has precise causal knowledge not only of the substantive variables but also of the mechanisms driving missingness. In many real-world applications, this is an exceedingly strong assumption that is difficult to verify. The paper does not discuss the robustness of its methods to misspecification of this graph, which is a critical concern for any practical application.
*   **Insufficient Comparison with Established Methods:** The paper situates its work in relation to classical methods (e.g., MI, EM, IPW) but does not provide a substantive comparison. It is noted that many methods assume data are MAR, but likelihood-based methods like EM can, in principle, be applied to MNAR problems provided a model for the missingness mechanism is specified. The paper fails to articulate the concrete advantages of its graphical approach over a carefully specified parametric MNAR model solved with a standard technique. It is unclear if the graphical conditions for recoverability are more or less restrictive than the conditions required for identification in parametric models. Without this comparison, the contribution remains disconnected from the vast body of existing work.
*   **Limited Scope of Some Results:** Several key theorems are presented with very specific graphical preconditions (e.g., Theorem 5 applies only to a single partially observed variable, |Vm|=1). While useful for building the theory, this raises questions about the scalability and applicability of the framework to more complex, realistic scenarios with multiple variables subject to intricate missingness patterns.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents novel, non-trivial theoretical results on an important problem.
*   The graphical criteria for non-recoverability and the analysis of causal query recovery are significant conceptual contributions to the literature on graphical models and missing data.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The work is entirely theoretical and lacks any empirical validation, making its practical significance difficult to assess. This is a substantial omission for a NIPS paper.
*   The reliance on a correctly specified causal graph for the missingness process is a very strong assumption that may limit the applicability of the method in practice.
*   The paper does not adequately compare its approach to established, well-understood methods for handling missing data, failing to demonstrate a clear advantage in terms of assumptions or performance.

In conclusion, while the paper presents elegant and technically sound theoretical work, its lack of empirical validation and insufficient engagement with established methods make it fall short of the standards for a strong contribution at NIPS. The proposed framework, while promising, remains an unproven approach. For the results to be impactful, they must be supported by evidence demonstrating their utility and advantages over the well-vetted techniques currently used by practitioners.
