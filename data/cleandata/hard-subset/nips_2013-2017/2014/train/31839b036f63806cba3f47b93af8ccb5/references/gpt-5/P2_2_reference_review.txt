Summary
The paper advances the graphical approach to inference with missing data by extending the “missingness graphs” (m-graphs) framework of Mohan, Pearl, and Tian (NIPS 2013). The core contributions are fourfold: (i) a stronger sequential factorization theorem that admits R-nodes (missingness mechanisms) in the ordered factorization, enabling recovery when missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) a constructive recovery formula for joint distributions in semi-Markovian models without admissible sequences, based on partitions of R and Markov blankets along collider paths, plus a corollary for models without latent confounding between R and substantive variables; (iii) new graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions; and (iv) conditions under which causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when related probabilistic queries are not, including necessary and sufficient results for “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1). The paper relates to, and builds on, foundational work on missing data (Rubin), ADMGs and m-separation (Richardson), and causal identification (Shpitser & Pearl), and corrects claims in prior attrition analyses (Garcia).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The theoretical development is careful and mostly well-justified. The extension of ordered factorizations to include R-nodes (Theorem 1) is natural and the minimality requirement is well-motivated by Example 1. Theorem 2 provides a constructive route to P(V) under broad semi-Markovian conditions, and the partition/Markov blanket construction is precise. The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3; Corollary 2) are useful in practice. The causal recoverability results (Example 3; Theorem 4) convincingly show that causal effects can be recoverable despite inseparability of Y and Ry, leveraging dormant independences. The necessary and sufficient conditions for |Vm|=1 (Theorems 5–6) are clean and practically valuable.
- Completeness: Proofs are deferred to the appendix; the main text provides intuition and worked algebraic reductions. While the results are theoretically complete for the stated settings, the paper stops short of an algorithmic completeness result (decidability/complexity) for recoverability in general m-graphs.
- Empirical validation: There is no empirical study. While not essential for a theoretical contribution, small synthetic experiments or case studies demonstrating finite-sample performance of the proposed estimands would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
- Organization: The paper follows a logical progression (probabilistic recovery → non-recoverability → causal recovery → attrition). Examples are helpful.
- Notation/definitions: Some notation is dense and occasionally inconsistent/typo-prone (e.g., “GX and GX,” Xr vs RXm in Theorem 1’s condition (2)). Definitions of “collider path” and “inducing path” rely on mixed-graph concepts; they are given, but cross-referencing to m-separation terminology could help. The assumption “R variables may not be parents of V∪U” is important and should be highlighted early and contrasted with allowed R→R and V→R edges.
- Readability: Adding more self-contained, labeled figures and one or two fully worked recovery derivations (beyond Example 1) would improve accessibility.

Originality
- The extension of sequential factorization to include R-nodes interacting with one another is novel and useful. The general recovery formula using R-partitions and collider-path Markov blankets is new, to my knowledge. The explicit negative results (non-recoverability criteria) fill a gap. The demonstration that causal effects can be recovered when corresponding probabilistic relations are not (via dormant independences) is a noteworthy conceptual contribution. The necessary and sufficient conditions for joint and causal recovery under simple attrition unify and correct prior work.

Significance
- Practical impact: The paper provides graphical, checkable conditions for when common queries are or are not recoverable under MNAR, and explicit estimands when they are. This is valuable for practitioners working with incomplete observational or experimental data, including longitudinal/attrition settings.
- Research impact: The results advance the state of the art in causal/missing data theory, likely to be built upon by both the causal inference and graphical models communities. The negative results will also save researchers from pursuing impossible recoveries.

Strengths
- Clear, general recovery and non-recoverability criteria derivable from the graph.
- Novel extension allowing interacting R-nodes in ordered factorizations.
- Constructive recovery formula for semi-Markovian models via R-partitions and Markov blankets.
- Important insight that causal recoverability can outstrip probabilistic recoverability.
- Clean necessary and sufficient conditions for the simple attrition case; correction of prior literature.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity/notation: Fix typos and standardize notation (GX, Xr/RXm, Wo/Wm, etc.). Clarify the role and scope of the “no R→V” restriction and where latent confounding is permitted.
- Algorithmics: Provide a concrete procedure (pseudocode) to (a) search for admissible sequences; (b) compute R-partitions and Mb(R(i)); and (c) output the estimand when conditions hold. Discuss computational complexity.
- Robustness: Discuss sensitivity to m-graph misspecification and connect to available testing procedures (Mohan & Pearl 2014).
- Empirics: Include small simulations illustrating (i) success/failure predicted by the criteria; (ii) bias of naive MAR methods; and (iii) variance/finite-sample behavior of the proposed estimators.
- Scope: Discuss limitations (e.g., positivity over complete cases, semi-Markovian assumption, single partially observed variable in Theorems 5–6) and prospects for extension to multiple partially observed outcomes in attrition-like designs.
- References: Clean up formatting and ensure completeness.

Arguments for acceptance
- Substantial, well-motivated theoretical advances over prior NIPS work on m-graphs.
- Both positive and negative results with constructive estimands and clear graphical checks.
- Addresses an important and pervasive problem (MNAR, attrition) with broad impact.

Arguments against acceptance
- Presentation/notation issues reduce accessibility.
- Lack of algorithmic/complexity discussion and empirical illustrations.
- Some results apply to restricted settings (e.g., |Vm|=1 in attrition section).

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contributions are significant and timely for NeurIPS, and the shortcomings are largely addressable in revision.

Reviewer confidence
Medium. I am familiar with causal identification and missing data in graphical models but did not verify the appendixed proofs.
