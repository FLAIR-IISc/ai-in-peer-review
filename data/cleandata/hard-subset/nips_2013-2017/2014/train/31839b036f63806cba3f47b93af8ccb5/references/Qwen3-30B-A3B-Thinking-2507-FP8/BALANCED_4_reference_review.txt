

**Review**

This paper extends the Missingness Graph (m-graph) framework introduced by Mohan et al. [2013] to develop general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors present theoretical results that significantly advance the understanding of recoverability under complex missingness mechanisms, with the key insight that causal queries (P(y|do(x))) can be recovered even when probabilistic queries (P(y)) cannot due to inseparable relationships between variables and their missingness mechanisms. The paper builds on foundational work in causal inference (Pearl [2009]) and missing data analysis (Rubin [1976]), addressing a fundamental challenge in experimental science.

**Strengths:** The paper's most significant contribution is its theoretical insight that causal queries may be recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not (Theorem 4 and Example 3). This challenges prevailing assumptions in the literature (e.g., Garcia [2013]) and provides a crucial distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability. The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) fill an important gap, while the application to attrition problems with necessary and sufficient conditions (Theorems 5 and 6) corrects previous misunderstandings. The paper is well-structured, with rigorous proofs (provided in the appendix), clear examples, and thorough connections to prior work. The extension of sequential factorization (Theorem 1) broadens the applicability of existing results to handle missingness mechanisms that influence each other.

**Weaknesses:** The paper assumes substantial prior knowledge of causal inference and graphical models, which may limit accessibility for some NIPS readers. While the theoretical development is sound, the paper lacks concrete examples with real or simulated data to illustrate practical implementation. The connection to machine learning applications remains theoretical without discussing algorithmic implementation, computational complexity, or empirical validation. The explanation of technical concepts like d*-separation could be more intuitive for a broader audience. The comparison to existing machine learning methods for missing data (e.g., imputation, inverse probability weighting) is superficial, missing opportunities to position this work within the ML community's approaches.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs and thorough theoretical development. The authors carefully distinguish between recoverable and non-recoverable cases, and the examples effectively illustrate key concepts. The work represents a complete theoretical contribution rather than a position paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with consistent notation. However, some technical concepts (e.g., d*-separation) would benefit from more intuitive explanations. The referenced figures (e.g., Figure 1-4) would be valuable for understanding, but are not included in the provided text.

**Originality:** The paper makes a substantial original contribution by extending the m-graph framework to handle complex missingness mechanisms and by establishing the crucial distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability. The non-recoverability criteria and attrition-specific results (Theorems 5-6) are novel compared to previous NIPS publications on missing data.

**Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental problem in experimental science with broad implications for causal inference in domains like healthcare, social sciences, and machine learning. The graphical criteria provide a clear framework for practitioners to assess recoverability, and the correction of previous misconceptions (e.g., Garcia [2013]) has direct practical impact.

**Recommendation:** This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in missing data analysis. While it would benefit from more concrete examples and clearer explanations of technical concepts, the theoretical insights are significant and well-supported. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Specific suggestions for improvement:**
1. Add a brief intuitive explanation of d*-separation and its connection to standard d-separation for readers unfamiliar with causal inference.
2. Include a small-scale simulation or concrete example demonstrating the application of the theorems to a practical dataset.
3. Discuss computational complexity of the proposed recovery algorithms, as this is crucial for real-world applicability.
4. Strengthen the connection to machine learning by discussing how these results could inform the development of more robust algorithms for missing data in ML settings.

The paper's theoretical advances are substantial and would be valuable to the NIPS community. The key insight about causal query recoverability despite probabilistic non-recoverability is particularly noteworthy and has the potential to influence future work in causal inference and missing data analysis.
