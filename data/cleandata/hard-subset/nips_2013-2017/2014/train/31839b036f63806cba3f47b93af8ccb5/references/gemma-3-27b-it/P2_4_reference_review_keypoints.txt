- Overall: Solid theoretical extension of m-graphs for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data; recommend accept with minor revisions.  
- Main idea: Extends m-graphs, refines sequential factorization, introduces non-recoverability criteria, and shows P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when P(y,x) is not.  
- Contribution: Demonstrates causal-effect recoverability under weaker assumptions than MAR, addressing attrition scenarios.  
- Rigour: Built on a formal m-graph framework with rigorous mathematical proofs (provided in appendix).  
- Causal focus: Strong connection to causal inference using do-calculus and explicit treatment of causal queries.  
- Practical value: Graphical criteria offer practitioners tools to assess recoverability; applied to attrition problems.  
- Related work: Thorough and comprehensive overview of existing missing-data approaches.  
- Strength — novelty: Novel result that causal effects can be recovered when Y and its missingness are not d-separable.  
- Weakness — accessibility: Very dense and mathematically involved; hard for readers unfamiliar with m-graphs and do-calculus.  
- Weakness — empirical evaluation: Entirely theoretical; lacks simulations or real-data experiments to demonstrate practical applicability.  
- Weakness — notation/readability: Heavy, overwhelming notation and some redundant repetitions; needs clearer notation summary.  
- Quality (review criteria): Technically sound proofs, but completeness limited by absence of empirical validation.  
- Clarity (review criteria): Weakest aspect — needs more intuitive explanations and motivating examples.  
- Originality (review criteria): Original extension of recoverability to causal settings and refinements to sequential factorization.  
- Significance (review criteria): Potentially high impact for causal inference with missing data under weaker assumptions.  
- For acceptance: Strong theoretical contribution, rigorous framework, addresses an important problem, provides practical graphical tools.  
- Against acceptance: High technical barrier, no empirical validation, dense writing style.  
- Actionable revisions requested: Add informal explanations and motivating examples, include empirical evaluation (simulations or real data), and streamline notation and writing.
