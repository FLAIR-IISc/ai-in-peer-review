## Peer Review: Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable extension to the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) for handling missing data, building upon the foundational work of Mohan et al. (2013). It refines existing theoretical results, introduces new graphical criteria for non-recoverability, and importantly, demonstrates that causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not. The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses a crucial problem in observational studies. While the mathematical notation can be dense, the core ideas are clearly presented with illustrative examples. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and accessibility.

**1. Summary of Main Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries in the presence of missing data. It leverages the concept of m-graphs to explicitly model the missingness process alongside the data-generating process. The central contribution lies in extending the sequential factorization theorem to accommodate more complex missingness mechanisms (interactions between R variables) and demonstrating conditions under which causal effects (P(y|do(x))) are identifiable even when the underlying probabilistic relationships are not. This contrasts with traditional approaches often relying on strong MAR assumptions.  The authors build directly on their previous work (Mohan et al., 2013; Mohan & Pearl, 2014) and connect to broader literature on missing data handling, including ML estimation (Dempster et al., 1977), inverse probability weighting (Robins et al., 1994), and graphical models (Koller & Friedman, 2009).  They differentiate their approach by providing graphical criteria for both recoverability *and* non-recoverability, a relatively unexplored area.  The discussion of attrition provides a concrete application of the theoretical results.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is built on a strong theoretical foundation, with clear definitions, theorems, and proofs (provided in the appendix).  The extension of the sequential factorization theorem is a significant contribution.
* **Causal Inference Focus:**  The finding that causal effects can be recovered even when probabilistic relationships are unidentifiable is novel and important.  This addresses a critical limitation of traditional methods.
* **Graphical Criteria:** The introduction of graphical conditions for non-recoverability is extremely valuable for practitioners. It allows them to assess the feasibility of recovery *before* attempting complex estimation procedures.
* **Clear Examples:**  The inclusion of examples (e.g., Figures 1, 2, 3) greatly aids understanding of the complex concepts and theorems.  Example 1, demonstrating the need for minimality in the ordered factorization, is particularly insightful.
* **Application to Attrition:**  Applying the results to the realistic problem of attrition highlights the practical relevance of the work.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The related work section is thorough and appropriately contextualizes the paper within the broader field of missing data.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Notationally Dense:** The paper employs a significant amount of notation, which can be challenging for readers unfamiliar with the m-graph framework.  While the authors attempt to define terms clearly, the initial learning curve is steep.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper primarily focuses on theoretical results. While examples are provided, a more substantial empirical evaluation demonstrating the performance of the proposed methods on real or simulated datasets would strengthen the claims.
* **Complexity of Theorems:**  Some of the theorems, particularly Theorem 2 and Corollary 1, are complex and require careful reading to fully grasp.  Simplifying the presentation or providing more intuitive explanations could improve accessibility.
* **Discussion of Assumptions:** While the paper highlights the importance of m-graph specification, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the challenges in accurately specifying the missingness mechanism and the sensitivity of the results to misspecification.



**4. Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound and presents a rigorous extension of existing theoretical results. The proofs appear correct, and the logical flow is generally clear.
* **Clarity (6/10):** The notation and complexity of some theorems hinder clarity. More intuitive explanations and potentially a gradual introduction of notation would be helpful.
* **Originality (9/10):** The extension of the sequential factorization theorem and the demonstration of causal query recoverability in the presence of non-recoverable probabilistic relationships are highly original contributions.
* **Significance (8/10):** The results have the potential to significantly impact the field of causal inference and missing data handling.  The ability to recover causal effects even in challenging scenarios is a valuable advancement.

**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and rigorous proofs.
* Novel and important findings regarding causal query recoverability.
* Useful graphical criteria for assessing recoverability.
* Relevant application to attrition problems.
* Thorough related work section.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Notationally dense and potentially inaccessible to some readers.
* Limited empirical validation.
* Complexity of some theorems hinders clarity.




**6. Recommendations:**

* **Minor Revisions:**
    * **Improve Clarity of Notation:** Consider introducing notation more gradually and providing a dedicated glossary of symbols.
    * **Expand Examples:**  Add a few more worked examples to illustrate the application of the theorems.
    * **Discuss Assumption Sensitivity:** Expand the discussion of the sensitivity of the results to misspecification of the m-graph.
    * **Consider Empirical Validation:**  If feasible, include a small-scale empirical evaluation to demonstrate the performance of the proposed methods.

* **No major revisions are required.** The paper is a significant contribution and is suitable for acceptance with the suggested minor revisions.



I believe this paper represents a valuable addition to the NIPS program and has the potential to influence future research in missing data and causal inference.
