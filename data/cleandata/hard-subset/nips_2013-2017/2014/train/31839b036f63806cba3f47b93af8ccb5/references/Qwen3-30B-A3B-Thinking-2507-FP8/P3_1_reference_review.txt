

# Peer Review: "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper makes significant theoretical contributions to the field of missing data analysis by extending Mohan et al. [2013] with formal graphical criteria for recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries. The authors introduce "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) as a formal representation to model missingness processes and establish precise conditions under which queries can be recovered from incomplete data. The paper's central insight is that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) may be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations (e.g., P(y,x)) are not, challenging conventional wisdom that causal recovery requires probabilistic recoverability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Rigor and Novel Contributions:** The paper provides a substantial theoretical extension to existing work. Theorems 1 and 2 generalize the sequential factorization approach to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact (e.g., longitudinal studies), while Theorem 3 and Corollary 2 establish the first formal non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions. The most significant contribution is Theorem 4, which demonstrates that causal effects may be recoverable despite inseparability between Y and Ry (a condition that blocks probabilistic recoverability).

2. **Clear Distinction Between Probabilistic and Causal Recoverability:** The paper effectively demonstrates that these are fundamentally different concepts. Example 3 is particularly compelling, showing how causal recovery can bypass probabilistic non-recoverability through do-calculus manipulations. This resolves an important ambiguity in the literature (e.g., Garcia [2013] incorrectly assumed non-recoverability in such cases).

3. **Practical Application to Attrition:** Section 7 provides a valuable case study for a common real-world problem. Theorems 5 and 6 establish necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects under "simple attrition," correcting misconceptions in existing literature (e.g., Garcia [2013]'s claim that X being an ancestor of Ry is necessary for non-recoverability).

4. **Comprehensive Related Work:** The paper thoroughly contextualizes its contributions against prior work in missing data (Rubin, Little & Rubin), graphical models (Daniel et al.), and causal inference (Shpitser & Pearl). The connection to the "coarsened at random" framework (Heitjan & Rubin) is particularly well-handled.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation:** As a purely theoretical contribution, the paper lacks empirical demonstrations. While this is acceptable for a NIPS paper focused on theoretical advances, it would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion of how these criteria could be applied in practice (e.g., a simple simulation showing recoverability conditions in action).

2. **Notational Complexity:** The paper employs dense notation (V, V*, R, R*, etc.) that may hinder accessibility. While precise, the definitions could be better motivated. For instance, the distinction between Rw and Wr is subtle but critical, and the paper could benefit from a clearer introductory explanation.

3. **Limited Connection to Machine Learning Practice:** The related work section mentions Marlin et al.'s work on collaborative filtering, but doesn't adequately address how these graphical criteria might inform or improve existing ML approaches to missing data (e.g., imputation or inverse probability weighting). This weakens the paper's relevance to the broader ML community.

4. **Ambiguity in "Recoverability" Definition:** The paper states that recoverability requires "consistently estimable" queries from the manifest distribution. However, it doesn't clarify whether this refers to consistency in the statistical sense or computational feasibility, which could be important for practitioners.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in missing data analysis. The paper successfully establishes formal graphical criteria for recoverability that resolve important ambiguities in the literature. The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability is particularly insightful and has significant implications for causal inference in missing data settings.

The paper's main limitation is its purely theoretical nature without empirical validation, though this is acceptable for a NIPS paper focused on foundational contributions. The notation could be slightly simplified for broader accessibility, and the connection to practical ML applications could be strengthened.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper makes a clear, significant theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental problem in data analysis. The work is well-motivated, technically sound, and represents a substantial advancement over prior work. It will be valuable to researchers working on causal inference, graphical models, and missing data problems. The paper's insights into the relationship between probabilistic and causal recoverability are particularly novel and impactful.

**Key Strengths:**
- Establishes formal non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3)
- Demonstrates causal queries may be recoverable when probabilistic queries are not (Theorem 4)
- Corrects misconceptions in existing literature (e.g., Garcia [2013])
- Provides necessary and sufficient conditions for attrition problems (Theorems 5-6)

**Key Weaknesses:**
- Lacks empirical validation or implementation discussion
- Notational complexity could hinder accessibility
- Limited connection to practical ML applications

This paper represents a significant step forward in our understanding of missing data mechanisms and should be of interest to the NIPS community, particularly those working at the intersection of graphical models, causal inference, and statistical theory.
