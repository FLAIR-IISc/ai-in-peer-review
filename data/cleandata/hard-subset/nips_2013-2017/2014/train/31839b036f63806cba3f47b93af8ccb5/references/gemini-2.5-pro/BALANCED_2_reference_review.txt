This paper presents a formal graphical framework, based on "missingness graphs" (m-graphs), for analyzing the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. Building upon the foundational work of Mohan et al. [2013], which introduced m-graphs and the concept of recoverability for Missing Not At Random (MNAR) data, this paper makes several significant extensions. First, it generalizes the sequential factorization theorem to handle dependencies between missingness mechanisms, which is common in settings like longitudinal studies. Second, for cases where sequential factorization is not applicable, it provides a more general theorem for recovering the joint distribution in semi-markovian models. Third, and notably, it introduces novel graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, formally identifying conditions under which a query cannot be consistently estimated given the model assumptions. Finally, the paper extends this framework to causal queries, demonstrating the remarkable result that a causal effect P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the corresponding observational distribution P(y|x) is not. These theoretical results are then applied to the specific problem of attrition, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability and correcting claims made in prior literature.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles the fundamental and ubiquitous problem of missing data. While the literature on this topic is vast (e.g., Rubin's MAR/MCAR framework, imputation methods), this work provides a principled, assumption-explicit approach using graphical models that can handle complex MNAR scenarios where traditional methods often fail. The key contributions are highly original:
    *   The formal criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) are a major conceptual advance. They provide researchers with a tool to understand when their assumptions are insufficient, preventing misguided attempts at estimation.
    *   The finding that causal queries can be recoverable when probabilistic ones are not (Section 6) is a deep and important insight. It connects the fields of causal inference and missing data in a non-trivial way and has significant practical implications for experimental sciences where causal questions are paramount.
    *   The application to attrition (Section 7) is concrete and demonstrates the power of the framework to clarify and correct existing results in a specific problem domain.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. It builds upon the established theoretical foundations of graphical models and Pearl's do-calculus. The theorems are stated clearly, and the provided examples effectively illustrate their application. The formalization of concepts like "general ordered factorization" and the use of Markov blankets and collider paths to derive recoverability conditions represent a solid theoretical contribution.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-organized, logically progressing from extending existing results (Section 3), to developing new recovery methods (Section 4), to establishing impossibility results (Section 5), and finally to handling the more complex case of causal queries (Section 6) and applications (Section 7). This structure makes the chain of reasoning clear and easy to follow for a reader familiar with graphical models.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the claims would be substantially more impactful if supported by empirical evidence. The absence of experiments makes it difficult to assess the practical utility and robustness of the proposed methods.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors could significantly strengthen the paper by including a simulation study. For instance, they could generate data from a known m-graph for which their theory predicts recoverability (e.g., the graph in Figure 3), and show that their derived estimator provides consistent estimates of the causal effect, while standard methods (like listwise deletion or imputation assuming MAR) produce biased results. Conversely, they could simulate data from a non-recoverable model (e.g., Figure 4(a)) to demonstrate that estimators are indeed biased, thus validating their non-recoverability criteria.

2.  **Accessibility:** The paper is very dense and relies on a large body of specialized knowledge (m-graphs, do-calculus, d*-separation, etc.). While appropriate for a specialized audience, its accessibility to the broader NeurIPS community could be improved. Some concepts, like the partitioning of R variables and the definition of their Markov blankets in Theorem 2, are introduced quickly and may be difficult to parse.
    *   **Suggestion:** While space is limited, a brief intuitive motivation for some of the more complex definitions could be helpful. Perhaps a single, more complex running example that is revisited in each section could help unify the different theorems and demonstrate how they apply to various aspects of the same problem.

3.  **Practicality of Model Specification:** The entire framework hinges on the practitioner's ability to specify a correct m-graph that encodes the true data generating and missingness processes. This is a very strong assumption. The paper does not discuss the challenges of this specification process or the sensitivity of the results to model misspecification.
    *   **Suggestion:** It would be beneficial to add a short discussion on the practical aspects of constructing an m-graph. The authors could briefly mention how domain knowledge is used to posit such a graph and allude to the methods for testing the graph's assumptions (as referenced in Mohan and Pearl [2014]). A sentence or two on the potential consequences of a misspecified graph would also add valuable context for the practitioner.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper provides novel, significant, and technically sound theoretical results for a fundamental problem in machine learning and statistics. The non-recoverability criteria and the results on causal query recovery are particularly strong contributions that advance the state of the art.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The primary weakness is the complete lack of experimental validation, which is a significant omission for a top-tier conference like NeurIPS. The paper's dense nature and strong assumptions about model specification might also limit its immediate practical impact.

**Decision:**

This is a very strong theoretical paper with important and original ideas. While the lack of experiments is a notable drawback, the quality and significance of the theoretical contributions are high enough to warrant acceptance. The paper opens up several new avenues for research at the intersection of causality, graphical models, and missing data. I recommend acceptance, but strongly encourage the authors to add empirical results to the final version to demonstrate the practical value of their framework.
