

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

## Summary
This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to provide general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (joint/conditional distributions) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))) from missing data. The authors build upon previous work by introducing refined graphical criteria for recoverability, demonstrating that causal effects may be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic relations are not. The paper makes significant theoretical contributions, particularly in showing that causal queries can circumvent the separability requirements that block probabilistic recovery. The work relates well to prior NIPS contributions in causal inference (Shpitser & Pearl, 2006; Pearl, 2009) and addresses a critical problem in data analysis.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical advance by establishing that causal queries may be recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not (Theorem 4 and Example 3). This distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability is novel and important for practitioners.

2. **Rigorous Formalism**: The paper provides clear definitions (e.g., general ordered factorization) and well-structured theorems with formal proofs. The extensions to Mohan et al. (2013) are logically sound and properly motivated.

3. **Comprehensive Coverage**: The paper thoroughly addresses both recoverability and non-recoverability conditions, which is valuable for practitioners who need to assess whether their missing data problem can be solved. The treatment of attrition as a special case (Section 7) is particularly relevant to longitudinal studies.

4. **Clarity of Key Insights**: The examples (particularly Example 3) effectively demonstrate the theoretical points about causal recoverability versus probabilistic recoverability, making the abstract concepts more tangible.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: The paper presents strong theoretical results but provides no empirical validation. The significance of the theoretical contributions would be substantially strengthened by demonstrating performance on real or synthetic datasets, showing how the proposed conditions would be applied in practice.

2. **Limited Practical Guidance**: While the paper introduces graphical criteria, it doesn't provide clear guidance on how practitioners might construct m-graphs from real data or handle cases where the missingness mechanism is unknown. The connection between theory and practice remains somewhat abstract.

3. **Computational Considerations**: The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms or how they scale with problem size. This is an important practical consideration for potential applications.

4. **Overly Technical Presentation**: The dense notation and heavy reliance on causal inference concepts may limit accessibility for a broader NIPS audience, including those working in machine learning but not causal inference. More intuitive explanations of the key concepts would help.

## Recommendations for Improvement
1. **Add Empirical Section**: Include experiments demonstrating the application of the theoretical results to synthetic or real datasets. This would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and demonstrate practical utility.

2. **Provide Implementation Guidance**: Add a section on how practitioners might use these results, including potential pitfalls and how to construct m-graphs from real data.

3. **Clarify Practical Significance**: Better articulate how these results improve upon existing missing data approaches (e.g., MAR/MCAR assumptions in standard literature) and what new problems they enable solving.

4. **Simplify Notation**: Consider using more intuitive notation for key concepts (e.g., the "R" variables) to improve accessibility for a broader audience.

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of missing data analysis by extending the m-graph framework to provide conditions for recovering both probabilistic and causal queries. The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries is particularly novel and important. However, the lack of empirical validation and limited practical guidance significantly limits the paper's impact. The theoretical results are sound and well-presented, but their practical significance would be much stronger with demonstration on real data.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments**

*Pro:*
- Makes a significant theoretical advance in understanding recoverability of causal queries under missing data
- Provides a comprehensive theoretical framework that extends previous work
- Clearly distinguishes between recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries
- Well-structured with clear theorems and proofs

*Con:*
- Lacks empirical validation of theoretical results
- Limited practical guidance for applying the theory
- Not sufficiently accessible to broader NIPS audience
- Does not address computational complexity of proposed methods

## Recommendation
This paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution to the field of missing data analysis. While the theoretical advances are significant, the lack of empirical validation and practical guidance prevents it from making a compelling contribution to NIPS, which prioritizes both theoretical and empirical rigor. The authors should add empirical validation and practical implementation guidance to strengthen their contribution before considering resubmission to NIPS.
