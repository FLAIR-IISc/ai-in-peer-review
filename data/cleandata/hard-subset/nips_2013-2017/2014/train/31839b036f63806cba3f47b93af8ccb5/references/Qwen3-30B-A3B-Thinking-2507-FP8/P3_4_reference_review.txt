

# Peer Review: Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to provide a comprehensive framework for determining recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors establish general conditions under which joint/conditional distributions can be recovered, and crucially demonstrate that causal effects P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separated - a condition that would prevent recovery of probabilistic queries.

**Strengths**

The paper makes significant theoretical contributions to the field of missing data analysis. The core novelty lies in the demonstration that causal queries can circumvent the separability requirement that blocks probabilistic recovery. Theorem 4 and Example 3 provide a compelling proof of concept for this important insight, showing how causal recovery leverages do-calculus and dormant independencies to bypass the usual constraints. 

The paper also makes a valuable contribution to the attrition literature (Section 7), providing necessary and sufficient conditions for causal effect recovery that correct a common misconception (Garcia, 2013) by showing that X being an ancestor of Ry is neither necessary nor sufficient for non-recoverability. Theorems 5 and 6 establish precise graphical conditions for simple attrition scenarios, which is particularly relevant for longitudinal studies.

Methodologically, the paper improves upon Mohan et al. (2013) by introducing general ordered factorization (Theorem 1), which handles cases where missingness mechanisms interact (e.g., longitudinal studies with dependencies between R variables). The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) fill an important gap in the literature by providing formal conditions for when recovery is impossible.

**Weaknesses**

While theoretically rigorous, the paper lacks empirical validation of its theoretical claims. For a NeurIPS submission, some simulation studies demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed conditions would significantly strengthen the contribution. How do these conditions perform in real-world datasets with varying degrees of missingness?

The paper does not adequately address computational complexity of the recovery algorithms. For practical implementation, it's important to know whether the recovery procedures scale to high-dimensional datasets, which is a significant concern in modern machine learning applications.

The connection to contemporary machine learning approaches for missing data is underdeveloped. While the authors cite some work on collaborative filtering (Marlin et al., 2007, 2009, 2011), they don't discuss how their graphical approach might integrate with or improve upon deep learning-based imputation methods that are increasingly popular in the ML community.

The notation could be clearer in places. For instance, the distinction between R, R(i), Rpart, and Rw is somewhat confusing, and the use of "R" to represent both missingness mechanisms and their values creates ambiguity. A more intuitive notation would improve readability.

**Significance and Contribution**

This paper addresses a fundamental problem in statistical inference with missing data, which affects nearly every empirical field. The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability is particularly significant for causal inference, where researchers often incorrectly assume that if a distribution isn't recoverable, then causal effects aren't either. The authors' demonstration that this isn't necessarily the case has important implications for fields like epidemiology and social science.

While the paper builds on the strong foundation of Mohan et al. (2013) and Pearl (2009), its key contribution is the causal recovery insight, which is genuinely novel and addresses an important gap in the literature. The work represents a significant step toward formalizing the conditions under which missing data can be handled without bias in causal inference.

**Overall Recommendation**

This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in missing data analysis. The paper is well-organized, technically sound, and makes a clear distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability - a distinction that has been previously overlooked. While the lack of empirical validation and limited discussion of computational aspects are notable weaknesses, the theoretical contributions are substantial and address a critical problem in statistical inference.

The paper is highly relevant to NeurIPS, particularly for the growing intersection of causal inference and machine learning. The formal graphical framework provides a valuable tool for researchers dealing with missing data in complex observational and experimental settings. The results on causal recovery have the potential to influence how researchers approach missing data in causal studies.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions (addressing the computational complexity discussion and clarifying notation), as the theoretical contribution is significant and the paper is well-executed. The authors should add a brief discussion of computational considerations and provide a simple simulation to illustrate their main theoretical findings.

This work represents an important advance in the field, and its publication would benefit the NeurIPS community by providing a rigorous framework for handling missing data in causal inference settings.
