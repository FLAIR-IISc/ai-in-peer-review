- Valuable extension of m-graph framework that generalizes recoverability conditions for probabilistic and causal queries.  
- Key contribution: shows P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when joint distributions like P(y,x) are not.  
- Extends sequential factorization to handle interacting missingness mechanisms.  
- Connects to coarsened data, inverse probability weighting, and multiple imputation, framed within Pearl-style causal inference.  
- Theoretical contributions are rigorous with theorems and proofs (appendix provided).  
- Introduces new graphical criteria for non-recoverability to help practitioners assess identifiability.  
- Application to attrition in longitudinal studies demonstrates practical relevance.  
- Comprehensive related work section situates the paper in missing data and causal inference literature.  
- Paper is dense with heavy notation and requires strong background in graphical models and causal inference.  
- Many concepts lack intuitive explanation and would benefit from additional illustrative examples.  
- No empirical validation—no experiments on real or simulated data to demonstrate practical effectiveness.  
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability of proposed algorithms.  
- The large number of theorems and corollaries can overwhelm readers; needs prioritization/consolidation.  
- Unclear practical comparison to existing imputation and weighting methods; relationship to them should be clarified.  
- Quality rated 8/10 (technically sound); clarity 5/10 (hard to read); originality 8/10; significance 9/10.  
- Pros: addresses a critical missing-data problem, novel causal recoverability results, builds on established m-graph framework.  
- Cons: lacks empirical validation, is dense and hard to read, and omits computational cost discussion.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions conditional on improved clarity and empirical validation.  
- Requested revisions: add more illustrative examples, include empirical experiments, discuss computational complexity, clarify connections to imputation/weighting methods, and streamline presentation.
