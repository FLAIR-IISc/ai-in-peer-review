This paper presents a significant theoretical extension of the 'missingness graph' (m-graph) framework for analyzing and recovering statistical and causal queries from datasets with missing values. Building directly on the foundational work of Mohan et al. (2013), the authors introduce a series of novel graphical criteria and algorithms that substantially broaden the scope and power of this approach. The core contributions include: (1) a generalized sequential factorization theorem that can handle dependencies among the missingness mechanisms themselves, a common scenario in longitudinal studies; (2) a new recovery algorithm for joint distributions when factorization-based methods fail; (3) novel and highly valuable graphical criteria for determining when a query is *non-recoverable*; and (4) the striking and important result that causal queries can be recoverable even when their constituent probabilistic queries are not. The authors apply these general results to the specific problem of attrition, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery that refine and correct previous claims in the literature.

This work sits at the intersection of graphical models, causal inference, and the statistics of missing data. It advances the state-of-the-art by providing a principled, non-parametric framework for reasoning about complex "Missing Not At Random" (MNAR) scenarios, moving beyond the standard MCAR and MAR assumptions that dominate much of the machine learning literature on this topic. By connecting the problem of recoverability to the formal machinery of do-calculus and d*-separation, the paper provides deep new insights into the relationship between causal structure, missingness mechanisms, and statistical estimation.

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance):**

1.  **High Originality and Boldness:** The paper's primary strength lies in its exceptional novelty. The introduction of formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability* (Theorem 3) is a bold and much-needed contribution. While much of the literature focuses on sufficient conditions for success, providing necessary conditions for failure is equally, if not more, valuable for practitioners, as it allows them to recognize when their assumptions are fundamentally insufficient for the task.

2.  **Significant Conceptual Contribution:** The demonstration in Section 6 that a causal effect P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the corresponding joint distribution P(x, y) is not, is a profound and counter-intuitive result. This insight elegantly separates the problem of statistical recoverability from causal identifiability and suggests that causal claims can be more robust to certain missingness patterns than purely associational ones. This is a major conceptual advance that is likely to stimulate further research.

3.  **Substantial Theoretical Extension:** The paper is not an incremental update. It tackles significant limitations of prior work. The generalization of sequential factorization to allow for edges between R-variables (Theorem 1) and the development of a completely new recovery strategy for non-factorizable models (Theorem 2) dramatically increase the applicability of the m-graph framework to more realistic and complex missing data problems.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. Complex theoretical ideas are introduced logically and supported by clear, concise graphical examples that make the abstract concepts tangible. The authors do an excellent job of defining their notation and guiding the reader through their chain of reasoning.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Absence of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the primary contribution is conceptual and algorithmic, the work would be strengthened by a simulation study. For instance, generating data from a known m-graph where standard methods (e.g., listwise deletion, standard imputation) are known to fail, and demonstrating that the proposed recovery algorithms produce consistent estimates, would provide compelling validation of the theory. While the proofs in the appendix are assumed to be sound, empirical demonstration adds a different and valuable layer of confidence.

2.  **Practicality of the M-Graph Assumption:** The entire framework relies on the assumption that a practitioner can correctly specify the m-graph. This is a strong requirement, as misspecification could lead to incorrect conclusions about recoverability. While the authors have addressed the testability of these graphs in other work, a brief discussion in this paper about the practical challenges of model specification would be helpful for grounding the theory.

**Recommendation:**

Accept.

This is a bold, highly original, and significant paper that pushes the boundary of how we think about missing data. Its key contributions—especially the non-recoverability criteria and the decoupling of causal and probabilistic recoverability—represent a major conceptual leap forward. While the lack of experiments is a minor weakness, the strength, novelty, and importance of the theoretical ideas are more than sufficient to warrant publication at NIPS. This work provides fundamental new tools for a problem that plagues all empirical science and is likely to be highly influential, opening up new avenues of research in both causality and machine learning. It is precisely the kind of thought-provoking, foundational work that the community should embrace.
