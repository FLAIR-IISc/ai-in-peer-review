Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from incomplete data using Missingness Graphs (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors (i) generalize the ordered/sequential factorization approach by allowing missingness indicators R to appear in the factorization (Theorem 1), enabling recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) provide a broader theorem (Theorem 2) for recovering joint distributions in semi-Markovian models without requiring an admissible sequence, using partitions of R and Markov blankets defined via collider paths; (iii) introduce graphical non-recoverability conditions for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2); (iv) establish that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when associated probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are not, leveraging do-calculus, dormant independence, and d*-separation (Theorem 4); and (v) derive necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability under “simple attrition” with a single partially observed outcome (Theorems 5–6), correcting claims in Garcia (2013). Examples illustrate how estimands are constructed from manifest data via proxy variables and R=0 conditioning.

Relative to prior work: Rubin’s MCAR/MAR/MNAR frames are recast graphically; Lauritzen (1995), EM/ML, IPW, and MI provide estimation under MAR or model-based assumptions; Shpitser & Pearl’s identification results and do-calculus underlie the causal parts. Mohan et al. (2013) established recoverability via ordered factorization when R nodes do not interact; the present paper extends that scope, adds non-recoverability criteria, and clarifies when causal queries can be recovered despite probabilistic non-recoverability.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: moving beyond MAR/MCAR by encoding missingness mechanisms explicitly and deriving graphical conditions that are checkable in principle.
- Generalization of sequential factorization to include R nodes is useful and nontrivial; the minimality requirement is well-motivated and demonstrated.
- Theorem 2 provides a complementary route when admissible sequences fail, broadening applicability to semi-Markovian models with latent confounding.
- Non-recoverability criteria fill an acknowledged gap in the literature and are valuable for diagnosing impossibility early.
- The causal results (Theorem 4, and the attrition case Theorem 6) are insightful: they separate probabilistic recoverability from causal recoverability and correct a misconception in prior work.
- Connections to dormant independence and d*-separation anchor the results in established causal identification theory.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and exposition: notation is dense and occasionally inconsistent (e.g., use of primes for R=0, mixing X′ with Rx=0; the definition of d*-separation is terse; some set notations for R-partitions/markov blankets could benefit from a figure-driven walkthrough). Several references and equations have formatting issues.
- Algorithmic details: Section 4 promises “general algorithms,” but the paper provides mainly existence results and closed-form estimands; there is little discussion of algorithmic procedures to test conditions, construct admissible sequences, or compute estimands automatically, nor complexity guarantees (contrast with the ID algorithm for causal effects).
- Empirical grounding: no simulations or empirical case studies to demonstrate finite-sample behavior, robustness to model misspecification, or advantages versus standard MAR-based EM, IPW, or MI under MNAR.
- Scope limitations: while Theorems 5–6 give necessary and sufficient conditions for |Vm|=1, the general multi-variable MNAR case lacks comparable completeness results. The non-recoverability conditions are necessary but not sufficient; guidance on the “gray zone” is limited.
- Practical guidance on specifying and validating m-graphs is brief; relying on Mohan & Pearl (2014) for testability/misspecification detection is fine, but practitioners would benefit from a more concrete workflow.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level; results are plausible and consistent with do-calculus and mixed-graph separation properties. The claims are supported by proofs in the appendix (not reviewed here). Examples are helpful, though more worked derivations would aid readers unfamiliar with collider paths/partitions. Lack of empirical validation is acceptable for a theory paper, but some synthetic demonstrations would strengthen credibility.

Clarity
Readable but demanding. The paper would benefit from:
- A canonical, consistently used notation for R=0 events (e.g., Rx=0 instead of R′x).
- Expanded, figure-driven examples for Theorem 2 (R partitions and Mb) and Theorem 4 (d*-separation).
- A summary “recipe” or flowchart for checking recoverability and constructing estimands.

Originality
Meaningful extensions over Mohan et al. (2013): inclusion of R in factorization, non-recoverability criteria, causal recoverability in inseparable cases, and tight attrition results. Combining dormant independence with missingness graphs to salvage causal effects is novel and insightful.

Significance
High potential impact: MNAR is common and poorly handled by standard tools. Graphical criteria that pinpoint when recovery is possible or impossible, and that sometimes recover causal effects even when probabilities are not, can substantially influence methodology in ML and causal inference.

Pros for acceptance
- Advances theory of MNAR recoverability with new sufficient and necessary conditions.
- Clarifies an important conceptual point: causal effects may be recoverable when P(V) is not.
- Provides corrections to prior claims and unifies several strands (m-graphs, do-calculus, dormant independence).

Cons for acceptance
- Exposition/notation issues; some results are hard to parse without the appendix/figures.
- Limited algorithmic and practical guidance; no empirical illustrations.
- Completeness is partial outside special cases (|Vm|=1); non-recoverability results are only necessary.

Questions for authors
1) Can you provide a simple, implementable procedure (or pseudocode) to test Theorem 1 admissibility and to construct an estimand automatically?
2) For Theorem 2, can you include a step-by-step example showing how to compute R partitions and Mb, and then derive the estimand?
3) Can you clarify whether your results extend when positivity fails on complete cases, beyond the brief remark?
4) Could you add a small synthetic experiment to illustrate finite-sample performance and robustness to mild m-graph misspecification?

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contributions are substantial and relevant to NeurIPS. With improved clarity, minor corrections to references/notation, and a bit more procedural guidance (even in an appendix), this paper would be a valuable addition.

Suggestions for improvement
- Tighten notation and fix reference formatting. Unify the use of Rx=0 and avoid overloaded primes.
- Add a “recoverability checklist” and pseudocode for constructing admissible sequences/estimands, plus complexity comments.
- Provide at least one detailed, fully worked example per major theorem (including Theorem 2 and 4), preferably with the corresponding figures.
- Include a brief synthetic study comparing your estimands to MAR-based EM/MI/IPW under MNAR.
- Expand the practical guidance on eliciting m-graphs and leveraging testable implications to reduce modeling errors; point to code if available.
