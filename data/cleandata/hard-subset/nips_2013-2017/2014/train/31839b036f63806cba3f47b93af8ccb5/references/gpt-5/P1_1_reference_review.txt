Summary
The paper extends the m-graph framework of Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) for reasoning about recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The main contributions are:
- A generalized sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) that permits ordered factorizations including missingness indicators R and allows interactions among R nodes; minimal separating sets are essential for admissibility. This extends the earlier “ordered factorization” beyond settings where R variables do not interact.
- New results for recovering joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists (Theorem 2 and Corollary 1), covering semi-Markovian models with latent confounding and R–R interactions. The result decomposes P(V) using partitions of R and their Markov blankets Mb(R(i)).
- Graphical non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) giving necessary conditions that preclude recovery of joint and conditional distributions, respectively.
- Recovery of causal effects even when associated probabilistic relations (e.g., P(y|x)) are not recoverable. Example 3 and Theorem 4 use do-calculus and dormant independences (d*-separation) to identify P(y|do(x)) despite inseparability between Y and its missingness Ry.
- Application to attrition: necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering P(V) (Theorem 5) and causal effects P(y|do(x)) (Theorem 6) when |Vm|=1 (“simple attrition”), rectifying claims in Garcia (2013).

Relation to prior work: The paper is a substantive extension of Mohan et al. (2013) by handling R–R interactions, supplying non-recoverability criteria, and giving necessary-and-sufficient conditions in single-missing-variable settings. It situates the results within the broader causal inference literature (do-calculus, identifiability) and connects to MAR/MCAR.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The statements are plausible and generally well grounded in graphical causal inference. The careful use of minimal separating sets in Theorem 1 is appropriate and the example illustrates why minimality matters. The partition/Markov blanket construction in Theorem 2 appears to generalize prior recoverability results to semi-Markovian graphs. The non-recoverability conditions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) fill an important gap.
- Rigor/completeness: Proofs are said to be in the appendix; the main text gives constructive estimands when recoverable. However, some conditions (e.g., Theorem 4’s d*-separation-based conditions and the use of Rule 3 in Example 3) would benefit from a more explicit checklist to ensure the rules apply (what independences in which mutilated graph are required).
- Algorithms: The abstract promises “algorithms” for joint recovery when sequential factorization fails; in the body, Theorem 2 provides constructive formulas but no explicit algorithmic procedure or complexity analysis for finding R partitions, Mb(R(i)), and checking conditions. It would help to present a step-by-step algorithmic test (input: m-graph; output: decision + estimand) and discuss decidability/complexity.
- Empirical evaluation: None. While theoretical papers are acceptable, even small synthetic demonstrations validating the constructions (e.g., finite-sample behavior, bias if assumptions fail, or variance under positivity constraints) would strengthen the work.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, with clear statements of assumptions (e.g., positivity on complete cases; R cannot be parents of V∪U; definition of proxy V*). The motivation and the flow from probabilistic to causal queries to attrition are coherent.
- Notation issues: There are some typographical/notation glitches that hinder readability:
  - In Definition 2, the index set alternates between k and n; please unify.
  - GX and GX “removing edges leaving and entering X” are ambiguous; use standard mutilations G\Xin/G\Xout or clarify.
  - The shorthand X′ for X=0 is unusual and may confuse when combined with R′y; consider standardizing on Ry=0.
  - The distinction between “collider path” vs “inducing path” should be emphasized; readers may conflate “all-collider path” (Theorem 3) with inducing paths (Theorem 6).
- Figures are referenced but not shown here; ensure they make the separations and collider paths clear. References have formatting errors (years with escape codes); please fix.

Originality
- Extending ordered factorization to include R interactions is novel and important for longitudinal/attrition settings.
- The non-recoverability criteria are new and valuable.
- The demonstration that P(y|do(x)) can be recovered despite Y–Ry inseparability generalizes the toolkit and corrects misconceptions in the literature.
- The attrition results (necessary and sufficient) for both probabilistic and causal queries when |Vm|=1 are crisp and practically relevant.

Significance
- The results advance the theory of missing data within graphical causal models and are likely to be used by both methodologists and applied researchers dealing with MNAR. The constructive estimands provide implementable recipes. The non-recoverability criteria can prevent misuse of MAR/MCAR assumptions and naive conditioning on complete cases.
- Wider impact could be increased by: (i) an algorithmic decision/estimand engine; (ii) examples beyond toy graphs; (iii) discussion of robustness to model misspecification and positivity violations.

Strengths
- Clear, principled extension of m-graph recoverability theory to R–R interactions.
- New necessary conditions preventing overclaiming recoverability.
- Elegant use of do-calculus/dormant independences to recover causal effects when probabilistic recovery fails.
- Necessary-and-sufficient results for simple attrition; correction of prior claims.

Weaknesses and questions
- No empirical illustrations or synthetic experiments.
- Lack of a full decision procedure and computational analysis; Theorem 2’s conditions may be hard to check for large graphs without an algorithm.
- The scope of Theorem 4: provide a constructive method to find Z and W1, and clarify identifiability checks required.
- Assumptions such as “R may not be parents of V∪U” limit some real-world measurement-error settings; discuss generalization.
- Positivity: beyond a brief remark, no guidance on handling violations or near-violations.
- Clarity/notation and reference formatting need polishing.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add an algorithmic pipeline that, given an m-graph and a query, (a) checks recoverability (using Theorems 1–6 and Corollaries), (b) outputs an estimand, or (c) returns a certificate of non-recoverability.
- Provide worked synthetic examples and, ideally, a small real attrition dataset demonstrating the different regimes (recoverable vs non-recoverable).
- Expand Theorem 4 with a step-by-step constructive search for Z, W1 or give sufficient graphical criteria that guarantee their existence.
- Improve notation consistency and fix reference formatting.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong theoretical contributions that extend a prior NIPS paper and fill important gaps (R–R interactions, non-recoverability, causal recovery under inseparability).
- Results are general, constructive, and practically relevant (attrition).
- Aligns with NeurIPS interest in causal inference and graphical models.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental relative to Mohan et al. (2013) without empirical validation.
- Lacks a complete algorithmic decision procedure and clarity on computational aspects.
- Presentation/notation issues and reliance on appendices for proofs may hinder accessibility.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on improving clarity and adding an explicit decision/estimand algorithm (even if only in the appendix) and tightening exposition. The theoretical advances are meaningful and relevant to the community.
