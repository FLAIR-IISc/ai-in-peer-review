**Summary:**
This paper extends the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. [2013] to address the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. The primary contributions are a set of new graphical criteria and algorithms for determining recoverability under complex, Missing Not At Random (MNAR) scenarios. Specifically, the authors generalize the sequential factorization theorem to handle dependencies between missingness mechanisms, introduce a novel theorem for recoverability in the absence of an admissible factorization sequence, and, notably, provide formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability. A key result is the demonstration that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y,x)) are not. The paper applies these results to the problem of attrition in longitudinal studies, presenting necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery in that context.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Methodological Rigor:** The paper is theoretically sound, building upon the established and rigorous framework of Pearl's structural causal models and do-calculus. The contributions are presented as formal theorems, with proofs provided in the appendix. This formal approach brings clarity and precision to the challenging problem of MNAR data.
2.  **Novelty of Non-Recoverability Criteria:** A significant contribution is the development of formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability* (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2). In a field often focused on sufficient conditions for success, providing clear, checkable conditions for failure is highly valuable. It allows researchers to quickly identify when their assumptions are insufficient, preventing wasted effort on impossible estimation tasks.
3.  **Important Causal Insight:** The demonstration that causal effects may be recoverable when joint distributions are not (Section 6) is an important and non-obvious insight. It highlights a fundamental difference between statistical and causal estimation and could have significant implications for fields like epidemiology and econometrics where causal questions are paramount and missing data is common.
4.  **Addressing a Difficult Problem:** The paper tackles the MNAR setting, which is notoriously difficult and often sidestepped in the broader machine learning literature in favor of the more convenient MAR assumption. The graphical approach provides a principled way to reason about the complex dependencies that characterize MNAR processes.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Complete Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the derivations are compelling, there are no experiments on either synthetic or real-world data. This is a critical weakness. It is unclear how these theoretical results translate to practice. For instance, how do the proposed estimation procedures perform with finite samples? How robust are they to near-violations of the graphical conditions? Without any empirical evidence, the practical utility of the proposed framework remains speculative.
2.  **Absence of Comparison to Established Baselines:** The paper fails to benchmark its approach against established methods for handling missing data. While methods like Multiple Imputation (MI) or Inverse Probability Weighting (IPW) typically rely on the MAR assumption, they are the standard in many applied fields. A thorough evaluation would involve creating synthetic datasets that violate the MAR assumption in ways specified by the paper's m-graphs and showing that the proposed methods succeed while standard baselines fail and produce biased estimates. The lack of such a comparison makes it difficult to assess the significance of the contribution over the existing state-of-the-art in practice.
3.  **Strong and Potentially Unrealistic Assumptions:** The entire framework hinges on the assumption that a practitioner can correctly specify the complete m-graph, including the causal relationships for the unobserved missingness-indicator variables (R). This is a very strong requirement. The paper does not discuss the sensitivity of the results to model misspecification. What happens if an edge is missed or an incorrect one is added to the m-graph? This raises significant questions about the robustness and applicability of the method in real-world settings where the true data generating process is unknown. While the authors cite a related paper on testing m-graphs, the current paper should stand on its own and address this limitation.
4.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** While technically precise, the paper is dense and assumes a very high level of expertise in graphical models and causality. The definitions for the conditions in Theorem 2, for example (collider paths, partitions of R variables, Markov blankets of these partitions), are complex and could benefit from more intuitive explanations and simpler running examples to build up to the main result.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a set of theoretically elegant and novel results for a well-known and difficult problem. The formalization of non-recoverability criteria and the insights into causal query recovery are clear strengths.

However, the complete absence of empirical validation is a significant barrier to acceptance for a conference like NIPS. The work, in its current form, reads more like a theoretical report than a complete research paper. The claims of recoverability need to be substantiated with demonstrations that the proposed estimators are indeed consistent and practically viable, at least in simulation. Furthermore, the lack of comparison to standard, widely-used methods makes it impossible to judge the practical advantage of this complex framework. While the theory is interesting, the NIPS community generally expects to see evidence that new methods offer tangible benefits.

**Recommendation:** Reject.

The theoretical contributions are valuable, but the paper is incomplete without an experimental section that validates the proposed algorithms and compares them against relevant baselines. I would encourage the authors to add a strong empirical evaluation and resubmit to a future conference.
