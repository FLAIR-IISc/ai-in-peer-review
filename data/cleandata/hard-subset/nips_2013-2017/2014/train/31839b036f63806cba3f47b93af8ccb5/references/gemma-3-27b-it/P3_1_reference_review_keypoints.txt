- Overall: Significant, rigorous extension to missingness graphs; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Contribution: Extends m-graphs and refines sequential factorization to recover probabilistic and causal queries under complex missingness.  
- Key result: Shows P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even without separability between variables and their missingness mechanisms.  
- Literature: Builds logically on Mohan et al. (2013) and Pearl & Mohan (2013) and situates well within missing-data and causal inference work.  
- Theorems: Theorem 1 (sequential factorization extension) and Theorem 3 (non-recoverability criteria) are valuable; proofs provided in appendix.  
- Theoretical rigor: Math is sound and clearly stated, with comprehensive proofs.  
- Causal focus: Demonstrates recoverable causal effects under non-separability, expanding real-world applicability.  
- Practical relevance: Attrition example and figures illustrate implications for longitudinal studies.  
- Completeness: Offers both sufficient recovery conditions and non-recoverability criteria, useful for practitioners.  
- Notation: Generally consistent and clear despite subject complexity.  
- Accessibility: Paper is very dense and technically demanding, especially early sections for readers new to m-graphs.  
- Example complexity: Some examples (e.g., Example 1) are overly complex and need simpler, stepwise walkthroughs.  
- Empirical evaluation: Lacks empirical/simulated demonstrations to illustrate practical recoverability.  
- Corollary overload: Numerous corollaries make the paper denser; consolidation or prioritization recommended.  
- Do-calculus reliance: Heavy use of do-calculus (Rules 2/3)—brief in-text explanations would help readers.  
- Detailed suggestions: Add an illustrative non-recoverable example, explain d*-separation relevance, and discuss limitations/extension of simple attrition.  
- Clarification needed: Specify what data is required to test Theorem 6 (complete data vs. information on missingness mechanism).  
- Pro acceptance points: Significant theoretical advance, addresses crucial observational-study problem, rigorous framework, novel causal recoverability result.  
- Con acceptance points: High technical barrier, limited empirical validation, and potential clarity improvements needed.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to improve clarity, add a small empirical demo, and simplify some examples.
