Summary
The paper studies recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data subject to missingness, using Missingness Graphs (m-graphs). It extends Mohan et al. (2013) by: (i) generalizing the ordered factorization approach to include missingness indicators R (Theorem 1), enabling recovery in models where missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) providing a general recovery formula for joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists, via partitions of R and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)) (Theorem 2) and a corollary under no latent confounding between R and V; (iii) giving graphical necessary conditions for non-recoverability of joint and certain conditional distributions (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2); (iv) showing that identifiable causal effects can be recoverable even when Y is inseparable from its missingness Ry (Theorem 4, with d*-separation/dormant independencies); and (v) specializing to “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1), with necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of joint distributions (Theorem 5) and causal effects (Theorem 6).

Relation to prior work is primarily Mohan et al. (2013) and Pearl/Shpitser identifiability theory. The paper aims to broaden recoverability results beyond MAR/MCAR, and to clarify when causal effects can be recovered despite non-recoverability of associated observational distributions.

Strengths
- Addresses an important, under-served area: recoverability with interacting missingness mechanisms, and clear non-recoverability criteria.
- The extension of ordered factorization to include R variables is natural and practically useful; Example 1 illustrates how admissibility/minimality matter.
- The general formula in Theorem 2 appears to strictly expand the class covered by prior results, and the corollary simplifies a useful special case (no bi-directed edges between R and V).
- The explicit non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) provide valuable negative results.
- The causal section highlights an important conceptual point: causal effects may be recoverable even when key probabilistic relations are not, leveraging do-calculus.
- The attrition specialization (Theorems 5–6) gives crisp necessary and sufficient graphical conditions.

Weaknesses and issues
Clarity/Notation
- Foundational notation is inconsistent or incorrect in multiple places:
  - “Let G(V,E) be the causal DAG where V = V ∪ U ∪ V* ∪ R” is circular; introduce a distinct node set N = V ∪ U ∪ V* ∪ R.
  - “GX and GX represent graphs formed by removing from G all edges leaving and entering X, respectively.” The two symbols are identical; standard do-calculus uses G_overline{X} (remove edges into X) and G_underline{X} (remove edges out of X).
  - R′x denotes Rx=0 while Ry (no prime) is defined as Ry=1. This asymmetry is confusing; use a consistent convention.
  - In Definition 2, “Yi⊥⊥({Yi+1,…,Yn}\Xi)|Xi holds in G” but O is said to be of length k and the product later uses n; indices are inconsistent.
  - Theorem 1 statement is hard to parse: terms like Ryi, Rxi, RXm, RXi, Z/∈Xi, and “Xr∩RXm=∅” are undefined or ambiguous; please formalize the mapping from variables to their R-sets and use consistent set notation.
  - Theorem 4 uses “for W = W \ X,” which is self-referential; likely a typo for W1 = W\X. Also “GX,W1” is undefined.
- Definitions are sometimes imprecise:
  - Definition 4 (d*-separation) departs from standard formulations and cites “Shpitser and Pearl [2008]” which is missing from references.
  - Inducing paths are defined, but later criteria sometimes refer to “path in which all intermediate nodes are colliders and substantive” vs “inducing path”; reconcile these carefully.

Technical completeness
- The paper repeatedly claims “algorithms,” but the main text only states theorems and examples; no algorithmic procedures or pseudocode are presented (e.g., how to find admissible sequences or compute R-partitions/Mb(R(i)) systematically; complexity).
- Proofs are deferred to the appendix; given the density/novelty, more intuition and at least proof sketches in the main text would aid verification (especially Theorems 2, 4, 6).
- The interplay with identifiability is asserted but not fully systematized: when Y and Ry are inseparable, Theorem 4’s conditions are intricate; guidance on how to check them in practice is missing.
- No empirical demonstrations or synthetic validations to show that the estimands lead to consistent estimators in finite samples, or that the conditions are checkable in realistic models.

Scope/Positioning
- The relation to selection diagrams/selection bias and “S-nodes” literature could be discussed, as R-nodes resemble selection mechanisms in causal diagrams.
- Some claims of novelty (e.g., non-recoverability criteria) would benefit from clearer contrasts with Mohan et al. (2013) and related work on CAR/identifiability conditions.

Formatting/References
- Numerous bibliographic entries are malformed or incomplete (question marks, backslashes, missing years/venues/pages, duplicated author strings). Example: “Allison.,? \Q2002\E,” “Dempster et al\.,? \Q1977\E,” “Pearl.,? \Q2009\E,” etc. Several in-text citations (e.g., Shpitser and Pearl 2008) are not in the reference list.
- Figures are referenced (Fig. 1–4) but not included here; text relies heavily on them.
- Minor typos abound (“m-graphs as discussed below was,” “in which R variables are contained in separating sets,” missing spaces around citations, inconsistent capitalization).

Quality
Conceptually solid and potentially correct, but the presentation is too error-prone to confidently verify or reproduce. The absence of algorithmic detail and proof sketches limits assessability.

Clarity
Currently below NeurIPS standards due to notation, definitions, and references. Substantial editing needed.

Originality
Moderately high: extends ordered factorization, provides broader joint recoverability and explicit non-recoverability criteria, and clarifies causal recoverability beyond separability.

Significance
High potential impact for causal/missing data communities; practical value hinges on clearer, checkable procedures and better exposition.

Pros
- Addresses important, difficult problem with generalizations beyond prior work.
- Useful negative results (non-recoverability criteria).
- Insightful point that causal effects can be recoverable despite non-recoverable probabilistic relations.

Cons
- Notational and definitional inaccuracies throughout.
- Missing or unclear algorithmic content; no complexity/implementation guidance.
- Proofs entirely in appendix; minimal intuition in main text.
- Reference list and formatting are far below venue standards.
- No empirical demonstrations.

Questions for authors
- Provide precise, consistent notation for intervention graphs (G_overline{X}, G_underline{X}) and R-sets; define all symbols in Theorem 1 and Theorem 4.
- Give an explicit algorithm to (a) test admissibility, (b) compute R-partitions and Mb(R(i)), and (c) derive estimands; analyze complexity.
- Clarify and properly cite d*-separation/dormant independence; include the 2008 reference.
- Add proof sketches or intuitive explanations for Theorems 2, 4, 6.
- Provide worked examples (with actual figures) and, ideally, a small simulation.

Recommendation
Reject (for now). The paper has promising ideas and likely substantive contributions, but the accumulation of notation/definition errors, unclear theorem statements, missing algorithmic details, and severely flawed references/formatting prevents reliable assessment and does not meet NeurIPS clarity standards. I encourage a thorough revision addressing the above; with improved rigor and presentation, this work could be impactful. Confidence: high.
