This paper investigates the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing data, leveraging and extending the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced in prior work. The authors build upon the sequential factorization theorem of Mohan et al. (2013) to handle more complex missingness mechanisms and introduce new graphical criteria for non-recoverability. A key contribution lies in demonstrating that causal effects, P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relationships are not, a situation where Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable. The paper applies these results to the problem of attrition, providing conditions for recovering causal effects in this common scenario.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear progression of theorems and definitions. The proofs are relegated to the appendix, allowing for a smooth reading experience. The extensions to sequential factorization (Theorem 1) and the introduction of Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 provide general conditions for recoverability, expanding on prior work. However, the numerous theorems and corollaries, while contributing to the completeness of the framework, can make the paper dense and difficult to follow without careful study. The discussion of attrition (Section 7) effectively demonstrates the practical relevance of the theoretical results.  The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations of existing methods and position their work as an advancement in handling MNAR data. The use of examples throughout, particularly in illustrating the application of the theorems, is beneficial. A potential weakness is the lack of empirical validation. While the theoretical framework appears solid, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed methods on real or simulated datasets would strengthen the contribution. 

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized, following a logical progression from background on m-graphs to the presentation of new results. The notation is consistent and well-defined, although the sheer number of definitions and shorthand notations (e.g., Vo, Vm, Rw) requires careful attention from the reader.  The examples, while helpful, could be more visually clear (e.g., improved formatting of the m-graphs). Some sections, particularly those containing multiple theorems, feel very dense and could benefit from further explanation of the intuition behind the results. The writing is technical and precise, which is appropriate for a NIPS submission, but may pose a barrier to readers unfamiliar with the m-graph framework. 

**Originality:** The work extends the existing theory of missing data and causal inference by providing more general conditions for recoverability and addressing scenarios where causal effects can be identified even when probabilistic relationships are not. The central finding that causal queries can be recovered despite non-separability between a variable and its missingness mechanism is novel. The paper builds on the foundation laid by Mohan et al. (2013) and Pearl & Mohan (2013) but significantly expands the scope and applicability of the m-graph framework. The authors carefully position their work in relation to existing approaches (deletion methods, ML estimation, imputation, inverse probability weighting), highlighting the advantages of their graphical modeling approach. The paper correctly cites relevant literature.

**Significance:** The problem of missing data is ubiquitous in many fields. Providing a theoretical framework for identifying recoverable queries, particularly causal effects, is a significant contribution. The results have implications for a wide range of applications, including observational studies, longitudinal data analysis, and causal inference in the presence of attrition. The potential to recover causal effects even when probabilistic relationships are not identifiable is particularly important. The paper moves beyond simply identifying *if* recovery is possible to providing algorithms and graphical criteria for determining recoverability and guiding estimation procedures.  The focus on causal queries, especially in the context of attrition, provides a valuable addition to the literature.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical results regarding the recoverability of causal queries under complex missingness mechanisms.
*   Extension of the m-graph framework, providing more general conditions for recoverability.
*   Clear and rigorous mathematical treatment of the problem.
*   Practical relevance demonstrated through the application to attrition.
*   Addresses a fundamental problem in statistical inference with broad implications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of empirical validation; the theoretical results should be demonstrated on real or simulated data.
*   The density of the presentation and the large number of definitions and theorems may make the paper difficult to understand.
*   The paper relies heavily on the reader's familiarity with the m-graph framework, potentially limiting its accessibility.



Overall, this is a strong technical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of missing data and causal inference. While the lack of empirical validation is a concern, the theoretical results are sound and the potential impact of the work is significant. I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the empirical validation concern (e.g. adding experiments to the submission) and considering revising the presentation for improved clarity.
