- Paper extends Mohan, Pearl & Tian (2013) with new sufficient/necessary graphical conditions for recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries using m-graphs.  
- Main contribution (i): Generalizes ordered factorization to include missingness indicators R, enabling recovery when missingness mechanisms influence each other (Theorem 1).  
- Main contribution (ii): Gives new sufficient conditions for joint recovery when no admissible factorization exists, using R partitions and Markov blankets connected via collider paths in ADMGs (Theorem 2, Corollary 1).  
- Main contribution (iii): Provides graphical non-recoverability criteria that serve as “no-go” tests for joint and conditional recoverability (Theorem 3, Corollary 2).  
- Main contribution (iv): Shows causal effects can be recovered even when probabilistic recovery fails, including inseparable Y and Ry cases via dormant independence/d*-separation (Theorem 4).  
- Main contribution (v): Gives necessary-and-sufficient conditions and explicit estimand for joint and causal recoverability in simple attrition (|Vm|=1) (Theorems 5–6).  
- Technical quality: Development is largely sound, well-tied to graphical criteria, with careful extension of ordered factorizations and nontrivial sufficient conditions.  
- Proofs: Proofs are deferred to appendix; including brief proof sketches in main text would increase confidence.  
- Clarification needed: Theorem 2 conditions are opaque; provide an algorithmic procedure to construct R partitions and Mb(R(i)) and check R(i) ∩ R_Mb(R(i))=∅.  
- Practical issue: Discuss positivity requirement implications when some strata are rare (e.g., P(R(i)=0 | Mb, R_Mb=0)).  
- Conceptual clarity: Emphasize differences between “all-collider path” and stronger “inducing path” conditions and provide examples illustrating the gap.  
- Notation: Fix typos (e.g., GX vs G_X), consolidate shorthand (X′, Wo/Wm/Wr), and reduce overloading of V/V∗/V.  
- Presentation: Provide a short algorithmic pipeline for applying the results and ensure key figures (Fig.2–4) clearly show separating sets.  
- Originality: Advances extend Mohan et al. by allowing R–R dependencies, new semi-Markovian sufficient conditions, explicit non-recoverability criteria, and attrition characterizations.  
- Significance: Results address central MNAR challenges and can guide practitioners on when consistent estimation is possible; impact would grow with automated tools and empirical demos.  
- Strengths: Principled graphical extensions, valuable non-recoverability tests, novel causal recoverability results for inseparable cases, and clean attrition characterizations.  
- Weaknesses: Algorithmic aspects under-specified, no empirical validation, some complex conditions lacking intuition and worked examples, and notation/typo issues.  
- Suggested improvements: Add pseudo-code for admissible sequence search and R partition construction, include small simulations and worked/counter examples, and fix notation/formatting.  
- Questions for authors: Provide complexity bounds/heuristics for searching admissible sequences, robustness to model misspecification, extendability of Theorem 6 beyond |Vm|=1, and potential practitioner checklists/tools.  
- Minor issues: Clean reference formatting, clarify d*-separation definition and practical verification, and define all notation (e.g., G_X,W1).  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — solid theoretical advances but needs clearer algorithms, empirical validation, and notational fixes.
