

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper proposes an extension of Mohan et al. [2013]'s "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) framework to provide general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors claim to establish sufficient conditions for recovering joint and conditional distributions, demonstrate that causal queries P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not, and apply their results to attrition problems. The paper presents several theorems and algorithms for recoverability analysis.

The paper's main theoretical contribution is extending the sequential factorization approach to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact with each other (Section 3), and establishing that causal queries may circumvent the separability requirement that blocks probabilistic query recovery (Section 6). The authors claim these results rectify previous misunderstandings in the literature (e.g., Garcia [2013]).

**Strengths:** The paper provides a theoretically rigorous framework for missing data analysis using graphical models, which is valuable for formalizing assumptions about missingness processes. The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries is conceptually interesting and potentially significant. The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions and logical flow from theoretical results to applications.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the theoretical elegance, the paper suffers from significant shortcomings that undermine its contribution to the machine learning community:

1. **Lack of empirical validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical with no experimental validation. For a NIPS submission, this is a critical deficiency. The authors claim to "rectify prevailing opinion" (Section 7.2) but provide no empirical evidence to support this claim. Without demonstrating performance on real datasets or simulations, the practical utility of these theoretical conditions remains unproven.

2. **Overstated claims about novelty:** The paper claims to extend Mohan et al. [2013], but the theoretical contributions appear incremental rather than transformative. The key advance (causal queries being recoverable when probabilistic queries are not) is presented as novel, but the paper fails to adequately distinguish this from previous work on causal inference with missing data.

3. **No comparison with established methods:** The paper doesn't compare its approach to standard missing data techniques like multiple imputation, maximum likelihood under MAR, or inverse probability weighting. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed methods offer practical advantages over well-established approaches.

4. **Practical limitations unaddressed:** The paper makes no mention of computational complexity, scalability to large datasets, or sensitivity to model misspecification. These are crucial considerations for any method proposed for real-world applications. The authors claim their methods "permit us to detect violations in modeling assumptions," but don't discuss how this detection would be implemented in practice.

5. **Overgeneralization of results:** The paper claims their conditions are "necessary and sufficient" for recoverability, but doesn't address the fundamental problem that the m-graph itself is typically unknown in practice. The paper fails to discuss how one would learn the m-graph from data, which is a major barrier to practical application.

6. **Misalignment with NIPS expectations:** NIPS emphasizes empirical validation alongside theoretical contributions. This paper's complete lack of experiments or comparisons with existing methods makes it unsuitable for NIPS, which prioritizes work with demonstrable practical impact.

**Comparison to prior work:** The paper builds on Mohan et al. [2013] but fails to clearly establish how its theoretical contributions advance the state of the art beyond what's already established in the missing data literature. The related work section (Section 8) adequately covers the literature but doesn't sufficiently contrast the paper's contributions with existing methods.

**Conclusion:** While the theoretical framework presented is logically sound and conceptually interesting, the paper's lack of empirical validation, failure to compare with established methods, and unaddressed practical limitations make it unsuitable for NIPS. The claims about recoverability of causal queries, while theoretically plausible, remain untested and unvalidated. The paper would benefit significantly from empirical experiments demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed conditions, particularly on real-world missing data problems where standard methods might fail.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's theoretical contribution is insufficiently validated for publication in NIPS, which requires empirical evidence to support claims about practical utility. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their submission with empirical results and comparisons to existing methods to be considered for publication.
