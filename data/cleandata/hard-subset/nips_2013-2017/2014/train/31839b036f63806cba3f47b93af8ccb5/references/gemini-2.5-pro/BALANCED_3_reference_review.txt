### Paper Review

**Summary**

This paper presents a formal graphical framework for reasoning about and recovering from missing data, particularly in the challenging Missing Not At Random (MNAR) setting. Building upon the "missingness graph" (m-graph) representation from prior work (Mohan et al., 2013), the authors derive novel conditions for the recoverability of both probabilistic and causal queries. The main contributions are: (1) An extension of the sequential factorization theorem for recovering joint distributions to cases where missingness mechanisms can influence each other. (2) A new method for recovery when sequential factorization is not possible, based on partitioning the missingness variables. (3) The first formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, providing necessary conditions that alert researchers when their assumptions are insufficient. (4) A significant finding that causal queries can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries are not, specifically when an outcome variable is statistically inseparable from its missingness mechanism. (5) Application of these results to the problem of attrition in longitudinal studies, yielding necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery and correcting claims from previous literature.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The problem of missing data is fundamental and pervasive across all empirical sciences, including the fields represented at NeurIPS. Most standard methods rely on strong, often untestable, assumptions like Missing At Random (MAR). This paper provides a principled, graphical language to articulate a much broader and more realistic class of missingness assumptions (MNAR) and rigorously analyze their consequences. The ability to determine *a priori* whether a target quantity can be consistently estimated is of immense practical and theoretical importance.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The paper makes several novel and important contributions.
    *   The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) are a key advance. While much of the literature focuses on sufficient conditions for success, providing necessary conditions for failure is equally important for guiding research and preventing flawed analyses.
    *   The decoupling of probabilistic and causal recoverability (Section 6) is a deep and insightful result. The demonstration that P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable when P(x, y) is not challenges common intuitions and highlights the unique properties of causal queries.
    *   The generalizations of existing theorems (Theorems 1 and 2) broaden the applicability of the m-graph framework to more complex and realistic scenarios, such as those with interacting missingness mechanisms.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and builds rigorously on the established foundations of graphical models and do-calculus. The theorems are stated formally, and the arguments are supported by clear graphical examples that effectively illustrate the core concepts. The work appears to be a complete and mature theoretical contribution. The application to attrition, which clarifies and corrects previous work, further attests to the technical quality and carefulness of the authors.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper would be significantly more impactful for the NeurIPS audience if it included an empirical component. A simulation study would be highly effective. For instance, the authors could:
    *   Generate data from a known m-graph where a query is recoverable by their proposed methods but not by standard approaches (e.g., a case that violates MAR).
    *   Introduce missingness according to the graph's specifications.
    *   Apply their recovery algorithm and demonstrate that the estimate converges to the true parameter value with increasing sample size.
    *   Show that standard methods like listwise deletion, mean imputation, or even model-based methods assuming MAR yield biased estimates.
    This would not only validate the correctness of the theorems but also provide a powerful, concrete demonstration of their practical utility over existing methods.

2.  **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is dense with specialized terminology from the graphical causality literature (e.g., d*-separation, inducing paths, Markov blankets). While precise, this can make the paper challenging for readers not already deeply familiar with this subfield.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors could improve accessibility by adding more intuitive explanations for these key concepts alongside their formal definitions. For example, a brief sentence explaining *why* a collider path between a variable and its missingness mechanism is problematic would greatly aid reader comprehension. A more detailed, step-by-step walkthrough of the application of Theorem 2 (as was done for Theorem 1 in Example 1) would also be beneficial in the main text.

3.  **Practical Considerations:** The power of this framework rests on the ability of a researcher to specify the correct m-graph. This is a strong assumption. While the authors cite other work on testing m-graphs, a short discussion in the paper about the practical aspects of model specification would be valuable.
    *   **Suggestion:** A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the results to misspecification of the m-graph could be included. For example, what happens if a researcher omits a crucial edge? Does recoverability gracefully degrade, or do the estimates become arbitrarily biased? Acknowledging this practical challenge and providing some guidance would strengthen the paper.

**Summary of Pros and Cons**

*   **Pros:**
    *   Addresses the critical and ubiquitous problem of missing data in a principled manner.
    *   Provides novel and significant theoretical results, including the first graphical criteria for non-recoverability.
    *   Reveals the important insight that causal queries can be recovered in situations where probabilistic queries cannot.
    *   Technically sound, rigorous, and well-structured.

*   **Cons:**
    *   Purely theoretical; lacks empirical validation through simulations or real-data experiments, which would substantially strengthen its claims and demonstrate practical value.
    *   High conceptual density may limit its accessibility to the broader machine learning community.
    *   Limited discussion of the practical challenges of specifying the correct graphical model.

**Recommendation**

This is a high-quality theoretical paper with significant and original contributions to an important problem. The graphical criteria for non-recoverability and the separation of causal from probabilistic recovery are particularly noteworthy. The main weakness is the absence of any empirical validation, which limits its immediate impact and convincingness for a large portion of the NeurIPS audience. However, the strength of the theoretical results is substantial. I lean towards acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to include a simulation study in the final version to demonstrate the practical implications of their framework. Such an addition would elevate this from a strong theoretical paper to an outstanding and highly impactful contribution.
