This paper tackles the important and notoriously difficult problem of recovering causal and probabilistic queries from data with missing values, leveraging the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs). The extension of prior work by refining sequential factorization and presenting new recoverability criteria, particularly the ability to identify causal effects even when the target variable is inseparable from its missingness mechanism, is a genuinely novel and exciting contribution. The paper demonstrates a strong theoretical grounding, with clear definitions and theorems, and applies these to the practical problem of attrition.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core contribution – recovering causal effects despite non-separability – is a significant advancement. This directly addresses a limitation of existing approaches and opens up new possibilities for causal inference in the presence of missing data.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper builds upon a solid foundation of graphical models and causal inference, presenting rigorous proofs for the derived theorems. The generalization of sequential factorization is a valuable extension.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition, a common problem in many fields, enhances the practical impact of the work. The theorems provide concrete guidance for assessing recoverability in such scenarios.
* **Clear Definitions:** The formal definitions of concepts like general ordered factorization and d*-separation contribute to the clarity and precision of the work.
* **Addressing Limitations:** The paper not only presents conditions for recoverability but also provides criteria for *non*-recoverability, which is crucial for practical application.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks empirical validation beyond the illustrative examples. While the theoretical results are compelling, demonstrating their performance on real-world datasets would strengthen the work considerably. The examples, while helpful, are relatively simple.
* **Complexity:** The mathematical notation and the chain of reasoning in some of the proofs are dense. While mathematically sound, this might hinder accessibility for readers less familiar with the technical details.
* **Scope of Attrition Examples:** The attrition examples are a bit simplistic. Real-world attrition scenarios often involve more complex missingness patterns than those considered here. Exploring more nuanced attrition models could be beneficial.
* **Reliance on Assumptions:** The applicability of the theorems hinges on correctly specifying the m-graph. In practice, accurately identifying the missingness mechanism can be challenging. The paper doesn't extensively discuss sensitivity analysis or methods for validating m-graph assumptions.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* The core theoretical result regarding causal effect recoverability is highly significant and potentially transformative.
* The paper offers a rigorous and well-defined framework for analyzing missing data problems.
* The potential impact on fields dealing with observational data with missing values is substantial.

**Con:**

* The lack of empirical validation is a significant concern.
* The complexity of the presentation might limit the paper's reach.
* The reliance on accurate m-graph specification could be a practical limitation.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the lack of extensive empirical validation and the inherent complexity of the material, I believe this paper represents a significant advance in the field of missing data analysis. The novelty of the central result – recovering causal effects even when Y and Ry are inseparable – is compelling and outweighs the weaknesses. The theoretical framework is sound, and the potential impact of the work is substantial. While further empirical work is needed to fully demonstrate the practical utility of the approach, the paper introduces a genuinely novel idea with the potential to significantly impact causal inference in the presence of missing data. I recommend **acceptance**. The program committee should encourage the authors to prioritize empirical validation in future work.
