This paper tackles the crucial problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing data, building upon the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced in prior work. The core contribution lies in extending the conditions for recoverability, particularly demonstrating that causal effects can be identified even when the underlying probabilistic relationships are not. The paper is theoretically rich, introducing refinements to sequential factorization, algorithms for cases where factorization fails, non-recoverability criteria, and crucially, a result showing causal query recoverability even when the target variable and its missingness mechanism are inseparable. The application to attrition problems provides a concrete example of the utility of these results.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Depth:** The paper presents a significant advancement in the theoretical understanding of recoverability under missing data mechanisms. The extended sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) and the generalization for problems where factorization fails (Theorem 2) are substantial contributions.
* **Causal Inference Focus:** The demonstration that causal effects can be recovered even with non-recoverable probabilistic relationships is a key strength. This addresses a critical limitation of many traditional missing data approaches.
* **Novelty:** The exploration of conditions for non-recoverability (Theorem 3 & Corollary 2) is valuable, as it provides guidance to practitioners on when their assumptions are insufficient.  The results concerning recoverability even when Y and Ry are inseparable (Example 3, Theorem 4) are particularly novel.
* **Attrition Application:** The application to attrition problems grounds the theoretical results in a practically relevant scenario, making the work more accessible.
* **Thoroughness:** The paper is comprehensive, covering various aspects of recoverability, from sufficient conditions to necessary conditions and algorithms for specific cases. The extensive related work section demonstrates awareness of the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity & Readability:** The paper is dense and mathematically demanding. While the theoretical rigor is commendable, it sometimes comes at the expense of clarity. Definitions and theorems are presented without sufficient intuitive explanation, making it difficult for a broader audience to grasp the core ideas. The frequent use of shorthand notation (e.g., `Wr`, `Rxi`) adds to this complexity.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:**  The paper is almost entirely theoretical. While the example in Section 7 provides some illustration, a more extensive empirical evaluation – even simulations – would significantly strengthen the claims. Demonstrating the practical impact of the new recoverability conditions on real or simulated datasets is crucial.  How does this framework perform compared to existing methods (e.g., multiple imputation, inverse probability weighting) in different settings?
* **Assumptions:** The reliance on causal diagrams and the assumptions underlying these diagrams are not always fully discussed. The paper assumes the causal diagram is known, which is a strong assumption in many practical settings. Addressing the sensitivity of the results to misspecification of the causal graph would be beneficial.
* **Notation Overload:** The extensive use of notation can be overwhelming. While notation is necessary for mathematical precision, the paper could benefit from a more streamlined and consistent use of symbols.
* **Corollary 2 limitation:** Corollary 2 states non-recoverability under certain conditions. It would be helpful to explicitly state when P(Y|X) *is* recoverable in those cases.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental and challenging problem in statistical inference with significant theoretical advancements.
* The results on causal query recoverability are particularly compelling and have the potential to impact the field.
* The thoroughness of the analysis and the comprehensive related work section are positive indicators of the paper's quality.
* The paper offers new tools and insights for researchers dealing with missing data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The high level of mathematical complexity and lack of empirical validation may limit the paper's impact.
* The strong assumptions regarding the causal graph need to be addressed more thoroughly.
* The paper's readability could be improved through clearer explanations and a more streamlined notation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of missing data and causal inference. However, the complexity and lack of empirical validation raise concerns. I believe the paper is currently borderline.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject, with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit.** 

The authors should prioritize:

1. **Improving readability:**  Add more intuitive explanations for key concepts and theorems. Reduce the reliance on shorthand notation.
2. **Adding empirical validation:** Conduct simulations to demonstrate the performance of the proposed methods in various scenarios. Compare the results to existing approaches.
3. **Discussing the sensitivity to causal graph misspecification:**  Analyze how the results are affected by errors in the assumed causal diagram.
4. **Clarifying the conditions for recoverability:** In cases where a non-recoverability criterion is met, explicitly state when recovery is still possible.
