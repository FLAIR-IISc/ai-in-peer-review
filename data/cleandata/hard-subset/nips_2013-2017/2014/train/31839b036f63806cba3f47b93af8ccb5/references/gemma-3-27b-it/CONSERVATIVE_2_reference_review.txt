This paper tackles the challenging problem of recovering causal and probabilistic queries from datasets with missing data, leveraging the framework of missingness graphs (m-graphs). While the topic is undeniably important and relevant to a wide range of scientific disciplines, I find the paper’s contributions incremental and, in parts, lacking sufficient justification given the existing literature. My overall assessment is skeptical, and I lean towards rejection.

The core idea – extending previous work on m-graphs to handle more complex missingness mechanisms and, crucially, demonstrating recoverability of causal effects even when probabilistic relations are non-recoverable – is conceptually appealing. However, the paper feels like a somewhat disjointed collection of theorems and corollaries, with limited demonstration of the practical impact or novelty of these results.

A key weakness lies in the lack of substantial empirical validation. The authors rely almost entirely on theoretical results. While the proofs are stated to be in the appendix, the absence of simulations or real-world experiments to illustrate the applicability and benefits of the proposed extensions is a significant drawback. The examples provided are largely illustrative and don't convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods over existing approaches like multiple imputation or inverse probability weighting, particularly in realistic settings.

The paper builds heavily on previous work by Mohan et al. (2013) and Pearl, and while acknowledging this is appropriate, the incremental contribution doesn't feel substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS. Theorem 1, extending sequential factorization, seems reasonable, but its practical implications are not clearly articulated. Similarly, Theorem 2, generalizing recovery conditions, feels abstract without concrete examples demonstrating situations where it unlocks previously intractable problems.

The discussion regarding recoverability of causal queries (Section 6) is perhaps the most interesting aspect. The claim that P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when Y and Ry are inseparable is noteworthy, but the proof relies on specific conditions (Theorem 4) that may not hold in many real-world scenarios. The example provided is helpful, but a more thorough exploration of the conditions under which this recovery is possible would strengthen the argument.

The treatment of attrition in Section 7 feels somewhat cursory. While linking the theoretical results to a practical problem is valuable, the discussion lacks depth and doesn’t offer clear guidance to practitioners on how to apply the proposed methods in real-world attrition scenarios. The claims of rectifying "prevailing opinion" are strong but require more robust evidence than presented.

The related work section is comprehensive but somewhat disjointed, listing a wide range of techniques without deeply connecting them to the specific contributions of this paper. It would be beneficial to more clearly position this work within the broader landscape of missing data research.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and practically relevant problem.
*   Extends the theoretical framework of m-graphs.
*   Demonstrates potential for recovering causal effects in challenging scenarios.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Lacks sufficient empirical validation.
*   Incremental contribution, particularly given the existing literature.
*   Theoretical results feel abstract and lack clear practical guidance.
*   Discussion of attrition is cursory.
*   Limited demonstration of superiority over existing methods.



**Overall, I believe the paper falls short of the NIPS standard.** It presents a mathematically sound but ultimately theoretical extension of existing work, lacking the empirical grounding and practical impact necessary for acceptance. The authors should consider conducting more extensive simulations and real-world experiments to demonstrate the value of their proposed methods before resubmitting. A more focused presentation, concentrating on the most novel aspects (e.g., causal query recovery) and providing clearer guidance for practitioners, would also improve the paper’s impact.
