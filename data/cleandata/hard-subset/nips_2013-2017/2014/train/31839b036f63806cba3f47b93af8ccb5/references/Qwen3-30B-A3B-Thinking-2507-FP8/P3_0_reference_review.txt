

# Peer Review: Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data

## Summary and Context

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in statistical analysis: handling missing data in a principled way. The authors extend the "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) framework introduced by Mohan et al. (2013) to develop general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (joint/conditional distributions) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))) from missing data. The key insight is that causal effects may be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations are not, specifically when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separated. The paper also provides necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability in attrition problems, correcting a misconception in Garcia (2013).

The work builds on a growing body of literature on missing data (Rubin, 1976; Little & Rubin, 2002), but makes a significant theoretical contribution by unifying probabilistic and causal inference under a graphical framework. It extends the causal inference literature (Pearl, 2009; Shpitser & Pearl, 2006) by addressing how missingness mechanisms interact with causal structures.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear and important theoretical advancement by showing that causal effects can be recoverable even when Y and Ry are connected by an inducing path (Theorem 4 and Example 3). This challenges the prevailing assumption that separability of Y from Ry is necessary for causal inference under missing data.

2. **Correcting a Misconception**: The paper effectively identifies and corrects a significant error in Garcia (2013), where the author incorrectly claimed that X being an ancestor of Ry was necessary for non-recoverability. The authors demonstrate through a counterexample (Figure 4a) that this is not the case, providing a more accurate characterization of the problem.

3. **Comprehensive Framework**: The paper provides a unified theoretical framework that handles multiple missingness mechanisms interacting with each other (via the general ordered factorization and Theorem 2), addressing limitations of previous work.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with precise definitions, well-constructed examples, and a logical flow from probabilistic to causal recovery.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: While the paper makes strong theoretical claims, it lacks any empirical validation or case studies demonstrating the practical utility of the methods. The absence of experiments with real or simulated data limits the assessment of the framework's practical impact, which is particularly important for a NIPS audience interested in both theory and application.

2. **Limited Connection to Machine Learning**: The paper focuses on causal inference but doesn't sufficiently connect to machine learning applications where missing data is prevalent (e.g., recommendation systems, collaborative filtering). While the authors reference Marlin et al. (2007, 2009, 2011), they don't demonstrate how their framework could improve existing ML approaches to missing data.

3. **Technical Depth of Key Concepts**: The introduction of "d*-separation" (Definition 4) and its role in causal recovery feels somewhat abrupt without sufficient intuitive motivation. The paper could better explain why this concept is necessary for causal recovery but not for probabilistic recovery.

4. **Computational Considerations**: The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of the proposed recovery procedures. For practical implementation, understanding the computational cost of these algorithms would be important, especially for larger datasets.

## Assessment Against Review Criteria

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs (referenced to appendix) and well-defined theorems. The theoretical development is thorough and addresses a significant gap in the literature. However, the lack of empirical validation is a notable limitation for a NIPS paper that often values both theoretical and empirical contributions.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the examples effectively illustrate the concepts. The main challenge for readers might be the high level of background knowledge required in causal inference and graphical models, but this is appropriate for a NIPS audience.

**Originality**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending the m-graph framework to handle causal queries under missing data. The insight that causal effects can be recoverable even when Y and Ry are not d-separated is novel and important. The correction of Garcia's misconception further strengthens the originality.

**Significance**: The problem of missing data is ubiquitous across scientific domains, making this work highly significant. The framework provides a principled way to diagnose when causal effects can be recovered from missing data, which has direct implications for fields like healthcare and social sciences. The work advances the state of the art by unifying probabilistic and causal inference under a single graphical framework.

## Recommendation

This is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses an important problem in statistics and causal inference. The paper makes a clear and significant advance by showing that causal effects can be recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not. The correction of Garcia's misconception is particularly valuable for the field.

However, the lack of empirical validation is a notable limitation for a NIPS submission. While theoretical contributions are welcome at NIPS, the absence of any experimental demonstration of the framework's utility makes it difficult to fully assess its practical impact. I recommend acceptance with the expectation that the authors would consider adding empirical validation in future work, but the theoretical contribution alone is sufficiently strong for NIPS.

**Pro**: Strong theoretical contribution, clear presentation, important correction of prior misconception, significant advancement in understanding recoverability of causal effects.

**Con**: No empirical validation, limited connection to machine learning applications, some technical concepts could be better motivated.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical foundations of missing data analysis and causal inference. With the addition of empirical validation, it would be an even stronger contribution to the field. As it stands, it represents a significant advance in the theoretical understanding of missing data problems, particularly for causal inference.
