

# Review: "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

## Summary
This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) to develop a formal framework for determining recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors introduce new theoretical conditions for recoverability of joint and conditional distributions, demonstrate that causal effects may be recoverable even when associated probabilistic relations are not, and apply their results to attrition problems. The paper makes significant contributions to the field by distinguishing between recoverability conditions for probabilistic versus causal queries and providing necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for recovery.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical development. The authors build logically on prior work while introducing novel concepts (like d*-separation) that address limitations in previous approaches. The proofs in the appendix are thorough, and the examples (particularly Example 3) effectively illustrate the distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability.

The paper carefully distinguishes between different missing data mechanisms (MCAR, MAR, MNAR) and provides clear conditions for when queries can or cannot be recovered. The authors are appropriately cautious in their claims, acknowledging limitations of their approach (e.g., the requirement for strictly positive distributions over complete cases).

The paper's greatest strength is its theoretical precision in distinguishing between recoverability conditions for probabilistic queries versus causal queries - a distinction that has not been adequately addressed in the missing data literature. The demonstration that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y and Ry are not d-separated (Example 3) represents a significant theoretical advance.

## Clarity
The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The authors provide careful definitions of key concepts (m-graphs, recoverability, d*-separation) and use examples effectively to illustrate complex ideas. The organization flows logically from problem statement to theoretical development to applications.

The notation is consistent throughout, though the introduction of R variables and their relationships to substantive variables may require careful reading for those unfamiliar with missing data literature. The appendix containing all proofs is a valuable resource for readers interested in the technical details.

## Originality
This paper makes several important original contributions:

1. It establishes that causal queries (P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations (P(y,x) and P(y|x)) are not recoverable - a fundamental insight that challenges conventional wisdom in missing data analysis.

2. It introduces the concept of d*-separation to handle cases where Y and Ry are connected by an inducing path, which is a significant extension of existing graphical criteria.

3. It provides necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of causal effects under simple attrition, correcting misconceptions in prior work (e.g., Garcia 2013).

The paper clearly differentiates its contributions from prior work, particularly Mohan et al. (2013) and Garcia (2013), by addressing limitations in their approaches and providing more general conditions.

## Significance
The problem of missing data is pervasive across scientific disciplines, making this work highly significant. The paper's framework provides researchers with a systematic way to determine whether a query can be recovered from missing data, which has direct implications for the validity of scientific conclusions.

The most significant contribution is the theoretical demonstration that causal effects may be recoverable even when probabilistic recovery fails. This has important practical implications for fields like epidemiology, economics, and social sciences where causal inference is crucial but missing data is common.

The application to attrition problems (Section 7) is particularly relevant, as attrition is a major concern in longitudinal studies. The paper provides necessary and sufficient conditions for causal effect recovery under simple attrition, which corrects previous misconceptions in the literature.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Strong theoretical foundation with careful definitions and rigorous proofs
- Clear distinction between recoverability of probabilistic vs. causal queries
- Novel results demonstrating causal recoverability when probabilistic recovery fails
- Appropriate application to attrition problems with necessary and sufficient conditions
- Thorough comparison with prior work and identification of limitations in existing approaches

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper is highly theoretical with limited discussion of practical implementation
- No empirical evaluation or comparison with existing missing data methods
- The notation for missingness mechanisms could be more intuitive for non-specialists
- Limited discussion of computational complexity for the proposed algorithms

## Recommendation
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of missing data analysis, particularly for causal inference. The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries is both novel and important, with clear implications for how researchers should approach missing data in causal studies.

While the paper is highly technical and may be challenging for readers without strong background in causal inference, its theoretical contributions are substantial and well-supported. The paper advances the state of the art by providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery that were previously unavailable.

I recommend acceptance for NeurIPS as this work represents a significant theoretical advance with clear potential for impact on how missing data is handled in causal inference across multiple scientific domains.

The paper would be strengthened by adding a brief discussion of computational considerations or practical implementation examples, but these are not essential for a theoretical contribution of this caliber. The work is sufficiently novel and significant to warrant publication at NeurIPS.
