This paper addresses the critical problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing entries. The work builds directly upon the "Missingness Graph" (m-graph) framework introduced by Mohan et al. [2013], extending it in several significant directions. The main contributions are: (1) more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries via a refined sequential factorization theorem that accommodates dependencies between missingness mechanisms; (2) graphical criteria for recovering joint distributions in cases where factorization fails; (3) novel graphical criteria for establishing non-recoverability; and (4) a demonstration that causal queries P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries are not.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper's most impactful contribution is decoupling the recoverability of causal and probabilistic queries. The insight that P(y|do(x)) may be identifiable from missing data even when P(y|x) is not (e.g., when Y and its missingness mechanism R_y are inseparable) is a powerful and non-obvious result. This significantly advances the understanding of inference under missingness. The introduction of formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability is also a novel and highly practical contribution, providing researchers with tools to identify when an estimate is impossible.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound, presenting formal theorems and clear graphical conditions. The arguments are built rigorously upon the established foundations of graphical models and do-calculus. The application of the theory to the problem of attrition demonstrates a clear grasp of a practical domain and rectifies misconceptions in existing literature, showcasing the work's quality and relevance.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. It systematically extends prior work, motivates each new theorem with examples, and clearly defines its terminology. The structure makes the logical progression of ideas easy to follow for a reader familiar with the domain.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / Suggestions for improvement):**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the work would be substantially more compelling with empirical validation. A simulation study demonstrating the proposed recovery algorithms on synthetic data—showing that they produce consistent estimates where the theory predicts recoverability and fail where it predicts non-recoverability—would provide crucial evidence of their practical utility and correctness. This is the paper's main weakness.
*   **Accessibility:** The paper is dense and leans heavily on definitions from the authors' prior NIPS paper. While appropriate for an extension paper, a slightly more self-contained presentation of the core m-graph concepts could improve accessibility for the broader NIPS audience.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper with significant contributions to the field of causal inference and missing data. The lack of experiments is a notable omission but does not invalidate the importance of the theoretical results.
