- The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation with clearly stated theorems and seemingly solid proofs.
- Key novelty: demonstrates causal queries can be recoverable even when underlying probabilistic relations are not.
- Shows recoverability even when the variable and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable.
- Practical relevance: applies results to attrition, clarifying when causal effects can be estimated under non-random missingness.
- Related work is comprehensive and situates the contribution well.
- Core definitions (recoverability, missingness graphs, d*-separation) are clearly presented.

- The theory is complex and notation is dense, making the paper hard to follow without more intuition.
- No empirical evaluation; lacks simulations or real-data examples to demonstrate practical impact.
- Some examples (e.g., Example 3) are overly involved and need simpler illustrative cases.
- The paper provides sufficient but not necessarily necessary conditions; the tightness of conditions is not discussed.
- Computational complexity of implementing recovery algorithms and identifying admissible sequences is not addressed.

- Suggest briefly explain why d*-separation matters for recovering causal effects with missing data.
- Clarify the practical implications and likelihood of the restrictive conditions in Theorem 4.
- Expand the attrition section with specific scenarios where the techniques apply.
- Add visual aids (diagrams for d-separation, inducing paths) to improve comprehension.
- Clarify ambiguous notation in Equation 5, particularly the meaning of "Z".

- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound contribution with potential significant impact; well-written and relevant to the conference audience.
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of empirical validation, high theoretical complexity, and missing discussion of computational feasibility.

- Overall assessment: strong theoretical contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity, add a small simulation, and discuss computational issues.
- Reviewer confidence: 7/10.
