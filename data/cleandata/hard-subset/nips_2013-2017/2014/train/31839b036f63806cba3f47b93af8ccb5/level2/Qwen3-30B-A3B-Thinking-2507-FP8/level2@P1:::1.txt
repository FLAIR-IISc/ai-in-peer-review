

This paper extends the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to provide more general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data, building upon Mohan et al. (2013). The authors introduce novel graphical criteria for recoverability of joint/conditional distributions (Theorems 1, 2, 3) and make a significant contribution by demonstrating that causal effects (e.g., $P(y|do(x))$) can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic relations (e.g., $P(y,x)$) are not, specifically when $Y$ and its missingness mechanism $R_y$ are not d-separated (Theorem 4). The paper applies these results to attrition problems, correcting misconceptions in prior work (e.g., Garcia 2013) through necessary and sufficient conditions (Theorems 5–6).

**Strengths:**  
1. **Originality and Significance:** The core insight—that causal queries may be recoverable despite inseparability of $Y$ and $R_y$—is novel and addresses a critical gap. Prior work (e.g., Garcia 2013) assumed causal effects require probabilistic recoverability, but the authors prove otherwise via do-calculus and d*-separation (Theorem 4). This advances the state-of-the-art in missing-data theory with clear practical implications for longitudinal studies.  
2. **Technical Quality:** The theoretical contributions are rigorously developed with proofs in the appendix. Theorems 1–6 generalize Mohan et al. (2013) to handle interactions among missingness mechanisms (e.g., R-variables influencing each other), and the examples (e.g., Figure 3) effectively illustrate the causal recoverability insight. The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) also fill a gap in the literature.  
3. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions (e.g., general ordered factorization) and logical flow. The application to attrition (Section 7) demonstrates practical utility, and the related work section (Section 8) adequately contextualizes the contributions against existing methods (e.g., MAR/MCAR assumptions, inverse weighting).  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Motivation for d*-separation:** The use of d*-separation (Definition 4) is pivotal to Theorem 4 but feels under-motivated. The paper should clarify *why* d*-separation (vs. standard d-separation) is necessary for causal recoverability, perhaps by contrasting it with the probabilistic case (where standard d-separation is required). A brief example showing the failure of standard d-separation in their causal context would strengthen the argument.  
2. **Empirical validation:** While the theoretical focus is justified, the paper lacks empirical demonstrations of the proposed algorithms (e.g., in Sections 3–4). Adding a small synthetic experiment (e.g., recovering causal effects under attrition with varying missingness mechanisms) would better showcase the practical utility of the conditions. The attrition application (Section 7) remains purely theoretical.  
3. **Ambiguity in Corollary 2:** The condition "all intermediate nodes in $p$ are in $X$" (Corollary 2) is unclear. If $X$ contains collider nodes, this might conflict with the collider path definition. A concrete example (e.g., modifying Figure 4(a)) would resolve this ambiguity.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution with clear significance to the field. The core insight on causal recoverability (Theorem 4) is compelling and corrects a misconception in prior work. While the empirical validation is limited, the theoretical rigor and novelty justify acceptance. The weaknesses noted are addressable in a revised version but do not undermine the paper’s scientific quality.  

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel causal recoverability insight; rigorous extension of Mohan et al. (2013); corrected misconceptions in attrition literature; strong theoretical foundation.  
*Con:* Limited empirical validation; d*-separation motivation could be strengthened; Corollary 2 requires clarification.  

Overall, this work is a valuable addition to the NIPS literature on missing data, advancing both theoretical understanding and practical applicability. It meets all criteria: technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant. I recommend acceptance.
