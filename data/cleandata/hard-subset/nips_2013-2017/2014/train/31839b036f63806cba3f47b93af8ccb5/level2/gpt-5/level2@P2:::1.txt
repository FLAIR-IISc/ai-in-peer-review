Summary
The paper studies when probabilistic and causal queries are recoverable from data with missing entries given a causal model of the missingness process encoded as a missingness graph (m-graph). It extends Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) along four fronts: (i) a generalized “sequential factorization” that admits R-nodes (missingness indicators) in admissible ordered factorizations, enabling recovery in settings with interacting missingness mechanisms; (ii) a new recovery theorem for joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists, based on partitioning R and conditioning on Markov blankets along collider paths; (iii) graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions; and (iv) sufficient conditions showing certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when the probabilistic quantities P(y,x) and P(y|x) are not, including cases where Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are inseparable. The paper further provides necessary and sufficient conditions for joint and causal recoverability under “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1), correcting some claims in the attrition literature. Proofs are in the appendix.

Relation to prior work is appropriate: it builds directly on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) and leverages do-calculus and dormant independences (Shpitser & Pearl), while contrasting with MAR/MCAR approaches and standard IPW/MI techniques that are model-blind to missingness mechanisms.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: Allowing R-nodes in admissible factorizations is a clean extension that broadens the scope beyond Mohan et al. (2013), especially for longitudinal/interactive missingness.
- New negative results: The non-recoverability criteria for P(V) and P(Y|X) give valuable “stop” conditions that are missing from much of the literature.
- Causal vs probabilistic distinction: Clear demonstration (Example 3 and Theorem 4) that causal effects may be recoverable even when relevant probabilistic quantities are not, using do-calculus and d*-separation/dormant independences.
- Attrition results: Necessary and sufficient conditions under simple attrition for both joint and causal queries are concise, intuitive, and practically useful; the paper correctly challenges prior necessary conditions in Garcia (2013).
- Clarity of graph-based conditions: Theorems articulate intuitive path-based criteria (neighbors, collider/inducing paths), aligning with practitioners’ reasoning.

Weaknesses and questions
- Algorithmic completeness and usability: While Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 provide constructive routes, the paper promises “general algorithms” but does not present a unified decision procedure with clear steps, termination, and complexity. For a practitioner, it is unclear how to systematically search over factorizations including R-nodes or to compute R partitions and Markov blankets along collider paths in large graphs.
- Scope and assumptions: Several results require semi-Markovian models and assume R has no arrows into V∪U, but interactions among R are allowed. Clarify the precise scope of Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 vis-à-vis latent confounding between V and R (disallowed in the corollary, allowed in the theorem) and discuss how sensitive the results are to these modeling restrictions.
- Theorem 4 accessibility: The sufficient conditions using d*-separation and dormant independences are technically heavy. A more explicit, checkable graphical criterion (or a derived corollary for common motifs) would improve practical adoption. Also, the paper cites Shpitser & Pearl (2008) but that reference is missing in the bibliography.
- Empirical validation: No empirical or simulation evidence is provided. While the paper is theoretical, small simulations demonstrating finite-sample behavior of the proposed recovery formulas (bias/variance under correct/incorrect m-graphs) would strengthen significance and help readers judge robustness.
- Clarity and notation: Some notation is dense or inconsistent (e.g., Xr vs RXm; use of primes for indicators; “d*-separability” vs “d*-separation”), and minimality in Definition 2 could use more intuition. Figures are central to understanding but not shown here; ensure they are legible and comprehensive. Reference formatting contains clear glitches and several entries are incomplete.

Quality
- Technical soundness appears high; theorems are coherent and arguments align with do-calculus and ADMG reasoning. However, with all proofs deferred, brief proof sketches would help build intuition (especially for Theorems 2, 3, 4, 6).
- Claims are generally well-supported, and counterexamples/non-recoverability are explicitly addressed.

Clarity
- Good high-level organization; however, definitions and conditions are dense. Adding a consolidated checklist/flowchart for query recovery would improve readability. Fix reference formatting and include the missing Shpitser & Pearl (2008) citation.

Originality
- The inclusion of R in admissible factorizations, the non-recoverability criteria, and the causal-under-inseparability results are novel and valuable extensions beyond Mohan et al. (2013).

Significance
- High potential impact for researchers handling MNAR data and for causal inference under attrition. The results offer principled guidance where heuristic MAR-based methods can fail.

Specific suggestions
- Provide an explicit decision procedure: given G and query Q, (1) test identifiability (for causal Q), (2) search for admissible sequences including R with minimal conditioning, (3) if none, construct R partitions and apply Theorem 2, (4) apply non-recoverability tests, (5) for causal effects, test Theorem 4 conditions. Pseudocode and complexity notes would be welcome.
- Add more examples illustrating Theorem 2 and Theorem 4, including cases with interacting R and latent confounding.
- Include small-scale simulations to illustrate recovery vs non-recovery and sensitivity to graph misspecification.
- Clean up notation and references; add Shpitser & Pearl (2008), standardize symbols, and clarify positivity assumptions and compatibility definitions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong theoretical contributions that extend prior NIPS work.
- Practical, interpretable graphical conditions for both recovery and non-recovery.
- Important correction and consolidation of results for attrition.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of a unifying, implementable algorithm and empirical validation.
- Accessibility of Theorem 4 may limit adoption without additional guidance.
- Presentation issues (notation/reference glitches) reduce clarity.

Overall, this is a solid, theoretically substantive extension with clear relevance to the NIPS community. With improvements to exposition, a unified procedure, and minimal empirical validation, it would be a strong addition. I lean toward acceptance.
