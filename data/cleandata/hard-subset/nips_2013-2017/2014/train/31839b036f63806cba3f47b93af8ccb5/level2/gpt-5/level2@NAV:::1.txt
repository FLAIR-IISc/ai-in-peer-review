Summary and relation to prior work: The paper studies when probabilistic queries P(y, x), P(y|x) and causal queries P(y|do(x)) are estimable from data with missing entries, given an explicit missingness graph (m-graph). It extends Mohan et al. (NIPS’13) in three directions: (i) a generalized sequential factorization (Theorem 1) that admits interactions among missingness mechanisms (R variables) and recovers distributions beyond the original ordered-factorization setting; (ii) recovery of joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists via a partition-based criterion (Theorem 2) and a practical corollary; and (iii) graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2). A key conceptual contribution is that certain causal effects are recoverable even when corresponding probabilistic factors are not, including cases where Y and its missingness Ry are not d-separated; this is formalized using dormant independences/d∗-separation (Theorem 4). The paper applies these results to attrition, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for joint and causal recovery when |Vm|=1 (Theorems 5–6). The work builds on and clarifies connections to do-calculus (Shpitser & Pearl, 2006; Pearl, 2009) and advances the graphical treatment of MNAR beyond MAR/MCAR and prior attrition analyses (e.g., Garcia, 2013).

Quality: The claims are supported by formal theorems with proofs (appendix) and illustrative examples. The logical structure is sound, and the non-recoverability criteria are especially valuable to prevent misuse. However, there is no empirical evaluation (simulation or case studies) to demonstrate finite-sample behavior or robustness to graph misspecification.

Clarity: The exposition is generally clear but dense. Notation is heavy; several references are inconsistently formatted. More explicit pseudo-code for constructing admissible sequences/partitions and a worked example of Theorem 4 would help. Clearly restating assumptions (semi-Markovian, positivity over complete cases, “R not parents of V”) up front would improve readability.

Originality: High. The generalized factorization over R, the partition-based recovery, and the negative criteria are novel and fill gaps since Mohan et al. (2013). Showing causal recoverability despite inseparable Y–Ry paths is conceptually important.

Significance and usefulness: High for researchers working on MNAR, causal inference under missingness, and longitudinal attrition. The results give actionable graphical checks and recovery formulas.

Suggestions: Add algorithmic procedures with complexity guarantees; provide simulations comparing recovery vs. baseline MAR/IPW/MI under MNAR; discuss sensitivity to m-graph misspecification and connections to structure learning; release a validator tool.

Pros: strong theory; novel non-recoverability; important insight on causal vs probabilistic recoverability; clear attrition characterization. Cons: no experiments; limited guidance on constructing m-graphs; minor clarity/citation issues.

Overall: Clear theoretical advance appropriate for NeurIPS; recommend acceptance after addressing presentation and evaluation.
