- Addresses recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries, extending Mohan et al. (2013).
- Main contribution: provides more general graphical conditions for recovery.
- Shows causal queries can be recoverable even when underlying probabilistic relations are not.
- Applies theoretical results to attrition, characterizing recovery of causal effects under attrition-related missingness.
- Well-structured paper that builds logically on Mohan et al. and clearly motivates contributions.
- Theoretical results are presented with helpful examples that aid understanding.
- Extends sequential factorization and introduces new non-recoverability criteria.
- Application to attrition increases practical relevance.
- Related work section is comprehensive and appropriately cites prior literature.
- Key strength: non-trivial demonstration that causal recoverability can hold independently of probabilistic recoverability.
- Theorems and corollaries are clearly stated; proofs are placed in the appendix to preserve readability.
- Examples, especially attrition cases, effectively illustrate the theoretical findings.
- Extends the missingness-graph framework, offering tools to identify recoverable queries and diagnose non-recoverability.
- Some concepts (e.g., “inducing path”) are defined with insufficient context and need brief in-text explanations.
- Discussion of Theorem 5 in Section 7.1 is abrupt and not well connected to prior causal recoverability arguments.
- Lacks empirical evaluation or simulations to demonstrate practical applicability of the derived conditions.
- Would benefit from an illustrative example comparing new conditions to Mohan et al. (2013) beyond the attrition case.
- Frequent citation of theorems and corollaries disrupts narrative flow; occasional paraphrasing is recommended.
- Strong claims about limitations of existing work (e.g., Garcia 2013) require more detailed justification.
- Overall: significant, novel, and well-supported theoretical contribution with practical relevance; recommend acceptance pending improvements in clarity, justification of strong claims, and empirical validation.
