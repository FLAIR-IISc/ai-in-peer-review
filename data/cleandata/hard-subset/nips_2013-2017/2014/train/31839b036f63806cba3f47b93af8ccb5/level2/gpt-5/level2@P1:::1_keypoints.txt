- Paper studies recoverability of probabilistic (P(V), P(Y|X)) and causal (P(y|do(x))) queries from data with missingness using m-graphs.  
- Extends Mohan et al. (2013) in three ways: generalized sequential factorization (Thm.1), new recoverability without admissible sequences (Thm.2), and graphical non-recoverability criteria (Thm.3).  
- Theorem 1: allows missingness indicators R within admissible factorizations, enabling recovery when missingness mechanisms interact.  
- Theorem 2: gives recoverability even without admissible sequences via partitioning R and Markov blankets defined through collider paths; includes a corollary for no latent V–R common causes.  
- Theorem 3 and Corollary 2: provide graphical conditions that preclude recovery of P(V) and P(Y|X).  
- Theorem 4: shows some causal effects are recoverable even when corresponding probabilistic relations are not, using d*-separation/dormant independences (includes case where Y and Ry are inseparable).  
- Theorems 5–6: for simple attrition (|Vm|=1), provide necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of P(V) and P(y|do(x)).  
- Conceptual advance: highlights that causal estimands can be recoverable despite non-recoverable probabilistic quantities.  
- Generalization: ordered factorization extended to include R unlocks new recoverable cases and stresses minimal conditioning sets.  
- Broader recoverability: Thm.2 and attrition specialization address cases outside previous admissible-sequence frameworks.  
- Non-recoverability criteria: actionable graphical tests complement sufficiency results.  
- Examples: worked examples (including attrition) effectively illustrate recovery and impossibility cases.  
- Builds on prior work: extends Mohan, Pearl, Tian (2013) and connects to causal ID and dormant independences literature.  
- Weakness: lacks a unified, general decision procedure for recoverability in arbitrary semi‑Markovian m-graphs.  
- Weakness: no algorithmic guidance for efficiently finding admissible sequences or enforcing minimality in Thm.1.  
- Weakness: needs procedures for constructing R partitions and Mb(R(i)) efficiently for Thm.2.  
- Weakness: scope of Thm.4 (d*-separation based causal recovery) is limited and its relation to existing identification algorithms could be clarified.  
- Weakness: absence of empirical/simulation results comparing proposed estimands to MI/IPW/ML under MNAR.  
- Weakness: important assumptions (e.g., positivity) and m-graph misspecification diagnostics need fuller discussion and connections to testability results.  
- Minor issues: some incomplete references, and notation could be summarized for readability.  
- Quality: technically sound with explicit graphical conditions; proofs in appendix but brief proof sketches for key theorems would help.  
- Clarity: generally clear but Thm.2 and Thm.4 need more intuition, figures, and a step-by-step recipe for constructing estimands.  
- Originality: novel extensions to ordered factorization, collider-path partition recoverability, non-recoverability criteria, and attrition results.  
- Significance: important for MNAR problems in ML/AI and causal inference; clarifies when graph-blind methods fail and when recovery is possible.  
- For acceptance: substantive theoretical advances, clear practical implications, and complements prior work.  
- Against acceptance: lacks unified algorithmic procedure, complexity analysis, empirical validation, and broader generality for some technical conditions.  
- Key questions for authors: provide an explicit algorithm for searching admissible minimal sequences and complexity; outline efficient procedure for R partitions and Mb(R(i)); integrate do-calculus with missingness machinery beyond |Vm|=1; include simulation comparing estimands to MI/IPW/ML under MNAR; discuss sensitivity to m-graph misspecification and recommend diagnostics.  
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution that would benefit from added algorithmic guidance and a minimal empirical illustration; reviewer leans toward acceptance.
