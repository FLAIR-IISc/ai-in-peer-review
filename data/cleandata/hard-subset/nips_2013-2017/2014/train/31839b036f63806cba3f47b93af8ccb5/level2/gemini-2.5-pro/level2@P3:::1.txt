This paper presents a formal graphical framework for determining whether probabilistic and causal queries are recoverable from datasets with missing values. The work builds upon the "m-graph" representation introduced in Mohan et al. [2013], which explicitly models the mechanisms responsible for missingness. The main contributions include: 1) A generalization of the sequential factorization theorem for recovering joint distributions, accommodating dependencies between missingness mechanisms. 2) A novel recovery algorithm for cases where sequential factorization is not possible, based on a partitioning of the missingness variables. 3) The first formal graphical criteria for establishing the *non-recoverability* of joint and conditional distributions. 4) A significant result demonstrating that causal effects, such as P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic quantities, such as P(y,x), are not. 5) An application of these theoretical results to the problem of attrition in experimental studies, yielding necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects that clarify and correct previous analyses in the literature.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles the fundamental and challenging problem of inference from data under Missing Not At Random (MNAR) conditions. The contributions are both significant and novel. The development of graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) is a particularly important step forward, as it provides a formal tool for deciding when an estimation problem is unsolvable under the given model assumptions. The most impactful result is arguably the decoupling of recoverability for causal and probabilistic queries (Section 6). The insight that P(y|do(x)) can be identified and estimated even when the joint P(y,x,z,...) is non-recoverable is a deep and valuable contribution that elegantly connects the theories of causal identification (do-calculus, d*-separation) with the problem of missing data.

*   **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound and rigorous. The theorems are stated formally, and the claims are well-supported by graphical definitions and illustrative examples. The progression from generalizing existing results to introducing entirely new conditions for both recoverability and non-recoverability demonstrates a complete and mature piece of research. The application to attrition provides a concrete demonstration of the framework's power and utility.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured, moving logically from probabilistic to causal queries and from general theory to a specific application. The use of figures to illustrate the graphical conditions discussed in the theorems is effective and greatly aids in understanding the main results. The abstract and introduction provide a clear summary of the paper's goals and achievements.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is technically dense, which is expected for the topic, but certain areas could be made more accessible to a broader audience without sacrificing rigor. These suggestions are intended to enhance readability and could be considered for a final version.

*   The concepts introduced for Theorem 2, specifically the partitioning `R(part)` and the substantive Markov blanket `Mb(R(i))`, are central to one of the main results but are defined very concisely. A slightly more intuitive explanation of what these sets represent and why their properties lead to recoverability would be beneficial. For example, a sentence explaining that this method works by separately correcting for the selection bias induced by each independent group of missingness mechanisms could improve comprehension.

*   In Section 6, the connection to d*-separation and dormant independence is made abruptly. While correct, readers not intimately familiar with the work of Shpitser and Pearl [2008] may find this transition difficult. Adding a brief clause explaining that d*-separation is the graphical criterion for the identification of conditional causal effects, P(y|do(x),z), could help bridge this conceptual gap.

*   The minimality condition in Definition 2 is noted as being important, but the rationale could be slightly expanded. The example provided is good, but explicitly stating that minimality is required to find a valid factorization path, as non-minimal parent sets may unnecessarily introduce dependencies on missingness mechanisms that block recoverability, would further clarify its purpose.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides several novel and important theoretical results for the challenging MNAR problem.
2.  Introduces the first graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, a significant contribution.
3.  Establishes a deep and impactful connection between causal identifiability and missing data recoverability.
4.  The work is technically solid, well-motivated, and clearly extends the state of the art.
5.  The results have clear practical relevance, as demonstrated by the analysis of attrition.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The presentation is very dense and assumes significant prerequisite knowledge, which may limit its immediate accessibility. However, this is largely a function of the topic's complexity and not a fundamental flaw.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes substantial contributions to the understanding of missing data within a formal graphical framework. It is original, significant, and technically sound. The potential impact on both theoretical research and applied practice in fields dealing with incomplete data is high.
