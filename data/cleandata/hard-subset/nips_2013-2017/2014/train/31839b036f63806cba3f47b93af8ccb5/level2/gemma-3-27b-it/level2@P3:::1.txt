This paper addresses the problem of recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries, extending previous work by Mohan et al. [2013]. The core contribution lies in providing more general graphical conditions for recovery, specifically demonstrating that causal queries can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not. The work applies these results to the problem of attrition, characterizing recovery of causal effects from data with missingness due to attrition.

The paper is well-structured and builds logically upon the foundation laid by Mohan et al. [2013]. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. The presentation of the theoretical results, accompanied by examples, enhances understanding. The extension of sequential factorization and the introduction of new non-recoverability criteria are valuable additions. The application to attrition, a practically relevant scenario, further strengthens the paper’s relevance.  The inclusion of related work is comprehensive, appropriately citing relevant literature.

A key strength is the demonstration that causal queries can be recovered even when probabilistic relations are not. This is a non-trivial result with significant implications for causal inference in the presence of missing data. The theorems and corollaries are clearly stated, and the proofs are relegated to the appendix, maintaining readability. The examples, particularly those involving attrition, aid in illustrating the theoretical findings. The paper builds upon and extends the important framework of missingness graphs, providing tools for both identifying recoverable queries and diagnosing non-recoverability.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification in a few areas. While the mathematical notation is generally clear, the definitions of some concepts (e.g., “inducing path”) are presented without sufficient context and rely heavily on the reader’s familiarity with related work. A brief explanation within the text, even with a citation, would be helpful. The discussion of Theorem 5 in Section 7.1 feels somewhat abrupt and doesn’t fully connect to the preceding arguments about causal recoverability. The paper could also benefit from a more thorough empirical evaluation, even if limited to simulations. Demonstrating the practical applicability of the derived conditions on synthetic data would bolster the claims. Although the authors state they are extending previous work, a brief illustrative example showing how the new conditions differ from those in Mohan et al. [2013] in a concrete scenario beyond the attrition example would be beneficial. The frequent reference to theorems and corollaries within the text, while technically correct, can disrupt the flow of reading; occasionally paraphrasing the core idea instead of always citing the formal result might improve readability.  Finally, some of the claims regarding the limitations of existing work (e.g., Garcia [2013]) are strong and warrant a more detailed justification.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound extension of existing work on missing data and causal inference.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation of complex concepts.
*   Important result demonstrating recoverability of causal queries even when probabilistic relations are not.
*   Practical application to the problem of attrition.
*   Comprehensive related work section.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Some concepts and notation could be better explained for a broader audience.
*   Lack of empirical evaluation to demonstrate practical applicability.
*   Strong claims regarding the limitations of existing work need more justification.
*   The flow is occasionally disrupted by excessive citation of theorems.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of causal inference with missing data. The theoretical results are novel and well-supported, and the application to attrition is relevant and insightful. While there are areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. It is recommended for acceptance, contingent upon addressing the aforementioned comments regarding clarity, justification of claims, and consideration of empirical validation.
