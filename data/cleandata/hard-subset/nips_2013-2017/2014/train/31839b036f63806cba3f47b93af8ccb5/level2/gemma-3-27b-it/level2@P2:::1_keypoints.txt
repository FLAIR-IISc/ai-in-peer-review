- Problem addressed: Recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries using and extending the Missingness Graph (m-graph) framework.

- Main contribution: Provides more general conditions for recoverability, including cases where causal queries P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even if underlying probabilistic relations are not.

- Key technical advance: Shows recoverability when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable, expanding prior recoverability criteria.

- Theoretical extensions: Introduces sequential factorization (Theorem 1) and methods for cases with no admissible sequence (Theorem 2), broadening m-graph applicability.

- Practical application: Applies theoretical results to the attrition problem, demonstrating real-world relevance.

- Theoretical rigor: Offers a series of theorems with proofs (in appendix), giving a solid formal foundation.

- Generalization of prior work: Successfully extends Mohan et al. (2013), addressing previous limitations.

- Novelty of causal results: Novel insight that causal queries can be recovered under weaker conditions than probabilistic queries.

- Clarity and exposition: Generally clear presentation of complex concepts (d*-separation, inducing paths) with helpful examples.

- Limited examples: Needs more diverse and detailed examples to illustrate practical implications and edge cases.

- Notational density: Heavy notation may hinder accessibility; a consolidated notation table or clearer explanations would help.

- Missing computational discussion: Paper focuses on identifiability but omits discussion of computational complexity or feasibility of recovery algorithms.

- Limited comparison to alternatives: Lacks a direct, nuanced comparison to common missing-data techniques (e.g., multiple imputation, IPW) and their assumptions.

- Theorem 3 underdeveloped: Non-recoverability results (Theorem 3) feel less complete and could use stronger discussion or additional conditions.

- Recommendation: Strong theoretical contribution; recommend accept with minor revisions to add examples, discuss computational aspects, and improve comparisons to related methods.
