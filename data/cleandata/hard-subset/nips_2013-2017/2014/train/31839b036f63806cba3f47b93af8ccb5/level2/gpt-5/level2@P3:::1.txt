Summary
The paper studies when probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y, x), P(y|x)) and causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) are recoverable from data with missing entries, given a causal missingness model represented as a Missingness Graph (m-graph). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the work extends recoverability conditions in several directions:

- A generalized sequential factorization (Theorem 1) that admits missingness indicators R within the ordered factorization, enabling recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms interact (e.g., longitudinal settings with edges among R-nodes).
- A new recoverability result without requiring an admissible sequence (Theorem 2), using partitions of R and Markov blanket sets Mb(R(i)) to construct the joint from complete-case and mechanism terms; a corollary (Corollary 1) provides sufficient conditions under absence of latent confounding between R and substantive variables.
- Graphical non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) for joint and conditional distributions, identifying when separability between a variable and its missingness indicator fails (neighbor or collider-path connections).
- New conditions for recovering causal effects even when associated probabilistic relations are not recoverable, including the case where Y is not d-separated from its missingness indicator Ry (Theorem 4, leveraging do-calculus and d*-separation).
- Application to attrition: necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of joint distributions (Theorem 5) and of causal effects (Theorem 6) in “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1), accompanied by constructive estimands.

Relation to prior work is well-situated: MAR/MCAR methods, inverse probability weighting and imputation, causal identification (Shpitser & Pearl 2006), dormant independences (Shpitser & Pearl 2008), and the prior NIPS 2013 m-graph framework.

Strengths
- Novelty: Incorporates R-nodes into ordered factorizations and provides general recoverability conditions beyond the 2013 results, plus explicit non-recoverability criteria. The observation that causal effects can be recoverable despite inseparable Y–Ry paths is valuable and nontrivial.
- Technical depth: Multiple theorems with precise graphical conditions; constructive estimands are given when recoverable. The attrition results supply necessary-and-sufficient criteria for both joint and causal queries in the single-missing-variable setting, clarifying misconceptions in the literature.
- Clarity of contribution: Distinguishes probabilistic vs causal recoverability, and when do-calculus circumvents obstacles that block P(V).
- Potential impact: Provides principled tools for MNAR scenarios where model-blind methods fail, especially in longitudinal and attrition contexts common in practice.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Scope of causal results: Theorem 4 and the attrition theorems focus on |Vm|=1 for the strongest statements. Guidance for multiple partially observed variables where Y and Ry are inseparable is limited.
- Algorithmic consolidation: While theorems are constructive, the paper stops short of presenting a unified, implementable decision procedure that, given an m-graph and a query, returns recoverable/not-recoverable with an estimand. Complexity and search strategies (e.g., for admissible sequences, partitions Rpart, or separating sets Z) are not analyzed.
- Practical validation: No empirical demonstrations or simulations illustrating finite-sample behavior, robustness to misspecification, or comparisons to IPW/MI/ML under MAR. Given the applied importance (e.g., attrition), examples beyond schematic figures would strengthen significance.
- Clarity and notation: The presentation is dense. Some definitions (e.g., semi-Markovian assumptions, positivity conditions) could be emphasized earlier. A few minor notational and reference-format inconsistencies appear.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Claims are carefully framed and supported by formal statements; proofs are in the appendix. Examples effectively illustrate when new results apply (e.g., Example 1, Example 3). The causal recovery despite inseparable Y–Ry is justified via do-calculus and d*-separation. Providing more intuition for Theorem 2 (role of collider paths and partitions) and a step-by-step application of Theorem 4 would improve accessibility.
- Clarity: Generally well-organized, but notation-heavy. Explicit algorithms or flowcharts would help readers apply the results. A consolidated glossary of symbols (Vm, Vo, V*, R, Rpart, Mb(·), Pasub(·)) and a running example would reduce cognitive load.
- Originality: Strong. The integration of R into ordered factorization, explicit non-recoverability criteria, and recoverability of causal effects under inseparability appear to be new extensions beyond NIPS 2013.
- Significance: High for researchers tackling MNAR data and causal inference under attrition. The results could influence both methodological development and practice in fields where missingness is non-ignorable.

Specific suggestions for improvement
Essential
1) Provide a unified decision procedure: a clear algorithm that, given an m-graph and a target query, (a) checks identifiability (for causal queries), (b) attempts admissible sequences (Theorem 1), (c) falls back to Theorem 2/Corollary 1 for joint recovery, (d) applies non-recoverability tests (Theorem 3/Corollary 2), and (e) for causal queries, tests Theorem 4/6 conditions. Include pseudocode and discuss complexity.
2) Clarify assumptions and scope: State upfront the semi-Markovian setting, whether R may have edges among themselves (allowed) but not into V or U, positivity over complete cases, and the role of latent confounding between R and V in Corollary 1. Explicitly delineate which results tolerate latent confounding via bi-directed edges.
3) Expand the causal example: For the inseparable Y–Ry case (Figure 3), work through the application of Rules 2 and 3 of do-calculus and the d*-separation conditions, showing how the estimand avoids non-recoverable factors.

Optional
- Provide heuristics for constructing admissible sequences and detecting R partitions and Mb(R(i)) efficiently; discuss worst-case complexity.
- Add simulated studies for the attrition setting, comparing the proposed estimands to IPW, MI, and complete-case analysis under MNAR, with sensitivity to misspecification of the m-graph (connecting to Mohan & Pearl 2014 tests).
- Improve presentation: unify notation, fix minor typos in references, and include a compact checklist/flowchart guiding practitioners through the recoverability steps.
- Discuss prospects for extending Theorem 4 beyond |Vm|=1 or characterize when multiple missing variables still admit causal recovery despite inseparability.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Substantive theoretical advances beyond prior NIPS work.
- Clear, constructive estimands for important classes (including attrition).
- New non-recoverability criteria and insight into causal vs probabilistic recoverability.

Cons:
- Lack of a unified, implementable algorithm and empirical illustrations.
- Strongest causal results rely on single partially observed variable; broader multi-missing settings remain open.
- Dense exposition may limit accessibility.

Overall, the work offers meaningful and original contributions to causal inference with MNAR data, with a clear path to strengthening through an algorithmic consolidation and illustrative validation.
