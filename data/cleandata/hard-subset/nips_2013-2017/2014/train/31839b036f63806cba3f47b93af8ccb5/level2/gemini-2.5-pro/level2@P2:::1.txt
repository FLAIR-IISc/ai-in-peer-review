This paper presents a graphical framework for determining whether probabilistic and causal queries can be recovered from data with missing values. Building on the "missingness graph" (m-graph) formalism from their prior work (Mohan et al., 2013), the authors derive several new theoretical results. The main contributions include: (1) A generalized sequential factorization theorem that allows for dependencies between missingness mechanisms, making it applicable to more complex settings like longitudinal studies. (2) A new theorem for recovering joint distributions in cases where factorization fails, based on partitioning the missingness mechanisms. (3) Novel graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, which are crucial for practitioners to know when their modeling assumptions are insufficient. (4) The key insight that causal queries `P(y|do(x))` may be recoverable even when their probabilistic counterparts `P(y,x)` are not. (5) The application of these results to the problem of attrition, leading to necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects in simple attrition models.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles the fundamental and ubiquitous problem of missing data. By providing a principled, model-based approach to determine recoverability for data that is Missing Not At Random (MNAR), it moves significantly beyond the standard assumptions of MAR and MCAR that dominate much of the literature. The results, particularly the non-recoverability criteria and the findings on causal queries, have important implications for any researcher conducting empirical studies.

2.  **Originality:** The contributions are novel and insightful. While the paper is an extension of previous work, the new results are substantial. The development of graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) is a particularly valuable and original contribution, as it provides a formal tool for identifying unsolvable problems. The distinction drawn between the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries (Section 6) is a deep and important insight that leverages the machinery of do-calculus in the context of missing data in a novel way.

3.  **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of research. The theorems are stated clearly, and the claims are supported by illustrative examples and proofs in the appendix. The arguments are logically coherent, and the authors are careful to connect their theoretical results to practical problems like attrition, demonstrating a solid grasp of both the theory and its application. The correction offered to a result in the existing literature (Garcia [2013]) further underscores the paper's technical rigor.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of New Concepts:** The concepts introduced for Theorem 2 in Section 4 are quite dense and could benefit from further explanation. The definitions of "collider path," the partitioning `R(part)`, and the Markov blanket `Mb(R(i))` are presented very concisely. The paper would be significantly clearer if the authors included a step-by-step walkthrough of how these sets are constructed for the example in Figure 2(b). A brief intuitive explanation for *why* the condition `R(i) ∩ R_Mb(R(i)) = ∅` is sufficient for recoverability would also strengthen the reader's understanding.

2.  **Assumed Background:** The paper assumes a high level of familiarity with graphical models and the do-calculus (e.g., d*-separation, inducing paths). While this is largely appropriate for the NIPS audience, a slightly more self-contained presentation in Section 6, perhaps briefly reminding the reader of the key rules of do-calculus being invoked in Example 3, could broaden the paper's accessibility and impact.

3.  **From Identification to Estimation:** The paper focuses exclusively on recoverability, which is analogous to identifiability in causal inference. It provides formulas for target queries in terms of the observed data distribution. A minor weakness is the lack of any discussion on the practicalities of estimation from finite samples. While a full analysis is beyond the scope of this work, a short paragraph in the conclusion discussing the path from these theoretical estimands to practical estimators (e.g., plugin estimators) and potential challenges (e.g., efficiency, curse of dimensionality) would be a valuable addition for practitioners seeking to apply these methods.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes significant theoretical contributions to the important problem of inference from missing data. The results are novel, technically sound, and provide the field with new tools for both establishing recoverability and, just as importantly, proving its impossibility under a given set of assumptions. The weaknesses are minor and related to clarity and scope, and they can be addressed with modest revisions. The paper is an excellent fit for NIPS and represents a clear advance in our understanding of missing data mechanisms.
