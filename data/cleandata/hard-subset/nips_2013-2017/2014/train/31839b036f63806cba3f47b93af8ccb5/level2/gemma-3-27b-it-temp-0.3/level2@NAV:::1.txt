## Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper extends previous work on recoverability of queries from data with missing entries, building upon the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. The core contribution lies in refining conditions for recovering both probabilistic queries (P(y|x), P(y,x)) and, crucially, causal queries (P(y|do(x))) under more general missingness assumptions. A key finding is that causal effects can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not, a result demonstrated through graphical conditions and applied to the specific problem of attrition.

The paper is technically sound and builds logically from the foundational work of Mohan et al. The extension of the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) to accommodate interactions between missingness mechanisms is a valuable addition. The introduction of Theorem 2, addressing cases where no admissible sequence exists, broadens the applicability of the framework. The novelty of demonstrating recoverability of causal queries even when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable (Theorem 4) is particularly significant, challenging conventional wisdom. The application to attrition (Section 7) provides a concrete example of the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in presentation. The numerous theorems and definitions, while technically correct, are densely packed and can be difficult to follow without careful study. The notation is heavy, and a more intuitive explanation of concepts like “d*-separation” and “inducing path” would be beneficial. While the authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., the focus on semi-Markovian models), a more thorough discussion of potential weaknesses and future directions would strengthen the work. For instance, the computational complexity of verifying the proposed graphical conditions is not addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical results regarding recoverability of causal queries.
*   Extension of existing framework to handle more complex missingness scenarios.
*   Clear connection to a practical problem (attrition).
*   Technically sound and builds on established literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Dense and challenging presentation.
*   Heavy notation and lack of intuitive explanations.
*   Limited discussion of limitations and future work.

**Overall:**

Despite the clarity issues, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of missing data analysis. The theoretical results are novel and potentially impactful. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, specifically focusing on improving the clarity of the presentation and expanding the discussion of limitations and potential future research directions. The authors should consider adding illustrative examples to accompany the more complex theorems and definitions.
