- Paper studies recoverability of probabilistic (P(y,x), P(y|x)) and causal (P(y|do(x))) queries from data with missingness using m-graphs, extending Mohan et al. (2013).
- Theorem 1: generalizes sequential/ordered factorization to allow missingness indicators R inside the factorization, enabling recovery when R-nodes interact.
- Theorem 2: provides recoverability without requiring an admissible sequence by partitioning R and using Markov blankets Mb(R(i)) to build the joint from complete-case and mechanism terms.
- Corollary 1: gives sufficient joint-recovery conditions when there is no latent confounding between R and substantive variables.
- Theorem 3 and Corollary 2: present graphical non-recoverability criteria, identifying when variable–missingness separability fails (neighbors or collider-paths).
- Theorem 4: gives conditions for recovering causal effects even when associated probabilistic relations are not recoverable, using do-calculus and d*-separation.
- Theorems 5–6: supply necessary-and-sufficient recoverability conditions and constructive estimands for joint distributions and causal effects in simple attrition (single missing variable).
- Relation to prior work: situates contributions relative to MAR/MCAR methods, IPW/MI/ML, Shpitser & Pearl identification, dormant independences, and NIPS 2013 m-graphs.
- Novelty: integrating R-nodes into ordered factorization and explicit non-recoverability criteria represent clear advances beyond prior results.
- Technical depth: multiple formal theorems with constructive estimands and proof appendices; attrition results clarify literature misconceptions.
- Clarity of contribution: effectively distinguishes probabilistic vs causal recoverability and highlights do-calculus advantages.
- Potential impact: offers principled tools for MNAR scenarios common in longitudinal and attrition studies.
- Limitation: strongest causal results focus on |Vm|=1, giving limited guidance for multiple partially observed variables with inseparable Y–Ry.
- Limitation: lacks a unified, implementable decision procedure that, given an m-graph and query, returns recoverable/not with an estimand and complexity analysis.
- Limitation: no empirical simulations or finite-sample studies to assess robustness, performance, or comparison to IPW/MI/ML under MAR/MNAR.
- Presentation issues: dense, notation-heavy exposition; some definitions and assumptions (semi-Markovian, positivity) should be stated earlier and notation unified.
- Essential suggestion 1: provide a unified decision algorithm/pseudocode that checks identifiability, finds admissible sequences or R partitions, applies non-recoverability tests, and reports estimands and complexity.
- Essential suggestion 2: clarify assumptions and scope upfront (semi-Markovian setting, allowed edges among R, positivity, role of latent confounding).
- Essential suggestion 3: expand the causal inseparable Y–Ry example with step-by-step do-calculus/d*-separation application to show how estimand avoids non-recoverable factors.
- Optional suggestions: give heuristics for constructing admissible sequences and R partitions, add simulations (e.g., attrition) comparing methods, and include a checklist/flowchart and glossary.
- Pros for acceptance: substantive theoretical advances, constructive estimands for important classes, and new insights separating causal vs probabilistic recoverability.
- Cons for acceptance: missing unified algorithm and empirical validation, strongest results limited to single missing variable, and dense presentation may hinder accessibility.
- Overall: meaningful, original contributions to causal inference with MNAR data with clear potential impact, but would be strengthened by algorithmic consolidation and empirical illustration.
