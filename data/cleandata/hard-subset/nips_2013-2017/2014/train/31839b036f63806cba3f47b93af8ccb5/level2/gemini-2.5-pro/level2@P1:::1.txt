This paper presents a formal graphical framework for determining whether probabilistic and causal queries can be recovered from data with missing values. The work builds directly on the "missingness graph" (m-graph) framework introduced in Mohan et al. [2013], which explicitly models the mechanisms responsible for missing data. The authors extend this prior work in several significant directions. First, they generalize a sequential factorization method for recovering joint distributions to handle cases where missingness mechanisms can influence each other. Second, for problems where this factorization method fails, they introduce a novel theorem for recovering the joint distribution based on graphical properties involving collider paths and Markov blankets of the missingness indicators. Third, and perhaps most importantly, the paper introduces graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, allowing a researcher to determine when a query is impossible to estimate given the model assumptions. Finally, the paper investigates the recoverability of causal queries, P(y|do(x)), and demonstrates the crucial and surprising result that such queries can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries (like P(y,x)) are not. These general results are then applied to the specific problem of attrition in experimental studies, yielding necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery that clarify and correct prior work in that area.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper makes several strong and original contributions to the study of missing data, a problem of broad importance to the machine learning community. The development of graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) is a particularly novel and useful contribution, as it provides a formal tool to identify problems that are fundamentally unsolvable under the given assumptions. The paper's most significant insight is arguably the decoupling of recoverability for causal and probabilistic queries. The demonstration that P(y|do(x)) can be estimated even when P(y) is not recoverable is a deep and important result at the intersection of causal inference and missing data analysis.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The proposed theorems are clear extensions of existing graphical model theory, properly leveraging concepts from d-separation, do-calculus, and d*-separation. The formal definitions and proofs (provided in the appendix) establish a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed recovery conditions and algorithms. The application to attrition is a compelling use case that demonstrates the practical value of the theoretical framework.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem and clearly stating their contributions in relation to prior work. The use of small graphical examples throughout the paper (e.g., Figures 2, 3, and 4) is very effective in illustrating the key concepts and the application of the main theorems.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the absence of any experiments on either synthetic or real-world data is a notable weakness. Simulation studies would be invaluable for demonstrating the practical behavior of the proposed recovery procedures. For example, showing that the proposed estimators are consistent (i.e., converge to the true value with increasing sample size) on data generated from a known m-graph would greatly strengthen the paper's claims. Furthermore, an experimental comparison against standard methods (e.g., multiple imputation, expectation-maximization) under conditions where their assumptions (e.g., MAR) are violated but the m-graph is correctly specified would powerfully illustrate the advantages of this model-based approach.

2.  **Practicality of Model Specification:** The entire framework relies on the assumption that one can specify a correct m-graph that captures the true data-generating and missingness processes. In practice, specifying such a model can be extremely challenging. While the authors cite related work on testing m-graphs, a brief discussion in the main paper on the challenges of model specification and the potential sensitivity of the results to model misspecification would be welcome.

3.  **Presentation of Complex Concepts:** Some of the technical conditions, particularly those for Theorem 2, are quite involved. The definitions of "collider path", "R(part)", and "Mb(R(i))" are given *after* the theorem statement. For clarity, it would be better to introduce and define these concepts before presenting the theorem that relies on them.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Pros:**
*   Addresses the fundamental and ubiquitous problem of missing data.
*   Provides novel and highly useful graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*.
*   Presents a significant and counter-intuitive result showing that causal queries can be recoverable even when their probabilistic counterparts are not.
*   The work is theoretically rigorous and builds upon a solid foundation of graphical models and causal inference.
*   The application to attrition provides a clear and impactful demonstration of the framework's utility.

**Cons:**
*   Complete lack of experimental validation on synthetic or real data.
*   The practical challenge of specifying a correct m-graph is not discussed in detail.
*   Minor clarity issues in the presentation of some complex definitions.

In conclusion, this is a strong theoretical paper that makes several novel and significant contributions to our understanding of inference from missing data. The results on non-recoverability and the special status of causal queries are particularly noteworthy. The primary weakness is the lack of empirical evaluation. However, the theoretical strength and importance of the contributions are high enough to warrant acceptance. I would strongly encourage the authors to include simulation studies in the final version of the paper to further bolster their claims and demonstrate the practical utility of their methods.
