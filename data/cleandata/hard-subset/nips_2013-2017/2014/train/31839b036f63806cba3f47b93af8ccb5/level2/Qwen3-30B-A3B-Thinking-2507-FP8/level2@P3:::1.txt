

The paper extends Mohan et al. (2013) by establishing graphical conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data, with a notable contribution being the demonstration that causal effects P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separated (Theorem 4). The work builds upon missingness graphs (m-graphs) to formalize recoverability conditions, addressing a gap where prior work (e.g., Mohan et al. 2013) required separability for causal recovery. Key advances include: (1) a generalized sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) applicable to missingness mechanisms interacting with each other; (2) non-recoverability criteria for joint/conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2); and (3) corrected necessary/sufficient conditions for attrition problems (Theorem 6), rectifying Garcia (2013).

Strengths are substantial. The theoretical framework is technically sound, with rigorous proofs in the appendix and clear connections to prior work (e.g., clarifying limitations of MAR/MCAR assumptions in Section 6). The counterintuitive result in Example 3—that P(y|do(z)) is recoverable despite non-separable Y-Ry (Figure 3)—is well-justified via do-calculus and d*-separation, resolving a key ambiguity in causal inference under missingness. The attrition application (Section 7) provides practical value by correcting Garcia’s (2013) error regarding necessary conditions for non-recoverability (e.g., Figure 4(a) shows X not being an ancestor of Ry yet non-recoverability persists due to collider paths). The related work section adequately contextualizes contributions against missing data literature (e.g., MAR/ML methods, causal graphs for attrition).

Weaknesses are minor but important. The minimality requirement in Definition 2 (for general ordered factorizations) lacks intuitive justification beyond Example 1; a brief discussion of why non-minimal factorizations fail would strengthen the argument. While Theorem 4 is compelling, its reliance on inducing paths (Definition 5) could be more clearly linked to existing causal graph concepts (e.g., Verma & Pearl 1991) to aid accessibility. The absence of empirical validation (e.g., a synthetic experiment illustrating Theorem 4) is a missed opportunity to demonstrate practical impact, though purely theoretical work is acceptable at NIPS if rigorously grounded—a point to acknowledge. Section 7.2’s focus on |Vm|=1 (single partially observed variable) is reasonable for foundational work but should explicitly note that generalizing to multiple missing variables (e.g., Vm > 1) remains an open direction.

The paper excels in originality (Section 3–6) and significance (Section 7). It clearly differentiates from prior NIPS work (e.g., Mohan et al. 2013, Garcia 2013) by resolving a previously unaddressed gap: causal queries circumventing probabilistic recoverability constraints. The graphical criteria are novel and directly applicable to real-world problems like attrition. Significance is reinforced by the correction of Garcia’s (2013) condition, which has influenced subsequent attrition analyses. The writing is clear, with well-structured theorems and examples; the appendix proofs support reproducibility.

Essential revisions: Clarify the role of minimality in Definition 2 (e.g., add a sentence explaining why non-minimal factorizations invalidate Theorem 1). Explicitly state the |Vm|=1 limitation in Theorems 5–6. Optional but recommended: Include a brief synthetic example (e.g., generating data from Figure 3 to demonstrate P(y|do(z)) recovery) to bridge theory and practice.

The work is a strong contribution to the field. It advances the theoretical foundation for causal inference under missing data, with implications for longitudinal studies (attrition) and graphical model-based missing data analysis. The corrections to prior literature (e.g., Garcia 2013) and novel conditions for causal recovery demonstrate significant, high-impact progress. The paper meets all NIPS criteria: technically sound (Quality), clearly articulated (Clarity), substantially novel (Originality), and addressing a critical problem with demonstrable utility (Significance).
