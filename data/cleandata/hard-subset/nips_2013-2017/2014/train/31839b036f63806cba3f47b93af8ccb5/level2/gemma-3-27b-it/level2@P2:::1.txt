This paper addresses the challenging problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries, building upon the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. The authors extend existing results, providing more general conditions for recoverability, particularly focusing on scenarios where causal queries (P(y|do(x))) can be recovered even when underlying probabilistic relations are not. A key contribution lies in showing recoverability even when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable, and applying these findings to the specific problem of attrition.

The paper is well-structured and builds logically from defining the problem and relevant background (m-graphs, recoverability) to presenting progressively more general theorems and addressing specific cases like attrition. The extensions to sequential factorization (Theorem 1) and handling cases where no admissible sequence exists (Theorem 2) are significant contributions, broadening the applicability of the m-graph framework. The focus on causal query recoverability, and especially the condition allowing recovery even with inseparable Y and Ry, is a notable advance, providing valuable theoretical insights. The treatment of attrition as a specific application is a helpful demonstration of the practical relevance of the work.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a series of theorems with clear conditions for recoverability, backed by proofs in the appendix. This rigorous approach provides a solid foundation for understanding the limitations and possibilities of recovering information from incomplete data.
* **Generalization of Existing Work:** The authors successfully extend the work of Mohan et al. [2013], addressing limitations and broadening the scope of applicability.
* **Novelty of Causal Query Results:**  The finding that causal queries can be recoverable even when underlying probabilistic relationships are not is a significant and novel contribution, potentially having a major impact on causal inference in the presence of missing data.
* **Clear Presentation of Complex Concepts:** Despite the technical nature of the material, the authors do a reasonably good job of explaining complex concepts like d*-separation and inducing paths. The use of examples (though more could be included) aids understanding.
* **Practical Relevance:** Applying the theoretical results to the problem of attrition demonstrates the practical utility of the framework.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Examples:** While the examples provided help illustrate some concepts, the paper could benefit from more diverse and detailed examples.  This would help readers grasp the practical implications of the theorems and potentially identify edge cases. The attrition example is good, but more would be beneficial.
* **Notationally Dense:** The paper uses a significant amount of notation, which can be daunting for readers unfamiliar with the field.  While necessary for precision, it could be made more accessible with more careful explanation and perhaps a consolidated notation table.
* **Discussion of Computational Complexity:** The paper focuses on *whether* recovery is possible, but doesn't address the *computational complexity* of implementing the recovery algorithms.  Even if a query is theoretically recoverable, it might be computationally intractable in practice.
* **Comparison to Alternative Approaches:** While the Related Work section is present, the paper could benefit from a more direct and nuanced comparison to other missing data imputation or weighting techniques (e.g., multiple imputation, inverse probability weighting).  How do the conditions for recoverability in this framework compare to the assumptions required for these other methods?
* **Theorem 3 feels incomplete:** While valuable, Theorem 3 regarding non-recoverability feels less fleshed out than the recoverability theorems. It's a good starting point, but could be strengthened with more detailed discussion and potentially additional conditions.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in the field of missing data analysis, particularly regarding causal inference.
* The rigorous mathematical framework and well-defined conditions for recoverability are valuable contributions.
* The extension of existing work and the novelty of the causal query results justify publication in a top-tier conference like NIPS.
* The problem addressed is important and has broad implications for a variety of applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The notationally dense presentation and limited number of examples may make the paper difficult for some readers to fully grasp.
* The lack of discussion regarding computational complexity is a limitation.
* A more comprehensive comparison to existing missing data techniques would strengthen the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions to the area of missing data analysis and causal inference. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the novelty of the results justifies acceptance. I recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly adding more detailed examples and discussing the computational aspects of the proposed framework. A more thorough comparison to related approaches would also enhance the paper's impact.
