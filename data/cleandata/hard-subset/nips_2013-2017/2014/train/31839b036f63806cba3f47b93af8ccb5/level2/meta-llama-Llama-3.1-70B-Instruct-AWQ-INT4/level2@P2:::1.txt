This paper presents a comprehensive framework for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries, using graphical models to represent the missingness process. The authors extend previous work by Mohan et al. [2013] and provide more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries and causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)). The paper also applies these results to problems of attrition, where missingness is a severe obstacle to sound inferences.

The main contributions of the paper are: (1) the extension of the sequential factorization theorem to handle cases where the missingness mechanisms interact with each other, (2) the introduction of new graphical criteria that preclude recoverability of joint and conditional distributions, and (3) the derivation of necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects in problems with attrition.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow of ideas and a good balance between theoretical results and practical applications. The authors provide several examples to illustrate the use of their results, which helps to clarify the concepts and make the paper more accessible to non-experts.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to bridge the gap between theoretical results and practical applications. The authors demonstrate the usefulness of their results in recovering causal effects in problems with attrition, which is a common issue in many fields. The paper also highlights the importance of graphical models in portraying the missingness process and deciding recoverability given a dataset afflicted with missingness.

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the conditions under which probabilistic and causal queries can be recovered. The proofs of the theoretical results are provided in the appendix, which is a good practice.

However, there are a few minor issues that could be improved. Some of the definitions and notations could be clarified or rephrased for better understanding. For example, the definition of "general ordered factorization" could be rephrased to make it clearer what is meant by "minimal set". Additionally, some of the examples could be expanded or rephrased to make them more accessible to non-experts.

Overall, this is a well-written and comprehensive paper that makes significant contributions to the field of graphical models and missing data analysis. The authors have done a great job in extending previous work and providing new insights into the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries.
