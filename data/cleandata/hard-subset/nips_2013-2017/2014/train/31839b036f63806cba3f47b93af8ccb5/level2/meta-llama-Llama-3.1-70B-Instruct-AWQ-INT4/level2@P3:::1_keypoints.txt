- Summary: Decides when causal or probabilistic queries are recoverable from data with missing entries using Missingness Graphs (m-graphs), extending Mohan et al. with more general recovery conditions and applications to attrition.
- Relation to previous work: Builds on Mohan et al. (2013) and Pearl & Mohan, and situates among deletion, maximum likelihood, and imputation approaches to missing data.
- Strength — Novel Contribution: Provides significant extensions to prior recoverability results for probabilistic and causal queries.
- Strength — Clear Exposition: Concepts and techniques are explained clearly and accessibly to readers familiar with the field.
- Strength — Applicability: Demonstrates practical relevance, e.g., to attrition in longitudinal studies.
- Weakness — Technical Complexity: Assumes strong background in graphical models, causal inference, and probability, limiting accessibility.
- Weakness — Limited Empirical Evaluation: Lacks extensive experiments to validate the proposed methods in practice.
- Weakness — Strong Assumptions: Relies on assumptions about the missingness process and data that may not hold empirically.
- Quality: Technically rigorous, well-organized, and sound derivations throughout.
- Clarity: Well-written with consistent notation and helpful figures for illustration.
- Originality: Novel contributions to graphical models and causal inference for missing-data recovery.
- Significance: Results have substantial potential impact across social sciences, epidemiology, and machine learning.
- Pro Acceptance: Novel, significant, and technically rigorous work that advances recoverability theory.
- Con Acceptance: Limited audience due to technical demands, insufficient empirical validation, and reliance on possibly unrealistic assumptions.
- Recommendations: Add a more extensive empirical evaluation, more detailed discussion of assumptions/limitations, and additional illustrative examples across domains.
