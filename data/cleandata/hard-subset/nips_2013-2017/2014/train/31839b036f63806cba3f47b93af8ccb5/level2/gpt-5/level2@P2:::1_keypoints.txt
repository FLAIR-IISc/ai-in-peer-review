- Paper studies recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missingness using m-graphs, extending Mohan et al. (2013).
- Main contributions: (i) generalized sequential factorization allowing R-nodes in admissible factorizations for interacting missingness.
- Main contributions: (ii) a new recovery theorem for joint distributions via R partitioning and conditioning on Markov blankets along collider paths.
- Main contributions: (iii) graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions.
- Main contributions: (iv) sufficient conditions showing some causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when P(y,x) and P(y|x) are not, including inseparable Y and Ry cases.
- Provides necessary and sufficient conditions for joint and causal recoverability under simple attrition (|Vm|=1), correcting prior claims.
- Builds directly on Mohan et al. (2013) and leverages do-calculus and dormant independences; contrasts with MAR/MCAR and IPW/MI approaches.
- Strength: Conceptual advance by including R-nodes in admissible factorizations, broadening applicability to longitudinal/interactive settings.
- Strength: New non-recoverability criteria give useful “stop” conditions absent in much of the literature.
- Strength: Clear demonstration that causal effects can be recoverable when probabilistic quantities are not, using do-calculus and d*-separation.
- Strength: Attrition results are concise, intuitive, practically useful, and correct prior literature errors.
- Strength: Theorems give intuitive, graph-based path criteria that align with practitioner reasoning.
- Weakness: No unified, implementable decision procedure—unclear how to systematically search factorizations including R-nodes or compute R partitions/Markov blankets at scale.
- Weakness: Scope and assumptions need clarification, especially regarding semi-Markovian models and allowed/disallowed latent confounding between V and R.
- Weakness: Theorem 4 is technically heavy; needs more explicit, checkable graphical criteria or corollaries for common motifs; missing Shpitser & Pearl (2008) citation.
- Weakness: No empirical or simulation validation to demonstrate finite-sample behavior, robustness, or sensitivity to m-graph misspecification.
- Weakness: Notation and presentation issues (inconsistent symbols, dense definitions, reference glitches); figures and proof sketches should be clearer.
- Quality: Technical soundness appears high and aligns with do-calculus and ADMG reasoning, but brief proof sketches would aid intuition.
- Clarity: Good high-level organization but dense definitions; a consolidated checklist/flowchart would improve readability.
- Originality: Novel extensions beyond Mohan et al. (2013), notably inclusion of R in factorizations, non-recoverability criteria, and causal-under-inseparability results.
- Significance: High potential impact for MNAR and causal inference with attrition; offers principled guidance where MAR-based methods can fail.
- Specific suggestion: Provide an explicit decision procedure/pseudocode with termination and complexity notes for query recovery.
- Specific suggestion: Add more examples of Theorems 2 and 4, including interacting R and latent confounding cases.
- Specific suggestion: Include small-scale simulations to illustrate recovery vs non-recovery and sensitivity to graph misspecification.
- Specific suggestion: Clean up notation, fix references (include Shpitser & Pearl 2008), and clarify positivity and compatibility assumptions.
- Arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical contributions, interpretable graphical conditions, and useful corrections for attrition literature.
- Arguments against acceptance: Lack of unified, implementable algorithm, limited accessibility of Theorem 4, and presentation/validation shortcomings.
- Overall: Solid, substantive theoretical work with a lean toward acceptance pending improvements to exposition, an implementable procedure, and minimal empirical validation.
