

This paper extends Mohan et al. (2013) by developing graphical conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data using missingness graphs (m-graphs). The key contributions are: (1) general conditions for recovering joint/conditional distributions beyond sequential factorization, (2) a novel insight that causal queries P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable (e.g., Example 3), and (3) necessary/sufficient conditions for causal effect recovery under attrition. The work builds on foundational missing data literature (Rubin, 1976; Pearl, 2009) and corrects misconceptions in Garcia (2013) regarding attrition.

The paper excels in technical quality and originality. Theorems 1–6 are rigorously proven, with Example 3 providing compelling evidence for causal recoverability despite probabilistic non-recoverability. The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) are particularly valuable for practitioners. The clarity is excellent, with well-structured theorems, intuitive examples (Figures 2–4), and precise definitions. The application to attrition (Section 7) demonstrates practical significance for longitudinal studies.

However, the paper lacks empirical validation. While theoretical conditions are sound, the absence of real-data experiments or comparisons to existing methods (e.g., multiple imputation) limits assessment of practical utility. The scope is also constrained—Theorem 4 requires |Vm|=1, and Section 4’s "generalization" lacks concrete implementation guidance. The discussion of limitations (e.g., collider path restrictions) is adequate but could better address computational feasibility.

The significance is high: the work resolves a critical gap in causal inference under missingness, directly addressing a pervasive challenge in experimental science. The novelty is clear—causal recoverability without Y-Ry separability is a substantial advance over prior work. The related work (Section 8) is comprehensive, though it misses recent causal inference methods for missing data (e.g., Van der Laan & Robins, 2003).

Overall, this is a technically strong, original contribution with clear practical implications. To strengthen it, the authors should add empirical validation on synthetic or real datasets and discuss computational aspects. The paper is well-suited for NIPS, advancing the state of the art in causal inference with missing data. I recommend acceptance.
