Summary
This paper studies when probabilistic queries (joint and conditional distributions) and causal queries (interventional distributions) are recoverable from data with missing values, given a graphical model of the missingness process (m-graph). It extends Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) along three axes: (i) a generalized sequential factorization (Theorem 1) that permits missingness indicators R to appear in admissible factorizations, enabling recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) a new recoverability result (Theorem 2) that applies even when no admissible sequence exists, based on a partitioning of R and Markov blankets defined via collider paths, plus a corollary for models without latent common causes of V and R; (iii) graphical conditions for non-recoverability (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) that preclude recovery of P(V) and P(Y|X). The paper then shows that some causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when corresponding probabilistic relations are not, including the case where Y is inseparable from its missingness mechanism Ry (Example 3), formalized via d*-separation/dormant independences (Theorem 4). Finally, it applies these ideas to “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1), giving necessary and sufficient criteria for recoverability of P(V) (Theorem 5) and P(y|do(x)) (Theorem 6) and clarifying when conventional beliefs can fail.

Relation to prior work
The work builds squarely on Mohan, Pearl, and Tian (NIPS 2013) by broadening the ordered factorization approach and providing complementary non-recoverability criteria. It also connects to causal identification (Shpitser & Pearl 2006) and dormant independences (Shpitser & Pearl 2008), and to classical missing data frameworks (Rubin, ML/EM, IPW/MI). The focus on graphical diagnosis of MNAR missingness and explicit modeling with m-graphs fits well with the growing literature on causal graphs for selection/attrition.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: Clear demonstration that causal effects can be recoverable even when key probabilistic quantities are not—an important and often overlooked distinction.
- Generalization of ordered factorization: Allowing R variables in admissible sequences is a natural and useful extension that unlocks new recoverable cases and clarifies the necessity of minimal conditioning sets.
- Broader recoverability result: Theorem 2 covers cases without admissible sequences via collider-path structure and partitions of R; the attrition specialization (Theorems 5–6) provides crisp necessary/sufficient conditions for a common practical scenario.
- Non-recoverability criteria: Theorem 3 and Corollary 2 give actionable graphical tests to detect impossibility, complementing sufficiency results.
- Clarity of examples: The worked examples (including attrition) concretely illustrate when and how recovery is achieved.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Algorithmic completeness: The paper provides sufficient and necessary conditions in specific classes but stops short of a unified, complete decision procedure for general semi-Markovian m-graphs (beyond |Vm|=1). A high-level algorithm (or pseudocode) that sequences Theorem 1, Theorem 2, non-recoverability tests, and causal recovery via do-calculus/d*-separation would strengthen practical applicability.
- Search procedures and complexity: For Theorem 1, guidance on how to find admissible sequences efficiently (beyond brute-force order search) and how to enforce “minimality” in Definition 2 would be valuable. Similarly, an efficient method for constructing R partitions and Mb(R(i)) in Theorem 2 could be outlined.
- Scope of Theorem 4: The recoverability condition for P(y|do(x)) when Y and Ry are connected is sufficient (and restricted to |Vm|=1 in the statement), but its generality and relation to existing identification algorithms could be discussed more fully. Are there broader classes where d*-separation-based recovery can be automated?
- Empirical illustration: While the paper is theoretical, a small synthetic study showing finite-sample behavior of the proposed estimands, compared to MAR-based ML/MI or IPW under MNAR, would help readers assess robustness and practical benefits.
- Assumptions and diagnostics: The positivity assumption (strictly positive over complete cases) is crucial; the text notes possible extensions—these could be elaborated with explicit conditions. Given the importance of correct m-graph specification, a short section connecting to available testability results (Mohan & Pearl 2014) with guidance on practical diagnostics would aid adoption.
- Notational and editorial issues: A few references appear incomplete and some notation (e.g., use of primes and shorthand) could be summarized in a compact table to aid readability.

Quality
The results appear technically sound and well-motivated. Theorems are stated with explicit graphical conditions, and examples illustrate necessity of minimality and the contrast between probabilistic and causal recoverability. Proofs are deferred to the appendix; brief proof sketches for key results (especially Theorems 2, 4, and 6) would help readers.

Clarity
Overall clear, with helpful examples. Some densest sections (Theorems 2 and 4) would benefit from more intuition, a figure illustrating collider paths/partitions, and a step-by-step recipe for constructing estimands after the graphical check passes.

Originality
The paper makes original contributions relative to Mohan et al. (2013): extending ordered factorization to include R, introducing general recoverability via collider-path partitions, offering non-recoverability criteria, and establishing recovery of certain causal effects despite inseparability of Y and Ry. The attrition results provide new necessary/sufficient conditions that refine existing claims.

Significance
Addressing MNAR with explicit graphical structure is important for many ML/AI and causal inference applications (longitudinal studies, recommender systems, surveys). The results should influence both methodology and practice by clarifying when graph-blind methods fail and when principled recovery is possible.

Arguments for acceptance
- Substantive theoretical advances with clear practical implications.
- Complements and extends prior NIPS work; fills gaps (non-recoverability, interacting R mechanisms).
- Clear examples, especially the causal/probabilistic recoverability contrast and attrition case.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of a unified, implementable decision procedure and complexity analysis.
- Limited empirical validation and practical guidance for model specification and diagnostics.
- Some technical conditions (Theorem 4) may feel specialized; broader generality remains open.

Questions for authors
1) Can you provide an explicit algorithm to search for admissible sequences with minimal conditioning sets, and discuss complexity?
2) For Theorem 2, can you outline an efficient procedure to compute R partitions and Mb(R(i)) from the graph?
3) Is there a way to integrate do-calculus-based search with the missingness machinery to automate causal recoverability checks beyond |Vm|=1?
4) Could you include a small simulation comparing your estimands to MI/IPW/ML under MNAR, highlighting bias and variance?
5) How sensitive are your estimands to modest misspecification of the m-graph, and what diagnostics would you recommend?

Overall evaluation
A strong theoretical paper that advances the state of the art on recoverability from MNAR via graphical models, with novel insights for causal effects. With added algorithmic guidance and a minimal empirical illustration, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I lean toward acceptance.
