This paper addresses the problem of recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values, building upon the framework of missingness graphs introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. The authors extend previous results to more general conditions and, importantly, demonstrate that causal queries can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not. A significant contribution lies in showing recoverability even when the variable of interest and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable. The paper concludes with an application to the problem of attrition.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a strong theoretical foundation, building upon existing work in causal inference and missing data. The theorems and definitions are clearly stated, and the proofs appear solid (though detailed verification would require significant effort).
* **Novelty:** The key contribution—showing recoverability of causal queries even when probabilistic relations aren’t recoverable—is a valuable and potentially impactful result. This challenges the common assumption that unrecoverable probabilistic relationships automatically preclude causal inference.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition is a highly relevant and practical problem, and the discussion clarifies scenarios where causal effects can be estimated even with non-random missingness.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The related work section is thorough and well-organized, placing the current work within the broader context of missing data analysis and causal inference.  It acknowledges relevant limitations of existing approaches.
* **Clear Definitions:** The paper provides clear definitions of core concepts like recoverability, missingness graphs, and d*-separation, making the technical aspects accessible.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The theoretical results, while rigorous, are quite complex.  The notation can be dense and challenging to follow, even for readers familiar with causal inference.  More intuitive explanations alongside the formal definitions would be helpful.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper lacks any empirical evaluation. While the focus is on theoretical results, illustrating the practical implications with simulations or real-world examples would significantly strengthen the paper. Demonstrating scenarios where the proposed conditions *actually* lead to recoverable causal effects (and comparing with standard methods) would be highly valuable.
* **Clarity of Examples:** While the examples are intended to clarify the theorems, some (e.g., Example 3) are still quite involved and require careful tracing of the arguments. Providing simpler, more illustrative examples would improve understanding.
* **Tightness of Conditions:** The paper presents sufficient conditions for recoverability.  It would be beneficial to discuss the tightness of these conditions – are they also necessary, or are there scenarios where the conditions are not met, but recoverability is still possible?
* **Potential for Computational Challenges:** The theorems don’t address the computational complexity of actually implementing the recovery algorithms. In practice, identifying admissible sequences or applying Theorem 2 might be computationally intractable for large graphs. Addressing this point, even briefly, would be valuable.



**Detailed Comments & Specific Suggestions:**

* **Definition 4 (d*-separation):** While correctly stated, it would be helpful to briefly explain *why* d*-separation is relevant in the context of recovering causal effects with missing data.
* **Theorem 4:** The conditions in Theorem 4 seem restrictive. Clarifying the practical implications of these restrictions and discussing scenarios where they are likely to hold would be helpful.
* **Section 7 (Attrition):**  The discussion of attrition is somewhat brief.  Expanding on the specific types of attrition scenarios where the proposed techniques are most applicable would increase the practical value of this section.
* **Throughout the paper:** Consider adding visual aids (e.g., diagrams illustrating d-separation, inducing paths, etc.) to help readers grasp the concepts more easily.
* **Equation 5:**  The notation in Equation 5 is somewhat ambiguous. Clarifying the meaning of "Z" (is it a set of variables, a single variable?) would improve readability.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of missing data and causal inference.
* The results have the potential to significantly impact how causal effects are estimated in the presence of non-random missingness.
* The paper is well-written and provides a thorough review of related work.
* The topic is highly relevant to the NIPS audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation limits the practical impact of the results.
* The complexity of the theory may make it difficult for many readers to fully grasp the contributions.
* The paper does not address the computational challenges of implementing the proposed algorithms.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution with significant theoretical value. While the lack of empirical evaluation is a concern, the novelty and rigor of the results justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity and computational feasibility issues outlined above.  Specifically, the authors should consider adding a small simulation study to demonstrate the practical implications of their findings and providing a brief discussion of potential computational challenges.
