

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. [2013] on missingness graphs (m-graphs) to develop conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors present theoretical results showing that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations (like P(y,x) and P(y|x)) are not recoverable. The paper introduces new graphical criteria for recoverability, particularly addressing cases where Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable. The authors apply their framework to attrition problems, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects in such settings.

The paper's main strengths lie in its theoretical contributions and the clear distinction it draws between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries. The key insight that causal effects may be recoverable despite inseparability between Y and Ry (Example 3) represents a significant advance over prior work. The paper builds logically on Mohan et al. [2013], extending their sequential factorization theorem to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact with one another. The formal definitions (e.g., recoverability, d*-separation) are well-motivated and carefully presented, and the examples (Examples 1 and 3) effectively illustrate the theoretical results.

The paper's clarity is generally good, with well-organized sections and consistent notation. The authors effectively relate their work to prior literature, particularly Mohan et al. [2013] and Pearl's causal inference framework. The application to attrition problems (Section 7) is particularly relevant, as attrition is a pervasive issue in longitudinal studies. The necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects under simple attrition (Theorem 6) correct a misconception in Garcia [2013], demonstrating the practical utility of the authors' framework.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the lack of empirical validation is notable. The authors provide no case studies, simulations, or real-world applications to demonstrate how their framework would be applied in practice. This is a significant limitation for a paper presented at NIPS, where empirical validation often complements theoretical work. The paper also doesn't adequately address how the proposed framework would be implemented in practice, which would strengthen its significance.

The originality of the work is somewhat limited by its direct extension of Mohan et al. [2013]. While the extension to causal queries and the specific conditions for recoverability are novel, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from related work in the missing data literature. For instance, the connection to CAR (Coarsened At Random) assumptions in Heitjan and Rubin [1991] and Robins and Rotnitzky [1992] could be more thoroughly discussed, as these frameworks also address missing data mechanisms.

The significance of the work would be strengthened by more concrete examples of how the framework could be applied to real missing data problems. While the attrition application is relevant, the paper doesn't demonstrate how the theoretical conditions would be used in practice. The authors could better articulate the practical impact of their work by providing a step-by-step example of applying their framework to a real dataset.

The paper also contains some minor issues that could be improved. The definition of "admissible sequence" (Definition 2) is somewhat confusing, and the minimality requirement could be better motivated. The proof of Theorem 4 is referenced to the appendix but could be briefly summarized in the main text to aid understanding. The notation for missingness mechanisms (Ry, R(i), etc.) is dense and could be made more intuitive.

In summary, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the problem of missing data by extending the framework of Mohan et al. [2013] to handle causal queries. The key insight about causal recoverability despite probabilistic non-recoverability is significant. However, the lack of empirical validation and insufficient connection to practical applications limits the paper's impact. The theoretical framework is sound and well-presented, but the paper would benefit from more concrete examples of application and clearer articulation of how researchers could use these conditions in practice.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution extending prior work on missing data; clear distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability; important correction to existing literature regarding attrition.
Con: No empirical validation or case studies; insufficient practical implementation details; some notational complexity; limited differentiation from related work in missing data literature.

The paper addresses an important problem with sound theoretical foundations, but its significance would be greatly enhanced by demonstrating practical utility through examples or empirical validation. As it stands, it's a solid theoretical contribution that advances the field but would be stronger with more concrete applications.
