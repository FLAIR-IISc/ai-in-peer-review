

# Review for NIPS 2014

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. [2013] on graphical models for missing data by introducing novel conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors build upon the missingness graph (m-graph) framework to develop general conditions for recoverability of both probabilistic queries (P(y|x), P(y,x)) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))), with particular emphasis on the surprising insight that causal queries may be recoverable even when their underlying probabilistic components are not.

The paper makes three main contributions. First, it refines the sequential factorization theorem to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact with one another. Second, it develops new graphical criteria for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions. Third, and most significantly, it demonstrates that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable, which challenges conventional wisdom in the field. The authors apply these results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects.

**Quality**: The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical results are well-motivated, rigorously proved, and presented with appropriate mathematical precision. The authors carefully distinguish between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries, which is a subtle but important contribution. The proof of Theorem 4, showing that causal effects can be recovered despite inseparability of Y and Ry, is particularly elegant and well-justified through the use of do-calculus. The paper correctly acknowledges the limitations of their approach (e.g., the restriction to |Vm|=1 in Theorem 5 and 6), demonstrating appropriate scholarly honesty. The experimental validation through examples (Figures 2-4) is sufficient for the theoretical nature of the work.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The authors effectively build on the m-graph framework introduced in Mohan et al. [2013], providing clear definitions and intuitive explanations of the key concepts. The running example (Figure 3) effectively demonstrates the theoretical insight that causal queries may be recoverable even when their probabilistic components are not. The organization follows a logical progression from basic concepts to more complex results, with each section building on the previous one. The appendix contains comprehensive proofs, making the results reproducible for the expert reader.

**Originality**: The paper makes significant contributions to the field. While Mohan et al. [2013] established conditions for recovering probabilistic queries, this paper extends those results to causal queries in a non-trivial way. The key insight that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y and Ry are inseparable (Theorem 4) represents a substantial theoretical advance. This finding contradicts the prevailing assumption in the literature (as noted in Garcia [2013]) that such inseparability prevents causal effect recovery. The paper's focus on incorporating the missing-data generating mechanism directly into the causal diagram represents a meaningful extension of prior work.

**Significance**: The paper addresses a fundamental problem in causal inference with missing data, which is ubiquitous in empirical research. The authors demonstrate how their results can be applied to attrition problems in longitudinal studies, a common challenge in social sciences and clinical trials. The theoretical insights have immediate practical implications for researchers dealing with missing data, particularly when the missingness mechanism is complex. The results provide a principled framework for determining when causal effects can be recovered from incomplete data, which is more general than the MAR/MCAR assumptions commonly used in practice. The paper's contribution advances the state of the art by providing necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for recoverability, moving beyond the ad-hoc methods that currently dominate the field.

**Arguments for Acceptance**:
- Provides novel necessary and sufficient conditions for causal query recovery from missing data
- Corrects a fundamental misconception in the literature (Garcia [2013]) regarding attrition
- Offers a theoretically sound framework that extends beyond MAR/MCAR assumptions
- Well-motivated, clearly written, and technically rigorous
- Makes a meaningful contribution to the field of causal inference with missing data

**Arguments against Acceptance**:
- The focus on m-graphs may be seen as limiting to researchers outside the causal inference community
- The paper's theoretical nature means it lacks empirical validation on real-world datasets
- Some readers may find the graphical conditions complex to apply in practice

Overall, this paper represents a significant theoretical advance in the field of causal inference with missing data. The results are well-motivated, rigorously proven, and address a problem of considerable practical importance. The paper makes a clear contribution to the state of the art by extending the m-graph framework to handle causal queries in a more general setting than previously possible. The insights about causal recoverability despite inseparability of Y and Ry are particularly noteworthy and challenge existing assumptions in the literature. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and builds coherently on previous work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
