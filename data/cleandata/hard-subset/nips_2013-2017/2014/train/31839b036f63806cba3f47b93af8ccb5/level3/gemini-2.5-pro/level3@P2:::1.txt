This paper addresses the problem of identifiability (termed "recoverability") of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. Building on the "Missingness Graph" (m-graph) framework from Mohan et al. [2013], the authors extend previous work by providing more general graphical conditions for recovering joint distributions, conditional distributions, and causal effects. The primary contributions include: (1) an extension of the sequential factorization theorem to handle dependencies between missingness mechanisms; (2) graphical criteria for recovering joint distributions in cases where factorization fails; (3) novel graphical conditions that preclude recoverability; and (4) a demonstration that causal queries may be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries are not. The results are applied to the problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a fundamental and highly relevant problem. Moving beyond the standard MCAR and MAR assumptions to provide a formal, graphical calculus for MNAR (Missing Not At Random) scenarios is an important research direction. The results in Section 6, which show that causal queries `P(y|do(x))` can be identified even when `P(y,x)` is not, are particularly novel and insightful.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper presents a series of non-trivial theoretical results that appear to be technically sound. The introduction of graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) is a valuable contribution, as it provides practitioners with clear, interpretable conditions under which an estimate is impossible to obtain given the model assumptions.
3.  **Potential Impact:** The methods and criteria developed could be highly useful for researchers in any empirical field where missing data is a concern, providing a principled way to reason about what can and cannot be learned from an incomplete dataset. The application to attrition highlights the practical relevance of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's main weakness is its clarity. It is exceptionally dense and attempts to cover too much material for a conference paper. This makes the key contributions difficult to parse and appreciate. Specific issues include:
    *   **Notation:** The notation is a significant barrier to understanding. There is an overabundance of similar-looking symbols (e.g., `V`, `Vi`, `Vo`, `Vm`, `V*`), and their definitions are not always provided before use. Subscripts are often difficult to read. The notation in Corollary 1, for instance, is particularly opaque.
    *   **Structure and Flow:** The paper reads like a collection of results rather than a cohesive narrative. Section 3 feels somewhat disconnected from the subsequent sections. The overall presentation would be more suited to a longer journal article where ideas can be introduced more gradually.
    *   **Missing Definitions:** Key notation used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4 (e.g., `G_X`, `G_W`) is not defined in the paper, forcing the reader to guess its meaning from context or prior work.

2.  **Incremental Aspects and Unifying Picture:** While some results are novel, others feel like incremental extensions of Mohan et al. [2013]. The paper presents a set of piecemeal sufficient and necessary conditions. A more impactful contribution would be to work towards a complete algorithm for recoverability, analogous to the existing algorithms for causal effect identification, which would provide a single, unifying framework.

3.  **Connection to Related Work:** The paper could be strengthened by drawing clearer connections to existing literature. The recovery formula in Theorem 2, which involves conditioning on the Markov blanket of the missingness indicators, is strongly reminiscent of inverse-probability weighting (IPW) methods. This connection should be made explicit. Similarly, the graphical conditions seem closely related to known results on selection bias in mixed graphs (e.g., Richardson [2003, 2009]), and discussing these relationships would better contextualize the work.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

*   **Improve Notation and Definitions:** I strongly urge the authors to overhaul the notation for clarity. A table of notation would be helpful. All terms should be defined before use (e.g., `Vo` and `Vm` are used in Section 2 before being formally defined).
*   **Focus the Contribution:** For a conference submission, it might be better to focus on the most significant and novel results—namely, the non-recoverability criteria (Section 5) and the recovery of causal queries (Section 6)—and present them with greater clarity, potentially moving other extensions to an appendix or a future publication.
*   **Clarify Critique of Prior Work:** The claim in Section 7.2 that the results "rectify prevailing opinion" regarding Garcia [2013] should be made with more care. Is Garcia's result incorrect, or does it simply follow from a different set of assumptions or a less general framework? This should be clarified to avoid misrepresenting prior work.
*   **Minor Corrections:**
    *   In the definition of `R_part` (Section 4), it should be stated that `Rx` and `Ry` belong to "distinct elements of the partition."
    *   Avoid casual language like "oftentimes."
    *   Use standard LaTeX commands like `\leftrightarrow` instead of `<-->` for bidirected edges.

**Summary (Arguments for and against acceptance):**

*   **Arguments for:** The paper addresses a significant problem with a rigorous, graphical approach. It contains novel and important results, especially the finding that causal effects can be identified when probabilistic queries cannot, and the useful non-recoverability criteria. The technical quality of the results appears high.
*   **Arguments against:** The paper is severely hindered by major issues in clarity, presentation, and notation. It is extremely difficult to read and verify. The lack of a clear, unifying narrative and the incremental nature of some results weaken its impact as a conference paper.

**Conclusion:**

This paper contains valuable and technically interesting ideas for a very important problem. However, its current presentation makes it inaccessible to a broad audience and difficult to evaluate thoroughly. The clarity issues are significant enough that I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. The work has strong potential but requires a major revision to simplify the presentation, clarify the notation, and focus the narrative on its most impactful contributions. It may be better suited for a journal submission where the space constraints are less restrictive.
