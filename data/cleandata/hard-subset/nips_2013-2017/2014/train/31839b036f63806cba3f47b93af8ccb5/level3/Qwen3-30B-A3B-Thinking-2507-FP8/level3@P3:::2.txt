

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in causal inference: determining when causal and probabilistic queries can be recovered from data with missing observations. Building on Mohan et al. (2013), the authors introduce "missingness graphs" (m-graphs) that explicitly incorporate the missing-data generating mechanism into causal diagrams. The paper provides novel necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of both probabilistic queries (P(y|x), P(y,x)) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))), demonstrating that causal queries may be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not.

The paper makes several significant theoretical contributions. First, it refines the sequential factorization theorem from prior work to handle cases where missingness mechanisms influence each other. Second, it establishes new graphical criteria for non-recoverability, which complements the sufficient conditions previously provided. Third, and most importantly, it demonstrates that causal queries can be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism (Ry) are not d-separable—a key insight that challenges previous assumptions in the field. The authors validate these insights with examples and provide applications to attrition problems, which are common in longitudinal studies.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical development. The authors provide formal definitions, theorems with proofs (in the appendix), and carefully constructed examples that illustrate both the applicability and limitations of their results. The paper distinguishes between recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries, and demonstrates how causal queries can circumvent the separability requirement that blocks probabilistic recovery. The authors are thorough in addressing both strengths and limitations, providing necessary conditions for non-recoverability that fill a significant gap in the literature.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors build coherently on Mohan et al. (2013), carefully defining all necessary concepts before presenting their extensions. The example in Section 3 effectively illustrates the importance of minimality in factorization, and the application to attrition in Section 7 provides concrete context for the theoretical results. The notation is consistent and well-motivated, and the paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

**Originality**: This work significantly advances the state of the art in causal inference with missing data. The key insight that causal queries may be recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not is novel and challenges prevailing assumptions. The paper distinguishes itself from prior NIPS work (including Mohan et al. 2013) by providing necessary and sufficient conditions rather than just sufficient conditions. The extension of graphical models to explicitly represent missingness mechanisms in causal diagrams represents a meaningful theoretical contribution that has not been adequately addressed in previous NIPS proceedings.

**Significance**: The results have immediate practical relevance for researchers dealing with missing data across disciplines, particularly in causal inference. The application to attrition problems (Section 7) demonstrates how the theoretical results can be applied to real-world scenarios. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a comprehensive framework for determining recoverability, which will enable researchers to better diagnose whether missing data in their studies will bias causal conclusions. This represents a demonstrable advance over existing methods that either assume MCAR or MAR without rigorous theoretical grounding.

**Strengths**:
- Provides necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability, filling a critical gap in the literature
- Demonstrates that causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not
- Clear, well-structured presentation of complex theoretical concepts
- Comprehensive treatment of attrition as a special case of missing data
- Rigorous proofs and examples that support theoretical claims

**Weaknesses**:
- The paper could provide more discussion of practical implementation challenges (e.g., how to estimate the missingness mechanism from data)
- While the theoretical results are comprehensive, the paper doesn't address computational complexity of the recovery algorithms
- The connection to practical statistical methods (beyond the theoretical framework) could be expanded

**Pro/Con Acceptance**:
Pro: This is a significant theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in causal inference with missing data. The paper is well-written, rigorous, and addresses a fundamental problem with clear practical implications. It represents a meaningful extension of prior work that is both novel and well-justified.

Con: While the theoretical contribution is strong, the paper could strengthen its practical relevance by addressing implementation considerations. However, this is a minor limitation given the paper's primary focus on theoretical foundations.

This paper makes a clear, important contribution to the field of causal inference with missing data. It builds coherently on prior work while providing novel theoretical insights that significantly advance the state of the art. The paper meets all criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission and represents a meaningful contribution to the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
