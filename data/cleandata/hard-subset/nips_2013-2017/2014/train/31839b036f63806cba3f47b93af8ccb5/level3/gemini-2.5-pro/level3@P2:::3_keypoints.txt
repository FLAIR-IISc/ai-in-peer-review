- Main contribution: Extends sequential factorization to handle dependencies between missingness indicators and gives more general graphical conditions for recovering joint and conditional distributions.  
- Main contribution: Presents novel graphical criteria for both recoverability and non-recoverability of distributions.  
- Main contribution: Shows causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the full joint distribution is not.  
- Main contribution: Applies results to characterize recoverability in attrition problems.  

- Strength: Provides novel, technically rigorous theoretical advances for inference with missing data, especially MNAR.  
- Strength: Distinguishes recoverability of causal versus probabilistic queries, a significant and insightful result.  
- Strength: Introduces useful graphical criteria for non-recoverability that help delimit what can be inferred.  

- Weakness: Strong assumption that the complete m-graph is known a priori; unclear how often this holds or how to elicit/test the model in practice.  
- Weakness: Framing may implicitly assume the full population is known, excluding problems like population size estimation — scope limitation not stated.  
- Weakness: Inconsistent definition/use of "m-graph" (V, U, R vs. inclusion of proxy variables V*) and need for formal, consistent presentation.  
- Weakness: Key terms like "neighbor" are used without formal definition in the graph context.  
- Weakness: Restriction that R variables cannot be parents of V or U is a strong constraint needing justification (fundamental vs. simplifying).  
- Weakness: Related-work comparisons are thin — e.g., the statement about ML under MAR is too general and needs clearer contrast with MNAR cases.  
- Weakness: Comparison to Garcia (2013) on attrition lacks detail about positivity assumptions and how they differ from this work.  
- Weakness: Several clarity and notation issues (phrasing like "can bias outcomes," derivation steps in Example 1, inconsistent R partition notation, dense Corollary 1) need fixing.  

- Argument for acceptance: Addresses a fundamental problem and substantially extends graphical models for missing data with sound theory.  
- Argument for acceptance: Demonstrates non-obvious insight that causal effects can be identified when joint distributions are not recoverable.  
- Argument for acceptance: Provides new, practical graphical tests for non-recoverability useful to practitioners.  

- Argument against acceptance: Reliance on possibly unrealistic assumption of a known missingness graph limits practical applicability.  
- Argument against acceptance: Clarity, definition, and notation problems hinder understanding and reproducibility.  
- Argument against acceptance: Practical implications and comparisons to alternatives (e.g., MLE) are not fully elaborated.  

- Overall: Technically solid and interesting work that requires clearer presentation and stronger justification/motivation of core assumptions to maximize practical impact.
