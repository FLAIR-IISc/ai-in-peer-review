This paper extends the graphical framework of Mohan et al. (2013) for identifying probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values. The authors present more general conditions for recovering queries under various missingness mechanisms, including a refined sequential factorization theorem. Key contributions include novel non-recoverability criteria and the important finding that causal queries may be recoverable even when the corresponding conditional distributions are not. The work provides useful examples and applies the results to the problem of attrition in experimental studies.

The paper tackles a significant and challenging problem. The technical quality is high, and the results appear sound. The contributions in Sections 5 and 6 are particularly valuable, offering clear non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) and interesting results on recovering causal queries that could have a substantial impact on the field.

However, the paper's main weakness is clarity, which significantly hinders its accessibility and impact. It attempts to cover too much material for a conference paper, resulting in a dense, poorly flowing manuscript that is difficult to parse. The notation is frequently confusing and inconsistent (e.g., the overuse of V for vertices and sets, as in Vi vs. Vo), which severely hampers readability; Corollary 1 is a prime example of this poor presentation. Some results, such as the hard-to-comprehend Theorem 1, feel incremental and do not yet reveal a larger, unifying picture of recoverability.

The work would be strengthened by making its connections to prior literature more explicit. For instance, Theorem 1 relates closely to Richardson (2009), while Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 resemble inverse-probability weighting and are likely related to conditioning on Markov blankets (Richardson 2003, 2009). The paper would also benefit from aiming for a more general recoverability criterion, analogous to the rules of do-calculus for causal-effect identifiability. Several specific corrections are also required: the notation for graphs with severed edges (used in Definition 4) is not defined; the critique of Garcia (2013) should be clarified; and minor notational issues should be addressed (e.g., define Vo and Vm before use, use \leftrightarrow for bidirected edges).

In summary, this paper presents important ideas and technically sound results. However, its significant presentational flaws obscure its contributions. The work seems better suited for a journal submission where the material can be developed more carefully and clearly.
