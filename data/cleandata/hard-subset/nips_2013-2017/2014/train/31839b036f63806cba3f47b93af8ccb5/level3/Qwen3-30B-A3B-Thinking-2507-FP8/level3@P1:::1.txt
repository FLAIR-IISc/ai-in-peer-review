

This paper extends Mohan et al.'s missingness graph (m-graph) framework to address recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries under missing data, particularly advancing causal effect identification when probabilistic queries are non-recoverable. The authors generalize prior results on joint/conditional distribution recovery (Section 3), establish novel non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3), and most significantly, demonstrate that causal queries like \(P(y|do(x))\) can be recoverable even when \(Y\) and its missingness mechanism \(R_y\) are inseparable (Section 6), contradicting the common assumption that probabilistic recoverability is required. The attrition application (Section 7) provides concrete necessary/sufficient conditions for causal effect recovery in longitudinal studies, correcting Garcia (2013).

Strengths are substantial. The technical quality is high: Theorem 3’s graphical non-recoverability conditions are clear, interpretable, and fill a critical gap in the literature. Section 6’s Example 3 is a compelling contribution—showing \(P(y|do(z))\) is recoverable in Figure 3 despite \(P(y,z,w)\) and \(P(y|z,w)\) being non-recoverable (via d*-separation and do-calculus), directly addressing a core limitation in missing data handling. The attrition analysis (Theorem 6) resolves ambiguities in prior work (e.g., Garcia’s ancestry requirement is unnecessary; Figure 4a’s non-recoverability stems from a collider path, not X’s ancestry). Sections 5–7 collectively deliver significant, usable results for practitioners dealing with MNAR data.

Weaknesses center on clarity and presentation, which substantially undermine the paper’s impact. The notation is inconsistent and poorly defined: \(V\), \(V_o\), \(V_m\) are referenced before definition; \(R^{part}\) is ambiguously described as "partitions" without clarifying that \(R_x, R_y\) belong to *distinct elements* of the partition (not "distinct partitions"). Corollary 1 exemplifies this—its conditions are opaque due to undefined sets like \(R_{Pasub(R(i))}\). Section 3’s sequential factorization (Theorem 1) feels disconnected from the rest of the paper, is hard to grasp without Richardson (2003/2009) context, and lacks intuitive motivation. The flow is disjointed: Sections 3–4 (probabilistic recovery) are overly technical and orthogonal to Sections 5–7 (causal insights), which are the paper’s strongest contribution. The 13-page appendix suggests the content is better suited for a journal, as NIPS expects conciseness.

Theoretical connections are underdeveloped. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 resemble inverse-probability weighting via Markov blankets (Richardson, 2003), but this link isn’t explicitly drawn, weakening the framework’s coherence. The missing definition of graphs formed by severing edges (used in Theorem 4) is a critical oversight. While the paper corrects Garcia (2013), it doesn’t clarify whether Garcia’s framework was fundamentally different or merely misapplied—this ambiguity needs resolution.

The paper’s core insight (causal queries bypassing probabilistic recoverability requirements) is highly significant and addresses a long-standing gap in missing data theory. However, the presentation issues—dense notation, poor flow, and overambitious scope for a conference paper—obscure this contribution. The technical rigor is evident, but the lack of clear exposition prevents readers from fully appreciating the novelty. For instance, the key message of Section 6 is buried under excessive formalism.

Pro/Con acceptance:  
*Pro:* Groundbreaking insight on causal recoverability under inseparability; rigorous non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3); practical attrition framework (Theorem 6) correcting prior literature.  
*Con:* Poor notation and flow; Section 3 distracts from stronger Sections 5–7; appendix too lengthy for NIPS; underdeveloped connections to existing graphical models literature.

The paper tackles an important problem with a valuable approach, but its current form risks being inaccessible. With substantial revisions to notation, flow, and focus (streamlining Sections 3–4 to emphasize Sections 5–7), it would be a strong NIPS contribution. As-is, the clarity issues significantly diminish its impact, though the core technical advances are sound and meaningful. The significance lies in enabling causal inference under previously deemed impossible missingness patterns, which is a major step for fields like epidemiology and social science where attrition is pervasive. However, the presentation must improve to match the quality of the results.
