- Extends Mohan et al. (2013) to give more general graphical conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data.  
- Shows causal queries (P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when associated probabilistic relations or joint distributions are not.  
- Introduces d*-separation and other novel graphical criteria, providing new theoretical insights into recoverability.  
- Demonstrates causal effects may be recovered when Y and its missingness mechanism R_y are not d-separable, challenging prior assumptions.  
- Applies results to attrition in longitudinal studies and provides necessary and sufficient conditions, with Theorem 6 correcting a misconception in Garcia (2013).  
- Theoretical development is rigorous and proofs appear correct.  
- Builds on established causal inference framework and do-calculus in a meaningful way.  
- Main limitation: requires knowledge of the missingness process, which may be unavailable in typical ML settings.  
- Inconsistent use of "mgraph" (V* variables sometimes omitted)—needs clarification.  
- Term "neighbor" is used without definition and should be formally defined.  
- Section 2 implicitly assumes knowledge of the full population (including entirely unobserved individuals), excluding population-size problems.  
- Restriction forbidding R variables as parents of V or U is stated without justification and needs explanation.  
- Claim that "maximum likelihood yields consistent estimates under MAR" is too vague given MNAR focus; applicability of MLE should be clarified.  
- Relation to Garcia et al.'s rectification needs clarification about positivity/assumption differences.  
- Unclear whether knowledge of the missingness process is typically available in ML—practical applicability should be discussed.  
- Several minor errors and edits needed (Example 1 wording and RHS, figure qualifications, notation fixes, corollary issue, missing reference for footnote).  
- Overall: technically strong and original but clarity issues and applicability concerns warrant revision before broader ML adoption.  
- Arguments for acceptance: substantial theoretical contribution, novel recoverability insights, rigorous proofs, useful attrition application.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on unknown missingness processes, unclear terminology and assumptions, implicit population assumption, and minor technical errors.
