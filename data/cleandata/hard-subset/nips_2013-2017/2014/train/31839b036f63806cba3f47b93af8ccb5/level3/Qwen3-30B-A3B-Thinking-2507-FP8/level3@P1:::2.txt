

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of causal inference with missing data by extending the graphical framework introduced by Mohan et al. (2013). The authors build upon the concept of "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) to develop novel necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering both probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing observations. The paper's core innovation lies in demonstrating that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations (P(y,x) and P(y|x)) are not recoverable, particularly when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable. This represents a meaningful theoretical advance beyond prior work, which typically required such separability for recoverability.

The paper's theoretical contributions are well-structured and rigorous. The authors present two main theorems (Theorem 1 and Theorem 2) that generalize the sequential factorization approach to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact with one another. The proof of Theorem 4, which establishes conditions for recovering causal effects when Y and Ry are connected by an inducing path, is particularly noteworthy. The authors illustrate their framework with compelling examples, including the gender wage-gap study and attrition problems, demonstrating how their conditions can be applied to real-world scenarios. The paper also provides necessary conditions for non-recoverability (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2), which is an important addition to the literature that helps practitioners recognize when consistent estimation is impossible.

The quality of this work is exceptional. The theoretical arguments are sound, the proofs are complete, and the authors carefully distinguish between recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries. The paper addresses a critical gap in the literature, as previous work (e.g., Garcia 2013) has incorrectly assumed that certain conditions (like X being an ancestor of Ry) are necessary for non-recoverability, while the authors demonstrate that these are neither necessary nor sufficient. The application to attrition problems in Section 7 is particularly valuable, as attrition is a pervasive issue in longitudinal studies and randomized trials.

In terms of clarity, the paper is exceptionally well-written. The notation is consistent and well-motivated, the examples effectively illustrate the theoretical concepts, and the organization flows logically from foundational concepts to novel contributions. The authors clearly explain the limitations of previous work and position their contributions within the broader context of causal inference. The appendix provides complete proofs, which is essential for reproducibility.

The originality of this work is substantial. While Mohan et al. (2013) introduced m-graphs and recoverability conditions for probabilistic queries, this paper extends the framework to handle causal queries under more general conditions. The key insight that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y and Ry are inseparable represents a significant conceptual advance that was not previously recognized in the literature. The authors' use of d*-separation and dormant independence to establish recoverability conditions for causal queries is particularly elegant.

The significance of this work is substantial. Missing data is a pervasive problem across scientific disciplines, and this paper provides a theoretically sound framework for handling missing data in causal inference. The results directly impact how researchers can diagnose whether causal effects are estimable from incomplete datasets, potentially saving countless hours of misguided analysis. The framework also enables better communication and refinement of assumptions about missingness mechanisms, which is crucial for the validity of causal inferences.

While the paper is strong overall, there are a few minor weaknesses to note. The authors could provide more discussion about the computational complexity of implementing their recovery procedures, particularly for larger graphs. Additionally, while the paper demonstrates theoretical recoverability, it would be valuable to include a brief discussion of practical implementation challenges in real-world applications. The authors also could more explicitly contrast their approach with recent work on missing data in machine learning (e.g., Marlin et al. 2011), though this is a minor point given the paper's focus on causal inference.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the state of the art in causal inference with missing data. The theoretical results are novel, rigorous, and well-motivated. The framework extends prior work in a meaningful way and provides practical guidance for researchers dealing with missing data in causal studies. The writing is clear, the organization is logical, and the examples effectively illustrate the theoretical concepts. Given the importance of missing data problems across scientific fields, this work has the potential to significantly impact how researchers approach causal inference with incomplete datasets.

This paper meets all the criteria for a strong NIPS submission: it is of high quality with sound theoretical foundations, exceptionally clear in its presentation, highly original in its approach to causal recovery with missing data, and of significant practical importance. The authors have made a meaningful contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in causal analysis with missing observations. I recommend acceptance.
