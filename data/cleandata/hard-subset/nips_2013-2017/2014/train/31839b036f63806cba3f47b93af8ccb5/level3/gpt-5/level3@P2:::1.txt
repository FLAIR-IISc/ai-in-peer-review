Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies recoverability (estimability) of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values using graphical models of the missingness process (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), it (i) generalizes “ordered factorization” to allow R nodes (missingness indicators), yielding Theorem 1 for sequential factorization; (ii) provides recovery formulas for joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists (Theorem 2 and Corollary 1), using partitions of R and Markov blankets in semi-Markovian models; (iii) introduces graphical non-recoverability criteria for P(V) and for P(Y|X) (Theorem 3, Corollary 2); (iv) shows that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when related probabilistic factors (e.g., P(y|x)) are not, including cases where Y is inseparable from Ry, via d*-separation/dormant independences and do-calculus (Theorem 4); and (v) applies these results to “simple attrition,” giving necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering both joint distributions (Theorem 5) and causal effects (Theorem 6).
This line of work is closely related to: (a) Pearl’s causal graphs and do-calculus; (b) ADMG/selection diagram machinery and Markov blankets in mixed graphs (Richardson, 2003); (c) prior missing-data frameworks (Rubin’s MCAR/MAR, IPW/ML/MI methods); and (d) Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) on m-graphs and recoverability. The results on non-recoverability and on causal recoverability despite inseparability from R extend what is typically assumed in MAR-centric ML practice. The general recovery forms in Theorem 2/Corollary 1 resemble inverse-probability weighting conditioned on appropriate blankets.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and under-explored problem at NIPS: principled recoverability with MNAR mechanisms explicitly modeled.
- The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3; Corollary 2) are clear, interpretable, and practically useful.
- Section 6 contains a genuinely interesting insight: causal effects may be recoverable when related probabilistic quantities are not, with a careful application of do-calculus and d*-separation.
- The attrition results (Theorems 5–6) give necessary and sufficient graphical conditions; these are likely to be useful to practitioners designing longitudinal studies.
- Technical development appears careful overall; proofs are provided in an extensive appendix.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and presentation: the paper tries to do too much for a conference format; the flow is dense, and notation is confusing and sometimes inconsistent (e.g., reuse of V, Vi as vertices vs. Vo as a set; illegible subscripts; heavy overload of symbols).
- Section 3 (general ordered factorization) is hard to follow; Theorem 1 is difficult to parse and feels orthogonal to the cleaner, more interpretable results in Sections 5–6.
- Several results feel incremental and piecemeal; the paper falls short of a unifying recoverability criterion (analogous to an ID-like algorithm for missing-data recoverability).
- Some missing or imprecise definitions and notational choices impede comprehension (e.g., notation for edge-severing/graph surgery in Definition 4/Theorem 4; Gw is used but not sufficiently defined; text uses “<-->” instead of ↔).
- The connection to existing mixed-graph literature (Richardson 2003; also related developments circa 2009) and to IPW could be better articulated.
- The 13-page appendix suggests the content may be better suited for a journal version.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The results appear technically sound; Theorem 3, Theorems 5–6 are especially clear. Theorem 1 would benefit from clearer statement, intuition, and examples; Section 4’s conditions (R partitions/Markov blankets) are plausible and align with IPW-like reasoning, but more explicit relation to ADMG separation and weighting would strengthen confidence.
- Clarity: Below the NIPS bar in current form. The notation overload, dense prose, and cross-references make it hard to extract the main messages. Sections 5–6 are clearer and could anchor a more focused paper.
- Originality: Mixed. Extensions beyond Mohan et al. (2013) are incremental in places, but the causal recoverability despite inseparability (Section 6) and the explicit non-recoverability criteria are novel and valuable.
- Significance: High potential impact if clarified. The guidance on when causal effects are recoverable with MNAR and attrition is important for both theory and practice.

Arguments for acceptance
- Important problem with practical stakes; advances understanding beyond MAR.
- Clear, actionable non-recoverability criteria.
- Novel and interesting causal-recoverability results; attrition conditions are tight.
- Builds on and extends a NIPS 2013 line of work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Presentation/notation issues significantly hinder accessibility and verification.
- Lacks a unifying decision procedure for general recoverability (piecemeal theorems).
- Section 3 is difficult and seems orthogonal; overall narrative feels unfocused.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Notation and definitions:
  - Define the graph operators used in d*-separation precisely (e.g., Gw, GX,W1) and make them consistent with GX/GX introduced earlier.
  - Replace “<-->” with ↔ for bidirected edges; avoid informal terms like “oftentimes.”
  - Clarify sets Vo, Vm before first use; avoid overloading V/Vi; ensure subscripts are legible.
  - Correct Rpart wording: since Rpart is a partition, say “if Rx and Ry belong to distinct elements of the partition,” not just “distinct partitions.”
- Relation to prior work:
  - Make the connection to Richardson (2003) ADMG Markov properties explicit; discuss how Theorem 2/Corollary 1 relate to conditioning on Markov blankets and to IPW.
  - For Theorem 4, cite Shpitser and Pearl (2008) on dormant independence/d*-separation; ensure the reference is included in the bibliography.
- Scope/unification:
  - Articulate whether a general decision procedure for recoverability (analogous to ID for causal effects) is within reach; position Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 as parts of that picture.
  - Provide a running example to thread Sections 3–6, with explicit estimands and how each theorem applies.
- Attrition section:
  - Clarify whether Garcia (2013) is incorrect under the same assumptions, or whether the frameworks differ (and why that leads to different conclusions).
- Minor:
  - State positivity assumptions where used; define “semi-Markovian” once and stick to it.
  - In Corollary 1, improve readability; current presentation is hard to parse.

Questions for the authors
- Can you outline a path toward a complete recoverability algorithm (beyond sufficient/necessary fragments), possibly leveraging ADMG separation and do-calculus with R-nodes?
- For Theorem 1, can you provide a simple, concrete example contrasting admissible vs. inadmissible factorizations, and an intuitive proof sketch?
- What are the computational implications of Theorem 2’s blanket/partition checks for large graphs?

Overall recommendation: Weak reject (borderline). The problem and several results (esp. Sections 5–6) are significant, but the paper’s clarity and lack of a cohesive unifying framework make it difficult to recommend in current form. A tightened version centered on the non-recoverability criteria and causal recoverability results, with improved notation and stronger connections to ADMG/IPW, would be a strong NIPS submission.

Confidence: High.
