- Advances graphical criteria for when P(y, x), P(y|x) and P(y|do(x)) are recoverable using missingness graphs (m-graphs) that explicitly model missingness indicators R.  
- (i) Generalizes ordered factorizations to include R, giving a new sufficient condition that handles interacting R-nodes (Theorem 1).  
- (ii) Provides an alternative route to recover joint distributions via partitions of R and collider-path Markov blankets when no admissible sequence exists (Theorem 2, Corollary 1).  
- (iii) Derives new non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2).  
- (iv) Shows certain causal effects are recoverable even when Y is inseparable from its missingness indicator Ry, using do-calculus and dormant independences (Theorem 4).  
- Gives necessary and sufficient conditions for “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1) for joint distributions and causal effects, clarifying and correcting prior claims (Theorems 5–6).  
- Connects to identifiability literature (Shpitser & Pearl) and classic MAR/MCAR frameworks (Rubin), extending the m-graph program.  
- Technical contributions are carefully stated with proofs; interplay of d-separation, collider paths, and do-calculus is sound.  
- Paper is primarily theoretical; lacks empirical/synthetic demonstrations of finite-sample behavior and robustness to graph misspecification.  
- Would benefit from algorithmizing construction of admissible sequences and R-partitions.  
- Clarity is generally good but notation could be tightened (e.g., G_X vs GX, X′ shorthand) and positivity assumptions should be restated.  
- Missing full citation for dormant independence/d*-separation (Shpitser & Pearl, 2008) invoked in §6.  
- Originality: inclusion of R-nodes in ordered factorizations, general recovery via collider-path partitions, and explicit non-recoverability criteria are novel beyond Mohan et al. (2013).  
- Significance: advances recoverability under MNAR, producing necessary/sufficient conditions and constructive estimands likely useful in practice.  
- Pros: strong theoretical advances, new non-recoverability criteria, insightful causal recoverability results, coherent attrition treatment.  
- Cons: limited empirical/computational validation, some results focused on |Vm|=1, minor notation/citation gaps, and limited practical guidance on learning/validating m-graphs and sensitivity to misspecification.
