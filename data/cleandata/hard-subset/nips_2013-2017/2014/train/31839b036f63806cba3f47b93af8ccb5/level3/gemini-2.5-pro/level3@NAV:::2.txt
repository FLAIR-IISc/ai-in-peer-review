This paper addresses the critical problem of determining whether a causal or probabilistic query is recoverable from a dataset with missing observations. Building directly upon the foundational work of Mohan et al. (2013), the authors extend the graphical framework of "m-graphs" to handle more complex scenarios. The key contributions include a generalization of the sequential factorization theorem to allow for dependencies between missingness mechanisms, a novel algorithm for recovery when such factorization is not possible, and, significantly, the introduction of graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*. A particularly insightful result is the demonstration that a causal query, P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the corresponding conditional probability, P(y|x), is not, leveraging the rules of do-calculus. The paper concludes by applying these theoretical results to the practical problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance & Originality:** This work provides a substantial advancement in the theory of causal inference with missing data. The proposed necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability are novel and important. The distinction between the recoverability of causal versus probabilistic queries is a key theoretical insight that deepens our understanding and is likely to influence future research in this domain. The formal treatment of non-recoverability criteria is a much-needed and original contribution.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with clear definitions and theorems. The claims are well-supported by the provided graphical arguments and illustrative examples, which effectively convey the intuition behind the formal results. The work is a complete and focused contribution that rigorously extends the state of the art.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized. It coherently builds on existing literature, making its novel contributions easy to identify and appreciate. The use of examples to walk through the application of theorems is particularly effective for reader comprehension.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The work is entirely theoretical. While this is appropriate for the nature of the contribution, the paper could be strengthened by including a brief discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms or a simulation study to demonstrate their performance on finite samples. This is a minor point, however, and does not detract from the paper's core theoretical value.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to an important area of research. It is clear, technically robust, and addresses a difficult problem with novel and elegant solutions.
