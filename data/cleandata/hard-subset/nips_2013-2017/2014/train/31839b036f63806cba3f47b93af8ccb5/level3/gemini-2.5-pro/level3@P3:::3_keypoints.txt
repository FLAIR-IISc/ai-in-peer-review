- Problem and contribution: Extends the m-graph framework to derive more general recoverability conditions and a novel identification formula for joint distributions without an admissible sequential factorization.  
- Key finding: Shows causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the corresponding joint distribution (e.g., P(x,y)) is not.  
- Scope extension: Handles a broader class of missingness mechanisms, including dependencies between missingness indicators, and gives new graphical criteria for recoverability and non-recoverability.  
- Significance: Addresses a fundamental problem and provides a significant, counter-intuitive theoretical result distinguishing associational vs. causal identifiability under missing data.  
- Originality: Meaningfully extends prior m-graph work (e.g., Theorem 2) and applies results to practical attrition models.  
- Technical quality: Formal definitions and theorems appear sound, supported by illustrative examples and non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3).  
- Practical applicability concern: Framework assumes the complete m-graph (including R variables) is known a priori, with no guidance on elicitation or learning from data.  
- Scope/assumption clarity: Appears to assume knowledge of the full population (including fully unobserved individuals), excluding problems like population size estimation.  
- Notation and definitions issues: Inconsistent use of "m-graph", unclear role of V* proxy variables, and ambiguity about which graph the criteria apply to.  
- Graph-theoretic terminology: "Neighbor" is used without definition for graphs with both directed and bi-directed edges.  
- Modeling restriction: The prohibition on R variables being parents of V or U needs justification (technical convenience vs. substantive assumption).  
- Related work comparison: Needs clearer comparison to Garcia et al. (2013) and explanation why standard MLE under MAR may not apply to the MNAR processes considered.  
- Clarify estimation implications: Should discuss whether identification formulas can be integrated into consistent estimation procedures like MLE.  
- Minor corrections: Multiple wording, notation, and consistency issues (e.g., phrasing in Introduction, Example 1 simplification, R partition notation, Corollary 1 clarity, and a more specific pointer to Mohan et al. (2013)).  
- Overall recommendation: Technically solid and novel; recommend acceptance subject to revisions addressing practicality, clarity, and minor errors.
