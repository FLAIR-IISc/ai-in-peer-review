This paper addresses the crucial problem of identifying causal and probabilistic queries from data with missing observations, building upon the framework of missingness graphs introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. It extends prior work by explicitly modeling the missing data mechanism within the causal diagram and provides novel, general conditions for recoverability. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that causal queries can be identifiable even when the underlying probabilistic relationships are not, a particularly important insight.

The paper is well-motivated, acknowledging the pervasive issue of missing data and its potential to bias inferences. It clearly articulates the problem and connects it to existing literature on missing data handling, including deletion methods, maximum likelihood estimation, inverse probability weighting, and imputation techniques. The authors appropriately position their work within the context of graphical models for missing data, referencing recent advancements and highlighting the novelty of their approach. The extension beyond simply identifying recoverable queries to characterizing *when* causal effects are recoverable despite non-recoverable probabilistic components is a significant contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical framework for analyzing recoverability, building on established concepts like d-separation and the do-calculus. The theorems are clearly stated and supported by logical derivations.
* **Novelty:** The key finding – that causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic queries aren't – is a genuinely novel and important result. This expands the applicability of causal inference in practical scenarios with missing data.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The introduction provides a good overview of the problem, and the subsequent sections build logically on each other. The use of examples, especially Figures 1-4, greatly enhances understanding.
* **Focus:** The paper maintains a sharp focus on the core problem of recoverability under missingness and avoids venturing into tangential topics.
* **Extension of Prior Work:** The paper successfully extends the work of Mohan et al. [2013] by refining the sequential factorization theorem and addressing more complex scenarios, including those with interacting missingness mechanisms.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper lacks empirical validation. Demonstrating the practical impact of the derived conditions with simulations or real-world datasets would strengthen the work considerably. It would be useful to see examples where the proposed criteria accurately predict recoverability/non-recoverability in scenarios beyond those illustrated in the figures.
* **Complexity:** The theoretical framework, while rigorous, can be challenging to follow for readers not deeply familiar with causal inference and graphical models. While the writing is clear, the density of notation and theorems might pose a barrier to entry.
* **Attrition Focus:** The application to attrition, while illustrative, feels somewhat tacked on. The discussion in Section 7 leans heavily on examples, and a more in-depth exploration of how the framework applies to specific attrition scenarios would be valuable.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and represents a complete piece of work regarding theoretical development. The authors are careful in their reasoning and acknowledge the limitations of their framework.

**Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is well-organized, and the concepts are explained clearly.

**Originality:** High. The core finding regarding causal query recoverability is novel and makes a significant contribution to the field.

**Significance:** High. The results have the potential to impact the practice of causal inference in situations with missing data, particularly in fields like epidemiology and social sciences where attrition is common.  The paper advances the state-of-the-art by providing a more nuanced understanding of when causal effects can be reliably estimated despite missing data.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Provides novel and theoretically grounded conditions for recoverability of causal queries.
* Extends existing work on missingness graphs and addresses a critical gap in the literature.
* The work is clearly written and well-motivated.
* The findings have potentially broad implications for causal inference.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Lacks empirical validation to support the theoretical claims.
* The theoretical complexity might limit its accessibility to a broader audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference with missing data. While the lack of empirical validation is a concern, the theoretical rigor, novelty, and clarity of the presentation outweigh this limitation. I recommend acceptance, with a suggestion to the authors to consider including empirical results in future work.
