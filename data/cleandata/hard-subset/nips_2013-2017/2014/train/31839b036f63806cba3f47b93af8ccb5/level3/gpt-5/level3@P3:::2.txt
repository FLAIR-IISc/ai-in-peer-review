Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies when probabilistic and causal queries can be consistently estimated from data with missing entries, given a causal model of the missingness process. Building on “missingness graphs” (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors:
- Generalize ordered factorizations to include missingness indicators (R nodes), yielding new sufficient conditions for recovering joint and conditional distributions via “admissible sequences.”
- Provide recovery results beyond cases where an admissible sequence exists, via a new theorem that partitions R variables using collider-path structure and expresses P(V) through complete-case terms and probabilities of completeness.
- Introduce graphical non-recoverability criteria (necessary conditions) for joint and conditional distributions.
- Analyze causal queries P(y|do(x)) with missing data. They show causal effects can be recoverable even when associated probabilistic factors are not, including cases where Y is not d-separated from its missingness mechanism Ry; recovery leans on do-calculus, dormant independences, and d*-separation (Shpitser & Pearl, UAI 2006; 2008).
- Specialize to attrition (|Vm|=1), giving necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for recoverability of both P(V) and P(y|do(x)), correcting some prior claims (e.g., Garcia 2013).

Relative to prior work at NIPS and elsewhere, the paper extends Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) by: (i) allowing dependencies among missingness mechanisms, (ii) supplying new non-recoverability criteria, (iii) treating causal effects with inseparable Y–Ry, and (iv) giving sharp results for simple attrition. The work complements classic MAR/MCAR treatments (Rubin 1976; Little & Rubin 2002), ML/EM approaches (Dempster et al. 1977; Lauritzen 1995; Koller & Friedman 2009), and IPW/semiparametric methods (Robins & Rotnitzky 1992; Van der Laan & Robins 1998), by leveraging explicit causal modeling of the missingness mechanism.

Strengths
- Novel theory: New sufficient and necessary conditions that substantially broaden the scope of recoverability beyond prior results, including interacting R variables and cases lacking admissible sequences.
- Explicit modeling of missingness: Incorporating the missing-data generating mechanism into the causal diagram is principled and clarifies assumptions, going beyond MAR/MCAR.
- Causal advances: Demonstrates that causal effects can be recoverable even when relevant probabilistic quantities are not, with clear do-calculus–based derivations and theorems specific to inseparable Y–Ry.
- Sharp results for attrition: Necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for both probabilistic and causal recovery under |Vm|=1 are practically useful and correct previous misconceptions.
- Focus and clarity: The paper is tightly scoped, well organized, and builds coherently on Mohan et al. (2013) with careful proofs in the appendix.

Weaknesses
- Practical evaluation: No empirical demonstrations or simulation studies showing how the criteria guide analysis or how errors arise when assumptions are violated.
- Usability and tooling: The paper does not provide a full decision procedure or software for checking the conditions algorithmically (e.g., automating partitioning of R, dormant independence checks, or generating estimands).
- Assumption burden: Results require a correct m-graph, strict positivity over complete cases, and semi-Markovian assumptions; guidance on elicitation and robustness to misspecification is limited.
- Scope restrictions: Some strongest guarantees (Theorems 5–6) hold only when |Vm|=1; it would be valuable to map how much extends to multiple partially observed variables.

Quality
Technically sound and rigorous. Claims are supported by formal theorems with proofs, consistent with the do-calculus and identifiability literature (Shpitser & Pearl 2006, 2008). The paper is a complete piece of work, though empirical validation and practical decision procedures would strengthen it.

Clarity
Generally clearly written, with motivating examples. The role of minimality in the generalized ordered factorization is well explained. Suggestions: provide a compact algorithmic checklist for each main theorem; add a small worked example for Theorem 2’s construction (R partitions, Mb(R(i))) in the main text; include a figure illustrating inseparable Y–Ry recovery using Theorem 4.

Originality
High. Incorporating interacting missingness mechanisms into recovery criteria, providing non-recoverability tests, and showing causal recoverability beyond probabilistic recoverability constitute novel contributions. The necessary-and-sufficient results for simple attrition are new and impactful.

Significance
High. Missing data is pervasive, and principled conditions for recoverability under MNAR with explicit mechanisms will influence both causal inference and ML practice. The work advances the state of the art theoretically and will be a reference point for future methodological and software developments.

Detailed comments and questions
- Please clarify computational complexity and provide pseudocode for checking admissible sequences and Theorem 2’s conditions on large graphs.
- Offer guidance for constructing m-graphs in practice (domain elicitation, testable implications per Mohan & Pearl 2014).
- Discuss robustness when positivity fails or when the m-graph is partially misspecified.
- Consider a small simulation demonstrating: (i) non-recoverability predicted by Theorem 3 leading to bias; (ii) causal recovery with inseparable Y–Ry outperforming naive methods.
- Minor: standardize notation (e.g., R′x), and ensure all figures are referenced with sufficient captions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Substantial, novel theoretical contributions that extend prior NIPS work.
- Clear, focused writing and strong connection to causal identifiability theory.
- Practical relevance, especially for attrition and MNAR scenarios where existing methods falter.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of empirical or software components may limit immediate adoption.
- Some results are specialized (e.g., |Vm|=1), and broader applicability could be elaborated.
- Assumes accurate causal modeling of missingness, which can be challenging in practice.

Overall recommendation
A strong theoretical paper that meaningfully advances causal analysis with missing data by unifying and extending recoverability conditions. I lean toward acceptance, with suggestions to add a brief algorithmic checklist and at least one illustrative experiment or case study.
