This paper addresses the fundamental problem of whether a probabilistic or causal query can be consistently estimated from a dataset with missing values. The authors build upon the formal framework of "missingness graphs" (m-graphs) introduced in Mohan et al. [2013], which explicitly model the causal mechanisms responsible for missing data. The paper extends this prior work by providing more general graphical conditions for the recoverability of probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y|x), P(y,x)) and, more importantly, causal queries (P(y|do(x))). The central and most significant contribution is demonstrating that a causal effect can be recoverable even when the underlying joint or conditional distributions are not, a crucial insight for causal analysis. The authors derive graphical criteria for these scenarios and apply their findings to the practical problem of attrition in experimental studies.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes several novel and important contributions to the study of missing data from a graphical model perspective. The primary contribution—showing that causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be identified even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not separable (a condition that would render P(Y) non-recoverable)—is a significant advancement. This moves the field beyond simply checking for recoverability of the full joint distribution and provides more nuanced tools for researchers focused on specific causal questions. The paper also introduces new graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3), which are valuable for alerting practitioners to situations where consistent estimation is impossible under the given model assumptions.

2.  **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a rigorous, principled approach to a difficult problem. The arguments are built formally through a series of theorems and corollaries, with proofs provided in the appendix. The generalization of the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) and the development of recovery methods for cases where no admissible sequence exists (Theorem 2) demonstrate a thorough treatment of the problem. The authors are careful to connect their theoretical results to established concepts in causality, such as the do-calculus and d*-separation, which grounds their work in the broader literature.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It starts by clearly defining the problem and reviewing the necessary background on m-graphs. The progression from probabilistic queries to causal queries, and finally to the application in attrition, is logical and coherent. The use of clear examples (e.g., Example 1 and 3) is effective in illustrating the core theoretical concepts, particularly the counter-intuitive result about the recoverability of causal effects. The abstract and introduction provide a clear roadmap for the reader, and the contributions are stated plainly.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper would be significantly strengthened by an empirical component. For instance, simulations on synthetic data generated from a known m-graph could be used to (a) demonstrate the correctness of the proposed recovery algorithms in practice and (b) show the bias that arises when attempting to estimate a non-recoverable query using standard methods like complete-case analysis or simple imputation. This would provide a more concrete illustration of the practical impact of the theoretical results.

2.  **Assumption of a Correct Model:** The entire framework relies on the assumption that the practitioner can specify the correct m-graph. While the authors cite other work on testing for misspecification, a brief discussion within this paper about the practical challenges of constructing such a graph from domain knowledge would be beneficial. It would help ground the work for practitioners who must grapple with the uncertainty of model specification in real-world scenarios.

**Summary of Arguments:**

**Pro-acceptance:**
*   Presents novel and significant theoretical results for causal inference with missing data.
*   The key finding that causal queries can be recoverable when probabilistic ones are not is a major conceptual advance.
*   Provides a rigorous, graphical framework with clear, testable conditions for recoverability and non-recoverability.
*   The paper is focused, clearly written, and builds coherently on existing literature.

**Con-acceptance:**
*   The paper is purely theoretical, and its practical utility is not demonstrated through experiments on synthetic or real data.

**Conclusion:**

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful theoretical contribution to an important and challenging area. It provides researchers with new conceptual and graphical tools to reason about what can and cannot be learned from incomplete data. The clarity of the presentation and the significance of the results, particularly regarding the recoverability of causal effects, make this a valuable addition to the literature. Despite the lack of empirical validation, the theoretical depth and novelty are substantial. I recommend acceptance.
