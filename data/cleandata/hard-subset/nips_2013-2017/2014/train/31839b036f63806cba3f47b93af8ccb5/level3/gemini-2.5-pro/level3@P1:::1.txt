This paper addresses the problem of recoverability—that is, the identifiability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values. The authors build upon the "missingness graph" (m-graph) framework introduced by Mohan et al. [2013] to formalize the assumptions about the missing data mechanism. The paper extends previous results by providing more general graphical conditions for recovering probabilistic queries like P(y|x) and P(y, x), as well as causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)). Key contributions include a generalized sequential factorization theorem, conditions for recoverability when such factorizations are not possible, graphical criteria for non-recoverability, and a demonstration that causal effects can be recoverable even when the underlying joint or conditional distributions are not. The results are applied to the specific problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a fundamental and widely-applicable problem. Moving beyond standard MAR/MCAR assumptions to provide formal graphical criteria for identifiability under MNAR (Missing Not At Random) settings is a significant research direction. The most interesting and novel result is presented in Section 6, which shows that a causal query P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the associated conditional distribution P(y|x) is not. This is a subtle and important insight that highlights the distinct nature of causal and statistical queries and could have a significant impact on how researchers approach missing data problems in causal inference.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical work appears to be of high quality. The proofs are provided in a lengthy appendix, and the theorems, while complex, seem sound. The graphical criteria derived, particularly the non-recoverability conditions in Theorem 3 based on collider paths, are clear, interpretable, and provide practitioners with concrete tools to assess their models. The application to attrition in Section 7 provides a useful and concrete demonstration of the framework's utility.

3.  **Extends Prior Work:** The paper successfully extends the work of Mohan et al. [2013]. The generalization of the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) to handle dependencies between missingness mechanisms (R variables) and the development of recovery methods for cases where this fails (Theorem 2) represent solid, incremental contributions to the existing framework.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's primary weakness is its clarity. The manuscript is extremely dense and attempts to cover too much ground for a conference paper. The flow between sections is not always smooth, with Section 3 feeling somewhat disconnected from the later sections. The result is a paper that is very difficult to read, understand, and verify. The lengthy 13-page appendix further suggests that the work might be better suited for a journal submission where the ideas can be developed more slowly and thoroughly.

2.  **Notation and Terminology:** The notation is often confusing and used inconsistently, which severely hampers readability. For instance, 'V' is used to denote the set of all vertices, while 'Vo', 'Vm' denote subsets, and 'Vi' denotes a single vertex. Subscripts are often difficult to read. The presentation of results like Corollary 1 is particularly opaque, making it hard to grasp the conditions and the resulting estimand without significant effort.

3.  **Scope and Unification:** While the paper presents several useful results, they feel somewhat piecemeal. The work provides a collection of sufficient or necessary conditions for specific scenarios rather than building towards a general, unifying theory of recoverability. An ideal contribution in this area would be a complete algorithm for recoverability, analogous to the algorithms for causal effect identifiability (e.g., Shpitser and Pearl [2006]), which could determine if *any* valid estimand exists. The current set of theorems do not yet achieve this level of completeness.

### Detailed Comments

*   **Theorem 1:** This theorem is particularly hard to comprehend. The conditions are complex, and its relation to existing work on factorization in mixed graphs (e.g., Richardson, 2009, "Factorization of Mixed Graphical Models") could be made more explicit to help orient the reader.
*   **Theorem 2 and Corollary 1:** The proposed recovery formula resembles an inverse-probability weighting (IPW) estimator. The conditioning on the Markov blanket of the R variables seems to be the key step. It would be helpful to discuss this connection explicitly, as it would link the proposed graphical approach to a well-known statistical framework. See, for example, Richardson (2003) on Markov properties for ADMGs.
*   **Notation in Theorem 2:** The text states, "...for every element Rx and Ry belonging to distinct partitions...". This should be rephrased for clarity, for example: "...for any two variables Rx and Ry that belong to distinct elements of the partition R(part)...".
*   **Missing Definitions:** The notation for graph modification (e.g., G_X and G^X used in the context of do-calculus rules in Definition 4 and Theorem 4) is used without being formally defined in the paper.
*   **Critique of Garcia [2013]:** The paper claims to rectify prevailing opinion, citing Garcia [2013]. It would be more constructive to clarify if Garcia's result is incorrect under its own assumptions, or if the apparent contradiction arises because the frameworks or assumptions are different.
*   There are minor presentational issues, such as the use of "<-->" for a bidirected edge where a standard symbol like `\leftrightarrow` would be clearer.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:**
    *   The problem is important and of broad interest to the NIPS community.
    *   The paper contains novel and significant results, especially the finding in Section 6 that causal queries can be recoverable when probabilistic ones are not.
    *   The technical contributions are solid and extend an established line of research. The non-recoverability criteria are particularly useful.

*   **Arguments Against Acceptance:**
    *   The paper suffers from severe clarity and presentation issues, making it very difficult for readers to absorb the contributions.
    *   The notation is confusing and inconsistently applied.
    *   The collection of results feels somewhat fragmented rather than parts of a single, unifying framework. The paper reads more like a technical report or a journal submission than a polished conference paper.

Overall, this paper contains important ideas and technically sound results on a significant problem. However, the poor presentation is a major barrier to its impact. The work in Sections 5 and 6 is particularly strong, but the manuscript as a whole needs substantial revision to improve clarity, notation, and flow before it can be considered a strong contribution to the conference.
