This paper presents a significant extension to the work of Mohan et al. [2013] on recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data using graphical models. The authors introduce a novel identification formula and provide conditions under which causal queries can be recovered even when the joint distribution is not recoverable. The paper is technically sound, and the results are well-supported by theoretical analysis.

The main contribution of the paper is the development of a more general missingness mechanism than prior work, allowing for the recovery of causal queries under certain conditions. The authors also provide a new graphical condition for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions, which is a useful addition to the existing literature.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide several examples to illustrate the application of their results. The writing is clear, and the notation is well-defined. However, there are some minor errors and corrections that need to be addressed, such as the wording in some places, figure qualifications, and notation inconsistencies.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to build on earlier work and provide a more comprehensive framework for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of related work and demonstrate the applicability of their results to problems of attrition.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. For example, the results require knowledge of the censoring/missingness process, which may not be available in typical machine learning applications. Additionally, the paper assumes that the full population is known, excluding problems like population size estimation.

Some minor suggestions for improvement include moving more figure details into the main text, clarifying the term "mgraph" and its usage, and defining the term "neighbor" which is used without a definition. Furthermore, the relation to Garcia et al.'s "rectification" needs clarification regarding positivity assumptions, and the claim that maximum likelihood yields consistent estimates under MAR needs to be explained in more detail.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques and provides new insights into the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The results are significant and advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

Overall, I would recommend acceptance of this paper, pending minor revisions to address the errors and inconsistencies mentioned above. The paper is a valuable contribution to the field and provides new insights into the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data.
