This paper addresses the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing entries. The authors build upon the "m-graph" framework introduced in Mohan et al. [2013], which provides a graphical representation of the data-generating process and the missingness mechanism. The primary contribution is a set of more general conditions for determining if a query is recoverable. The paper extends the sequential factorization theorem to handle cases where missingness mechanisms influence each other, a common scenario in longitudinal studies. For cases where this theorem does not apply, a novel recovery algorithm is proposed based on partitioning the missingness variables. Importantly, the authors introduce graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, a topic not formally treated in prior work. A key finding is that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can sometimes be recovered even when the corresponding joint or conditional distributions (P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are not, which is a significant and counter-intuitive result. The utility of these theoretical results is demonstrated through an application to the problem of attrition in experimental studies.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound and presents a solid theoretical contribution. The proofs provided in the appendix appear correct, and the derived theorems are non-trivial extensions of previous results. The work is a complete piece of research, moving from sufficient conditions for recoverability to necessary ones, and from probabilistic to causal queries.

However, the primary weakness concerning quality is the strong assumption that the full causal graph of the missingness process (the m-graph) is known *a priori*. While the authors acknowledge that m-graphs can be refined, it is unclear how a practitioner would arrive at the correct graph in most real-world machine learning applications. This assumption limits the immediate practical applicability of the proposed methods, even if the theoretical contribution is strong. For example, the motivating gender-wage gap example in Figure 1 requires knowledge of subtle dependencies between qualifications, sex, income, and their respective missingness indicators, which may not be available.

Additionally, a few underlying assumptions could be better justified:
*   The framework seems to implicitly assume a known population from which samples are drawn, even if some individuals are entirely unobserved. This excludes problems like population size estimation where the set of individuals itself is unknown.
*   The model forbids R variables from being parents of variables in V (substantive) or U (latent). Since R variables are treated as real quantities that cause missingness, a justification for this structural constraint would be beneficial. Is this for theoretical convenience, or does it represent a fundamental modeling assumption?

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written, but several points of clarification would significantly improve its readability and precision.
*   The term "m-graph" is used inconsistently. In Section 2, it is defined to include the set of proxy variables V*, but in many subsequent figures and discussions, these variables are omitted without explanation. The authors should clarify this convention.
*   The term "neighbor" is used in Theorem 2 and Theorem 3 without a formal definition (e.g., are nodes connected by any edge, or only directed edges?).
*   The motivating example in Figure 1 would be more effective if more details were moved from the caption into the main text to better frame the problem for the reader.
*   The discussion on page 8 regarding related work could be more precise. The claim that maximum likelihood yields consistent estimates under MAR is too general. A more nuanced discussion explaining why standard MLE is or is not applicable to the MNAR (Missing Not At Random) processes considered in this paper would be more informative.
*   Also on page 8, the comparison with Garcia [2013] needs more detail. When stating that the results here rectify prevailing opinions, it's important to clarify the assumptions made by Garcia. For instance, does Garcia [2013] also assume positivity, or a stronger condition that might explain the difference in conclusions?

**Originality:**
The paper makes several original contributions. While it directly extends Mohan et al. [2013], the extensions are significant. Theorem 1 (general ordered factorization including R variables), Theorem 2 (recovery in the absence of an admissible sequence), and Theorem 3 (graphical non-recoverability criteria) are all novel. The most original and perhaps surprising contribution is the analysis in Section 6, demonstrating that causal queries can be identified even when the underlying joint distribution is not. This insight, exemplified in Example 3 and formalized in Theorem 4, decouples causal and statistical recoverability and is a valuable addition to the literature.

**Significance:**
The results presented are theoretically significant for the fields of causality, graphical models, and missing data research. The paper pushes the boundary of what is provably recoverable beyond the standard MCAR and MAR assumptions. The non-recoverability criteria are particularly useful, as they provide researchers with formal tools to understand when a problem is fundamentally unsolvable with the given assumptions. The finding about the recoverability of causal queries is a key conceptual advance.

The practical significance is less clear due to the strong assumption of a known m-graph. However, in domains where causal modeling is already prevalent and domain expertise can be leveraged to construct such graphs (e.g., epidemiology, social sciences), these results could be highly impactful. The application to attrition is a well-chosen example that demonstrates the potential for practical relevance.

### Arguments for Acceptance
1.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper presents novel, technically sound theorems that significantly generalize previous work on recovering queries from data with non-random missingness.
2.  **Important Conceptual Insights:** The distinction between recoverability for probabilistic vs. causal queries is a key finding. The introduction of formal non-recoverability criteria is also a valuable contribution.
3.  **Clear Problem Formulation:** The use of the m-graph framework provides a clear and principled way to reason about complex missing data problems.
4.  **Relevant Application:** The analysis of attrition in randomized trials demonstrates the utility of the framework for a common and difficult problem in experimental science.

### Arguments Against Acceptance
1.  **Limited Practical Applicability:** The core assumption that the exact m-graph is known may not hold in many machine learning settings, limiting the immediate use of the methods.
2.  **Clarity Issues:** Several points of ambiguity, inconsistent notation, and undefined terms (as detailed below) detract from the paper's overall quality and require revision.

### Detailed Comments and Suggested Corrections
*   Section 1, paragraph 1: The phrase "can bias outcomes" is slightly awkward. Consider "can introduce bias" or "can bias estimates."
*   Example 1, last paragraph: The RHS of the final equation should be P(Y|X,Z,R'=0)P(Z|R'=0)..., which simplifies to the expression given, but the intermediate step should be explicit that you are conditioning on R=0. Also, the text states `P(Y|X,Z,R'x, R'y, R'z)`, but the graph has no Rz. Please clarify.
*   Theorem 1: The condition `Y_i ⊥⊥ (R_yi, R_xi)` should probably be `Y_i ⊥⊥ R_yi`, as R_xi is not necessarily defined for all sets Xi. Please check the notation.
*   Theorem 2: The partition notation `R(N)` is used in the text, but the product is over `R(i)`. Also, the definition of `Rpart` uses `Rx` and `Ry` belonging to distinct partitions, but then uses `R(i)` and `R(j)` in the conditions. Please ensure consistency.
*   Corollary 1: The corollary requires a partially observed variable for context, but none is mentioned in the premise. Please clarify, perhaps by stating "Let Y be the only partially observed variable..."
*   Corollary 2, condition (2): Clarify if the intermediate nodes are *substantive variables* in X, or just any variables in X.
*   Theorem 4: The shorthand `Y ⊥w Ry|Z` is non-standard. The definition of d*-separation is `X ⊥w Y |Z`. Please clarify the notation here.
*   Footnote 2 seems to be missing. If Footnote 1 is meant, please correct the numbering.
*   Please provide a formal definition for "neighbor" and "collider path" in the preliminaries.
*   Please clarify the convention regarding the inclusion/omission of V* variables in the m-graphs shown in the figures.
