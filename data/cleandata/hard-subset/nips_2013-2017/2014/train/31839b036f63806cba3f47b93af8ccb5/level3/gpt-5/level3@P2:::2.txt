Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies when probabilistic and causal queries can be consistently estimated (recovered) from data with missing entries, given an explicit graphical model of the missingness process (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors:

- Generalize the sequential factorization approach by introducing “general ordered factorizations” that may include missingness indicators R in the separating sets, enabling recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms interact (edges among R’s).
- Provide a new recoverability result for joint distributions in semi-Markovian models via partitions of R and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)), covering cases with no admissible sequence and with latent confounding. A practical corollary handles models with no bi-directed edges between R and substantive variables.
- Establish graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions, giving necessary conditions that warn when assumptions are insufficient.
- Show that some causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when associated probabilistic quantities (e.g., P(y|x), P(y, x)) are not, particularly when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separated; they leverage do-calculus and dormant independences (d*-separation).
- Provide necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery under simple attrition (|Vm|=1), for both joint distributions and causal effects, and correct common misconceptions in the literature (e.g., Garcia 2013).
The work is situated within causal graphical modeling (Pearl 2009; Shpitser & Pearl 2006) and the missing data literature (Rubin 1976; ML/EM methods, inverse probability weighting, imputation). Compared to the prior NIPS work by Mohan et al. (2013), this paper handles interacting missingness mechanisms and offers explicit non-recoverability criteria and new conditions for causal recovery without separability of Y and Ry.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Theorems are stated precisely with clear graphical conditions (e.g., minimality in ordered factorizations; collider paths; inducing paths). The claim that causal effects can be recoverable despite non-recoverability of related probabilistic factors is well-motivated via do-calculus and dormant independence. Proofs are relegated to the appendix; given the reliance on established tools (ADMGs, d-/d*-separation, do-calculus), the results appear credible.
- Completeness: The paper provides sufficient conditions, non-recoverability criteria, and necessary-and-sufficient results for the single-missing-variable (attrition) case. Examples illustrate how to apply the criteria. Algorithms are described at a high level; more explicit procedural guidance would help practitioners.
- Evaluation: The paper is theoretical; there are illustrative examples but no empirical studies (synthetic or real) to assess finite-sample performance or robustness to m-graph misspecification. This is acceptable for NIPS theory, but some empirical illustration would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
- The manuscript is clearly written, focused, and builds coherently on prior contributions. The use of running examples (e.g., sequential factorization with minimality; attrition) is helpful. Some notation is dense (e.g., Rpart, Mb(R(i)), distinctions among Rw vs Wr); adding a compact notation table and pseudocode for the recoverability checks (Theorem 1 and 2) would improve readability. References contain minor formatting inconsistencies.

Originality
- The general ordered factorization that includes R, the non-recoverability criteria for joint/conditional distributions, and the recovery of causal effects when Y and Ry are inseparable are novel and impactful extensions beyond Mohan et al. (2013). The necessary and sufficient conditions for simple attrition consolidate and clarify a widely encountered special case, correcting misstatements in the literature.

Significance
- The results advance the state of the art in causal analysis with missing data, especially beyond MAR/MCAR, and provide actionable graphical diagnostics for both recoverability and non-recoverability. They are likely to influence subsequent methodological work and inform practice in longitudinal studies, surveys, and observational inference where attrition or nonrandom missingness is prominent.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid theoretical advances with clear, general graphical conditions (sufficient, necessary, and in some cases N&S).
- Addresses practically important settings (interacting missingness mechanisms; attrition).
- Clarifies when causal effects are recoverable even when probabilistic factors are not, leveraging do-calculus/dormant independence.
- Extends prior NIPS work in a substantive and coherent way; well written.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited guidance on automating the proposed checks (e.g., algorithmic steps, complexity) and on selecting Z,W sets in Theorem 4 in practice.
- No empirical validation or simulation studies to illustrate finite-sample behavior, robustness to positivity violations, or sensitivity to m-graph misspecification.
- Assumes correct specification of the m-graph; while tests are cited (Mohan & Pearl 2014), practical elicitation remains challenging.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide pseudocode or an explicit algorithmic checklist for:
  - Constructing admissible sequences (Theorem 1) and verifying minimality.
  - Building partitions R(i) and Mb(R(i)) and checking Theorem 2’s conditions.
  - Finding Z,W satisfying Theorem 4, possibly with references to automated identifiability procedures.
- Add a small-scale synthetic study demonstrating recovery vs non-recovery under the proposed criteria, and illustrating finite-sample effects and positivity issues.
- Discuss robustness to m-graph misspecification and practical strategies for eliciting/validating missingness assumptions.
- Include a concise notation table and normalize reference formatting.
- Offer software or a prototype implementation to perform the graphical checks.

Overall recommendation
This is a strong theoretical contribution that meaningfully advances graphical approaches to missing data and causal inference. Despite the lack of empirical studies, the clarity and breadth of the theoretical results and their practical relevance argue for acceptance.
