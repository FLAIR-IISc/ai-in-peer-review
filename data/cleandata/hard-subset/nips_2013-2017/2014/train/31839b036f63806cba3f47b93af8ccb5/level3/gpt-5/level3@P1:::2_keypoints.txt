- Paper studies recoverability of probabilistic (P(y,x), P(y|x)) and causal (P(y|do(x))) queries under missing data using m-graphs.  
- Generalizes ordered factorization to allow missingness indicators R in admissible sequences (Theorem 1), enabling recovery with interacting missingness mechanisms.  
- Provides semi‑Markovian joint-distribution recovery via R-partitions and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)), yielding a compact product-form estimand (Theorem 2).  
- Derives graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2), filling a literature gap.  
- Shows some causal effects are recoverable even when related probabilistic factors are not, using do-calculus and dormant independences (Example 3, Theorem 4).  
- Applies results to attrition, giving necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering P(V) and P(y|do(x)) with a single partially observed outcome (Theorems 5–6) and correcting prior misconceptions.  
- Builds directly on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) and connects to identification work (Shpitser & Pearl) and classical missing‑data frameworks (Rubin).  
- Complements IPW/MI approaches by providing graphical recoverability tests under MNAR.  
- Technical development is rigorous, with necessary minimality conditions illustrated and proofs provided in the appendix.  
- Theorem 1 cleanly extends prior factorization results to dependent R variables; Theorem 2 offers a compact estimand via R-partitions.  
- Causal recovery results (Example 3, Theorem 4) are insightful, showing do-calculus can bypass probabilistic non-recoverability.  
- Notational issues: a few inconsistencies (e.g., duplicate notation, Rw vs Wr) that could be tightened.  
- Suggestion to add pseudo-code for checking admissible sequences, constructing R-partitions/Mb(R(i)), and applying Theorem 4.  
- Recommend a small table mapping MCAR/MAR/MNAR to graphical conditions to aid readers.  
- Original contributions include allowing R in admissible sequences, semi‑Markovian product-form recovery, and first formal graphical non-recoverability criteria under MNAR.  
- Significant practical impact: helps decide recoverability before estimation and informs design, analysis, and auxiliary variable selection.  
- Weakness: reliance on correct m-graph specification; more guidance on eliciting/validating m-graphs is needed.  
- Weakness: Theorem 4 is stated for a single partially observed variable; extensions to multiple variables would strengthen the work.  
- Weakness: limited algorithmic detail and complexity analysis for the proposed procedures.  
- Weakness: lacks empirical illustrations or synthetic examples demonstrating numerical recovery/failure.  
- Weakness: needs discussion of robustness to positivity violations and m-graph misspecification, and which results are testable from data.  
- Acceptance arguments: clear theoretical advances, constructive estimands, novel non‑recoverability criteria, and practical relevance for MNAR.  
- Rejection arguments: limited empirical/algorithmic instantiation, restricted scope for key causal theorem, and heavy reliance on accurate m-graphs.  
- Overall evaluation: strong theoretical contribution deserving acceptance with minor enhancements in algorithmic presentation and practical guidance.
