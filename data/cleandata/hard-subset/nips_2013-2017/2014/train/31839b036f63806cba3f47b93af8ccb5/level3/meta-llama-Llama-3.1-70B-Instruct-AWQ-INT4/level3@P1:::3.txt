This paper proposes a novel approach to recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data, extending the results of Mohan et al. [2013] to more general conditions. The authors introduce a graphical representation called "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) to model the missingness process and provide conditions for recovering joint and conditional distributions. They also show that some causal queries can be recovered even when the joint distribution is not recoverable.

The paper is technically sound and builds upon earlier work in the field. However, there are several areas that require clarification and improvement. Firstly, the authors assume knowledge of the censoring/missingness process, which may not be available in typical machine learning applications. It would be beneficial to discuss the implications of this assumption and potential ways to relax it.

Additionally, the paper implicitly assumes that the full population is known, which may not always be the case. The authors should explicitly state this assumption and discuss its limitations.

The term "m-graph" is used inconsistently throughout the paper, sometimes including V* variables and sometimes not. The authors should clarify whether V* variables are being omitted and why.

The paper also forbids R variables as parents of V or U, which may be an unnecessary restriction. The authors should justify this restriction or state that it is for convenience.

Furthermore, the term "neighbor" is used without a definition, which may cause confusion for readers unfamiliar with the terminology. The authors should provide a clear definition of this term.

On page 8, the relation to Garcia et al.'s "rectification" needs clarification regarding positivity assumptions. The authors should explain whether Garcia et al. also assume positivity or a stronger condition.

The claim that maximum likelihood yields consistent estimates under MAR is too vague and requires further explanation. The authors should discuss why MLE could or could not be applied to the missingness processes considered in this paper.

Finally, there are several minor errors and corrections that need to be addressed, including wording, figure qualifications, and notation.

Overall, this is an interesting paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of missing data analysis. However, it requires several clarifications and fixes to improve its readability and applicability.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data.
* The authors provide conditions for recovering joint and conditional distributions, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The paper is technically sound and builds upon earlier work in the field.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper assumes knowledge of the censoring/missingness process, which may not be available in typical machine learning applications.
* The paper implicitly assumes that the full population is known, which may not always be the case.
* There are several areas that require clarification and improvement, including the use of the term "m-graph", the restriction on R variables, and the definition of "neighbor".
* The paper requires several minor corrections and fixes to improve its readability and applicability.
