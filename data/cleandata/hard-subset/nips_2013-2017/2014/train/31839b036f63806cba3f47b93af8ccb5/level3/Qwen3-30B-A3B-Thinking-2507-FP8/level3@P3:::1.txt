

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper extends Mohan et al.'s (2013) work on missingness graphs (m-graphs) to address the problem of determining whether probabilistic and causal queries can be recovered from datasets with missing values. The authors build upon the framework of m-graphs, which explicitly represent missingness mechanisms, and provide more general conditions for recoverability of queries such as P(y|x) and P(y|do(x)). Their work differs from prior work (e.g., Rubin's MCAR/MAR framework and Garcia's attrition analysis) by providing graphical conditions for recoverability rather than relying on statistical assumptions.

**Quality** - The technical quality is high. The authors present rigorous theorems with clear proofs (e.g., Theorem 3 on non-recoverability is particularly well-structured and interpretable). The paper demonstrates a deep understanding of graphical models and causal inference. However, some results appear incremental rather than transformative. Theorem 1 (Section 3) is hard to comprehend and feels disconnected from the rest of the paper, closely resembling Richardson (2009) on Markov blankets. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 resemble inverse-probability weighting methods, which are well-established in the missing data literature (e.g., Robins et al., 1994). While the paper's technical content is sound, it doesn't yet reveal a larger unifying picture that would significantly advance the field.

**Clarity** - The paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The notation is inconsistent (e.g., overuse of capital V, Vi as vertex vs Vo as set, illegible subscripts), and key concepts like R^part are poorly defined. Corollary 1 exemplifies poor presentation, and the paper contains numerous grammatical errors ("oftentimes," "R(i) is a partition of R, so Rx and Ry should be stated to 'belong to distinct elements of the partition'"). The flow is poor, with Section 3 feeling orthogonal to the rest of the paper. The 13-page appendix is excessive for a conference paper, suggesting the work would be better suited for a journal. The authors should use \leftrightarrow instead of <--> and avoid casual language.

**Originality** - The paper extends Mohan et al.'s framework but doesn't present a truly novel approach. The most significant contributions appear in Sections 5 and 6, particularly the demonstration that causal queries may be recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not (e.g., Theorem 4). This insight is valuable and original, though it doesn't yet form a complete unifying criterion. The authors should clarify whether Garcia (2013) is wrong or simply using a different framework, as their results contradict Garcia's Theorem 3 regarding simple attrition.

**Significance** - The problem of missing data is highly significant, and the paper addresses an important practical challenge. The results in Sections 5 and 6 are potentially impactful for researchers dealing with missing data in causal inference, particularly showing that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable. However, the presentation issues significantly limit the paper's impact. The authors should clarify the missing definition of graphs formed by severing edges (used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4) and fix the premature references to Vo and Vm.

**Strengths**: The causal query results (Section 6) are particularly strong and address a genuine gap in the literature. The paper provides clear examples (e.g., Example 3) that illustrate how causal queries can be recoverable when probabilistic queries are not. The application to attrition (Section 7) is relevant and well-motivated.

**Weaknesses**: The paper's major weakness is clarity, which significantly hinders its accessibility. The notation is confusing, the flow is poor, and the paper is too long for a conference format. The incremental nature of some results (e.g., Theorem 1) and the lack of a unifying framework diminish the paper's impact.

**Overall Verdict**: This is an important problem with potentially useful results, particularly regarding causal queries. However, the presentation and notation issues significantly mar the paper. The authors should substantially revise the paper to improve clarity, fix notation inconsistencies, and streamline the content for a conference format. While the technical content is strong, the paper currently fails to communicate its contributions effectively. I recommend rejection for NIPS, but with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit to a journal (e.g., JMLR or JASA) with a focus on improving clarity and presentation. The core ideas have merit, but the current form does not meet NIPS's standards for clarity and accessibility.
