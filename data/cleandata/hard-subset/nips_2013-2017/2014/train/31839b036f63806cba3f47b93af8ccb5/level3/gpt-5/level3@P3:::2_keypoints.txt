- Paper extends m-graphs to include R nodes and generalizes ordered factorizations, giving new sufficient conditions for recovering joint and conditional distributions via admissible sequences.
- Introduces a new theorem partitioning R variables using collider-path structure to express P(V) through complete-case terms and completeness probabilities, enabling recovery beyond admissible-sequence cases.
- Presents graphical non-recoverability (necessary) criteria for joint and conditional distributions.
- Analyzes causal queries P(y|do(x)) with missing data, showing causal effects can be recoverable even when related probabilistic factors are not, using do-calculus, dormant independences, and d*-separation.
- Provides necessary-and-sufficient graphical conditions for recoverability under attrition (|Vm|=1), correcting some prior claims.
- Extends Mohan et al. (2013) by allowing dependencies among missingness mechanisms, adding non-recoverability tests, treating inseparable Y–Ry, and giving sharp attrition results.
- Complements classical MAR/MCAR, ML/EM, and IPW approaches by explicitly modeling the missingness mechanism causally.
- Strength: novel theoretical contributions that substantially broaden recoverability beyond prior results, including interacting R variables.
- Strength: principled explicit modeling of missingness clarifies assumptions and goes beyond MAR/MCAR.
- Strength: clear causal advances showing recoverability of causal effects in cases where probabilistic recovery fails.
- Strength: sharp, practically useful attrition results with necessary-and-sufficient conditions.
- Strength: well scoped, organized manuscript with formal proofs and careful linkage to identifiability literature.
- Weakness: no empirical demonstrations or simulations to show practical guidance or behavior under assumption violations.
- Weakness: lacks a full decision procedure or software for algorithmically checking conditions (R partitioning, dormant independence, estimand generation).
- Weakness: relies on strong assumptions (correct m-graph, positivity on complete cases, semi-Markovian), with limited guidance on elicitation or robustness to misspecification.
- Weakness: strongest guarantees mainly for |Vm|=1; unclear how much extends to multiple partially observed variables.
- Quality: technically sound and rigorous; theorems are supported by proofs consistent with do-calculus and identifiability literature.
- Clarity: generally clear with motivating examples; suggests adding algorithmic checklists, a worked Theorem 2 example, and a figure for inseparable Y–Ry recovery.
- Originality: high — interacting missingness mechanisms, non-recoverability tests, and causal recoverability beyond probabilistic limits are novel.
- Significance: high — advances theoretical understanding of MNAR and will influence causal inference and ML practice.
- Detailed request: clarify computational complexity and provide pseudocode for admissible-sequence and Theorem 2 checks on large graphs.
- Detailed request: offer guidance for constructing m-graphs in practice and discuss testable implications.
- Detailed request: discuss robustness when positivity fails or the m-graph is partially misspecified.
- Detailed request: include a small simulation showing predicted non-recoverability bias and causal recovery with inseparable Y–Ry.
- Acceptance pro: substantial, novel theoretical contributions well connected to causal identifiability theory.
- Acceptance con: lack of empirical/software components may limit adoption; some results are specialized and rely on accurate causal modeling.
- Overall recommendation: lean toward acceptance, recommending adding an algorithmic checklist and at least one illustrative experiment or case study.
