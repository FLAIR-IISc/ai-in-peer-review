- Introduces "general ordered factorizations" that allow missingness indicators R in separating sets, enabling recovery when missingness mechanisms interact.  
- Provides a new joint-distribution recovery result in semi‑Markovian models via partitions of R and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)), covering cases with no admissible sequence and with latent confounding, plus a practical corollary for models without bi-directed edges between R and substantive variables.  
- Establishes graphical necessary conditions for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions.  
- Shows some causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when related probabilistic quantities (e.g., P(y|x), P(y,x)) are not, using do‑calculus and dormant independences (d*-separation).  
- Gives necessary-and-sufficient recovery conditions for the single‑missing‑variable (attrition) case for both joint distributions and causal effects, correcting prior misconceptions.  
- Theorems are precise and credible, relying on established tools (ADMGs, d-/d*-separation, do‑calculus); proofs are in the appendix.  
- Paper supplies sufficient conditions, non‑recoverability criteria, N&S attrition results, and illustrative examples, but algorithmic procedures are described only at a high level.  
- The work is theoretical with illustrative examples but lacks empirical/simulation studies to assess finite‑sample performance and robustness to model misspecification.  
- Manuscript is clearly written and coherent with helpful running examples, though notation is dense and would benefit from a compact notation table and pseudocode for key checks.  
- Novel contributions include R‑inclusive ordered factorization, explicit non‑recoverability criteria, and recovery of causal effects when Y and Ry are inseparable; N&S attrition results consolidate and clarify a common special case.  
- Advances graphical approaches to missing data beyond MAR/MCAR and provides actionable diagnostics likely to influence methodology and practice in settings with attrition or nonrandom missingness.  
- Arguments for acceptance: solid theoretical advances with clear graphical conditions, relevance to interacting missingness and attrition, novel causal recovery insights, and substantive extension of prior work.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient automation/algorithmic guidance for the checks, no empirical validation or simulations, and reliance on correct specification of the m‑graph.  
- Suggestions for improvement: add pseudocode/algorithms for Theorems 1–4 (admissible sequences, R partitions, finding Z/W), include a small synthetic study, discuss robustness to m‑graph misspecification and elicitation strategies, supply a notation table and normalized references, and provide software or a prototype implementation.  
- Overall recommendation: strong theoretical contribution with practical relevance; recommend acceptance despite the lack of empirical studies.
