This paper addresses the fundamental problem of deciding whether a given probabilistic or causal query is recoverable from a dataset with missing entries. The authors build upon the framework of "missingness graphs" (m-graphs) introduced in Mohan et al. [2013], which explicitly model the causal mechanisms responsible for missing data. The work extends this prior research by providing more general graphical conditions for the recoverability of both probabilistic queries, such as `P(y|x)` and `P(y, x)`, and causal queries of the form `P(y|do(x))`.

A central and significant contribution of the paper is the demonstration that a causal query may be recoverable even when the underlying joint or conditional distributions involving its variables are not. Specifically, the authors derive graphical conditions under which the causal effect `P(y|do(x))` can be identified, even when the outcome variable `Y` and its corresponding missingness mechanism `Ry` are not d-separable in the m-graph—a condition that typically precludes the recovery of `P(Y)`. The paper also introduces novel graphical criteria for establishing the *non-recoverability* of queries, which is a valuable contribution for practitioners. Finally, these theoretical results are applied to the practical and common problem of attrition in longitudinal studies, providing clear necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects in such settings.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Significance:** The problem of missing data is ubiquitous across all empirical sciences that rely on data, making this work highly relevant to the NIPS community and beyond. The paper provides a principled, model-based approach to handling data that is Missing Not At Random (MNAR), moving beyond the more restrictive and often unrealistic MCAR and MAR assumptions. The key insight—that causal query recoverability can be decoupled from probabilistic query recoverability—is a major conceptual advance that could change how researchers approach causal inference with incomplete data.

2.  **Originality:** The contributions are novel and substantially advance the state of the art. While building on Mohan et al. [2013], this paper offers significant extensions:
    *   Theorem 1 generalizes the sequential factorization method to handle dependencies between missingness mechanisms, a common scenario in longitudinal studies.
    *   Theorems 2 and 3 provide new, powerful conditions for recoverability and, importantly, non-recoverability in cases where sequential factorization fails. The use of collider paths and Markov blankets in this context is novel.
    *   Theorem 4 formally characterizes the conditions under which a causal effect can be recovered despite the non-recoverability of the corresponding joint distribution. This is a powerful and non-obvious result.
    *   The application to attrition corrects and clarifies previous results in the literature (e.g., Garcia [2013]).

3.  **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of theoretical work. The claims are well-defined and supported by formal theorems, with proofs provided in the appendix. The authors are careful in their definitions and the step-by-step development of their arguments is logical and convincing. The examples provided throughout the text (e.g., Example 3) are instrumental in illustrating the core theoretical concepts and their practical implications, making complex ideas more accessible.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins with a clear review of the necessary background on m-graphs and recoverability before methodically presenting its new contributions. The structure allows the reader to follow a coherent path from probabilistic recovery to the more nuanced topic of causal recovery. The graphical models are used effectively to support the text, and the notation is consistent.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for Improvement):**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong on their own, the work would be significantly strengthened by including experiments on synthetic or real-world data. Such experiments could demonstrate the practical application of the proposed recovery algorithms, illustrate the biases that arise when non-recoverable queries are naively estimated, and show how the proposed methods successfully correct for this bias. This would also provide insight into the finite-sample performance of the proposed estimators.

2.  **Assumption of a Correctly Specified Model:** The entire framework relies on the assumption that the practitioner possesses a correctly specified m-graph. This is a strong assumption. While the paper references other work on testing m-graphs (Mohan and Pearl [2014]), a brief discussion in the main text regarding the sensitivity of the recoverability conditions to model misspecification would be valuable. For instance, how robust are the conclusions if a weak dependency is missed or a non-existent one is added?

3.  **Computational Complexity:** The paper presents conditions and formulas for recovery but does not discuss the computational complexity of the required steps. For instance, finding an "admissible sequence" for Theorem 1 or identifying the relevant partitions and Markov blankets for Theorem 2 could be computationally challenging for large, dense graphs. A brief discussion on the algorithmic complexity would be a useful addition for practitioners considering these methods.

**Summary:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the theory of causal inference with missing data. Its key findings, particularly regarding the recoverability of causal effects in MNAR settings, are important and advance the field. The paper is technically solid and clearly written. The main weakness is the absence of empirical validation, but the strength of the theoretical results makes it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
