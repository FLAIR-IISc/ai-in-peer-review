## Review: Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data

This paper tackles the important problem of identifiability of probabilistic and causal queries under missing data, extending the framework of Mohan et al. [2013]. The authors present graphical conditions for recovering queries, demonstrating that causal effects can be identifiable even when probabilistic relationships are not. A key strength lies in showing recoverability of P(y|do(x)) even when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable, a result with practical implications, particularly in attrition scenarios. Theorems 3 and 4, particularly Theorem 3’s clarity, provide interpretable conditions for non-recoverability and causal query recovery, respectively. The application to attrition in Section 7, and the discussion of how this differs from Garcia [2013], are valuable contributions.

However, the paper suffers significantly from issues of clarity and presentation. While the technical quality is high – the results appear correct and are involved – the sheer volume of material (13-page appendix!) feels excessive for a conference paper. The flow is often disrupted by dense text and a lack of intuitive explanation. Notation is a major weakness: the overuse of ‘V’ and inconsistent use of subscripts (e.g., Vo vs. Vi) create confusion. Corollary 1 is a prime example of poor presentation. Section 3 feels somewhat orthogonal to the main thrust of the paper, and Theorem 1 is difficult to grasp, closely resembling Richardson [2009] without sufficient acknowledgement. 

The results, while valuable, are somewhat piecemeal. The paper would benefit from striving for a more general identifiability criterion, analogous to those used in causal effect identification, rather than a series of theorems addressing specific cases. The connection to inverse-probability weighting (Theorem 2, Corollary 1) and conditioning on Markov blankets should be explicitly discussed, referencing Richardson [2003, 2009]. A minor correction: R^part is a partition, so Rx and Ry should belong to distinct elements of the partition. Language should be refined (avoiding “oftentimes”) and standard notation (\leftrightarrow) used. The authors should clarify whether Garcia [2013] is genuinely incorrect or operates within a different framework. Finally, the notation for graphs formed by severing edges (used in Definitions 4 and 4) requires definition. Vo and Vm are referenced before being defined.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Important problem, useful approach, potentially significant results in Sections 5 & 6, and addresses a gap in the literature regarding causal query recovery under missingness.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** Clarity issues, excessive length, confusing notation, incremental results, and Section 3’s lack of integration.

**Verdict:** Reject for this conference, but strongly encourage submission to a journal after substantial revision addressing the clarity and presentation issues. The core ideas are valuable, but currently obscured by the paper’s execution.
