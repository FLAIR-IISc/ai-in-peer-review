

This paper extends Mohan et al. (2013) by developing graphical conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data using "missingness graphs" (m-graphs). It addresses a critical gap in missing data literature by providing formal criteria for when causal effects (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recovered even when the underlying probabilistic distributions (e.g., P(y,x)) are not recoverable—a key insight distinguishing it from prior work like Garcia (2013). The paper builds on Mohan et al.’s framework, generalizing sequential factorization (Section 3) and introducing novel non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2), while applying results to attrition problems (Section 7). It relates to Richardson (2003) in its use of Markov blankets and to Shpitser & Pearl (2008) via d*-separation.

**Strengths:**  
- **Quality:** The technical content is rigorous and well-supported. Theorems (e.g., Theorem 3, Theorem 4) are clearly stated, and proofs (in the appendix) appear sound. The causal recovery result (Example 3, Theorem 4) is particularly significant, demonstrating that causal queries may be recoverable despite probabilistic non-recoverability—a novel contribution with practical implications for fields like clinical trials.  
- **Significance:** Sections 5–6 address a major unresolved problem: causal identifiability under MNAR when standard probabilistic recovery fails. The attrition analysis (Section 7) corrects Garcia (2013) by showing X need not be an ancestor of Ry for non-recoverability (Figure 4a). This advances the field by providing actionable graphical criteria for real-world missing-data scenarios.  
- **Originality:** The non-recoverability conditions (Theorem 3) and causal recovery under inseparability (Theorem 4) are new. While Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 resemble inverse-probability weighting, their graphical formulation is novel and extends Mohan et al.’s work beyond MAR/MCAR settings.

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Clarity:** The paper’s presentation severely undermines its impact. Notation is inconsistent (e.g., V, V*, Vo, Vm introduced late and ambiguously; "R^part" should specify "partition elements" instead of "R(i)"). Section 3 (sequential factorization) is disjoint from the rest and overly dense, with minimal intuition. The appendix (13 pages) is excessive for NIPS; key results (e.g., Theorem 2) could be condensed.  
- **Structure:** The flow is disjointed. Section 3’s "general ordered factorization" feels like an add-on, not a unifying principle. The claim that Theorem 1 "extends Mohan et al." lacks justification for why this specific extension is necessary.  
- **Incrementalism:** While Sections 5–6 are strong, the paper does not synthesize a *unifying identifiability criterion* (e.g., analogous to causal effect identification). Theorem 1 and Corollary 1 are largely technical extensions without a broader framework, reducing significance.  
- **Critical Omissions:** The definition of "G_X" (used in Theorem 4) is missing. Garcia (2013)’s error is not clearly contextualized (is it a framework difference, or a substantive error?).

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:*  
- Groundbreaking result: Causal effects recoverable even when Y and Ry are inseparable (Theorem 4, Example 3).  
- First formal non-recoverability criteria for joint/conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2).  
- Directly corrects prior work (Garcia 2013) with actionable graphical conditions.  
*Con:*  
- Overly dense presentation obscures key contributions (e.g., Section 3’s complexity).  
- Notation and structure hinder reproducibility and adoption.  
- Appendix too lengthy; NIPS expects concise, self-contained arguments.

**Recommendation:** **Borderline (weak reject)**. The paper tackles an important problem with technically sound contributions (Sections 5–6), but its presentation flaws prevent it from meeting NIPS’s quality and clarity standards. The core insights are valuable, but the current form would confuse even experts. If the authors substantially restructure the paper—streamlining notation, moving Section 3 to appendix, clarifying the unifying goal (e.g., "a general criterion for causal recoverability"), and condensing the appendix—it could be a strong acceptance. As-is, the clarity issues outweigh the technical merit for a conference venue.

**Specific Suggestions:**  
1. Define Vo, Vm, V*, and G_X *before* their first use (Section 2).  
2. Replace "R^part" with "a partition of R into sets R(1), R(2),..." and clarify "R(i) ∩ R_Mb(R(i))" as "R(i) ∩ R_{Mb(R(i))}".  
3. Merge Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 into a single unified result, emphasizing their connection to Markov blankets (Richardson 2003).  
4. Remove Section 3’s "general ordered factorization" as a standalone contribution; integrate its essence into Theorem 1’s motivation.  
5. Replace "Ry" with "R_y" for readability and avoid "d-separable" (use "d-separated" consistently).  

This work has high potential but requires significant polishing to match NIPS’s standards. The authors should prioritize clarity and narrative cohesion over incremental technical extensions.
