### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of identifiability for probabilistic and causal queries in the presence of missing data. Building directly on the "m-graph" framework introduced in Mohan et al. (2013), the authors derive graphical conditions for recovering quantities like P(y|x), P(y, x), and the causal effect P(y|do(x)) under various missingness mechanisms, including those that are Missing Not At Random (MNAR).

The main contributions are: (1) An extension of the sequential factorization theorem to handle dependencies between missingness indicators (R-variables). (2) A new theorem for recovering joint distributions when sequential factorization fails, based on partitioning R-variables and their Markov blankets. (3) Novel graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, providing necessary conditions that must be met for a query to be estimable. (4) A significant finding that causal queries P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y|x)) are not, a situation that arises when a variable is not separable from its own missingness mechanism. The authors apply these results to the specific problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

### **Qualitative Evaluation**

#### **Quality**

The technical quality of the paper is high. The theoretical results are involved and, upon inspection, appear to be correct. The proofs provided in the lengthy appendix seem rigorous. Theorem 3, which provides simple graphical criteria for non-recoverability (e.g., a variable Y cannot be a neighbor of its missingness indicator Ry), is particularly clear, interpretable, and useful. The work is a complete piece of theoretical research, not a position paper, with well-supported claims.

#### **Clarity**

This is the paper's most significant weakness. The manuscript is exceptionally dense and attempts to cover too much material for a conference paper, making it very difficult to follow. The flow is disjointed; for instance, Section 3 feels orthogonal to the main narrative, and its core result, Theorem 1, is hard to comprehend. The paper's quality improves noticeably in Sections 5 and 6, where the questions asked are clearer and the answers are more direct. The massive 13-page appendix is a strong indicator that this work would be better suited for a journal submission where the ideas could be developed more carefully and with more space.

The notation is a major barrier to understanding. It is often confusing and inconsistent. For example:
*   The symbol 'V' is overused for the set of all substantive variables, a specific vertex, and in subscripts. The distinction between a vertex Vi and a set of observed variables Vo is notationally awkward.
*   Corollary 1 is a prime example of poor presentation; the formula is dense and the context is insufficient, making it nearly impossible to parse without significant effort.
*   Subscripts are often illegible or used without clear definition.

#### **Originality**

The paper is an extension of Mohan et al. (2013), and as such, some results are incremental improvements on that work. For instance, the generalization of the sequential factorization theorem is a useful but not a fundamental leap. However, the results presented in Sections 5 and 6 are highly original and significant. The graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) are new and provide practical tools for researchers. Most importantly, the demonstration that causal queries can be identified even when their constituent probabilistic parts are not is a novel and powerful insight that clearly distinguishes this work from prior literature.

#### **Significance**

The problem of identifying causal and probabilistic queries from data with non-random missingness is of great practical and theoretical importance. The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing new graphical tools for this task. The results in Sections 5 and 6 are particularly significant and are likely to be used and built upon by researchers in causality and missing data. The finding about the recoverability of causal queries opens up a new avenue of investigation. However, the paper's poor clarity may unfortunately limit its immediate impact. The collection of results feels somewhat piecemeal; a more significant contribution would be a general, complete criterion for recoverability, analogous to the completeness of the do-calculus for causal effect identification.

### **Arguments For/Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Addresses an important, challenging problem at the intersection of graphical models, causality, and missing data.
2.  The technical contributions, especially in Sections 5 and 6, are novel, significant, and appear to be correct. The insight about causal vs. probabilistic query recovery is a major strength.
3.  Provides useful, concrete graphical criteria (e.g., Theorem 3) that can be readily applied.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The clarity of the paper is extremely poor. It is dense, badly organized, and uses confusing notation, making it inaccessible to a broad NIPS audience.
2.  The paper feels overstuffed with material and would be much stronger as a longer journal article. The current presentation does not do justice to the technical content.
3.  Some of the results (e.g., Section 3) are incremental and detract from the main, more exciting contributions.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Structure and Focus:** For a conference version, I would strongly recommend focusing the paper on the most novel and impactful results: the non-recoverability criteria (Section 5) and the recovery of causal queries (Section 6). Sections 3 and 4 could be moved to the appendix or saved for a journal version to improve flow and clarity.
*   **Notation:** The notation needs a complete overhaul. Please reconsider the use of 'V', 'Vi', 'Vo', etc., to make them distinct and intuitive. Define all terms clearly before use (e.g., Vo and Vm are referenced in the abstract and intro before being defined in Section 2).
*   **Clarity of Theorems:** Theorem 1 is very difficult to parse. An intuitive explanation and a simpler running example would be beneficial. The connection to Richardson (2009), which seems close, should be made explicit.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 have a strong resemblance to inverse-probability weighting (IPW) schemes. The conditioning on the Markov blanket of the R-variables seems related to constructing the weights. This connection should be discussed, perhaps referencing work like Richardson (2003, 2009) on Markov properties of mixed graphs.
*   **Specific Corrections:**
    *   In the definition of R^part (Section 4), the condition should state that Rx and Ry "belong to distinct elements of the partition."
    *   The notation for graph manipulation (severing edges, e.g., G_X) used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4 is standard in causality but is not defined in this paper. Please add a definition.
    *   The paper claims Garcia (2013) is incorrect. This is a strong claim. Please clarify whether the result is genuinely wrong or if Garcia is simply operating under a different set of assumptions or a different framework. The current wording is ambiguous.
    *   Please replace casual language like "oftentimes" with more formal alternatives.
    *   Please use standard LaTeX for symbols, e.g., `\leftrightarrow` instead of `<-->`.

Overall, this paper contains important ideas and results, but its potential is severely hampered by major presentation issues. A thorough revision focused on clarity would turn this into an excellent contribution.
