This paper, "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data," tackles the problem of deciding whether a causal or probabilistic query is estimable from data corrupted by missing entries. The authors extend the results of Mohan et al. [2013] by presenting more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries and causal queries.

The paper's technical quality is high, and the results are involved and appear correct. Theorem 3, which provides necessary conditions for the recoverability of the joint distribution, is clear and interpretable. The authors also provide useful examples, such as the recoverability of causal queries when conditional distributions are unavailable.

However, some results are incremental and do not yet reveal a larger unifying picture. Section 3 feels orthogonal to the rest of the paper, and Theorem 1 is hard to comprehend. Additionally, the paper's clarity is its main weakness. The text is dense, and the flow is poor, making it difficult to follow. The notation is also confusing and inconsistent, which detracts from the overall quality of the paper.

Despite these weaknesses, the paper has some significant contributions. Sections 5 and 6 are particularly noteworthy, as they provide clear answers and interesting causal-query results. The authors also demonstrate the applicability of their results to problems of attrition, which is an important area of research.

To improve the paper, the authors should aim for a general identifiability criterion, rather than piecemeal results like Theorem 1. They should also clarify the relationship between their work and previous research, such as Richardson [2003, 2009]. Additionally, the authors should address the notation issues and provide a clearer and more concise presentation of their results.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with major revisions to address the issues mentioned above.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles an important problem in the field of graphical models and missing data.
* The technical quality of the paper is high, and the results are involved and appear correct.
* The authors provide useful examples and demonstrate the applicability of their results to real-world problems.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

* The paper's clarity is its main weakness, making it difficult to follow.
* Some results are incremental and do not yet reveal a larger unifying picture.
* The notation is confusing and inconsistent, which detracts from the overall quality of the paper.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

* Aim for a general identifiability criterion, rather than piecemeal results like Theorem 1.
* Clarify the relationship between the paper's results and previous research, such as Richardson [2003, 2009].
* Address the notation issues and provide a clearer and more concise presentation of the results.
* Consider revising the paper to improve its clarity and flow.
