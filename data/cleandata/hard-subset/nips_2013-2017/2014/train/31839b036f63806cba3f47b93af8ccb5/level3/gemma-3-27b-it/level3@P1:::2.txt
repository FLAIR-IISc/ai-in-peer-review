This paper addresses the critical problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing data, extending the work of Mohan et al. [2013]. The authors present refined and generalized conditions for recoverability, focusing particularly on causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)).  A key contribution lies in demonstrating that causal effects can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations (e.g., P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are not, a result with significant implications for practical applications. The work utilizes and builds upon the framework of “Missingness Graphs” (m-graphs) to formally represent and analyze the missing data process.  This approach is grounded in the well-established field of causal inference, leveraging concepts such as d-separation and the do-calculus. Related work in handling missing data, including deletion methods, maximum likelihood estimation, and inverse probability weighting, is discussed; however, the paper positions itself as advancing beyond these approaches through the explicit modeling of the missingness mechanism.

The paper’s strengths are considerable. First, the technical presentation is rigorous and thorough.  The authors carefully define key concepts – recoverability, admissible sequences, d*-separation – and build their theoretical results upon these foundations. The multiple theorems (1-6) and corollaries provide a detailed and nuanced understanding of the conditions under which recovery is possible.  Second, the introduction of m-graphs as a formal representation for missingness is a valuable contribution. This allows for a clear and unambiguous communication of assumptions about the missing data process. Third, the paper goes beyond simply identifying conditions for recovery, also providing criteria for *non*-recoverability, which is crucial for practitioners to understand the limitations of their analyses. Finally, the application of these results to the specific problem of attrition, particularly in longitudinal studies, demonstrates the practical relevance of the work. The examples provided (Figures 1-4) effectively illustrate the theoretical concepts and their application to real-world scenarios.

However, the paper isn’t without minor weaknesses.  The notation is somewhat dense and can be challenging to follow, particularly for readers less familiar with causal inference. While the authors aim for completeness, the sheer number of theorems and definitions may make it difficult to grasp the overall message without careful study. Further, while the paper acknowledges related work, the discussion on recent advancements in missing data imputation techniques could be more comprehensive, especially given their widespread use in practice. The reliance on the semi-Markovian model assumption might also be a limitation, though the authors do address a broader class of problems than prior work. While the paper makes a compelling case for the advantage of recovering causal effects even when probabilistic relationships are unrecoverable, the practical benefits are not fully explored, and an empirical demonstration would have strengthened the arguments. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The results regarding the recoverability of causal queries when underlying probabilistic relations are not recoverable are original and significant.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper is built on a solid theoretical foundation, with rigorous proofs and well-defined concepts.
*   **Clarity:** Despite some notational complexity, the paper is generally well-organized and clearly written.
*   **Significance:** The findings have the potential to impact how researchers approach causal inference with incomplete data, particularly in fields where missingness is prevalent.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Notational Density:** The extensive notation might present a barrier to entry for some readers.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The lack of empirical validation weakens the claims about practical benefits.
*   **Assumption of Semi-Markovian Model:** The reliance on a specific model class might limit the applicability of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference and missing data analysis. The technical contributions are significant, the presentation is generally clear, and the results have the potential to be highly impactful. While minor weaknesses exist, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve the accessibility of the notation and consider adding a discussion of potential empirical validation strategies.
