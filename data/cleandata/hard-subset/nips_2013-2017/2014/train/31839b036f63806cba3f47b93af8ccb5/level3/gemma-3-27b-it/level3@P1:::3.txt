This paper investigates the problem of identifying probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values, given a model of the missingness process represented by ‘Missingness Graphs’ (m-graphs). The authors extend previous work by Mohan et al. (2013) to provide more general conditions for recovering queries like P(y|x), P(y,x), and P(y|do(x)). A key contribution is demonstrating that causal effects can be identified even when the underlying probabilistic relationships are not, and when Y is not d-separable from its missingness mechanism. The work culminates in applying these results to the specific problem of attrition.

The paper builds directly on the foundation laid by Mohan et al. (2013) and Pearl & Mohan (2013), formalizing the notion of ‘recoverability’ – whether consistent estimates of queries can be obtained despite missing data. It also relates to broader literature on handling missing data, including maximum likelihood estimation (Dempster et al., 1977), inverse probability weighting (Robins et al., 1994), and multiple imputation (Little & Rubin, 2002).  However, the unique aspect is the explicit modeling of the missingness process itself via m-graphs and the derivation of graphical criteria for identification.  Recent work leveraging graphical models for missing data (Daniel et al., 2012; Garcia, 2013) is acknowledged, but the authors differentiate their work by explicitly considering interactions among multiple missingness mechanisms.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical framework for analyzing recoverability, building upon existing causal inference techniques. The extension of the sequential factorization theorem and the derivation of new graphical criteria are mathematically sound and potentially impactful.
* **Novelty:** The demonstration that causal queries can be identifiable even when probabilistic ones aren't is a significant finding. This challenges the intuitive assumption that identification of causal effects necessitates identification of underlying probabilities.  The handling of cases where Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable is also novel.
* **Clear Formalization:** The use of m-graphs provides a clear and unambiguous way to represent the missingness process, facilitating communication and precise reasoning about assumptions.
* **Practical Application:**  The application of the results to attrition, a common problem in observational studies, strengthens the relevance of the work and provides a concrete illustration of its utility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions and Applicability:** The paper relies heavily on knowing the missingness process (and being able to represent it as an m-graph). In many real-world machine learning scenarios, this information is unavailable or difficult to ascertain.  Expanding the discussion to acknowledge this limitation and exploring the sensitivity of the results to misspecification of the m-graph would be beneficial.  The initial assumption that the full population is known (even entirely unobserved individuals) feels restrictive.
* **Inconsistency and Clarity:**  The use of “m-graph” is inconsistent throughout the paper; sometimes including V* variables, sometimes not.  The authors need to clarify whether V* variables are being omitted for simplicity and, if so, why.  Similarly, the term "neighbor" is used without a definition.
* **Restrictive Constraints:**  The prohibition of R variables as parents of V or U is a significant restriction. While the authors might justify this as simplifying the analysis, the rationale should be explicitly stated. Given that R variables are treated as actual quantities, a justification or relaxation of this constraint would broaden the applicability.
* **Related Work Discussion:** The discussion of Garcia (2013) lacks nuance. A clarification regarding positivity assumptions and whether Garcia et al. also require them is crucial. The statement regarding maximum likelihood estimates under MAR also requires elaboration – while generally true, its applicability to the specific missingness processes considered here needs to be addressed.
* **Minor Errors:** Several minor errors (e.g., wording in the introduction, errors in equations, notation inconsistencies) detract from the polish of the paper and require correction.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of causal inference and missing data.
* The results have the potential to be impactful in situations where the missingness mechanism is well-understood.
* The use of m-graphs provides a valuable framework for reasoning about missing data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The strong assumptions about knowing the missingness process limit the practical applicability of the work in many real-world scenarios.
* The paper suffers from some inconsistencies and lacks clarity in certain areas.
* The related work discussion could be more thorough and nuanced.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is an interesting and technically sound paper that extends existing work on recovering causal and probabilistic queries from missing data.  However, the strong assumptions and minor issues with clarity and precision prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form.  I recommend **rejection with request for revision**.  The authors should address the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding the assumptions, clarity, and related work discussion. A more detailed exploration of the applicability of the results to more realistic scenarios would also strengthen the paper significantly.  Specifically, addressing how sensitive the results are to misspecification of the m-graph would be helpful.
