This paper presents novel necessary and sufficient conditions for determining when a causal query is identifiable from data with missing observations, given a model of the missing-data generating mechanism. The authors extend prior work by explicitly incorporating the missing-data generating mechanism into the causal diagram, thereby offering additional theoretical insights that meaningfully advance the state of the art in causal analysis with missing data.

The authors begin by introducing the concept of "missingness graphs" (m-graphs), which are graphical representations that explicitly portray the missingness process as well as the dependencies among variables in the available dataset. They then present a refined sequential factorization theorem that extends the applicability of the theorem to a wider range of problems in which missingness mechanisms may influence each other. The authors also introduce new graphical criteria that preclude recoverability of joint and conditional distributions.

One of the key contributions of this paper is the demonstration that causal queries may be recoverable even when the factors in their identifying estimands are not recoverable. Specifically, the authors derive graphical conditions for recovering causal effects of the form P(y|do(x)) when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable. They also apply their results to problems of attrition and characterize the recovery of causal effects from data corrupted by attrition.
