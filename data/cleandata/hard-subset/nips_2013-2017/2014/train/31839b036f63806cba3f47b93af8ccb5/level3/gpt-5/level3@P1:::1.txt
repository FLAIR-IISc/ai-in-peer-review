This paper studies when probabilistic queries P(y, x), P(y|x), and causal queries P(y|do(x)) are recoverable from data with missing entries, given a causal model of the missingness process (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (2013), the authors (i) generalize the sequential/ordered factorization approach to allow R-nodes (missingness indicators) to appear in separating sets (Theorem 1), addressing settings where missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) give conditions for recovery of joint distributions in the absence of an admissible sequence by partitioning R into components and conditioning on Markov blankets Mb(R(i)) (Theorem 2, Corollary 1); (iii) provide graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2); (iv) show that some causal effects are recoverable even when associated probabilistic relations (e.g., P(y, x), P(y|x)) are not, leveraging dormant independences via do-calculus and d*-separation (Theorem 4); and (v) apply these results to attrition, giving necessary and sufficient conditions for joint and causal recoverability when |Vm|=1 (Theorems 5–6). The results relate to earlier work on MAR/MNAR, do-calculus (Pearl), and ADMG Markov properties (e.g., Richardson 2003 and subsequent work on ADMGs), and help move beyond graph-blind assumptions.

Quality. The technical development is substantial and careful overall. The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) are clear and interpretable. The attrition results (Theorems 5–6) are crisp and practically relevant. The central conceptual contribution—that causal effects may be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic factors are not—is well-illustrated and sound, using do-calculus and dormant independences. That said, Theorem 1 is difficult to parse, and Section 3’s general ordered factorization feels less integrated with the later, cleaner conditions. The claims about Garcia (2013) require clearer reconciliation of assumptions.

Clarity. The paper is dense, with heavy notation and many case-wise results. Flow suffers, and several definitions/notations are introduced late or inconsistently. The exposition improves markedly in Sections 5–6 where the answers are more definitive. The 13-page appendix suggests this may be better suited to a journal paper. Notational issues (e.g., overloading V, illegible subscripts, mixing node vs. set notation) add friction.

Originality. Extending recoverability to interactive missingness mechanisms and, especially, establishing recoverability of causal effects despite inseparability of Y and Ry, is novel and important for MNAR regimes. The non-recoverability criteria fill a gap in a literature dominated by sufficiency results. Some parts (Theorem 2/Corollary 1) resemble known inverse-probability weighting rationales and conditioning on Markov blankets in mixed graphs; positioning relative to ADMG theory (e.g., Richardson 2003 and later work on factorization/Markov blankets) would help sharpen novelty.

Significance. Missing data is pervasive, and principled graphical criteria for (non-)recoverability are valuable for both theory and practice. The attrition application and examples are useful. A more unified identifiability criterion for missing-data recoverability (analogous to complete ID algorithms for interventions) would amplify impact.

Specific feedback and suggestions:
- Unification: Aim for a general decision procedure for P(y|do(x)) and P(V) under m-graphs (even if partial), rather than multiple piecemeal theorems. Can Theorems 1–3 and 5–6 be subsumed by a single graphical criterion or algorithm?
- Section 3: Clarify the role and scope of Theorem 1 vis-à-vis Theorem 2. Provide a compact, self-contained definition of “admissible sequence,” with a small running example and an explicit procedure to test admissibility. Explicitly state minimality and why chain-rule factorizations fail.
- Relation to ADMGs: Make explicit connections to ADMG Markov properties and factorization/Markov blanket results (Richardson 2003; see also subsequent ADMG factorization work). The expression in Theorem 2/Corollary 1 resembles IPW; cite and contrast with IPW and with ADMG conditioning on Markov blankets.
- Non-recoverability: Theorem 3 and Corollary 2 are a strength; add a brief intuition and a figure highlighting collider-path failures.
- Causal recoverability: For Theorem 4, define the graph-surgery notation used (e.g., G_W, edge deletions) in the main text before use. Provide a schematic checklist for applying the theorem.
- Attrition vs. Garcia (2013): Clarify whether the discrepancy stems from different graphical assumptions (e.g., allowing collider paths/inducing paths) or from a mistake; avoid categorical “rectify prevailing opinion” language unless frameworks are aligned. A table contrasting assumptions would help.
- Notation and language: Define Vo and Vm before first use; maintain consistent fonts and subscripts; avoid casual terms (“oftentimes”); use ↔ instead of <–> for bidirected edges; distinguish Vi as a node versus V as a set; ensure legibility of R-subscript notation. Corollary 1’s presentation should be simplified and typeset carefully.
- Precise correction: Since R^part is a partition of R, rephrase “for Rx and Ry belonging to distinct partitions” as “belonging to distinct elements of the partition.”
- Examples: Add 1–2 end-to-end recoverability/non-recoverability walkthroughs (with numeric sketches) to anchor the theory.
- Scope: Briefly discuss computational aspects (testing conditions, search over separating sets) and robustness to mild positivity violations.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, under-explored problem (recoverability under MNAR with interacting mechanisms).
- Clear, useful non-recoverability criteria.
- Novel and insightful causal recoverability results (Sections 6, 7).
- Practical application to attrition with necessary and sufficient conditions.

Cons for acceptance:
- Presentation and notation issues significantly hinder readability.
- Fragmented narrative; Section 3 feels orthogonal and Theorem 1 is hard to digest.
- Incremental feel in parts; stronger unifying criterion/algorithm would increase impact.
- Some claims vs. prior work (Garcia 2013) need careful alignment of assumptions.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The contribution is promising and likely impactful with improved organization and a more unified treatment, but the current presentation and piecemeal structure make it hard to assess and apply. A revised version that tightens the narrative and clarifies connections to ADMGs/IPW and prior attrition work would be strong.
