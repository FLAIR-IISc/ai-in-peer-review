## Review of “Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data”

This paper addresses the critical problem of causal and probabilistic query estimation under missing data, building upon the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. The core contribution lies in providing more general and refined conditions for query recoverability, explicitly incorporating the missing data mechanism into the causal diagram. Specifically, the authors demonstrate that causal queries (P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic factors are not, a significant advancement over prior work.

The paper extends the sequential factorization theorem from Mohan et al. [2013] to handle more complex missingness patterns and introduces new graphical criteria to identify non-recoverable queries. Importantly, it tackles the challenging scenario where a variable and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable, demonstrating recoverability of causal effects under such circumstances. The application of these results to the specific problem of attrition is a practical and valuable illustration of the method’s utility. 

The strengths of this work are readily apparent. The paper is technically sound, with clear theoretical derivations and logical progression of arguments. The writing is focused and the paper coherently builds upon existing literature, appropriately referencing prior work including recent advancements in handling missing data. The results meaningfully advance the state-of-the-art by providing nuanced conditions for causal inference in the presence of missingness. The inclusion of theorems and corollaries, accompanied by illustrative examples, greatly enhances clarity.

However, a potential weakness lies in the somewhat dense notation and mathematical formalism. While rigorous, the presentation could benefit from more intuitive explanations of some of the concepts, particularly for readers less familiar with causal inference and graphical models. While the paper demonstrates applicability to attrition, exploring other real-world examples beyond this domain would further strengthen the impact. Additionally, a discussion on the computational complexity of determining recoverability based on the proposed criteria could be valuable. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and significant theoretical contributions to the field of causal inference with missing data.
*   Clear extension of existing work, providing more general and applicable results.
*   Strong technical foundation and rigorous proofs.
*   Practical application to a relevant problem (attrition).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Dense notation might limit accessibility for some readers.
*   Limited exploration of real-world applications beyond attrition.
*   Lack of discussion regarding the computational tractability of recoverability determination.

**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical advancements are significant, and the presentation is clear despite the inherent complexity of the topic. I recommend acceptance.
