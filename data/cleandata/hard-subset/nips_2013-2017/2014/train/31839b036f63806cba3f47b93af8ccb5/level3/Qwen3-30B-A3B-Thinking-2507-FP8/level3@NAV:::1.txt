

This paper extends Mohan et al.'s work on missingness graphs to address identifiability of probabilistic and causal queries under various missing-data mechanisms. The authors provide graphical conditions for recovering joint/conditional distributions and demonstrate that causal queries P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable—a significant insight that challenges conventional wisdom. The paper builds upon Mohan et al. (2013) and Richardson (2003, 2009), particularly in Section 6 where they establish conditions for causal recoverability beyond probabilistic recoverability.

The technical quality is high, with rigorous proofs and correct results. Theorem 3 (non-recoverability criteria) is particularly clear and interpretable, and Sections 5-6 present the strongest contributions with useful examples. The paper addresses an important problem in missing data analysis, with potential impact for causal inference practitioners.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The notation is inconsistent (e.g., overuse of V, Vi vs. Vo, illegible subscripts), and Section 3 feels disconnected from the rest of the paper. Theorem 1 and Corollary 1 present confusing notation (e.g., R^part not properly defined as a partition of R) and could be better motivated. The paper attempts to cover too much for a conference format—13-page appendix, dense text, and poor flow suggest it would be better suited for a journal. Some results (e.g., Theorem 1) appear incremental and lack a unifying framework, while the relation to Garcia (2013) needs clarification.

The main strengths are the technical soundness, the novel causal recoverability result in Section 6, and the useful applications to attrition problems. The weaknesses are primarily presentation-related: confusing notation, poor organization, and excessive detail for a conference paper. The authors should focus on creating a more cohesive narrative and simplifying the presentation.

Pro: Important problem, technically sound, significant causal recoverability result in Section 6. Con: Poor presentation, confusing notation, excessive length, incremental results in some sections. The paper's contribution is valuable but is significantly obscured by its presentation, making it difficult to assess the full merit of the work. For a conference venue, it would require substantial revision to improve clarity and focus.
