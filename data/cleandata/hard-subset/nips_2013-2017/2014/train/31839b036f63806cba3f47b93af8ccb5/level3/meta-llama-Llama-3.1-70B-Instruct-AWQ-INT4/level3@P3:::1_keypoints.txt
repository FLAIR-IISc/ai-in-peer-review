- Addresses estimability of causal and probabilistic queries under missing data, extending Mohan et al. (2013).  
- Presents more general recovery conditions; technical quality is high and results appear correct.  
- Uses Mohan et al.'s framework to handle various missing-data mechanisms, improving some existing results and giving useful examples.  
- Demonstrates that causal queries can be recoverable even when associated probabilistic relations are not; Theorem 3 gives a clear non-recoverability condition for joint distributions.  
- Some results are incremental and lack a unifying picture; Section 3 feels orthogonal to the rest of the paper.  
- Theorem 1 is hard to comprehend and closely related to Richardson (2009).  
- Sections 5 and 6 are stronger, providing clear answers and interesting causal-query results.  
- Main weakness is clarity: dense text, poor flow, and confusing, inconsistent notation (e.g., capital V, Vi vs Vo sets, illegible subscripts).  
- Presentation problems exemplified by Corollary 1 and a lengthy 13-page appendix that does not improve clarity.  
- Paper should aim for a general identifiability criterion rather than piecemeal results like Theorem 1.  
- Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 resemble inverse-probability weighting and likely relate to conditioning on Markov blankets for R(i) as in Richardson (2003, 2009).  
- Specific corrections: explicitly state R^part is a partition of R and that Rx and Ry belong to distinct partition elements.  
- Language and notation fixes needed (avoid casual terms like "oftentimes"; use \leftrightarrow instead of <-->).  
- Notation for graphs formed by severing edges is undefined yet used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4; Vo and Vm are referenced before being defined.  
- Clarify whether Garcia (2013) is actually incorrect or just uses a different framework.  
- Overall: addresses an important problem with potentially significant results, but requires major revisions to improve clarity, notation, and presentation.  
- Recommendation: revise and resubmit after substantial improvements; paper has potential to make a valuable contribution.
