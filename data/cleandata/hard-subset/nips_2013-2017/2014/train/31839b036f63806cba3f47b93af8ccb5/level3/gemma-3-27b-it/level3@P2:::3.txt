This paper investigates the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries, building upon the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. The authors extend previous results by providing more general conditions for recovering various types of queries, notably demonstrating that causal effects can be identified even when the underlying joint or conditional distributions are not. This is a significant contribution, as it expands the scope of recoverable information in challenging missing data scenarios, particularly in the context of attrition.

The paper is well-structured, progressing logically from definitions and background on m-graphs to increasingly complex scenarios and theoretical results. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and highlights the importance of accurate handling of missing data. The subsequent sections systematically refine existing theorems and introduce new criteria for recoverability and non-recoverability. The application to attrition problems in Section 7 provides a concrete example of the practical relevance of the developed framework. The related work section is comprehensive, situating the paper within the broader landscape of missing data techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis, building upon established concepts in causal inference and graphical models. The theorems are clearly stated and seem well-justified.
* **Novel Contribution:** The demonstration that causal queries can be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations are not is a key novelty. This extends the applicability of graphical models in situations where traditional approaches might fail.
* **Generality:** The extension of the sequential factorization theorem to handle more complex missingness mechanisms, including interactions between them, is a valuable improvement.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition problems illustrates the potential of the framework to address real-world challenges in longitudinal studies.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of the existing literature on missing data and related techniques.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions & Applicability:** A core limitation is the reliance on knowledge of the missingness process, encoded in the m-graph. The paper doesn't fully address the practical difficulties of accurately specifying this process in many real-world applications, particularly in large-scale machine learning settings. Moving more detail from Figure 1 into the main text regarding the necessary assumptions would be helpful.
* **Population Scope:** Section 2 implicitly assumes the existence of a well-defined full population, even including entirely unobserved individuals. This excludes scenarios where the population size itself is unknown or evolving.
* **Notational Inconsistencies:** The use of “m-graph” is inconsistent (sometimes including V* variables, sometimes not), leading to potential confusion. Clarification on whether V* variables are omitted and why is needed.
* **Restrictions & Justifications:** The restriction that R variables cannot be parents of V or U is somewhat arbitrary. The authors should justify this assumption, or explicitly acknowledge its limitations and potential extensions.
* **Undefined Terminology:** The term “neighbor” is used without a clear definition. Providing a precise definition would enhance clarity.
* **Comparison to Garcia et al.:** The discussion of Garcia [2013] on page 8 needs further clarification. Specifically, a comparison of positivity assumptions—does Garcia et al. also assume positivity, or a stronger condition?
* **MLE Discussion:** The claim regarding maximum likelihood estimation under MAR is too general. A more nuanced discussion of when MLE can or cannot be applied to the considered missingness processes is required.
* **Minor Errors:** Numerous minor errors and corrections are present, including: wording (“can bias outcomes”), qualifications needed for Figure 1, a RHS error in Example 1 (should be P(Y|X,Z)), notation issues (Y || R should be Y || R_y, R^{(N)} and R^{(j)}), a missing partially observed variable in Corollary 1, and a missing reference for the variable-level definition in Footnote 2. These should be addressed for polish.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension to existing work on missing data and causal inference.
* The results offer valuable insights into the conditions under which causal effects can be identified even in complex missing data scenarios.
* The application to attrition problems demonstrates the practical relevance of the proposed framework.
* The paper is well-written and logically structured.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The strong assumption of knowing the missingness process limits the broad applicability of the results.
* Several notational inconsistencies and minor errors detract from the overall clarity and polish of the paper.
* The lack of justification for certain restrictions (e.g., R variables not being parents of V or U) raises questions about the generality of the framework.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is an interesting and technically sound paper that builds on earlier work in a significant way. However, the practical limitations related to knowledge of the missingness process and the presence of several minor issues require addressing. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent upon the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly clarifying the assumptions, resolving the notational inconsistencies, and correcting the minor errors. Addressing these points will significantly enhance the clarity and impact of this valuable contribution.
