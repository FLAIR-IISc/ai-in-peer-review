- Addresses recoverability (identifiability) of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values using the m-graph framework.
- Extends Mohan et al. [2013] with more general graphical conditions for recovering P(y|x), P(y,x), and causal queries P(y|do(x)).
- Key contributions: generalized sequential factorization theorem, recovery conditions when factorization fails, graphical non-recoverability criteria, and demonstration that causal effects can be recoverable even when statistical distributions are not.
- Applies the theoretical results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.
- Strength: Tackles an important, widely applicable problem beyond MAR/MCAR into MNAR settings.
- Strength: Novel and significant result in Section 6 showing P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable when P(y|x) is not.
- Strength: Technical work appears sound with detailed proofs and interpretable non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3).
- Strength: Extends prior m-graph work via a generalized sequential factorization (Theorem 1) and recovery methods when dependencies among R variables exist (Theorem 2).
- Weakness: Paper is dense, covers too much for a conference, and suffers from poor flow and readability.
- Weakness: Notation is confusing and inconsistent (e.g., V, Vo, Vm, Vi usage; hard-to-read subscripts).
- Weakness: Results feel piecemeal rather than part of a unifying, complete recoverability algorithm akin to identifiability algorithms in causal inference.
- Detailed: Theorem 1 is hard to interpret and should relate more explicitly to existing factorization results for mixed graphs.
- Detailed: Theorem 2/Corollary 1 resemble IPW estimators with conditioning on R variables' Markov blankets; this connection should be discussed.
- Detailed: Notation like G_X and G^X (do-calculus graph modifications) is used without formal definition.
- Detailed: The critique of Garcia [2013] should clarify whether Garcia is incorrect or operating under different assumptions.
- Detailed: Minor presentation issues (e.g., nonstandard symbols for bidirected edges) reduce clarity.
- For acceptance: Problem importance, novel results (especially Section 6), and solid technical contributions including useful non-recoverability criteria.
- Against acceptance: Severe clarity and notation issues, and lack of a unifying, complete recoverability framework make the manuscript unsuitable in current form.
- Overall: Important and technically solid contributions, particularly in Sections 5–6, but the manuscript needs substantial revision for clarity, notation, and organization before acceptance.
