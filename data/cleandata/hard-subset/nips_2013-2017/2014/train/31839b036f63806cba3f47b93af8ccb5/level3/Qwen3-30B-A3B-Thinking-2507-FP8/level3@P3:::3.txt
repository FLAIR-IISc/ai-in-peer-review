

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. [2013] on missingness graphs (m-graphs) to provide more general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. Building on the framework of missingness graphs, the authors derive novel graphical conditions for recoverability of both probabilistic queries (P(y|x), P(y,x)) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))), showing that causal queries may be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations are not. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by demonstrating that causal effects can be recovered even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable, a finding that challenges previous assumptions about the relationship between recoverability of joint distributions and causal queries. The authors apply their results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability.

## Strengths

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical development. The extensions to Mohan et al. [2013] are substantial and well-motivated, particularly the demonstration that causal queries can be recoverable despite the inseparability of Y and Ry. The graphical criteria presented for recoverability are novel and provide valuable theoretical insights. The connection to causal inference through do-calculus and the introduction of d*-separation represent important theoretical advances. The paper's application to attrition problems in longitudinal studies is timely and relevant, with Theorem 6 rectifying a misconception in Garcia [2013] about necessary conditions for recoverability.

## Weaknesses

**Clarity Issues:**
- The paper uses "mgraph" inconsistently, sometimes including V* variables and sometimes not. The authors should clarify whether V* variables are being omitted and why.
- The term "neighbor" is used without definition and should be formally defined.
- Section 2 implicitly assumes the full population is known (including entirely unobserved individuals), which excludes problems like population size estimation.
- The restriction forbidding R variables as parents of V or U needs justification; the authors should explain why this is not a general limitation.

**Technical Concerns:**
- The claim that "maximum likelihood yields consistent estimates under MAR" is too vague. The authors should explain why MLE could or could not be applied to the missingness processes considered here, especially given that the paper deals with MNAR mechanisms.
- The relation to Garcia et al.'s "rectification" on p.8 needs clarification regarding positivity assumptions—do Garcia et al. also assume positivity or a stronger condition?
- The paper requires knowledge of the missingness process, but it's unclear whether this is typically available in ML applications. This limitation should be addressed more explicitly.

**Minor Errors:**
- Wording in Example 1 should be corrected from "can bias outcomes" to "can bias results."
- Figure 1 qualifications should be moved into the main text.
- Example 1 RHS should be P(Y|X,Z), not P(Y|X).
- Replace Y || R with Y || R_y.
- Fix R^(N) and R^(j) notation.
- Corollary 1 lacks a partially observed variable.
- Provide a reference for the variable-level definition in Footnote 2.

## Overall Assessment

This is an interesting and technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on missing data and causal inference. It builds effectively on Mohan et al. [2013] and provides novel theoretical insights about the recoverability of causal queries. The paper is well-structured and the proofs appear correct.

The main limitation is the unclear applicability to typical ML settings where the missingness process is often unknown. While the paper is theoretically rich, the practical implementation requirements may limit its broader adoption. The clarity issues identified above need to be addressed for the paper to be fully accessible to the ML community.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**For Acceptance:**
- Significant theoretical contribution extending Mohan et al. [2013]
- Novel insight that causal queries can be recoverable when joint distributions are not
- Rigorous theoretical development with correct proofs
- Clear application to attrition problems with corrected misconceptions
- Builds on established causal inference framework

**Against Acceptance:**
- Requires knowledge of missingness process, which may not be available in typical ML applications
- Several clarity issues with inconsistent terminology and undefined terms
- Section 2 implicitly assumes full population knowledge, excluding important problems
- Unclear connection to practical implementation in ML settings
- Minor technical errors that need correction

The paper makes an important theoretical contribution to the field of causal inference with missing data, but would benefit from addressing the clarity issues and better articulating the practical applicability of the results. With the suggested clarifications and corrections, this would be a strong contribution to NIPS. As it stands, the paper is technically sound and original, but the clarity issues and applicability concerns warrant some reservations.
