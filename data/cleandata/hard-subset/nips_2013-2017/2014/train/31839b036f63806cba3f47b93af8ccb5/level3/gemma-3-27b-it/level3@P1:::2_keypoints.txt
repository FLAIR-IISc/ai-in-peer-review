- Addresses recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing-data datasets, extending Mohan et al. (2013).
- Main contribution: shows causal effects (P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when underlying probabilistic relations are not.
- Uses and extends “Missingness Graphs” (m-graphs) to model the missing-data process formally.
- Builds on causal-inference tools such as d-separation and do-calculus.
- Provides refined and generalized recoverability conditions with multiple theorems and corollaries (Theorems 1–6).
- Carefully defines key concepts (recoverability, admissible sequences, d*-separation) as a theoretical foundation.
- Gives criteria for non-recoverability, clarifying limits for practitioners.
- Applies results to attrition in longitudinal studies and uses Figures 1–4 to illustrate concepts.
- Technical presentation is rigorous and thorough, with solid proofs.
- Introduction of m-graphs improves explicit communication of missingness assumptions.
- Notation is dense and the number of theorems/definitions may hinder readability for non-experts.
- Related-work discussion could better cover recent missing-data imputation advances used in practice.
- Reliance on the semi-Markovian model assumption may limit general applicability.
- Lacks empirical validation demonstrating practical benefits.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to simplify notation and consider empirical validation strategies.
