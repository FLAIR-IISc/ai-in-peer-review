This paper extends the framework of Mohan et al. (2013) for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values, represented using ‘Missingness Graphs’ (m-graphs). The authors present refined conditions for recoverability, demonstrating that causal queries (P(y|do(x))) can be identified even when associated probabilistic relations (P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are not. A key contribution lies in showing that causal effects can be recoverable even when the target variable and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable – a condition typically required for identifiability. The work also applies these results to the specific problem of attrition.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work on missing data, including deletion-based methods (Rubin, 1976), Maximum Likelihood estimation (Dempster et al., 1977), and inverse probability weighting (Robins & Rotnitzky, 1992). It also connects to the growing area of graphical models for handling missing data (Daniel et al., 2012; Garcia, 2013). However, unlike many existing approaches that rely on assumptions like MAR, this work provides a more formal, graphical framework for assessing recoverability under potentially more complex missingness mechanisms. The authors correctly position their work relative to these existing contributions, providing relevant citations.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper demonstrates a strong technical foundation. The derivations and theorems appear logically sound, and the proofs (though relegated to the appendix) presumably support the claims. The extension of the sequential factorization theorem and the conditions for recovering causal queries, particularly when Y and Ry are inseparable, are noteworthy.
*   **Novelty:** The demonstration that causal queries can be identifiable even with non-d-separability is a significant result, expanding the scope of recoverable queries beyond what’s possible with purely probabilistic approaches. This is a genuine contribution to the field.
*   **Significance:** The problem of missing data is ubiquitous, and a more robust framework for assessing identifiability and recoverability has the potential to be impactful, particularly in causal inference applications. The application to attrition is a practical and relevant example.
*   **Clear Non-Recoverability Criteria:** Establishing clear conditions *preventing* recoverability (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) is equally important as identifying sufficient conditions, providing users with practical guidance.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Organization:** This is the paper’s biggest weakness. The presentation is dense and difficult to follow. The sheer amount of material feels excessive for a conference paper. Section 3, in particular, feels disjointed from the rest of the paper, and Theorem 1 is challenging to grasp. The flow could be significantly improved by streamlining the presentation and providing more intuitive explanations.
*   **Notation and Presentation:** The notation is inconsistent and often confusing. The overuse of capital V, the ambiguous use of subscripts (Vi as a vertex vs. Vo as a set), and illegible subscripts hinder comprehension. Corollary 1 is a particularly poor example of unclear presentation.
*   **Incremental Nature of Some Results:** While the core contribution regarding causal queries is strong, some of the other results feel incremental. The paper would benefit from focusing on a more unifying framework or a broader identifiability criterion rather than a collection of separate theorems.
*   **Lack of Intuition:** While the mathematical results are rigorous, the paper lacks sufficient intuitive explanation of *why* these graphical conditions lead to (non-)recoverability. More illustrative examples, beyond those currently provided, would be valuable.

**Specific Points:**

*   The definition of R<sup>part</sup> requires clarification – it should state that Rx and Ry belong to *distinct elements* of the partition.
*   The paper uses casual language (“oftentimes”) which is inappropriate for a scientific publication. Standard mathematical notation (↔) should be used instead of <-->
*   The claim about Garcia (2013) requires careful examination. The authors should clarify whether Garcia’s results are actually incorrect or if they operate within a different framework.
*   The notation for graphs formed by severing edges (used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4) is not defined.
*   Vo and Vm are referenced before they are defined, seemingly unnecessarily.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in causal inference.
*   Presents novel and technically sound results, particularly regarding the recovery of causal queries under conditions of non-d-separability.
*   Provides useful graphical criteria for both recoverability and non-recoverability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper suffers from significant clarity and presentation issues, making it difficult to follow.
*   The notation is confusing and inconsistent.
*   Some results are incremental and lack a unifying framework.
*   The length and density of the paper are unsuitable for a conference presentation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper addresses a significant problem with a technically sound and potentially impactful approach. However, the substantial clarity and presentation issues significantly detract from its overall quality. While the core contributions are valuable, the paper is currently difficult to digest and understand.  **I recommend rejection, but strongly encourage the authors to revise and resubmit to a journal, addressing the concerns outlined above.** A significant overhaul of the presentation, notation, and organization is needed to fully realize the potential of this work.
