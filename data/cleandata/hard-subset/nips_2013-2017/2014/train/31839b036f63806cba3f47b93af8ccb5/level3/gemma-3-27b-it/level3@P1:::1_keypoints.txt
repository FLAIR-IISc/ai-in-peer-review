- Extends Mohan et al. (2013) m-graphs framework to recover probabilistic and causal queries from data with missingness.  
- Refines conditions showing P(y|do(x)) can be identified even when P(y,x) or P(y|x) are not recoverable.  
- Demonstrates causal effects can be recoverable despite non-d-separability between a target variable and its missingness mechanism.  
- Applies the theoretical results to the practical problem of attrition.  
- Builds on and situates itself within prior missing-data and graphical-model literature with relevant citations.  
- Technical soundness: derivations and theorems appear logically sound; extension of sequential factorization is noteworthy.  
- Novelty: expands the set of recoverable causal queries beyond what probabilistic approaches allow.  
- Significance: offers a potentially impactful framework for causal inference under complex missingness.  
- Provides clear non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) alongside sufficient conditions.  
- Major weakness: clarity and organization are poor; presentation is dense and parts (Section 3, Theorem 1) are hard to follow.  
- Notation problems: inconsistent/confusing notation (overuse of V, ambiguous subscripts, unclear Corollary 1).  
- Some results are incremental and the paper lacks a unifying framework tying the theorems together.  
- Insufficient intuition and illustrative examples explaining why the graphical conditions imply (non-)recoverability.  
- Specific: Rpart definition unclear—need to state Rx and Ry belong to distinct partition elements.  
- Specific: informal language ("oftentimes") and nonstandard symbols (<-->) should be replaced with formal notation.  
- Specific: claim about Garcia (2013) needs clarification on whether it’s incorrect or in a different framework.  
- Specific: notation for graphs formed by severing edges (Definition 4, Theorem 4) is undefined.  
- Specific: Vo and Vm are referenced before being defined.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, presents novel technically sound results, and gives useful graphical criteria.  
- Arguments against acceptance: significant clarity/presentation issues, confusing notation, incremental contributions, and excessive length/density for a conference.  
- Overall assessment: technically valuable and potentially impactful but currently hard to digest; recommend rejection with encouragement to substantially revise and resubmit to a journal.
