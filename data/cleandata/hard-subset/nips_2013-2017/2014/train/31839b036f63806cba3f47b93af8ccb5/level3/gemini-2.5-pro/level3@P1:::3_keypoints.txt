- Problem: recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing entries using the m-graph framework.
- Main contribution: more general conditions for recoverability that extend Mohan et al. (2013).
- Extension: generalization of sequential factorization to handle interacting missingness mechanisms (common in longitudinal studies).
- Algorithmic contribution: a novel recovery algorithm based on partitioning missingness variables when the sequential theorem does not apply.
- Novelty: introduction of graphical criteria for non-recoverability, not treated formally in prior work.
- Key finding: causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can sometimes be recovered even when joint/conditional distributions are not.
- Empirical/demo: application to attrition in randomized trials to illustrate theoretical results.
- Quality: technically sound with correct proofs and non-trivial theorem extensions; completes the line from sufficient to necessary conditions and probabilistic to causal queries.
- Major limitation: strong assumption that the full m-graph is known a priori, limiting practical applicability and posing challenges for real ML use cases.
- Modeling concerns: implicit assumption of a known population (excludes problems like unknown population size) needs justification.
- Structural constraint concern: forbidding R variables as parents of substantive (V) or latent (U) variables needs explanation (theory convenience vs modeling assumption).
- Clarity issue: inconsistent use of "m-graph" (sometimes includes proxy V* variables, often omitted in figures) — convention should be clarified.
- Notation issue: "neighbor" is used without formal definition (unclear whether any edge or only directed edges counts).
- Presentation: motivating example (Figure 1) needs more detail moved into main text for better framing.
- Related work: the claim about MLE being consistent under MAR is too general; discussion should clarify MLE applicability under MAR vs MNAR.
- Comparison with Garcia (2013): needs more detail on Garcia’s assumptions (e.g., positivity) to explain differing conclusions.
- Originality: novel theorems (Theorems 1–3) and the result decoupling causal and statistical recoverability (Theorem 4 / Section 6) are significant contributions.
- Significance: theoretical impact high for causality and missing data; non-recoverability criteria provide formal tools to detect unsolvable problems.
- Practical significance: less clear because of known-graph assumption, but valuable in domains with strong causal modeling (epidemiology, social sciences).
- Acceptance arguments: strong theoretical contribution, important conceptual insights, clear m-graph problem formulation, and a relevant attrition application.
- Rejection arguments: limited practical applicability due to the required known m-graph and several clarity/notation issues needing revision.
- Specific corrections requested: fix wording ("can bias outcomes" → "can introduce bias"), correct Example 1 conditioning and notation, and clarify missing/incorrect R variable mentions.
- Theorem/notation fixes requested: check and correct independence and indexing notation in Theorem 1, Theorem 2 (partition notation), Corollary 1 premise, Corollary 2 intermediate-node specification, and Theorem 4’s shorthand and d*-separation definitions.
- Miscellaneous fixes: missing/misnumbered footnote(s), provide formal definitions for "neighbor" and "collider path" in preliminaries, and clarify convention about including/omitting V* variables in figures.
