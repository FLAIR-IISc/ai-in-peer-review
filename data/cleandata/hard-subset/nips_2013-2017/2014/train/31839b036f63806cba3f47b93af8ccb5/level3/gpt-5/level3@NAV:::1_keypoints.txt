- Extends Mohan et al. (2013) to study identifiability/recoverability under missing data using m-graphs, proposing a generalized ordered factorization (Thm.1).
- Introduces a partition-based criterion (Thm.2) to recover P(V) in semi-Markovian models and gives clear necessary non-recoverability conditions (Thm.3).
- Shows causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when P(V) is not, using d*-separation/dormant independencies, with applications to attrition (Thms.5–6).
- Connects results to inverse probability weighting (Robins et al., 1994; van der Laan & Robins, 2003) and mixed-graph separation notions (Richardson, 2003, 2009).
- Technical quality is high; results are involved and appear correct, with Thm.3 particularly clear and interpretable.
- Some contributions (notably parts of Sec.3 and Thm.1) feel incremental, orthogonal to the main narrative, or hard to parse.
- Main weakness is clarity: dense exposition, overloaded notation, and a 13-page appendix make the paper hard to follow.
- Specific notation problems: overuse/confusion of V, Vi vs Vo, illegible subscripts, and poor presentation of Corollary 1.
- Need to unambiguously define graph operations (severing edges), avoid referencing Vo/Vm before definition, and fix casual language/symbols (use ↔ not <-->).
- Correct R^part statement: Rx and Ry should be in distinct partition elements; clarify the status of Garcia (2013).
- Novelty: non-recoverability criteria and causal-recoverability when Y and Ry are inseparable are new and useful.
- Some of Thm.2 and Cor.1 resemble IPW and conditioning on R(i) Markov blankets in mixed graphs and should be better situated in prior work.
- Significance: Sections 5–6 and attrition results are practically important and relevant to NIPS readership.
- Paper would be stronger with a unified identifiability criterion for missingness rather than piecemeal results.
- Pros: strong technical results, impactful Thm.3 and causal-recoverability findings, and useful examples; Cons: major clarity/notation issues, Sec.3’s weaker novelty, and better suited for a journal-length revision.
- Verdict: Borderline reject for NIPS due to presentation issues, with encouragement to revise (tighten scope, unify criteria, fix notation) or submit to a journal.
