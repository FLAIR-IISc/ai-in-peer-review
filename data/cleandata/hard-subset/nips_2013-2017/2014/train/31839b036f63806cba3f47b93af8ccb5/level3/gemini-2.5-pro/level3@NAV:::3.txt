This paper addresses the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing entries. Building directly on the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on missingness graphs (m-graphs), the authors generalize the conditions for recoverability. The primary contributions are a novel identification formula for the joint distribution under a more complex missingness mechanism than previously considered, and the important finding that certain causal queries can be identifiable even when the full joint distribution is not.

The paper's strengths lie in its technical quality and originality. It provides a solid theoretical extension to an established line of research in causal inference and missing data. The insight that causal queries may be recoverable when probabilistic ones are not is a significant and non-obvious result that deepens our understanding of the relationship between these query types.

However, the paper has several weaknesses in clarity and its stated significance that need to be addressed. The primary concern is the practical applicability of the results. The entire framework rests on having knowledge of the true causal graph for the missingness process, and it is unclear how often this is available in typical machine learning applications. The authors should discuss this limitation and perhaps move more detail from Figure 1 into the main text to better motivate the model.

Furthermore, several key assumptions and definitions are not adequately justified or clarified.
*   The framework implicitly assumes the full population is known, which excludes problems like population size estimation.
*   The definition of an "m-graph" is used inconsistently regarding the inclusion of V* variables.
*   The restriction that missingness indicators (R variables) cannot be parents of substantive variables (V, U) should be justified or framed as a simplifying assumption.
*   The term "neighbor" is used without a formal definition.
*   The comparison to Garcia et al.'s work needs to clarify the role of positivity assumptions.
*   The claim on page 8 that maximum likelihood yields consistent estimates under MAR is too vague; the authors should discuss why MLE is or is not applicable to the missingness processes considered in this work.

Finally, the manuscript requires several minor corrections: the phrasing "can bias outcomes" is awkward; the RHS of the equation in Example 1 should be P(Y|X,Z); Y || R should be Y || R_y; the notation for R^{(N)} and R^{(j)} needs to be consistent; Corollary 1 is missing a partially observed variable in its statement; and a reference is needed for the definition in Footnote 2.

In summary, this is an interesting and technically sound paper with novel contributions. However, its current impact is limited by questions of practical applicability and a lack of clarity in its definitions and assumptions. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper.
