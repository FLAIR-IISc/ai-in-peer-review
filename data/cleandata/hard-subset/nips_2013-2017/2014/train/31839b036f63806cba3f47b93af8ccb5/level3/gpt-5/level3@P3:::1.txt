Summary
The paper studies recoverability (identifiability from incomplete data) of probabilistic queries (P(y, x), P(y|x)) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))) under a graphical model of missingness (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (2013), the authors (i) extend sequential factorization to include R-nodes (missingness indicators) in the ordering, yielding a broader sufficient condition for recovering probabilistic queries (Theorem 1); (ii) provide recovery formulas for settings without an admissible sequence via partitions of R and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)) (Theorem 2 and Corollary 1); (iii) give clean graphical necessary conditions for non-recoverability of P(V) and P(Y|X) (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2); (iv) show that some causal effects are recoverable even when associated probabilistic relations are not, using d*-separation/dormant independences and do-calculus (Theorem 4); and (v) analyze “simple attrition,” providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery of P(V) (Theorem 5) and P(y|do(x)) (Theorem 6). The work relates closely to the ADMG/latent projection literature (Richardson 2003, 2009) and to identifiability of interventional distributions (Shpitser & Pearl 2006; dormant independences, Shpitser & Pearl 2008). It also connects conceptually to inverse-probability weighting (Robins & Rotnitzky 1992; van der Laan & Robins 1998, 2003).

Quality
- Technical soundness: High. The results are involved but appear correct. Theorem 3, giving simple necessary conditions for non-recoverability via adjacency and collider paths between X and Rx, is especially clear and interpretable. The do-calculus-based recovery for causal queries when P(Y|X) is not recoverable is convincing.
- Scope and completeness: Sections 5 and 6 provide crisp criteria and practically useful insights. Section 3 (general ordered factorization including R) is technically interesting but feels orthogonal to the later, more impactful results.
- Validation: Formal proofs (in a long appendix) support the claims. Examples help, though more worked, step-by-step examples for the non-recoverability criteria and the causal recovery results would aid readers.

Clarity
- Main weakness. The paper tries to do too much for a conference format. The text is dense, the flow is uneven (Section 3 vs. Sections 5–6), and the appendix is 13 pages.
- Notation is confusing/inconsistent:
  - Overuse of V: V as the universe vs. Vi as a vertex vs. Vo as a set; subscripts are hard to parse. Corollary 1 is a prime example of presentation complexity.
  - Rpart is a partition of R; the text should state explicitly that Rx and Ry “belong to distinct elements of the partition.”
  - Use ↔ instead of <--> for bidirected edges; avoid casual language such as “oftentimes.”
  - The notation for graphs formed by severing edges under interventions is missing. You define GX and GX (remove outgoing vs incoming edges of X), but Gw (used implicitly in Definition 4/Theorem 4) is not defined. Please define Gw and GX,W1 explicitly.
  - Vo and Vm are referenced before being clearly introduced; move their definitions earlier.
- Presentation suggestions:
  - Move most of Section 3 to the appendix or integrate it with later results, motivating clearly when “including R in the order” is essential.
  - Lead with a concise statement of contributions and a running example that recurs across sections (including an attrition example).
  - Recast Theorem 2/Corollary 1 with a diagrammatic aid and a short proof sketch; explicitly relate the formula to weighting by 1/P(R(i)=0|Mb(R(i))).

Originality
- Extends Mohan et al. (2013) in several directions and contributes the first crisp non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3).
- The demonstration that causal queries may be recoverable even when probabilistic conditionals are not (Section 6) is novel and valuable.
- Some results feel incremental and piecemeal (especially Theorem 1), and the paper does not yet articulate a unifying identifiability criterion analogous to ID for causal effects.

Significance
- High potential impact. Sections 5 and 6 give clear, actionable guidance for practitioners facing MNAR mechanisms, including attrition.
- The results on causal identifiability without recoverable conditionals are particularly useful.
- Broad relevance across ML, causal inference, and applied sciences.

Relation to prior work
- Please situate Theorem 2/Corollary 1 more explicitly relative to inverse-probability weighting and selection-model factorization (Robins & Rotnitzky 1992; van der Laan & Robins 1998, 2003). The product over P(R(i)=0|Mb(R(i))) resembles IPW with graphically derived denominators.
- Clarify the connection to ADMG Markov properties and conditioning on Markov blankets of R(i) (Richardson 2003, 2009). The present criteria appear to be special cases of conditioning on graphical blankets in mixed graphs.
- Clarify whether Garcia (2013) is actually incorrect under your assumptions or is working under a different graphical/identification framework. In Sec. 7.2, be precise about which assumption differences lead to the discrepancy.

Specific comments and corrections
- Theorem 1: As written, it is hard to parse. Provide a small schematic example, and explicitly motivate minimality in Definition 2 (why the chain-rule factorization fails).
- Theorem 2: Fix the partition statement as noted; define “collider path” once and use consistent notation. Consider moving the definitions of Rpart, Mb(R(i)), and Pasub(R(i)) before the theorem.
- Corollary 1: Improve readability, avoid nested subscripts/superscripts that become illegible. Provide a one-line intuition and a toy graph.
- Definition 4/Theorem 4: Define Gw and GX,W1 explicitly; restate dormant independence assumptions to make the result self-contained.
- Language/notation cleanup: avoid “oftentimes,” use ↔ for bidirected edges, standardize capitalization of sets vs. variables, and ensure all symbols are defined before use.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, timely problem with clear practical implications.
- Strong, easy-to-apply non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3).
- Valuable insight that causal effects can be recoverable when probabilistic conditionals are not (Section 6).
- Attrition results (Theorems 5–6) give necessary and sufficient criteria.

Cons for acceptance
- Clarity and organization issues: too dense for conference length; heavy notation and missing definitions.
- Some contributions are incremental and do not yet form a unifying identifiability theory for missing data.
- Section 3 feels orthogonal and obscures the main narrative.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper tackles an important problem and contains useful, technically sound results, especially in Sections 5 and 6. However, clarity, organization, and notation issues substantially hinder accessibility. I encourage the authors to revise toward a journal version, emphasizing a clearer unifying criterion, tightening notation/definitions, and foregrounding the practically significant results.
