

This paper extends Mohan et al. (2013) by developing graphical conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data under a more general missingness mechanism. The authors present novel identification formulas showing that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when the joint distribution P(y,x) is not, which represents a significant theoretical contribution. The work applies these results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for causal effect recovery.

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical development. The core contributions—especially the demonstration that causal effects can be recovered without d-separability between Y and Ry—represent meaningful advances over prior work. The extension of the sequential factorization theorem to handle missingness mechanisms that interact with each other is particularly noteworthy.

However, several issues require clarification. The applicability of the results to typical ML applications is questionable, as they require knowledge of the missingness process, which is rarely available in practice. The authors should move more details about Figure 1 into the main text to improve accessibility. Section 2 implicitly assumes knowledge of the full population, excluding important problems like population size estimation. The inconsistent use of "mgraph" terminology (sometimes including V* variables, sometimes not) needs resolution. The restriction forbidding R variables as parents of V or U requires justification, as R variables are treated as real quantities. The term "neighbor" appears without definition, and the relation to Garcia et al.'s "rectification" on p.8 needs clarification regarding positivity assumptions.

Minor but important issues include inconsistent notation (R^{(N)} vs R^{(j)}), incorrect RHS in Example 1 (should be P(Y|X,Z)), and missing reference for the variable-level definition in Footnote 2. The claim that maximum likelihood yields consistent estimates under MAR is too vague and requires elaboration.

While the paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution that builds on prior work, its practical applicability remains unclear. The technical quality is strong, but the paper would benefit from addressing these clarifications before being considered for publication. The significance lies in advancing the theoretical understanding of missing data mechanisms, though the path to practical implementation requires more work.
