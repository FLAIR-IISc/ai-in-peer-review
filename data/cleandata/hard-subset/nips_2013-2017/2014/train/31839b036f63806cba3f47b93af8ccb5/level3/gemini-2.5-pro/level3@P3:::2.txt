This paper addresses the fundamental problem of recoverability: determining whether a probabilistic or causal query can be consistently estimated from a dataset with missing values. The authors build upon their previous work (Mohan et al., 2013), which introduced "missingness graphs" (m-graphs) as a formal tool to explicitly model the data-generating process alongside the missingness-generating process. This paper significantly extends that framework by providing more general and powerful theoretical results.

The main contributions are:
1.  A generalized sequential factorization theorem for recovering probabilistic queries, which now accommodates models where missingness mechanisms can influence each other (a common scenario in longitudinal studies).
2.  A novel method for recovering joint distributions in cases where no admissible factorization sequence exists, based on the graphical structure of the m-graph.
3.  The first formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*. These necessary conditions are a crucial addition to the literature, allowing researchers to identify when a query is fundamentally inestimable under a given set of assumptions.
4.  A key and perhaps surprising result demonstrating that a causal query, `P(y|do(x))`, can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic query, `P(y,x)`, is not. The paper provides a sufficient condition for this based on `d*-separation`.
5.  The application of these general theorems to the specific problem of attrition, deriving necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects in this common setting and correcting some claims in prior literature.

This work connects the graphical models approach for missing data, pioneered by the authors, to the rich literature on causal identification (e.g., Pearl's do-calculus, Shpitser & Pearl's work) and provides a principled, model-based alternative to the standard statistical taxonomy of MCAR, MAR, and MNAR.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. It presents a series of theoretical results (theorems and corollaries) that are rigorously derived and appear sound (proofs are provided in the appendix). The work is complete and self-contained, building logically from one result to the next. The authors are careful to delineate the scope of each theorem and its relationship to prior results. The primary strength is the formal, principled approach to a problem often handled with heuristics or overly broad assumptions. A minor weakness is the absence of any empirical validation. While this is a theoretical paper, simulation studies demonstrating the bias that arises when attempting to estimate a non-recoverable query, or showing the successful application of the recovery formulas, could have made the practical implications even more tangible.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem and situating their contributions. The logical flow is clear: from recovering probabilistic queries with and without factorization, to establishing non-recoverability criteria, to tackling the more nuanced problem of causal queries. The use of small, illustrative graphical models for each key concept (e.g., Figures 2, 3, and 4) is highly effective at making abstract graphical conditions concrete and understandable. Definitions are stated precisely, and the notation is consistent. The manuscript provides sufficient detail for an expert to follow the arguments clearly.

**Originality:** The work is highly original and represents a significant advance. While it extends the authors' NIPS 2013 paper, the new contributions are substantial and non-trivial. The key novelties are:
*   The introduction of necessary conditions for recoverability (Theorem 3). This moves beyond simply providing sufficient conditions for success and allows one to prove failure, which is a critical capability.
*   The formal demonstration that causal and probabilistic recoverability are not equivalent (Section 6). This is a deep and important insight that opens up new avenues for causal inference in the presence of missing data.
*   The generalization of recovery theorems to handle dependencies among missingness mechanisms, which broadens the applicability of the entire framework.

The work is well-situated within the literature and clearly distinguishes its model-based graphical approach from traditional statistical methods and other graphical model approaches.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are of high significance to researchers in causal inference, graphical models, and any field that contends with missing data in a principled way. The framework provides practitioners with a formal language to encode their assumptions about missingness and a set of tools to determine what can be validly concluded from their data. The insight that an outcome variable's missingness mechanism being dependent on the outcome itself does not necessarily preclude identification of a causal effect on that outcome is a powerful and liberating result. This work provides foundational theory upon which practical estimation algorithms can be built and helps prevent researchers from drawing invalid conclusions from incomplete data.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   **Strong theoretical advance:** The paper offers novel, non-trivial necessary and sufficient conditions for query recoverability that significantly advance the state of the art.
*   **Important conceptual contribution:** The decoupling of causal and probabilistic recoverability is a major insight with significant implications for causal analysis.
*   **High clarity and quality:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making complex theoretical ideas accessible and the results easy to understand.
*   **Broad relevance:** The problem of missing data is ubiquitous, and this paper provides a principled, graphical framework for reasoning about it that is superior to the standard MCAR/MAR/MNAR classification.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
*   The paper is purely theoretical and lacks experimental results. While not essential for this type of contribution, it limits the immediate demonstration of its practical utility.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents significant and original theoretical results for a fundamental problem. Its strengths in quality, clarity, originality, and significance far outweigh the minor weakness of lacking an experimental section. It is a clear and valuable contribution to the field.
