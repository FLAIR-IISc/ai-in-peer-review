- Paper extends recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries under missing data m-graphs with several new theorems generalizing Mohan et al. (2013).
- Theorem 1: generalizes ordered factorizations to include R nodes and gives sufficient conditions for joint and conditional recoverability when missingness mechanisms interact.
- Theorem 2: offers a complementary path-based sufficient condition for recovering P(V) in semi-Markovian models when no admissible sequence exists.
- Theorem 3 and Corollary 2: provide new necessary conditions for non-recoverability.
- Theorem 4: shows some causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when associated probabilistic quantities and Y’s separability from Ry are not.
- Theorems 5–6: give necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for attrition problems with one partially observed outcome.
- Technical development is sound with proofs in the appendix and useful non-recoverability criteria.
- Clarify positivity assumptions: global strict positivity over complete cases is stated, but local conditional positivity needed by algorithms (e.g., P(Rx=0|Z)>0) must be specified.
- All results assume a known m-graph; discuss robustness, practicability, and make Figure 1’s modeling details explicit in the main text.
- Section 2 implicitly assumes a fixed, known population and thus does not cover population-size estimation; scope this limitation.
- Inconsistent use of “m-graph”: state whether V* is included and justify any exclusions across theorems.
- Justify forbidding R→V (or R→U) edges or explicitly state it’s a convenience restriction and discuss possible extensions when R→V is plausible.
- Define “neighbor” formally as adjacency in the mixed graph (directed and bidirected edges).
- Clarify relation to Garcia (2013): align assumptions about “rectification” and specify required positivity or monotonicity differences.
- Refine statement about MLE under MAR: explicitly state MAR plus parameter distinctness/ignorability and positivity are required; contrast with MNAR implications for MLE/EM.
- Novelty: generalized sequential factorization and non-recoverability theorems extend prior work and appear original.
- Novel insight: causal effects can be identifiable even when probabilistic components are not and when Y and Ry are inseparable.
- Significance: advances theory with potential impact in epidemiology, econometrics, and causal ML, but practical use depends on eliciting credible m-graphs.
- Recommend discussing strategies for eliciting/partially testing m-graphs and adding a small synthetic or real-data illustration using the formulas.
- Specific wording/notation fixes: change “can bias outcomes” to “can bias estimates/inferences”; move Figure 1 modeling assumptions into main text.
- Example/notation corrections: fix Example 1 RHS to P(Y|X,Z); replace Y || R with Y ⟂⟂ Ry; unify R(i)/R(j)/R(N) notation and define Rpart.
- Corollary 1 needs explicit mention of a partially observed variable in Pasub(R(i)) and consistency check with Theorem 2’s Mb(R(i)).
- Add a reference for variable-level missingness definitions (e.g., Heitjan & Rubin 1991; Little & Rubin 2002).
- Be explicit about the different notions of positivity required by each estimand (global vs. local conditional).
- Pros: clear theoretical extensions, novel causal recoverability insights, and useful non-recoverability criteria.
- Cons: heavy reliance on a fully specified m-graph, definitional/notation inconsistencies, unclear claims about MLE/MAR and prior attrition work, and no empirical illustrations.
- Recommendation: borderline accept pending clarifications on assumptions, notation, relation to prior work, and addition of a small illustrative experiment.
