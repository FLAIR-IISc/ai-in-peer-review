This paper studies when probabilistic and causal queries are recoverable from data with missing entries, given an explicit graphical model of the missingness process (m-graph). It extends Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) in several directions: (i) a generalized “sequential factorization” that admits R-variables (missingness indicators) in separating sets, enabling recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) a new recovery formula for the joint distribution in semi-Markovian models without an admissible sequence, based on partitions of R and Markov blankets; (iii) the first explicit non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions; (iv) recovery of causal effects P(y|do(x)) even when the corresponding probabilistic quantities (e.g., P(y|x)) are non-recoverable, including cases where Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separated; and (v) necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery under simple attrition. The work is grounded in do-calculus and d∗-separation, relates to identifiability results (Shpitser & Pearl 2006, 2008), and refines earlier MNAR recovery conditions (Mohan et al. 2013).

Relation to prior work: Compared to MAR/MCAR-based approaches (Rubin 1976; Little & Rubin 2002), the paper targets MNAR by encoding missingness causally via m-graphs. It generalizes earlier graphical recovery conditions (Mohan et al. 2013) to handle edges among R variables and semi-Markovian structure, and complements model-blind ML/EM approaches (Lauritzen 1995; Dempster et al. 1977; Koller & Friedman 2009) by showing where such methods cannot succeed without modeling the missingness mechanism. The attrition analysis connects to Garcia (2013) and clarifies when causal effects are (non-)recoverable in randomized trials with dropout.

Quality: The contributions are technically substantial and, as far as I can tell from the statements and examples, sound. Theorems are clearly stated and proofs are relegated to the appendix; worked examples (e.g., Example 1 and the do-calculus illustration) help. The non-recoverability criteria are particularly valuable. However, several assumptions and definitions need clarification to ensure correctness and reproducibility:
- The paper assumes strict positivity over complete cases; please make explicit where each theorem relies on positivity, and contrast with any stronger/alternative assumptions in related work (e.g., Garcia 2013).
- The constraint “R variables may not be parents of variables in V ∪ U” is central. Given that R are treated as real causal variables, justify this as a substantive modeling assumption (e.g., R encodes measurement/viewing of Vi, not causal influence on Vi), or state it is a simplifying restriction and discuss consequences if violated.
- Provide a precise definition of “neighbor” (adjacent via any directed or bidirected edge).
- Clarify when V∗ are included in the “m-graph” versus treated separately; as written, m-graph sometimes includes V∗ and sometimes not. If the canonical m-graph excludes V∗, state so and explain how V∗ enter estimands via equation (1).

Clarity: The exposition is generally clear, but several notational and presentational issues hinder readability:
- Figure 1 is central; move its explanatory details into the main text. Label fully/partially observed variables and R nodes explicitly.
- In Example 1, the RHS should read P(Y|X,Z) rather than P(Y|X,Z,Ry) after applying the conditional independence; also, replace uses of “Y || R” with “Y ⟂⟂ Ry” and ensure consistency of R′ notation.
- Unify the superscripts for R partitions (e.g., R(i) throughout; avoid mixing R(N) and R(j)). Check that Corollary 1 consistently refers to partially observed variables in Pasub(R(i)) and that a partially observed variable actually appears in the corollary statement.
- Define collider path and inducing path exactly where used, and ensure the “minimality” requirement in Definition 2 is emphasized before Example 1 (you use it critically).
- Provide a reference for the variable-level recoverability/positivity definition used in Footnote 2.

Originality: High. Allowing R variables inside admissible separating sets, the general joint recovery in semi-Markovian models via R partitions/Markov blankets, the non-recoverability criteria, and the demonstration that causal estimands can be recoverable when the associated probabilistic factors are not, are all novel and insightful contributions beyond Mohan et al. (2013).

Significance: Theoretical significance is strong: the results sharpen our understanding of MNAR recovery and expand the catalog of identifiable/recoverable cases. The attrition results are practically relevant for longitudinal studies. However, applicability in mainstream ML remains limited by the need to know or credibly elicit the missingness graph. It would help to:
- Discuss how to learn/validate m-graphs from data and assumptions (e.g., tie in more concretely with Mohan & Pearl 2014 tests).
- Describe robustness to plausible misspecifications (e.g., sensitivity analysis if a weak edge between Y and Ry exists).
- Clarify when standard ML/EM can/cannot be used: the claim “MLE yields consistent estimates under MAR” is true only under correct model specification and appropriate missingness; explain why ML is inadequate in the MNAR cases addressed here, and whether your recovery formulas can serve as constraints or targets in an estimation procedure.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, nontrivial extensions that broaden recoverability in MNAR settings.
- First explicit non-recoverability criteria for joint/conditional distributions.
- Conceptually important result that causal effects may be recoverable despite non-recoverable probabilistic factors.
- Useful, tight characterization under simple attrition.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Practical impact limited without discussion on how m-graphs are obtained in typical ML workflows.
- Several clarity/notation issues (inconsistent “m-graph” scope, undefined terms) and minor errors.
- Positivity and modeling assumptions (no R→V/U) need justification and clearer placement.

Detailed comments and requested fixes:
- Define “neighbor” and standardize whether it includes bidirected adjacency.
- Clarify the scope of “m-graph” (with or without V∗); restate equation (1) and when V∗ appear only in estimands.
- Justify the prohibition of R→V and R→U edges or mark it as a modeling restriction; discuss implications if violated.
- Specify positivity assumptions per theorem; in Section 7 (p.8), explain whether your “rectification” of Garcia depends on strict positivity over complete cases and whether Garcia assumes the same or stronger support.
- Expand the MAR/MLE discussion: when would EM/ML apply to your models (if at all), and why not under MNAR structures violating your recoverability conditions?
- Notational/typo fixes: “can bias outcomes” → “can bias estimates”; Example 1 RHS P(Y|X,Z); Y ⟂⟂ Ry not Y ⟂⟂ R; unify R(i); ensure Corollary 1’s statement includes a partially observed variable; add a citation for the variable-level positivity definition (e.g., Little & Rubin 2002; Pearl & Mohan 2013).
- Consider adding pseudo-code for the recovery procedures (admissible sequence search; application of Theorem 2), with complexity notes.

Overall: An interesting and technically solid paper that advances the theory of recoverability for MNAR data and clarifies important edge cases for causal inference. With the suggested clarifications and corrections, I lean toward acceptance for its theoretical contribution and potential to influence practice in domains where m-graphs can be credibly specified.
