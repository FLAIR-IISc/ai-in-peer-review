## Review of “Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data”

This paper tackles the important problem of identifying estimable queries in the presence of missing data, extending previous work by Mohan et al. [2013] to more general missingness mechanisms. The authors derive a novel identification formula and demonstrate that causal queries can be recoverable even when the underlying joint distribution is not. This builds on the foundational work of Pearl and others in causal inference and missing data theory, and attempts to provide more flexible tools for practitioners dealing with incomplete datasets. The core contribution lies in refining the sequential factorization theorem and providing conditions for recoverability even when standard assumptions (like MAR) are insufficient.

The strengths of the paper are its rigorous theoretical foundation and the clear presentation of extensions to existing identification frameworks. The introduction of Theorem 4, showing causal query recovery even with inseparable Y and Ry, is a particularly interesting and potentially impactful result. The authors appropriately connect their work to related areas like attrition analysis and discuss limitations of existing approaches (Garcia et al., imputation methods).

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses affecting its broader applicability and clarity. A major concern is the reliance on knowing the missingness process. While theoretically sound, the practical assumption of complete knowledge of ‘R’ variables seems limiting. More discussion on the sensitivity of results to misspecification of the missingness model is needed. While Figure 1 illustrates the concept, the details are crucial and should be integrated into the main text as suggested. 

Furthermore, the paper’s assumptions require careful scrutiny. The exclusion of R as parents of V or U feels arbitrary and requires a stronger justification. The lack of definition for “neighbor” is a minor but important oversight. Several technical points need addressing: clarifying the relationship to Garcia et al.'s rectification, providing a more precise explanation of MLE applicability, and correcting the numerous minor errors (notation, missing references, etc.) listed in the reviewer guidelines. Section 2’s implicit assumption of a known full population is a significant limitation, excluding many real-world scenarios. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper presents a technically sound extension to existing theory on missing data and causal inference, offering potentially valuable tools for identifying estimable queries.
**Arguments for Rejection:** The strong assumptions, particularly regarding knowledge of the missingness process, limit the practical applicability of the results. The clarity issues and numerous minor errors detract from the overall quality of the presentation.

**Overall:** This is an interesting paper with a solid technical foundation. However, it requires significant clarification and revisions to address the concerns regarding applicability and presentation before it can be considered for acceptance. A clearer articulation of the practical limitations and a more robust discussion of sensitivity analysis are crucial.
