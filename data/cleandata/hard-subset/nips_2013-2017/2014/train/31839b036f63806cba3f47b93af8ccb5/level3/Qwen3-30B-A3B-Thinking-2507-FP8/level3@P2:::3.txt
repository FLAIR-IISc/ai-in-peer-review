

# Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on missingness graphs (m-graphs) to provide more general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. Building on the foundational work of Mohan et al. (2013) and Pearl (2009), the authors develop graphical conditions for recovering probabilistic queries of the form P(y|x) and P(y,x), and crucially demonstrate that causal queries P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations are not recoverable. The paper introduces novel graphical criteria for recoverability and applies these results to problems of attrition, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects in such scenarios.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical contributions. The authors provide formal definitions, theorems, and proofs that extend prior work in a meaningful way. The main technical contributions include:
1. A generalization of the sequential factorization theorem to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact with one another
2. New graphical criteria for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions
3. A demonstration that causal queries may be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable
4. Necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects under simple attrition

The paper properly addresses both strengths and weaknesses of the approach, particularly noting that the methodology requires knowledge of the missingness process, which may not be available in typical machine learning applications. However, the paper could better address the positivity assumptions required for the results to hold, as the relation to Garcia et al.'s "rectification" of attrition problems is not sufficiently clarified.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, though several points could be improved. The term "mgraph" is used inconsistently (sometimes including V* variables, sometimes not), and the authors should clarify whether V* variables are being omitted and why. The paper implicitly assumes the full population is known (even entirely unobserved individuals are assumed to exist), which excludes problems like population size estimation. The term "neighbor" is used without definition and should be clarified. The authors should move more details about Figure 1 into the main text for better understanding. Several minor notational errors need correction (e.g., R^{(N)} vs R^{(j)}, fixing the RHS of Example 1 to P(Y|X,Z), and replacing Y || R with Y || R_y).

**Originality:** The paper makes significant contributions to the literature on missing data by extending the results of Mohan et al. (2013) to a broader class of problems. The key original contribution is demonstrating that causal queries may be recoverable even when the joint distribution is not, which contradicts the prevailing intuition that recoverability of causal queries requires recoverability of the underlying probabilistic relations. The paper also provides necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability under simple attrition, rectifying previous claims in the literature (e.g., Garcia, 2013). The approach represents a novel combination of graphical models and causal inference to handle missing data.

**Significance:** The results have important implications for experimental science and causal inference, particularly in longitudinal studies where attrition is common. The paper addresses a difficult problem (recovering causal effects from missing data) in a better way than previous research by providing clear graphical conditions for recoverability. The work advances the state of the art by formalizing the relationship between probabilistic and causal recoverability. The application to attrition problems is particularly significant as it provides practitioners with clear criteria for determining when causal effects can be recovered from attrition-corrupted data.

**Strengths:**
- Clear extension of Mohan et al. (2013) to more general missingness mechanisms
- Novel demonstration that causal queries may be recoverable even when the joint distribution is not
- Comprehensive treatment of attrition problems with necessary and sufficient conditions
- Rigorous theoretical development with proper proofs
- Well-motivated examples and applications

**Weaknesses:**
- The assumption that the missingness process is known may not hold in many practical ML applications
- The paper implicitly assumes the full population is known, excluding certain important applications
- Inconsistent use of "mgraph" terminology and notation
- Insufficient clarification of positivity assumptions and relation to Garcia et al.'s work
- The claim that "maximum likelihood yields consistent estimates under MAR" is too vague without explaining why MLE could be applied to the specific missingness processes considered
- Several minor notational errors and inconsistencies that need correction

**Recommendation:** This is an interesting, technically sound paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on missing data and causal inference. The results extend prior work in a meaningful way and provide important theoretical insights. However, the paper needs several clarifications and corrections to fully realize its potential impact. The main issue is the unclear applicability to typical machine learning scenarios due to the requirement for knowledge of the missingness process. With the suggested revisions, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Provides the first general conditions for recovering causal queries when the joint distribution is not recoverable
- Solves a long-standing problem in missing data analysis by rectifying previous misconceptions (e.g., Garcia, 2013)
- The graphical approach provides intuitive conditions that can guide practitioners
- Theorems and proofs are technically sound and well-motivated

*Con:*
- The requirement for knowledge of the missingness process may limit applicability to many machine learning contexts
- The implicit assumption that the full population is known excludes important applications
- Several notational inconsistencies and unclear definitions reduce the paper's accessibility
- The connection to practical implementation is not sufficiently developed

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution to the field that would benefit from the suggested clarifications and corrections before publication. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
