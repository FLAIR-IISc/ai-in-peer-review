This paper addresses the critical problem of recovering probabilistic and, importantly, causal queries from datasets corrupted by missing data. The authors build upon their prior work (Mohan et al. [2013]) and extend the theoretical framework for determining identifiability and recoverability under a missingness graph (m-graph) representation. A key contribution is demonstrating that causal queries, specifically P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relationships (e.g., P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are not. The work is well-motivated, rigorously presented, and makes a clear advance over existing literature, particularly concerning causal inference with missing data.

The paper’s connection to previous NIPS work is strong, appropriately citing Mohan et al. [2013] as a foundation and positioning itself as a significant extension. The broader context of missing data handling is also well-established, referencing relevant work in maximum likelihood estimation (Dempster et al. [1977]), inverse probability weighting (Robins et al. [1994]), and graphical modeling (Koller and Friedman [2009]). Notably, the authors distinguish their work from the commonly assumed MAR framework and highlight contributions addressing more complex missing data mechanisms. The acknowledgement of related work in collaborative filtering (Marlin et al. [2011]) is also appreciated, demonstrating an awareness of applications outside traditional causal inference.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for its claims. The definitions of recoverability, general ordered factorization, and d*-separation are clear, and the theorems are logically presented and well-justified. The introduction of Theorem 2 and Corollary 1, expanding upon prior work to handle more complex scenarios, is a significant strength.
*   **Novelty:** The core finding – that causal queries can be recoverable even when underlying probabilistic queries are not – is novel and impactful. This challenges the common assumption that identifiability of the causal effect requires identifiability of the associated probabilistic relationships.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The use of examples, such as those in Sections 3 and 6, greatly enhances understanding of the theoretical concepts. The notation is consistent and the definitions are precise.
*   **Practical Implications:** The application of these results to the problem of attrition (Section 7) demonstrates the practical relevance of the work. The authors correctly point out limitations of existing approaches (Garcia [2013]) and offer a more nuanced perspective on recovering causal effects in the presence of attrition.
*   **Comprehensive Related Work:** The related work section is thorough and covers a wide range of relevant literature, demonstrating a solid understanding of the field.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** While the theoretical framework is rigorous, it can be complex and challenging to fully grasp without a strong background in causal inference and graphical models. The numerous definitions and theorems require careful study.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper primarily focuses on theoretical results. While examples are provided, the lack of empirical validation or simulations to demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed methods is a limitation. It would be valuable to see how these results translate to real-world datasets.
*   **Assumptions:** The reliance on the m-graph representation and the assumptions underlying its construction are critical. The paper acknowledges the need for careful specification of the missingness process but could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the challenges in accurately modeling these mechanisms.
*   **Potential for Computational Challenges:** While the paper presents theoretical conditions for recoverability, it does not explicitly address the computational complexity of implementing the proposed algorithms, particularly in large-scale datasets.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in the field of causal inference with missing data.
*   The core finding regarding the recoverability of causal queries under specific conditions is novel and impactful.
*   The paper is well-written, rigorously presented, and clearly connected to existing literature.
*   The application to attrition demonstrates the practical relevance of the work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The complexity of the theoretical framework may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
*   The lack of empirical validation is a limitation.
*   The computational challenges of implementing the proposed algorithms are not fully addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference. The theoretical results are rigorous, the core finding is novel, and the work is clearly presented. While the lack of empirical validation and potential computational challenges are limitations, they do not outweigh the significant strengths of the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest that the authors consider adding a discussion of the computational complexity of their algorithms and potentially including a small-scale simulation study to illustrate the practical benefits of their approach.



**Rating:** 7/10 (Strong Accept)
