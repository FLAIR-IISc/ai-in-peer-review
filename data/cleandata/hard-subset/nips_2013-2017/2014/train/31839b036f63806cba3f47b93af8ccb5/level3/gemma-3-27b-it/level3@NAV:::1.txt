## Review: Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data

This paper tackles the important problem of identifiability of probabilistic and causal queries under missing data, extending the framework of Mohan et al. [2013]. The authors aim to provide more general conditions for query recovery, demonstrating scenarios where causal effects are identifiable even when underlying probabilistic relationships are not. A key contribution lies in showing causal query recoverability even when the target variable and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable. The inclusion of examples, such as the attrition scenario, adds practical relevance.

The technical quality is high, with involved results that appear correct. Theorem 3, regarding non-recoverability, is particularly clear and interpretable, offering a useful diagnostic for researchers. Sections 5 and 6 represent the strongest parts of the paper, offering concrete, potentially significant results addressing a challenging problem. The exploration of causal query recovery, especially circumventing the d-separation requirement, is a valuable contribution.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of unifying structure. While the individual results are solid, they feel piecemeal and don’t coalesce into a broader, overarching identifiability criterion akin to those found in causal effect identification. Section 3 feels somewhat orthogonal to the main line of argument and Theorem 1 is difficult to grasp, closely mirroring Richardson [2009]. 

A major weakness is clarity. The paper is dense and suffers from poor flow, packing too much material into a conference format. The 13-page appendix exacerbates this issue. Notation is inconsistent and often confusing – the overuse of 'V' and the varied use of subscripts (Vo vs. Vm, Vi vs. Rx, Ry) hinder comprehension. Corollary 1 is a prime example of poor presentation. There are also minor issues – casual language ("oftentimes") should be avoided, and "<-->" replaced with "\leftrightarrow". The authors should clarify whether Garcia [2013] presents a genuinely conflicting result or operates within a different framework. Crucially, the notation for graphs formed by severing edges (used in Definitions 4 and Theorem 4) is missing a formal definition. The introduction of Vo and Vm before their definition is also unnecessary.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Important problem, useful approach, potential significance of Sections 5 & 6, and solid technical foundation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** Lack of overall clarity and structure, overly dense for a conference paper, problematic notation, and incremental nature of some results.

**Verdict:** Reject for now. This work has merit and addresses an important problem. However, substantial revisions focusing on clarity, organization, and notation are necessary before it is suitable for a conference publication. A journal submission would be a more appropriate venue in its current form. The authors should prioritize synthesizing the results into a more general identifiability framework.
