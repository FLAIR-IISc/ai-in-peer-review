## Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper addresses the critical problem of identifying estimable causal and probabilistic queries in the presence of missing data, building upon the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. The core contribution lies in providing more general and refined conditions for recoverability, explicitly incorporating the missing data mechanism into the causal diagram. Notably, the authors demonstrate that causal queries can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic factors are not, a significant and potentially impactful finding.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, logically extending prior work. The authors meticulously refine the sequential factorization theorem and introduce new graphical criteria for non-recoverability, offering a more complete picture of the conditions under which estimation is possible. The application to the specific problem of attrition is a practical and relevant demonstration of the theory. The progression of theorems and corollaries is coherent, and the inclusion of examples aids understanding. 

A key strength is the theoretical depth and advancement of the state-of-the-art in causal inference with missing data. The authors go beyond simply extending existing results; they provide genuinely new insights, particularly regarding the recoverability of causal effects when the missingness mechanism is intricately linked to the outcome variable. The paper also thoughtfully addresses limitations and provides criteria for identifying non-recoverable scenarios, a crucial aspect often overlooked. 

However, while the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper could benefit from a more extensive discussion of computational complexity. Implementing the proposed algorithms, particularly for larger graphs, may present challenges, and addressing this aspect would strengthen the practical relevance of the work. Additionally, while related work is covered, a more detailed comparison with recent advancements in multiple imputation and other missing data handling techniques (beyond those explicitly mentioned) would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and significant theoretical contributions to the field of causal inference with missing data.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation of complex concepts.
*   Extends and refines existing work in a meaningful way.
*   Addresses a practically important problem with a rigorous theoretical framework.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability.
*   Could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with alternative missing data handling methods.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical insights are significant, and the presentation is clear and well-motivated. Despite minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work outweigh the concerns, and I recommend acceptance.
