- Paper studies recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from MNAR data using m-graphs, extending Mohan et al. (2013).
- Main contributions: generalize ordered factorization to include R nodes (Theorem 1) for sequential factorization.
- Main contributions: provide recovery formulas for joint distributions without admissible sequences using R partitions and Markov blankets (Theorem 2, Corollary 1).
- Main contributions: present graphical non-recoverability criteria for P(V) and P(Y|X) (Theorem 3, Corollary 2).
- Main contributions: show some causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when related probabilistic factors are not, via d*-separation/dormant independences and do-calculus (Theorem 4).
- Main contributions: give necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for simple attrition for joint distributions and causal effects (Theorems 5–6).
- Situates work relative to Pearl/do-calculus, ADMG/selection diagrams and Markov blankets (Richardson 2003), Rubin’s MCAR/MAR and IPW/ML/MI, and Mohan et al. (2013).
- Strength: tackles an important, under-explored MNAR recoverability problem with practical stakes.
- Strength: non-recoverability criteria are clear, interpretable, and practically useful.
- Strength: Section 6 insight that causal effects can be recoverable when related probabilistic quantities are not is novel and interesting.
- Strength: attrition results (Theorems 5–6) provide tight necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for practitioners.
- Strength: technical development appears careful and accompanied by extensive appendix proofs.
- Weakness: clarity and presentation problems—dense flow, confusing and inconsistent notation, symbol overload.
- Weakness: Section 3 and Theorem 1 are hard to follow and feel orthogonal to clearer results in Sections 5–6.
- Weakness: several results seem incremental and paper lacks a unifying, decision-procedure-like recoverability criterion.
- Weakness: missing or imprecise definitions and graph-operator notation (e.g., Gw, GX,W1) impede comprehension.
- Weakness: connections to ADMG literature (Richardson 2003) and to IPW could be better articulated.
- Weakness: long (13-page) appendix suggests content may be more suitable for a journal-length treatment.
- Quality assessment: technically sound overall; Theorem 3 and Theorems 5–6 are especially clear; Theorem 1 needs better statement and intuition.
- Clarity assessment: currently below NIPS bar due to notation overload and dense prose; Sections 5–6 could anchor a focused revision.
- Originality assessment: mixed—incremental beyond prior work in parts, but causal-recoverability despite inseparability and explicit non-recoverability criteria are novel.
- Significance assessment: high potential impact if clarity and connections to ADMG/IPW are improved.
- Pros for acceptance: addresses important problem, gives actionable non-recoverability criteria, novel causal results, tight attrition conditions.
- Cons for acceptance: presentation and notation hinder accessibility and verification; lacks a cohesive general recoverability algorithm.
- Major suggestions: define graph operators precisely, fix notation (Vo, Vm, Rpart wording), replace “<-->” with ↔, and state positivity/semi‑Markovian assumptions.
- Major suggestions: make explicit connections to Richardson (2003), IPW, and cite Shpitser & Pearl (2008) for dormant independences.
- Major suggestions: consider framing toward a general recoverability decision procedure, add a running example across Sections 3–6, and clarify relation to Garcia (2013) in attrition section.
- Questions for authors: can you outline a path to a complete recoverability algorithm using ADMG separation and do-calculus with R-nodes?
- Questions for authors: can you give a simple example contrasting admissible vs. inadmissible factorizations for Theorem 1 and an intuitive proof sketch?
- Questions for authors: what are the computational implications of Theorem 2’s blanket/partition checks for large graphs?
- Overall recommendation: weak reject (borderline); significant results but clarity, cohesion, and presentation issues need to be addressed.
- Reviewer confidence: high.
