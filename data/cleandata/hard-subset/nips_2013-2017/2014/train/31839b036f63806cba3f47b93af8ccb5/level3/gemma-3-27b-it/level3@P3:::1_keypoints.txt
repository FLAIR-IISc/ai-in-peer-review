- Tackles identifiability of probabilistic and causal queries with missing data, extending Mohan et al. (2013).  
- Shows causal effects can be identifiable even when probabilistic relations are not.  
- Builds on graphical-model approaches to missing data (Rubin, Pearl, etc.).  
- Technical quality is high; results appear mathematically sound.  
- Theorem 3 is particularly clear and interpretable.  
- Sections 5–6 give practically useful recovery conditions, including identifying P(y|do(x)) when Y and its missingness are not d-separable.  
- Paper lacks a unifying vision; results feel piecemeal rather than a general identifiability criterion.  
- Section 3 feels orthogonal to the rest; Theorem 1 depends on Richardson (2009) sequential factorization without sufficient explanation.  
- Major clarity problems: dense text, poor flow, and an unwieldy 13-page appendix.  
- Notation is inconsistent and confusing (overuse of V, Vi/Vo/Vm; undefined “severed-edge” graphs; Corollary 1 poorly presented).  
- Recommend stricter notational standards (e.g., \leftrightarrow instead of <-->, avoid casual language like “oftentimes”).  
- Assertion about Garcia (2013) needs clarification—distinguish being wrong vs. using different assumptions/framework.  
- Definitions of Vo and Vm are referenced before they are formally defined; reorder definitions.  
- Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 connect to inverse-probability weighting and conditioning on Markov blankets, suggesting routes to simplification/unification.  
- Specific correction: clarify that “Rx and Ry” means Rx and Ry belong to distinct elements of the partition R^part.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a critical problem, extends theory, demonstrates recoverability of causal effects, and Sections 5–6 are valuable.  
- Arguments against acceptance: poor clarity/organization, overly dense for a conference, inconsistent notation, lack of unified theory, Section 3 disconnected.  
- Overall: valuable contributions but presentation and notation issues make it unsuitable for conference; recommend major revision and possible journal submission.
