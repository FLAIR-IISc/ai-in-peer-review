This paper addresses the problem of deciding whether a causal or probabilistic query is estimable from data corrupted by missing entries, given a model of missingness process. The authors extend the results of Mohan et al. [2013] by presenting more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries and causal queries. The paper is technically sound, and the results appear to be correct.

The authors provide a clear and concise summary of the main ideas in the paper. They introduce the concept of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to explicitly portray the missingness process and the dependencies among variables in the available dataset. They also define the notion of recoverability and derive conditions under which queries would be recoverable when datasets are categorized as Missing Not At Random (MNAR).

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the technical results. However, the paper suffers from some clarity issues, particularly in the notation and presentation. The overuse of capital V, Vi as a vertex vs Vo as a set, and illegible subscripts make it difficult to follow the paper. Additionally, the lengthy 13-page appendix and the dense text make it challenging to understand the paper.

The main strength of the paper is the technical quality of the results. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the conditions under which queries would be recoverable. The results are involved, but they appear to be correct. Theorem 3 is particularly clear and interpretable.

The paper also has some weaknesses. Some results are incremental and do not yet reveal a larger unifying picture. Section 3 feels orthogonal to the rest, and Theorem 1 is hard to comprehend. The paper would benefit from a more general identifiability criterion, analogous to causal-effect identifiability, rather than piecemeal results like Theorem 1.

The paper is related to previous work on missing data, particularly the work of Mohan et al. [2013] and Garcia [2013]. However, the authors do not provide a clear comparison of their results with previous work. It is unclear whether Garcia [2013] is actually wrong or simply uses a different framework.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in the field of missing data. The results are potentially significant, particularly in Sections 5 and 6, which address an important problem with useful results. The paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field, but it needs to be revised to address the clarity issues and to provide a more general identifiability criterion.

Overall, I would rate the paper as follows:

* Quality: 8/10
* Clarity: 6/10
* Originality: 7/10
* Significance: 8/10

I would recommend the paper for acceptance, but with major revisions to address the clarity issues and to provide a more general identifiability criterion.
