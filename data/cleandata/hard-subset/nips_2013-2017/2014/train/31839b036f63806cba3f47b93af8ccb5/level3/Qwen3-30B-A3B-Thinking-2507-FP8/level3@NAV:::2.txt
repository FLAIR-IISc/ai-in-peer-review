

This paper makes a significant contribution to causal inference with missing data by extending Mohan et al. [2013] through the development of novel necessary and sufficient conditions for determining when causal queries are identifiable from data with missing observations. The authors introduce a formal framework that explicitly incorporates the missing-data generating mechanism into causal diagrams through "missingness graphs" (m-graphs), which represents a substantial advancement over prior work that treated missingness as a separate process.

The paper's primary strength lies in its theoretical rigor and clarity. The authors establish precise graphical conditions for recoverability of both probabilistic and causal queries, with the key insight that causal effects may be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not. This is particularly valuable as it challenges conventional wisdom about the necessity of separability between variables and their missingness mechanisms. The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions, well-motivated examples, and comprehensive proofs provided in the appendix.

In terms of quality, the theoretical claims are carefully supported with rigorous proofs and the authors are appropriately cautious about the scope of their results. The clarity is excellent, with the paper being accessible to causal inference researchers while providing sufficient detail for reproducibility. The originality is strong, as the necessary and sufficient conditions represent a meaningful extension beyond Mohan et al. [2013], particularly in handling cases where missingness mechanisms interact with one another.

The significance of this work is substantial, as it provides a theoretical foundation for practitioners to determine whether causal effects can be recovered from missing data without making restrictive assumptions. While the paper focuses on theoretical contributions, additional empirical validation would strengthen the practical impact. However, the paper's clear contribution to the theoretical foundations of causal inference with missing data makes it a valuable addition to the field.

Overall, this paper meets all four criteria: it is of high quality, exceptionally clear, highly original, and significantly advances the state of the art in causal analysis with missing data. The authors have made a compelling case for their novel conditions, demonstrating meaningful progress beyond prior work. I recommend acceptance.
