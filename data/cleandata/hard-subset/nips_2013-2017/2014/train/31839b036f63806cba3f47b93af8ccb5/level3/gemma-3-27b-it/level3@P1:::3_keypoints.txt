- Paper studies identification of probabilistic and causal queries with missing data using missingness graphs (m-graphs).
- Extends Mohan et al. (2013) to give more general conditions for recovering P(y|x), P(y,x), and P(y|do(x)).
- Key contribution: shows causal effects can be identifiable even when underlying probabilistic quantities are not.
- Novel handling of cases where Y is not d-separable from its missingness mechanism.
- Formalizes “recoverability” and derives graphical criteria using m-graphs.
- Explicit modeling of multiple interacting missingness mechanisms is emphasized as distinctive.
- Applies theoretical results to the practical problem of attrition.
- Theoretical rigor: solid extensions (sequential factorization theorem) and mathematically sound derivations.
- Novelty: challenges assumption that causal identification requires probabilistic identification.
- Clear formalization: m-graphs offer precise representation of missingness assumptions.
- Practical relevance: useful when the missingness mechanism is well-understood.
- Major weakness: heavy reliance on knowing the missingness process limits real-world applicability.
- Inconsistency/clarity issues: inconsistent use of m-graph (V* inclusion), undefined term “neighbor,” and other notation problems.
- Restrictive modeling choice: forbidding R variables as parents of V or U is a significant limitation needing justification.
- Related work discussion is incomplete or imprecise (e.g., Garcia 2013, positivity assumptions, MLE under MAR).
- Several minor errors in wording, equations, and notation reduce polish.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound contribution with potential impact and a useful framework.
- Arguments against acceptance: strong assumptions, clarity/inconsistency problems, and insufficient related-work nuance.
- Overall verdict: interesting and technical but not ready; recommend rejection with invitation to revise.
- Recommended revisions: clarify assumptions/notation, justify or relax R-parent restriction, analyze sensitivity to m-graph misspecification, and improve related-work discussion and minor errors.
