- Addresses identifiability of probabilistic and causal queries under missing data, extending Mohan et al. (2013).  
- Demonstrates scenarios where causal effects are identifiable even when probabilistic relationships are not.  
- Key contribution: causal query recoverability when target and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable.  
- Includes practical examples (e.g., attrition) that add relevance.  
- Technical quality is high and results appear correct.  
- Theorem 3 (non-recoverability) is clear, interpretable, and a useful diagnostic.  
- Sections 5 and 6 are the strongest, offering concrete and potentially significant results.  
- The work’s exploration of circumventing d-separation for causal recovery is valuable.  
- Lacks a unifying structure—results feel piecemeal without an overarching identifiability criterion.  
- Section 3 is somewhat orthogonal to the main thread; Theorem 1 is hard to grasp and resembles Richardson (2009).  
- Major clarity issues: dense presentation, poor flow, and too much material for a conference format.  
- A 13-page appendix exacerbates readability and clarity problems.  
- Notation is inconsistent and confusing (overuse of 'V'; unclear subscripts like Vo/Vm, Vi vs Rx/Ry).  
- Corollary 1 is poorly presented and hard to follow.  
- Minor presentation issues include casual language and improper symbols ("oftentimes", "<-->").  
- Unclear whether Garcia (2013) conflicts with the results or uses a different framework—needs clarification.  
- Missing formal definition for graphs formed by severing edges used in Definitions 4 and Theorem 4.  
- Vo and Vm are introduced before being defined, which is unnecessary and confusing.  
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, useful approach, solid technical foundation, and promising Sections 5–6.  
- Arguments against acceptance: poor clarity/organization, overly dense, problematic notation, and some incremental results.  
- Verdict: Reject for now; substantial revisions required (clarity, organization, notation); consider journal submission and synthesize results into a general identifiability framework.
