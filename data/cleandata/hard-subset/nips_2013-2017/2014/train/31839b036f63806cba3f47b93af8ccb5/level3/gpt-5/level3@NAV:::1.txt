Summary and relation to prior work: The paper studies identifiability/recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries under missing data using m-graphs, extending Mohan et al. (2013). It proposes a generalized ordered factorization (Theorem 1) to handle interacting missingness mechanisms, a partition-based criterion (Theorem 2) for recovering P(V) in semi-Markovian models, and clear necessary conditions for non-recoverability (Theorem 3). It further shows that causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when P(V) is not, leveraging d*-separation/dormant independencies (Shpitser & Pearl, 2006/2008), and gives applications to attrition (Theorems 5–6). The work connects to inverse probability weighting (Robins et al., 1994; van der Laan & Robins, 2003) and to mixed graph separation notions (Richardson, 2003, 2009).

Quality: Technical quality is high; the results are involved and appear correct, and Theorem 3 is particularly clear and interpretable. The causal examples in Sections 5–6 are convincing and align with do-calculus-based identifiability. Some contributions (e.g., in Section 3) feel incremental and orthogonal to the main narrative, and Theorem 1 is difficult to parse.

Clarity: Clarity is the main weakness. The manuscript attempts to cover too much for a conference paper, with dense exposition and a 13-page appendix. Notation is inconsistent and confusing: overuse of V; Vi as a vertex versus Vo as a set; illegible subscripts; and Corollary 1 exemplifies poor presentation. Define unambiguously the notation for graphs formed by severing edges (used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4); avoid referencing Vo and Vm before defining them. Replace casual language (“oftentimes”) and use standard symbols (use \leftrightarrow instead of <-->). Correct R^part: Rx and Ry should be stated to belong to distinct elements of the partition. Clarify whether Garcia (2013) is incorrect or adopting a different framework.

Originality: The non-recoverability criteria and the causal-recoverability results when Y and Ry are inseparable are novel and useful. However, parts of Theorems 2 and Corollary 1 resemble IPW and conditioning on Markov blankets of R(i) in mixed graphs (cf. Richardson, 2003, 2009); the paper should situate these more explicitly.

Significance: Sections 5 and 6 address important questions and provide practical guidance; the attrition results could impact applied work. A more unified identifiability criterion for missingness (analogous to causal-effect identification) would elevate the contribution beyond piecemeal results.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong technical results; Theorem 3 and causal-query recoverability are impactful.
- Useful examples and clear answers in later sections.
- Relevant to NIPS audiences working on causality and missing data.

Cons for acceptance:
- Significant clarity and notation issues; poor flow.
- Section 3 feels orthogonal and hard to digest; close to prior work.
- Better suited to a journal-length treatment.

Verdict: Borderline reject for NIPS due to clarity/presentation, with encouragement to revise (tighten scope, unify criteria, fix notation) or submit as a journal article.
