This paper studies when probabilistic queries (P(y, x), P(y|x)) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))) are recoverable from data with missing entries, given a causal model of the missingness process encoded as an m-graph. Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors (i) generalize the ordered factorization approach to allow missingness indicators R to appear in admissible sequences (Theorem 1), enabling recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) provide a broader, semi-Markovian treatment of joint-distribution recovery using partitions of R and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)) (Theorem 2), subsuming special cases previously handled only under more restrictive parent-set assumptions; (iii) derive graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2), filling a gap in the literature; and (iv) show that certain causal effects are recoverable even when related probabilistic factors are not, including when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are inseparable (via do-calculus and dormant independences; Example 3, Theorem 4). The paper applies these insights to attrition, giving necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery of both P(V) and P(y|do(x)) in simple attrition with a single partially observed outcome (Theorems 5–6), correcting misconceptions in prior work (e.g., Garcia, 2013).

Relation to prior work and NIPS: The paper squarely extends the m-graph framework introduced at NIPS 2013 (Mohan et al.), connecting with identification results in causal graphs (Shpitser and Pearl, 2006) and clarifying when MAR/MCAR assumptions suffice (Rubin, 1976). It contrasts with common ML practice that assumes MAR for ML/EM/Bayesian inference (Dempster et al., 1977; Lauritzen, 1995; Koller and Friedman, 2009) and complements IPW/MI (Robins et al., 1994; Little and Rubin, 2002) by offering graphical recoverability tests under MNAR. It also refines recent graphical treatments of attrition (Garcia, 2013; Thoemmes and Rose, 2013).

Quality: The technical development is rigorous and well-motivated. The general-ordered factorization (Theorem 1) is a clean extension that captures cases with dependencies among R variables; the minimality condition is necessary and is illustrated clearly. Theorem 2 provides a compact product-form estimand via R-partitions and Mb(R(i)), and the accompanying non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) are valuable for delimiting the boundary of what is possible. The causal recovery results are particularly insightful: Example 3 demonstrates how do-calculus can bypass non-recoverability of probabilistic components, and Theorem 4 makes this precise using dormant independences (d*-separation). The attrition results give necessary and sufficient conditions and practical estimands. Proofs are said to be in the appendix; the main text gives convincing sketches and examples. Assumptions (e.g., strict positivity on complete cases) are stated.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written, focused, and coherent. The examples (e.g., Fig. 2(a) factorization, attrition diagrams) help. Notational choices are mostly consistent, though a few places could be tightened (e.g., duplicate notation “GX and GX,” careful distinction between Rw and Wr; reiterate when independence is in a mutilated graph vs. original). It would help to provide pseudo-code for (a) checking admissible sequences; (b) constructing R-partitions and Mb(R(i)); and (c) deciding applicability of Theorem 4. A small table mapping common missingness taxonomies (MCAR/MAR/MNAR) to the graphical conditions would also aid readers.

Originality: The results advance the state of the art. Allowing R in admissible sequences and the semi-Markovian product-form recovery via R-partitions appear novel extensions beyond NIPS 2013. The non-recoverability criteria are, to my knowledge, the first formal graphical necessary conditions for joint and conditional recoverability under MNAR. The insight that causal effects may be recoverable despite inseparable Y–Ry (with explicit conditions) is both conceptually and practically important.

Significance: Deciding recoverability before committing to estimation is crucial in scientific applications with MNAR data. The graphical criteria and constructive estimands can directly inform design, analysis, and the choice of auxiliary variables. The attrition results target a pervasive use case. These contributions are likely to influence both methodological research (e.g., automated recoverability decision procedures) and practice.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Dependence on correct m-graph specification: While acknowledged, the paper could provide more guidance on eliciting and validating m-graphs, including practical diagnostics (cf. Mohan and Pearl, 2014).
- Scope limitations: The strongest causal recoverability result (Theorem 4) is stated for |Vm| = 1; extensions or discussion for multiple partially observed variables would strengthen the contribution.
- Algorithmic detail: Provide explicit procedures and complexity for finding admissible sequences, computing R-partitions/Mb(R(i)), and applying Theorem 4 (including how to verify dormant independences algorithmically).
- Empirical illustration: Even simple synthetic experiments or a small real example demonstrating numerical recovery (and failure) under the derived conditions would make the results more accessible.
- Robustness: Discuss sensitivity to violations of positivity and mild misspecification of the m-graph; clarify whether any results can be made testable from observed data (beyond existing tests).
- Presentation: Clean up minor notation typos; standardize references.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, nontrivial theoretical advances with constructive estimands and new non-recoverability criteria.
- Demonstrates an important phenomenon: causal effects can be recoverable when related probabilistic quantities are not.
- Extends prior NIPS work coherently and corrects misconceptions in related literature (attrition).
- Likely to impact both theory and practice for MNAR modeling.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited empirical or algorithmic instantiation; practical adoption may require more tooling and examples.
- Key causal recovery theorem focuses on single partially observed variable; broader completeness is not addressed.
- Reliance on correct m-graph specification, which can be challenging in practice, is not deeply discussed.

Overall evaluation: Strong theoretical paper that advances graphical treatment of missing data and causal inference. With minor enhancements in algorithmic presentation and practical guidance, it would be an excellent addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
