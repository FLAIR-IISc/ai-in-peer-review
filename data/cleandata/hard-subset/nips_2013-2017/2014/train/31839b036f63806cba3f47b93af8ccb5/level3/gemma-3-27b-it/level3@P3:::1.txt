This paper tackles the important problem of identifiability of probabilistic and causal queries in the presence of missing data, extending the framework introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. The authors aim to provide more general conditions for recovery, and successfully demonstrate cases where causal effects are identifiable even when probabilistic relations are not. This work builds upon a growing body of research applying graphical models to missing data problems, referencing key contributions from Rubin, Pearl, and others.

The technical quality of the paper is high. The results, while complex, appear mathematically sound, and Theorem 3 is particularly clear in its statement and interpretability. The exploration of conditions for causal query recovery, especially the ability to identify P(y|do(x)) when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable (Section 6), represents a significant potential contribution. Sections 5 and 6 showcase a definite improvement in clarity and offer practically useful results. 

However, the paper suffers from a lack of a unifying vision. While individual results are interesting, they feel piecemeal and do not currently coalesce into a broader, overarching identifiability criterion analogous to those existing for causal effects. The approach feels like a collection of theorems addressing specific scenarios rather than a general solution.

Section 3 feels somewhat orthogonal to the rest of the paper. Theorem 1, building on Richardson [2009], is difficult to grasp without a strong prior understanding of sequential factorization, and its connection to the core themes isn't immediately apparent. The reliance on Richardson’s work is acknowledged, but a more detailed explanation of the interplay would be beneficial.

A significant weakness is clarity. The paper attempts to cover too much material for a conference format, resulting in dense text and a poor flow. The 13-page appendix exacerbates this issue. Notation is consistently confusing and inconsistent: the overuse of capital ‘V’ (and its variants Vi, Vo, Vm) is problematic, as is the lack of clear definition for graphs formed by severing edges (used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4). Corollary 1 is a particularly egregious example of poor presentation. The authors should adhere to stricter notational standards (e.g., using \leftrightarrow instead of <-->) and avoid casual language like "oftentimes." 

The assertion regarding Garcia [2013] needs clarification. It’s unclear whether Garcia’s work is fundamentally *wrong* or simply operates within a different framework. The authors should explicitly state the differences in assumptions or methodology. The definitions of Vo and Vm are referenced before being formally defined, an unnecessary complication.

Despite these issues, the core problem is important, and the proposed approach shows promise. The potential significance of Sections 5 and 6, addressing a challenging problem with potentially useful results, justifies further investigation. The authors should strive for a more general identifiability criterion, moving beyond the current collection of specific theorems. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 resonate with inverse-probability weighting and conditioning on Markov blankets (as highlighted by Richardson [2003, 2009]), suggesting a potential avenue for simplification and unification.

**Specific Correction:** As noted, R^part is a partition of R, and therefore the statement "Rx and Ry" should be clarified to "Rx and Ry belong to distinct elements of the partition."

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical problem in statistical inference: missing data.
*   Extends existing theoretical frameworks for identifiability.
*   Demonstrates cases where causal effects are recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not.
*   Sections 5 and 6 provide clear and useful results.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Poor clarity and organization.
*   Overly dense and lengthy for a conference paper.
*   Inconsistent and confusing notation.
*   Lack of a unifying theoretical framework.
*   Section 3 feels disconnected from the rest of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents valuable contributions but is significantly hampered by presentation and notation issues. It is currently unsuitable for a conference presentation due to its length, density, and lack of clarity. It would be a stronger submission as a journal article, allowing for more detailed explanations, rigorous notation, and a more cohesive presentation of the results. A major revision focusing on clarity, notation, and unification is required.
