This paper addresses the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. Building on the "m-graph" framework from Mohan et al. (2013), which explicitly models the data-generating and missingness processes, the authors derive more general conditions for recoverability. The main contributions include a novel identification formula for joint distributions in the absence of an admissible sequential factorization and, most notably, a demonstration that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the corresponding joint distribution (e.g., P(x,y)) is not. This extends prior work by handling a broader class of missingness mechanisms, including those with dependencies between missingness indicators, and provides new graphical criteria for both recoverability and non-recoverability.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and pervasive problem in machine learning and statistics. The core result—that causal queries may be identifiable even when the full joint distribution is not—is a significant, counter-intuitive, and important theoretical contribution. It clarifies the distinct requirements for identifying associational versus causal quantities in the presence of missing data.
*   **Originality:** The work meaningfully extends the m-graph framework, providing new theorems (e.g., Theorem 2) that cover cases not handled by prior work. The application of these results to characterize recoverability in attrition models is also a valuable contribution that connects the theory to a practical and common problem.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper appears technically sound, with formal definitions and theorems supported by graphical criteria. The use of examples to illustrate key concepts, especially the subtle case of causal query recovery in Example 3, is effective. The introduction of non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) provides a useful counterpart to the sufficient conditions for recovery.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

While the paper is strong technically, its clarity and practical relevance could be improved by addressing the following points:

1.  **Practical Applicability:** The entire framework rests on the assumption that the complete m-graph, including the causal structure of the missingness mechanisms (the R variables), is known a priori. It is unclear how practitioners in typical machine learning applications would obtain this knowledge. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion on the elicitation of this graph from domain experts or potential avenues for learning it. To make the framework more accessible, I would also recommend moving more explanatory detail for the motivating example in Figure 1 from the appendix or supplementary material into the main text.

2.  **Implicit Assumptions and Scope:** The formulation in Section 2 seems to assume that the full population is known, including individuals who might be entirely unobserved in the dataset. This framing excludes problems common in other fields, such as population size estimation, where the existence of unobserved records is the primary quantity of interest. A brief statement clarifying this scope would be helpful.

3.  **Clarity of Definitions and Notation:**
    *   The term "m-graph" is used inconsistently. The formal definition includes the set V* of proxy variables, but subsequent graphical criteria seem to operate on a graph over V, U, and R. The authors should clarify the role of V* variables in the graphical model and be precise about which graph the criteria apply to.
    *   The term "neighbor" is used in Theorem 2 and Theorem 3 without a formal definition for a graph that contains both directed and bi-directed edges. This should be explicitly defined.
    *   The restriction that R variables cannot be parents of variables in V or U is a strong modeling constraint. As R variables are treated as real quantities, this restriction should be justified. Is it for technical convenience, or does it represent a fundamental assumption about the world?

4.  **Comparison to Related Work:**
    *   On p.8, the relationship to Garcia et al.'s (2013) work on "rectification" needs more detail. To make a clear comparison, please clarify the assumptions made in that work. For instance, do their results also assume positivity, or do they rely on a stronger condition?
    *   The claim on p.8 that "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption" is too vague in this context. It would be valuable to briefly explain why standard MLE might not be applicable to the more general MNAR processes considered here, and whether the identification formulas provided in the paper could be integrated into a consistent estimation procedure like MLE.

5.  **Minor Errors and Corrections:** I have noted several small issues that should be addressed to improve the manuscript's polish:
    *   **Introduction:** The wording "can bias outcomes" is imprecise. Consider "can introduce bias into estimates" or similar phrasing.
    *   **Example 1:** Given the condition Y⊥⊥Ry|X,Z, the term P(Y|X,Z,R'_x, R'_y, R'_z) in the final equation could be simplified to P(Y|X,Z,R'_x, R'_z) for clarity.
    *   **Notation:** In Section 6, the phrasing "Y and its missingness mechanism (Ry) are not d-separable" is clear, but in general, please ensure that notation like `Y || R` is avoided in favor of the more specific `Y || Ry`. The notation for partitions of R, `R^{(N)}` vs. `R^{(i)}`, should be made consistent.
    *   **Corollary 1:** The condition in this corollary seems to lack a partially observed variable in `Pasub(R(i))`, which might make it difficult to interpret. Please review its statement for clarity.
    *   **Footnote 1:** The reference to Mohan et al. (2013) regarding non-positive datasets is helpful, but a more specific pointer (e.g., to a theorem or section) would allow readers to locate the result more easily.

**Overall Assessment**

This is an interesting and technically solid paper that makes a novel contribution to the theory of inference with missing data. It builds upon and significantly extends earlier work. The main weaknesses are the lack of discussion regarding the practical feasibility of its core assumptions and several points that require clarification. Nonetheless, the theoretical results are significant. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly encourage the authors to address the points raised above to improve the paper's clarity, impact, and accessibility.
