This paper investigates the identifiability of probabilistic and causal queries under missing data, extending prior work by Mohan et al. [2013] with more general conditions and demonstrating that causal effects can be recoverable even when joint distributions are not. The authors introduce “missingness graphs” (m-graphs) as a formal representation of the missing data process and derive novel graphical criteria for recoverability. The work builds on the growing area of causal inference with incomplete data and addresses a crucial problem in observational and experimental studies.

The paper is technically sound and well-motivated. The progression of theorems and corollaries feels logical, and the mathematical derivations appear correct. The core contribution – identifying conditions for recovering causal queries despite non-recoverable joint distributions – is interesting and potentially impactful. The application to attrition problems provides a concrete example of the theory’s utility. The numerous examples, like those in Sections 3, 6, and 7, are helpful in illustrating the concepts and theorems.

However, the paper suffers from several clarity and applicability concerns. A major issue is the reliance on knowledge of the censoring/missingness process (R variables). While theoretically sound, this assumption is often unrealistic in many machine learning applications where the missing data mechanism is unknown or difficult to model accurately. Expanding on the limitations of this assumption and discussing scenarios where it might be relaxed would strengthen the paper. Further, the detail currently in Figure 1 regarding different m-graph scenarios and recoverability should be integrated into the main text to provide a more intuitive understanding of the core concepts without constant reference to the figure.

Section 2 implicitly assumes knowledge of the full population (including entirely unobserved individuals, U). This is a strong assumption, excluding important problems like population size estimation which are often intertwined with missing data issues. Acknowledging this limitation would improve the paper’s scope and prevent misinterpretation.

The term "mgraph" is used inconsistently. Sometimes it includes V* variables (proxy variables), while at other times these variables seem to be omitted. The authors need to clarify whether V* variables are consistently included in the m-graph representation and, if not, why they are sometimes excluded. This ambiguity creates confusion.

The restriction that R variables cannot be parents of V or U is also problematic. While the authors mention this, they don’t justify it. Given that R variables represent real quantities related to the missingness process, it seems counterintuitive to exclude them as potential causes of the observed variables. The authors should either provide a strong justification for this restriction or acknowledge it as a simplifying assumption.

The term "neighbor" is used without definition. A precise definition is needed for clarity.

Regarding related work, the discussion of Garcia et al. [2013] on page 8 needs clarification. The authors state that Garcia et al.’s “rectification” approach assumes positivity. It would be valuable to detail *what* positivity assumption Garcia et al. make and whether it’s equivalent to or stronger than the positivity conditions required in the current work.

The claim on page 8 that maximum likelihood yields consistent estimates under MAR is too vague. The applicability of MLE to the missingness processes considered here depends critically on correctly specifying the missing data mechanism. This should be explained. The authors should detail *why* MLE could or could not be applied in these scenarios and how their graphical criteria provide an advantage.

Minor errors and corrections are needed: "can bias outcomes" should be “can introduce bias”; Figure 1 qualifications should be explicitly stated in the text; Example 1’s RHS should be P(Y|X,Z); replace Y || R with Y || R_y; fix R^{(N)} and R^{(j)} notation for consistency; Corollary 1 lacks a partially observed variable in its statement; and a reference should be provided for the variable-level definition mentioned in Footnote 2.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical results on recoverability of causal queries.
*   Technically sound and well-presented.
*   Builds on and extends existing work in a meaningful way.
*   Provides a formal framework (m-graphs) for analyzing missing data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong assumption of knowing the missingness process.
*   Limited applicability in real-world scenarios where missing data mechanisms are unknown.
*   Clarity issues regarding notation (m-graph, "neighbor") and assumptions (R variables as parents).
*   Restricted scope due to assumptions about the full population.



Overall, this is an interesting and technically solid paper with a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference with missing data. However, its practical applicability is questionable without addressing the concerns regarding the knowledge of the missingness process and clarifying several technical aspects. I recommend acceptance with major revisions addressing the points above.
