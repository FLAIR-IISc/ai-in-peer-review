- Extends m-graph framework to give more general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries.
- Shows causal effects can be identified even when joint or conditional distributions are not recoverable, expanding recoverability scope.
- Paper is well-structured, progressing logically from definitions to complex theoretical results and applications.
- Introduction effectively motivates the problem and importance of handling missing data accurately.
- Theorems are theoretically sound, clearly stated, and build on established causal inference and graphical model concepts.
- Novel contribution: demonstrating recoverability of causal queries under harder missingness scenarios is a key innovation.
- Generalizes sequential factorization to handle complex and interacting missingness mechanisms.
- Practical relevance illustrated via an application to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.
- Related work section is comprehensive and situates the contribution within the literature.
- Major limitation: relies on knowing the missingness process (m-graph), which is often impractical in real-world or large-scale ML settings.
- Implicit assumption of a well-defined full population (including unobserved individuals) excludes evolving or unknown-population scenarios.
- Notational inconsistencies around “m-graph” usage (inclusion/omission of V* variables) create confusion and need clarification.
- Restriction that R variables cannot be parents of V or U is arbitrary and requires justification or acknowledgment of limits.
- Terminology issue: “neighbor” is used without a formal definition.
- Need clearer comparison to Garcia et al. (2013), specifically regarding positivity assumptions and whether they differ or are stronger.
- Overgeneralized claim about MLE under MAR; requires a nuanced discussion of when MLE is applicable for considered missingness processes.
- Several minor errors and typos (e.g., wording, figure qualifications, RHS error in Example 1, notation mistakes, missing variable/reference) need correction.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically sound extensions with practical relevance and clear writing.
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on knowing missingness process, notational/terminology issues, and unjustified restrictions reduce generality and clarity.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on clarifying assumptions, fixing notational inconsistencies, and correcting errors.
