Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies when probabilistic and causal queries can be consistently recovered from data with missing entries, given a graphical model of the missingness process (m-graph). It extends Mohan et al. (2013) by: (i) generalizing ordered factorizations to include R nodes and giving sufficient conditions for recoverability of joint and conditional distributions even when missingness mechanisms influence each other (Theorem 1); (ii) providing a complementary path-based sufficient condition for recovering P(V) in semi-Markovian models when no admissible sequence exists (Theorem 2) and new necessary conditions for non-recoverability (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2); (iii) showing that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when associated probabilistic quantities (e.g., P(y,x)) are not, including cases where Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are inseparable (Theorem 4); and (iv) characterizing necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for attrition problems with one partially observed outcome (Theorems 5–6). The work connects to and builds on Pearl’s causal calculus and identifiability (Shpitser and Pearl, 2006), dormant independencies (Shpitser and Pearl, 2008), the MAR/MCAR literature (Rubin, 1976; Little and Rubin, 2002), likelihood-based methods for incomplete data (Dempster et al., 1977; Lauritzen, 1995; Koller and Friedman, 2009), and prior attrition analyses (Garcia, 2013).

Quality
The technical development appears sound and well-situated in prior theory, with proofs in the appendix. The extension to include R in admissible sequences is clearly motivated and the non-recoverability criteria are useful. The causal recoverability results (Theorem 4 and the attrition theorems) are particularly interesting, showing separability is not universally required for causal effects. Some aspects need clarification to ensure rigor and reproducibility:
- Assumptions: Recoverability is defined under strict positivity over complete cases, P(Vo,Vm,R=0)>0. Please clarify where additional positivity is needed locally for the proposed algorithms (e.g., P(Rx=0|Z)>0).
- Knowledge of the missingness/censoring process: All results assume the m-graph is known. In many ML applications this is unavailable. Please discuss robustness and practicability, and consider moving more of Figure 1’s modeling detail—including explicit assumptions on R parents and V*—from the caption to the main text.
- Section 2 implicitly presumes a fixed, known population with potentially entirely unobserved individuals. This excludes tasks like estimating population size. Please call this out and scope your results accordingly.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized, but several definitions and notational choices need tightening:
- “m-graph” is used inconsistently: sometimes the node set includes V* and sometimes it seems omitted. Please state clearly whether V* is part of the graph and why; if V* is excluded in later theorems, explain the rationale.
- You forbid R variables as parents of V or U. Since R are treated as real variables with their own causes and can be parents of proxy variables V*, justify the exclusion of R→V (or U) edges. If it is merely a convenience restriction for this paper’s theorems, please state so and discuss when R→V would be plausible and how results might extend.
- “Neighbor” is used without a formal definition. Please define neighbor as adjacency in the mixed graph (including directed and bidirected edges).
- Relation to Garcia (2013), p.8: The term “rectification” and its assumptions need to be aligned. Do Garcia’s results assume the same positivity (e.g., P(R=0|X,Z)>0) or a stronger condition (e.g., positivity within post-attrition strata or monotonicity)? Please clarify with a precise comparison.
- MLE under MAR, p.8: The statement that MLE yields consistent estimates under MAR is too vague. Please explicitly state the standard conditions (MAR plus parameter distinctness/ignorability and appropriate positivity; Little and Rubin, 2002) and contrast with the MNAR settings you study, where (i) MLE on the substantive model without R is inconsistent, and (ii) MLE/EM can be applied to the full joint including R when the m-graph is known/parametrized.

Originality
- The generalized sequential factorization and theorems for non-recoverability appear novel and useful extensions to Mohan et al. (2013).
- The demonstration that P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when Y and Ry are inseparable, and when probabilistic components are not recoverable, is an insightful contribution linking d*-separation/dormant independence to missing data recoverability.
- The necessary and sufficient conditions for attrition with one partially observed variable offer a clean resolution of when causal effects are recoverable, contrasting with prior claims (Garcia, 2013).

Significance
The results advance theory on missing data with causal graphs, with potential impact in epidemiology, econometrics, and causal ML. Practical applicability hinges on having a credible m-graph; please discuss strategies for eliciting or partially testing m-graphs (e.g., Mohan and Pearl, 2014), and consider adding a small synthetic or real-data illustration showing estimation with your formulas.

Specific comments and corrections
- Wording: “can bias outcomes” → “can bias estimates/inferences.”
- Figure 1: Move key modeling assumptions (which variables are partially observed, where V* appear, allowable R edges) into the main text; clarify whether edges among R are allowed and how they are interpreted.
- Definition 2 minimality: Good motivation; consider adding a short intuitive example in-text before Example 1.
- Example 1: The RHS should read P(Y|X,Z), not P(Y|X,Z,Ry). Later, replace Y || R with Y ⟂⟂ Ry to avoid ambiguity.
- Notation: Unify R(i)/R(j)/R(N) notation for partitions; define Rpart once and use consistently.
- Corollary 1: As written, it lacks an explicit mention of a partially observed variable in Pasub(R(i)). Please restate conditions to make the role of partially observed variables explicit and check consistency with Theorem 2’s Mb(R(i)).
- Footnote 2: Provide a reference for the variable-level definition (e.g., Heitjan and Rubin, 1991; Little and Rubin, 2002).
- Positivity: Be explicit about strict positivity over complete cases vs. local conditional positivity required by each estimand.

Pros
- Clear, meaningful extensions to the graphical recoverability literature (Theorems 1–4).
- Novel insight: causal effects identifiable despite non-recoverable probabilistic components.
- Useful non-recoverability criteria to flag impossibility early.
- Clean, instructive treatment of simple attrition with necessary and sufficient conditions.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on a fully specified m-graph; limited guidance on learning/eliciting it.
- Some definitional and notation inconsistencies (m-graph contents, neighbor, R parent restrictions).
- Claims about MLE/MAR and relation to prior attrition work need clarification.
- No empirical illustrations demonstrating practical estimation or robustness.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. This is an interesting, technically solid extension of Mohan et al. (2013) with clear theoretical contributions, especially for causal recoverability. Addressing the clarity issues, aligning assumptions with related work, and adding a small illustrative experiment would strengthen the paper and its applicability.
