- Paper addresses recoverability: determining when probabilistic or causal queries can be consistently estimated from data with missing values using extended m-graphs.  
- Contribution: generalized sequential factorization theorem allowing dependencies among missingness mechanisms (important for longitudinal studies).  
- Contribution: new method to recover joint distributions when no admissible factorization sequence exists, based on m-graph structure.  
- Contribution: first formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability (necessary conditions to prove a query is inestimable).  
- Contribution: shows P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when P(y,x) is not, providing a sufficient condition via d*-separation.  
- Contribution: applies the theory to attrition, deriving necessary and sufficient conditions for causal effect recovery and correcting prior claims.  
- Quality: technically strong, rigorous proofs, self-contained and logically structured; minor weakness is lack of empirical validation.  
- Clarity: exceptionally well-written and organized, with effective illustrative graphs and precise definitions.  
- Originality: highly original—introduces necessary conditions, decouples causal and probabilistic recoverability, and generalizes to dependent missingness.  
- Significance: high impact for causal inference and missing-data analysis, offering a formal framework and practical identification insights.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical advance, major conceptual insight, high clarity/quality, and broad relevance.  
- Argument against acceptance: purely theoretical with no experiments or simulations demonstrating practical utility.  
- Overall judgment: excellent, significant, and original work whose strengths substantially outweigh the minor lack of empirical results.
