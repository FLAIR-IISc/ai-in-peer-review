This paper addresses the problem of deciding whether a causal or probabilistic query is estimable from data corrupted by missing entries, given a model of the missingness process. The authors extend the results of Mohan et al. (2013) by presenting more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries and causal queries. The technical quality of the paper is high, and the results are involved and appear correct.

The authors tackle the identifiability problem with various missing-data mechanisms using Mohan et al.'s framework, improving some existing results and providing useful examples. For instance, they show that causal queries may be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations are not recoverable, and they provide a clear and interpretable theorem (Theorem 3) for non-recoverability of joint distributions.

However, some results are incremental and do not yet reveal a larger unifying picture. Section 3 feels orthogonal to the rest of the paper, and Theorem 1 is hard to comprehend, relating closely to Richardson (2009). The paper quality improves in Sections 5 and 6, which provide clear answers and interesting causal-query results.

The main weakness of the paper is its clarity. The text is dense, and the flow is poor, making it difficult to follow. The notation is confusing and inconsistent, with overuse of capital V, Vi as a vertex vs Vo as a set, and illegible subscripts. Corollary 1 exemplifies poor presentation. The lengthy 13-page appendix does not help to alleviate these issues.

The paper would benefit from a more focused approach, aiming for a general identifiability criterion rather than piecemeal results like Theorem 1. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 resemble inverse-probability weighting and are likely related to conditioning on Markov blankets for R(i) in the mixed graphs, as seen in Richardson (2003, 2009).

There are also some specific corrections needed, such as stating that R^part is a partition of R, so Rx and Ry should be stated to "belong to distinct elements of the partition." Additionally, the language and notation need to be fixed, avoiding casual terms like "oftentimes" and using \leftrightarrow instead of <-->.

The authors should also clarify whether Garcia (2013) is actually wrong or simply uses a different framework. Furthermore, the notation for graphs formed by severing edges is not defined and is used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4. Vo and Vm are referenced before being defined, apparently unnecessarily.

Despite these issues, the paper addresses an important problem and provides a useful approach. The results in Sections 5 and 6 are potentially significant and address an important problem with useful results. Overall, the paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field, but it requires significant revisions to improve its clarity, notation, and overall presentation.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper addresses an important problem in the field of graphical models for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data.
* The technical quality of the paper is high, and the results are involved and appear correct.
* The paper provides useful examples and improves some existing results.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper lacks clarity, with dense text, poor flow, and confusing notation.
* The paper is too long and would benefit from a more focused approach.
* The paper requires significant revisions to improve its presentation and notation.

Recommendation:

I recommend that the authors revise the paper to address the issues mentioned above, focusing on improving the clarity, notation, and overall presentation. With significant revisions, the paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field.
