This paper advances graphical criteria for deciding when probabilistic queries P(y, x), P(y|x) and causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable from data with missingness by explicitly modeling the missing-data mechanism via missingness graphs (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors (i) generalize ordered factorizations to include missingness indicators R, yielding a new sufficient condition for recovery that handles interacting R-nodes (Theorem 1); (ii) develop an alternative route to recover joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists, using partitions of R and collider-path Markov blankets (Theorem 2, Corollary 1); (iii) provide new non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2); and (iv) show that certain causal effects are recoverable even when Y is inseparable from its missingness indicator Ry, via do-calculus and dormant independences (Theorem 4). Necessary and sufficient conditions for “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1) for both joint distributions and causal effects (Theorems 5–6) clarify and correct prior claims (e.g., Garcia, 2013). The work connects to identifiability results (Shpitser & Pearl, 2006) and to classic MAR/MCAR considerations (Rubin, 1976), while extending the m-graph program.

Quality: The technical contributions are carefully stated, with proofs in the appendix, and the interplay between d-separation, collider paths, and do-calculus is sound. The paper is primarily theoretical; empirical demonstrations (even synthetic) of finite-sample behavior and robustness to graph misspecification would strengthen it. It would help to algorithmize construction of admissible sequences and R-partitions.

Clarity: The paper is focused and readable. Some notation could be tightened (e.g., consistent use of G_X versus GX; X′ shorthand; explicit restatement of positivity assumptions). Please add a full citation for dormant independence/d*-separation (Shpitser & Pearl, 2008), which is invoked in §6 but missing from the references.

Originality: The inclusion of R-nodes in ordered factorizations, general recovery via collider-path partitions, and explicit non-recoverability criteria are novel and meaningful advances beyond Mohan et al. (2013).

Significance: Recoverability with MNAR mechanisms is central in causal inference and ML; the provided necessary/sufficient conditions and constructive estimands are likely to be used by researchers and practitioners modeling attrition and complex missingness.

Pros:
- Strong theoretical advances with clear connections to prior work
- New non-recoverability criteria
- Insightful result that causal effects can be recoverable when probabilistic factors are not
- Coherent treatment of attrition with sharp conditions

Cons:
- Limited empirical or computational validation
- Some results focus on |Vm|=1 for causal recovery
- Minor notation and citation gaps
- Practical guidance on learning/validating m-graphs and sensitivity to misspecification could be expanded
