This paper addresses the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries. Building on previous work by Mohan et al. (2013), the authors use a graphical representation called 'm-graphs' to model both the data-generating process and the missingness mechanism. The main contributions are: (1) providing more general graphical conditions for recovering joint and conditional distributions, extending the sequential factorization theorem to handle dependencies between missingness indicators; (2) presenting novel graphical criteria for both recoverability and non-recoverability of distributions; (3) demonstrating the important result that causal queries, such as P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the full joint distribution is not; and (4) applying these findings to characterize recoverability in problems of attrition.

**Strengths**

The paper's primary strength lies in its novel theoretical contributions to the important and challenging problem of inference with missing data. The work is technically sound and rigorously extends the graphical modeling framework for missingness, particularly for Missing Not At Random (MNAR) scenarios where most standard methods fail. The distinction drawn between the recoverability of causal and probabilistic queries is particularly insightful and significant; showing that causal effects can be identified even when the joint distribution is not recoverable is a key result that advances our understanding. The paper also provides new graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3), which is a useful contribution for practitioners trying to understand the limits of what can be inferred from their data.

**Weaknesses**

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper could be improved in several areas, primarily concerning clarity and the practical applicability of the assumptions.

1.  **Assumptions and Applicability:** The entire framework rests on the strong assumption that the complete causal structure of the missingness process (the m-graph) is known a priori. It is unclear how often this assumption holds in typical machine learning applications. The authors could strengthen the paper by discussing how one might elicit or test such a model. To this end, moving some of the motivating detail from the caption of Figure 1 into the main text would be beneficial for grounding the assumed model in a practical example. Furthermore, the framing in Section 2 seems to implicitly assume the full population is known (even if some individuals are entirely unobserved), which may exclude certain problems like population size estimation. This scope limitation should be stated.

2.  **Clarity and Formalism:** Several aspects of the presentation could be improved for clarity:
    *   The definition and usage of 'm-graph' seem inconsistent. The text defines it on a set of variables V, U, R, but the figures and discussion sometimes seem to include the proxy variables V* and at other times omit them. Please clarify the formal definition and use it consistently.
    *   The term 'neighbor' is used in key theorems (e.g., Theorem 2 and 3) without a formal definition in the context of the graphs being used. This should be explicitly defined (e.g., are two nodes neighbors if they are connected by any edge, or only a directed one?).
    *   The model restricts R variables from being parents of substantive (V) or latent (U) variables. This is a strong constraint, and the authors should justify whether this is a fundamental limitation of the theory or a simplifying assumption for the current analysis.

3.  **Comparison to Prior Work:**
    *   The discussion of related work (p. 8) states that maximum likelihood (ML) yields consistent estimates under MAR. While true, this is too general. Given that this paper tackles complex MNAR scenarios, it would be valuable to explain more concretely why standard ML is insufficient for the problems considered here and how the proposed graphical criteria offer an advantage.
    *   The comparison to Garcia [2013] on attrition needs more detail. When discussing their "rectification," please clarify the nature of their positivity assumptions and how they compare to the assumptions made in this work (e.g., `P(R=0) > 0`). This would better delineate the contribution.

4.  **Minor Corrections:** Several minor fixes would improve readability:
    *   The wording "can bias outcomes" could be more precise, e.g., "can introduce bias into estimates."
    *   In Example 1, the derivation of the final expression for P(X,Y,Z) could be made clearer, particularly the step where `P(Y |X,Z,R′x, R ′ y, R ′ z)` simplifies to `P(Y|X,Z)`.
    *   Please ensure consistent notation for partitions of R variables (the paper uses R(1), R(2), R(i) which is fine, but this should be maintained consistently if used elsewhere).
    *   The statement of Corollary 1 is a bit dense; it might be helpful to briefly state what a partially observed variable in `Pasub(R(i))` would represent in this context.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and prevalent problem in statistics and machine learning.
*   Provides novel, technically sound theoretical results that significantly extend prior work on graphical models for missing data.
*   The insight on the separate recoverability of causal vs. probabilistic queries is a significant and non-obvious contribution.
*   The introduction of graphical criteria for non-recoverability is valuable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The work relies on strong, potentially unrealistic assumptions about knowing the full missingness graph, limiting its direct practical application without further guidance.
*   The paper suffers from several clarity issues in its definitions, notation, and assumptions that hinder understanding and reproducibility.
*   The practical implications and comparison to alternative methods like MLE are not fully elaborated.

In summary, this is an interesting and technically solid paper with significant theoretical contributions. Its main weakness is a lack of clarity in its presentation and a need to better motivate its core assumptions. If the authors can address the points raised above, particularly regarding the definitions and justifications for their modeling choices, this would be a strong addition to the conference.
