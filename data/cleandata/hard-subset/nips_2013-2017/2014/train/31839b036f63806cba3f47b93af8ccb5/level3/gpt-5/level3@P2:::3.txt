Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data subject to missingness, when the missingness process is explicitly modeled via missingness graphs (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors (i) generalize ordered/sequential factorization to include R-variables, enabling recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) provide a new identification strategy for joint distributions in semi-Markovian models without an admissible sequence, using partitions of R and Markov blankets defined through collider paths; (iii) give graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions; (iv) show that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when related probabilistic quantities (e.g., P(y,x), P(y|x)) are not, including cases where Y is not d-separated from its own missingness mechanism Ry; and (v) derive necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability under simple attrition with |Vm|=1. The causal recoverability results leverage do-calculus and dormant independences (Shpitser & Pearl 2006/2008). The work is squarely in the NeurIPS lineage on graphical models and missing data, extending Mohan et al. (2013) and connecting to classical MAR/MCAR (Rubin 1976), ML/EM (Dempster et al. 1977; Lauritzen 1995), and recent ML treatments of MNAR (e.g., Marlin & Zemel).

Strengths
- Clear technical contribution: generalized sequential factorization (Theorem 1) and a complementary recovery formula for joint distributions without admissible sequences (Theorem 2), both crisply stated and broadly applicable within semi-Markovian m-graphs.
- Useful negative results: new graphical non-recoverability criteria for P(V) and P(Y|X) (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2).
- Conceptually important insight: causal effects can be recoverable when the corresponding probabilistic relations are not, with precise conditions (Theorem 4) and an instructive example illustrating do-calculus–based recovery.
- Practical relevance: precise conditions for attrition (Theorems 5–6) correct common misconceptions in the applied literature and provide actionable guidance.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Assumptions about knowledge of the missingness process: Many ML applications do not have a well-specified m-graph. Please discuss how one can elicit, test (e.g., Mohan & Pearl 2014), or partially specify the missingness graph, and the robustness of your procedures to misspecification. Consider moving more of the Fig. 1 exposition into the main text to help practitioners translate domain knowledge into an m-graph.
- Scope of data-generating setting (Section 2): The setup seems to assume a fixed cohort with item missingness and known population of units, excluding unit nonresponse or unknown population size (e.g., population size estimation). Please make this explicit and discuss whether/how the results extend to settings with unit-level missingness or selection into the dataset.
- Definition and use of m-graph: The term appears to inconsistently include V* in some places and not in others. Please define once whether an m-graph includes V*, R, V, and U together (as in the formal definition) and stick to consistent notation thereafter, clarifying when the “substantive subgraph” (over V,U) is intended.
- Structural restriction on R: You forbid R variables from being parents of V or U. Since R are treated as real causal mechanisms, please justify this restriction (e.g., interpret R as selection/reporting only, with effects mediated via V*), or state that it is a modeling convenience and discuss how results would change if R→V edges were allowed.
- Missing definitions: The term “neighbor” is used without definition. Please define it (adjacent nodes in the mixed graph) early, since several theorems rely on it.
- Relation to Garcia (p.8): Your rectification of prior claims under simple attrition is persuasive, but please clarify the role of positivity/overlap. You assume positivity over complete cases. Did Garcia also assume positivity, and if not, is your correction contingent on stronger or different support conditions? A brief comparative statement would help.
- MLE under MAR (p.8): The claim that maximum likelihood yields consistent estimates under MAR is too general without context. Please (i) state the precise ignorability conditions under which the missingness mechanism can be ignored in likelihood; (ii) explain why, in your MNAR m-graphs, ML would typically require an explicit model for R and thus may or may not be consistent depending on correct specification; and (iii) clarify when your graphical recoverability can be paired with likelihood-based estimation.
- Example 1 and notation issues:
  - In the factorization around Example 1, the RHS should read P(Y|X,Z) (not conditioning on R when invoking minimality), before applying the admissibility conditions that re-introduce R′ terms via Equation 1.
  - Replace ambiguous statements of the form “Y ⟂⟂ R” with “Y ⟂⟂ Ry” when you mean the missingness mechanism of Y.
  - Unify the notation R(i), R(j), R(N), and the partition operator to avoid ambiguity.
  - In Corollary 1, the condition “Pasub(R(i)) does not contain a partially observed variable whose missing mechanism is in R(i)” needs careful wording; as written it is easy to misread. Please restate succinctly (e.g., R(i) ∩ RPasub(R(i)) = ∅) and add one illustrative example with Pasub containing both fully and partially observed parents.
  - Provide a citation for the variable-level definition referenced in Footnote 2.
- Practical guidance: Consider adding a short algorithmic summary/checklist for users: (a) test admissible sequence; (b) attempt Theorem 2 partitioning; (c) apply non-recoverability checks; (d) causal recovery via Theorem 4/6 if the effect is identifiable. A small synthetic example contrasting recoverable vs non-recoverable cases would improve accessibility.

Quality
- Technically sound with correct use of do-calculus and d-/d*-separation. Proofs are deferred to the appendix; from the main text the results appear carefully stated. The negative criteria are particularly valuable.

Clarity
- Generally well organized and readable for experts, but clarity suffers from notation inconsistencies (m-graph scope, “neighbor,” R-parent restriction) and a few imprecise statements about MLE/MAR. Addressing the above points would substantially improve readability.

Originality
- The extension of sequential factorization to include R and the recovery formula via R-partitions/Markov blankets are novel. The demonstration that P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable when associated probabilistic quantities are not is conceptually important, though related to dormant independence; here it is tailored to missing data.

Significance
- High potential impact for causal inference and missing data with explicit mechanisms, including longitudinal attrition. The results will inform both methodology and practice. Adoption in broader ML will hinge on clearer guidance for specifying/testing m-graphs.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid theoretical advances beyond Mohan et al. (2013), including both positive and negative results.
- Important insight on causal recoverability under MNAR.
- Clear, corrective guidance for attrition scenarios common in practice.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on a correctly specified m-graph; practical elicitation and robustness are under-discussed.
- Notational inconsistencies and several unclear statements reduce accessibility.
- Limited empirical or synthetic illustration demonstrating use in practice.

Recommendation
- Weak accept. The technical contributions are strong and relevant to NeurIPS, and the paper will be valuable after addressing the clarity and notation issues.

Minor comments and typos
- “can bias outcomes” → “can bias estimates and distort conclusions.”
- Qualify Figure 1 captions to indicate which edges are assumed and which are illustrative; ensure V*, R, and substantive variables are clearly distinguished.
- Replace Y || R with Y ⟂⟂ Ry throughout.
- Fix indexing consistency for partitions R(i).
- Ensure Example 1’s final expression matches the intended minimal conditioning sets.
- Add a brief discussion of strict positivity over complete cases and its empirical checkability.
