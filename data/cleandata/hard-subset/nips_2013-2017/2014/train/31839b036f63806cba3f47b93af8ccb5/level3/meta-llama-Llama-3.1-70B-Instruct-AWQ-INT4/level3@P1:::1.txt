This paper presents a comprehensive framework for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data, building upon the work of Mohan et al. (2013). The authors introduce a formal representation called "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) to depict the missingness process and define the notion of recoverability. They provide sufficient conditions for recovering joint and conditional distributions, as well as causal queries, and demonstrate the applicability of their results to problems of attrition.

The technical quality of the paper is high, and the results appear correct. Theorem 3, which provides a clear and interpretable condition for non-recoverability, is a notable contribution. The paper also includes useful examples, such as the causal identifiability of queries when conditional distributions are unavailable.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. Some results, such as Theorem 1, are incremental and do not yet reveal a larger unifying picture. The notation is confusing and inconsistent, with overuse of capital V and poor presentation of corollaries. The paper is also dense and poorly organized, with a lengthy 13-page appendix.

Despite these issues, the paper addresses an important problem and presents a useful approach. The results in Sections 5 and 6, particularly Theorem 4, are potentially significant and address an important problem with useful results. The paper would benefit from a more general identifiability criterion, analogous to causal-effect identifiability, rather than piecemeal results like Theorem 1.

In terms of related work, the paper builds upon the work of Mohan et al. (2013) and provides a more comprehensive framework for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The paper also references other works, such as Garcia (2013), but it is unclear whether Garcia's results are actually incorrect or simply use a different framework.

Overall, the paper presents a useful approach to recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data, but its presentation and notation issues significantly mar the paper. With revisions to address these issues, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
