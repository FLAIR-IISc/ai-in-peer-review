

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to provide more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (P(y|x), P(y,x)) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))) from missing data. The authors present novel graphical conditions showing that causal queries may be recoverable even when the joint distribution P(y,x) is not recoverable, specifically when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable. This represents a significant theoretical advance over prior work, as it demonstrates that causal inference can sometimes proceed despite obstacles that would block probabilistic inference.

The paper's theoretical contributions are technically sound, with well-constructed proofs and clear theorems. The extension of the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) to handle cases where R variables interact with one another is particularly valuable. The authors also provide necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of joint distributions (Theorem 2) and causal queries (Theorem 4), with the latter showing that causal effects can be recovered through d*-separation even when Y and Ry are connected by an inducing path (Example 3). This directly addresses a key limitation in prior work on missing data.

However, several important clarifications and improvements are needed. First, the paper's requirement for knowledge of the missingness process (R variables) is not adequately contextualized for typical machine learning applications, where such information is rarely available. The authors should more clearly discuss the practical feasibility of obtaining this information in real-world settings.

The paper's implicit assumption that the full population is known (Section 2) excludes important problems like population size estimation. This limitation should be acknowledged. Additionally, the inconsistent use of "mgraph" terminology (sometimes including V* variables, sometimes not) and the undefined term "neighbor" create confusion that should be addressed. The authors should clarify whether V* variables are being omitted and why, and provide a formal definition of "neighbor."

The restriction that R variables cannot be parents of V or U (Section 2) is presented without justification. Since R variables represent missingness mechanisms, it's unclear why they couldn't be causal parents of substantive variables. The authors should either justify this restriction or explicitly state it's for convenience.

The relation to Garcia (2013) regarding "rectification" needs clarification, particularly regarding positivity assumptions. The claim that "maximum likelihood yields consistent estimates under MAR" (Section 8) is too vague; the authors should explain why MLE could or could not be applied to the specific missingness processes considered here.

Minor issues include inconsistent notation (R^{(N)} vs. R^{(j)}), an error in Example 1 (RHS should be P(Y|X,Z) rather than P(Y|X,Z,R_y)), and a missing partially observed variable in Corollary 1. The paper would benefit from moving more Figure 1 detail into the main text for better clarity.

The paper's significance is substantial for causal inference and missing data problems, particularly in longitudinal studies with attrition (Section 7). The authors' application to simple attrition (Theorem 6) rectifies a previous misconception in Garcia (2013) that X must be an ancestor of Ry for non-recoverability.

Strengths:
- Theoretical novelty in showing causal queries can be recoverable when probabilistic queries are not
- General conditions for recoverability that extend prior work
- Clear application to attrition problems with necessary and sufficient conditions
- Well-structured theoretical development with proofs in the appendix

Weaknesses:
- Overlooks practical limitations of requiring complete knowledge of missingness mechanisms
- Implicit assumption about full population knowledge limits applicability
- Inconsistent terminology and undefined terms
- Insufficient discussion of practical implementation challenges
- Some minor technical errors that need correction

Overall, this is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful theoretical contribution to the field of missing data and causal inference. The results significantly extend the work of Mohan et al. (2013) and provide important new insights into recoverability conditions. However, the paper would benefit from addressing the clarity issues and better contextualizing its assumptions for broader applicability. The significance is high for researchers working with causal inference and missing data, though the practical utility in typical machine learning settings needs more careful consideration.

For acceptance, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity and technical issues noted above, particularly regarding the assumptions about missingness mechanisms and the consistency of terminology. The paper's theoretical contributions are strong enough to warrant acceptance, but the authors should improve the presentation to make the results more accessible and applicable to a wider audience.
