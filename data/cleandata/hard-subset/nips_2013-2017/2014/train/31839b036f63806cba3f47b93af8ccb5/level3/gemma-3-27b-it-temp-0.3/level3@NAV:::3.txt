## Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper tackles the important problem of identifying estimable queries from data with missing entries, extending prior work by Mohan et al. [2013] to more general missingness mechanisms. The authors derive a novel identification formula and demonstrate that causal queries can be recoverable even when the underlying joint distribution is not. This builds upon the established framework of missingness graphs and contributes to a more nuanced understanding of recoverability under complex missing data scenarios. The work is technically sound, leveraging concepts from causal inference and graphical models to provide rigorous results.

A key strength lies in the extension of sequential factorization to handle interactions between missingness mechanisms (Theorem 1) and the demonstration that causal effects can be identified even when the missingness mechanism is inseparable from the outcome (Theorem 4). The application to attrition problems (Section 7) provides a concrete example of the practical relevance of the theoretical findings. The paper is well-structured and builds logically from definitions and background to the presentation of new results.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity regarding the practical applicability of its results. A major weakness is the strong reliance on knowledge of the censoring/missingness process (R variables). While theoretically valuable, it’s unclear how often this detailed knowledge is available in typical machine learning applications. Moving more detail from Figure 1 into the main text to illustrate the complexities of these m-graphs would improve understanding. 

Several technical points require clarification. Section 2 implicitly assumes a known population, which limits applicability to problems involving population size estimation. The inconsistent use of “mgraph” (including/excluding V* variables) needs addressing. The restriction forbidding R variables as parents of V or U requires justification. Furthermore, the term “neighbor” is undefined. Specific issues on p.8 regarding the relation to Garcia et al.’s work and the vagueness of the MLE claim need to be addressed. Numerous minor errors (wording, notation, missing references) detract from the polish of the manuscript.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper presents a novel and technically sound contribution to the field of missing data analysis, extending existing theoretical frameworks and offering new insights into the recoverability of causal queries.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The practical applicability of the results is unclear, and several technical clarifications and corrections are needed before the paper can be considered a robust contribution. 

**Overall:** This is an interesting and potentially impactful paper. However, significant revisions are required to improve clarity, address technical concerns, and better articulate the practical implications of the work. I recommend acceptance contingent on major revisions.
