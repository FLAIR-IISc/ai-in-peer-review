This paper extends the results of Mohan et al. [2013] by presenting more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries and causal queries from data corrupted by missing entries. The authors introduce a formal representation called 'Missingness Graphs' (m-graphs) to explicitly portray the missingness process and dependencies among variables in the available dataset. They provide sufficient conditions for recovering joint and conditional distributions, and sufficient conditions for recovering causal queries. The authors also apply their results to problems of attrition and characterize the recovery of causal effects from data corrupted by attrition.

The paper builds on earlier work by Mohan et al. [2013] and Pearl and Mohan [2013], and is related to other work on handling missing data, such as listwise deletion, maximum likelihood method, expectation maximization algorithm, and inverse probability weighted methods. The use of graphical models for handling missing data is a relatively new development, and this paper contributes to this area by providing a more general framework for recovering probabilistic and causal queries.

The strengths of the paper include its technical soundness, its extension of earlier work, and its application to problems of attrition. The paper provides a clear and well-organized presentation of the results, and the authors have made an effort to provide a thorough discussion of the related work.

However, there are several weaknesses and areas for improvement. The paper assumes knowledge of the censoring/missingness process, which may not be available in typical machine learning applications. The term "m-graph" is used inconsistently, and the authors should clarify whether V* variables are being omitted and why. The paper forbids R variables as parents of V or U, and the authors should justify this restriction or state it is for convenience. The term "neighbor" is used without a definition and should be defined.

Additionally, the paper could benefit from more detail and clarification in several areas. For example, the relation to Garcia et al.'s "rectification" needs clarification regarding positivity assumptions. The claim that maximum likelihood yields consistent estimates under MAR is too vague, and the authors should explain why MLE could or could not be applied to the missingness processes considered here.

The paper also contains several minor errors and corrections that need to be addressed. These include wording errors, figure qualifications, and notation errors.

Overall, the paper is interesting and technically sound, but its broad applicability is unclear, and it needs several clarifications and fixes.
