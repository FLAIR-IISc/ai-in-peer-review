

# Peer Review

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. [2013] on missingness graphs (m-graphs) to develop more general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries. The authors build upon the formal representation of missingness processes using graphical models, which was introduced in Mohan et al. [2013] and has been cited in subsequent work (e.g., Mohan and Pearl [2014]). The paper makes several key contributions: 1) refined conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (joint and conditional distributions) using sequential factorization; 2) demonstration that causal queries (P(y|do(x))) may be recoverable even when underlying probabilistic relations are not; 3) new graphical criteria for non-recoverability; and 4) application to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical development. The authors provide formal definitions, theorems, and proofs for all key results, with detailed examples illustrating their application. The paper carefully distinguishes between probabilistic and causal recoverability, showing how causal queries can bypass limitations that prevent probabilistic recovery. The authors are transparent about the conditions under which their results hold and acknowledge the limitations of their framework. The paper's approach is well-grounded in the causal inference literature, building on Pearl's do-calculus and previous work on missing data mechanisms. The theoretical framework is complete and consistent, with proofs provided in the appendix.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written for readers familiar with causal inference and graphical models. The introduction effectively frames the problem and motivation. Key concepts like "general ordered factorization" and "d*-separation" are well-defined and illustrated with examples. However, the paper assumes a strong background in causal graphical models, which might limit accessibility for some readers. The examples (particularly Example 1 and Example 3) are especially helpful in clarifying the technical content. The figures (Figures 1-4) are well-designed to illustrate the graphical conditions. The paper would benefit from a brief glossary of key terms for readers less familiar with the field.

**Originality**: The paper makes significant theoretical contributions that extend Mohan et al. [2013] in several important ways. The key novelty is demonstrating that causal queries may be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not (e.g., Theorem 4 and Example 3). This insight challenges the prevailing assumption that causal recoverability requires probabilistic recoverability. The paper also develops new graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2), which fill a gap in the literature. While building on Mohan et al. [2013], the authors clearly articulate how their work differs and extends previous results. The application to attrition problems (Section 7) provides a concrete context for the theoretical results.

**Significance**: The problem of missing data is pervasive across scientific disciplines, and the paper's framework provides a formal methodology for determining when causal inference is possible despite missing data. The results have significant practical implications for researchers dealing with missing data, particularly in longitudinal studies where attrition is common. The paper's demonstration that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y and Ry are inseparable (Figure 3) challenges previous assumptions in the literature (e.g., Garcia [2013]). The work provides a valuable theoretical foundation that could guide practical approaches to missing data problems, potentially improving the validity of causal inferences in fields like epidemiology, social sciences, and machine learning.

**Strengths**: 
- Rigorous theoretical development with clear proofs
- Novel insight that causal queries may be recoverable when probabilistic queries are not
- Comprehensive treatment of both probabilistic and causal recoverability
- Clear application to attrition problems, a common practical challenge
- Well-structured presentation with helpful examples

**Weaknesses**:
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges (how to apply these conditions in real-world datasets)
- No empirical validation on real datasets (only theoretical results)
- Some technical terms (e.g., "general ordered factorization") require careful reading to fully grasp
- The connection to existing software for causal inference could be strengthened

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance**:

*Pro*:
- Addresses a significant problem (missing data) with a theoretically sound approach
- Makes a novel and important contribution to the understanding of causal inference with missing data
- Provides necessary and sufficient conditions that advance the field
- Well-motivated and rigorously developed
- Clear connection to previous work at NIPS (Mohan et al. [2013])

*Con*:
- Limited discussion of practical implementation
- No empirical validation on real datasets
- Assumes strong background in causal inference, which may limit accessibility
- Theoretical focus without sufficient discussion of computational considerations

**Recommendation**: This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of causal inference with missing data. The results extend and refine previous work at NIPS (Mohan et al. [2013]) in meaningful ways, with the key insight about causal recoverability bypassing probabilistic recoverability being particularly significant. While the paper would benefit from more discussion of practical implementation and empirical validation, the theoretical contribution is substantial and well-executed. I recommend acceptance for NIPS.

The paper's theoretical framework provides a valuable tool for researchers to determine when causal inference is possible with missing data, addressing a problem that affects nearly all empirical research. The work has the potential to influence both theoretical developments and practical approaches to missing data in causal inference.
