Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper extends the graphical framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) to decide recoverability of probabilistic queries (P(y, x), P(y|x)) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))) from data with missingness. The main contributions are: (i) a generalization of the sequential (ordered) factorization approach to include missingness indicators R in the ordering (Theorem 1), enabling recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) a new sufficient condition for recovering joint distributions in semi-Markovian models without an admissible sequence, based on partitioning R and conditioning on Markov blankets along collider paths (Theorem 2) and a corollary for models without latent confounding between R and substantive variables; (iii) graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2); (iv) a key insight that certain causal effects are recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic factors are not—specifically when Y is not d-separable from Ry—using do-calculus and dormant independences (Example 3, Theorem 4); and (v) necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of joint and causal queries in simple attrition (|Vm|=1) (Theorems 5–6). These results go beyond MAR/MCAR and build on causal identification (Shpitser & Pearl 2006), d*-separation/dormant independence (Shpitser & Pearl 2008), and ADMG separation (Richardson 2003), filling important gaps left by prior NIPS work (Mohan et al. 2013).

Quality:
The theoretical results are clearly stated, with proofs deferred to the appendix. The general ordered factorization (Def. 2, Thm. 1) is technically sound and the Example 1 convincingly shows why minimality is crucial. Theorem 2’s construction via R-partitions and Markov blankets along collider paths provides a workable sufficient condition beyond admissible sequences and appears consistent with ADMG separation rules. The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) are valuable and align with intuition from selection bias via colliders. The insight that causal effects can be recoverable despite inseparability of Y and Ry (Example 3, Theorem 4) is particularly strong, relying on do-calculus to condition on Ry=0 in the estimand without introducing bias when dormant independences hold.

However, some conditions are intricate and could be hard to check by a practitioner. The “general algorithms” claim (Section 4) reads more as constructive formulas/criteria rather than a complete decision procedure; a more explicit algorithm (with pseudocode) for finding admissible sequences, building R-partitions, and verifying the d*/do-calculus conditions would strengthen the contribution. The reliance on strict positivity over complete cases is noted, but the brief remark about extensions beyond positivity is not developed. Theorem 4’s conditions (existence of Z, W satisfying d*-separation and identifiability of P(W1,Z|do(X)) and P(Y|do(W1),do(X),Z,Ry=0)) are powerful but abstract; more guidance on verifying them graphically would help. Lastly, no empirical or synthetic demonstrations are provided; while this is a theory paper, small illustrative simulations would improve accessibility.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-organized with a clear progression from probabilistic to causal queries and to attrition. Notation is heavy and occasionally confusing. Examples:
- “GX and GX represent graphs formed by removing from G all edges leaving and entering X, respectively” appears to use the same symbol twice; please differentiate (e.g., G\X_out and G\X_in).
- The use of X′ to mean X=0 and R′x to mean Rx=0 is nonstandard and may confuse readers; consider clearer notation or an explicit conventions table.
- The condition statements in Theorem 1 are dense; a short graphical checklist would aid readers.
Adding a high-level intuition for each theorem (one sentence on why it works) and a summary figure showing where each result applies would help. Reference formatting has inconsistencies (years/venues).

Originality:
The paper makes several original advances beyond Mohan et al. (2013): allowing interacting R mechanisms in ordered factorizations, new sufficient conditions without admissible sequences, explicit non-recoverability criteria, and the demonstration that causal effects can be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic distributions are not, via conditioning on Ry in the estimand. The necessary and sufficient results for simple attrition refine and correct claims in prior literature (e.g., Garcia 2013). To my knowledge, these contributions were not previously available in a unified graphical treatment.

Significance:
The results are important for practitioners working with MNAR data and causal inference: they provide principled criteria to decide when recovery is possible, and constructive estimands when it is. The characterization for attrition is practically relevant. The insight about causal recoverability despite probabilistic non-recoverability could influence how analysts approach MNAR problems. Adoption may be limited by the technical nature of the conditions; providing tool support or a decision procedure could broaden impact.

Specific suggestions/questions:
- Provide an explicit algorithmic procedure to (a) search for admissible sequences (Def. 2), (b) construct R-partitions and Mb(R(i)) (Thm. 2), and (c) verify Theorem 4’s conditions graphically.
- Clarify assumptions on edges among R: Section 3 leverages R→R edges; ensure consistency with the restriction “R variables may not be parents of V∪U.”
- Discuss complexity and scalability of the proposed recovery procedures, especially when summing over high-dimensional R.
- Expand on the positivity assumption: how sensitive are the estimands to near-violations?
- Integrate or point to concrete tests for model misspecification (Mohan & Pearl 2014) in the workflow.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, nontrivial theoretical advances beyond prior NIPS work.
- Clear new non-recoverability criteria.
- Novel causal recoverability results under inseparability (important conceptual insight).
- Practical application to attrition with necessary and sufficient conditions.
- Good fit for NIPS topics in causal inference and probabilistic modeling.

Cons for acceptance:
- Heavy notation and occasional ambiguities; some conditions are hard to check without tooling.
- “Algorithms” are mostly criteria; lacks a unified decision procedure and empirical demonstrations.
- Theorem 4’s applicability may be perceived as niche without more examples/guidelines.

Recommendation: Accept. A solid theoretical contribution that extends the frontier of recoverability from MNAR data, with both conceptual and practical implications for causal inference.
