- Addresses recovery of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries using and extending "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs).  
- Shows more general recoverability conditions, including cases where causal queries are recoverable even if underlying probabilistic relations are not.  
- Key contribution: P(y | do(x)) can be recovered without Y and its missingness mechanism being d-separable, relaxing common identifiability assumptions.  
- Applies theoretical results to the practical problem of attrition.  
- Well-motivated: missing data is pervasive and m-graphs offer a formal, intuitive representation of missingness plus causal structure.  
- Builds logically on Mohan et al. [2013] and Pearl & Mohan [2013], incrementally expanding the theory.  
- Paper contains numerous theorems and corollaries, providing a rigorous mathematical foundation.  
- Technical depth is high, but dense notation and complex proofs impede readability.  
- Presentation feels fragmented; sections lack explicit narrative links and smooth transitions between concepts.  
- Several theorems are presented consecutively without a cohesive discussion of their overall implications.  
- Does not provide practical algorithms or detailed implementation strategies for estimating recoverable queries.  
- Examples are helpful but relatively simple and may not reflect real-world missing data complexity.  
- Related work section is extensive but reads as a list rather than a critical comparison showing how this work advances the field.  
- Mentions Garcia [2013] but fails to discuss nuances and limitations of the simple attrition assumption.  
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretical significance, novelty (relaxing d-separability), rigor, and relevance to attrition problems.  
- Arguments against acceptance: clarity issues, lack of practical guidance/algorithms, limited example complexity, and weakly connected related work.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions—improve clarity with a high-level overview, add discussion of implementation strategies, and provide a more analytical related-work comparison.
