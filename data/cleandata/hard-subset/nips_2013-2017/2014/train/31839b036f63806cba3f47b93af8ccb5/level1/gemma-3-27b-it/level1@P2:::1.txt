This paper tackles the important problem of recovering causal and probabilistic queries from datasets with missing data, building upon the framework of missingness graphs introduced by Mohan et al. (2013). The core contribution lies in extending existing recoverability conditions to more general scenarios, particularly those where the missingness mechanism is complex and interacts with the substantive variables. Specifically, the authors demonstrate that causal queries (P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relationships (P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are not, a result with significant practical implications. The paper is well-structured, covering necessary definitions, theoretical extensions, and applications to attrition.

**Quality:** The paper is generally technically sound. The extensions to the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) and the introduction of new non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3) appear logically derived and appropriately generalized. The treatment of causal queries and their potential recoverability despite non-recoverable probabilistic relationships is a strong point. However, the numerous theorems and corollaries, while contributing to the theoretical depth, sometimes lack sufficient intuitive explanation alongside the mathematical formalism. The reliance on examples to illustrate the concepts is helpful but could be more systematic. The discussion of attrition in section 7 feels somewhat cursory, simply applying the derived theorems without delving into the nuances of different attrition models. While the proofs are relegated to the appendix, a more concise justification of key steps within the main text would strengthen the argument. The claim that the results "rectify prevailing opinion" in Garcia (2013) requires more rigorous substantiation.

**Clarity:** The paper is mostly clearly written, but suffers from density. The notation is consistent, but numerous definitions and theorems are presented without sufficient motivation or “big picture” explanation. A reader unfamiliar with the m-graph framework would likely struggle to grasp the core concepts without substantial effort. The examples are helpful, but more visual aids illustrating the graphical models would enhance understanding.  The transitions between sections could be smoother, and the organization occasionally feels fragmented.  For example, the jump from Theorem 2 to Corollary 1 is not particularly well-motivated.

**Originality:** The paper builds directly upon the work of Mohan et al. (2013) and Pearl & Mohan (2013), extending their framework rather than introducing a radically new approach. However, the extensions—particularly the demonstration of causal query recoverability under conditions where probabilistic queries are not—are novel and represent a valuable contribution. The identification of specific graphical criteria for non-recoverability is also a novel addition. The paper thoroughly cites related work, including both classical missing data literature (Rubin, Little & Rubin) and more recent advances in graphical models and causal inference. It correctly identifies a gap in the literature regarding interactions among missingness mechanisms.

**Significance:**  The results presented have the potential to be significant. The ability to recover causal effects even when probabilistic relationships are unidentifiable is a crucial finding, particularly in fields like epidemiology and social science where missing data is pervasive. The work addresses a difficult problem in a more general and nuanced way than previous research. The applicability to attrition scenarios is a concrete example of the paper's practical relevance. The formalization of non-recoverability criteria is also valuable, providing guidance for practitioners when faced with limited data and assumptions. However, the practical impact will depend on the adoption of the m-graph framework by researchers and the development of efficient algorithms for applying these recoverability criteria in real-world datasets.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound extensions to the existing m-graph framework.
*   Significant result regarding recoverability of causal queries despite non-recoverable probabilistic relationships.
*   Identifies useful graphical criteria for non-recoverability.
*   Addresses an important problem with broad applicability.
*   Thorough related work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is dense and potentially inaccessible to readers unfamiliar with the background.
*   The connection between the theoretical results and practical implications (e.g., attrition scenarios) could be strengthened.
*   Lack of intuitive explanation alongside the mathematical formalism.
*   The claim of “rectifying prevailing opinion” requires more compelling evidence.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of missing data and causal inference. While the paper could benefit from improved clarity and more thorough illustration of the practical implications, the theoretical advancements are significant and the results are potentially impactful. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the clarity and explanatory issues. Specifically, the authors should consider adding more intuitive explanations of the theorems and incorporating more visual aids to illustrate the graphical models. They should also provide more detailed discussion of the limitations and potential challenges of applying these results in real-world settings.
