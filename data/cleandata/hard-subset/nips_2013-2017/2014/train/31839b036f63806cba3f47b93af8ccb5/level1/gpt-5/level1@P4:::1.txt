Summary
The paper studies when probabilistic queries (joint P(V), conditional P(y|x)) and causal queries P(y|do(x)) are estimable from data with missing entries, given a causal m-graph that encodes the missingness process. It extends Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) in four main ways:

1) Generalized sequential factorization: The authors introduce “general ordered factorization,” allowing missingness indicators R to appear in the admissible factorization. Theorem 1 gives sufficient conditions under which each conditional factor is estimable from the manifest distribution, enabling recovery of joint/conditional distributions in settings where missingness mechanisms interact (e.g., R nodes with edges among them), which were not handled by the 2013 result.

2) Recovery without admissible sequences: For models where no admissible sequence exists, Theorem 2 uses partitions of R and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)) defined via collider paths to give sufficient conditions and a constructive formula for recovering P(V). A corollary covers the case with no latent confounding between R and substantive variables.

3) Non-recoverability criteria: Theorem 3 provides necessary graphical conditions for P(V) to be recoverable (no adjacency and no all-collider paths between X and Rx for any partially observed X). Corollary 2 yields necessary conditions for non-recoverability of P(y|x), using adjacency or certain collider paths between Y and Ry with intermediates in X.

4) Causal effects despite inseparability: The paper shows that some causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when P(V) or P(y|x) are not, exploiting do-calculus, d*-separation, and dormant independences. Theorem 4 formalizes recovery of P(y|do(x)) when Y and Ry are connected by an inducing path, under identifiability of certain interventional distributions. Applications to simple attrition (|Vm|=1) yield necessary and sufficient conditions: Theorem 5 for P(V) and Theorem 6 for P(y|do(x)). These results contrast with claims in Garcia (2013).

Relation to prior work: Builds directly on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) by broadening admissible factorizations and adding non-recoverability criteria. It leverages identifiability results via do-calculus (Shpitser & Pearl 2006; Pearl 2009) and d*-separation (Shpitser & Pearl 2008), and contrasts with common MAR/MCAR-based approaches (Rubin 1976; Little & Rubin 2002) and ML/EM methods (Dempster et al. 1977; Lauritzen 1995; Koller & Friedman 2009). The attrition analysis relates to recent causal graph treatments (Garcia 2013; Thoemmes & Rose 2013).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The statements are precise, and conditions are graph-theoretic and checkable. Theorems 1–3 provide clear sufficient/necessary conditions; Theorems 5–6 give tight characterizations for |Vm|=1. The constructive formulas (e.g., in Theorems 1–2) are consistent with the m-graph semantics and positivity assumptions. The use of do-calculus to recover causal effects under MNAR mechanisms is principled. Proofs are deferred to the appendix; from the text and examples, the arguments appear coherent and consistent with prior theory.
- Completeness: The work provides sufficient (not complete) criteria for general semi-Markovian graphs; a full decision procedure for recoverability with arbitrary |Vm| remains open. The paper covers identifiability prerequisites but does not offer algorithmic complexity or full automation for checking theorems (though definitions imply a polynomial-time graph check is plausible).
- Empirics: Results are theoretical; no simulations or case studies quantify finite-sample performance of the proposed estimators. This limits practical validation but is not uncommon for theory-focused NIPS work.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written, with consistent notation (Vo, Vm, R, V*, Rx, etc.) and clearly stated assumptions (positivity on complete cases). The motivation for minimality in the generalized factorization is well illustrated. Definitions of collider paths, R partitions, and Mb(R(i)) are provided with examples. 
- Suggestions: 
  - Provide pseudocode or a checklist to (i) construct admissible sequences including R, and (ii) build R partitions and Mb(R(i)) to apply Theorem 2. 
  - Clarify the scope of latent confounding allowed in Theorem 2 vs. Corollary 1 with an explicit example. 
  - Improve citation formatting and minor typos in references.

Originality
- Novel generalized factorization including R nodes for recovery under interacting missingness mechanisms.
- New necessary non-recoverability criteria for P(V) and P(y|x).
- Demonstration that causal effects can be recoverable even when associated probabilistic factors are not, with formal conditions (Theorem 4).
- Tight necessary and sufficient results for simple attrition that correct prior claims (Garcia 2013).
These contributions extend NIPS 2013 in meaningful, nontrivial ways.

Significance
- Understanding recoverability under MNAR with explicit missingness graphs is important for causal inference and ML applications (e.g., longitudinal studies, surveys, recommender systems with nonrandom missingness).
- The results provide actionable graphical criteria and constructive estimands that practitioners can use when MAR/MCAR assumptions fail.
- Limitations include lack of a complete decision procedure for general graphs and absence of empirical validation or robustness analysis to m-graph misspecification (though related testability work is cited).

Strengths
- Clear theoretical advances over Mohan et al. (2013) with broader applicability.
- Both positive (recoverability) and negative (non-recoverability) criteria, increasing diagnostic value.
- Insightful causal recovery results leveraging do-calculus, including cases where P(V) is non-recoverable.
- Necessary and sufficient characterizations for |Vm|=1 are crisp and useful.

Weaknesses
- No experimental or synthetic validation to assess estimator variance and sample complexity.
- Practical guidance for automatically finding admissible sequences/partitions is limited; complexity not analyzed.
- Assumes correct m-graph specification; sensitivity to misspecification not explored within this paper.
- Some notation and reference formatting issues; figures are central but not fully elaborated in the text.

Questions/clarifications for authors
- Can you provide an explicit algorithm (and complexity) for constructing R partitions and Mb(R(i)) and deciding applicability of Theorem 2?
- Is there a systematic procedure to search for admissible sequences per Theorem 1 beyond manual inspection?
- How sensitive are the causal recovery formulas to violations of positivity over complete cases?
- Could you add a small simulated example comparing recovered estimators with ground truth under MNAR?

Arguments for acceptance
- Substantive theoretical contributions with clear novelty over prior NIPS work.
- Practical, checkable graphical criteria and constructive estimands.
- Addresses an important and common setting (MNAR, attrition) with tight results.
- Bridges probabilistic and causal recovery, showing cases where causal effects remain estimable.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of empirical demonstrations or practical algorithms with complexity guarantees.
- Incomplete general decision procedure for arbitrary semi-Markovian graphs.
- Dependence on correctly specified m-graphs without in-paper sensitivity analysis.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution extending the graphical treatment of missing data and causal recoverability, with notable insights and useful criteria, albeit with limited empirical validation and automation details.
