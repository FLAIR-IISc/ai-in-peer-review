This paper presents a formal graphical framework for determining whether probabilistic and causal queries can be recovered from data with missing values. Building directly on the m-graph framework introduced by Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors provide several significant extensions. Key contributions include more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries, particularly through a generalized sequential factorization theorem that handles dependencies between missingness mechanisms. The paper also introduces novel and useful graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, a crucial aspect often overlooked. The most striking result is showing that a causal query, P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic distribution, P(y,x), is not, due to dependencies between a variable and its missingness mechanism. These theoretical results are then applied to provide necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery in problems of attrition.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles the fundamental and highly practical problem of missing data. By providing formal, graphical criteria for recoverability under general Missing Not At Random (MNAR) conditions, it advances the field beyond the more restrictive MAR/MCAR assumptions common in practice. The finding that causal queries can be recoverable when probabilistic queries are not is a particularly important and insightful contribution for researchers in causality and applied sciences.
*   **Originality:** This work is a non-trivial and valuable extension of prior art. The introduction of non-recoverability criteria and the demonstration of the disconnect between probabilistic and causal recoverability are novel. The application to attrition also appears to correct and clarify previous results in that domain.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, presenting a series of formal theorems and corollaries. The claims are well-defined within the do-calculus and m-graph frameworks, and the graphical examples effectively illustrate the utility and intuition behind the main theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** While generally well-written, the paper is notationally dense and assumes significant familiarity with the m-graph formalism. A brief, intuitive recap of the core concepts in Section 2 could improve accessibility for the broader NIPS audience. Additionally, defining concepts like `R(part)` before presenting the theorems that rely on them would improve readability.
*   **Evaluation:** The work is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a simulation study. Demonstrating the proposed recovery procedures on synthetic data would provide empirical validation of the theorems and give readers a more concrete sense of their practical applicability and performance.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of inference with missing data. The strengths in originality and significance are clear, and the weaknesses are primarily related to presentation and lack of empirical validation, which can be addressed.
