- Paper studies recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missingness using m-graphs, extending Mohan et al. (2013).
- Contribution (i): Generalizes ordered factorization to include missingness indicators R, yielding a sequential factorization theorem handling interacting R nodes (Theorem 1).
- Contribution (ii): Provides recoverability results without an admissible sequence via R partitions and Markov blankets in ADMGs (Theorem 2 and corollary).
- Contribution (iii): Derives graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2), addressing prior gaps.
- Contribution (iv): Shows some causal effects are recoverable even when corresponding probabilistic relations are not, using do-calculus and dormant independencies (Theorem 4).
- Contribution (v): Gives necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for joint distributions and causal effects under simple attrition, correcting prior claims (Theorems 5–6).
- Strength: Extends recoverability theory to interacting missingness mechanisms with well-motivated generalized factorization and minimality requirement.
- Strength: Introduces practical non-recoverability criteria that help diagnose impossibility, filling a literature gap.
- Strength: Conceptually important demonstration that causal effects can be recoverable under MNAR when probabilistic relations are not.
- Strength: Crisp, necessary-and-sufficient attrition results for |Vm|=1 and correction of a prevalent misconception.
- Strength: Graphical framing enables implementable checks and clearer communication of assumptions.
- Weakness: Many results are sufficient but not complete; gap remains between necessary and sufficient conditions for P(V).
- Weakness: Claims of “general algorithms” are unsupported by explicit constructive procedures, pseudocode, or complexity analysis.
- Weakness: Theorem 4’s assumptions can be strong; scope and comparison to general ID algorithm need clarification.
- Weakness: Positivity assumption limited to complete cases; effects of violations and extensions need more explicit treatment or examples.
- Clarity issue: Notation is dense; a compact notation table and a running example across sections are recommended.
- Clarity issue: Minimality requirement needs an algorithmic test and more illustrative examples where it matters.
- Clarity issue: A decision flowchart for recoverability of P(V), P(Y|X), and P(Y|do(X)) would aid readers; references and figure labeling need cleanup.
- Originality: Novel extensions to interacting R nodes, non-recoverability criteria, and causal recoverability under inseparability beyond prior work.
- Significance: Broadly relevant to causal inference and ML with MNAR data; graphical tests can guide estimation/design decisions.
- Questions for authors: Can Theorem 2 be tightened or bounded via counterexamples; how to search for admissible sequences efficiently; can Theorem 4 conditions be automated; how do auxiliary variables affect recoverability?
- Pros for acceptance: Clear theoretical advances, addresses interacting missingness, and provides elegant causal vs probabilistic insights plus strong attrition results.
- Cons for acceptance: Limited algorithmic exposition, no empirical demonstrations, some results restricted (e.g., |Vm|=1), and presentation/notation issues.
- Overall: Solid theoretical contribution with recommendation for acceptance pending improved algorithmic clarity and presentation fixes.
