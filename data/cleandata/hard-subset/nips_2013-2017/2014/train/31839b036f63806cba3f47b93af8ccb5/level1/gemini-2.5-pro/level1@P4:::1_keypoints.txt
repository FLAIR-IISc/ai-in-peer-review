- Tackles recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries with missing data using m-graphs, building on Mohan et al. (2013).  
- Generalizes sequential factorization to handle interdependent missingness mechanisms common in longitudinal studies.  
- Gives a new sufficient condition for recovering the full joint in semi‑Markovian models via partitioning missingness variables and analyzing their Markov blankets.  
- Introduces graphical criteria for non‑recoverability (Theorem 3) to identify queries that cannot be estimated.  
- Shows causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the joint P(y,x) is not, using a theorem based on d*-separation.  
- Applies the theoretical results to attrition in experiments, providing necessary and sufficient conditions and correcting prior claims.  
- Strength — originality/significance: provides novel, impactful tools (non‑recoverability criteria and causal vs probabilistic distinction).  
- Strength — technical soundness: rigorous theorems grounded in graphical models, do‑calculus, and illustrative examples.  
- Strength — clarity/organization: well‑structured progression from background to contributions with examples and an applied section.  
- Weakness — no empirical evaluation: entirely theoretical with no simulations or real‑data experiments to validate practical behavior.  
- Weakness — dense presentation: assumes deep familiarity with causality literature and introduces some terms before formal definitions.  
- Weakness — practical applicability unclear: lacks discussion of algorithms, computational complexity, and scalability for applying the graphical checks.  
- Overall: substantial theoretical advance in missing‑data causality with important tools for researchers, but would be strengthened by experiments and clearer, more practical guidance.
