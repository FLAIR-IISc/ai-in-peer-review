- Paper studies when P(y, x), P(y|x) and P(y|do(x)) are recoverable from data with missingness given an m-graph.  
- Contribution (i): Generalizes ordered factorization to include missingness indicators R, giving a stronger sequential factorization that handles interactions among R variables.  
- Contribution (ii): Introduces a recovery formula for joint distributions in semi-Markovian models via partitions of R and Markov blankets, enabling recoverability without an admissible sequence.  
- Contribution (iii): Provides graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions.  
- Contribution (iv): Shows certain causal effects are recoverable even when associated probabilistic factors are not, using d*-separation and dormant independences.  
- Applies results to attrition, giving necessary and sufficient recoverability conditions when |Vm| = 1 and correcting prior claims (Garcia 2013).  
- Connects to identification in ADMGs, do-calculus, and extends the MNAR line from Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013); complements MAR/MCAR and ML MNAR work.  
- Quality: Theoretical claims are carefully stated with clear counterexamples and constructive formulas; non-recoverability conditions fill an important gap.  
- Limitation: Limited guidance on algorithmic implementation and complexity analysis.  
- Limitation: No empirical validation, simulations, or software to test criteria under finite samples or misspecification.  
- Clarity: Generally readable with illustrative figures, but notation is dense, some concepts are defined late, and references/typography need polishing.  
- Reproducibility suggestion: A procedural checklist or pseudocode for checking admissible sequences and applying Theorems 2–6 would help.  
- Originality: Extensions (factorization with R), general semi-Markovian recovery, and causal recoverability under Y–Ry inseparability are novel.  
- Significance: Advances MNAR recoverability theory with practical implications for longitudinal studies with attrition.  
- Pros for acceptance: Strong novel theoretical contributions, clear extension of prior work, and impactful necessary/sufficient conditions for simple attrition.  
- Cons against acceptance: Lack of algorithmic automation/complexity analysis, absence of empirical demonstrations/software, and clarity/formatting issues.  
- Overall: A solid theoretical paper; reviewer leans toward acceptance subject to improvements in clarity and implementation guidance.
