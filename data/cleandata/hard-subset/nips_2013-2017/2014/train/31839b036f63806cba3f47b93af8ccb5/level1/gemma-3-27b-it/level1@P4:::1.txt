This paper addresses the problem of recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries, building upon the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. The authors extend previous work by presenting more general conditions for recovery, particularly demonstrating that causal queries can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not. A key contribution is showing recoverability of P(y|do(x)) even when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable. The work focuses on refining sequential factorization theorems and handling cases where these fail, including applications to attrition problems.

**Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The theoretical results are presented with accompanying theorems and, importantly, proofs are relegated to the appendix. The extension of sequential factorization and the introduction of new criteria for non-recoverability contribute to a more comprehensive understanding of query recoverability. The analysis of attrition, while building directly on the theoretical contributions, provides a concrete application. However, the presentation relies heavily on existing notation from Mohan et al. [2013], which might hinder accessibility for readers unfamiliar with that prior work. The discussion of trivially recoverable queries and the extension to non-recoverability criteria are well-developed. The authors attempt to address potential limitations and caveats, enhancing the overall rigor.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized, following a logical progression from definitions and background to extensions and applications. However, the density of notation and the reliance on prior work necessitate a strong background in causal inference and graphical models to fully grasp the arguments. Several examples are provided, which help illustrate the concepts, but some could be elaborated further for greater clarity. The discussion of definitions (e.g., d*-separation, inducing path) feels somewhat abrupt and assumes prior knowledge. While the section on attrition provides a practical application, it could benefit from more detailed explanations of how the theoretical results are specifically applied in this context.

**Originality:** The paper presents a significant extension of the work by Mohan et al. [2013]. The key novelty lies in demonstrating the recoverability of causal queries under weaker conditions than previously known, specifically when d-separation between Y and its missingness mechanism does not hold. The generalization of sequential factorization and the introduction of criteria for non-recoverability also contribute to originality.  While building on a defined framework, the authors push the boundaries of that framework and offer valuable insights into the limitations and possibilities of recovering causal effects from incomplete data. The identification of scenarios where causal queries *are* recoverable while probabilistic queries aren't is a particularly strong contribution.

**Significance:** The results are potentially important for researchers and practitioners dealing with missing data, particularly in causal inference. The ability to recover causal effects even when underlying probabilistic relationships are not recoverable represents a significant advancement. This has practical implications for a wide range of fields, including epidemiology, social sciences, and econometrics, where attrition and other forms of missing data are common. The paper provides a more nuanced understanding of the conditions under which causal inference is possible with incomplete data, moving beyond simple MAR/MCAR classifications.  The connection to attrition provides a compelling real-world application, highlighting the relevance of the theoretical results.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of previous work on recoverability of causal queries.
*   Demonstrates recoverability of P(y|do(x)) under weaker conditions than previously known.
*   Provides valuable theoretical contributions to the field of missing data and causal inference.
*   Strong technical soundness and rigorous proofs.
*   Clear application to the important problem of attrition.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   High reliance on prior work (Mohan et al. [2013]), potentially limiting accessibility.
*   Dense notation and technical presentation may be challenging for some readers.
*   Examples, while helpful, could be more detailed and pedagogical.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the challenging problem of causal inference with missing data. While the technical presentation might require a strong background in the field, the novelty and significance of the results outweigh this drawback. The authors have clearly identified a gap in the existing literature and provided a valuable extension to the theoretical framework for handling missing data.
