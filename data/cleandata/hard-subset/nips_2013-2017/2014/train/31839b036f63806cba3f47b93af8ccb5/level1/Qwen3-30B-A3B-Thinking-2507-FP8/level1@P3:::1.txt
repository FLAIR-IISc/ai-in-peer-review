

**Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"**

This paper extends the foundational work of Mohan et al. (2013) on missingness graphs (m-graphs) to develop more general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. The authors address a critical problem in empirical research: determining whether a query can be consistently estimated from missing data, given a model of the missingness process. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by demonstrating that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can sometimes be recovered even when the probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y,x)) required to compute them are not recoverable—a key insight that challenges prevailing assumptions in the field.

**Strengths:**

*Technical Soundness and Theoretical Contribution:* The paper provides rigorous theoretical results with detailed proofs in the appendix. The extension of the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact (e.g., R variables influencing each other) is a meaningful technical advance. The most novel contribution is Theorem 4 and Example 3, which demonstrate that causal queries may be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable. This directly challenges the intuition that recoverability of probabilistic queries is a prerequisite for causal recoverability. The paper correctly identifies and addresses a gap in the literature (e.g., correcting Garcia's 2013 misconception about attrition).

*Clarity and Organization:* The paper is exceptionally well-structured, with clear definitions, theorems, and examples. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, and the section on causal queries (Section 6) is particularly strong, with Example 3 providing a compelling illustration of the key insight. The use of m-graphs to visualize the missingness process is intuitive and helps communicate assumptions about the missingness mechanism. The related work section (Section 8) comprehensively covers previous approaches to missing data (MCAR, MAR, MNAR), graphical models for missing data, and causal approaches to attrition.

*Significance:* Missing data is a pervasive issue in scientific research, and this work provides a formal framework for diagnosing whether queries can be recovered from missing data. The application to attrition (Section 7) is particularly relevant, as attrition is a common problem in longitudinal studies. The paper's results have clear implications for practitioners conducting causal inference with missing data, especially in fields like epidemiology and social sciences.

**Weaknesses:**

*Limited Empirical Validation:* While the paper provides strong theoretical results, it lacks empirical validation. A small simulation study demonstrating the practical application of the theorems to synthetic datasets with missingness would strengthen the paper by showing how the conditions in the theorems correctly identify recoverable vs. non-recoverable queries in practice. This is especially important given the complexity of the conditions.

*Complexity of Conditions:* The conditions for recoverability (particularly Theorem 4) are quite complex and may be challenging for practitioners to apply directly. While the paper provides examples, it would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how to implement these conditions in practice, perhaps with a simple algorithm or checklist for practitioners.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the problem of missing data in causal inference. The key insight that causal queries may be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic queries are not is novel, important, and well-supported by the theoretical results. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a problem of clear significance to the field.

The main weakness is the lack of empirical validation, which is a common limitation for theoretical papers but would enhance the practical relevance of the work. However, this does not undermine the theoretical contribution, which is substantial and well-executed.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Novel theoretical contribution showing causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not
- Rigorous proofs and well-defined conditions for recoverability
- Addresses a significant problem in empirical research (missing data)
- Corrects a misconception in the literature regarding attrition (Garcia, 2013)

*Con:*
- Limited empirical validation to demonstrate practical application
- Conditions for recoverability may be complex for practitioners to implement

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend acceptance. The theoretical contribution is significant, the paper is well-written and technically sound, and the results have clear implications for causal inference with missing data. The paper extends the important work of Mohan et al. (2013) in a meaningful way, providing more general conditions that advance our understanding of missing data in causal inference. It would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
