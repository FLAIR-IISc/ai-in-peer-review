This paper addresses the problem of determining whether a probabilistic or causal query can be recovered (i.e., consistently estimated) from a dataset with missing values. The work builds directly upon Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), which introduced "missingness graphs" (m-graphs) as a formal framework for representing dependencies between variables and their missingness mechanisms.

The main contributions of this paper are several significant extensions to the theory of recoverability. First, it generalizes the sequential factorization theorem from prior work to handle cases where missingness mechanisms can influence each other, a common scenario in longitudinal studies. Second, for cases where sequential factorization is not applicable, it provides a new sufficient condition for recovering the full joint distribution in semi-markovian models, based on partitioning the missingness variables and analyzing their Markov blankets. Third, and perhaps most importantly, the paper introduces novel graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, which serve as formal warnings that a query cannot be estimated from the given data and model assumptions. Finally, the paper makes a crucial distinction between the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries. It demonstrates, through a new theorem based on d*-separation, that a causal effect P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the corresponding joint P(y, x) is not. These theoretical results are then applied to the specific problem of attrition in experimental studies, yielding necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects and correcting claims made in related literature.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes several novel and important contributions. The introduction of graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) is a significant step forward, as it provides researchers with formal tools to identify impossible estimation tasks, complementing existing tools for identifying recoverable ones. The insight that causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not (Section 6) is a deep and impactful result, connecting the theory of missing data with advanced concepts from causal identification (e.g., dormant independence). This substantially advances the state of the art in understanding the interplay between missing data and causal inference.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The paper is theoretically rigorous. It presents a series of formal theorems and corollaries that logically extend previous work. The contributions are well-grounded in the established frameworks of graphical models (d-separation) and causal inference (do-calculus, d*-separation), lending credibility to the results. The arguments are supported by illustrative examples that clarify the necessity and application of the new theorems.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-structured. It begins by reviewing the necessary background on m-graphs and recoverability before progressively introducing its new contributions. Each theoretical result is typically followed by an example that demonstrates its utility and distinguishes it from previous conditions. The application of the general theory to the specific problem of attrition in Section 7 provides a clear demonstration of the framework's practical relevance.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the absence of any experiments on either synthetic or real-world data is a notable omission. Simulation studies could have been used to empirically validate the theoretical claims, for example, by demonstrating that an estimator for a query deemed "recoverable" is consistent, while a naive estimator for a "non-recoverable" query is biased. Such experiments would strengthen the paper's practical impact and provide a more complete picture of the proposed framework.

2.  **Density of Material:** The paper is technically dense and presumes significant familiarity with the graphical causality literature (e.g., semi-markovian models, collider paths, do-calculus, d*-separation). While precise, some definitions and concepts are introduced in a slightly unconventional order. For example, the terms `R(part)` and `Mb(R(i))` are used in Theorem 2 before they are formally defined, which may disrupt the reading flow. A more streamlined presentation of definitions could improve accessibility for a broader audience.

3.  **Practical Applicability:** The paper focuses on the theoretical conditions for recoverability but does not discuss the algorithmic aspects of applying these results. For instance, it is not immediately clear what the computational complexity would be to check the graphical criteria presented in the theorems for a large, complex m-graph. A discussion on the practical implementation and scalability of these checks would make the work more directly useful for practitioners.

In summary, this paper presents a set of strong, novel, and significant theoretical results that advance the formal understanding of inference with missing data. The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability and the introduction of non-recoverability criteria are particularly important contributions. While the paper would have benefited from empirical validation and a clearer exposition of some technical concepts, its theoretical merits are substantial. It is a solid piece of work that researchers in causality and machine learning will likely build upon.
