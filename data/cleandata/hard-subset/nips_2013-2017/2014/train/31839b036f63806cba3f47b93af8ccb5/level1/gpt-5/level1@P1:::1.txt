Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies when probabilistic queries (P(y, x), P(y|x)) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))) are estimable (“recoverable”) from data with missing entries, given a graphical representation of the missingness process via missingness graphs (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors (i) generalize the ordered factorization approach to include missingness indicators R, yielding a new sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) that handles interacting missingness mechanisms; (ii) provide recoverability results in the absence of an admissible sequence (Theorem 2, with an associated corollary) by exploiting partitions of R variables and their Markov blankets in semi-Markovian models (ADMGs; Richardson, 2003); (iii) derive graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2), filling a gap in prior work that focused on sufficiency; (iv) show that some causal effects are recoverable even when associated probabilistic relations are not, leveraging do-calculus and dormant independencies (Theorem 4), hence overcoming inseparability between Y and its missingness mechanism Ry; and (v) apply these ideas to attrition, providing necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for joint distributions (Theorem 5) and causal effects (Theorem 6) under “simple attrition,” and correcting claims in Garcia (2013). The work connects to identification theory (Shpitser & Pearl, 2006) and extends the m-graph framework introduced by Mohan et al. (2013), with implications for MAR/MCAR versus MNAR regimes (Rubin, 1976; Little & Rubin, 2002).

Strengths:
- Extends recoverability to cases with interacting R nodes. The generalized ordered factorization and the minimality requirement are clearly motivated and illustrated (Example 1).
- Provides new non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3; Corollary 2), which are practical for diagnosing impossibility and have been underdeveloped in the literature.
- Demonstrates that causal effects can be recovered even when corresponding probabilistic relations are not, using do-calculus and d*/dormant independencies. This is a conceptually important message for handling MNAR data.
- Attrition results are crisp, including necessary and sufficient conditions for |Vm|=1 (Theorems 5–6), and they correct a prevalent misconception.
- The results are framed graphically, enabling implementable checks and facilitating communication of assumptions.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
Quality (technical soundness):
- Many results are sufficient but not complete; the gap between necessary (Theorem 3) and sufficient (Theorem 2) conditions for P(V) remains, and the practical tightness could be better discussed. Are there examples where Theorem 3’s conditions hold but Theorem 2 fails, yet P(V) is still recoverable?
- Section 4 claims “general algorithms,” but the paper presents conditions and formulas rather than explicit constructive procedures and complexity guarantees. Providing algorithmic pseudocode to (a) detect admissible sequences, (b) construct R partitions R(i), and (c) output estimands would strengthen the contribution and reproducibility.
- Theorem 4’s assumptions (|Vm|=1, existence of W,Z satisfying d*-separation, identification of P(W1,Z|do(X)) and P(Y|do(W1),do(X),Z,Ry=0)) can be strong. A clearer characterization of the scope where these conditions hold, and a comparison with the general ID algorithm’s output in missingness-expanded graphs, would help.
- The positivity assumption is noted (positive over complete cases). The impact of violations and the extension hinted in Mohan et al. (2013) deserve a more explicit treatment or at least examples.

Clarity:
- Notation is dense. A compact “notation crib” table and a small running example that appears in each section would help readers track R-partitions, Markov blankets, collider paths, and minimal separating sets.
- The minimality requirement in Definition 2 is central; an algorithmic test and further examples where chain-rule factorizations fail but minimal ones succeed would aid understanding.
- A flowchart for deciding recoverability of P(V), P(Y|X), and P(Y|do(X)) would be valuable.
- Several references are formatted inconsistently; please standardize (years, venues). Some figure references (e.g., 2(b),(c), 3, 4(a),(b)) are crucial; ensure they are clear and self-contained.

Originality:
- Extending recoverability to interacting R nodes, introducing non-recoverability criteria, and showing causal recoverability under inseparability are novel and useful advances beyond Mohan et al. (2013) and related graphical missingness works.

Significance:
- The results are broadly relevant to causal inference and machine learning with MNAR data, including longitudinal studies with attrition. The graphical tests can guide practitioners to decide whether to proceed with estimation, re-design measurement, or collect auxiliary variables.
- Offering necessary and sufficient conditions for the attrition setting is impactful and rectifies prior guidance.

Questions for the authors:
- Can Theorem 2 be tightened toward necessity, or can you offer counterexamples delimiting its boundary?
- How should one search for admissible sequences efficiently in large graphs? Any heuristics or guaranteed procedures?
- For Theorem 4, can you provide an automated test for the conditions using standard d-separation/dormant independence checks?
- Do your criteria accommodate auxiliary variables collected to aid recoverability (as in Thoemmes & Rose, 2013), and can you formalize when an auxiliary actually helps?

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear theoretical advances with practical graphical criteria.
- Addresses an important, underexplored regime (interacting missingness mechanisms).
- Illuminates causal-versus-probabilistic recoverability differences, with elegant use of do-calculus.
- Strong, definitive results for attrition.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited algorithmic exposition and no empirical demonstrations or case studies.
- Some results apply to restricted settings (e.g., |Vm|=1) without broader completeness.
- Presentation/notation could be streamlined; references need cleanup.

Overall assessment:
A solid theoretical contribution that extends the m-graph program in meaningful ways and provides both positive and negative results for recoverability, including novel insights on causal effects under MNAR. With improved algorithmic clarity and minor presentation fixes, this would be a valuable addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
