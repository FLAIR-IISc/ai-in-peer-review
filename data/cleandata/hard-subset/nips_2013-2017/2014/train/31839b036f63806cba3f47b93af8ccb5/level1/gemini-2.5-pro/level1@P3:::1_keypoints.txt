- Problem addressed: recoverability of probabilistic/causal queries from data with missing values using m-graphs.
- Contribution: generalizes sequential factorization to handle dependencies among missingness mechanisms common in longitudinal studies.
- Contribution: provides a new method (Theorem 2) to recover the full joint distribution P(V) when sequential factorization fails.
- Contribution: introduces graphical criteria sufficient for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2).
- Contribution: shows causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when observational P(y,x) is not, using do-calculus to bypass missingness dependencies.
- Contribution: applies theory to attrition, giving necessary and sufficient conditions for causal effect recovery and correcting prior literature.
- Strength: high significance and impact by moving beyond MAR and giving a principled framework for MNAR inference.
- Strength: originality in providing the first graphical criteria for non-recoverability and linking do-calculus/d*-separation to missing data.
- Strength: strong technical quality with clear theorems, rigorous proofs, effective examples, and correction of prior results.
- Weakness: entirely theoretical with no empirical evaluation on simulated or real data to assess finite-sample performance or robustness to misspecification.
- Weakness: dense presentation assuming deep familiarity with Pearl’s causal framework; some definitions introduced too briefly.
- Weakness: several key causal/attrition results are limited to a single partially observed variable (|Vm|=1) and Theorem 2’s preconditions are specific.
- Argument for acceptance: addresses a fundamental problem beyond MAR with novel, impactful theoretical advances.
- Argument for acceptance: introduces definitive non-recoverability criteria and a novel connection between causal identification and missing-data recovery.
- Argument for acceptance: technically rigorous and advances state-of-the-art from Mohan et al. [2013].
- Argument for acceptance: provides deep conceptual insight, notably that causal queries can be easier to recover than observational ones.
- Argument for rejection: lacks experiments to validate methods in finite samples.
- Argument for rejection: dense, specialized presentation may limit the paper’s audience.
- Argument for rejection: some results limited to the single missing-variable case.
- Conclusion: strong theoretical paper with significant, novel contributions; weaknesses (no experiments, density) are outweighed and reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
