

This paper extends Mohan et al. [2013] (NIPS 2013) by developing more general graphical conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data using Missingness Graphs (m-graphs). The authors demonstrate that causal queries P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations (like P(y,x)) are not, specifically when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable. They present new theorems (Theorems 1-6) that characterize recoverability for joint/conditional distributions and causal effects, with particular application to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.

Strengths: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending Mohan et al.'s framework to handle complex missingness mechanisms where missingness processes interact. The key insight that causal queries can be recoverable despite non-recoverable probabilistic components is novel and important. The work is technically sound with complete proofs in the appendix, well-organized, and clearly motivated by practical problems in causal inference. The application to attrition (a common problem in longitudinal studies) is particularly relevant.

Weaknesses: While the theoretical development is strong, the paper lacks empirical validation beyond illustrative examples. The computational complexity of applying the proposed conditions is not discussed, which would help practitioners. The connection to practical implementation could be strengthened with more concrete guidance on how to apply the theorems to real datasets.

Originality: The paper clearly differentiates itself from Mohan et al. [2013] and other related work (e.g., Garcia [2013]) by establishing that causal queries may circumvent the separability requirement needed for probabilistic queries. The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic vs. causal queries is a significant theoretical advance not present in previous NIPS work on missing data.

Significance: Missing data is a pervasive problem across scientific domains, and this work provides a formal framework for diagnosing recoverability of causal queries from missing data. The results resolve a key ambiguity in the literature (e.g., correcting Garcia [2013]'s claim about necessary conditions for attrition problems), with clear implications for researchers designing studies with potential attrition.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution, novel insight about causal vs. probabilistic recoverability, well-motivated application to attrition.

Con: Limited empirical validation, minimal discussion of computational implementation.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that significantly advances the understanding of recoverability in missing data problems. The paper meets all NIPS criteria with its technical soundness, clarity, and significance for the field. I recommend acceptance.
