- Builds on prior m-graph formalism to address recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries under MNAR missingness.  
- Generalizes sequential factorization to allow dependencies among missingness indicators (R-variables), relevant for longitudinal settings.  
- Introduces a new partition-based method (Theorem 2) to recover the joint distribution when sequential factorization fails.  
- Provides graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3), giving formal conditions when queries cannot be consistently estimated.  
- Shows causal queries P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when corresponding probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y|x), P(y,x)) are not.  
- Applies theoretical results to attrition, giving necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects and correcting a prior claim.  
- High technical quality: formally stated theorems and proofs, grounded in d-separation and do-calculus.  
- Balanced presentation: supplies sufficient and necessary conditions, preventing futile estimation attempts.  
- Lacks empirical validation; a simulation demonstrating key results (e.g., causal recoverability when probabilistic recovery fails) would strengthen the paper.  
- Clearly written and well-organized overall, with effective examples illustrating theorems.  
- Paper is technically dense and assumes familiarity with prior m-graph work; Section 4 introduces complex concepts somewhat abruptly.  
- Highly original: non-trivial extensions over 2013 work, novel non-recoverability criteria, and the causal vs. probabilistic recoverability distinction.  
- Significant impact: advances reasoning about MNAR missingness, useful for researchers and practitioners who can specify causal graphs.  
- Provides algorithmically checkable graphical criteria that can guide real-world inference decisions under missing data.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical result on causal recoverability, first formal non-recoverability criteria, substantial generalization, technical soundness, and practical application to attrition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: entirely theoretical with no experiments and high technical density limiting accessibility.  
- Conclusion: strong, original contributions with high significance; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
