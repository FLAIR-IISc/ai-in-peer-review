

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. [2013] on missingness graphs (m-graphs) to develop more general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors build on m-graphs, which explicitly model the missingness process alongside variable dependencies, to present new theoretical results for determining when queries like P(y|x), P(y,x), and P(y|do(x)) can be consistently estimated from incomplete data. Key contributions include generalizing the sequential factorization theorem to handle interacting missingness mechanisms, introducing new graphical criteria for non-recoverability, and demonstrating that causal queries may be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations (like P(y,x)) are not recoverable—specifically, when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable.

The paper is technically sound with well-defined concepts, clear theorems, and detailed proofs in the appendix. The authors carefully distinguish between recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries, which is a significant contribution. The example in Section 6.1 (Figure 3) compellingly demonstrates a causal query that is recoverable despite the non-recoverability of the underlying joint distribution. The application to attrition problems in Section 7 is relevant and well-motivated, with Theorem 6 correcting a misconception in Garcia [2013] about simple attrition.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical advance in showing causal queries can be recoverable when probabilistic relations are not, a non-intuitive and important result.
2. Novel graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) fill a significant gap in the literature.
3. Clear organization with helpful examples and consistent notation.
4. Corrects a prevailing misconception in attrition literature (Garcia [2013]), demonstrating practical significance.

Weaknesses:
1. Lacks empirical validation; the paper presents theoretical results without demonstrating their practical utility through experiments on real or simulated datasets.
2. Some notation (e.g., R(i), Rpart) is dense and could benefit from additional explanation for readers unfamiliar with prior work.
3. Related work section (Section 8) is somewhat cursory and doesn't fully contextualize how this work differs from other graphical approaches to missing data (e.g., Marlin et al. [2007, 2009, 2011] on collaborative filtering).

The paper is highly original in its specific focus on causal queries in missing data contexts. While missing data is well-studied, the extension to causal inference with the specific graphical conditions presented represents a meaningful advance beyond prior NIPS work. The paper clearly differentiates itself from Mohan et al. [2013] by addressing causal queries and non-recoverability criteria not previously covered.

The significance of this work is substantial. The authors provide a formal framework for determining when causal effects can be estimated from missing data—a critical problem in experimental sciences. Their results have direct implications for fields like epidemiology, social sciences, and clinical trials where missing data commonly biases causal inferences. The correction of Garcia's [2013] misconception about attrition problems is particularly valuable for practitioners.

Pro and con for acceptance:
- Pro: Strong theoretical contribution addressing an important gap, well-structured, builds meaningfully on prior work.
- Con: Lacks empirical validation, some notation could be clarified, related work section could be more comprehensive.

This paper makes a clear and important theoretical contribution to the intersection of graphical models, causal inference, and missing data. The results are technically sound, well-motivated, and address a problem of significant practical importance. While the lack of empirical validation is a minor weakness, the theoretical advances are substantial enough to warrant acceptance. The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, particularly for researchers working on causal inference and graphical models. I recommend acceptance.
