- Extends prior m-graph framework to determine recoverability of statistical and causal queries from missing data.  
- Generalizes sequential factorization to allow edges among missingness mechanisms (R variables), addressing longitudinal settings.  
- Presents a more general joint-recovery algorithm (Theorem 2) based on partitioning R variables and their Markov blankets.  
- Introduces formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) for the first time in this framework.  
- Shows causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when corresponding probabilistic queries (P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are not.  
- Applies results to the practical problem of attrition, providing necessary and sufficient conditions and correcting recent literature.  
- Builds on Pearl’s causal inference and connects missing-data graphical models with do-calculus and advanced identification tools.  
- High technical quality: formal theorems, logical development, and clear graphical examples (e.g., Example 3).  
- Highly original: non-recoverability criteria and the decoupling of causal/probabilistic recoverability are novel contributions.  
- Significant practical value: enables model-based reasoning about MNAR scenarios and helps decide recoverability before analysis.  
- Clear and well-organized exposition, with a logical flow from theory to application for readers familiar with graphical models.  
- Theoretical density and challenging notation (e.g., RMb(R(i)), Pasub(R(i))) may hinder accessibility for some readers.  
- Relies on correctness of user-specified m-graph; a brief reminder of testability implications would be helpful.  
- Focuses on identifiability only and omits discussion of practical statistical estimation of identified quantities.  
- Pros for acceptance: significant novel theory, practical relevance (attrition), first non-recoverability criteria, technically sound and clear.  
- Cons for acceptance: theoretical density may limit accessibility; lacks discussion of estimation methods.  
- Overall recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance—paper makes multiple important, demonstrable advances.
