This paper addresses the important problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries, building upon the framework of “Missingness Graphs” (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. The authors extend previous results by providing more general conditions for recoverability, notably showing that causal queries can be recovered even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not. A key contribution is demonstrating recoverability of P(y|do(x)) even when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable – a significant departure from typical assumptions requiring separability for identifiability. The paper applies these findings to the specific case of attrition.

The work is well-motivated, as missing data is a pervasive issue across many scientific disciplines.  The use of m-graphs provides a formal and visually intuitive way to represent the missingness process alongside the causal relationships between variables. This is a substantial improvement over ad-hoc approaches to handling missing data.  The paper builds logically on the previous work of Mohan et al. [2013] and Pearl & Mohan [2013], incrementally improving the theory and expanding its applicability. The inclusion of a detailed discussion of attrition is particularly relevant, as it’s a common and challenging problem in observational studies. The numerous theorems and corollaries provide a rigorous foundation for the proposed methods.

However, the paper’s strength—its technical depth—also presents a challenge for clarity. The notation is dense, and while definitions are provided, following the logical flow through the theorems requires significant effort from the reader.  The presentation feels somewhat fragmented; while each section focuses on a specific extension or application, the overall narrative could be strengthened by more explicit connections between the sections.  For example, the transition between discussing general factorization and the case where no admissible sequence exists could be smoother. The several theorems feel presented one after another without a cohesive discussion on the overall implications.  

Furthermore, while the paper rigorously establishes *conditions* for recoverability, it doesn’t offer practical algorithms for *implementing* the recovery process when these conditions are met. The paper states consistent estimation is *possible* but doesn’t detail how to achieve it.  This is a crucial gap – knowing a query is recoverable is valuable, but having a concrete method for computing it is essential for practical application. The examples, while helpful, are relatively simple and may not fully capture the complexity of real-world missing data scenarios. The discussion of related work, while extensive, is somewhat disconnected from the core contribution; it’s a lengthy list of references rather than a critical analysis of how this work advances the state-of-the-art. Finally, although the authors acknowledge Garcia [2013], they do not discuss the nuances of the simple attrition assumption and its potential limitations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Significance:**  The paper extends the theory of recoverability under missing data, providing new and more general conditions, especially for causal queries.
*   **Novelty:** The finding that causal queries can be recovered even when probabilistic relations are not, and the relaxation of the d-separability requirement, are significant contributions.
*   **Rigor:** The paper’s reliance on formal graphical models and rigorous mathematical proofs enhances its credibility.
*   **Relevance:** The application to attrition highlights the practical importance of the work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The dense notation and fragmented presentation make the paper challenging to follow.
*   **Lack of Practical Guidance:** The paper doesn’t provide algorithms for implementing the recovery process.
*   **Limited Scope of Examples:** The examples are relatively simple and may not represent real-world complexities.
*   **Weak Connection to Related Work:** The related work discussion is exhaustive but lacks critical analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of missing data.  The extension of recoverability conditions, particularly for causal queries, is a significant advancement. However, the lack of practical guidance and the challenges with clarity hinder its immediate impact.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should focus on improving the clarity of the presentation, perhaps by adding a high-level overview of the key results and a more detailed discussion of potential implementation strategies. A more focused and analytical discussion of related work would also strengthen the paper. Addressing these issues would make the paper more accessible and impactful for a wider audience.
