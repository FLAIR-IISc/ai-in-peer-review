- Addresses recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries using Missingness Graphs (m-graphs).  
- Extends prior work (Mohan et al. 2013) with more general recoverability conditions.  
- Shows causal queries can be recoverable even when underlying probabilistic relations are not.  
- Key technical contribution: recoverability of P(y | do(x)) even when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable.  
- Refines sequential factorization theorems and handles cases where they fail.  
- Introduces new criteria for non-recoverability.  
- Applies theoretical results to attrition problems as a concrete example.  
- Theoretical results are technically sound with theorems and proofs (proofs in appendix).  
- Heavy reliance on Mohan et al. [2013] notation may limit accessibility for readers unfamiliar with that work.  
- Dense notation and technical presentation require strong background in causal inference and graphical models.  
- Several illustrative examples are provided but some need more elaboration for clarity.  
- Definitions (e.g., d*-separation, inducing path) are introduced somewhat abruptly and assume prior knowledge.  
- Novel contribution: weaker conditions for recovering causal effects than previously known.  
- Significant practical implications for fields facing missing data and attrition (epidemiology, social sciences, econometrics).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, rigorous proofs, and real-world applicability to attrition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: accessibility issues due to reliance on prior work and dense presentation.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — solid theoretical contribution despite presentation challenges.
