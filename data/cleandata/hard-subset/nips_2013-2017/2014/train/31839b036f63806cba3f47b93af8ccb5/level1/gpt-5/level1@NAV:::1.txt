This paper studies when probabilistic queries P(y, x), P(y|x) and causal queries P(y|do(x)) are recoverable from data with missingness, given a specified missingness graph (m-graph). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors (i) generalize the ordered factorization approach to include missingness indicators R, providing a stronger sequential factorization theorem that handles interactions among R variables; (ii) introduce a new recovery formula for joint distributions in semi-Markovian models via partitions of R and Markov blankets Mb(R(i)), yielding recoverability even when no admissible sequence exists; (iii) provide graphical non-recoverability criteria for joints and conditionals; and (iv) show that certain causal effects are recoverable even when associated probabilistic factors are not, by leveraging d*-separation and dormant independences (Shpitser & Pearl, 2008). They apply these results to attrition, giving necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of joint and causal queries when |Vm|=1, correcting claims in Garcia (2013). The work connects to identification in ADMGs (Richardson, 2003), do-calculus (Pearl, 2009), and extends the MNAR line inaugurated at NIPS 2013; it complements model-blind MAR/MCAR strategies (Rubin, 1976; Little & Rubin, 2002) and ML treatments of MNAR in recommender systems (Marlin et al., UAI 2007; IJCAI 2011).

Quality: The theoretical claims appear carefully stated, with clear counterexamples and constructive formulas. The non-recoverability conditions fill a gap. However, there is limited guidance on algorithmic implementation and complexity, and no empirical validation or simulation to stress-test the criteria under finite samples or model misspecification.

Clarity: The exposition is generally readable, with illustrative figures and examples, but the notation (V, Vm, Vo, V*, R) is dense; minimality and collider path are defined late; and some references and typography need polishing. A procedural checklist or pseudocode for checking admissible sequences and applying Theorems 2–6 would aid reproducibility.

Originality: The extension of ordered factorization to include R, the general recovery in semi-Markovian settings without admissible sequences, and the recovery of causal effects under Y–Ry inseparability are novel and timely.

Significance: The results advance the theory of MNAR recoverability and have practical implications for longitudinal studies with attrition, a common setting in ML applications.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, novel theoretical contributions (generalized factorization, non-recoverability, causal recoverability).
- Clear connection to and extension of prior NIPS work.
- Necessary and sufficient conditions for simple attrition are impactful.

Cons against acceptance:
- Lack of algorithmic automation and complexity analysis.
- No empirical demonstrations or software.
- Clarity/notation and reference formatting can be improved.

Overall: a solid theoretical paper; I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarity and implementation guidance improvements.
