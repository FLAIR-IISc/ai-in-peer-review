- Extends Mohan et al. [2013] on missingness graphs to derive more general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries.  
- Introduces refined conditions for recovering joint and conditional distributions via sequential factorization.  
- Shows causal queries P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when underlying probabilistic relations are not.  
- Proposes new graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3, Corollary 2).  
- Applies the framework to attrition problems in longitudinal studies (Section 7).  
- Theoretical development is rigorous with formal definitions, theorems, and proofs (appendix).  
- Carefully distinguishes probabilistic vs. causal recoverability and when each holds.  
- Builds on Pearl’s do-calculus and prior missing-data graphical models, clearly situating contributions.  
- Well-organized and clearly written for readers familiar with causal graphical models.  
- Key concepts like “general ordered factorization” and “d*-separation” are well-defined and exemplified.  
- Examples (notably Example 1 and Example 3) effectively clarify technical content.  
- Figures (1–4) are well-designed to illustrate graphical conditions.  
- Assumes strong background in causal inference, which may limit accessibility to some readers.  
- Would benefit from a brief glossary of key terms for less-expert readers.  
- Significant practical implications for researchers dealing with missing data, especially in longitudinal studies.  
- Strengths: rigorous proofs, novel insight on causal recoverability, comprehensive probabilistic/causal treatment, useful attrition application, clear presentation.  
- Weaknesses: limited discussion of practical implementation, no empirical validation on real datasets, some technical terms require careful reading, weak connection to existing causal-inference software.  
- Recommendation: strong theoretical contribution; recommend acceptance despite need for more practical/empirical follow-up.
