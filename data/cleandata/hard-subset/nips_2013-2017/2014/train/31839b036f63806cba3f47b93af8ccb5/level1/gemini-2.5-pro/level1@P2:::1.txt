### Review

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper addresses the problem of recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing values. The authors build directly upon their previous work (Mohan et al., NIPS 2013), which introduced the "missingness graph" (m-graph) formalism for modeling the missing data process. The m-graph provides a graphical language to reason about missingness mechanisms that go beyond the standard Missing Completely at Random (MCAR) and Missing at Random (MAR) assumptions, into the Missing Not at Random (MNAR) regime.

The main contributions of this paper are a set of significant extensions to their prior work:
1.  It generalizes the sequential factorization theorem for recovering joint distributions, allowing for dependencies between the missingness mechanisms themselves (the R-variables). This is particularly relevant for settings like longitudinal studies.
2.  It provides a new method for recovering the joint distribution (Theorem 2) for cases where sequential factorization fails, relying on partitioning the R-variables based on graphical criteria (collider paths and Markov blankets).
3.  Crucially, it introduces graphical criteria for *non-recoverability* (Theorem 3). This is a valuable contribution, as it provides formal conditions under which a query is impossible to estimate consistently from the observed data, given the model assumptions.
4.  Perhaps the most significant contribution is the analysis of causal queries. The paper demonstrates that a causal query, P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries, like P(y,x) or P(y|x), are not. This is a powerful and non-obvious result.
5.  Finally, the paper applies these theoretical results to the practical problem of attrition in experimental studies, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects and correcting a claim in the existing literature.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The arguments are presented formally through theorems and corollaries, with proofs deferred to an appendix. The theoretical claims are well-supported by the graphical logic, building upon established frameworks like d-separation and Pearl's do-calculus. The definitions are precise, and the use of examples to illustrate the application of the theorems is effective.

The work is a complete piece of research that substantially advances the graphical modeling approach to missing data. The authors are careful in their claims, providing sufficient conditions for recoverability and, importantly, necessary conditions for non-recoverability. This provides a balanced perspective, telling the user not only when recovery is possible but also when it is futile. The one area where the quality could be enhanced is the lack of empirical validation. While this is a theoretical paper, a small simulation study demonstrating the key result—the recoverability of P(y|do(x)) when P(y|x) is not recoverable—would have provided a compelling and concrete illustration of the theory's power.

**Clarity**

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with a logical flow from probabilistic queries to causal queries and finally to a practical application. The introduction effectively summarizes the contributions and provides a roadmap for the reader.

However, the paper is technically very dense. It assumes a high level of familiarity with the authors' previous work on m-graphs and with advanced topics in causal inference (e.g., do-calculus, d*-separation, inducing paths). While this is expected for a NIPS audience, some sections, particularly Section 4, introduce complex concepts like `R(part)` and `Mb(R(i))` rather abruptly. A bit more intuition or a motivating example before diving into the formal theorem could improve readability for a broader audience. The examples that are present, such as Example 3 for causal queries, are very helpful in clarifying the main ideas.

**Originality**

The paper is highly original. While it is an extension of the authors' own NIPS 2013 paper, the extensions are non-trivial and represent significant new contributions.
-   The generalization of sequential factorization to handle interacting missingness mechanisms is a novel and necessary step.
-   The graphical conditions for *non-recoverability* appear to be the first of their kind to be formally stated in this framework. This moves the field beyond just finding sufficient conditions.
-   The central result distinguishing the recoverability of causal versus probabilistic queries is the most original and impactful part of the paper. It establishes a new and deep connection between the theories of causal inference and missing data, showing that the structure required for causal identification can sometimes bypass the problems that missingness introduces for purely statistical estimation.
-   The application to attrition, including the correction of a previous result by Garcia [2013], demonstrates original application and careful scholarship.

**Significance**

The significance of this work is high. Missing data is a ubiquitous problem in machine learning and all empirical sciences. The standard MAR assumption is often violated in practice, and this paper provides a principled, model-based framework for reasoning about the more complex MNAR case.
-   **For researchers:** The paper provides a powerful theoretical toolkit. The non-recoverability criteria are particularly important, as they can prevent researchers from attempting to answer questions that are fundamentally unanswerable from their data. The results on causal queries open up new avenues for research at the intersection of causality and missing data.
-   **For practitioners:** While the methods require specifying a causal graph of the missingness process (which is a non-trivial modeling task), the payoff is enormous. If the model is believed to be correct, the graphical criteria provide clear, algorithmically checkable conditions for deciding whether a desired quantity can be consistently estimated. This is far more powerful than relying on heuristic diagnostics or untestable assumptions like MAR.

The ideas presented here are likely to be built upon by others working on robust inference from imperfect data. The separation of probabilistic and causal recoverability is a fundamental insight that could influence how causal effect estimation is approached in fields like econometrics, epidemiology, and social sciences, where attrition and other forms of missing data are common.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Presents a novel and significant theoretical result: causal queries can be recoverable even when their probabilistic counterparts are not.
2.  Provides the first formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability*, a valuable contribution for both theory and practice.
3.  Substantially generalizes previous work on graphical models for missing data, making the framework applicable to a wider class of problems.
4.  The work is technically sound, rigorous, and addresses a problem of high importance to the NIPS community.
5.  Connects theory to practice through a clear application to the problem of attrition.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The paper is entirely theoretical, lacking any simulation or real-world data experiments to validate the proposed recovery algorithms.
2.  The high technical density and reliance on specialized prior knowledge may limit its immediate accessibility, though the core ideas are well-explained through examples.

**Conclusion:**

This is a strong paper that makes several significant and original contributions to the theory of handling missing data. The distinction it draws between the recoverability of causal and probabilistic queries is a particularly deep insight. Despite the lack of experiments, the theoretical strength and importance of the results make it an excellent fit for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
