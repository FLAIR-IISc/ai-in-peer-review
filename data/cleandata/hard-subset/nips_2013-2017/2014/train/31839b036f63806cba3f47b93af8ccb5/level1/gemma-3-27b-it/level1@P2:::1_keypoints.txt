- Tackles recovering causal and probabilistic queries from datasets with missing data using the m-graph framework.  
- Core contribution: extends recoverability conditions to more complex missingness mechanisms that interact with substantive variables.  
- Key theoretical result: causal queries (P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when probabilistic queries (P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are not.  
- Extends sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) and introduces new non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3).  
- Theorems and corollaries are logically derived and appropriately generalized but can be dense.  
- Lack of sufficient intuitive explanation alongside heavy mathematical formalism.  
- Examples help illustrate concepts but are not used systematically enough.  
- Treatment of attrition (section 7) is somewhat cursory and lacks nuance across different attrition models.  
- Proofs are relegated to the appendix; key steps need more concise justification in the main text.  
- The claim of “rectifying prevailing opinion” in Garcia (2013) needs stronger substantiation.  
- Writing is mostly clear and notation consistent, but the paper is dense and can be hard for readers new to m-graphs.  
- More visual aids and smoother section transitions (e.g., Theorem 2 → Corollary 1) would improve accessibility.  
- Builds on Mohan et al. and Pearl & Mohan rather than introducing a radically new approach, but includes novel extensions.  
- Identification of graphical criteria for non-recoverability is a novel and useful contribution.  
- Results are potentially significant for fields with pervasive missing data (epidemiology, social science), especially recovering causal effects.  
- Practical impact depends on community adoption of m-graphs and development of efficient algorithms for applying the criteria.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound extensions; significant causal recoverability result; useful non-recoverability criteria; thorough related work.  
- Arguments against acceptance: density and accessibility issues; weak connection to practical implications; insufficient intuitive exposition; overstated claims needing more evidence.  
- Overall: solid and potentially impactful contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity, add intuition and visuals, and discuss practical limitations.
