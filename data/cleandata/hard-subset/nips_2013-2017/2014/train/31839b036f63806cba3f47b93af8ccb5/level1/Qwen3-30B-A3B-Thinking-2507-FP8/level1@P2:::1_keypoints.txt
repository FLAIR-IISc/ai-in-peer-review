- Extends Mohan et al. (2013) on missingness graphs (m-graphs) to derive general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data.  
- Develops theoretical results for when queries like P(y|x), P(y,x), and P(y|do(x)) can be consistently estimated from incomplete data.  
- Generalizes the sequential factorization theorem to handle interacting missingness mechanisms.  
- Introduces new graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3).  
- Demonstrates that causal queries can be recoverable even when underlying probabilistic relations (e.g., P(y,x)) are not, particularly when Y and Ry are not d-separable.  
- Provides a compelling example (Section 6.1, Figure 3) showing a recoverable causal query despite non-recoverable joint distribution.  
- Paper is technically sound with clear definitions, theorems, and detailed proofs in the appendix.  
- Carefully distinguishes recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries—a significant conceptual contribution.  
- Applies results to attrition problems and corrects a misconception in Garcia (2013) via Theorem 6.  
- Strength: novel theoretical advance showing non-intuitive scenarios where causal recoverability holds.  
- Strength: clear organization, consistent notation, and helpful examples.  
- Weakness: lacks empirical validation on real or simulated datasets to demonstrate practical utility.  
- Weakness: some notation (e.g., R(i), Rpart) is dense and could use more explanation for non-experts.  
- Weakness: related work section is cursory and does not fully contrast with other graphical approaches (e.g., Marlin et al.).  
- Originality: high—meaningfully extends prior work by addressing causal queries and non-recoverability criteria.  
- Significance: substantial implications for fields (epidemiology, social sciences, clinical trials) where missing data bias causal inference.  
- Acceptance pros: strong theoretical contribution, well-structured, builds on prior work.  
- Acceptance cons: missing empirical validation, notation clarity issues, and incomplete related-work contextualization.  
- Recommendation: accept—theoretical advances warrant publication despite minor weaknesses.
