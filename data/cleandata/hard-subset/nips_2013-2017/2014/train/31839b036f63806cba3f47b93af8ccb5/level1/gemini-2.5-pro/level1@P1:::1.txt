**Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"**

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper addresses the problem of determining whether a statistical or causal query can be consistently estimated from a dataset with missing values, a problem known as "recoverability". The work is a direct and substantial extension of the authors' previous NIPS paper (Mohan et al., 2013), which introduced the "missingness graph" (m-graph) framework. An m-graph is a causal DAG that explicitly models the relationships between substantive variables (V) and the mechanisms causing them to be missing (R).

Building on this foundation, the current paper makes several key contributions:
1.  It generalizes the "sequential factorization" theorem from the 2013 paper to handle cases where missingness mechanisms can influence each other (i.e., edges between R variables), which is common in longitudinal studies.
2.  It provides a new, more general algorithm (Theorem 2) for recovering joint distributions in cases where the sequential factorization approach fails, based on partitioning the R variables and their Markov blankets.
3.  It introduces, for the first time in this framework, formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability* (Theorem 3, Corollary 2), allowing researchers to identify when a query is impossible to recover under the given model assumptions.
4.  Most significantly, it demonstrates that the recoverability of a causal query P(y|do(x)) is not necessarily tied to the recoverability of the corresponding probabilistic queries P(y,x) or P(y|x). It shows that causal effects can sometimes be recovered even when the outcome variable Y is inseparably linked to its missingness mechanism Ry, a condition that would preclude the recovery of P(Y).
5.  Finally, it applies these general results to the specific, practical problem of attrition in experimental studies, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects and correcting claims made in recent literature.

This work fits squarely within the causal inference literature pioneered by Pearl and extends the graphical model approach to the fundamental problem of missing data, connecting it with advanced identification techniques like the do-calculus and d*-separation (Shpitser and Pearl, 2006, 2008). It offers a principled alternative to the traditional statistical trichotomy of MCAR/MAR/MNAR by allowing for fine-grained, model-based reasoning about specific MNAR (Missing Not At Random) scenarios.

**Strengths**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theorems are stated formally and build logically upon one another and upon prior work. The claims are well-supported by graphical examples that clearly illustrate the concepts (e.g., Example 3 is an excellent demonstration of the main causal result). The work feels complete, addressing sufficient conditions, a more general algorithm, and necessary (non-recoverability) conditions.

*   **Originality:** The paper contains several highly original and important contributions. While it builds on the m-graph framework, the extensions are non-trivial. The development of graphical criteria for non-recoverability is a significant and novel step, providing a crucial counterpart to the existing recovery algorithms. The central finding—that causal queries can be recovered even when probabilistic queries cannot—is a profound and non-obvious insight that elegantly weds the graphical missing data framework with the logic of do-calculus. This is a genuinely new idea.

*   **Significance:** The results presented are highly significant. Missing data is a ubiquitous problem across all empirical sciences, and this paper provides a powerful, principled framework for reasoning about it. The ability to graphically determine whether a query of interest is recoverable before attempting analysis is of immense practical value. The result on causal queries is particularly impactful, as it shows that a study suffering from certain types of missingness (e.g., attrition related to the outcome) may still yield valid causal conclusions, even if the marginal distribution of the outcome is biased. This could prevent researchers from unnecessarily discarding valuable data or entire studies. The clarification and correction of results on attrition further underscore the paper's relevance.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and very well-organized. The introduction clearly lays out the structure and contributions. Each section tackles a specific theoretical advance, supported by definitions and examples. The flow from extending existing results to presenting new ones, and finally to a practical application, is logical and easy to follow for a reader familiar with graphical models.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Density:** The paper is theoretically dense, and the notation can be challenging (e.g., `RMb(R(i))`, `Pasub(R(i))`). While the definitions are provided, a little more intuition behind the construction of these sets in Section 4 would be helpful for the reader. For instance, explaining *why* the Markov blanket of the R-partitions is the key object could improve accessibility.

*   **Assumptions:** The framework relies on the correctness of the user-specified m-graph. While the authors have addressed the testability of these graphs elsewhere (Mohan and Pearl, 2014), a brief sentence reminding the reader of this critical assumption and its implications would be beneficial.

*   **Practical Implementation:** The paper focuses entirely on the theory of recoverability (i.e., identifiability from the manifest distribution). It does not discuss the practical statistical estimation of the identified quantities. While this is appropriate for a theoretical paper, a brief discussion of the potential estimation challenges or pointers to relevant literature would strengthen its connection to practitioners.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance**

**Pro:**
*   Presents significant, novel theoretical results on a fundamental problem in machine learning and statistics.
*   The decoupling of causal and probabilistic recoverability is a major conceptual advance.
*   Provides the first formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability in this framework.
*   Extends a powerful and promising graphical framework for missing data in non-trivial ways.
*   Demonstrates immediate practical relevance through the analysis of attrition, correcting prior work.
*   The paper is technically sound, clear, and well-written.

**Con:**
*   The material is theoretically dense and may be challenging for readers not already expert in graphical causal models.
*   The practical aspects of estimation, given a recovery formula, are not discussed.

In my view, the arguments in favor of acceptance are overwhelming. This is an excellent paper that makes multiple important contributions to an area of great significance to the NIPS community. It advances the state-of-the-art in a demonstrable way and provides powerful new tools for both theorists and practitioners dealing with missing data. I strongly recommend acceptance.
