- Paper generalizes Mohan et al. (2013) to give recoverability conditions for probabilistic and causal queries from m-graphs, introducing Theorems 1–6.  
- Main conceptual advance: causal effects can be recoverable even when associated probabilistic quantities are not, via dormant independences/do-calculus (Theorem 4).  
- Theorem 1: generalized ordered factorization including missingness indicators R and interacting missingness mechanisms.  
- Theorem 2: recovery of joint distributions using partitions of R and Markov blankets along collider paths when admissible sequences fail; corollary for no-latent-common-cause case.  
- Theorem 3 and Corollary 2: necessary graphical conditions that preclude recoverability of joint and conditional distributions.  
- Attrition results (Theorems 5–6): necessary-and-sufficient conditions for joint distribution and causal effect recovery when a single variable is partially observed, correcting prior claims.  
- Connects to identifiability/do-calculus/AM/ADMG separations and dormant independences, and clarifies relationships to MAR/MCAR and selection/IPW intuitions.  
- Strength: provides both positive and negative graphical criteria, helping practitioners avoid futile estimation attempts.  
- Strength: extends sequential factorization to settings with interacting missingness mechanisms common in longitudinal/attrition studies.  
- Strength: examples and referenced proofs support technical soundness and use of established graphical causal theory.  
- Weakness: lacks a concrete decision algorithm (input: m-graph, query; output: recoverability verdict and estimand) and complexity analysis.  
- Weakness: Theorem 2’s preconditions are strong; paper should map where it generalizes prior work and where it fails, possibly with minimal counterexamples.  
- Weakness: Theorem 3 gives only necessary (not sufficient) non-recoverability criteria; more guidance on closeness to sufficiency is needed.  
- Weakness: no empirical validation or simulations demonstrating finite-sample behavior of proposed estimands.  
- Weakness: notational inconsistencies/ambiguities (e.g., G_X/G^X, undefined symbols like W1); recommend consolidated notation table.  
- Suggestion: include brief proof sketches for Theorems 2, 4, and 6 in main text to aid reader assessment without appendix deep dives.  
- Suggestion: add worked examples illustrating R-partitions, Mb(R(i)), and d*-separation with explicit Z and W.  
- Questions for authors: provide explicit admissible-sequence search algorithm and complexity; robustness/sensitivity to m-graph misspecification; relax |Vm|=1 in Theorem 4 or provide counterexample; relate Theorem 2 to IPW/selection models.  
- Minor issues: fix reference formatting; clarify modeling restrictions on R variables being parents of V ∪ U; ensure consistent definitions of d-separation/d*-separation and collider/inducing paths.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong, novel theoretical contributions with clear practical implications and necessary-and-sufficient results in important special cases.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of fully specified algorithm, empirical demonstrations, and some exposition/notation issues.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept, subject to improved exposition, algorithmic specification, minor polishing, and optional empirical validation.
