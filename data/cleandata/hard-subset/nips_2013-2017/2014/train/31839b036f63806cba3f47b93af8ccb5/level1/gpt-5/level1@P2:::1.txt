Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies when probabilistic queries (P(y, x), P(y|x)) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))) are estimable from data with missing entries, given a causal model of the missingness process encoded by Missingness Graphs (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), it generalizes: (i) sufficient conditions for recoverability of probabilistic queries via a generalized “ordered factorization” that includes missingness indicators R and allows interactions among missingness mechanisms (Theorem 1); (ii) sufficient conditions for recovering joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists, using partitions of R and Markov blankets along collider paths (Theorem 2), with a corollary for the no-latent-common-cause case; (iii) necessary conditions that preclude recoverability of joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2). A key conceptual contribution is that causal effects may be recoverable even when associated probabilistic quantities are not: the paper gives criteria (Theorem 4) leveraging d*-separation/dormant independences to recover P(y|do(x)) despite Y being inseparable from its missingness mechanism Ry. The paper then applies these insights to attrition, giving necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering joint distributions (Theorem 5) and causal effects (Theorem 6) when a single variable is partially observed.

This work advances the m-graphs framework of Mohan et al. (2013) and connects with identifiability (Shpitser & Pearl, 2006), do-calculus (Pearl, 2009), AM/ADMG separations (Richardson, 2003), and dormant independences (Shpitser & Pearl, 2008). It also clarifies relationships to MAR/MCAR, and to selection/inverse probability weighting intuitions common in the missing data and epidemiology literatures.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual advance: shows causal recoverability can hold even when probabilistic recoverability fails, and formalizes when and how.
- Generalizes sequential factorization to include R nodes, covering settings with interacting missingness mechanisms that are common (e.g., longitudinal/attrition).
- Provides both positive (Theorems 1–2) and negative (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) graphical criteria, helping practitioners avoid fruitless attempts at estimation.
- Attrition results are crisp (necessary and sufficient) for |Vm|=1, correcting prior claims (e.g., Garcia 2013).
- Examples clarify how to use do-calculus with R variables to recover causal effects.
- Proofs are referenced and the results appear technically sound and consistent with established graphical causal theory.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Algorithmic specificity: Section 4 refers to “general algorithms,” but the paper largely presents formulas/conditions. A concrete decision procedure (inputs: m-graph, query; outputs: recoverability verdict and estimand) with complexity discussion would improve usability.
- Scope of Theorem 2: Preconditions (no neighbor/collider-path between each Vm and its own Ry) are strong; it would help to map exactly where this generalizes earlier results, and where it still fails (e.g., minimal counterexamples).
- Non-recoverability criteria: Theorem 3 provides necessary conditions, but not sufficient ones. More guidance on how “close” these are to sufficiency (or additional sufficient conditions for non-recoverability) would be valuable.
- Empirical validation: No simulations/case studies. While the paper is theoretical, a small suite of synthetic scenarios demonstrating finite-sample behavior (bias/variance of the proposed estimands) would substantiate practical significance.
- Notational clarity: Some notation appears inconsistent or ambiguous (e.g., “GX and GX represent graphs formed by removing from G all edges leaving and entering X, respectively” likely intends G_X and G^X). Define all symbols when first used (e.g., W1 in Theorem 4).
- Reference formatting has multiple errors and inconsistencies; please fix for camera-ready.

Quality
The technical development is careful, grounded in standard causal graphical machinery, and proofs are promised in the appendix. The extension of ordered factorizations (with minimality) is well-motivated and the Example 1 explicitly shows why minimality matters. The causal recoverability results make correct use of do-calculus and dormant independences. The attrition theorems provide clean necessary-and-sufficient characterizations. I did not find contradictions; however, including brief proof sketches for Theorems 2, 4, and 6 in the main text would help readers assess correctness without deep appendix dives.

Clarity
The paper is mostly well-written, but dense. Suggestions:
- Consolidate notation in a table; fix the G_X/G^X notation; ensure all sets (e.g., W, W1) are defined before use.
- For Theorem 2, illustrate R-partitions and Mb(R(i)) with a fully worked example (graph + step-by-step computation of P(V)).
- For Theorem 4, give a visual explanation of d*-separation in the presented example and explicitly list Z and W employed.
- Improve figure captions to be self-contained.

Originality
The work provides novel generalizations beyond Mohan et al. (2013): (i) admissible sequences including R; (ii) recovery via R-partitions/Markov blankets over collider paths; (iii) graphical non-recoverability criteria; and (iv) showing causal effects can be recoverable despite non-recoverability of associated probabilistic factors, with explicit conditions. To my knowledge, these are new and nontrivial contributions in the MNAR/graphical causality space.

Significance
Understanding when MNAR queries are recoverable is both theoretically important and practically relevant. The conditions here can guide study design and analysis (e.g., when attrition can be handled without bias). The explicit estimands enable application without full-blown parametric modeling. I expect these results to influence subsequent work on automated recoverability decision procedures and to inform practice in epidemiology, social science, and recommendation systems where MNAR is common.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide an explicit algorithm that, given G and a query, searches for an admissible sequence per Theorem 1 or certifies failure, and constructs R-partitions/Mb(R(i)) per Theorem 2? What is its complexity?
- How robust are the estimands to mild violations of the assumed m-graph (e.g., weak extra edges)? Any sensitivity analysis suggestions?
- In Theorem 4, can you relax the |Vm|=1 assumption, or provide a counterexample showing why it is needed?
- Can you relate Theorem 2’s formula to inverse probability weighting/selection models, clarifying similarities and differences?

Minor issues and typos
- Fix reference formatting (escapes, missing venues/years).
- Clarify the statement “R variables may not be parents of variables in V ∪ U” (is this a modeling restriction by definition of m-graphs?).
- Ensure all uses of d-separation/d*-separation and inducing/collider paths are consistently defined.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, novel theoretical contributions with clear practical implications.
- Bridges probabilistic and causal recoverability in MNAR, advancing state of the art.
- Provides both positive and negative graphical criteria and necessary-and-sufficient results in an important special case (attrition).

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of a fully specified algorithm and empirical demonstrations may limit immediate usability.
- Some clarity/notation issues and reliance on appendices for key proofs.

Overall recommendation: Accept. The paper makes substantive, original contributions to graphical models for missing data and causal inference, and fits NIPS’s scope and standards. With improved exposition and minor polishing, it will be a valuable addition.
