- Core contribution: Provides more general graphical conditions for recovering probabilistic (P(y|x), P(y,x)) and causal (P(y|do(x))) queries from missing data.  
- Key finding: Shows causal effects can be recoverable even when corresponding probabilistic relationships are not.  
- Framework: Extends and leverages Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to represent missing-data processes.  
- Theoretical strength: Presents theorems (1–6) that are mathematically sound and supported by illustrative examples.  
- Practical relevance: Applies results to attrition problems, demonstrating real-world applicability.  
- Literature integration: Thoroughly connects to and expands existing missing-data literature, including ML, IPW, and graphical-model approaches.  
- Detailed analysis: Offers a clear discussion of recoverability, non-recoverability, and sequential factorization nuances.  
- Presentation weakness: Dense exposition and heavy notation make the paper hard to follow without strong causal-inference background.  
- Example limitation: Examples are helpful but should be expanded to cover more complex scenarios for better intuition.  
- Context gap: Limited discussion of recent imputation advances (e.g., deep-learning multiple imputation) reduces contemporary context.  
- Claim support: Some bold claims (e.g., “rectifies prevailing opinion”) lack extensive direct rebuttal or comparative evidence.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel extension to causal queries, rigorous theory, practical impact, and strong ties to prior work.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Accessibility issues due to density/notation, insufficient discussion of recent techniques, and some claims needing stronger comparison.  
- Overall recommendation: Significant theoretical contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions focused on clarity and contextualization.
