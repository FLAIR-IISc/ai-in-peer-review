## Review of "Graphical Models for Recovering Probabilistic and Causal Queries from Missing Data"

This paper extends previous work on recoverability of queries from data with missing entries, building upon the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. The authors aim to provide more general conditions for recovering both probabilistic queries (P(y|x), P(y,x)) and, crucially, causal queries (P(y|do(x))) in the presence of missing data. A key contribution is demonstrating that causal effects can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not, a scenario often encountered in practice. The paper systematically refines the sequential factorization theorem and introduces new graphical criteria for non-recoverability, culminating in applications to the specific problem of attrition.

The paper’s strength lies in its rigorous theoretical development and extension of existing results. The authors clearly articulate the conditions under which recoverability is possible, and the introduction of Theorem 4, demonstrating recoverability of causal queries even with inseparable Y and Ry, is a significant advancement. The detailed exploration of attrition scenarios and the correction of prevailing opinions in the literature (citing Garcia [2013]) showcase the practical relevance of the work. The writing is generally clear, and the organization logically progresses through definitions, theorems, and applications.

However, the paper suffers from a degree of density and assumes a strong prior understanding of the existing literature on graphical models and causal inference. While the authors reference Mohan et al. [2013] extensively, a more accessible introduction to m-graphs for readers less familiar with this specific framework would be beneficial. Furthermore, while the theoretical results are strong, the empirical validation is absent. Including even a small simulation study demonstrating the practical implications of the theorems would significantly strengthen the paper. The extensive referencing, while thorough, occasionally feels overwhelming and could be streamlined.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of recoverability conditions to causal queries.
*   Rigorous theoretical foundation and clear presentation of results.
*   Addresses a practically important problem (missing data) with a formal framework.
*   Corrects misconceptions in the literature regarding attrition.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   High level of technical detail and assumes significant prior knowledge.
*   Lack of empirical validation to demonstrate practical applicability.
*   Dense writing style and potentially overwhelming referencing.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of causal inference with missing data. While the lack of empirical validation and the density of the presentation are weaknesses, the novelty and rigor of the work justify acceptance, particularly if the authors consider adding a small empirical component in a future version. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
