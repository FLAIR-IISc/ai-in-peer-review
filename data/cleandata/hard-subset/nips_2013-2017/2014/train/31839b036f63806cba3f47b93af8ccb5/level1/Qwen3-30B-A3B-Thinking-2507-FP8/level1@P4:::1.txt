

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to develop more general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors address a fundamental problem in statistical analysis: determining whether a query (probabilistic or causal) can be consistently estimated from data with missing entries. They build upon the m-graph framework, which explicitly models the missingness process alongside observed variables, and extend previous recoverability conditions to handle more complex missingness mechanisms, particularly when missingness mechanisms interact with each other.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it refines the sequential factorization theorem from Mohan et al. (2013) to handle cases where missingness mechanisms influence each other. Second, it introduces new graphical criteria for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions, which complements existing sufficient conditions. Third, and most significantly, it demonstrates that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) may be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations (e.g., P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are not recoverable. This is a crucial insight, as it shows that causal effects can sometimes be estimated despite obstacles that would prevent estimation of the underlying probabilistic relations. The paper further applies these results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects under simple attrition.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The theoretical contributions are rigorously developed with precise definitions, theorems, and proofs (referenced to the appendix). The examples (e.g., Example 1 and Example 3) effectively illustrate the application of the theorems. The paper carefully distinguishes between probabilistic and causal recoverability, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of the relevant literature, properly situating their work within the context of missing data theory and causal inference.

The paper's primary strength lies in its theoretical novelty, particularly the demonstration that causal queries can be recoverable when the probabilistic relations are not. This insight challenges previous assumptions about the relationship between probabilistic and causal recoverability. The paper also provides comprehensive graphical conditions for recoverability and non-recoverability, which represent a substantial extension of Mohan et al. (2013).

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The notation is consistent and well-defined, and the logical flow from theoretical foundations to specific applications is coherent. The examples effectively illustrate the theoretical concepts, and the connection to attrition problems demonstrates practical relevance.

The significance of this work is substantial. Missing data is a pervasive challenge across scientific disciplines, and the paper provides a principled framework for determining when queries can be recovered from missing data. The distinction between recoverable probabilistic and causal queries has important implications for causal inference with missing data, particularly in fields like epidemiology and social sciences where attrition is common. The paper's results rectify previous misconceptions about attrition (e.g., the paper shows that Garcia's (2013) necessary condition for non-recoverability is incorrect).

The paper's main limitation is the lack of empirical validation beyond theoretical examples. While the theoretical framework is sound, demonstrating the practical utility of these conditions through simulations or real-world applications would strengthen the paper's contribution. The authors could also provide more guidance on how to apply these conditions in practice, particularly regarding the construction of m-graphs from real data.

The paper is clearly original and makes a significant contribution to the field. It extends the m-graph framework in meaningful ways that address limitations in previous work, particularly regarding interactions among missingness mechanisms and the recoverability of causal queries. The paper's main contributions are distinct from those in previous NIPS proceedings, particularly the work by Mohan et al. (2013) and Garcia (2013), which the paper properly acknowledges and extends.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs and well-supported claims. The authors carefully address both strengths and limitations of their approach, particularly in the non-recoverability criteria. The paper is complete as a theoretical contribution, though empirical validation would be a valuable addition.

The paper's significance is high as it provides a framework for determining whether a query can be recovered from missing data, which is a fundamental question in statistical analysis. The results have direct implications for practitioners working with missing data, particularly in causal inference settings.

Pro: The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution by extending the m-graph framework to handle complex missingness mechanisms and demonstrating the recoverability of causal queries when probabilistic relations are not recoverable. The results are rigorously proven and provide necessary and sufficient conditions that advance the field.

Con: The lack of empirical validation limits the paper's practical impact, as the theoretical conditions need to be tested in real-world scenarios to demonstrate their utility.

Overall, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of missing data analysis and causal inference. It provides a principled framework for determining when probabilistic and causal queries can be recovered from missing data, with particular emphasis on the novel insight that causal effects may be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and represents a meaningful extension of previous work. While empirical validation would strengthen the contribution, the theoretical advances are substantial and significant for the field. The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS.
