- Problem and contribution: Studies recoverability of causal and probabilistic queries from MNAR data using graphical m-graphs, extending Mohan et al. [2013] with new criteria and algorithms.  
- Key theoretical finding: Shows causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when associated probabilistic relations are not.  
- Application: Provides necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects in attrition problems.  
- Strength — theoretical contributions: Extends the sequential factorization theorem and introduces novel graphical criteria for recoverability and non-recoverability.  
- Strength — causal query recovery: Novel and impactful demonstration that causal effects may be recoverable despite non-recoverable probabilistic components.  
- Strength — clarity of framework: Uses missingness graphs (m-graphs) for formal, precise reasoning about missingness.  
- Strength — application to attrition: Well-motivated application showing practical utility.  
- Strength — thoroughness: Comprehensive paper with detailed proofs, examples, and related-work discussion.  
- Weakness — presentation complexity: High technical density and formalism may hinder readers unfamiliar with graphical models or causal inference.  
- Weakness — limited empirical validation: Heavy theoretical focus with few or no experiments on real-world datasets.  
- Weakness — limited application scope: Does not deeply explore other common missing-data scenarios (e.g., imputation, collaborative filtering).  
- Weakness — reliance on correct m-graphs: Assumes accurate m-graph specification and does not address misspecification handling.  
- Argument for acceptance: Significant, novel, and rigorous theoretical advancement in missing data and causal inference.  
- Argument against acceptance: Accessibility concerns, lack of empirical validation, and dependence on accurate m-graphs may limit practical impact.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to improve clarity and address practical applicability concerns.
