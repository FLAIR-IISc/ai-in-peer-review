This paper tackles the problem of recoverability—determining whether a probabilistic or causal query can be consistently estimated from a dataset with missing values. The authors use the m-graph formalism, introduced in Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), which explicitly models the missingness mechanisms. This work significantly extends their previous results by providing more general graphical conditions for recoverability and, importantly, introduces the first formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability.

The main contributions are:
1.  A generalization of the sequential factorization theorem to handle dependencies among missingness mechanisms, which is common in longitudinal studies.
2.  A new method for recovering the full joint distribution, P(V), for a class of problems where sequential factorization is not applicable (Theorem 2).
3.  Novel graphical criteria that are sufficient for *non-recoverability* of joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2). This provides a necessary counterpart to the previously known sufficient conditions for recoverability.
4.  A key and surprising finding that causal queries, such as P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the corresponding observational distribution P(y,x) is not. This is demonstrated by showing how do-calculus can be used to circumvent problematic dependencies between a variable and its missingness mechanism.
5.  An application of these theoretical results to the problem of attrition, yielding necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects in simple attrition models and correcting a claim in the existing literature.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem in statistics and machine learning. Moving beyond the standard, and often unrealistic, Missing At Random (MAR) assumption is crucial for robust inference. The results presented provide a powerful, principled framework for reasoning about what can and cannot be learned from incomplete data under more general Missing Not At Random (MNAR) conditions. The insight that causal queries can be "easier" to recover than observational ones is profound and could have a significant impact on how researchers in various fields approach problems with missing data, particularly in experimental settings.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The paper makes several highly original contributions. The development of graphical criteria for non-recoverability is a major step forward, as it allows researchers to definitively conclude when their assumptions are insufficient, rather than simply failing to find a recovery strategy. The application of do-calculus and d*-separation to the missing data problem in Section 6 is both elegant and novel, connecting two important lines of research in causality in a new and insightful way.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The theorems are clearly stated, and the proofs (provided in the appendix) build logically on the established framework of graphical models and causal inference. The use of carefully constructed examples and diagrams is extremely effective in illustrating complex theoretical concepts and demonstrating the power of the proposed criteria. The correction of a result from prior work on attrition (Garcia [2013]) further showcases the authors' technical command and the carefulness of their analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are substantial, the work would be significantly strengthened by including experiments on either simulated or real-world data. Such experiments could demonstrate how the proposed recovery formulas perform with finite sample sizes and could provide insights into the robustness of the methods when the graphical model is slightly misspecified. This would greatly enhance the paper's practical value.

2.  **Accessibility and Density:** The paper is very dense and assumes a high level of familiarity with the technical machinery of Pearl's causal inference framework (e.g., d-separation, do-calculus, semi-markovian models, inducing paths, d*-separation). While this is acceptable for the NIPS audience, it may limit the paper's immediate accessibility to a broader machine learning audience. Some definitions, like that of the Markov Blanket (Mb) in Section 4, are introduced very quickly and could benefit from slightly more exposition.

3.  **Scope of Certain Results:** Some of the key theorems for causal recovery and attrition (Theorems 4, 5, 6) are limited to the case of a single partially observed variable (|Vm|=1). While this is an important and common case (e.g., simple attrition), a discussion on the challenges and potential paths toward generalizing these results to multiple interacting missing variables would be valuable. Similarly, the preconditions for Theorem 2 are quite specific, and it is not immediately clear how broad the class of models it covers is in practice.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   High significance: Addresses the fundamental problem of inference from incomplete data beyond MAR.
    *   Excellent originality: Introduces the first graphical criteria for non-recoverability and a novel link between causal identification and missing data recovery.
    *   Strong technical quality: Rigorous theoretical results that substantially advance the state-of-the-art established by Mohan et al. [2013].
    *   The results provide deep conceptual insights, particularly the finding that causal queries may be recoverable when observational ones are not.

*   **Arguments for rejection:**
    *   The paper is purely theoretical, with no experiments to validate the proposed estimation strategies on finite-sample data.
    *   The presentation is dense and assumes specialized knowledge, potentially limiting its audience.
    *   Some key results are limited to the single missing variable case.

**Conclusion:**

This is a very strong theoretical paper that makes significant and novel contributions to our understanding of inference with missing data. The weaknesses, primarily the lack of experiments and the dense presentation, are outweighed by the importance, originality, and technical quality of the results. The paper provides a powerful new set of conceptual and graphical tools for a problem of broad relevance. I strongly recommend acceptance.
