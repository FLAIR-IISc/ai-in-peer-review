This paper tackles the important problem of recovering causal and probabilistic queries from data with missing entries, building upon the “Missingness Graphs” (m-graphs) framework introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. The authors extend previous results by providing more general conditions for recoverability, and crucially, demonstrate scenarios where causal queries are recoverable even when probabilistic ones are not. The focus on attrition as a concrete application is also valuable.

The paper is well-motivated; handling missing data correctly is fundamental to reliable inference. The extension of the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) to incorporate interactions between missingness mechanisms is a significant step. The introduction of Theorem 2, addressing cases where no admissible sequence exists, broadens the applicability of the framework. The key contribution – demonstrating recoverability of causal queries when probabilistic ones are not – is a compelling result. The detailed exploration of attrition scenarios (Sections 7.1 and 7.2) effectively illustrates the practical implications.

However, the paper suffers from a few weaknesses. The presentation is very dense and often relies heavily on formal notation without sufficient intuition. While the proofs are relegated to the appendix, the core arguments could benefit from more illustrative examples *within* the main text, particularly for the more complex theorems. For example, while Example 1 is helpful, more diverse and less contrived examples would increase clarity. The connection between d*-separation and the recoverability of causal queries (Theorem 4) feels somewhat abrupt without further explanation of why this condition holds. 

The related work section is extensive but reads more as a catalog of papers than a critical assessment. A more focused discussion of how this work *differs* from, and builds upon, existing approaches (e.g., MAR estimation, multiple imputation) would strengthen the contribution.  The contrast with Garcia [2013] is highlighted, but a more nuanced comparison of advantages and disadvantages would be beneficial. The paper frequently references Mohan et al. [2013] and Mohan & Pearl [2014], and while this is appropriate given the extension, it sometimes feels like the paper is written *for* readers already intimately familiar with those works.

The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) are valuable additions, but their practical application could be improved. Providing guidelines for identifying when these conditions are met in real-world datasets would be useful.  The terminology, such as "inducing path" (Definition 5) and "dormant independence," is introduced without sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with Shpitser and Pearl [2008].

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the theoretical results appear rigorously developed. The extension of existing theorems and the novel result concerning causal query recovery are substantial. However, the density of the presentation sometimes obscures the underlying logic.

**Clarity:**  The clarity is the primary weakness. The paper is difficult to follow without a strong background in causal inference and missing data. More intuitive explanations and illustrative examples are needed. The notation is heavy, and a more gradual introduction of concepts would improve readability.

**Originality:** The work is original in extending the m-graph framework and demonstrating the recoverability of causal queries under conditions where probabilistic queries are not recoverable. This represents a significant contribution to the field.

**Significance:** The results are important because they provide a more nuanced understanding of the conditions under which inferences can be drawn from incomplete data. The application to attrition problems highlights the practical relevance of the work. The findings have the potential to impact fields where missing data is prevalent, such as epidemiology, social sciences, and longitudinal studies.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel and significant theoretical results regarding causal query recoverability.
*   Extension of the existing m-graph framework to more general scenarios.
*   Clear and rigorous mathematical development (though presentation is dense).
*   Practical application to a relevant problem (attrition).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Poor clarity and density of presentation.
*   Limited intuition and illustrative examples.
*   Related work section lacks critical assessment.
*   Practical guidance for applying the results is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The theoretical contributions are substantial, but the paper needs significant improvements in clarity and presentation to reach its full potential. Specifically, the authors should focus on adding more intuitive explanations, illustrative examples, and streamlining the notation. Addressing the weaknesses in the related work section would also strengthen the paper. The core ideas are valuable and warrant publication in NIPS, but the paper requires substantial polishing to become accessible to a broader audience.
