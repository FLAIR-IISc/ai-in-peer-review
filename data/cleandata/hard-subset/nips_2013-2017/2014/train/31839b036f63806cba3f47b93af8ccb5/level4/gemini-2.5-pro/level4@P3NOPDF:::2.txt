This paper addresses the critical and challenging problem of causal inference from data with missing observations. The authors present a novel and comprehensive graphical framework for determining the identifiability of causal queries. The core contribution is the derivation of necessary and sufficient conditions that allow a researcher to ascertain whether a specific causal effect can be computed from the available (incomplete) data, given a causal model that explicitly incorporates the missingness mechanism.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this work lies in its theoretical rigor and completeness. By providing both necessary and sufficient conditions, the paper moves beyond previous results that often focused only on sufficient conditions. This provides a complete characterization of identifiability within this framework, which is a substantial advance for the field. It equips researchers with a definitive tool to assess the feasibility of their causal questions before attempting estimation.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, focused, and logically structured. The authors do an excellent job of situating their work within the existing literature, clearly building upon previous contributions while highlighting the novelty of their own. The formalisms are introduced systematically, making the core ideas accessible despite their technical nature.

3.  **Elegant Methodological Framework:** The approach of augmenting the causal diagram to explicitly represent the missingness-generating process is powerful and intuitive. This unified framework simplifies the analysis by treating missingness not as a peripheral nuisance but as an integrated part of the causal system, which is the correct and most principled way to approach the problem.

**Areas for Improvement:**

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper could be further enhanced in a few areas to broaden its impact:

1.  **Practical Illustration:** The manuscript would benefit from a more developed, running example or a small case study. Demonstrating how the proposed graphical criteria are applied step-by-step to a tangible problem (e.g., from epidemiology or social sciences) would make the theoretical results more accessible and highlight their practical utility for applied researchers.

2.  **Discussion of Assumptions:** The framework relies on the correctness of the specified causal graph, including the model of the missingness mechanism. A more detailed discussion on the sensitivity of the results to potential model misspecification would be valuable. For instance, how robust are the conclusions if there is uncertainty about the structure of the causal graph?

3.  **Algorithmic Implementation:** The paper establishes the graphical conditions for identifiability, but a brief discussion on the computational aspects of verifying these conditions would be a welcome addition. Is there an efficient algorithm for checking the criteria on a given graph, and if so, what is its complexity? This would provide a practical pathway for implementing these checks in software.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable and fundamental contribution to the literature on causal inference and missing data. The work is theoretically sound, clearly presented, and addresses a problem of significant importance. The establishment of complete graphical criteria for identifiability is a noteworthy achievement. I believe this work will be of great interest to the causality and machine learning communities. I recommend acceptance.
