The paper tackles the problem of identifiability in the presence of various mechanisms for missing data, using a framework introduced by Mohan and others. It improves upon some existing results and provides nice examples, such as the identifiability of causal distributions even when conditional distributions are unavailable.

Quality
The technical quality of the paper appears to be high, with involved but correct results. Theorem 3, for instance, is clear and interpretable. However, some results are incremental improvements upon existing ones and do not contribute to a broader understanding of the subject. Section 3 seems unrelated to the rest of the paper, and its main result, Theorem 1, is difficult to comprehend. It appears to be closely related to the Markov blanket results in Richardson (2009). The quality improves in later sections, with Section 5 providing clear answers and Section 6 offering interesting results about causal queries.

Clarity
The paper's main weakness lies in its clarity. The amount of material presented is likely too extensive for a conference paper, as evidenced by the dense text and the 13-page technical appendix. The article lacks a clear flow, making it challenging to follow. This issue might be resolved by expanding the paper into a longer, more considered journal article.

Notation is another area that requires improvement. Assigning distinct notations for the numerous quantities discussed is challenging, but the current notation could be enhanced. The repeated use of the capital letter 'V' to denote different objects makes it difficult for the reader to follow, and in some cases, the subscripts become illegible. The fact that 'Vi' represents a vertex while 'Vo' represents a set of vertices is particularly confusing. The set equation in Corollary 1 exemplifies poor presentation.

Originality and Significance
Sections 5 and 6 are clear and interesting, and potentially significant. The paper addresses an important problem and contains genuinely interesting and useful results. However, it might be beneficial to take a step back and analyze the general case, aiming to develop a complete criterion for identifiability within this framework, similar to the one established for finding causal effects without missingness. It is difficult to imagine a statistician searching through a series of papers to find the precise result that demonstrates (non-)identifiability of the causal effect they are interested in, especially if that result is as complicated as Theorem 1.

Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to be a form of inverse probability weighting. The reason these results work is likely related to the conditioning sets being Markov blankets for R(i) in the mixed graphs used by the author. Richardson (2003) and (2009) might be relevant.

Other Comments
On page 4, line 214, 'R^part' is a partition of 'R', not "a set containing partitions." The condition on line 214 should be rephrased to state that 'Rx' and 'Ry' "belong to distinct elements of the partition."

The language used is occasionally casual, such as 'oftentimes' on page 4, line 213. Instead of '<-->', the symbol '\leftrightarrow' should be used.

It is unclear whether Garcia (2013) is incorrect or simply operates within a different framework.

A notation for graphs constructed by severing edges is used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4 but is not defined anywhere.

'Vo' and 'Vm' are used in the sentence before they are defined, which appears unnecessary.
