### Summary

This paper addresses the important problem of identifiability in graphical models under various missing data mechanisms, building upon the framework introduced by Mohan et al. The work contains several technically involved results, improves upon existing findings, and presents valuable examples, such as establishing the identifiability of causal distributions when certain conditional distributions are unavailable. While the technical contributions, particularly in Sections 5 and 6, are potentially significant, the paper's overall impact is severely undermined by significant issues with clarity, notation, and presentation. The density of the material and the lack of a clear, overarching narrative make the paper extremely difficult to follow.

### Quality and Technical Merit

The technical quality of the paper appears to be high. The derivations are intricate, and the results, where comprehensible, seem correct. Results such as Theorem 3 are presented clearly and are readily interpretable. Sections 5 and 6 are the strongest parts of the paper, providing clear answers to specific identifiability questions and offering interesting and useful results concerning causal queries.

However, some of the contributions feel like incremental improvements on existing work, and it is not always clear how they contribute to a broader, more unified understanding of identifiability under missingness. Section 3, in particular, seems disconnected from the rest of the paper. Its main result, Theorem 1, is exceptionally complex and difficult to comprehend, which limits its practical utility. This result appears to be closely related to the Markov blanket results for mixed graphs in Richardson (2009), and a discussion of this connection would be beneficial.

### Clarity and Presentation

Clarity is the paper's most significant weakness. The authors have attempted to fit an extensive amount of material into a conference paper format, resulting in a dense and convoluted presentation. The 13-page technical appendix is further evidence that the work might be better suited for a longer, more thoroughly elaborated journal article. The logical flow is frequently disrupted, making it a challenge for the reader to follow the main thread of the argument.

The notational choices exacerbate these clarity issues. While acknowledging the difficulty of devising notation for the many quantities involved, the current system is a major obstacle to understanding.
*   The symbol 'V' is heavily overloaded to denote multiple distinct objects, forcing the reader to constantly rely on context and creating ambiguity.
*   Subscripts are often small to the point of being illegible.
*   The distinction between `Vi` as a single vertex and `Vo` as a set of vertices is particularly confusing and error-prone.
*   The set equation presented in Corollary 1 is an extreme example of presentation that hinders, rather than aids, comprehension.

### Originality and Significance

The paper tackles an important and challenging problem in causal inference and graphical models. The results presented in Sections 5 and 6 are original, interesting, and possess the potential for significant impact.

However, the paper could be made much more significant by stepping back to develop a more general theory. The current approach provides a collection of specific conditions and results. It is difficult to imagine a practitioner searching through a series of such papers to find the one specific, highly complex theorem that applies to their problem. The field would be better served by a contribution that works toward a complete, general criterion for identifiability in this framework, analogous to the completeness results established for identifying causal effects in the absence of missing data.

Furthermore, Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to be a form of inverse probability weighting. The underlying reason for their validity is likely related to the conditioning sets being Markov blankets for the missingness indicators `R(i)` in the corresponding mixed graphs. Explicitly discussing this connection and citing relevant work (e.g., Richardson, 2003; Richardson, 2009) would strengthen the paper by placing its results within a broader theoretical context and acknowledging related literature.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   **p. 4, l. 214:** The description of `R^part` as "a set containing partitions of R" seems imprecise. Is `R^part` intended to be a single partition of the set `R`? If so, the condition on line 214 should state that `Rx` and `Ry` "belong to distinct sets *within* the partition `R^part`."
*   **Undefined Notation:** The notation for graphs constructed by severing edges (e.g., `G_V'`) is used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4 but does not appear to be defined anywhere.
*   **Undefined Variables:** `Vo` and `Vm` are used in the text before their definitions are provided.
*   **Reference to Garcia (2013):** Is the work by Garcia (2013) claimed to be incorrect, or does it simply operate under a different framework? Clarification on this point would be helpful.
*   **Formatting:** On p. 4, l. 213, please use the standard LaTeX command `\leftrightarrow` for the bidirected edge symbol instead of `<-->`.
*   **Tone:** The language is occasionally informal (e.g., "oftentimes"). A more formal academic tone would be appropriate.
