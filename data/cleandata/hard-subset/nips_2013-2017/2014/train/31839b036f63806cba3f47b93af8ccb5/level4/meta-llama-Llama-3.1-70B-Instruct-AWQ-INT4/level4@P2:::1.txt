This paper addresses the problem of deciding whether a causal or probabilistic query is estimable from data corrupted by missing entries, given a model of missingness process. The authors extend the results of Mohan et al. [2013] by presenting more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries of the form P(y|x) and P(y, x) as well as causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)). The paper's main contribution is the derivation of graphical conditions for recovering causal effects of the form P(y|do(x)) when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable.

The technical quality of the paper is high, and the results appear to be correct. The authors provide several examples to illustrate their results, including an application to problems of attrition. The paper's main weakness is its clarity, with too much material packed into a conference paper format. The notation could be improved, and some of the results are hard to comprehend.

The paper's originality and significance lie in its clear and interesting results, particularly in Sections 5 and 6. The authors deal with an important problem and provide results that seem genuinely interesting and useful. However, it would be beneficial to step back and work out what is going on in the general case, and try to come up with a complete criterion for identifiability in this framework.

Some minor comments and suggestions for improvement:

* The notation could be improved, particularly the use of the same capital 'V' to denote different objects.
* The set equation in Corollary 1 is poorly presented and could be clarified.
* The language is sometimes casual, and more formal language could be used.
* The reference to Garcia [2013] could be clarified to indicate whether the result is incorrect or just a different framework.
* A notation for graphs constructed by severing edges is used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4 but not defined anywhere.
* Vo and Vm are used in the sentence before they are defined, apparently unnecessarily.

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and provides useful results, but could benefit from improvements in clarity and notation.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper addresses an important problem in the field of graphical models and missing data.
* The authors provide clear and interesting results, particularly in Sections 5 and 6.
* The paper's technical quality is high, and the results appear to be correct.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper's clarity is poor, with too much material packed into a conference paper format.
* The notation could be improved, and some of the results are hard to comprehend.
* The paper could benefit from a more general and complete criterion for identifiability in this framework.
