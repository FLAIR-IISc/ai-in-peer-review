Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies when probabilistic and causal queries are recoverable from data with missing entries, given an explicit model of the missingness process encoded by a missingness graph (m-graph). It extends Mohan et al. (2013) in several directions: (i) a generalized ordered factorization that includes R variables (missingness indicators), enabling recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) recovery of joint distributions in models without an admissible sequence via a new theorem based on collider paths and partitions of R; (iii) graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions; and (iv) recovery of causal effects P(y|do(x)) even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable, leveraging do-calculus and dormant independencies. The paper also applies these results to attrition, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering effects in “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1).

The work builds on Mohan et al. (2013) and Mohan & Pearl (2014) on m-graphs and recoverability, on Shpitser & Pearl (2006, 2008) for causal identifiability and d*-separation, and contrasts with MAR/MCAR approaches (Rubin, 1976; Little & Rubin, 2002) and typical ML/EM treatments (Lauritzen, 1995; Dempster et al., 1977; Koller & Friedman, 2009), by handling MNAR mechanisms in a graph-aware way.

Evaluation:
- Quality: The results appear technically sound, with proofs provided. The generalization of sequential factorization and the non-recoverability criteria are carefully stated. Some notational and definitional glitches impede readability in places (see below).
- Clarity: Generally well written and well structured, but some definitions are missing or inconsistent (e.g., “neighbor,” inclusion of V* in figures). A few statements are too vague (e.g., the ML discussion), and some examples/figures could use more detail.
- Originality: High. The extension to include R variables in ordered factorizations, the general recovery theorem without admissible sequences, explicit non-recoverability criteria, and the causal-recoverability results under inseparability are novel and important.
- Significance: Potentially significant for causal inference with missing data, especially in MNAR and attrition settings. Practical impact depends on the availability and credibility of m-graph assumptions in typical ML applications.

Strengths:
- Clear generalization of prior results, allowing interactions among missingness mechanisms.
- Useful non-recoverability criteria to bound what is possible.
- Conceptually important demonstration that causal effects can be recoverable even when probabilistic quantities are not.
- Concrete application to attrition with necessary and sufficient graphical conditions.

Main comments and suggestions:
1) Assumptions about the missingness process. The recovery results require a correctly specified m-graph, i.e., knowledge of how data are censored/missing. Please discuss how realistic this is in typical ML applications and provide guidance on model elicitation and sensitivity. It would help to bring more of the Figure 1 detail into the main text (e.g., by reducing its size and expanding the discussion), to illustrate how such assumptions are encoded and tested.

2) Population and observation assumptions. Section 2 seems to assume that the full set of sampling units is known: even if Vo=∅ and Rv=1 for all V∈Vm for an individual, the individual’s existence is recorded. Please make this explicit and note that problems like estimating the total population size (e.g., capture–recapture) are outside scope.

3) m-graph content (V* variables). The paper uses “m-graph” inconsistently: sometimes V* nodes are shown (Figure 1), elsewhere they are not (Figure 2). If V* are always part of the formal m-graph but omitted from some figures for clarity, please state this clearly and specify when/why they are omitted.

4) No edges from R to V or U. You prohibit R variables from being parents of V or U, while also treating R as genuine variables (not mere indicators). Please justify this restriction (e.g., substantive reasons, identifiability/pathologies it avoids), or clarify that it is for convenience and discuss how results might extend when R→V or R→U edges are allowed.

5) Define “neighbor.” The term “neighbor” appears (bottom of p.4) but is not defined. Please define it formally (e.g., adjacent in the relevant mixed graph).

6) Positivity and the comparison to Garcia. p.8, first paragraph: you “rectify” Garcia (2013). Your recoverability results assume positivity over complete cases. Does Garcia assume the same? If not, clarify that your results are under positivity and explain whether Garcia’s statements implicitly assume stronger or different conditions.

7) Maximum likelihood discussion. p.8, second paragraph: “Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR” is too vague. ML can be applied more broadly, but consistency requires correctly modeling the missingness and data-generating processes (often MAR plus correct parameterization). Please clarify precisely when ML/EM is valid in your framework, and why ML is not sufficient for the MNAR mechanisms your results handle.

Minor comments (typos/clarifications):
- p.1, line 33: “can bias outcomes” (tense/wording).
- p.2, Figure 1: “Qualifications” → check label/legend for correctness.
- p.3, Example 1 (line ~158): “P(Y|X,Z,Ry) = P(X,Z)” should be “P(Y|X,Z,Ry) = P(Y|X,Z).”
- p.3, line ~168: “Since Y ⟂⟂ R | X,Z” should be “Since Y ⟂⟂ Ry | X,Z.”
- p.3, line ~214: “Rpart = {R(1), R(2), …, R^N}” should be “Rpart = {R(1), R(2), …, R(N)}” (consistent parentheses).
- p.3, line ~215: “R^j” should be “R(j).”
- p.4, Corollary 1: “does not contain a partially observed variables” → “does not contain a partially observed variable.”
- p.6, Footnote 2: Please add a reference for the variable-level definition mentioned.

Pros:
- Solid and original theoretical advances beyond Mohan et al. (2013).
- Clear articulation of when recovery is impossible.
- Useful causal results under MNAR, with concrete attrition application.

Cons:
- Heavy reliance on accurate m-graph specification; guidance on elicitation/testing could be expanded.
- Some definitional inconsistencies and presentation issues (V* inclusion, “neighbor”).
- A few imprecise statements (e.g., ML under MAR) that should be tightened.

Overall: An interesting and technically sound paper that extends prior work and clarifies important boundaries between probabilistic and causal recoverability. The breadth of practical applicability hinges on how often reliable missingness models can be specified, but the theoretical contributions are valuable. I recommend addressing the clarity and assumption-justification issues above.
