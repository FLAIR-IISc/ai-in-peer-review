Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies recoverability: when probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y, x), P(y|x)) and causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be consistently estimated from data with missing values, given a graphical model of the missingness process (m-graph). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors generalize the sequential factorization approach to allow interactions among missingness mechanisms (R variables), and provide new algorithms for cases without admissible sequences. They introduce graphical conditions that preclude recoverability of joint and conditional distributions. A key conceptual contribution is that certain causal effects are recoverable even when associated probabilistic relations are not (e.g., P(y|do(x)) when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are inseparable), leveraging do-calculus and dormant independences (Shpitser & Pearl, 2006; Pearl, 2009; Shpitser & Pearl, 2008). The paper applies these results to attrition settings and characterizes necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering joint distributions and causal effects under “simple attrition.” Relative to prior work on MAR/MCAR, IPW, and imputation (Rubin; Robins & Rotnitzky; Little & Rubin), this paper provides principled, graph-based conditions for MNAR settings.

Strengths
- Extends sequential factorization to include R variables, enabling recoverability in models with interacting missingness mechanisms.
- Provides algorithms and constructive formulas for joint recovery when ordered factorizations fail.
- Introduces explicit non-recoverability criteria—useful guardrails for practitioners.
- Demonstrates that causal effects can be recoverable despite inseparability of Y and Ry, clarifying a nuanced but important distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability.
- Clear, relevant application to attrition with necessary and sufficient conditions.

Weaknesses / limitations
- Assumes an accurately specified m-graph; in many ML applications, the missingness process is only partially known.
- Positivity (strictly positive over complete cases) is assumed; the discussion of when and how results extend beyond positivity could be expanded.
- Some notational and expository inconsistencies (e.g., inclusion of V* in figures; undefined terms) impede readability.
- Discussion of maximum likelihood under MAR is too brief and may be misleading without clarifying its limits under MNAR graphs considered here.

Quality
The technical contributions appear sound and well-motivated, with proofs in the appendix. The non-recoverability results and the causal recoverability despite inseparability are particularly convincing. Clarifications requested below would strengthen the presentation and practical guidance.

Clarity
Generally well written, but several definitions, notation choices, and figure conventions should be clarified for self-containment (see Detailed comments). Bringing some key examples and definitions into the main text would help the broader NIPS audience.

Originality
High. The generalization to interacting missingness mechanisms, explicit non-recoverability criteria, and the recovery of causal effects in cases where joint distributions are not recoverable are novel and valuable.

Significance
Moderate to high. The work advances principled inference under MNAR using graphical models. Its impact will be strongest where investigators can credibly specify an m-graph; clearer guidance on eliciting/validating such graphs would broaden applicability.

Detailed comments (major)
1) Assumptions about knowledge of the missingness process
- The results require a correctly specified m-graph, including dependencies among R variables. Please discuss how such knowledge is obtained in typical ML applications, practical diagnostics (e.g., Mohan & Pearl, 2014), and the robustness of recoverability to partial misspecification.
- Suggestion: Include more detail from Figure 1 in the main text (even if the figure is slightly reduced), to concretely illustrate how assumptions map to recoverability.

2) Population and observation model
- It appears the framework assumes the sampling frame (population units) is known, even if all substantive variables for some units are missing (Vo = ∅ and Rv = 1 for all V ∈ Vm). Please state this explicitly and contrast with settings where units themselves may be unobserved (e.g., capture–recapture), which are out of scope.

3) Definition and use of m-graphs, inclusion of V*
- The term “m-graph” is used both with and without explicit inclusion of proxy variables V*. Please standardize: either consistently include V* in figures or explicitly state when V* is omitted for readability, and explain how Equation (1) maps underlying to manifest distributions in both depictions.

4) Prohibition of edges from R to V or U
- The model disallows R variables as parents of V or U, yet R are treated as real (not purely notational) variables. Please justify this restriction (e.g., conceptual reasons, identifiability, or convenience), and clarify whether allowing R → V would change any theorems materially.

5) Undefined terminology
- “Neighbor” is first used without definition. Please define it precisely (e.g., adjacent nodes connected by an edge in the mixed graph) and ensure consistency with Markov blanket and inducing/collider path definitions introduced later.

6) Positivity and comparisons to Garcia (2013)
- Recoverability here assumes positivity (strict positivity over complete cases). When contrasting with Garcia (2013) (p. 8, first paragraph), please clarify whether Garcia also assumes positivity; if not, explain how differing assumptions affect the “rectification” claim.

7) Maximum likelihood and MAR
- The statement “Maximum Likelihood yields consistent estimates under MAR” is too broad. Please clarify: ML is consistent if the model for the full data and the MAR mechanism are correctly specified, but ML generally fails under MNAR mechanisms considered here. Explain why standard ML/EM cannot be directly applied to your MNAR classes without modeling the missingness process, and relate this to your recoverability formulas.

Minor comments and typos
- p.1 line 33: “can bias outcomes” → acceptable; consider “can bias estimates and distort conclusions.”
- p.2 Figure 1: “Qualifications” (ensure correct spelling and label).
- p.3 Example 1, line 158: P(Y|X, Z, Ry) = P(Y|X, Z) (RHS currently mistyped).
- p.3 line 168: use Ry explicitly: Y ⊥ Ry | X, Z (not Y ⊥ R | X, Z).
- p.3 line 214: Condition (ii) Rpart = {R(1), R(2), …, R(N)} (not R^N).
- p.3 line 215: use R(j) (not R^j).
- p.4 Corollary 1: “does not contain a partially observed variables” → “does not contain a partially observed variable.”
- p.6 Footnote 2: Please provide a reference for the variable-level definition.
- Throughout: standardize “m-graph” (vs “mgraph”).

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid, novel theoretical contributions addressing important MNAR cases.
- Clear articulation of both positive and negative (non-recoverability) conditions.
- Valuable insight that causal effects may be recoverable even when related probabilistic queries are not.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on correctly specified m-graphs; practical guidance on elicitation/validation could be strengthened.
- Expository issues (notation, undefined terms, figure conventions) hinder accessibility.
- Limited empirical illustrations; more hands-on examples or simulations would aid adoption.

Overall
An interesting and technically sound extension of graphical methods for missing data that advances both theory and practice in MNAR settings. With clarifications to assumptions, terminology, and the discussion of ML/MAR, this paper would make a strong NIPS contribution.
