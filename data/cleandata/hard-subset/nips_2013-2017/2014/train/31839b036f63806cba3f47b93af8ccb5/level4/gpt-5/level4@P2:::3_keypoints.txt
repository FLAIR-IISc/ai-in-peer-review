- Summary: Extends Mohan et al. (2013) by generalizing ordered factorizations to include R-variables, giving algorithms/graphical conditions for joint recovery without admissible sequences, and providing non-recoverability criteria; shows some causal effects can be recovered even when related probabilistic quantities cannot, with attrition analysis.

- Strength: Provides substantive technical advances beyond prior work, including a more general factorization criterion (Theorem 1) and recovery formulas (Theorem 2).

- Strength: Introduces novel non-recoverability conditions that clarify limits of inference (Theorem 3; Corollary 2).

- Strength: Demonstrates the insightful result that certain causal effects are recoverable despite non-recoverability of key probabilistic relations, with concrete attrition applications (Theorem 4; Theorems 5–6).

- Strength: Well-motivated and rigorous presentation with proofs in the appendix.

- Weakness: Assumes a correctly specified missingness model (m-graph) without enough discussion of how such models are obtained or validated in practice.

- Weakness: Some definitional and notation issues (e.g., “neighbor,” inconsistent inclusion of V* in m-graphs) need clarification.

- Weakness: Restrictions on parents of R-variables are asserted but not fully justified.

- Weakness: Statements about maximum likelihood under MAR are overly broad and may mislead.

- Quality: Results appear technically sound, consistent with existing causal/missing data theory, and carefully stated.

- Clarity: Generally well written but needs tightened definitions, notation, and corrections in some examples/figures.

- Originality: Novel generalization to include R-variables in ordered factorization, broader recoverability algorithms for semi-Markovian models, and non-recoverability criteria.

- Significance: Graphical criteria could meaningfully guide analysts in MNAR settings (e.g., longitudinal studies, attrition) and influence methods and practice.

- Argument for acceptance: Meaningful theoretical advances on MNAR recoverability.

- Argument for acceptance: Clear connection to and extension of established causal identification tools.

- Argument for acceptance: Practical value via explicit criteria and applied examples (attrition).

- Argument against acceptance: Heavy reliance on a known, correct missingness graph with limited practical guidance on elicitation/assessment.

- Argument against acceptance: Presentation issues (definitions, notation, minor errors) hinder readability.

- Argument against acceptance: Scope of empirical applicability could be further illustrated.

- Main comment 1: Discuss how m-graphs are elicited/assessed in ML workflows, sensitivity to misspecification, and whether graphical tests (e.g., Mohan & Pearl 2014) apply; bring Figure 1 details into main text.

- Main comment 2: Explicitly state the population-frame assumption (full population known even if substantive variables are missing) and clarify out-of-scope problems like unknown-population-size estimation.

- Main comment 3: Clarify convention on whether m-graphs include V* (proxies) or omit them for clarity, and state this upfront.

- Main comment 4: Justify the restriction disallowing R→V or R→U edges (interpretation, identifiability, or convenience) and discuss possible extensions if such edges are permitted.

- Main comment 5: Provide a formal definition of “neighbor”/adjacency in the semi-Markovian setting.

- Main comment 6: Clarify positivity assumptions in recoverability definition and how this compares to Garcia (2013), specifying whether the paper’s condition is stronger.

- Main comment 7: Temper the ML-under-MAR claim: note ML consistency depends on correct likelihood specification and contrast with the paper’s graphical recoverability focus.

- Minor comments/typos: Multiple specific wording and notation corrections needed (examples: p.1 line 33 “can bias outcomes” → “can bias estimates and conclusions”; Example 1 typo P(Y|X,Z,Ry)=P(Y|X,Z); R/notation inconsistencies; Corollary 1 grammar; cite variable-level definition in Footnote 2).

- Overall evaluation: Strong, technically solid paper that advances MNAR recoverability theory; leans toward acceptance pending clarifications on assumptions, definitions, and practical elicitation of m-graphs.
