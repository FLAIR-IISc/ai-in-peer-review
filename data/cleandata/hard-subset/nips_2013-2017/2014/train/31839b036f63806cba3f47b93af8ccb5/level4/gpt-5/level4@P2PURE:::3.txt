This paper addresses when a distribution can be recovered given knowledge of both the data-generating mechanism and the missingness process. It considers a broader class of missingness mechanisms than prior work by Mohan et al., yielding a new identification formula. The authors also study identification of causal queries and show that these can be identified even when the observational joint distribution is not. The paper is generally well written, though there are several issues and opportunities for clarification noted below.

MAIN COMMENTS:
(1) The results rely on knowing the censoring (missingness) mechanism. Is such knowledge available in typical machine learning applications? It would help to include more detail about Figure 1 in the main text (e.g., by shrinking the figure to make room).

(2) Section 2: The missing-data setup implicitly assumes the full population is known. Even if V_o = ∅ and R_v = 1 for all V ∈ V_m (so no variables are observed for a given individual), the framework assumes we still know that this individual exists. This excludes problems like estimating the size of a population (e.g., number of animals).

(3) The term “mgraph” is used ambiguously. Sometimes the graph includes the V* variables (Figure 1), and sometimes it does not (Figure 2). If V* variables are merely being omitted for presentation, please state this clearly and specify when V* nodes are part of the graph.

(4) The paper disallows R variables as parents of variables in V or U in mgraphs. Since the R variables are treated as real quantities rather than mere indicators, please justify this restriction (or clarify if it is primarily for convenience).

(5) The term “neighbor,” used at the bottom of p.4, is not defined. Please define it.

(6) p.8, first paragraph: On “rectification” of observations by Garcia et al. The definition of recoverability here assumes positivity. Do Garcia et al. also assume positivity? If not, they may be relying on a stronger condition—please clarify the relationship.

(7) p.8, second paragraph: “Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption” is too vague. Why can’t maximum likelihood be applied to the missingness processes considered here? Please be specific about the limitations and assumptions.

MINOR COMMENTS:
- p.1, line 33: “can bias outcomes.”
- p.2, Figure 1: “Qualifications.”
- p.2; Example 1 on p.3, line 158: P(Y | X, Z, R_y) = P(X, Z). The RHS should be P(Y | X, Z).
- p.3, line 168: “Since Y ⟂⟂ R | X, Z” should be “Y ⟂⟂ R_y | X, Z.”
- p.3, line 214: Condition (ii) R^{part} = {R^{(1)}, R^{(2)}, …, R^N} should be R^{(N)}.
- p.3, line 215: “R^j respectively” should be “R^{(j)}.”
- p.4, Corollary 1: “does not contain a partially observed variables” → “does not contain partially observed variables.”
- p.6, Footnote 2: Please provide a reference for the variable-level definition.

Overall: An interesting paper that builds on earlier work. Not clear how broad the application is, but technically sound.
