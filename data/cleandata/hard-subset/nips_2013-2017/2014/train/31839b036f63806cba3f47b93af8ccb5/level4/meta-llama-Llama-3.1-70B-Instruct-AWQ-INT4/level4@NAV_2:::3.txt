This is an interesting paper that addresses the problem of determining when a distribution may be recovered from knowledge of the generating process and the process leading to missing data. The authors consider a more general class of missingness processes than previously studied, leading to a novel identification formula. Additionally, they examine the identification of causal queries and demonstrate that these can be identified even when the observational joint distribution is not identified.

The paper is generally well-written, but there are some areas for improvement. The results presented require knowledge of the process by which observations are censored, which may not be readily available in typical machine learning applications. Furthermore, the authors assume that the full population is known, even when no variables are observed for an individual. This excludes certain missing data problems, such as estimating the population size.

The term "m-graph" is used ambiguously, sometimes including the V* variables and sometimes not. The authors should clarify whether the V* variables are being omitted in certain graphs and explain this omission. Moreover, the restriction on R variables as parents of variables in V or U should be justified, given that R variables are treated as real quantities.

Some minor comments include the need to define the term "neighbor" and to clarify the assumption of positivity in the definition of recoverability. Additionally, the statement about the Maximum Likelihood method yielding consistent estimates under the MAR assumption is too vague and should be expanded upon.

Overall, the paper is technically sound, but its broad applicability is not entirely clear. The authors provide a novel contribution to the field by extending the results of Mohan et al. to a more general class of missingness processes. However, further clarification is needed to fully appreciate the significance of these results.
