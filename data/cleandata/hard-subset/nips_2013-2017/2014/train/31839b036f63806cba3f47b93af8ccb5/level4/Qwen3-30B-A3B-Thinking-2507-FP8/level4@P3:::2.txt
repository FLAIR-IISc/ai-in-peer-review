

This paper makes a significant contribution by extending the graphical framework for missing data analysis to address recoverability of causal queries under non-ignorable missingness. Building directly on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), which introduced "missingness graphs" (m-graphs) to model missingness processes, the authors derive novel necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering both probabilistic (e.g., $P(y|x)$, $P(y,x)$) and causal queries (e.g., $P(y|do(x))$) from incomplete data. The key theoretical advance is demonstrating that causal queries may be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not—specifically, when $Y$ and its missingness mechanism $R_y$ are not d-separated (Theorem 4). The paper further applies these results to attrition problems, providing corrected necessary/sufficient conditions that rectify misconceptions in prior work (e.g., Garcia 2013).

**Strengths**  
*Quality*: The theoretical contributions are robust and well-motivated. The authors systematically extend Mohan et al. (2013) by: (1) generalizing the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) to handle missingness mechanisms interacting with one another (e.g., longitudinal data with $R$-variable dependencies); (2) introducing novel non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) to formally identify cases where consistent estimation is impossible; and (3) establishing the critical insight that causal queries can bypass the d-separation requirement for $Y$ and $R_y$ (Theorem 4). All proofs are rigorous and the paper consistently distinguishes between probabilistic and causal recoverability—a nuance often overlooked. The application to attrition (Section 7) is particularly compelling, with Theorems 5–6 resolving ambiguities in Garcia (2013) by showing that causal effects are recoverable *only* when $Y$ and $R_y$ are neither neighbors nor connected by an inducing path (not merely when $X$ is not an ancestor of $R_y$, as previously claimed).

*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-structured and written. Key concepts (e.g., m-graphs, recoverability, admissible sequences) are introduced precisely with clear notation. The examples (e.g., Example 1, Figure 2) effectively illustrate the necessity of minimality in factorization and the limitations of prior methods. The distinction between probabilistic and causal recoverability is consistently reinforced, and the logical flow from theoretical foundations to applications is seamless. Minor suggestions for clarity: the definition of $R_{\text{part}}$ (Section 4) could be slightly expanded to clarify the "collider path" condition for partitioning $R$ variables.

*Originality*: This work represents a substantial conceptual advance over Mohan et al. (2013) and related NIPS contributions (e.g., Mohan & Pearl 2014). The core novelty—showing causal queries can be recoverable when probabilistic ones are not—directly addresses a gap in the literature and is not implied by prior work. The graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) also fill a critical void, as the paper correctly notes that "such criteria have not been treated formally in the literature." The extension to attrition (Section 7) further demonstrates the practical relevance of these theoretical insights.

*Significance*: The results have immediate applicability to high-stakes causal inference problems, particularly in longitudinal studies where attrition is pervasive. By providing explicit graphical conditions for recoverability (e.g., Theorem 6 for simple attrition), the paper enables researchers to diagnose whether causal effects can be estimated from missing data *before* collecting expensive longitudinal samples. This directly advances the state of the art beyond MAR/MCAR assumptions (common in ML literature) and corrects misconceptions in applied work (e.g., Garcia 2013). The framework also opens new pathways for integrating missing data models with causal discovery algorithms.

**Weaknesses**  
The primary limitation is the lack of empirical validation beyond theoretical examples. While the paper’s focus is theoretical, a brief case study (e.g., applying Theorem 6 to a real attrition dataset) would strengthen the significance argument. Additionally, the discussion of "simple attrition" (Section 7) assumes $|V_m|=1$; extending the results to multiple missing variables (e.g., $|V_m|>1$) would broaden applicability, though this may be beyond the paper’s scope.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pros*:  
- Resolves a fundamental gap in causal inference under missingness (causal queries recoverable when probabilistic are not).  
- Provides first formal non-recoverability criteria for missing data.  
- Corrects and extends prior work (Garcia 2013) with precise graphical conditions.  
- Highly relevant to real-world problems (attrition in clinical trials, surveys).  
- Rigorous, well-motivated, and clearly written.  

*Cons*:  
- Limited empirical validation (theoretical only).  
- Scope restricted to $|V_m|=1$ in attrition application (though this is justified as a "special case").  

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a strong, original contribution to causal inference with missing data. It rigorously extends the m-graph framework to answer a critical open question about causal recoverability, with clear implications for applied researchers. The theoretical advances are significant, well-supported, and directly address limitations in prior NIPS work. While empirical validation would be a welcome addition, the paper’s theoretical completeness and clarity fully justify acceptance. I recommend **acceptance**.
