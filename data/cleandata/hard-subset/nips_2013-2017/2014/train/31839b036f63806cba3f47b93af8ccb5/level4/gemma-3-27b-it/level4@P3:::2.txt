This paper addresses the important problem of determining whether a causal or probabilistic query is estimable from data corrupted by missing entries, given a model of the missingness process. The authors extend previous work by Mohan et al. [2013] and present more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (P(y|x), P(y,x)) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))). A key contribution is demonstrating that causal queries can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not, a result illustrated with applications to attrition.

The paper builds upon the existing literature on missing data and causal inference, particularly the work of Pearl and colleagues on graphical models and the do-calculus.  The extension of recoverability conditions to encompass interactions between missingness mechanisms is a notable advancement, addressing a limitation of prior work. The focus on conditions for *non*-recoverability is also valuable, providing practical guidance for researchers. The use of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) provides a clear and formal framework for representing the missing data process.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis, with clear definitions and theorems supporting the claims. The proofs, while lengthy (in the appendix), appear well-structured.
* **Novelty:** The results regarding the recoverability of causal queries even when probabilistic relations are unrecoverable are original and insightful.  Extending the sequential factorization theorem to encompass interacting missingness mechanisms is also a significant contribution.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and summarizes the contributions. The use of examples, such as those related to attrition, helps to illustrate the concepts.
* **Practical Relevance:** The work has direct implications for researchers dealing with missing data in observational and experimental studies, particularly in situations where causal inference is the primary goal.  The identification of non-recoverability criteria is particularly useful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The theoretical framework, while rigorous, can be challenging to grasp without a strong background in causal inference and graphical models. Some definitions (e.g., d*-separation) could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
* **Extensive reliance on notation:** The paper uses a lot of notation, which while precise, can make it difficult to follow the derivations and understand the key ideas quickly. A more gradual introduction of notation might improve readability.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:**  The paper is primarily theoretical. While examples are provided, a more comprehensive empirical evaluation – perhaps through simulations or applications to real-world datasets – would strengthen the claims and demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed methods.
* **Related Work Depth:** While the related work section is reasonably comprehensive, it could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of recent advances in multiple imputation and other missing data techniques, and how this work compares/contrasts.  Specifically, the connection to general missing data frameworks beyond just MNAR isn’t clearly articulated.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of causal inference with missing data.
* The results are novel and address an important practical problem.
* The paper is well-written and clearly organized, demonstrating a strong understanding of the relevant literature.
* The development of non-recoverability criteria is a valuable addition to the toolkit for researchers.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The theoretical complexity may limit the accessibility of the work to a broader audience.
* The lack of empirical evaluation weakens the claims and makes it difficult to assess the practical impact of the research.
* The dense notation could hinder understanding for some readers.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with valuable theoretical contributions to the field of causal inference with missing data. The novelty of the results, combined with the clarity of the presentation, makes it a worthy candidate for acceptance. While the complexity and lack of empirical validation are weaknesses, they are not severe enough to outweigh the strengths. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, I suggest the authors consider:

1.  Providing more intuitive explanations of key concepts, such as d*-separation.
2.  Adding a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms.
3.  Considering including a small-scale simulation study to illustrate the practical benefits of the proposed methods.
4.  Expanding the related work section to include a more detailed comparison with existing missing data techniques.
