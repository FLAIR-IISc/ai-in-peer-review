Summary
This paper studies when probabilistic and causal queries can be consistently estimated from data with missing entries, given an explicit model of the missingness process encoded via missingness graphs (m-graphs). It extends Mohan et al. (2013) in several directions: (i) a generalized sequential factorization that admits missingness indicators (R-nodes) in the ordering, enabling recovery of joint and conditional distributions in settings where missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) algorithms for recovering joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists, using partitions of R variables and their Markov blankets; (iii) new graphical conditions that certify non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions; and (iv) sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects P(y|do(x)) even when the outcome Y is inseparable from its missingness mechanism Ry (via do-calculus, d*-separation, and dormant independence). The paper applies these results to attrition and gives necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of joint distributions and causal effects under simple attrition.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on Mohan et al. (2013) and Pearl and Mohan (2013) by enriching the recoverability toolbox for MNAR settings, and connects to causal identification (Shpitser and Pearl, 2006; 2008; Pearl, 2009). It contrasts with graph-blind MCAR/MAR methods (Rubin, 1976; Little & Rubin, 2002) and complements likelihood, IPW, and imputation approaches by supplying graphical criteria that determine when such procedures can, in principle, succeed. The attrition analysis clarifies and corrects claims in Garcia (2013).

Strengths
- Technical contributions: Clear generalization of ordered factorizations to include R-nodes; constructive recovery formulas beyond the admissible-sequence regime; and crisp non-recoverability criteria for joint/conditional distributions.
- Novel insight: Demonstrates that causal effects can be recoverable even when related probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y|x)) are not, due to leveraging interventions and dormant independences.
- Practical relevance: The attrition results (|Vm| = 1) provide necessary and sufficient graphical conditions and transparent estimands useful for randomized studies with dropout.
- Clarity and organization: The paper is generally well-structured, with motivating examples, clear statements of theorems, and proofs collected in the appendix.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Assumption communication: While m-graphs compactly encode assumptions, the paper could offer more guidance on eliciting and validating these assumptions in practice (e.g., checklists or examples of testable implications).
- Intuition and examples: Theorem 2 (partition-based recovery) would benefit from more intuition and a worked example in the main text (not only the appendix), illustrating how collider paths and Markov blankets drive the construction.
- Scope and limitations: A brief discussion clarifying the gap between necessary and sufficient conditions in the general MNAR case (beyond simple attrition) would help set expectations about completeness of the toolkit.
- Algorithmic aspects: Provide high-level pseudocode or a decision flowchart for practitioners (identify admissible sequences, apply Theorem 2 partitions, check non-recoverability criteria, then attempt causal recovery).
- Empirical illustration: A small simulation demonstrating finite-sample behavior, robustness to mild model misspecification, and sensitivity to positivity violations would increase practical credibility.
- Notation and references: Some notation is dense (e.g., minimality in Definition 2) and could be streamlined with a table of symbols. The reference list has formatting inconsistencies that should be corrected.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The results appear technically sound, with careful use of graphical criteria, do-calculus, and identifiability arguments. Theorems are precisely stated; proofs are indicated (appendix).
- Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized, with helpful examples. Minor improvements in exposition and notation would further aid readability.
- Originality: Introduces new recoverability and non-recoverability criteria, extends sequential factorization to interacting missingness mechanisms, and establishes recoverability of causal effects under inseparability—distinct from prior work.
- Significance: Addresses a pervasive problem (MNAR), providing actionable graphical tests and estimands. The attrition results are likely to see uptake in practice and influence methodological work on missing data and causal inference.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros
- Substantive theoretical advances with clear graphical criteria and constructive estimands.
- Clarifies a subtle and important point: causal effects can be recoverable when associated probabilistic quantities are not.
- Relevant to multiple NeurIPS communities (causal inference, graphical models, learning with missing data).

Cons
- Limited practical guidance on model building and validation; no empirical demonstration.
- Some expository density and notation complexity may hinder accessibility.
- General completeness of the framework (outside special cases like simple attrition) could be discussed more explicitly.

Overall assessment
A focused and well-written paper that meaningfully advances the theory of recoverability under MNAR using m-graphs. The contributions are original and significant, and the insights on causal recoverability despite inseparability are particularly valuable. With minor improvements in exposition, guidance, and references, this paper would make a strong addition to the NeurIPS program.
