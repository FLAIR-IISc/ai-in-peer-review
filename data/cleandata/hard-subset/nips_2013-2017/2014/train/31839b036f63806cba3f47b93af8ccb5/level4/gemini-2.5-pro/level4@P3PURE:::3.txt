This paper addresses the important problem of identifying a probability distribution when faced with data subject to a missingness process. The authors build upon earlier work by considering a more general class of missingness mechanisms, leading to a novel identification formula. A key contribution is showing that causal queries can sometimes be identified even when the full observational joint distribution is not.

The paper is generally well-written and the results are technically sound. However, there are several areas that would benefit from clarification and improvement, and the practical applicability of the proposed methods could be discussed in more detail.

**MAIN COMMENTS:**

1.  The proposed results hinge on knowing the causal graph for the process by which observations are censored. The authors should comment on the feasibility of this assumption in typical machine learning applications, where such detailed knowledge may not be readily available. Furthermore, it would be helpful to include more of the detail associated with Figure 1 in the main body of the paper, perhaps by reducing the figure's size to create space.

2.  In Section 2, it should be explicitly stated that the missing data processes under consideration assume the full population is known. That is, even if an individual has no observed variables (i.e., Vo = ∅ and Rv = 1 for all V in Vm), their existence is still known. This clarification is important as it excludes certain types of missing data problems, such as population size estimation (e.g., estimating the number of animals in a habitat).

3.  The term "m-graph" appears to be used inconsistently. For instance, the m-graph in Figure 1 includes the V* variables, whereas the one in Figure 2 does not. The authors should clarify this distinction. If V* variables are simply omitted for brevity in some figures, this should be explicitly stated.

4.  The m-graphs in this paper do not permit R variables to be parents of variables in V or U. Since the authors treat the R variables as substantive quantities rather than mere indicators, they should justify this structural restriction. Is it fundamental to the theory, or is it made for convenience?

5.  The term "neighbor," used at the bottom of page 4, is not formally defined.

6.  On page 8, in the discussion of Garcia et al.'s work on "rectification," the authors should clarify the underlying assumptions. The definition of recoverability in this paper assumes positivity. Do Garcia et al. make the same assumption? If not, their results might rely on a stronger condition, and this difference should be noted.

7.  The statement on page 8 that the "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption" is too vague. The authors should be more specific about why standard maximum likelihood estimation cannot be applied to the more general missingness processes described in this paper.

**MINOR COMMENTS:**

*   Page 1, line 33: "can bias outcomes" should be revised for clarity, e.g., "can bias the outcomes."
*   Page 2, Figure 1: The label "Qualifications" appears to be a typo.
*   Page 3, line 158 (Example 1): The equation P(Y | X, Z, R_y) = P(X, Z) seems incorrect. The right-hand side should likely be P(Y | X, Z).
*   Page 3, line 168: The independence statement "Y || R | X, Z" should likely be "Y ⊥ R_y | X, Z."
*   Page 3, line 214: In Condition (ii), there is a typo in the set definition: R^N should be R^{(N)}.
*   Page 3, line 215: R^j should be R^{(j)}.
*   Page 4, Corollary 1: The phrase "does not contain a partially observed variables" is grammatically incorrect.
*   Page 6, Footnote 2: A reference should be provided for the variable-level definition of MAR.
