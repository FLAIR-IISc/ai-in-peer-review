- Paper studies identifiability of probabilistic and causal queries under missing data using Missingness Graphs.  
- Extends prior results and provides more general recovery conditions.  
- Shows causal queries can be identifiable even when underlying probabilistic relationships are non-recoverable.  
- Includes illustrative examples, including an application to attrition.  
- Technical quality is generally high and results appear rigorously derived.  
- Theorems 3 and 4 are particularly clear and insightful.  
- Some contributions feel incremental; several sections lack clear broader impact.  
- Section 3 (Theorem 1) is dense, hard to follow, and needs more intuitive explanation or links to related concepts (e.g., Markov blanket).  
- Heavy reliance on a 13-page appendix suggests the work may be better suited to a journal.  
- Main weakness is clarity: dense writing, overloaded notation (especially variable V) and complex subscripts.  
- Notation-heavy equations (e.g., Corollary 1) severely hinder readability.  
- Organization and flow of ideas need refinement for better comprehension.  
- Sections 5 and 6 are the most original and potentially significant contributions.  
- A unified identifiability criterion (instead of many separate theorems/corollaries) would improve practical utility.  
- Theorems relate to inverse probability weighting; connection to Richardson’s graphical work should be emphasized.  
- Strengths: rigorous theoretical development, demonstration of causal identifiability despite non-recoverable probability, clear results in Sections 5–6, practical application to attrition.  
- Weaknesses: poor clarity/readability, particularly Section 3; fragmented presentation; heavy appendix dependence.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, offers novel and impactful results (Sections 5–6), leverages a useful Missingness Graphs framework.  
- Arguments against acceptance: significant clarity issues, some incremental results, and overall length/complexity may be more appropriate for a journal.  
- Overall recommendation: reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit after improving notation, presentation, and unifying the theoretical framework.  
- Specific practical fixes suggested: correct p4 l214 wording, use ↔ instead of <-->, check Garcia (2013) for conflicts, define GX/Vo/Vm before use, and avoid informal wording like “oftentimes.”  
- Relevant references to cite/consider: Richardson (2003) and Richardson (2009).
