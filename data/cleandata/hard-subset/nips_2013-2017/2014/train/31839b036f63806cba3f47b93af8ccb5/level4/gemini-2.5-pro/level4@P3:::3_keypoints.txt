- Addresses recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries, extending the Missingness Graph (m-graph) framework of Mohan et al. (2013).
- Extends prior work to handle more complex missingness processes where missingness mechanisms can influence each other.
- Contribution: presents a refined sequential factorization theorem and a new recovery formula (Theorem 2) for joint distributions when prior methods fail.
- Contribution: introduces the first formal graphical criteria that rule out recoverability, providing necessary conditions for non-recoverability.
- Contribution: shows that causal queries P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when corresponding observational distributions are not, including cases where Y and Ry are not d-separable.
- Applies theoretical results to attrition in longitudinal studies, yielding necessary and sufficient recovery conditions and clarifying prior literature.
- Strength: original and significant — reveals counterintuitive separations between causal and probabilistic recoverability and adds practical graphical tools.
- Strength: technically sound with formal theorems and proofs; non-trivial extensions enabling more realistic missingness scenarios.
- Strength: generally clear and well-organized, with effective examples illustrating theorems.
- Weakness: framework relies on having a correctly specified causal m-graph; more discussion on practical feasibility of specifying such graphs is needed.
- Weakness: scope appears to assume known population membership (fixed dataset rows), excluding problems where existence of units is missing (e.g., capture-recapture).
- Weakness: assumption that R variables cannot be parents of substantive or latent variables needs justification—unclear if technical convenience or fundamental.
- Weakness: notational/definition issues — ambiguity whether V* nodes are part of the m-graph and "neighbor" is not formally defined.
- Weakness: comparison with prior work (Garcia 2013) should clarify whether both use the same positivity assumption, as differences may explain conflicting conclusions.
- Weakness: related-work discussion could better motivate the framework by explaining why standard ML methods fail under the MNAR models handled by m-graphs.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical results, important insight on causal vs probabilistic recoverability, generalizes prior work, and provides first graphical non-recoverability criteria.
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on strong/possibly unrealistic assumptions about the true missingness process and clarity/presentation issues around definitions and model scope.
- Minor issues/typos: multiple small corrections suggested (e.g., wording changes on p.1, Figure 1 caption, corrections in Example 1 and lines 158/168/214, Corollary 1 phrasing, and softer language regarding "rectification" of Garcia).
