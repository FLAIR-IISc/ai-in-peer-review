- Paper studies identifiability in missing-data mechanisms using Mohan et al.'s framework and gives illustrative examples (e.g., causal-identifiability without conditional distributions).  
- Technical results are high-quality and appear correct despite complexity; Theorem 3 is notably clear and interpretable.  
- Some contributions are incremental and do not advance a broader conceptual understanding of the problem.  
- Section 3 feels disconnected from the main thread; Theorem 1 is hard to understand and closely resembles Richardson (2009) Markov blanket results.  
- Sections 5 and 6 are strong: they provide clear solutions and interesting, potentially significant findings for causal queries.  
- Main weakness is clarity: the paper is overly dense for a conference paper, with a 13-page appendix and poor overall flow—more suitable for a journal.  
- Notation is confusing (overuse of capital V for different concepts, illegible subscripts) and presentation of results (e.g., Corollary 1) is poor.  
- The work tackles an important problem and yields useful results but lacks a unified, complete identifiability criterion analogous to missingness-free causal effect solutions.  
- It's impractical for practitioners to consult multiple papers to find specific identifiability results; Theorem 1’s complexity exacerbates this.  
- Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 seem to implement inverse probability weighting and likely rest on conditioning sets being Markov blankets, relating to Richardson (2003, 2009).  
- Specific technical corrections: R^part is a partition of R (phrasing), use \leftrightarrow instead of <-->, and clarify whether Garcia (2013) is incorrect or addressing a different framework.  
- Definition 4 and Theorem 4 use undefined graph notation for edge-severing; Vo and Vm are referenced before being defined.  
- References to relevant prior work: Richardson 2003 (Markov properties for ADMGs) and Richardson 2009 (factorization criterion for ADMGs).  
- Overall: important problem and useful approach, but paper is marred by poor presentation and confusing notation.
