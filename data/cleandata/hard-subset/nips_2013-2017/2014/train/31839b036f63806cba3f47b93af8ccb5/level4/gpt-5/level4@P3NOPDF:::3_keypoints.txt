- Paper contribution: extends prior work on missingness by allowing a broader class of missingness processes and deriving a novel identification formula showing some causal queries can be identified even when the observational joint is not.

- Technical quality and clarity: generally clear, readable, and technically sound with useful figures.

- Strength: provides a clear theoretical generalization of missingness processes and a new identification result.

- Strength: insightful result that causal effects may be identifiable despite non-identifiable observational joint, important for causal inference under missingness.

- Issue (known censoring mechanism): the assumption that the censoring/missingness mechanism is known needs discussion of realism and concrete applied examples.

- Suggestion: include more detail from Figure 1 in the main text so readers can assess plausibility and use of a known missingness mechanism.

- Issue (scope/population): the framework appears to assume known full population membership (units are known even if all variables missing); make this explicit and note it excludes problems like population size estimation (capture–recapture).

- Issue (mgraph ambiguity): inconsistent use of the term “mgraph” and whether V* (proxy/recorded) variables are included; provide a single precise definition and clarify figure omissions.

- Issue (R as parents restriction): R variables are not allowed to be parents of V or U but treated as real quantities—justify whether this is substantive or technical and discuss possible extensions and difficulties if R can be parents.

- Issue (neighbor definition): the term “neighbor” is undefined—specify whether it means adjacent node, parent/child, or another relation.

- Issue (relation to Garcia et al. and positivity): clarify alignment of assumptions with Garcia et al., explicitly state whether their approach assumes positivity or requires stronger/alternative conditions.

- Issue (ML and MAR): the claim that maximum likelihood is consistent under MAR is too vague—clarify why standard ML fails under broader mechanisms, state which assumptions are violated, give a concrete counterexample, and identify any subclasses where ML remains consistent.

- Minor edits: list of phrasing and typographic corrections (e.g., “can bias inferences and outcomes,” clarify “Qualifications” label, correct conditional equality in Example 1, fix notation/superscripts, wording in Corollary 1, add reference in Footnote 2, and maintain consistent terminology for R, V*, V, U and explicit conditioning on R components).

- Suggestion (worked example): include a small worked example showing joint distribution non-identifiable but a causal query identifiable using the missingness mechanism.

- Suggestion (robustness): briefly discuss sensitivity of identification results to misspecification of the missingness graph and suggest practical diagnostics or handling of partial knowledge.

- Suggestion (estimation sketch): sketch how the identification formula could translate into estimation procedures (e.g., weighting, augmentation, g-formula analogs) and note computational implications.

- Overall assessment: an interesting, technically sound extension with clear theoretical contributions; practicality limited by the known censoring mechanism assumption, and addressing clarifications would improve readability and impact.
