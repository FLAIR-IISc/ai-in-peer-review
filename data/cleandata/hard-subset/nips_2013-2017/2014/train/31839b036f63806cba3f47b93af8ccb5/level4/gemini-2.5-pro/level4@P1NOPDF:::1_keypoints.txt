- Addresses identifiability with various missing-data mechanisms, building on Mohan et al., and gives examples showing causal distributions can be identifiable even when some conditionals are unavailable.
- Technical quality is high; results are involved and appear correct, with Theorem 3 being clear and interpretable.
- Some results are incremental improvements on prior work and don’t clearly advance broader understanding.
- Section 3 feels orthogonal to the rest; Theorem 1 is hard to understand and seems related to Richardson (2009) Markov blanket results.
- Paper improves substantially in later sections: Section 5 gives clear solutions and Section 6 provides interesting causal-query results.
- Main weakness is clarity: overly dense, tries to cover too much for a conference paper, and would suit a longer journal article.
- Notation is confusing and overloaded (capital V used for multiple objects; V_i vs V_o inconsistency; illegible subscripts), with Corollary 1 as a notable example.
- Lacks a unifying, general identifiability criterion that would make the work more useful for practitioners.
- Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 resemble inverse-probability weighting; likely tied to conditioning sets acting as Markov blankets in mixed graphs, making Richardson (2003, 2009) relevant.
- p4 l214: R^part should be defined as a partition and the condition should state R_x and R_y belong to distinct partition elements.
- p4 l213: use \leftrightarrow instead of <--> for bidirected edges.
- Clarify whether Garcia (2013) is incorrect or simply uses a different framework.
- Define the notation for graphs obtained by severing edges used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4.
- Avoid using V_o and V_m before they are defined; their early use seems unnecessary.
- Tone is occasionally too casual (e.g., “oftentimes”).
- Overall: useful and important contributions but undermined by poor presentation and confusing notation.
