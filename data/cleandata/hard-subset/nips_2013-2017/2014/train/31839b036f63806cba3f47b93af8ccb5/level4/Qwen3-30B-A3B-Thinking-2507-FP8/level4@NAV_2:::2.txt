

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on missingness graphs to establish novel necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal queries (e.g., $P(y|do(x))$) and probabilistic queries from missing data. It generalizes previous results by deriving graphical conditions under which causal effects remain recoverable even when the target variable $Y$ and its missingness mechanism $R_y$ are not d-separable—a scenario where probabilistic queries like $P(y,x)$ or $P(y|x)$ become non-recoverable. The authors introduce a refined sequential factorization theorem applicable to missingness mechanisms that interact with one another, present algorithms for recovery in the absence of admissible sequences, and establish non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions. The paper further applies these results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies, providing necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects under simple attrition.

The paper excels in technical quality: all theoretical claims are rigorously proven (with complete proofs in the appendix), and the results logically extend Mohan et al. (2013) while resolving a critical gap in causal inference under missing data. Clarity is exceptional—the paper is well-structured, with precise definitions, illustrative examples (e.g., Example 3 demonstrating recoverability of $P(y|do(z))$ despite inseparability), and explicit connections to prior work. Originality is strong: the insight that causal queries may bypass the separability requirement for probabilistic recovery is novel and addresses a significant limitation in existing frameworks (e.g., correcting Garcia, 2013). Significance is high, as the results directly advance causal inference practice in domains with missing data (e.g., clinical trials with attrition), offering actionable graphical criteria for practitioners.

Pro: Technical soundness, clarity, originality of causal recovery insights, and practical relevance to attrition problems.  
Con: None identified; the paper thoroughly addresses scope and limitations.  

This work represents a meaningful contribution to NIPS' focus on causal inference under missing data, with implications for both theory and applications. It should be accepted.
