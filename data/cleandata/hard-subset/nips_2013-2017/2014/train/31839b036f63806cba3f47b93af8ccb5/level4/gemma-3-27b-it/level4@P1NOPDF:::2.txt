This paper presents novel necessary and sufficient conditions for determining whether a causal query can be identified from observational data with missing values. It extends prior work that explicitly models the missing data mechanism within a causal diagram. The paper is clearly written and, while building upon existing contributions in this area, the additional insights it provides represent, in this reviewer’s opinion, a valuable contribution to the state of the art in analyzing causal mechanisms under missing data. The work is focused and well-presented.

The main strength of the paper lies in its rigorous derivation of these conditions. The authors convincingly demonstrate how incorporating the missing data mechanism into the causal graph allows for a more complete and accurate assessment of identifiability. I particularly appreciated the clear explanation of how these conditions relate to, and improve upon, previously known results. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of these findings. While the theoretical results are strong, it's not immediately obvious how easily these conditions can be checked in real-world scenarios, especially with complex causal diagrams. Providing some examples or guidance on applying these conditions to practical problems would significantly strengthen the paper. For instance, could automated tools be developed to assist in this process? 

Furthermore, the authors assume knowledge of the missing data mechanism. Although explicitly modeling this mechanism is a step forward, this assumption may be unrealistic in many applications. A discussion of the robustness of the proposed conditions to misspecification of the missing data mechanism – or potential approaches to handle uncertainty in this mechanism – would be valuable. Even acknowledging this limitation and discussing its potential impact would be beneficial.

Minor points: The notation is generally clear, but a glossary of symbols might be helpful for readers less familiar with causal inference terminology. Also, while the paper is well-written, some sentences are quite long and could be broken down for improved readability.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution with the potential to significantly impact the field of causal inference with missing data. Addressing the points above, particularly regarding practical applicability and the sensitivity to the missing data mechanism assumption, would further enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance.
