The paper addresses the challenging problem of identifiability in the presence of various missing data mechanisms, building upon the framework introduced by Mohan and others. It improves upon existing results and provides insightful examples, notably demonstrating identifiability of causal distributions even when full conditional distributions are unavailable.

**Quality**

The technical quality appears high; the results are intricate but seem correct. Theorems like Theorem 3 are clearly stated and interpretable. However, some results represent incremental improvements and, to my understanding, do not contribute to a broader unifying perspective. 

Section 3 feels somewhat disconnected from the rest of the paper, and its central result (Theorem 1) is difficult to grasp. It bears a strong resemblance to Markov blanket results found in Richardson (2009). The paper's quality improves in later sections, with Section 5 offering clear and valuable insights, and Section 6 presenting interesting results concerning causal queries.

---
**Clarity**

Clarity is this paper’s primary weakness. The material presented feels overly extensive for a conference paper—its importance is not always immediately apparent—as evidenced by the dense text and a 13-page technical appendix. The article lacks a smooth flow, making it difficult to follow; it would likely be better suited as a longer, more thoroughly developed journal publication.

While formulating notation for numerous quantities is inherently difficult, the current notation could be significantly improved. The repeated use of the capital ‘V’ to denote different objects places a heavy burden on the reader, and in some instances, the subscripts become illegible. The inconsistency of ‘Vi’ representing a vertex and ‘Vo’ representing a set of vertices is particularly confusing. The set equation in Corollary 1 exemplifies particularly poor presentation.

---
**Originality and Significance**

Sections 5 and 6 are well-articulated, interesting, and potentially significant. The paper tackles an important problem and delivers results that appear genuinely novel and useful. 

However, a more holistic approach to understanding the general case would be beneficial. Ideally, the authors could strive for a comprehensive identifiability criterion within this framework, mirroring the success achieved in determining causal effects without missingness. It is challenging to envision a statistician navigating a series of papers to identify the precise result pertaining to the (non-)identifiability of a specific causal effect, particularly if that result is as complex as Theorem 1.

Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to be applications of inverse probability weighting. The reason for their validity likely stems from the conditioning sets functioning as Markov blankets for R(i) within the mixed graphs employed by the authors. Relevant work by Richardson (2003) and (2009) should be considered.
