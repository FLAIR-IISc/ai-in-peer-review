

Quality  
The paper addresses identifiability in missing data mechanisms using Mohan et al.'s framework, offering improved results and illustrative examples (e.g., causal distribution identifiability without available conditional distributions). Technical quality is high; the results are complex but appear correct. Theorem 3 is clear and interpretable. However, some contributions are incremental and do not advance a broader understanding of the underlying problem. Section 3 appears orthogonal to the rest of the paper, with Theorem 1 being particularly difficult to comprehend. It closely relates to Richardson (2009)'s Markov blanket results. Quality improves in Sections 5 (clear results) and 6 (interesting causal query analysis).  

Clarity  
This is the paper's primary weakness. The content is overly dense for a conference paper, evidenced by the 13-page technical appendix and dense prose, suggesting it would better suit a journal format. Notation requires significant improvement: the repeated use of capital 'V' for diverse objects (e.g., V_i as a vertex versus V_o as a set) creates confusion, and subscripts become illegible. The set equation in Corollary 1 exemplifies poor presentation.  

Originality and Significance  
Sections 5 and 6 present clear, interesting, and potentially significant results. The paper tackles an important problem with genuinely useful contributions. However, it would benefit from stepping back to develop a comprehensive identifiability criterion for the general case (as achieved in non-missingness causal effect settings). It would be difficult for a statistician to navigate multiple papers to locate a specific result on (non-)identifiability, especially given the complexity of Theorem 1. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 resemble inverse probability weighting; their validity likely stems from conditioning sets being Markov blankets for R(i) in the mixed graphs used, suggesting relevance to Richardson (2003, 2009).
