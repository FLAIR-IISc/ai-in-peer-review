This paper addresses the problem of deciding whether a causal or probabilistic query is estimable from data corrupted by missing entries, given a model of the missingness process. The authors extend the results of Mohan et al. (2013) by presenting more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries and causal queries. They also apply their results to problems of attrition and characterize the recovery of causal effects from data corrupted by attrition.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem it addresses is important. The technical quality of the paper is high, and the results appear to be correct. However, the paper's main weakness is its clarity. The text is dense, and the notation could be improved. The use of the same capital 'V' to denote different objects makes it hard to follow, and the subscripts become illegible in places.

The paper's organization could also be improved. Section 3 seems orthogonal to the rest of the paper, and its main result (Theorem 1) is quite hard to comprehend. The quality improves later on, with Section 5 giving some nice clear answers, and Section 6 providing interesting results about causal queries.

The paper's originality and significance are clear. Sections 5 and 6 are interesting and potentially significant, and the paper deals with an important problem. However, it would be better to step back and work out what is going on in the general case, and try to come up with a complete criterion for identifiability in this framework.

Some minor comments:

* p4, l214: R^part is a partition of R, not "a set containing partitions". The condition on line 214 should then be that Rx and Ry "belong to distinct elements of the partition".
* Language is sometimes a bit casual: e.g. 'oftentimes'
* p4 l213: Use \leftrightarrow rather than <-->
* Is Garcia (2013) actually wrong, or just a different framework?
* A notation for graphs constructed by severing edges is used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4, but not defined anywhere.
* Vo and Vm are used in the sentence before they are defined (apparently unnecessarily).

Overall, the paper is well-motivated and technically sound, but its clarity and presentation could be improved.
