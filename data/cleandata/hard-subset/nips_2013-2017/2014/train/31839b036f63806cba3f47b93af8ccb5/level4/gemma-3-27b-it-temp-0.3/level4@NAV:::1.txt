The paper addresses the problem of identifiability under various missing data mechanisms, building upon the framework of Mohan et al. It improves upon existing results and provides insightful examples, notably demonstrating identifiability of causal distributions even without complete conditional distributions.

**Quality:**

The technical quality is generally high; the results are complex but appear correct. Theorems like Theorem 3 are clearly stated and interpretable. However, some results represent incremental advances and their contribution to a broader understanding remains unclear. Section 3 feels somewhat disconnected from the main body of the work, and its central result (Theorem 1) is difficult to follow, appearing related to Markov blanket results found in Richardson (2009). The quality improves in later sections, with Section 5 offering clear and valuable answers, and Section 6 presenting interesting findings regarding causal queries.

**Clarity:**

Clarity is the paper’s primary weakness. The scope of the material, potentially exceeding what is appropriate for a conference paper, contributes to a dense and challenging read, further compounded by a 13-page technical appendix. The paper lacks a clear narrative flow, hindering comprehension – it might be better suited as a more expansive journal article. While developing suitable notation for the numerous quantities is challenging, improvements are possible. The repeated use of ‘V’ to represent different objects, and the resulting illegibility of subscripts, creates confusion. Specifically, the distinction between *Vi* as a vertex and *Vo* as a set of vertices is problematic. The set equation in Corollary 1 exemplifies particularly poor presentation.

**Originality and Significance:**

Sections 5 and 6 are particularly strong, offering clear and potentially significant contributions. The paper tackles an important problem and contains genuinely interesting and useful results. However, a more holistic approach to identifying a general criterion for identifiability within this framework – similar to the work on causal effect identification without missingness – would be beneficial. It would be challenging for a practitioner to navigate a series of papers to locate the specific result concerning the identifiability of a causal effect of interest, especially given the complexity of results like Theorem 1. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to leverage inverse probability weighting, likely due to the conditioning sets acting as Markov blankets for *R(i)* within the author’s mixed graphs; Richardson (2003) and (2009) may offer relevant context.

**Other Comments:**

*   p4, l214: *R^part* is a partition of *R*, not “a set containing partitions.” The condition on line 214 should state that *Rx* and *Ry* “belong to distinct elements of the partition.”
*   The language is occasionally informal (e.g., ‘oftentimes’).
*   p4 l213: Use ↔ rather than <-->
*   Clarify whether Garcia (2013) is demonstrably incorrect or operates within a different framework.
*   The notation for graphs constructed by severing edges, used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4, is not defined.
*   *Vo* and *Vm* are used before their definitions (seemingly unnecessarily).

Overall, this is an important problem addressed with a useful approach, but the presentation and notation significantly detract from its impact.
