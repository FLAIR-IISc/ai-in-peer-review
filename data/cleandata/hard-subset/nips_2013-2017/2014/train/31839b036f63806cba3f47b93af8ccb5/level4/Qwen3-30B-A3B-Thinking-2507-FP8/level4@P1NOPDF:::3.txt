

This paper presents an interesting contribution to the problem of recovering distributions from data with missingness mechanisms. It extends earlier work by Mohan et al. by considering a more general class of missingness processes, leading to a novel identification formula. The authors further demonstrate that causal queries can be identified even when the full observational joint distribution is not identifiable, a significant technical advance. The paper is generally well-written, though several minor issues require clarification.

**MAIN COMMENTS:**

(1) The results rely on knowledge of the censoring mechanism. How commonly is this available in practical machine learning applications? The main text should include more detail about Figure 1 (e.g., by reducing its size).

(2) Section 2 assumes the full population is known: even when no variables are observed for an individual (e.g., \(V_o = \emptyset\) and \(R_v = 1\) for all \(v \in V_m\)), the individual’s existence is still known. This excludes problems like population estimation (e.g., estimating animal counts), which should be acknowledged.

(3) The term "mgraph" is used ambiguously. It includes \(V^*\) variables in Figure 1 but omits them in Figure 2. If \(V^*\) is consistently excluded in later figures, this convention must be explicitly stated.

(4) The mgraphs prohibit \(R\) variables from being parents of \(V\) or \(U\) variables. Given the authors treat \(R\) as real-valued quantities (not just indicators), they should justify this restriction or clarify its purpose.

(5) The term "neighbor" (bottom of p.4) is undefined.

(6) On p.8, the reference to "rectification" by Garcia et al. requires context. The authors’ definition of recoverability assumes positivity. It is unclear whether Garcia et al. make the same assumption; if not, their approach may require stronger conditions.

(7) The claim that "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption" (p.8) is overly vague. The text should specify why ML cannot be applied to the general missingness processes described here.

- p.2, Figure 1: Add label "Qualification: " to the caption

- p.3, line 168: "Since \(Y \perp\!\!\!\perp R \mid X,Z\)" → "Since \(Y \perp\!\!\!\perp R_y \mid X,Z\)"
- p.3, line 214: \(R^{\text{part}} = \{R^{(1)}, R^{(2)}, \ldots, R^N\}\) → \(R^{\text{part}} = \{R^{(1)}, R^{(2)}, \ldots, R^{(N)}\}\)
- p.3, line 215: "\(R^j\) respectively" → "\(R^{(j)}\) respectively"
- p.4, Corollary 1: "does not contain a partially observed variables" → "does not include partially observed variables"
- p.6, Footnote 2: The reference to the "variable-level definition" is unclear; provide a citation. The paper is technically sound but its applicability to broader problems remains unclear.
