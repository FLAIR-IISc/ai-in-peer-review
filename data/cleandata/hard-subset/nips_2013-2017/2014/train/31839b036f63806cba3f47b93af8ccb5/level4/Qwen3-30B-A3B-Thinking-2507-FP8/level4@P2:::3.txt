

This paper makes a significant contribution to the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data using graphical models. It extends Mohan et al.'s (2013) work on missingness graphs (m-graphs) by providing more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (P(y|x) and P(y,x)) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))). The key theoretical advance is demonstrating that causal queries may be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations (e.g., P(y,x)) are not recoverable—a finding with important implications for causal inference under missing data. The authors establish new graphical criteria for recoverability, provide algorithms for recovering joint distributions when sequential factorization fails, and apply their results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.

**Quality**. The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical development. The authors successfully extend Mohan et al.'s framework to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact (e.g., R variables influencing each other), which was not covered in prior work. The proofs are thorough, and the examples (e.g., Example 3) effectively demonstrate the theoretical insights. However, the paper assumes knowledge of the missingness process, which may not be realistic in many machine learning applications. This limitation should be more thoroughly discussed, as the paper's applicability hinges on this assumption.

**Clarity**. The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, but some ambiguities need clarification. The term "m-graph" is used inconsistently—sometimes including V* variables (Figure 1) and sometimes not (Figures 2-3). The definition of "neighbor" (p.4) is missing, creating confusion. Several minor typographical errors exist (e.g., P(Y|X,Z,Ry) = P(X,Z) on p.3 should be P(Y|X,Z)), which undermine the paper's precision. The authors should clarify whether V* variables are omitted in some figures or if they're part of the m-graph definition.

**Originality**. The work is highly original in extending the m-graph framework to handle more complex missingness processes. The key insight—that causal queries may be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations are not—represents a substantial theoretical advance. This differs significantly from Mohan et al. (2013) and resolves an important gap in the literature. The paper also corrects previous misconceptions about attrition (e.g., Garcia 2013), demonstrating that non-recoverability can occur due to collider paths rather than just ancestry relationships.

**Significance**. The results have substantial significance for causal inference in longitudinal studies where attrition is common. By providing necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for recoverability, the paper offers practical guidance for researchers dealing with missing data. However, the paper could strengthen its significance by addressing whether the assumption of knowing the missingness process is reasonable in real-world applications, particularly in machine learning contexts where missingness mechanisms are often unknown.

**Strengths**: 
- Rigorous theoretical development extending Mohan et al.'s framework
- Novel insight that causal queries may be recoverable when probabilistic queries are not
- Comprehensive treatment of recoverability conditions for joint, conditional, and causal queries
- Clear application to attrition problems with necessary and sufficient conditions

**Weaknesses**:
- Ambiguous use of "m-graph" (including/excluding V* variables)
- Missing definition of "neighbor" (p.4)
- Insufficient discussion of the assumption that missingness process is known (critical for real-world applicability)
- Minor typographical errors affecting precision
- Overgeneralization of ML methods (p.8: "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption" is too vague)

**Pro/Con Acceptance**:
- PRO: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to a well-known problem, with clear implications for causal inference under missing data. The results are technically sound and extend prior work in meaningful ways.
- CON: The assumption of knowing the missingness process limits practical applicability without further justification. Some definitions are unclear, and the paper could better address real-world constraints.

**Recommendation**: Accept. This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances the field of missing data analysis. The paper would benefit from clarifying the m-graph definition, addressing the missingness process assumption, and fixing minor errors, but these are easily rectifiable in the camera-ready version. The insights about causal query recoverability represent a substantial advance that will interest researchers working at the intersection of causal inference and missing data. The paper's framework provides valuable tools for practitioners to assess recoverability in their own analyses, particularly in longitudinal studies with attrition.
