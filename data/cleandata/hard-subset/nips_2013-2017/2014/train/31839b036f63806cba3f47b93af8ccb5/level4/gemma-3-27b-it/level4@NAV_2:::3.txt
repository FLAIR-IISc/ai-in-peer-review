This is an interesting paper that addresses the problem of determining when a distribution can be recovered given knowledge of the data-generating process and the process leading to missing data. The authors extend previous work by Mohan et al., considering a more general class of missingness processes and deriving novel identification formulas. A key contribution is demonstrating that causal queries can be identified even when the underlying (observational) joint distribution is not. The paper is generally well-written, though there are some areas for improvement in clarity and detail, as outlined below.

The results presented rely on knowledge of the process by which observations are censored. It would be beneficial to discuss the practicality of this assumption in typical machine learning applications. Including more detail regarding Figure 1 within the main paper—perhaps by reducing its size—would also improve accessibility.

In Section 2, it’s important to note that the missing data processes considered always assume a known full population. Even in scenarios where *V<sub>o</sub>* is empty and *R<sub>v</sub>* = 1 for all *V* in *V<sub>m</sub>* (meaning no variables are observed for a given individual), the framework still assumes knowledge of that individual’s existence. This excludes missing data problems such as estimating population size.

The term “m-graph” appears to be used ambiguously. Sometimes the graph includes the *V*<sup>*</sup> variables (Figure 1), while at other times it does not (Figure 2). If the *V*<sup>*</sup> variables are being omitted for illustrative purposes, this should be explicitly stated.

This paper restricts m-graphs to exclude *R* variables as parents of variables in *V* or *U*. Given that the authors treat *R* variables as real quantities rather than simple indicators, a justification for this restriction—or a discussion of its potential limitations—would be valuable.

The term “neighbor,” appearing near the bottom of page 4, is not defined. Providing a formal definition would enhance clarity.

On page 8, the first paragraph references a “rectification” of Garcia’s observation. It's important to clarify whether Garcia et al. also assume positivity, as recoverability as defined in this paper hinges on that assumption. If not, their required condition may be stronger.

On page 8, the second paragraph states that “Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption.” This statement is too vague. A more detailed explanation is needed to explain why maximum likelihood might not be applicable to the missingness processes described in this paper.

Minor comments:
*   P.1, line 33: “can bias outcomes” – consider rephrasing for stronger phrasing.
*   P.2, Figure 1: Provide additional context or clarification for the qualifications.
*   P.3, Example 1, line 158: *P(Y |X,Z,R<sub>y</sub>) = P(X,Z)* should be *P(Y|X,Z)*.
*   P.3, line 168: *Y || R | X,Z* should be *Y || R<sub>y</sub> | X,Z*.
*   P.3, line 214: *R<sup>part</sup> ={R<sup>(1)</sup>,R<sup>(2)</sup>, \ldots , R<sup>N</sup>}* should be *R<sup>part</sup> ={R<sup>(1)</sup>,R<sup>(2)</sup>, \ldots , R<sup>(N)</sup>}*.
*   P.3, line 215: *R<sup>j</sup> respectively* should be *R<sup>(j)</sup> respectively*.
*   P.4, Corollary 1: “…does not contain a partially observed variables…” – correct grammar.

Overall, this is a technically sound and interesting paper building upon earlier work. While the broad applicability isn’t immediately clear, the contributions are valuable, and the presentation is generally strong.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper extends existing theoretical frameworks for handling missing data, providing more general conditions for recoverability.
*   The demonstration that causal queries can be identified even when the underlying joint distribution is not is a significant result.
*   The work is technically rigorous and well-motivated.
*   The authors clearly articulate the assumptions and limitations of their approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The assumption of known missingness processes may limit the practical applicability of the results.
*   The notation and some explanations could be clearer.
*   The connection to broader machine learning practices could be more explicitly discussed.
