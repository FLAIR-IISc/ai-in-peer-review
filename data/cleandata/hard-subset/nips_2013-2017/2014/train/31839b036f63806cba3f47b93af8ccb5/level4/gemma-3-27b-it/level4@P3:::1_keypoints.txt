- Core contribution: extends Missingness Graphs to give more general recoverability conditions, showing causal queries can be identified even when probabilistic relationships are not.  
- Builds on prior causal inference and missing-data work, refining theorems and introducing new graphical criteria for non-recoverability.  
- Technical quality: results are intricate and proofs appear rigorous; Theorems 3 and 4 are particularly clear and insightful.  
- Some sections feel incremental and disconnected from the main narrative, notably Section 3 and Theorem 1.  
- Clarity is a major weakness: dense prose, a 13-page appendix, and overloaded/inconsistent notation hinder readability.  
- Notation issues: repeated use of “V” for different sets and hard-to-parse subscripts make equations cumbersome (e.g., Corollary 1).  
- Organization could be improved; a restructure or narrower focus would make the key contributions easier to follow.  
- Originality/significance: Sections 5–6 are the most original and show that causal queries can sometimes be recovered even when probabilities cannot.  
- The paper lacks a unified, comprehensive criterion for identifiability, limiting its overall impact.  
- Potential connections to inverse probability weighting and Richardson’s work on mixed graphs are underexplored and could strengthen the paper.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important problem, presents original results in Sections 5–6, and demonstrates strong technical competence.  
- Arguments against acceptance: severe clarity/notation problems, some detached material, absence of a unified theory, and practical complexity for users.  
- Specific issues/typos: fix wording on p4 l214 about partitions; use ↔ instead of <-->; clarify the Garcia (2013) claim; formally define the graph-severing notation; introduce Vo and Vm before use.  
- Overall assessment: valuable and potentially important contribution but hampered by clarity and framing—recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit after major improvements.
