Summary
This paper studies when probabilistic queries (joint and conditional distributions) and causal effects are recoverable from data with missing entries, given an explicit graphical model of the missingness process (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors: (i) extend the sequential factorization approach by allowing missingness indicators (R variables) to appear in admissible factorizations (Theorem 1); (ii) provide a general recoverability result for joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists, via a partitioning of R variables and Markov blankets defined through collider paths (Theorem 2), with a corollary for models without latent common causes between R and substantive variables; (iii) introduce the first general non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2); (iv) show that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when related probabilistic quantities are not, including cases where Y is inseparable from its missingness mechanism Ry (Theorem 4), leveraging dormant independences and d*-separation (Shpitser & Pearl, 2008); and (v) apply these results to attrition, giving necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering joint distributions and causal effects when a single outcome is partially observed (Theorems 5 and 6), and correcting claims in prior work (e.g., Garcia, 2013).

Relation to prior work
The paper is squarely in the line of research that uses explicit causal/missingness graphs to reason about recoverability under MNAR, extending Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013) and Pearl & Mohan (2013). It connects to identifiability results via do-calculus (Pearl, 2009; Shpitser & Pearl, 2006), clarifies when MAR/MCAR allow model-blind recovery, and contrasts with mainstream strategies such as ML/EM under MAR, IPW, and multiple imputation (Rubin, 1976; Little & Rubin, 2002; Lauritzen, 1995; Robins et al., 1994). The non-recoverability criteria and the recoverability of causal effects despite inseparability from missingness mechanisms appear novel.

Quality
The technical contributions are clearly stated, with proofs deferred to the appendix. The extension of ordered factorization to include R variables is carefully motivated (minimality condition is important and well-illustrated). Theorem 2 generalizes earlier recoverability results to semi-Markovian models using collider paths and R partitions; the required constructs (Rpart and Mb) are well-defined. The non-recoverability criteria are valuable and appear to fill a gap in the literature. The causal recoverability results that exploit dormant independences are plausible and supported by do-calculus arguments. The attrition results (|Vm|=1) provide crisp necessary and sufficient conditions.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally well-organized and readable. The notation is heavy but mostly consistent; examples (e.g., Example 1 and the attrition cases) help. Some definitions could benefit from more intuition (e.g., why minimality in ordered factorization is essential; operational guidance for constructing R partitions and Markov blankets). Several references are improperly formatted or incomplete and should be fixed. Figures are central; ensure they are legible and fully referenced in text.

Originality
The paper contributes: (1) an extended sequential factorization that admits R variables; (2) a general recoverability theorem beyond admissible sequences; (3) the first general non-recoverability criteria for P(V) and P(Y|X); and (4) new causal recoverability results when Y and Ry are inseparable, with necessary and sufficient conditions under simple attrition. These go beyond Mohan et al. (2013) and clarify the landscape of MNAR recoverability.

Significance
Determining recoverability before estimation is crucial in practice. The provided criteria can guide analysts in complex MNAR settings, especially longitudinal studies with interacting missingness mechanisms. The non-recoverability results can prevent misuse of model-blind methods, and the causal results broaden the set of scenarios where causal effects can be estimated despite MNAR complications.

Strengths
- Clear extension of prior NIPS work with substantive new theory.
- Practical, graphical conditions for both recoverability and non-recoverability.
- Insightful demonstration that causal effects can be recoverable when probabilistic quantities are not.
- Clean, necessary and sufficient results for attrition with |Vm|=1.
- Good use of examples to motivate conditions (e.g., minimality in factorization).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Practical guidance: Provide an explicit algorithmic checklist for applying Theorems 1–3 (e.g., how to search for admissible sequences including R, how to construct R partitions and Mb(R(i)) efficiently, and computational complexity).
- Accessibility: Add intuitive explanations/diagrams for collider paths, inducing paths, d*-separation, and dormant independence; more step-by-step worked examples beyond toy cases would help practitioners.
- Scope and assumptions: Clarify the assumption that R variables are not parents of V∪U and discuss implications/limitations; comment on continuous variables and required positivity conditions beyond “strictly positive over complete cases.”
- Empirical illustration: Although the contribution is theoretical, a small simulation study demonstrating finite-sample behavior and robustness to mild m-graph misspecification would increase impact.
- Attrition section: Include a simple end-to-end example showing how the conditions are checked and how the estimand in Eq. (5) is computed from observed data.
- Presentation: Clean up reference formatting and minor typographical issues; ensure figures are self-contained and precisely match theorems cited.

Questions for the authors
- Can the algorithms for identifying admissible sequences and R partitions be automated reliably for moderately sized graphs? Any complexity bounds?
- How sensitive are the causal recoverability results (Theorem 4) to small violations in the assumed m-graph (e.g., an unmodeled weak edge)?
- Do the non-recoverability criteria in Theorem 3 remain necessary when positivity is violated or approximate (common in practice)?

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, novel theory addressing an important and underexplored aspect of MNAR.
- Complements existing identifiability literature with practical criteria for recoverability and non-recoverability.
- Clarifies misconceptions in attrition analysis and extends the toolkit for causal inference under missingness.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical or algorithmic demonstration may hinder immediate applicability.
- Some concepts (d*-separation, inducing paths) may be challenging for non-experts without additional exposition.

Overall assessment
A solid and timely theoretical contribution to causal inference with missing data. With minor revisions to improve accessibility and presentation, this paper would make a valuable addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
