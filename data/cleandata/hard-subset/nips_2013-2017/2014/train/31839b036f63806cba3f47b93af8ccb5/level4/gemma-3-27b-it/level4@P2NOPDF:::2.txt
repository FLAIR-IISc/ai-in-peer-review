This paper presents novel and comprehensive criteria for determining identifiability of causal effects when faced with missing data. The work builds upon and extends previous approaches that incorporate the missing data mechanism directly into the causal diagram, moving beyond scenarios where the missingness is assumed to be ignorable. Specifically, the authors establish both necessary *and* sufficient conditions for inferring causal queries in the presence of missing observations—a significant advancement over existing literature. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly articulates a complex theoretical contribution. While it logically follows from prior work in this area—specifically, research leveraging causal diagrams to model missing data—the added insights offered represent a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference. The focus is sharp and the presentation is concise, allowing for a clear understanding of the core theoretical results. 

The strength of this work lies in its completeness. Prior approaches often provided necessary, but not sufficient, conditions, leaving open the possibility that satisfying the conditions would *not* guarantee identifiability. This paper closes that gap, providing a definitive framework for assessing whether a causal query can be reliably answered given the available data and assumptions about the missingness process. This is particularly useful for practitioners attempting to apply causal inference techniques in real-world settings where missing data is almost ubiquitous. 

Potential weaknesses are minor. While the theoretical results are strong, the paper would benefit from a more extensive discussion of their practical implications. Illustrative examples – perhaps including scenarios where the proposed conditions *fail* and demonstrating why – could further solidify understanding. Furthermore, while the writing is clear, the mathematical notation, though standard in the field, could pose a barrier to readers less familiar with causal inference formalism. A brief glossary or more intuitive explanation of key symbols would improve accessibility.

Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in causal inference. The authors provide a theoretically sound and practically relevant contribution to the literature. The clarity of the presentation further enhances its value. I recommend acceptance.
