This is an interesting research paper. It addresses the problem of how to recover a distribution that has been generated, and the process that leads to missing data, from the knowledge of the process.

This paper considers a more general class of missingness processes than the work previously done by Mohan et al.

This leads to a new identification formula.

Furthermore, the authors consider the identification of causal questions and show that these can be identified even when the (observational) joint distribution is not identified.

This paper is generally well-written, although it has some shortcomings and areas for improvement that I describe below.

Key Comments:

(1) The results here require knowledge of the process by which the observations are censored. Is this knowledge available in specific machine learning applications? It would be helpful to include more information about Figure 1 in the main paper (this can be done by reducing the image).
(2) Section 2. It should be mentioned here that missing data processes always assume that the entire population is known:

Even if Vo = emptyset, and Rv = 1 in V_m for all V (therefore no variables are seen for this individual), it is still assumed that we know that this individual exists. (This does not include missing data problems such as estimating the number of animals in the population.)
(3) The term "igraph" seems to be used ambiguously. In particular, sometimes the graph includes the V* variable (Figure 1), and sometimes it does not (Figure 2). If the V* variable is being omitted in these graphs, this should be explained.
(4) This paper does not allow the mgraph to have the R variable as the parent of the V or U variables.

Given that the authors consider the R variable not only as an indicator but as actual quantities, they should explain why this restriction is justified (or whether it is mainly for convenience).
(5) The term 'neighbor', which appears at the bottom of page 4, is not defined.
(6) Page 8, first paragraph. Garcia's "correction" of the observation.

The recovery defined by the authors assumes positivity. Do Garcia et al. also assume positivity?

If not, they may need a stronger condition.
(7) Page 8, second paragraph. "Maximum likelihood method is known to give consistent estimates under the MAR assumption".

This is very vague. Why cannot maximum likelihood be applied to any of the missingness processes described here?
