

This paper addresses the important problem of determining when probabilistic and causal queries can be recovered from missing data using graphical models. The authors extend Mohan et al.'s (2013) work on "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) to provide more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (P(y|x) and P(y,x)) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))). A key contribution is showing that causal queries may be recoverable even when the factors in their identifying estimands are not recoverable, specifically when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable. The paper applies these results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.

**Quality**  
The paper presents technically sound results with rigorous proofs and clear theorems. The authors successfully extend Mohan et al.'s work with novel conditions for recoverability, particularly in Theorem 6 which provides necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects under simple attrition. The theoretical framework is well-developed, with careful attention to the nuances of missingness mechanisms. The paper demonstrates thorough understanding of the problem space and provides well-constructed examples (e.g., Example 3 showing causal recoverability despite inseparability of Y and Ry). While some results like Theorem 1 appear incremental, they represent important technical extensions that address limitations in prior work. The paper is complete in its theoretical contributions, though the extensive appendix (13 pages) suggests some material might be better suited for a journal submission.

**Clarity**  
The paper's main weakness is its poor clarity and organization. The notation is confusing and inconsistent—using the same symbol V for different concepts (e.g., V as a set of nodes, V* as proxy variables, Vo as observed variables), with subscripts that become illegible. The distinction between Vi (a vertex) and Vo (a set of vertices) is particularly problematic. The presentation of Theorem 1 is especially opaque, with its technical conditions and admissible sequences difficult to parse. The paper would benefit from a more structured presentation that clearly motivates each theorem and its relationship to prior work. The flow between sections is disjointed, with Section 3 appearing somewhat disconnected from the rest of the paper. The authors could improve clarity by: (1) introducing a consistent notation system; (2) providing more intuitive explanations before presenting theorems; (3) better organizing the flow of ideas to show how each result builds on previous ones.

**Originality**  
The paper makes several valuable contributions to the literature on missing data. The most significant originality lies in Theorem 6, which establishes necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects under simple attrition—correcting a misconception in Garcia (2013). The paper's key insight that causal queries may be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not (Example 3) is novel and important. While some results (e.g., Theorem 2 and Corollary 1) resemble inverse probability weighting approaches, the paper provides a graphical framework that offers deeper theoretical insights than previous work. The authors properly contextualize their work against Mohan et al. (2013), Garcia (2013), and other related literature. The paper significantly advances the state of the art in understanding the relationship between missing data mechanisms and identifiability of causal effects.

**Significance**  
This paper addresses a critical problem in empirical research where missing data can bias results. The ability to determine when causal effects can be recovered from missing data has direct practical implications for fields like medicine, social sciences, and economics. The authors' results are particularly relevant for longitudinal studies with attrition, a common and serious problem in experimental research. The paper's framework provides a systematic way to diagnose recoverability of causal effects, which could inform study design and data analysis practices. The results have potential to influence both methodological development and applied research, making this a significant contribution to the field.

**Overall Assessment**  
This paper makes important theoretical contributions to understanding recoverability of causal queries from missing data. The technical quality is high, and the results are well-supported. The main weaknesses are poor clarity, inconsistent notation, and an overly dense presentation that makes the paper difficult to follow. The paper would benefit from a more structured presentation with improved notation, better motivation for each result, and clearer connections between theorems.

The authors should consider that the current presentation makes it challenging for practitioners to apply the results. A more accessible framework that synthesizes the conditions for recoverability into a clear decision procedure would increase the paper's impact. The paper would be substantially stronger if the authors could distill the key conditions into a more intuitive graphical criterion that practitioners could use to assess recoverability for their specific missing data problems.

**Recommendation**  
The paper addresses an important problem with technically sound results that advance the field. While the current presentation is problematic, the core contributions are significant and valuable. I recommend acceptance, with the authors making substantial revisions to improve clarity, notation, and presentation. The paper has the potential to be a major contribution to the field if presented more accessibly.
