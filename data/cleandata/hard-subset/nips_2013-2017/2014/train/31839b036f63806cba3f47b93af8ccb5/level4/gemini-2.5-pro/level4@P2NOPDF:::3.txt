This paper addresses the important problem of recovering a probability distribution when both the data-generating process and the missing data mechanism are known. The authors generalize prior work by considering a broader class of missingness processes than those in earlier results, leading to a novel identification formula. A key contribution is showing that specific causal queries can be identified even when the full observational joint distribution is not recoverable. The paper is technically sound and generally well-written, though it could be significantly strengthened by addressing several points regarding practical assumptions, clarity of definitions, and justification for certain modeling choices.

**Main Comments**

1.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** A central assumption of the proposed method is that the graphical model for the censoring process is known. It would significantly strengthen the paper to discuss the practicality of this assumption. In which typical machine learning applications is this knowledge readily available? Providing concrete examples would help ground the theoretical contributions.

2.  **Scope of the Missingness Model:** The framework appears to assume that the total number of individuals in the population is known, even if an individual has no observed variables (i.e., Vo is empty and Rv=1 for all V in Vm). This is an important distinction, as it excludes problems common in other fields, such as capture-recapture models for estimating population size. It would be beneficial to explicitly state this assumption and its implications for the scope of the method in Section 2.

3.  **Justification for Model Restrictions:** The paper restricts m-graphs by disallowing R variables from being parents of variables in V or U. Given that R variables are treated as real quantities and not merely indicators, the justification for this structural constraint is not immediately clear. Is this restriction made for technical convenience, or does it reflect a fundamental assumption about the data-generating process? A brief explanation would be very helpful.

4.  **Clarity of Presentation and Definitions:**
    *   The term 'm-graph' appears to be used inconsistently. In Figure 1, it includes the V* variables, whereas in Figure 2, it does not. Please clarify if V* variables are simply omitted for brevity in some diagrams and state this explicitly.
    *   The term 'neighbor' is used at the bottom of page 4 without a formal definition.
    *   It would be helpful to include more detail regarding Figure 1 in the main paper to aid the reader's understanding, perhaps by reducing the figure's size to create space.

5.  **Comparison to Prior Work:**
    *   In the discussion on page 8 comparing the proposed method to the 'rectification' by Garcia et al., it is noted that the authors' definition of recoverability assumes positivity. To make the comparison clearer, please clarify whether Garcia et al. make a similar positivity assumption. If not, their conditions for identification might be stronger, which would be an important point of contrast.
    *   The statement on page 8 that the "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption" is overly general. The paper would be more compelling if it elaborated on why standard MLE is insufficient for the more general missingness processes introduced here. What specific challenges prevent a direct application of MLE in the MNAR settings considered?
