This paper addresses the problem of recovering a distribution from knowledge of the generating process and the process leading to missing data, a topic of interest in the field. The authors consider a more general class of missingness processes than those previously studied by Mohan et al., leading to a novel identification formula. Furthermore, they examine the identification of causal queries and demonstrate that these can be identified even when the observational joint distribution is not identified.

The paper is generally well-written, although there are some areas for improvement that I will outline below.

MAIN COMMENTS:

(1) The results presented in this paper require knowledge of the process by which observations are censored. However, it is unclear whether this knowledge is readily available in typical machine learning applications. To enhance the paper, the authors could provide more details regarding Figure 1 in the main text. This could be achieved by reducing the figure's size to accommodate additional information.

(2) In Section 2, it should be noted that the missing data processes considered here always assume that the full population is known. Specifically, even if Vo = ∅, and Rv = 1 for all V in Vm (i.e., no variables are observed for a particular individual), it is still assumed that the existence of this individual is known. This assumption excludes certain missing data problems, such as estimating the number of animals in a population.

(3) The term "mgraph" appears to be used ambiguously throughout the paper. In some instances, the graph includes the V* variables (as seen in Figure 1), while in other cases, it does not (as seen in Figure 2). If the V* variables are simply being omitted in these graphs, this should be explicitly stated to avoid confusion.

(4) The authors restrict mgraphs from having R variables as parents of variables in V or U. Given that they treat R variables as real quantities rather than simply indicators, they should provide justification for this restriction or acknowledge that it is primarily a matter of convenience.

(5) The term "neighbor" is introduced at the bottom of page 4 without a clear definition. The authors should provide a definition for this term to ensure clarity.

(6) On page 8, the first paragraph mentions the "rectification" of the observation by Garcia. However, the authors' definition of recoverability assumes positivity. It is unclear whether Garcia et al. also assume positivity, and if not, they may require a stronger condition.

(7) The second paragraph on page 8 states that the "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under the MAR assumption." This statement is overly vague, as it does not explain why maximum likelihood cannot be applied to any of the missingness processes described in the paper.

MINOR COMMENTS:

Page 1, line 33: "can bias outcomes" could be rephrased for clarity.

Page 2, Figure 1: The figure caption could be expanded to include more details.

Page 2, Example 1 on page 3, line 158: The equation P(Y | X, Z, R_y) = P(X, Z) should be corrected to P(Y | X, Z).

Page 3, line 168: The statement "Since Y || R | X, Z" should be revised to "Y || R_y | X, Z" for consistency.

Page 3, line 214: The notation "R^{part} ={R^{(1)},R^{(2)}, \ldots , R^N}" should be corrected to "R^{(N)}".

Page 3, line 215: The notation "R^j" should be revised to "R^{(j)}" for consistency.

Page 4, Corollary 1: The statement does not contain partially observed variables, which could be clarified.

Page 6, Footnote 2: A reference for the variable-level definition should be provided.

This paper is an interesting contribution that builds upon earlier work. While the technical aspects are sound, the breadth of its application is unclear.
