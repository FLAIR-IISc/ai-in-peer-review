The paper addresses the problem of identifiability in the presence of missing data, building upon a framework introduced by Mohan and others. It presents improvements to existing results and offers illustrative examples, notably demonstrating the identifiability of causal distributions even when conditional distributions are unavailable.

**Quality**

The technical quality appears high; the results are complex but seem correct. Results such as Theorem 3 are clear and interpretable. However, some contributions feel incremental and do not readily integrate into a broader understanding of the field.

Section 3 feels somewhat disconnected from the rest of the paper, and its central result (Theorem 1) is challenging to grasp. It appears related to Markov blanket concepts discussed in Richardson (2003). The quality improves in later sections, with Section 5 providing clear criteria and Section 6 presenting interesting findings regarding causal queries.

**Clarity**

Clarity is the paper’s primary weakness. The scope of material presented – evidenced by the density of the text and a 13-page technical appendix – may be too extensive for a conference paper. This contributes to a lack of narrative flow and makes the paper difficult to follow; it might be better suited as a longer journal publication.

While finding concise notation for numerous quantities is challenging, improvements are possible. The repeated use of ‘V’ to denote different objects burdens the reader, and in some instances, subscripts become illegible. The inconsistent usage of Vi as both a vertex and a component of the set Vo is particularly confusing. The set equation in Corollary 1 exemplifies a lack of clear presentation.

**Originality and Significance**

Sections 5 and 6 stand out as clear, interesting, and potentially significant. The paper tackles an important problem and presents results that appear genuinely useful. 

A more holistic approach to identifiability within this framework would be valuable. Aiming for a comprehensive criterion—similar to those established for causal effect identification without missingness—could elevate the work. Currently, it's difficult to envision a practitioner navigating a series of papers to locate the specific result determining (non-)identifiability for their causal effect of interest, especially given the complexity of results like Theorem 1.

Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to be related to inverse probability weighting. The effectiveness of these results likely stems from the conditioning sets being Markov blankets within the mixed graphs employed. Relevant work by Richardson (2003) and (2009) could be acknowledged.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of missing data inference. Despite issues with clarity and presentation, the technical quality is strong, and the results in certain sections are both original and significant. Addressing the clarity concerns would substantially enhance the impact of this work.
