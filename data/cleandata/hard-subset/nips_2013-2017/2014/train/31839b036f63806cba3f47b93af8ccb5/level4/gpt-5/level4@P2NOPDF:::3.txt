This paper tackles the problem of when a distribution is recoverable given knowledge of both the data-generating process and the missingness mechanism. It generalizes prior work by Mohan et al. to a broader class of missingness processes, yielding a new identification formula. The paper also considers identification of causal queries and shows that certain causal effects can be identified even when the observational joint distribution is not. The exposition is generally clear, but several aspects need clarification or refinement.

Main comments:
1) Assumed knowledge of the censoring/missingness process:
- The identification results rely on knowing the process by which observations are censored. Please discuss how realistic this assumption is in typical machine learning settings, and provide concrete examples where such knowledge is available.
- It would help to move more detail from Figure 1 into the main text (e.g., by shrinking the figure and elaborating on its components and assumptions).

2) Population-level assumption in Section 2:
- The missingness framework appears to assume that the full population is known: even when Vo = ∅ and Rv = 1 for all V in Vm (no variables observed for this individual), the individual’s existence is still known.
- Please state this explicitly and clarify that the scope excludes problems like population size estimation (e.g., capture–recapture).

3) Ambiguity in the term “mgraph”:
- Sometimes the mgraph includes the V* variables (Figure 1), while at other times it does not (Figure 2). If V* nodes are omitted for brevity in some figures, please say so explicitly and standardize the usage throughout.

4) Restrictions on parents of R variables:
- The current setup disallows R variables from being parents of variables in V or U. Since R variables are treated as substantive (not mere indicators), please justify this restriction (e.g., conceptual rationale vs. technical convenience) and discuss implications if it were relaxed.

5) Undefined terminology:
- The term “neighbor” at the bottom of p.4 is not defined. Please define it precisely (e.g., graphical adjacency or some other relation).

6) p.8, first paragraph:
- The notion of “rectification” (Garcia et al.) should be clarified or referenced.
- Recoverability here assumes positivity. Do Garcia et al. also assume positivity? If not, specify what additional or alternative conditions they require and how they compare to yours.

7) p.8, second paragraph (Maximum Likelihood under MAR):
- The statement “Maximum Likelihood is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR” is too vague. Please specify the precise assumptions (e.g., correct model specification, positivity, MAR definition used) and explain why standard ML approaches cannot be directly applied to the more general missingness processes studied here.

Minor comments:
- p.1, line 33: “can bias outcomes” — consider specifying which outcomes or in what sense (estimands, predictions) to be more precise.
- p.2, Figure 1: “Qualifications” — please clarify the label or caption wording; it is unclear as written.
- p.3, Example 1 (line 158): P(Y | X, Z, R_y) = P(X, Z) should read P(Y | X, Z, R_y) = P(Y | X, Z).
- p.3, line 168: “Since Y ⟂ R | X, Z” should be “Since Y ⟂ R_y | X, Z”.
- p.3, line 214: Condition (ii) R^{part} = {R^{(1)}, R^{(2)}, …, R^N} should be R^{(N)}.
- p.3, line 215: “R^j respectively” should be R^{(j)}.
- p.4, Corollary 1: “does not contain a partially observed variables” should be “does not contain any partially observed variables.”
- p.6, Footnote 2: Please provide a reference for the variable-level definition.

Overall assessment:
- The paper presents a technically strong extension of prior work, offering a novel identification result and clear implications for causal queries under missingness. The practical scope and typical availability of the required missingness-process knowledge remain somewhat unclear and merit further discussion. With clarifications on assumptions, consistent terminology/notation, and sharper comparisons to existing methods (including ML under MAR), the paper would be significantly strengthened.
