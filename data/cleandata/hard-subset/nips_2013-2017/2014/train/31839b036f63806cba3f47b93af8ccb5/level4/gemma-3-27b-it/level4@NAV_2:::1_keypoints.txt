Addresses identifiability of probabilistic and causal queries with missing data using and extending the missingness graph framework.  
Provides rigorous theoretical results (e.g., Theorem 3) and demonstrates causal queries can be identified even if constituent probabilistic relations are not.  
Technical quality is high with careful derivations, but contributions feel incremental and not unified into a broader criterion.  
Section 3 is disconnected and Theorem 1 is dense and hard to follow; later sections (5 and 6) show stronger technical merit.  
Section 5 offers useful non-recoverability criteria; Section 6 contains novel and intriguing causal-identification results.  
Clarity is a major weakness: dense notation, repeated use of 'V' for different sets, illegible subscripts, and a disjoint narrative hinder comprehension.  
Corollary 1 and other set equations are notationally dense and difficult to parse.  
Paper may be better suited to a longer journal format given its volume and technical detail.  
Significant originality in Sections 5–6, especially showing causal effects can be identified despite unrecoverable conditionals.  
Work would benefit from aiming for a complete, unifying identifiability criterion analogous to causal identification without missingness.  
Connections to existing methods (e.g., inverse probability weighting, Markov blankets) should be made explicit.  
Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important problem, extends theory, provides novel causal-identification results, includes attrition examples, and is technically rigorous.  
Arguments against acceptance: poor clarity/organization, dense notation, incremental contributions lacking unifying framework, unclear relevance of Section 3, and possible suitability for a journal.  
Specific corrections requested: fix wording on p4 l214, use ↔ instead of <-->, verify Garcia (2013) claim, define graphs formed by severing edges, and avoid using Vo/Vm before defining them.  
Recommendation: accept with major revisions focused on clarity, streamlined presentation, and a more holistic framing of results.
