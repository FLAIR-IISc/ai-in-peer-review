This paper presents an intriguing exploration of the problem of recovering a distribution from knowledge of the generating process and the process leading to missing data.

The authors expand on earlier work by Mohan et al. by considering a more general class of missingness processes, which yields a novel identification formula. Furthermore, they examine the identification of causal queries, demonstrating that these can be identified even when the observational joint distribution is not identified.

The paper is generally well-written, although there are some areas that require attention, as outlined below.

MAIN COMMENTS:

(1) The results in this paper rely on knowledge of the process by which observations are censored. It is unclear whether this knowledge is readily available in typical machine learning applications. Including more details about Figure 1 in the main paper would be beneficial, potentially by reducing the figure's size.

(2) In Section 2, it should be noted that the missing data processes always assume that the full population is known. This assumption excludes certain missing data problems, such as estimating the population size of animals.

(3) The term 'mgraph' appears to be used ambiguously, as it sometimes includes V* variables (Figure 1) and sometimes does not (Figure 2). If the V* variables are being omitted in these graphs, this should be clarified.

(4) The restriction that mgraphs cannot have R variables as parents of variables in V or U warrants explanation, particularly given that the authors treat R variables as real quantities rather than indicators.

(5) The term 'neighbor' (bottom of p. 4) is not defined and requires clarification.

(6) On p. 8, the first paragraph mentions the "rectification" of the observation by Garcia. However, it is unclear whether Garcia et al. assume positivity, which is a requirement for recoverability as defined by the authors.

(7) The statement on p. 8 (second paragraph) that the Maximum Likelihood method yields consistent estimates under the MAR assumption is overly vague. A more detailed explanation of why maximum likelihood cannot be applied to the missingness processes described in this paper is necessary.

MINOR COMMENTS

* P. 1, line 33: "can bias outcomes" could be rephrased for clarity.
* P. 2, Figure 1: Adding qualifications would enhance the figure's usefulness.
* P. 2, Example 1 on p. 3, line 158: The RHS should be P(Y|X,Z) instead of P(X,Z).
* P. 3, line 168: The correct notation should be Y || R_y | X,Z instead of Y || R | X,Z.
* P. 3, line 214: The correct notation should be R^{(N)} instead of R^N.
* P. 3, line 215: The correct notation should be R^{(j)} instead of R^j.
* P. 4, Corollary 1: The statement that it does not contain partially observed variables requires clarification.
* P. 6, Footnote 2: A reference for the variable-level definition would be helpful.

Overall, this is an interesting paper that builds upon earlier work, although the breadth of its application is unclear. From a technical standpoint, the paper is sound.
