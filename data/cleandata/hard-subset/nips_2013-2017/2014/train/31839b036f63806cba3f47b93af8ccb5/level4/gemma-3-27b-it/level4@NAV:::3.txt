This is a valuable paper addressing the fundamental problem of distribution recovery given knowledge of the generative process and the mechanisms causing missing data. The authors extend previous work, notably that of Mohan et al., by considering a more general class of missingness processes. This generalization leads to a novel identification formula, and importantly, the paper demonstrates the identifiability of causal queries even when the observational joint distribution itself is not identifiable. The paper is generally well-written, though some clarifications and minor revisions would enhance its impact.

**MAIN COMMENTS:**

(1) A key requirement of the presented results is knowledge of the censoring process. It’s important to discuss the practicality of this assumption within typical machine learning applications where such knowledge is often unavailable. Furthermore, the details regarding Figure 1 could be more prominently displayed within the main text, potentially by adjusting the figure’s size.

(2) In Section 2, it would be beneficial to explicitly state that the missing data processes considered here assume a known full population. Even in scenarios where no variables are observed for an individual (Vo = emptyset, Rv =1 for all V in V_m), the framework assumes knowledge of that individual's existence, effectively excluding problems involving estimation of population size (e.g., estimating animal populations).

(3) The term “mgraph” appears to be used with some ambiguity. The inclusion of V* variables varies between figures (present in Figure 1, absent in Figure 2). If the omission is intentional, this should be clearly explained.

(4) The restriction prohibiting R variables from being parents of variables in V or U within the mgraphs requires justification. Given the authors treat R variables as continuous quantities rather than simple indicators, an explanation of the rationale behind this restriction—whether for simplification or a more fundamental reason—would strengthen the paper.

(5) The term “neighbor,” appearing at the bottom of page 4, is undefined and should be clarified.

(6) On page 8, the first paragraph discusses the “rectification” of observation by Garcia et al.  The authors’ definition of recoverability relies on the positivity assumption. It’s important to determine whether Garcia et al. also rely on positivity, and if not, whether they employ a stronger condition to achieve their results.

(7) The statement on page 8, second paragraph, that “Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption” is overly broad. A more specific explanation is needed to clarify why maximum likelihood cannot be directly applied to the missingness processes described in this paper.

**MINOR COMMENTS:**

P.1 line 33: “can bias outcomes” could be rephrased for clarity.
P.2 Figure 1: Add “Qualifications” to the figure caption.
P.2 Example 1 on p.3 line 158:  The equation P(Y |X,Z,R_y) = P(X,Z) should be P(Y|X,Z).
P.3 line 168: “Since Y || R | X,Z” should be “Y || R_y | X,Z”.
P.3 line 214: Condition (ii) R^{part} ={R^{(1)},R^{(2)}, \ldots , R^N} should be R^{(N)}.
P.3 line 215: “R^j respectively” should be “R^{(j)}”.
P.4 Corollary 1:  The corollary statement lacks mention of partially observed variables.
P.6 Footnote 2:  Please provide a reference for the variable-level definition.



Overall, this is a technically sound and interesting paper that builds upon existing work in the field. The breadth of its applicability remains somewhat unclear, but the presented theoretical contributions are noteworthy.
