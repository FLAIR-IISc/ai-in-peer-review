- Extends sequential factorization to allow missingness indicators (R variables) in admissible factorizations (Theorem 1).  
- Provides a general recoverability result for joint distributions via R-variable partitioning and collider-path-based Markov blankets (Theorem 2), plus a corollary for models without latent common causes.  
- Introduces the first general non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2).  
- Shows certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when related probabilistic quantities are not, using dormant independences and d*-separation (Theorem 4).  
- Gives necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering joint distributions and causal effects in attrition with one partially observed outcome (Theorems 5 and 6) and corrects prior claims.  
- Builds on and extends Mohan et al. (2013) and Pearl & Mohan (2013), connecting to do-calculus and standard missing-data literature.  
- Clarifies when MAR/MCAR permit model-blind recovery and contrasts with ML/EM, IPW, and multiple imputation approaches.  
- Non-recoverability criteria and causal recoverability despite inseparability are novel contributions.  
- Technical contributions are clearly stated and proofs are provided in the appendix.  
- The minimality condition in the extended factorization is well-motivated and illustrated.  
- Theorem 2 generalizes prior recoverability results to semi-Markovian models using well-defined constructs (Rpart and Mb).  
- Causal recoverability results are plausible and supported by do-calculus arguments; attrition results are crisp.  
- Manuscript is generally well-organized and readable, with helpful examples.  
- Notation is heavy but mostly consistent; some definitions need more intuition and operational guidance.  
- Several references are improperly formatted or incomplete and should be fixed; figures need to be legible and fully referenced.  
- Original contributions include extended factorization, a general recoverability theorem, first general non-recoverability criteria, and new causal recoverability results.  
- The work is significant for guiding analysts on recoverability under MNAR and preventing misuse of model-blind methods.  
- Strengths: substantive theoretical advances, practical graphical conditions, novel insight on causal vs probabilistic recoverability, and clear attrition results.  
- Weaknesses: lacks an explicit algorithmic checklist and complexity analysis for applying main theorems.  
- Suggest adding intuitive explanations/diagrams for collider/inducing paths, d*-separation, and more step-by-step examples for practitioners.  
- Clarify assumptions about R not being parents of V∪U, discuss continuous variables and positivity conditions beyond “strictly positive over complete cases.”  
- Recommend a small simulation study to show finite-sample behavior and robustness to m-graph misspecification.  
- For attrition, include an end-to-end example showing condition checks and computation of the estimand in Eq. (5).  
- Presentation fixes needed: reference formatting, typographical issues, and self-contained, clearly labeled figures.  
- Questions for authors: Can identification of admissible sequences and R partitions be automated and what are complexity bounds?  
- Questions for authors: How sensitive are Theorem 4 results to small mis-specifications (e.g., an unmodeled weak edge)?  
- Questions for authors: Do the non-recoverability criteria remain necessary when positivity is violated or only approximately satisfied?  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong novel theory on MNAR recoverability, practical criteria, and clarification of attrition misconceptions.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical/algorithmic demonstration and potentially difficult concepts for non-experts without extra exposition.  
- Overall recommendation: solid and timely theoretical contribution; accept with minor revisions to improve accessibility and presentation.
