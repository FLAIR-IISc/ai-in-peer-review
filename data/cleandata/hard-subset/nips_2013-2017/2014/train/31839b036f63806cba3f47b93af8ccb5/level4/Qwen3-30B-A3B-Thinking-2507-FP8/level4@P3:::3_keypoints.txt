- Problem: Determines when probabilistic and causal queries can be recovered from data with missing entries given a model of the missingness process.  
- Approach: Extends Mohan et al. (2013) and introduces "missingness graphs" (m-graphs) to represent the missingness process and variable dependencies.  
- Key theoretical contribution: Shows causal queries can be recoverable even when associated probabilistic relations (P(y,x), P(y|x)) are not, especially with inseparability between Y and Ry.  
- Main contributions: (1) Refines sequential factorization to handle interacting missingness mechanisms.  
- Main contributions: (2) Provides new graphical criteria for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions.  
- Main contributions: (3) Establishes conditions for recovering causal effects despite Y–Ry inseparability.  
- Main contributions: (4) Applies results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.  
- Strength: Technically sound with careful proofs and a well-structured presentation.  
- Strength: Makes an important distinction between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries, filling a literature gap.  
- Strength: Novel insight that causal effects may be recoverable even when underlying probabilistic distributions are not.  
- Strength: Practical value demonstrated via application to attrition and thorough related-work context.  
- Weakness: Practical applicability of the m-graph framework is under-discussed; assuming knowledge of the missingness process may be unrealistic.  
- Weakness: Ambiguous use of "m-graph" (sometimes includes V* variables, sometimes not), which may confuse readers.  
- Weakness: Restriction that R variables cannot be parents of V or U needs stronger justification given R treated as real quantities.  
- Weakness: Missing definition of "neighbor" and lack of explicit comparison of positivity assumptions with prior work (e.g., Garcia 2013).  
- Clarity issues: Notational and typographical errors (e.g., incorrect equations and inconsistent notation like R^j vs. R^(j)) that should be fixed.  
- Clarity issue: Claim about maximum likelihood methods is vague and should be supported with specific references or clarification.  
- Originality & significance: Strong extension of prior work with high significance for missing-data and causal inference researchers.  
- Pro acceptance: Novel theoretical contributions, comprehensive treatment of missingness mechanisms, clear graphical criteria, practical application, and detailed proofs.  
- Con acceptance: Ambiguous m-graph terminology, insufficient discussion of practical assumptions, unjustified R-variable restriction, notational errors, and limited comparison with practical ML methods.  
- Overall: Technically strong paper that advances the field; recommend minor revisions to improve clarity, justify assumptions, and better address practical applicability.
