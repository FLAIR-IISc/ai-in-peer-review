- Paper studies identifiability under missing-data mechanisms in a graphical framework, strengthening existing results and giving instructive examples.

- Technical development appears sound and intricate; Theorem 3 is clear and interpretable, but some contributions feel incremental.

- Section 3 and Theorem 1 are hard to parse and seem orthogonal to the main thrust; their role should be clarified or deferred.

- Sections 5 and 6 are the strongest, offering novel, clear, and potentially significant results on causal queries.

- Clarity is the main weakness: dense prose, overloaded notation, and a 13-page technical appendix impede readability.

- Notation problems: overuse of capital V for multiple objects, confusing Vi vs Vo, and opaque set expressions (e.g., Corollary 1).

- Concrete readability fixes: reduce scope for the conference version, move technical material to the appendix, and prioritize Sections 5–6.

- Adopt distinct symbols/fonts for individual variables, variable sets, and observed/missing partitions; avoid overloading V.

- Define all graph operations (e.g., edge severing) and ensure Vo, Vm and other sets are defined before first use.

- Add a running example to illustrate definitions and results as they appear.

- Make connections to prior work explicit: relate results to Markov-blanket separation in ADMGs and to Richardson (2003, 2009).

- Explain when Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 correspond to inverse probability weighting (IPW) and discuss necessity/sufficiency.

- Discuss prospects for a complete identifiability criterion in the missing-data graphical setting or provide a roadmap toward one.

- Specific manuscript fixes: correct wording about R^part as a partition, replace casual phrasing (e.g., “oftentimes”), use \leftrightarrow instead of ASCII arrows, and clarify Garcia (2013)’s relation.

- Improve proof presentation by breaking dense algebraic displays into steps or moving long set-theoretic expressions to the appendix with summaries in the main text.

- Overall: important and promising contributions, especially in Sections 5–6, but presentation, scope, and literature connections need tightening to improve accessibility.
