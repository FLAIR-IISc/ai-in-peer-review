This is an interesting paper. It discusses the problem of how to recover from a process that generates a distribution process and processes missing data.

This paper considers a more general class of missing data processes compared to the previous work of Mohan and others.

It leads to a new identification formula.

Also, the authors consider the identification of causal queries and show that although (observational) joint distributions cannot be identified, they can be identified.

The paper is generally well-written, although there are some flaws and areas for improvement that I describe below.

Major Comments:
(1) The results here require knowledge of the process by which the observations were censored.
Is this knowledge available in common machine learning applications?
It would be helpful to include more detailed information related to Figure 1 in the main paper. (This figure can be made smaller.)
(2) Section 2. It should be mentioned here that missing data processes always assume that the complete population is known:
Even if Vo = emptyset, and Rv = 1 for all V in V_m (so no variable is observed for this person), it is still assumed that we know this person exists.
(This excludes missing data problems such as estimating the number of animals in the population.)
(3) The term mgraph seems to be used ambiguously. In particular, sometimes the V* variables (Figure 1) are included in the graph, and sometimes they are not (Figure 2). If the V* variables are simply omitted in these graphs, it should be explained.
(4) In this paper, mgraphs are not allowed to have R variables as parents of V or U.
Since the authors describe considering R variables as real quantities, not just indicators, they should explain why this restriction is justified (or whether it is primarily for convenience).
(5) The term 'neighbor', which appears below p.4, is not defined.
(6) p.8 first paragraph. "Correction" of observations by Garcia.
The recoverability positivity defined by the authors assumes positivity. Do Garcia and others also assume positivity?
If not, they may need stronger conditions.
(7) p.8 second paragraph. "The maximum likelihood method is known to give consistent estimates under MAR assumptions".
This is very vague. Why cannot maximum likelihood be applied to any of the missing data processes described here?
