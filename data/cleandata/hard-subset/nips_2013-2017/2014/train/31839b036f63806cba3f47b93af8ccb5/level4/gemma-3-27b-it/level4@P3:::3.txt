This paper addresses the important problem of identifying estimable probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries, given a model of the missingness process. The authors extend prior work by Mohan et al. [2013] to provide more general conditions for recovery, particularly demonstrating that causal queries can be recoverable even when underlying probabilistic relationships are not. This is a potentially significant contribution, and the paper is generally well-written, though improvements to clarity and precision would strengthen it.

The core idea is to leverage “Missingness Graphs” (m-graphs) to formally represent the missingness process and dependencies. The authors refine the sequential factorization theorem from their previous work and introduce new graphical criteria for identifying non-recoverability. A key result is that causal effects, P(y|do(x)), can be identified even when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable, a scenario where traditional approaches fail. The application to attrition problems further demonstrates the practical relevance of their findings.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of recoverability conditions to causal queries, particularly the demonstrated ability to identify causal effects even with non-d-separability, is a significant and novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper builds upon a solid theoretical foundation established in previous work (Mohan et al. [2013]) and presents its extensions in a rigorous manner with clear theorems and definitions.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition, a common challenge in observational studies, highlights the practical utility of the proposed framework.
*   **Clear Formalization:** The use of m-graphs provides a clear and formal representation of the missing data process and dependencies.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions and Scope:** The reliance on knowledge of the missingness process, encoded in the m-graph, is a key limitation. The paper does not fully address how practical it is to accurately specify these graphs in real-world applications, especially in scenarios beyond controlled experiments. The assumption that the full population is known, even with completely missing data for some individuals, limits the applicability to scenarios like population estimation. This should be explicitly acknowledged.
*   **Ambiguity in Notation:** The inconsistent inclusion of V\* variables in m-graphs (Figures 1 vs. 2) introduces ambiguity. The authors should clearly state when and why these variables are included or omitted, and maintain consistency throughout the paper.
*   **Restriction on R Variables:** The restriction prohibiting R variables from being parents of variables in V or U is not adequately justified.  The authors should explain the rationale behind this restriction, or discuss the implications of relaxing it.
*   **Vagueness in Related Work:** The discussion of Maximum Likelihood estimation on p.8 is too vague. The authors should clarify why Maximum Likelihood may not be directly applicable to the missingness processes considered in the paper.
*   **Minor Issues:** Several minor typographical errors and inconsistencies exist throughout the paper (detailed in the original review). Addressing these would improve readability.



**Specific Comments & Suggestions:**

*   **Section 2:** Explicitly state the assumption that the full population is known, even if all data are missing for some individuals. Discuss the limitations of this assumption.
*   **m-graphs:** Clarify the consistent representation of m-graphs, specifically regarding the inclusion or omission of V\* variables.
*   **R Variable Restriction:** Provide a justification for the restriction on R variables as parents of V or U.
*   **Garcia [2013] comparison:** When discussing Garcia [2013], explicitly state whether Garcia's work also assumes positivity. If not, clarify the differences in assumptions.
*   **Figure 1:**  Consider reducing the size of Figure 1 and adding more explanatory detail directly in the main text to improve accessibility.
*   **Terminology:** Define the term “neighbor” before its use on p.4.
*   **Typos:** Correct the following typographical errors: p.1 line 33 ("can bias outcomes" -> "can introduce bias"), p.2 (Figure 1: add qualifications regarding the variables in the graph), p.3 line 158 (P(Y|X,Z) instead of P(X,Z)), p.3 line 168 (Y || R<sub>y</sub> | X,Z), p.3 line 214 (R<sup>(N)</sup>), p.3 line 215 (R<sup>(j)</sup>), p.4 (partially observed variables instead of variables).



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of existing work on recoverability of queries with missing data.
*   The ability to identify causal effects even in the presence of non-d-separability is a particularly valuable contribution.
*   The paper’s rigorous treatment of the problem and clear formalization make it a valuable addition to the literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong reliance on accurate specification of the missingness process (m-graph) may limit the practical applicability of the framework.
*   Some ambiguities and limitations in the scope and assumptions need to be further clarified.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a solid paper with the potential to make a significant contribution to the field. While there are some weaknesses, they are not insurmountable. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions** to address the points raised above, particularly regarding clarification of assumptions, notation consistency, and justification of restrictions. Addressing these points will greatly enhance the clarity and impact of the work.
