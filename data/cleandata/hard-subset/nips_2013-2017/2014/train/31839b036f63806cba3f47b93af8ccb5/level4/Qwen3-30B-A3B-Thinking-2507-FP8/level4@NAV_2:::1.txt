

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on missingness graphs (m-graphs) to develop graphical conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors build upon the foundational work of Mohan and Pearl (2014) by providing more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (P(y|x) and P(y,x)) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))), with particular emphasis on cases where Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable. The paper makes several key contributions: (1) it demonstrates that causal queries may be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations are not, (2) it establishes new graphical criteria for non-recoverability, and (3) it applies these results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.

Quality: The technical quality of the paper is high, with well-motivated theorems and proofs that appear sound. The authors successfully extend Mohan et al.'s (2013) results to more general cases where missingness mechanisms interact. Theorems 3-6 are particularly clear and interpretable, providing valuable insights into the relationship between recoverability and graph structure. However, some results (particularly Theorem 1) appear to be incremental improvements rather than substantial advances, and the connection to broader theoretical frameworks could be more explicitly articulated. The paper's technical depth is impressive, but the complexity of Theorem 1 and its presentation in Section 3 makes this section difficult to follow.

Clarity: The paper's primary weakness is its poor clarity and organization. The notation is inconsistent and confusing (e.g., using V for multiple distinct concepts, Vi as a vertex versus Vo as a set of vertices), and the presentation is overly dense for a conference paper. The set equation in Corollary 1 is particularly problematic. The paper contains approximately 13 pages of technical content plus a lengthy appendix, suggesting it would be better suited for a journal publication. The flow is disjointed, with Section 3 appearing somewhat disconnected from the rest of the paper, making it challenging for readers to follow the logical progression of arguments.

Originality: The paper addresses an important problem in missing data analysis with significant potential impact for researchers working with incomplete datasets. Sections 5 and 6 present genuinely novel results, particularly the demonstration that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y and Ry are not d-separable. The work builds meaningfully on Mohan et al. (2013) and related literature, though the authors could have better situated their contributions within the broader theoretical landscape. The results have the potential to significantly advance the field, but the complexity of the conditions makes them difficult to apply directly without further simplification.

Significance: The problem addressed is highly significant for empirical researchers across disciplines who routinely deal with missing data. The paper provides concrete graphical conditions for recovery that could guide practitioners in assessing whether their causal or probabilistic queries can be reliably estimated from missing data. The application to attrition problems (Section 7) is particularly relevant to longitudinal studies. However, the lack of a unified framework for identifiability (as exists in the standard causal inference literature) limits the paper's immediate utility. A more comprehensive criterion for identifiability across all cases would substantially increase the paper's impact.

Pro arguments for acceptance: The paper makes important theoretical contributions to the understanding of recoverability in the presence of missing data, particularly regarding causal queries. The results are technically sound, and the application to attrition problems is well-motivated and relevant. The paper extends Mohan et al. (2013) in meaningful ways, providing conditions that apply to more general missingness mechanisms.

Con arguments for rejection: The paper suffers from poor clarity and notation, making it difficult to follow the technical arguments. The presentation is overly dense for a conference paper, with too much material for the available space. Some results appear to be incremental rather than transformative. The lack of a unified criterion for identifiability limits the paper's practical utility for researchers.

The paper would benefit from significant improvements in presentation, including clearer notation, better organization, and a more focused argument. The technical contributions are valuable, but the current presentation significantly impedes their accessibility and impact.
