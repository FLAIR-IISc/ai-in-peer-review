This paper addresses the problem of identifiability for causal and statistical queries in the presence of data missing under various mechanisms. Building on a framework introduced by Mohan et al., the authors improve upon several existing results and provide insightful examples, such as demonstrating the identifiability of causal distributions even when certain conditional distributions are unavailable.

**Quality**

The technical quality of the work is high; the results are intricate but appear to be correct. Some theorems, such as Theorem 3, are presented with clarity and are readily interpretable. However, other results are incremental improvements on existing work and, from my perspective, do not contribute to a broader conceptual understanding of the underlying identifiability principles.

Section 3 feels disconnected from the rest of the paper, and its main result, Theorem 1, is particularly difficult to comprehend. This result appears closely related to the work on Markov blankets in mixed graphs by Richardson (2009). The paper's quality improves significantly in the later sections. Section 5 provides clear and valuable answers to specific problems, and Section 6 contains interesting results on identifying causal queries.

**Clarity**

Clarity is the paper's primary weakness. The density of the text and the 13-page technical appendix suggest that there is too much material for a conference paper, and the relative importance of each contribution is not always clear. The article lacks a coherent flow, which makes it difficult to follow. The work would likely be more effective if presented as a longer, more thoroughly elaborated journal article.

While developing a consistent notation for this topic is challenging, the current notation could be significantly improved. The symbol 'V' is overloaded to represent many different objects, which creates ambiguity for the reader. In several places, subscripts become illegible due to their complexity. The inconsistency where V_i denotes a single vertex while V_o denotes a set of vertices is especially confusing. The set equation in Corollary 1 is a prominent example of these presentational issues.

**Originality and Significance**

The paper addresses an important and challenging problem. The results presented in Sections 5 and 6 are clear, novel, and potentially significant.

The paper's impact could be greater if the authors aimed for a more general and complete theory. Rather than presenting a series of specific results, a more valuable contribution would be a complete criterion for identifiability within this framework, analogous to what has been achieved for causal effect identification in the absence of missing data. It is difficult to imagine a practitioner navigating a collection of highly complex results like Theorem 1 to determine the identifiability of a specific quantity of interest.

There appears to be a connection between Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 and the principle of inverse probability weighting. The validity of these results likely stems from the conditioning sets acting as Markov blankets for the missingness indicators R(i) in the associated mixed graphs. The work of Richardson (2003, 2009) may be relevant here.

**Other Comments**

*   On page 4, line 214, R^part should be defined as a partition of R, not "a set containing partitions." The condition on that line should then state that R_x and R_y "belong to distinct elements of the partition."
*   The language is sometimes informal (e.g., "oftentimes").
*   On page 4, line 213, the standard LaTeX command `\leftrightarrow` should be used for the bidirected edge symbol instead of `<-->`.
*   Regarding the reference to Garcia (2013): is the result incorrect, or does it simply apply to a different framework? This should be clarified.
*   A notation for graphs constructed by severing edges is used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4 but is not defined.
*   The variables V_o and V_m are used in the sentence preceding their definition.

**Summary**

This paper addresses an important problem with a useful and technically sound approach. However, the work is significantly marred by poor presentation and unclear notation, which obscures the value of its contributions.
