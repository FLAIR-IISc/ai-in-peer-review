This is an interesting paper that addresses the problem of distribution recovery from knowledge of the generating process and the process leading to missing data. The paper considers a more general class of missingness processes than previously explored, notably extending work by Mohan et al., and this leads to a novel identification formula. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that causal queries can be identified even when the observational joint distribution is not. The paper is generally well-written, though there are some minor issues and areas for improvement detailed below.

**MAIN COMMENTS:**

(1) The results presented require knowledge of the process by which observations are censored. Is this knowledge readily available in typical machine learning applications? It would be helpful to include more detail regarding Figure 1 within the main paper; this could potentially be achieved by reducing the figure’s size.

(2) Section 2 would benefit from explicitly stating that the missing data processes considered here always assume knowledge of the full population. Even in the case where V<sub>o</sub> = ∅ and R<sub>v</sub> = 1 for all V ∈ V<sub>m</sub> (meaning no variables are observed for a given individual), the framework still assumes knowledge of that individual’s existence. This excludes missing data problems related to estimating population size, such as estimating the number of animals in a population.

(3) The term “mgraph” appears to be used ambiguously. Specifically, sometimes the graph includes the V* variables (as in Figure 1), while at other times it does not (as in Figure 2). If the V* variables are simply being omitted in certain graphs for clarity, this should be explicitly stated.

(4) The authors restrict mgraphs such that R variables are not allowed as parents of variables in V or U. Given that the authors treat the R variables as real quantities and not merely indicators, they should provide justification for this restriction, or clarify whether it is primarily for convenience.

(5) The term “neighbor,” appearing at the bottom of page 4, is not defined.

(6) On page 8, the first paragraph references the “rectification” of the observation by Garcia et al. The authors’ definition of recoverability assumes positivity. Do Garcia et al. also assume positivity? If not, their work may require a stronger condition for identification.

(7) On page 8, the second paragraph states that “Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption.” This statement is overly vague. The paper should explain why maximum likelihood cannot be directly applied to any of the missingness processes described herein.

**MINOR COMMENTS:**

P.1 line 33: “can bias outcomes” – consider rephrasing for clarity.

P.2 Figure 1: The caption requires qualifications/clarification.

P.2 Example 1 on p.3 line 158: The equation P(Y |X,Z,R<sub>y</sub>) = P(X,Z) appears incorrect; the right-hand side should be P(Y|X,Z).

P.3 line 168: “Since Y || R | X,Z” should be “Y || R<sub>y</sub> | X,Z”.

P.3 line 214: Condition (ii) R<sup>part</sup> ={R<sup>(1)</sup>,R<sup>(2)</sup>, \ldots , R<sup>N</sup>} should be R<sup>(N)</sup>.

P.3 line 215: “R<sup>j</sup> respectively” should be “R<sup>(j)</sup>”.

P.4 Corollary 1: This result does not extend to cases with partially observed variables.

P.6 Footnote 2: Please provide a reference for the variable-level definition.

Overall, this is an interesting and technically sound paper that builds on earlier work. While the breadth of potential applications is not entirely clear, the work is a valuable contribution to the field.
