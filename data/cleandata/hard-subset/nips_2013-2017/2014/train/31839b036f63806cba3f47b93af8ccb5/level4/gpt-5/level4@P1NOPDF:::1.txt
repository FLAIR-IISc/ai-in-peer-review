The paper addresses identifiability under various generative mechanisms for missing data, using the framework introduced by Mohan and colleagues. It strengthens some existing results and offers instructive examples (e.g., identifiability of causal distributions even when certain conditional distributions are unavailable).

Quality
- Technical quality appears high. The results are intricate but seem correct; statements like Theorem 3 are clear and interpretable.
- Some results are incremental extensions of prior work and, as far as I can tell, do not yet cohere into a broader unifying picture of what is happening.
- Section 3 feels orthogonal to the rest of the paper, and its main result (Theorem 1) is quite hard to digest. It seems closely related to Markov blanket results in Richardson (2009).
- The exposition improves later: Section 5 offers some clear answers, and Section 6 presents interesting results on causal queries.

Clarity
This is the paper’s main weakness. There is likely too much material for a conference paper, as evidenced by the density of the text and the 13-page technical appendix. The narrative does not flow well and is hard to follow; this would likely work better as a more carefully developed journal article.
- Notation could be improved. The repeated use of the capital V for many different objects makes reading difficult, and some subscripts are nearly illegible. The fact that Vi denotes a vertex while Vo denotes a set of vertices is particularly confusing. The set equation in Corollary 1 is perhaps the most extreme example of poor presentation.

Originality and Significance
- Sections 5 and 6 are clear, interesting, and potentially significant. The paper tackles an important problem and contains results that seem genuinely useful.
- It may be better to step back and develop a general theory—ideally, a complete criterion for identifiability in this framework (analogous to the complete criteria for causal effects without missingness). Otherwise, it is hard to imagine a practitioner sifting through a series of papers to locate a specialized result establishing (non-)identifiability for a specific query, especially when the result is as complex as Theorem 1.
- Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to instantiate inverse probability weighting. Their validity presumably hinges on the conditioning sets being Markov blankets for R(i) in the mixed graphs the author uses. Richardson (2003, 2009) may be relevant.

Other Comments
- p. 4, l. 214: R^part is a partition of R, not “a set containing partitions.” The condition should be that Rx and Ry belong to distinct elements of the partition.
- Language is sometimes too casual (e.g., “oftentimes”).
- p. 4, l. 213: Use \leftrightarrow rather than <-->.
- Is Garcia (2013) actually wrong, or does it use a different framework?
- Notation for graphs constructed by severing edges is used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4, but never defined.
- Vo and Vm are used before they are defined (apparently unnecessarily).

References
- Richardson, T. S. (2003). Markov properties for acyclic directed mixed graphs. Scandinavian Journal of Statistics, 30(1), 145–157.
- Richardson, T. S. (2009). A factorization criterion for acyclic directed mixed graphs. In UAI 25.

Overall: An important problem and a useful approach, but the presentation and notation significantly hinder readability and impact.
