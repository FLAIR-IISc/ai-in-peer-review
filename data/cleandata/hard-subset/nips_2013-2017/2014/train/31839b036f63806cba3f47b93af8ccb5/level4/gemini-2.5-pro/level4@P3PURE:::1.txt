This paper addresses the problem of identifiability in the presence of various mechanisms for generating missing data, utilizing a framework introduced by Mohan et al. The paper improves upon some existing results and provides several insightful examples, such as the identifiability of causal distributions even when conditional distributions are unavailable.

**Quality**

The technical quality of the paper appears to be high. The results are complex but seem correct. For instance, results like Theorem 3 are clear and interpretable. However, some results appear to be incremental improvements on existing work and, from my perspective, do not contribute significantly to a broader understanding of the underlying principles.

Section 3 seems disconnected from the main narrative of the paper, and its primary result, Theorem 1, is particularly difficult to comprehend. This result appears to be closely related to the Markov blanket results in Richardson (2009). The quality of the presentation improves in the later sections; Section 5 provides clear answers to specific problems, and Section 6 presents interesting results on causal queries.

**Clarity**

Clarity is the paper's main weakness. First, the paper attempts to cover too much material for a conference publication, a point underscored by the dense text and a 13-page technical appendix. The significance of some of this material is not immediately apparent. As a result, the article lacks a clear narrative flow and is difficult to follow. The work would likely be better suited for a longer, more comprehensive journal publication.

While acknowledging the difficulty of devising notation for numerous quantities, the current system could be substantially improved. The repeated use of the capital letter 'V' to denote different objects places an unnecessary burden on the reader, and in some places, the subscripts are illegible. Furthermore, the convention where Vi represents a single vertex while Vo represents a set of vertices is particularly confusing. The set equation in Corollary 1 serves as a particularly striking example of the presentation issues.

**Originality and Significance**

The contributions in Sections 5 and 6 are clear, interesting, and potentially significant. The paper addresses an important problem and presents results that appear both genuinely interesting and useful.

The work could be strengthened by taking a step back to analyze the general case, with the goal of establishing a complete criterion for identifiability within this framework. This would be analogous to the complete criteria that have been developed for causal effect identification in settings without missing data. It is hard to envision a practitioner searching through a series of papers like this one to find the specific result that establishes the (non-)identifiability of their causal query, particularly if that result is as complex as Theorem 1.

Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to be a form of inverse probability weighting. Presumably, these results hold because the conditioning sets are Markov blankets for R(i) in the mixed graphs employed by the authors. The work of Richardson (2003, 2009) may be relevant here.

**Other Comments**

*   On page 4, line 214, R^part is a partition of R, not "a set containing partitions". The condition on that line should state that Rx and Ry "belong to distinct elements of the partition".
*   The language is occasionally informal (e.g., "oftentimes").
*   On page 4, line 213, please use `\leftrightarrow` instead of `<-->`.
*   The paper should clarify whether the result from Garcia (2013) is incorrect or simply derived within a different framework.
*   The notation for graphs constructed by severing edges, used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4, is not defined.
*   The variables Vo and Vm are used (apparently unnecessarily) in the sentence immediately preceding their definitions.

**Summary**

This paper addresses an important problem with a useful approach, but its contributions are marred by poor presentation and confusing notation.
