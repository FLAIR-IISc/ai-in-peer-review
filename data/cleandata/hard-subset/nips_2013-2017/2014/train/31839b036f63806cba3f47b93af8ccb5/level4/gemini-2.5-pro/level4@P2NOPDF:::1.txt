This paper investigates the problem of identifiability for statistical and causal queries in the presence of various missing data mechanisms. Building upon the framework of Mohan et al., the authors extend existing results and provide several illustrative examples, including the identifiability of causal distributions even when certain conditional distributions are unavailable.

**Quality and Significance**

The paper addresses an important and challenging problem. The technical quality of the work is high, and the presented results appear to be correct. The contributions in Sections 5 and 6 are particularly clear, interesting, and potentially significant. Results such as Theorem 3 are both interpretable and insightful, and the investigation into the identifiability of causal queries is a valuable contribution.

However, the overall impact is diminished by a few factors. While technically sound, some of the results feel like incremental improvements on existing work. The paper would be strengthened if these results were more clearly situated within a broader conceptual framework, explaining how they contribute to a more general understanding of identifiability.

Furthermore, Section 3 seems disconnected from the rest of the paper's narrative. Its main result, Theorem 1, is exceptionally complex and difficult to comprehend, which limits its practical utility. In contrast, the later sections provide much clearer answers to well-posed questions.

**Clarity and Presentation**

Clarity is the primary weakness of this manuscript. The paper attempts to cover a substantial amount of material, which, combined with a dense writing style, makes the overall argument difficult to follow. The inclusion of a 13-page technical appendix suggests that the content might be better suited for a longer journal article, where the ideas could be developed more thoroughly and paced more appropriately for the reader.

The notation is a significant barrier to comprehension. The capital letter 'V' is overloaded to represent multiple distinct objects (e.g., vertices, sets of vertices), which forces the reader to constantly re-evaluate its meaning based on context. The distinction between Vi (a vertex) and Vo (a set of vertices) is particularly confusing. In some places, the density of subscripts makes them illegible. The complex set equation in Corollary 1 is an extreme example of these presentational issues, rendering it nearly incomprehensible.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

The authors are encouraged to consider a higher-level synthesis of their results. A significant contribution would be to work towards a complete, general criterion for identifiability within this framework, analogous to the established criteria for causal effects in settings without missing data (e.g., do-calculus). As it stands, it is difficult to imagine a practitioner navigating a series of complex, specific theorems to determine the identifiability of their particular query. A more unified theory would be far more impactful.

Additionally, the mechanisms behind Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to be a form of inverse probability weighting. It is likely that these results hold because the conditioning sets are Markov blankets for the missingness indicators R(i) in the corresponding mixed graphs. The authors should consider discussing this connection and citing relevant work on Markov properties in acyclic directed mixed graphs, such as Richardson (2003, 2009).

**Minor Comments**

*   **p. 4, l. 214:** The text describes R^part as "a set containing partitions," but it appears to be a partition of R itself. Consequently, the condition on the same line should state that Rx and Ry "belong to distinct elements of the partition."
*   **p. 4, l. 213:** Please use the standard LaTeX command `\leftrightarrow` for the bi-directed edge symbol instead of `<-->`.
*   The notation for graphs constructed by severing edges, which is used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4, is not defined anywhere in the text.
*   The terms Vo and Vm are used in the sentence preceding their definitions.
*   Regarding the reference to Garcia (2013): Is the cited work incorrect, or does it simply use a different framework? Clarification would be helpful.
*   The language is occasionally informal (e.g., "oftentimes"). A more formal academic tone would be appropriate throughout the paper.
