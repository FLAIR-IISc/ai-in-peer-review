This paper presents novel and rigorous necessary and sufficient conditions for identifying causal effects from observational data when faced with missing observations. The work extends existing approaches that incorporate the missing data mechanism directly into the causal diagram, moving beyond simply modeling it as an external process. This integration allows for a more nuanced and powerful analysis of identifiability.

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, presenting complex theoretical results in an accessible manner. While building upon a foundation of prior work utilizing causal diagrams to address missing data, the additional insights provided represent a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference. Specifically, the derived conditions offer a substantial improvement in understanding *when* causal queries can be reliably answered despite incomplete data, and crucially, *when they cannot*. 

The key strength of this work lies in its theoretical completeness. Previous approaches often relied on assumptions regarding the missingness mechanism or offered only sufficient, rather than necessary, conditions for identification. This paper addresses these limitations by providing a comprehensive framework grounded in d-separation and the graphical representation of causal relationships. The authors meticulously articulate the conditions under which identification is possible, and equally importantly, precisely delineate the scenarios where identification fails. This negative result – knowing when an effect *cannot* be estimated – is often overlooked but extremely valuable in practice.

The paper’s contribution is significant because missing data is pervasive in real-world applications. Being able to confidently assess the identifiability of causal effects under missingness is crucial for avoiding misleading conclusions and guiding data collection efforts. However, a few minor points could be further addressed. While the theoretical results are strong, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical challenges in *applying* these conditions in complex, real-world causal diagrams. Determining whether the required conditions hold often necessitates strong assumptions about the underlying causal structure, which may be difficult to verify.  Further elaboration on how sensitivity analysis could be used to assess the robustness of the identification results given potential model misspecification would be beneficial.

Furthermore, while the connection to existing work on missing data is well-established, a slightly more thorough comparison to related approaches dealing with missing not at random (MNAR) data, perhaps highlighting how this graphical approach differs in its assumptions and implications, could strengthen the paper. For example, contrasting the approach with parametric modeling of the missingness mechanism could be instructive.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the area of causal inference with missing data. The clarity of presentation, combined with the completeness of the results, make it a valuable addition to the literature. Despite the minor suggestions for improvement, I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance and has the potential to influence future research in this important area.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
