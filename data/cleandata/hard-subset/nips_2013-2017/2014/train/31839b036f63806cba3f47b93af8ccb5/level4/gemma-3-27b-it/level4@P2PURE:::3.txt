This is an interesting paper addressing the problem of distribution recovery given knowledge of the generative process and the mechanism leading to missing data.
The paper generalizes the missingness process considered in previous work, such as that of Mohan et al., and derives a novel identification formula as a result. Furthermore, the authors investigate the identification of causal queries, demonstrating identifiability even when the observational joint distribution is not. The paper is generally well-written, though some minor issues and potential areas for clarification are noted below.

MAIN COMMENTS:

(1) The results presented rely on knowledge of the process governing the censoring of observations. Is this level of knowledge commonly available in typical machine learning applications? Providing more detail regarding Figure 1 within the main text would be beneficial; this could be achieved by reducing the figure’s size.

(2) Section 2 should explicitly state that the missing data processes assume knowledge of the full population. Even in cases where Vo = emptyset and Rv = 1 for all V in V_m (meaning no variables are observed for a given individual), the existence of that individual is still assumed. This limits applicability to missing data problems involving estimation of population size, such as estimating the number of animals in a population.

(3) The term “mgraph” appears to be used ambiguously. Specifically, the graph sometimes includes the V* variables (as in Figure 1), while at other times it does not (as in Figure 2). If the V* variables are being intentionally omitted in certain graphs, this should be explicitly explained.

(4) The paper restricts mgraphs to prevent R variables from being parents of variables in V or U. Given the authors treat R variables as continuous quantities rather than simple indicators, they should justify this restriction or clarify whether it is primarily for convenience.

(5) The term “neighbor,” which appears at the bottom of page 4, is not defined.

(6) On page 8, the first paragraph refers to “rectification” of the observation by Garcia et al. The authors’ definition of recoverability assumes positivity; does Garcia et al. also assume positivity? If not, their work may require a stronger condition.

(7) On page 8, the second paragraph states that “Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption.” This is overly broad. The paper should explain why maximum likelihood cannot be applied to the missingness processes described herein.

MINOR COMMENTS:

P.1 line 33: “can bias outcomes” – rephrase for clarity.
P.2 Figure 1: Add clarifying qualifications.
P.2 Example 1 on p.3 line 158: The equation P(Y |X,Z,R_y) = P(X,Z) should be P(Y|X,Z).
P.3 line 168: “Since Y || R | X,Z” should be “Y || R_y | X,Z”.
P.3 line 214: Condition (ii) R^{part} ={R^{(1)},R^{(2)}, \ldots , R^N} should be R^{(N)}.
P.3 line 215: “R^j respectively” should be “R^{(j)}”.
P.4 Corollary 1: The statement does not account for partially observed variables.
P.6 Footnote 2: Please provide a reference for the variable-level definition.

Overall, this is an interesting and technically sound paper building on earlier work. The breadth of potential application is not entirely clear, but the technical arguments are well-developed.
