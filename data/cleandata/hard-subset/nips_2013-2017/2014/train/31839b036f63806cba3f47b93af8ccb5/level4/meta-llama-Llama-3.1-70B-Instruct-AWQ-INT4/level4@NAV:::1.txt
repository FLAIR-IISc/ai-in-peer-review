The paper addresses the issue of identifiability in the presence of various mechanisms for missing data, utilizing a framework established by Mohan et al. It builds upon existing results and provides insightful examples, such as the identifiability of causal distributions even when conditional distributions are not available.

Quality
The technical quality of the paper appears to be high, with complex yet seemingly accurate results. Theorem 3, for instance, presents a clear and interpretable outcome. However, some results represent incremental improvements over existing ones, without contributing to a broader understanding of the subject matter. 
Section 3 appears to be unrelated to the rest of the paper, with Theorem 1 being difficult to comprehend due to its close relation to the Markov blanket results in Richardson (2009). The quality improves in later sections, with Section 5 providing clear answers and Section 6 offering interesting results on causal queries.

Clarity
The paper's primary weakness lies in its clarity. The sheer amount of material presented, combined with the dense text and the 13-page technical appendix, suggests that it may be more suitable for a journal paper. The article's flow is disrupted, making it challenging to follow. 
Improving notation would enhance readability. The repeated use of the capital 'V' to denote different objects causes confusion, while illegible subscripts and the inconsistent use of Vi as a vertex and Vo as a set of vertices are particularly perplexing. The set equation in Corollary 1 exemplifies poor presentation.

Originality and Significance
Sections 5 and 6 are clear, interesting, and potentially significant, as they tackle an important problem and contain genuinely interesting and useful results. 
A more comprehensive approach to understanding the general case and developing a complete criterion for identifiability within this framework would be beneficial. The complexity of results like Theorem 1 makes it difficult to imagine a statistician navigating through multiple papers to find specific results on (non)-identifiability. 
Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 seem to be related to inverse probability weighting, possibly due to the conditioning sets being Markov blankets for R(i) in the mixed graphs used. Richardson (2003) and (2009) may be relevant.

Language is sometimes informal, such as the use of 'oftentimes' (p4, l213). The symbol \leftrightarrow should be used instead of <-->.
