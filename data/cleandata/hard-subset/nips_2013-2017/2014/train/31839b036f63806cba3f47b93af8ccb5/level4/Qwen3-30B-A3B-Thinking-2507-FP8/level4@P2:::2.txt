

This paper makes a significant contribution to the analysis of causal inference under missing data mechanisms by extending the graphical framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. (2013). The authors present novel necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing entries, addressing a critical gap in the literature. Specifically, they demonstrate that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations P(y,x) and P(y|x) are not recoverable—a counterintuitive insight that challenges conventional wisdom about the relationship between probabilistic and causal recoverability.

The paper builds upon Mohan et al.'s foundational work by introducing general conditions for recovering joint and conditional distributions (P(y,x) and P(y|x)), as well as causal queries. The authors extend the sequential factorization theorem to handle cases where missingness mechanisms interact with one another (e.g., longitudinal studies with dependencies between missingness indicators), and they develop new graphical criteria for non-recoverability. Their key theoretical contribution is Theorem 4, which establishes conditions for recovering causal effects even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable—a scenario that would typically render probabilistic queries unrecoverable.

The paper is exceptionally well-structured and clearly written, with each section building logically upon the previous one. The authors provide detailed examples (e.g., Example 3) that illustrate their theoretical results in concrete settings, making complex concepts accessible to readers. The notation is consistent and well-motivated, and the appendix contains complete proofs for all theoretical results, enhancing the paper's technical rigor.

Regarding quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The authors carefully distinguish between probabilistic and causal recoverability, providing precise graphical conditions that are both necessary and sufficient. The treatment of attrition as a special case of missing data (Section 7) demonstrates practical relevance and shows how their framework can be applied to real-world problems in longitudinal studies. The paper avoids overclaiming by carefully delineating the boundaries of their results and acknowledging limitations.

The clarity of the paper is outstanding. The authors provide excellent intuitive explanations alongside formal definitions, and the figures (particularly Figures 1-4) effectively illustrate key concepts. The paper is sufficiently detailed for an expert reader to reproduce the results, and the writing avoids unnecessary jargon while maintaining technical precision. The authors also provide clear algorithmic procedures for recovering queries, which enhances the paper's utility.

In terms of originality, the paper makes several important advances over prior work. The key insight that causal queries may be recoverable when probabilistic queries are not represents a substantial theoretical contribution. The authors also provide the first formal non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions, which fills a significant gap in the literature. While Mohan et al. (2013) established conditions for recoverability under certain assumptions, this paper generalizes those results to handle more complex missingness mechanisms and provides necessary conditions for non-recoverability—both of which are novel contributions.

The significance of this work is substantial. Missing data is a pervasive challenge across scientific disciplines, and the paper provides a systematic framework for determining when causal inferences can be made from incomplete datasets. The authors' application to attrition problems (Section 7) is particularly compelling, as attrition is a common issue in longitudinal studies and randomized trials. Their results resolve a longstanding confusion in the literature (e.g., Garcia 2013) regarding necessary conditions for causal effect recovery under attrition. The paper's framework has clear potential for practical application in fields ranging from clinical trials to social sciences.

Strengths:
- Novel theoretical insights connecting probabilistic and causal recoverability
- Comprehensive treatment of missingness mechanisms beyond traditional MCAR/MAR assumptions
- Rigorous proofs and clear algorithmic procedures
- Practical application to attrition problems with concrete examples
- Excellent organization and writing that makes complex concepts accessible

Weaknesses:
- The paper could benefit from more extensive empirical validation (though theoretical work in this domain often prioritizes formal proofs over empirical demonstrations)
- The connection to practical implementation challenges (e.g., computational complexity) could be elaborated
- While the attrition application is compelling, the paper could more explicitly discuss how their framework compares to existing missing data methods (e.g., multiple imputation, inverse probability weighting)

Pro/Con for Acceptance:
Pro: This paper represents a major theoretical advance in understanding causal inference under missing data. The insights are novel, the theory is sound, and the writing is exemplary. The results will be of significant interest to the causal inference and machine learning communities.

Con: While the theoretical contribution is strong, the paper would be even stronger with empirical validation or more detailed discussion of implementation challenges.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a comprehensive graphical framework for determining recoverability of causal queries under missing data. The authors' key insight—that causal effects can sometimes be recovered even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not recoverable—represents a fundamental advance with important practical implications. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a critical gap in the literature. I strongly recommend acceptance for NIPS.
