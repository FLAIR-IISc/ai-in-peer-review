This paper investigates the challenging problem of identifiability with missing data, building upon the framework introduced by Mohan and colleagues. The authors improve upon existing results and provide insightful examples – notably demonstrating identifiability of causal distributions even when complete conditional distributions are unavailable.

**Quality**

The technical quality of the paper is generally high; the results are complex but appear to be correct. Theorem 3, for instance, is clearly stated and readily interpretable. However, some contributions feel incremental, lacking a cohesive narrative or broader theoretical advancement. 

Section 3 feels somewhat disconnected from the rest of the paper. Its central result, Theorem 1, is difficult to grasp and bears a strong resemblance to Markov blanket results found in Richardson (2009). The paper’s quality improves significantly in later sections. Section 5 offers clear and valuable answers, while Section 6 presents interesting findings regarding causal queries.

**Clarity**

Clarity is the paper's primary weakness. The scope of the material feels excessive for a conference format, contributing to a dense text and necessitating a lengthy 13-page technical appendix. The paper lacks a smooth, logical flow, hindering comprehension – it might be better suited as a more expansive journal publication.

While finding concise notation for numerous quantities is inherently difficult, the current notation could be significantly improved. The repeated use of ‘V’ to represent different objects creates considerable confusion, and in several instances, subscripts become illegible. The distinction between *Vi* (a vertex) and *Vo* (a set of vertices) is particularly problematic. The set equation in Corollary 1 serves as a prime example of unclear presentation.

**Originality and Significance**

Sections 5 and 6 stand out as clear, interesting, and potentially significant contributions. The paper addresses an important problem and contains results that appear genuinely useful. 

However, a more overarching perspective would be beneficial. Pursuing a comprehensive criterion for identifiability within this framework – analogous to the work on causal effect identification without missingness – would elevate the paper’s impact. Currently, it’s difficult to envision a practitioner systematically navigating a series of papers to determine the identifiability of a specific causal effect, especially given the complexity of results like Theorem 1.

Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to leverage inverse probability weighting. The underlying reason for their success likely relates to the conditioning sets functioning as Markov blankets for *R(i)* within the employed mixed graphs. Richardson (2003, 2009) may offer relevant insights in this regard.

**Other Comments**

*   The language occasionally adopts a casual tone (e.g., “oftentimes”).
*   p4 l213: Use  <binary data, 1 bytes><binary data, 1 bytes><binary data, 1 bytes> rather than <binary data, 1 bytes><binary data, 1 bytes><binary data, 1 bytes>.
*   Regarding Garcia (2013), it's unclear whether the work is fundamentally incorrect or operates within a different framework. This distinction should be clarified.
*   Definition 4 and Theorem 4 utilize notation for graphs constructed by edge severing, but this notation is not defined.
*   *Vo* and *Vm* are used before their definitions, and their introduction appears unnecessary.

In summary, this paper tackles an important problem with a potentially valuable approach, but its presentation and notation require significant improvement to fully realize its impact.
