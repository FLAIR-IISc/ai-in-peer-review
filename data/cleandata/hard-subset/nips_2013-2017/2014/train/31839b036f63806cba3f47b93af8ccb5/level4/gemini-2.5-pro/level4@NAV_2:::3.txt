This paper addresses the problem of determining whether a probabilistic or causal query can be consistently estimated from data with missing values, given a graphical model of the missingness process. Building on the framework of 'missingness graphs' (m-graphs) from Mohan et al. [2013], the authors present several key extensions. They introduce a more general sequential factorization theorem to handle dependencies between missingness mechanisms, provide a new theorem for recoverability when factorization fails, and establish novel graphical criteria for non-recoverability. A central contribution is the demonstration that causal queries, such as P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the corresponding joint or conditional distributions are not. The authors apply these theoretical results to the specific problem of attrition in experimental studies.

The paper provides a significant and technically sound extension to an important line of work on missing data. The generalization of the sequential factorization theorem is a valuable contribution, expanding the applicability of the m-graph framework to a wider class of problems. The result showing that causal queries can be identified even when the full joint distribution is not is particularly insightful and novel.

While the paper is generally well-written, several points could be clarified to improve its quality and impact.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Practical Applicability:** The proposed methods rely on detailed knowledge of the m-graph, which encodes the causal process generating the missing data. The authors should discuss the practicality of this assumption. How would a practitioner specify this graph in typical machine learning applications? Providing more motivation or examples for the assumed graph structures would strengthen the paper's significance. It would also be helpful to include more of the detailed explanation for Figure 1 in the main paper, as it serves as a motivating example.

2.  **Scope of Missingness Process:** The framework appears to assume that the existence of every individual in the population is known, even if all their variables are missing (e.g., if R_v=1 for all v in V_m for a given record). It would be helpful to explicitly state this assumption and clarify that the work does not cover settings where the population size itself is unknown (e.g., capture-recapture problems).

3.  **Clarity of 'm-graph' Definition:** The term 'm-graph' seems to be used inconsistently. For instance, Figure 1 includes the proxy variables V*, whereas other figures (e.g., Figure 2) do not. Please clarify if V* nodes are simply omitted for readability in some diagrams and confirm the canonical definition of an m-graph used throughout the paper.

4.  **Model Justification:** The paper states a restriction that R variables cannot be parents of variables in V or U. While this may be for technical convenience, the R variables are treated as representing real-world mechanisms. Could the authors provide a justification for this structural constraint or discuss the implications of relaxing it?

5.  **Undefined Terminology:** The term 'neighbor' is used in Theorem 2 and elsewhere (e.g., bottom of p.4) but is not formally defined. Please provide a precise definition (e.g., are two nodes neighbors if they are adjacent?).

6.  **Comparison to Garcia [2013]:** On page 8, the paper claims to rectify a finding from Garcia [2013]. However, recoverability as defined here relies on a positivity assumption. Does the work by Garcia make the same assumption? If not, their potentially stronger conditions for non-recoverability might be justified under different assumptions. A more detailed comparison of the underlying assumptions is needed to substantiate this claim.

7.  **Discussion of Maximum Likelihood:** The related work section states that the "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption." This statement is rather general. It would be more informative to discuss why standard ML estimation is insufficient for the Missing Not At Random (MNAR) models considered in this paper, which would better motivate the need for the proposed graphical criteria and recovery algorithms.

**Minor Comments:**

*   p.1, line 33: "can bias outcomes" could be more specific.
*   p.2, figure 1 caption: "Qualifications" appears to be a typo.
*   p.3, Example 1, line 158: The equation should likely be `P(Y|X,Z,Ry) = P(Y|X,Z)`.
*   p.3, line 168: The d-separation statement should specify the R variable, i.e., `Y ⊥⊥ Ry | X,Z`.
*   p.3, line 214: "R^N" should be `R^{(N)}`.
*   p.3, line 215: "R^j" should be `R^{(j)}`.
*   p.4, Corollary 1: "does not contain a partially observed variables" should be "does not contain a partially observed variable".
*   The paper would benefit from a final proofreading pass to catch minor typographical errors.

In summary, this is a strong, technical paper with interesting theoretical contributions that advance the graphical modeling approach to missing data. Addressing the points on clarity and practical motivation would further improve the work and broaden its impact.
