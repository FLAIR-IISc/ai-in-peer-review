The paper addresses identifiability under a range of missing-data mechanisms within the framework introduced by Mohan et al. It strengthens some existing results and provides useful examples (e.g., identifiability of causal distributions even when certain conditional distributions are unavailable).

Quality
The technical quality appears high. Although the arguments are intricate, they seem correct, and results such as Theorem 3 are clear and interpretable. Some contributions are incremental extensions of prior work and do not yet cohere into a broader unifying view. Section 3 feels largely orthogonal to the rest of the paper, and its main result (Theorem 1) is difficult to digest; it seems closely related to Markov blanket results in Richardson (2009). The exposition improves later: Section 5 offers clear, concrete answers, and Section 6 presents interesting results on causal queries.

Clarity
Clarity is the main weakness. The paper likely contains too much material for a conference format, as suggested by the dense prose and a 13-page technical appendix. The narrative flow is uneven, making the paper hard to follow; this might be better suited to a longer journal article with a more deliberate structure.

The notation can also be improved. Overloading the capital V to denote multiple objects makes reading laborious, and subscripts are at times illegible. It is particularly confusing that Vi denotes a vertex while Vo denotes a set of vertices. The set equation in Corollary 1 exemplifies the presentation issues. Overall, clearer, more consistent notation would substantially improve readability.

Originality and Significance
Sections 5 and 6 are clear, interesting, and potentially significant. The paper tackles an important problem and contains results that are both nontrivial and useful. That said, it would help to step back and work toward a general, preferably complete, identifiability criterion within this framework, analogous to the completeness results for causal effects without missingness. In practice, it is hard to expect a practitioner to search through multiple papers to locate the precise (non-)identifiability result for a given causal query, especially when key statements (e.g., Theorem 1) are highly complex.

Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 read as a form of inverse probability weighting. Their validity likely hinges on the conditioning sets serving as Markov blankets for R(i) in the mixed graphs considered. Related insights from Richardson (2003, 2009) may be directly relevant and could help unify the presentation.

Other Comments
- p4, l214: R^part is a partition of R, not “a set containing partitions.” The condition should state that Rx and Ry belong to distinct elements (blocks) of the partition.
- The prose can be overly informal in places (e.g., “oftentimes”).
- p4, l213: Use ↔ rather than <-->.
- Clarify whether Garcia (2013) is actually incorrect or simply operates under a different framework.
- A notation for graphs constructed by severing edges is used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4 but is not defined; please define it when first introduced.
- Vo and Vm are used before they are defined (apparently unnecessarily); introduce all notation before use.

References
- Richardson, T.S. (2003). Markov properties for acyclic directed mixed graphs. Scandinavian Journal of Statistics, 30(1), 145–157.
- Richardson, T.S. (2009). A factorization criterion for acyclic directed mixed graphs. Proceedings of UAI 25.

Overall assessment: An important problem and a promising approach, with several valuable results, but the contribution is undermined by presentation and notation issues. Strengthening the exposition and unifying the results around a clearer general criterion would substantially increase impact.
