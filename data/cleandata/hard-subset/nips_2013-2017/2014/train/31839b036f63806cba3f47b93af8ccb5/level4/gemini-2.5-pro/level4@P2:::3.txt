### Review Summary

This paper extends the graphical modeling framework of Mohan et al. [2013] for analyzing recoverability of queries from data with missing values. The authors use "missingness graphs" (m-graphs) to represent the causal process generating the data and the process leading to missingness. The main contributions are: (1) a generalized sequential factorization theorem that handles dependencies between missingness mechanisms, (2) novel graphical conditions for recovering joint distributions in cases where factorization fails, (3) new graphical criteria for establishing non-recoverability, and (4) a significant result showing that causal queries, such as P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the corresponding joint or conditional distributions are not. The theory is then applied to the problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

### Quality and Originality

The paper makes a solid and original contribution to the growing literature on causal inference with missing data. The work is a non-trivial extension of previous results, tackling more complex and realistic scenarios where missingness indicators can be interdependent. The theoretical results appear to be technically sound, and the proofs are provided in the appendix.

A key and insightful result is that causal queries can sometimes be recovered even when the full joint distribution is not. This finding, which stems from the logic of do-calculus and d*-separation, highlights the distinct nature of causal and probabilistic queries and could have significant implications for experimental design and analysis where missingness is a concern. The introduction of formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability is also a welcome and novel contribution, providing researchers with tools to determine when an estimate is impossible under a given set of assumptions.

### Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and the arguments are clearly presented. The use of examples to illustrate the application of new theorems is effective. However, there are several areas where clarity could be improved.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel Theoretical Extensions:** The paper significantly generalizes the conditions for recoverability beyond the scope of Mohan et al. [2013], particularly by allowing for interactions between missingness mechanisms.
2.  **Causal Query Recoverability:** The demonstration that P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable when P(y,x) is not is a powerful and important result. It separates the problem of causal identification from that of full probabilistic specification in the context of missing data.
3.  **Non-recoverability Criteria:** The paper provides the first formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3), which is a valuable contribution for practitioners to understand the limits of their models.
4.  **Application to Attrition:** The application of the general theory to the specific, practical problem of attrition provides a clear and useful case study.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

1.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** A primary concern is the strong assumption that the full causal graph of the missingness process (the m-graph) is known. Could the authors comment on the applicability of these methods in typical machine learning settings where this graph may not be available a priori? How sensitive are the results to misspecification of the m-graph?
2.  **Scope of Missingness:** The methodology appears to assume that the existence of all individuals in the population is known, even if all their variables are missing (e.g., if V_o is empty and R_v=1 for all v). It would be helpful to explicitly state this assumption and clarify that the framework does not address problems like population size estimation where the existence of observational units is itself unknown.
3.  **Justification of Model Constraints:** The framework restricts R variables from being parents of variables in V or U. While this may be for technical convenience, it seems plausible that the mechanism causing missingness (e.g., a patient's decision to drop out of a study) could directly influence future substantive variables (e.g., subsequent health outcomes). Could the authors justify this structural constraint or discuss the implications of relaxing it?
4.  **Comparison with Prior Work:** On page 8, the authors state their results "rectify prevailing opinion" from Garcia [2013]. However, recoverability as defined here relies on a positivity assumption (P(V, R=0) > 0). Does the work by Garcia et al. make the same assumption? If not, their stronger conditions for non-recoverability might be justified in a non-positive setting. A more detailed comparison of the underlying assumptions would strengthen this claim.
5.  **Clarity on Maximum Likelihood:** The discussion of related work states, "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption." This is overly vague. It would be more precise to clarify why standard ML estimation, which can be applied to any fully specified probabilistic model, is not a straightforward solution for the MNAR models presented here (assuming the m-graph is part of the model specification).

### Minor Comments and Typos

*   The term **'neighbor'**, used on page 4 and elsewhere, should be formally defined (e.g., as two nodes connected by an edge).
*   **Inconsistent Graph Depictions:** There appears to be an inconsistency in the depiction of m-graphs. Figure 1 includes the proxy variables V*, while subsequent figures (e.g., Figure 2) omit them. Please clarify if V* variables are simply omitted for visual simplicity in the later figures.
*   p. 1, l. 33: "can bias outcomes" -> "can bias results" or "can introduce bias".
*   p. 2, Figure 1 caption: "Qualifications" -> "Qualification".
*   p. 3, l. 158 (Example 1): `P(Y|X,Z,R_y) = P(X,Z)`. The right-hand side should be `P(Y|X,Z)`.
*   p. 3, l. 168: `Y || R | X,Z`. For clarity, this should probably be `Y || R_y | X,Z`.
*   p. 3, l. 214: `R^N` should be `R^{(N)}`.
*   p. 3, l. 215: `R^j` should be `R^{(j)}`.
*   p. 4, Corollary 1: "partially observed variables" -> "partially observed variable".
