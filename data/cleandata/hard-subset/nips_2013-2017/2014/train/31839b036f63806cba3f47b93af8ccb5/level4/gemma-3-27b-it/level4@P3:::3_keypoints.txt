- Paper tackles recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries using models of the missingness process.  
- Extends Mohan et al. (2013) to give more general recovery conditions, showing causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic relationships are not.  
- Uses Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) and refines sequential factorization to provide new graphical criteria and non-recoverability tests.  
- Key result: causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be identified despite non-d-separability between Y and its missingness mechanism.  
- Demonstrates practical relevance through application to attrition problems.  
- Strength — Novelty: significant extension showing causal recoverability in previously intractable cases.  
- Strength — Theoretical soundness: builds on prior work with rigorous theorems and definitions.  
- Strength — Practical relevance: addresses common observational study challenge (attrition).  
- Strength — Clear formalization: m-graphs provide a formal representation of missingness and dependencies.  
- Weakness — Assumes knowledge of the missingness process (m-graph), limiting practical applicability and generality.  
- Weakness — Assumes full population is known even for entirely missing individuals, restricting use cases like population estimation.  
- Weakness — Inconsistent notation: inclusion/omission of V* variables in m-graphs (Figures 1 vs. 2) creates ambiguity.  
- Weakness — Restriction that R variables cannot parent V or U is unjustified and needs rationale or discussion of relaxation.  
- Weakness — Related-work discussion (Maximum Likelihood on p.8) is vague and needs clarification on applicability.  
- Weakness — Multiple minor typographical errors and inconsistencies reduce readability.  
- Suggestion — Section 2: explicitly state and discuss limitations of assuming the full population is known.  
- Suggestion — Clarify consistent m-graph representation, specifying when V* variables are included or omitted.  
- Suggestion — Justify or discuss implications of forbidding R variables as parents of V or U.  
- Suggestion — When comparing to Garcia (2013), state whether Garcia assumes positivity and clarify assumption differences.  
- Suggestion — Reduce Figure 1 size and add more explanatory detail in the main text for accessibility.  
- Suggestion — Define “neighbor” before first use (p.4) and correct listed typos (e.g., “can introduce bias”, figure qualifications, several formula and notation fixes).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound contribution with practical relevance and rigorous formalization.  
- Arguments against acceptance: heavy reliance on accurately specified m-graphs and remaining ambiguities/assumption limitations.  
- Overall recommendation: solid paper worthy of acceptance with minor revisions to clarify assumptions, notation, and justifications.
