- Tackles identifiability with multiple missingness mechanisms using the m-graph framework, strengthening prior results and providing instructive examples (e.g., causal effects identifiable even when conditional distributions are not recoverable).  
- Technical work appears solid and arguments seem correct; Theorem 3 is crisp and interpretable.  
- Some contributions feel incremental and lack a cohesive, unifying picture; Section 3 is somewhat detached from the rest.  
- Theorem 1 is difficult to parse and appears closely related to Markov blanket–type results (cf. Richardson, 2009).  
- Presentation tightens in later sections: Section 5 gives clear, actionable criteria and Section 6 has engaging results on causal queries.  
- Main weakness is clarity: too much content for a conference submission, dense exposition, and an oversized technical appendix—better suited to a journal.  
- Notation is overloaded and confusing (V used for multiple roles; Vi vs Vo ambiguous; some subscripts hard to parse), with Corollary 1 exemplifying presentation issues.  
- Sections 5–6 are original, interesting, and potentially impactful, but the work would benefit from a general identifiability criterion for the framework.  
- Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 resemble inverse probability weighting; their validity seems tied to conditioning sets being Markov blankets for R(i) in the mixed graphs.  
- Richardson (2003, 2009) appears relevant and should be connected to the results.  
- Specific corrections: p4 l214 — R^part is a partition of R; state that Rx and Ry “belong to distinct parts of the partition.”  
- Style and clarity edits: avoid informal prose (“oftentimes”); use ↔ rather than <--> (p4 l213); clarify whether Garcia (2013) is contradicted or simply uses a different modeling framework.  
- Missing/unclear definitions: notation for graphs obtained by severing edges (Definition 4, Theorem 4) is not defined; Vo and Vm are referenced before definition.  
- Overall: important problem and useful methodology, but weakened by presentation and notation choices.
