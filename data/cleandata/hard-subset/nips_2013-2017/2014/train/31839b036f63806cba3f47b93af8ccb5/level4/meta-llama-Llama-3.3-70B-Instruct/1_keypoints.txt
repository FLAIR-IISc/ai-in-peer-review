Paper studies identifiability with missing data, builds on Mohan et al., improves some results and gives examples (e.g., causal distributions identifiable even when conditional distributions are not).  
Technical quality is high with complex but seemingly correct results; Theorem 3 is clear, but some contributions are incremental and Section 3 feels disconnected.  
Theorem 1 is hard to understand and resembles Richardson (2009) Markov-blanket-type results; later sections (5 and 6) are stronger.  
Clarity is a major weakness: dense presentation, excessive material for a conference paper, and a 13-page technical appendix—better suited for a journal.  
Notation and presentation are confusing (reuse of capital V, illegible subscripts, Vi vs Vo ambiguity), and Corollary 1 has poor set-equation presentation.  
Sections 5 and 6 contain clear, interesting, and potentially significant results addressing an important problem.  
Recommend developing a general identifiability criterion for the framework instead of many fragmented, hard-to-find results.  
Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to use inverse-probability-weighting-like arguments with conditioning sets as Markov blankets for R(i); Richardson (2003, 2009) may be relevant.  
Correction: R^part is a partition of R, so the condition should state Rx and Ry belong to distinct partition elements.  
Style/formalism issues: informal language (e.g., "oftentimes") and notation suggestions (use ↔ instead of <--> on p.4 line 213).  
Unclear whether Garcia (2013) is incorrect or operates in a different framework.  
Definitions/notation missing: the operation for severing edges in graphs is used but not defined; Vo and Vm are introduced before definition.  
Overall judgment: tackles an important problem with useful approaches but is marred by poor presentation and confusing notation.
