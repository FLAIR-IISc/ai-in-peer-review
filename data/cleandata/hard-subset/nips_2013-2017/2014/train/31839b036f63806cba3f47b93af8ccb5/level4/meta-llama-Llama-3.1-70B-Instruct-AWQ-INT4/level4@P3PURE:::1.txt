The paper addresses the problem of identifiability in the presence of various mechanisms for missing data, using a framework introduced by Mohan and others. The authors improve upon some existing results and provide nice examples, such as the identifiability of causal distributions even when conditional distributions are unavailable.

Quality
The technical quality of the paper appears to be high, with involved but seemingly correct results. Theorem 3, for instance, is clear and interpretable. However, some results are incremental improvements upon existing ones and do not contribute to a broader understanding of the topic. Section 3 seems orthogonal to the rest of the paper, and its main result, Theorem 1, is difficult to comprehend. This result appears to be closely related to the Markov blanket results in Richardson (2009). The quality improves in later sections, with Section 5 providing clear answers and Section 6 offering interesting results about causal queries.

Clarity
The paper's main weakness lies in its clarity. The text is dense, and the 13-page technical appendix suggests that there may be too much material for a conference paper. The article lacks a clear flow, making it challenging to follow. This issue could be mitigated by expanding the paper into a longer, more considered journal article. Notation is another area for improvement. With many quantities to denote, finding suitable notation is a challenge, but the use of the same capital 'V' for multiple objects is confusing. In some cases, subscripts become illegible. The distinction between Vi, a vertex, and Vo, a set of vertices, is particularly unclear. The set equation in Corollary 1 exemplifies poor presentation.

Originality and Significance
Sections 5 and 6 are clear and interesting, with potentially significant results. The paper addresses an important problem and contains genuinely interesting and useful results. However, it might be beneficial to take a step back and work towards a complete criterion for identifiability in this framework, as was achieved in the case of finding causal effects without missingness. It is difficult to imagine a statistician navigating a series of papers to find the precise result that shows (non)-identifiability of the causal effect they are interested in, especially if that result is as complicated as Theorem 1. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 seem to be a form of inverse probability weighting, likely due to the conditioning sets being Markov blankets for R(i) in the mixed graphs used. Richardson (2003) and (2009) might be relevant.

Other Comments
On page 4, line 214, R^part is a partition of R, not "a set containing partitions." The condition on line 214 should be rephrased to state that Rx and Ry "belong to distinct elements of the partition." The language is sometimes casual, such as the use of 'oftentimes' on page 4, line 213. Instead of '<-->', the symbol '\leftrightarrow' should be used. It is unclear whether Garcia (2013) is incorrect or simply uses a different framework. The notation for graphs constructed by severing edges, used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4, is not defined anywhere. Vo and Vm are used before their definitions, which appear to be unnecessary.

The paper tackles an important problem with a useful approach, but its poor presentation and notation detract from its overall quality.
