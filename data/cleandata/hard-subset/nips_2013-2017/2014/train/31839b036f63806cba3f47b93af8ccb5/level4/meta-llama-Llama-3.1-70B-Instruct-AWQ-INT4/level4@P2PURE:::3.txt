This is an interesting paper that addresses the problem of recovering a distribution from knowledge of the generating process and the process leading to missing data.

The paper considers a more general class of missingness processes than earlier work by Mohan et al., leading to a novel identification formula. Additionally, the authors consider the identification of causal queries and show that these can be identified even when the (observational) joint distribution is not identified.

The paper is generally well-written, though there are some areas for improvement that I describe below.

MAIN COMMENTS:

(1) The results presented in this paper require knowledge of the process by which observations are censored. However, it is unclear whether this knowledge is readily available in typical machine learning applications. Furthermore, it would be helpful to include more details regarding Figure 1 in the main paper, potentially by reducing the figure's size.

(2) Section 2 assumes that the full population is known, even in cases where no variables are observed for an individual (i.e., Vo = emptyset, and Rv = 1 for all V in V_m). This assumption excludes certain missing data problems, such as estimating the number of animals in a population. This limitation should be explicitly mentioned.

(3) The term "mgraph" is used ambiguously, as it sometimes includes V* variables (Figure 1) and sometimes does not (Figure 2). If the V* variables are being omitted in these graphs, this should be explained to avoid confusion.

(4) The paper restricts mgraphs from having R variables as parents of variables in V or U. Given that the authors treat R variables as real quantities, not just indicators, they should justify this restriction or acknowledge that it is primarily for convenience.

(5) The term "neighbor," introduced at the bottom of page 4, is not defined. A clear definition should be provided to ensure understanding.

(6) On page 8, the first paragraph mentions the "rectification" of the observation by Garcia. However, the authors' definition of recoverability assumes positivity. It should be clarified whether Garcia et al. also assume positivity, and if not, whether they require a stronger condition.

(7) The second paragraph on page 8 states that the "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption." This statement is too vague, as it does not explain why maximum likelihood cannot be applied to any of the missingness processes described in the paper.

MINOR COMMENTS:

- Page 1, line 33: "can bias outcomes" should be rephrased for clarity.
- Page 2, Figure 1: The figure could benefit from additional qualifications.
- Page 2, Example 1, line 158: The equation P(Y |X,Z,R_y) = P(X,Z) should be corrected to P(Y|X,Z).
- Page 3, line 168: The statement "Since Y || R | X,Z" should be revised to "Y || R_y | X,Z" for consistency.
- Page 3, line 214: Condition (ii) R^{part} ={R^{(1)},R^{(2)}, \ldots , R^N} should be corrected to R^{(N)}.
- Page 3, line 215: "R^j" should be revised to "R^{(j)}" for consistency.
- Page 4, Corollary 1: The statement does not contain partially observed variables.
- Page 6, Footnote 2: A reference should be provided for the variable-level definition.

Overall, this is an interesting paper that builds upon earlier work. While the technical soundness is clear, the breadth of application is less apparent.
