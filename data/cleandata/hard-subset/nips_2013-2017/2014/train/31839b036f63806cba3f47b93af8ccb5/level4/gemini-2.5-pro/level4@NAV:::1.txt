This paper addresses the problem of identifiability under various missing data mechanisms, employing the framework introduced by Mohan et al. The work builds upon existing results and provides several compelling examples, such as demonstrating the identifiability of causal distributions even when specific conditional distributions are not available.

**Quality**

The technical quality of the work is high; the results are intricate but appear sound. Specific findings, such as Theorem 3, are notably clear and interpretable. However, a portion of the results represent incremental advances on existing work, and their contribution to a broader conceptual understanding is not immediately apparent. Section 3 feels disconnected from the paper's main thrust, and its central result, Theorem 1, is particularly difficult to parse. This result appears to be closely related to the work on Markov blankets by Richardson (2009). The paper strengthens significantly in its later parts, with Section 5 providing clear insights and Section 6 presenting interesting findings on causal queries.

---

**Clarity**

The paper's primary weakness is its clarity. It contains a substantial amount of material, likely too much for a conference paper, as evidenced by the dense text and a 13-page technical appendix. Consequently, the article lacks a clear narrative flow and is difficult to follow. The content would likely be more impactful if presented as a more thorough journal publication.

The notation is a significant barrier to comprehension. The use of the same capital letter 'V' for multiple distinct objects is problematic, and some subscripts are difficult to read. Furthermore, the inconsistent convention where Vi represents a single vertex while Vo denotes a set of vertices adds to the confusion. The set equation in Corollary 1 exemplifies these presentational shortcomings.

---

**Originality and Significance**

Sections 5 and 6 are clear, interesting, and potentially significant. The paper addresses an important problem and presents findings that appear both novel and useful.

The work could be more impactful if it aimed for a complete, general criterion for identifiability within this framework, analogous to what has been achieved for causal effect identification without missing data. It is challenging to envision practitioners consulting a collection of papers with highly specific results, such as the complex Theorem 1, to determine the identifiability of their particular causal query.

On a technical note, Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to be related to inverse probability weighting. The validity of these results may stem from the conditioning sets acting as Markov blankets for R(i) in the utilized mixed graphs, suggesting that the work of Richardson (2003, 2009) could be relevant here.
