This paper addresses the problem of determining whether a causal or probabilistic query is estimable from data corrupted by missing entries, given a model of the missingness process. It extends the work of Mohan et al. [2013] by presenting more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries – joint and conditional distributions – and causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)). A key result is the demonstration that causal queries can be recoverable even when the factors in their identifying estimands are not. Specifically, the authors derive graphical conditions for recovering causal effects when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable. The findings are applied to the problem of attrition, characterizing the recovery of causal effects from data corrupted by this common issue.

The paper builds logically upon prior work in the field, particularly Mohan et al. [2013] and Pearl and Mohan [2014], and clearly situates itself within the existing literature on missing data and causal inference. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the importance of accurately diagnosing and addressing missing data mechanisms. The use of ‘Missingness Graphs’ (m-graphs) provides a formal and intuitive framework for representing the missingness process. The theoretical contributions, specifically Theorems 1-6 and the associated corollaries, are well-defined and provide valuable insights into the conditions for recoverability. The inclusion of examples, such as those in Sections 3 and 6, aids in understanding the practical implications of the theoretical results.

A significant strength of the paper is its rigorous mathematical treatment of the problem. The definitions are precise, and the proofs (available in the appendix) appear sound. The authors carefully consider both probabilistic and causal queries, and the distinction between recoverability of these different types of queries is clearly articulated. The demonstration that causal queries can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not is a particularly noteworthy contribution. Furthermore, the exploration of scenarios beyond the standard MAR/MCAR categories is a valuable extension.

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the computational complexity of implementing the proposed algorithms. While the theoretical conditions for recoverability are established, the practical challenges of applying these conditions to large-scale datasets are not fully addressed. Additionally, while the related work section is comprehensive, a brief discussion of alternative approaches for handling attrition, such as multiple imputation (Little and Rubin [2002]), would provide a more complete context for the presented work. A clarification on how the proposed methodology performs when the assumptions of the m-graph are violated would also strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents novel graphical conditions for recovering causal queries, extending previous work and addressing a significant gap in the literature.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The mathematical framework and proofs are sound and provide a strong foundation for the presented results.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition, a common problem in observational studies, demonstrates the practical importance of the work.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper does not fully address the computational challenges of applying the proposed algorithms to large datasets.
*   **Limited Discussion of Alternatives:** The related work section could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of alternative methods for handling missing data.
*   **Sensitivity Analysis:** The robustness of the proposed methodology to violations of the m-graph assumptions is not explored.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite minor weaknesses, this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference with missing data. The theoretical results are significant, the presentation is clear, and the application to attrition is compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
