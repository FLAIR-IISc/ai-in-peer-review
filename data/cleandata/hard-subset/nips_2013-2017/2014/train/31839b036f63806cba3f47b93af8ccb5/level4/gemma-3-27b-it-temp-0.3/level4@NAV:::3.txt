This is a compelling paper that investigates the recoverability of distributions given knowledge of the generative process and the mechanisms causing missing data. The work extends previous research, notably that of Mohan et al., by considering a broader class of missingness processes. This generalization leads to a novel identification formula, and importantly, the authors demonstrate the identifiability of causal queries even when the observational joint distribution remains unidentified. The paper is generally well-written, though some refinements would enhance its clarity and impact.

**MAIN COMMENTS:**

(1) A key requirement of the presented results is knowledge of the censoring process. It is worth discussing the practical availability of this knowledge in typical machine learning applications. Furthermore, providing more detail regarding Figure 1 within the main text – potentially by adjusting its size – would be beneficial.

(2) In Section 2, it should be explicitly stated that the missing data processes considered here inherently assume knowledge of the full population. Even in scenarios where the observed set, Vo, is empty and Rv = 1 for all V in V_m (meaning no variables are observed for a given individual), the existence of that individual is still presupposed. This limits the applicability of the framework to problems involving estimating population size, for example.

(3) The term “mgraph” appears to be used with some ambiguity. Specifically, the graphs sometimes include the V* variables (as seen in Figure 1), while at other times they do not (Figure 2). If the omission of V* variables is intentional, this should be clearly explained.

(4) The paper imposes a restriction that mgraphs cannot have R variables as parents of variables in V or U. Given the authors treat R variables as continuous quantities rather than simple indicators, a justification for this restriction – or a clarification of whether it is primarily for convenience – is warranted.

(5) The term “neighbor,” appearing at the bottom of page 4, is not defined and should be clarified.

(6) On page 8, the first paragraph references the “rectification” of observation by Garcia et al. The authors’ definition of recoverability relies on the positivity assumption. It would be valuable to clarify whether Garcia et al. also assume positivity, and if not, whether their approach requires a stronger condition.

(7) The statement on page 8, second paragraph, that “Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption” is overly broad. A more precise explanation is needed regarding why maximum likelihood cannot be directly applied to the missingness processes described in this paper.

**MINOR COMMENTS:**

P.1 line 33: “can bias outcomes” – consider rephrasing for clarity.
P.2 Figure 1: Add clarifying “Qualifications” to the figure caption.
P.2 Example 1 on p.3 line 158: The equation P(Y |X,Z,R_y) = P(X,Z) should be P(Y|X,Z).
P.3 line 168: “Since Y || R | X,Z” should be “Y || R_y | X,Z”.
P.3 line 214: Condition (ii) R^{part} ={R^{(1)},R^{(2)}, \ldots , R^N} should be R^{(N)}.
P.3 line 215: “R^j respectively” should be “R^{(j)}”.
P.4 Corollary 1: The statement that it “does not contain a partially observed variables” is unclear and should be revised.
P.6 Footnote 2: Please provide a reference for the variable-level definition.



Overall, this is a technically sound and interesting contribution that builds upon existing work in the field. While the breadth of its practical application isn’t immediately apparent, the theoretical results are valuable.
