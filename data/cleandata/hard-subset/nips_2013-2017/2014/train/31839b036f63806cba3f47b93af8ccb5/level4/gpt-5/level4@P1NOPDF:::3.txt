This is an interesting and technically sound paper. It studies when a distribution can be recovered given knowledge of both the data-generating process and the process inducing missingness. The paper considers a broader class of missingness mechanisms than in prior work by Mohan et al., leading to a novel identification formula. It also shows that certain causal queries are identifiable even when the (observational) joint distribution is not. The paper is generally well written, though there are a few glitches and clarifications needed, as outlined below.

MAIN COMMENTS:
1) Assumptions about the censoring mechanism. The main results require full knowledge of the process by which observations are censored/made missing. Is such knowledge realistic in typical machine learning applications? Please discuss practical scenarios and limits. Also, it would help to move more of the detail currently in Figure 1 into the main text (potentially by shrinking the figure), so readers can follow the assumptions without flipping between sections.

2) Population-frame assumption. Section 2 should explicitly state that the missingness processes considered assume a known population frame: even if Vo = ∅ and Rv = 1 for all V in Vm (so no variables are observed for an individual), the existence of that individual in the dataset is still known. This excludes settings like estimating the size of a hidden population (e.g., capture–recapture problems).

3) Ambiguity in “mgraph.” The term mgraph appears to be used inconsistently. In some places (e.g., Figure 1) the graph includes the V* variables; in others (e.g., Figure 2) it does not. If V* variables are merely being suppressed for readability, please say so explicitly and clarify the convention used throughout.

4) Restrictions on parent sets. The paper disallows R variables as parents of variables in V or U. Since the R variables are treated as substantive variables (not just indicators), please justify this restriction (e.g., to avoid paradoxes, ensure acyclicity, or for identifiability) or state that it is a simplifying assumption for this work and discuss implications if R → V or R → U edges were allowed.

5) Undefined terminology. The term “neighbor,” introduced at the bottom of p.4, is not defined. Please provide a formal definition (e.g., graphical adjacency vs. Markov blanket) and use it consistently.

6) Positivity and “rectification.” On p.8 (first paragraph), the discussion of “rectification” due to García et al. needs clarification. Recoverability as defined here assumes positivity. Do García et al. make the same assumption? If not, please clarify whether your results rely on a stronger condition than theirs, and how the assumptions align.

7) Maximum likelihood under MAR. On p.8 (second paragraph), the statement “Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption” is too vague. Please specify the precise MAR conditions, model assumptions, and why standard ML approaches cannot be directly applied (or how they fail) in the more general missingness processes you consider. Concrete examples would help.

MINOR COMMENTS:
- p.1, line 33: “can bias outcomes.” Consider revising the wording (e.g., “can bias estimates/inferences”) for precision.
- p.2, Figure 1: “Qualifications.” Please check this label/caption for clarity or typos.
- p.3, Example 1 (line 158): P(Y | X, Z, R_y) = P(X, Z). The RHS should be P(Y | X, Z).
- p.3, line 168: “Since Y ⫫ R | X, Z” should be “Y ⫫ R_y | X, Z.”
- p.3, line 214, Condition (ii): R^{part} = {R^{(1)}, R^{(2)}, …, R^N} should be R^{(N)}.
- p.3, line 215: “R^j respectively” should be R^{(j)}.
- p.4, Corollary 1: “does not contain a partially observed variables” should be “does not contain any partially observed variables.”
- p.6, Footnote 2: Please provide a reference for the variable-level definition.

Overall assessment: An interesting paper that extends prior work and offers a novel identification result. While the technical development is solid, broader discussion of when the censoring mechanism is known and clarification of several definitions and assumptions would strengthen the paper and help readers gauge the scope of applicability.
