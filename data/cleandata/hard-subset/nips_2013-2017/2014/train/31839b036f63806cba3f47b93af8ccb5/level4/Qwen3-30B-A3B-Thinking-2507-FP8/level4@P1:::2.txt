

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending Mohan et al.'s (2013) framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to establish novel necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors address a critical gap: while prior work focused on probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y|x)), they demonstrate that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations (e.g., P(y,x)) are not—specifically, when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are connected by an inducing path (e.g., Figure 3). This insight fundamentally challenges the assumption that causal recoverability requires probabilistic recoverability, resolving a key ambiguity in the literature.

The paper excels in technical rigor and clarity. It systematically builds upon Mohan et al. (2013) by:  
(1) Generalizing the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) to handle missingness mechanisms that interact (e.g., R variables influencing each other),  
(2) Providing new non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) to identify when consistent estimation is impossible, and  
(3) Applying these results to attrition problems (Section 7), where they correct misconceptions in Garcia (2013) by showing that causal effects may be non-recoverable due to collider paths (e.g., Figure 4a), not merely because X is an ancestor of Ry. The exposition is exceptionally clear, with well-motivated examples (e.g., Example 3) that illustrate how do-calculus enables recovery despite probabilistic non-recoverability. Theorems are precisely stated, and the proofs in the appendix provide sufficient technical depth.

**Strengths:**  
- **Originality:** The core advance—causal recoverability without probabilistic recoverability—is genuinely novel and addresses a well-known limitation in missing data literature (e.g., Garcia 2013). The graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3) also fill a critical gap, as prior work lacked formal necessary conditions.  
- **Significance:** The results directly impact causal inference in longitudinal studies (e.g., attrition in randomized trials), where missingness mechanisms are often complex. The framework provides a unified approach for practitioners to diagnose recoverability without relying on restrictive MAR/MCAR assumptions.  
- **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with each section logically building on the last. Key concepts (e.g., d*-separation, inducing paths) are defined precisely and contextualized within the m-graph framework. The application to attrition (Section 7) makes the theory immediately actionable.  
- **Quality:** The theoretical results are thoroughly validated via formal proofs and concrete examples. The authors carefully distinguish recoverability from identifiability (Definition 3) and address edge cases (e.g., minimality in Definition 2).

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Limited empirical validation:** While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper lacks real-world case studies or simulations to demonstrate the framework's practical utility in complex datasets (e.g., comparing recovery rates under different missingness mechanisms).  
- **Technical density:** Some proofs (e.g., Theorem 4) assume familiarity with do-calculus and inducing paths, which may hinder accessibility for readers less versed in causal inference. A brief illustrative example of Theorem 4’s application would aid comprehension.  
- **Scope of attrition analysis:** The "simple attrition" model (Section 7) is a useful special case, but the paper does not address more complex attrition patterns (e.g., time-varying missingness), which are common in longitudinal data.

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances the state of the art in causal inference with missing data. The central insight—that causal queries may bypass probabilistic recoverability barriers—has profound implications for fields like epidemiology and social science, where attrition is pervasive. The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), genuinely novel (Originality), and addresses a significant practical problem (Significance). The minor weaknesses in empirical validation and accessibility are outweighed by the paper’s conceptual rigor and precision.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The work provides indispensable theoretical tools for researchers dealing with missing data, and its graphical approach offers a clear path for communication and assumption refinement—exactly the kind of contribution NIPS values. The authors have successfully extended Mohan et al. (2013) to resolve a critical limitation in the field, and their results are immediately applicable to real-world causal inference challenges.
