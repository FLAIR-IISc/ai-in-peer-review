This is an interesting paper that addresses the problem of determining when a distribution can be recovered given knowledge of the generating process and the process leading to missing data. The paper considers a more general class of missingness processes than previously explored, notably by Mohan et al. [2013], leading to a novel identification formula. Importantly, the authors demonstrate that causal queries can be identified even when the underlying (observational) joint distribution is not. The paper is generally well-written, though some phrasing could be improved for clarity and flow, as detailed below.

**MAIN COMMENTS:**

(1) The results presented here require knowledge of the process by which observations are censored. Is this knowledge readily available in typical machine learning applications? It would be helpful to include more detail regarding Figure 1 within the main paper, perhaps by reducing the figure's size.

(2) Section 2 implicitly assumes that the full population is known. Even if Vo = ∅, and Rv = 1 for all V in Vm (meaning no variables are observed for a given individual), it is still assumed that we know this individual exists. This excludes missing data problems such as estimating population sizes, where the very existence of individuals may be uncertain. Clarifying this assumption would be beneficial.

(3) The term “m-graph” appears to be used somewhat ambiguously. In some instances, the graph includes the V* variables (e.g., Figure 1), while in others, it does not (e.g., Figure 2). If the V* variables are being omitted for graphical simplicity, this should be explicitly stated.

(4) The paper restricts m-graphs to not have R variables as parents of variables in V or U. Given that the authors treat the R variables as real quantities rather than simply indicators, it would be helpful to justify this restriction or clarify whether it is primarily for convenience.

(5) The term ‘neighbor’, which appears at the bottom of page 4, is not formally defined.

(6) Page 8, first paragraph: The authors describe a “rectification” of an observation by Garcia [2013]. The authors’ definition of recoverability assumes positivity. Do Garcia et al. also assume positivity? If not, their work might require a stronger condition.

(7) Page 8, second paragraph: The statement that “Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption” is too vague. The paper should explain why maximum likelihood may not be applicable to any of the missingness processes described here.

**MINOR COMMENTS:**

P.1 line 33: “can bias outcomes” could be rephrased as “can introduce bias” or “may bias results”.

P.2, Figure 1: Add a clarifying caption.

P.2, Example 1, line 158: P(Y |X,Z,Ry) = P(X,Z) appears to be a typo; it should be P(Y|X,Z).

P.3 line 168: “Since Y || R | X,Z” should be “Since Y || Ry | X,Z”.

P.3 line 214:  “R^{part} ={R^{(1)},R^{(2)}, \ldots , R^N}” should be “R^{part} ={R^{(1)},R^{(2)}, \ldots , R^{(N)}}”.

P.3 line 215: “R^j respectively” should be “R^{(j)} respectively”.

P.4, Corollary 1: “does not contain a partially observed variables” should be “does not contain any partially observed variables”.

P.6, Footnote 2: Please provide a citation for the variable-level definition.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an interesting and technically sound paper that builds on earlier work in the field of missing data. While not entirely clear how broad the potential application is, the presented theory is solid and the extension to causal queries is a valuable contribution. The paper demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature and provides a well-reasoned argument for its contributions. 

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   Presents a generalization of previous work on recoverability under missing data.
*   Provides novel graphical criteria for both recoverability and non-recoverability.
*   Demonstrates that causal queries can be identified even when observational queries are not.
*   Applies results to the important practical problem of attrition.
*   Technically sound with thorough proofs.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on knowledge of the missingness process may limit the applicability of the results in some real-world scenarios.
*   The assumptions regarding the nature of the missingness process should be clearly stated and justified.
*   Some of the writing could be clearer and more concise.
