This paper addresses the challenging problem of determining whether a probabilistic or causal query can be recovered (i.e., consistently estimated) from data with missing entries. The work builds directly upon the framework of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. The authors extend this prior work by providing more general conditions for recoverability, applicable to a broader class of missingness processes where the mechanisms for missingness can influence one another.

The main contributions are threefold. First, the paper presents a refined "sequential factorization" theorem and a novel recovery formula (Theorem 2) for joint distributions in cases where the previous methods fail. Second, it introduces the first formal graphical criteria that preclude recoverability, offering necessary conditions that help practitioners identify when a query is fundamentally unanswerable from the data. Third, and perhaps most significantly, the paper demonstrates that causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the corresponding observational joint or conditional distributions are not. This is shown to be the case even when a variable Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable, a condition that would prevent the recovery of P(Y). These theoretical results are then applied to the specific problem of attrition in longitudinal studies, leading to necessary and sufficient conditions for recovery that clarify and correct observations from prior literature.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes several novel and significant contributions. The finding that causal queries may be recoverable even when their constituent probabilistic parts are not is a powerful and counter-intuitive result that advances our understanding of the interplay between causality and missing data. The development of graphical criteria for non-recoverability is also a highly valuable contribution, as it provides researchers with a practical tool to assess the feasibility of their analysis before attempting estimation.
*   **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound, presenting a series of formal theorems with proofs provided in the appendix. The extensions to the work of Mohan et al. [2013] are non-trivial and allow the framework to handle more complex and realistic missingness scenarios, such as those where missingness in one variable depends on the missingness status of another.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The use of examples to illustrate the application of new theorems (e.g., Example 1 for Theorem 1) is effective in making the abstract concepts more concrete.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

While the paper is strong, several points could be clarified to improve its impact and accessibility.

1.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** The entire framework hinges on having access to a correctly specified causal m-graph. It would strengthen the paper to include a more detailed discussion on the practical feasibility of specifying this graph in typical machine learning or statistical applications. For instance, the gender wage-gap example in Figure 1 is a good motivator, and expanding on how an analyst might arrive at this graph from domain knowledge could make the framework feel more accessible.

2.  **Scope of the Missing Data Problem:** The paper's formulation appears to assume that the existence of every individual in the population is known, even if all their associated variables are missing (i.e., the rows in the dataset are fixed). It would be beneficial to explicitly state this scope, as it excludes certain types of missing data problems, such as population size estimation (e.g., capture-recapture methods), where the existence of an unobserved unit is itself the missing information.

3.  **Model Constraints:** The framework assumes that R variables (missingness mechanisms) cannot be parents of substantive variables in V or latent variables in U. Could the authors provide a justification for this restriction? Clarifying whether this is a simplifying assumption for technical convenience or a fundamental constraint based on the interpretation of R variables would be helpful.

4.  **Consistency and Definitions:**
    *   The use of "m-graph" seems slightly ambiguous across figures. Figure 1 includes the proxy variables V*, while Figure 2 does not. Please clarify whether V* nodes are formally part of the m-graph definition or are omitted in later diagrams for simplicity.
    *   The term "neighbor," which is central to Theorems 2 and 3, is not formally defined in the paper. Adding a definition would improve precision.

5.  **Comparison with Prior Work:**
    *   The paper claims to rectify a result from Garcia [2013] regarding attrition. The recoverability conditions presented here depend on a positivity assumption (Definition 1). To ensure a fair comparison, it should be clarified whether Garcia's work operates under the same assumption. If not, the difference in conclusions might be attributable to this difference in foundational assumptions.
    *   The discussion of related work states that the Maximum Likelihood (ML) method yields consistent estimates under the MAR assumption. This statement is correct but could be more incisive. A more powerful motivation for the proposed framework would be to briefly explain *why* standard ML methods fail for the MNAR (Missing Not At Random) models enabled by m-graphs, thereby highlighting the necessity of this work.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides novel and significant theoretical results for recovering queries from data with complex missingness patterns.
*   The distinction between the recoverability of causal versus probabilistic queries is a key insight with high potential impact.
*   Significantly generalizes prior work and introduces the first formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability.
*   The work is technically sound and rigorously presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The practical application of the framework relies on strong, and potentially unrealistic, assumptions about knowledge of the true missingness process.
*   Certain aspects of the presentation, particularly regarding definitions and model scope, could be improved for clarity and precision.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper that makes important contributions to the graphical modeling literature for missing data. The weaknesses are primarily related to clarity and the framing of assumptions, which can be addressed in a revised version.
