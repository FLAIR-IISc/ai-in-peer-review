- Extends Mohan et al. (2013) m-graphs to give necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data.  
- Shows causal queries P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations (e.g., P(y,x)) are not, overturning a common assumption.  
- Identifies inducing paths (e.g., between Y and Ry) as key structures allowing causal recoverability despite probabilistic non-recoverability.  
- Generalizes the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) to handle interacting missingness mechanisms (R variables influencing each other).  
- Provides new non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3, Corollary 2) that give formal necessary conditions for when consistent estimation is impossible.  
- Demonstrates via examples (e.g., Example 3) how do-calculus enables recovery in cases where probabilistic recovery fails.  
- Applies theoretical results to attrition problems and corrects misconceptions in Garcia (2013) by highlighting collider paths as a cause of non-recoverability.  
- Theorems are precisely stated and supported by detailed proofs in the appendix, showing strong technical rigor.  
- Clearly distinguishes recoverability from identifiability (Definition 3) and addresses edge cases like minimality (Definition 2).  
- Originality: introduces a genuinely novel core insight about causal recoverability without probabilistic recoverability.  
- Significance: directly relevant to longitudinal studies and practitioners dealing with complex missingness beyond MAR/MCAR assumptions.  
- Clarity: well-organized exposition with precise definitions (d*-separation, inducing paths) and motivated examples.  
- Weakness — limited empirical validation: lacks real-world case studies or simulations demonstrating practical utility.  
- Weakness — technical density: some proofs assume do-calculus familiarity; an illustrative example for Theorem 4 would improve accessibility.  
- Weakness — limited scope of attrition analysis: focuses on a simple attrition model and does not treat time-varying missingness common in longitudinal data.  
- Overall assessment: high-quality theoretical contribution with profound implications for causal inference under missing data.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
