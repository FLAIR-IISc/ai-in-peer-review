This paper addresses the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values. Building on the "missingness graph" (m-graph) framework from Mohan et al. [2013], the authors extend previous work by providing more general graphical conditions for recoverability. The key contributions include: (1) a generalized sequential factorization theorem to handle dependencies between missingness mechanisms; (2) criteria for recovering joint distributions in cases where factorization fails; (3) novel and valuable graphical criteria for establishing *non-recoverability* of queries; and (4) the interesting and important result that causal effects can be recoverable even when the underlying joint or conditional distributions are not. The paper concludes by applying these results to the problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

### **Evaluation**

**Quality:** The paper appears to be of high technical quality. The proofs in the extensive appendix are detailed, and the derived graphical criteria seem correct. The results presented in Section 5 (Non-recoverability Criteria) and Section 6 (Recovering Causal Queries) are particularly strong, offering clear, interpretable conditions and insightful new findings. For instance, Theorem 3 provides a simple graphical check to rule out recoverability, which is very useful. The demonstration that `P(y|do(z))` can be recovered when `P(y,z)` is not is a significant theoretical contribution.

However, some results feel more incremental. The main result of Section 3 (Theorem 1) is a generalization of a previous theorem, but its complexity makes it very difficult to comprehend and its relationship to the rest of the paper is not immediately clear. This raises the question of whether all the presented material contributes equally to a cohesive and understandable narrative.

**Clarity:** The paper's primary weakness is its clarity. The density of the material, coupled with a large number of theorems addressing different special cases, makes the paper challenging to read and digest. It feels more like a comprehensive journal article than a focused conference paper, and the core message is somewhat diluted by the sheer volume of technical results. The narrative does not flow smoothly between sections, leaving the reader to connect the dots between the various theorems.

The notation significantly contributes to the difficulty.
*   The letter 'V' is overloaded to represent the set of all nodes, subsets of nodes, and individual nodes, making expressions hard to parse. For example, the distinction between a variable `Vi` and a set of variables `Vo` is confusing.
*   The expressions can become almost illegible due to complex subscripts, as seen in the equation in Corollary 1.
*   Some notation, such as `G_X` and `G^X` used in Section 6, appears to be used without a formal definition.
Improving the notational system would substantially enhance the paper's readability.

**Originality:** The work is original and extends the state-of-the-art in principled, model-based handling of missing data. While it builds directly on Mohan et al. [2013], it makes several novel contributions. The introduction of graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Section 5) is a significant step forward, as much of the literature focuses only on sufficient conditions for recoverability. The results in Section 6, which decouple the recoverability of causal effects from that of their corresponding probabilistic estimands, are highly original and counter-intuitive.

**Significance:** The problem of missing data is of great practical and theoretical importance, and this paper offers valuable tools for addressing it beyond the standard MAR/MCAR assumptions. The results in Sections 5 and 6, in particular, are likely to be influential for researchers working on causal inference and graphical models.

The broader significance, however, is somewhat limited by the paper's "cookbook" style of presenting a series of specific results for different graphical structures. The field of causal identification greatly benefited from the development of complete algorithms (e.g., Shpitser and Pearl, 2006) that provide a definitive answer for any query. This paper provides a collection of useful but disparate criteria. It is difficult to imagine a practitioner sifting through this and related papers to find the one specific theorem that applies to their problem, especially if the conditions are as complex as in Theorem 1. A more unified theory or a step towards a complete algorithm for recoverability would have a much greater impact.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Focus the Narrative:** To improve clarity for a conference audience, I suggest restructuring the paper to focus on the most impactful and novel results, namely the non-recoverability criteria (Section 5) and the recovery of causal queries (Section 6). The more incremental generalizations, such as Theorem 1, could be briefly summarized and detailed in the appendix.
*   **Improve Notation:** A thorough revision of the notational conventions is essential. Please consider using distinct symbols for single variables vs. sets of variables (e.g., `v_i` vs `\mathcal{V}_o`) and ensure all graphical notation is defined.
*   **Clarify Connections:** The results in Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear related to inverse probability weighting, where one re-weights the complete cases. It would be beneficial to explicitly discuss this connection and clarify the role of the Markov blanket (citing, for instance, Richardson, 2003) in identifying the conditioning sets.
*   **Minor Corrections:**
    *   p4, l213: The symbol for a bi-directed edge should be `\leftrightarrow`.
    *   p4, l214: The definition of `R_part` is slightly confusing. It should be defined as a partition of `R`, and the condition should state that `Rx` and `Ry` belong to distinct sets *in* the partition.
    *   Regarding Garcia [2013]: The paper claims to rectify prevailing opinions. It would be helpful to clarify whether Garcia's result is incorrect within its own framework or if the framework presented here is simply more general.
    *   The variables `Vo` and `Vm` are used in the text before they are formally defined in Section 2.

### **Conclusion**

This paper contains significant and technically sound contributions to the important problem of inference with missing data. The results on non-recoverability and the special status of causal queries are particularly noteworthy. However, the paper's impact is severely hindered by issues of clarity, stemming from dense presentation and confusing notation. With a substantial revision focused on improving the narrative and presentation, this work has the potential to be a very strong contribution to the field.
