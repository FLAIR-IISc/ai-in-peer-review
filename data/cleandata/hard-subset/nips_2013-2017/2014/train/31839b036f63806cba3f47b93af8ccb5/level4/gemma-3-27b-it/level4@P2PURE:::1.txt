The paper addresses the problem of identifiability under various missing data mechanisms, building upon the framework of Mohan et al. It improves upon existing results and provides insightful examples, notably demonstrating identifiability of causal distributions even without complete conditional distributions.

**Quality**

The technical quality is generally high; the results are complex but appear to be correct. Theorems like Theorem 3 are clearly stated and interpretable. However, some results represent incremental improvements and, to my knowledge, do not contribute to a broader understanding of the underlying principles. 

Section 3 feels somewhat disconnected from the rest of the paper, and its central result (Theorem 1) is difficult to follow. It appears closely related to Markov blanket results presented in Richardson (2009). The quality improves in subsequent sections, with Section 5 offering clear and valuable conclusions, and Section 6 presenting interesting results concerning causal queries.

---
**Clarity**

The primary weakness of this paper is its clarity. The scope appears overly ambitious for a conference format, a factor contributing to the density of the text and the extensive 13-page technical appendix. The paper lacks a cohesive flow, making it challenging to follow; it might be more suitable as a longer, more comprehensive journal article.

While finding suitable notation for a large number of quantities is inherently difficult, improvements are possible. The repeated use of the capital letter ‘V’ to denote different objects places a significant burden on the reader, and the subscripts sometimes become illegible. The inconsistency in representing *V<sub>i</sub>* as a vertex and *V<sub>o</sub>* as a set of vertices is particularly confusing. The set equation in Corollary 1 exemplifies poor presentation.

---
**Originality and Significance**

Sections 5 and 6 are particularly clear, interesting, and potentially significant. The paper tackles an important problem and contains results that seem genuinely useful.

However, a more holistic approach to identifying a general criterion for identifiability within this framework would be valuable, mirroring the success achieved in establishing criteria for identifying causal effects without missing data. It is unlikely that a statistician would efficiently navigate a series of papers to determine the identifiability of a specific causal effect, especially given the complexity of results like Theorem 1. 

Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to leverage inverse probability weighting. The effectiveness of these results likely stems from the conditioning sets serving as Markov blankets for *R<sub>i</sub>* within the author's mixed graphs; Richardson (2003) and (2009) may provide relevant background.
