

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on missing data analysis using "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) to develop graphical conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values. The authors build upon the framework introduced by Mohan et al. (2013) and Mohan and Pearl (2014), who formalized the missingness process through graphical models and introduced the concept of recoverability. The paper makes three key contributions: (1) general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (joint and conditional distributions), (2) novel insights showing that causal queries P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations are not, and (3) application of these results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.

The paper demonstrates technical soundness through rigorous theoretical development. The authors establish clear sufficient conditions for recoverability (Theorems 1, 2, 4, 5, and 6), complemented by necessary conditions (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2). The results are well-supported with examples that illustrate both the utility and limitations of the proposed methods. The paper successfully shows that causal effects may be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable (Example 3), which represents a significant departure from conventional wisdom. The theoretical framework is carefully constructed, with proofs provided in the appendix, and the authors appropriately acknowledge related work throughout.

The paper's main strength lies in its novel insights regarding causal query recoverability, particularly in Section 6. The demonstration that P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when Y and Ry are connected by an inducing path (Theorem 4) is a substantial contribution that challenges existing assumptions about the relationship between probabilistic and causal recoverability. These results have clear practical implications for handling attrition in randomized trials (Section 7), where the authors provide necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects under simple attrition.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues that impede its accessibility. The notation is inconsistent and overloaded: for example, the repeated use of "V" to denote different sets (observable variables, entire variable set, etc.) creates confusion. The distinction between "Vo" (set of fully observed variables) and "Vi" (a single vertex) is particularly problematic, as is the use of "R" to denote both the set of missingness mechanisms and individual mechanisms. The presentation of Theorem 1 is especially dense and difficult to follow, with the minimality requirement in Definition 2 being crucial but not sufficiently emphasized. The paper's organization could be improved by grouping related results and providing more intuitive explanations of the key concepts before presenting the formal theorems.

The paper's significance is high due to its direct relevance to a pervasive problem in empirical research. Missing data is ubiquitous across scientific disciplines, and the ability to formally determine recoverability of queries from missing data represents a substantial advance. The application to attrition problems (Section 7) is particularly compelling, as it directly addresses a common challenge in longitudinal studies where missing data due to dropout can invalidate causal inferences. The results rectify previous misconceptions in the literature, such as Garcia (2013)'s incorrect condition for non-recoverability in attrition problems.

The paper's incremental nature is its weakest aspect. While the results are technically sound, the authors do not clearly articulate how their framework contributes to a more comprehensive understanding of missing data problems. The paper presents a series of specialized results rather than building toward a unified theory of recoverability. A more cohesive narrative that connects the various theorems and demonstrates how they fit together would strengthen the paper's contribution. For instance, the relationship between Theorems 2 and 5, and how they relate to the Markov blanket concepts in Richardson (2003), could be clarified.

The paper would benefit from several specific revisions to improve clarity. First, the authors should standardize their notation to avoid the current confusion (e.g., using "V" for the variable set and "V_obs" for observed variables). Second, they should provide more intuitive explanations of key concepts before presenting formal definitions. Third, the paper should include a high-level summary of the main results and their relationships to guide the reader through the technical content.

Overall, this is an important paper that makes valuable theoretical contributions to the field of missing data analysis. The results on causal query recoverability are particularly significant and well-motivated. While the paper's presentation could be improved, its technical content is strong, and its insights are likely to be useful to researchers working with missing data. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity, particularly regarding notation and the organization of the technical results. The paper addresses a fundamental problem in empirical research with meaningful theoretical advances that should be shared with the NIPS community.
