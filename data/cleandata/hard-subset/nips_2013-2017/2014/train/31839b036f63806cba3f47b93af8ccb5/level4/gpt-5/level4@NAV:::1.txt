Review- The manuscript investigates identifiability under multiple generative missingness mechanisms, building on the framework of Mohan et al. It sharpens some known results and offers useful illustrative cases (e.g., identifiability of causal distributions even when certain conditional distributions are not available).

Quality
The technical standard appears high; the arguments are intricate yet seem sound. Statements such as Theorem 3 are well stated and interpretable. Some contributions strike me as incremental extensions of prior work and, at least from this presentation, do not yet coalesce into a broader understanding of the phenomenon. Section 3 feels somewhat tangential to the remainder, and its central result (Theorem 1) is difficult to parse; it appears closely connected to Markov blanket ideas in Richardson (2009). The exposition improves later: Section 5 offers clear takeaways, and Section 6 presents interesting findings for causal queries.

Clarity
This is the principal weakness. There is likely too much material for a conference format (and the relative importance of parts is not always evident), as suggested by the dense prose and a 13-page technical appendix. The narrative lacks flow and is hard to follow; this might be better developed as a longer, carefully structured journal article.
Although finding compact notation for many quantities is challenging, the current notation can be substantially improved. Overloading the capital “V” for multiple objects burdens the reader, and some subscripts are nearly unreadable. In particular, the convention that Vi is a single vertex while Vo denotes a set of vertices is confusing. The set expression in Corollary 1 is perhaps the most extreme instance of problematic presentation.

Originality and Significance
Sections 5 and 6 are clear, engaging, and potentially impactful. The paper addresses an important problem and contains results that seem genuinely useful. 
It may be beneficial to step back to articulate what happens in the general case and aim for a complete identifiability criterion within this framework (analogous to the complete characterization of causal effects without missingness). Expecting users to sift through a sequence of papers to locate a specific (non-)identifiability result—especially one as intricate as Theorem 1—seems impractical.
Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 read as instances of inverse probability weighting. Presumably, these work because the conditioning sets act as Markov blankets for R(i) in the mixed graphs employed. Richardson (2003) and (2009) appear relevant.

Other Comments
p4, l214: R^part is a partition of R, not “a set containing partitions.” The condition should be that Rx and Ry belong to distinct elements of the partition.
Language is occasionally informal, e.g., “oftentimes.”
p4, l213: Use \leftrightarrow rather than <-->.
Is Garcia (2013) actually incorrect, or operating within a different framework?
Notation for graphs formed by severing edges is used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4 but is not defined.
Vo and Vm appear in a sentence before they are defined (and seemingly without necessity).
