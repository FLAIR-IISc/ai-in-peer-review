

Quality  
The paper addresses identifiability in missing data mechanisms using Mohan et al.'s framework, improving upon existing results and providing illustrative examples (e.g., identifiability of causal distributions without available conditional distributions). Technical quality is high, with results appearing correct despite their complexity. Theorem 3 is clear and interpretable. However, some results constitute incremental improvements without advancing a broader conceptual understanding of the problem. Section 3 appears disconnected from the main thread; its primary result (Theorem 1) is difficult to comprehend and closely resembles Richardson (2009)'s Markov blanket results. Quality improves substantially in Sections 5 and 6, which offer clear solutions and interesting findings on causal queries.  

Clarity  
This is the paper's primary weakness. The content is overly dense for a conference paper, evidenced by the text's density and a 13-page technical appendix, suggesting it would better suit a journal format. The paper lacks clear flow and is challenging to follow. Notation requires significant improvement: the overuse of capital 'V' to denote multiple distinct concepts (e.g., Vi as a vertex versus Vo as a vertex set) creates confusion, and subscripts often become illegible. Corollary 1 exemplifies poor presentation through its set equation.  

Originality and Significance  
Sections 5 and 6 present clear, engaging, and potentially significant contributions. The paper tackles an important problem with genuinely useful results. However, it would benefit from a more general unification of the framework—specifically, developing a complete identifiability criterion (analogous to the solution for causal effects without missingness). It is impractical for a statistician to navigate multiple papers to locate the precise identifiability result for their specific causal query, especially given the complexity of results like Theorem 1. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to implement inverse probability weighting; their validity likely stems from conditioning sets being Markov blankets for R(i) in the mixed graphs used, suggesting relevance to Richardson (2003, 2009).
