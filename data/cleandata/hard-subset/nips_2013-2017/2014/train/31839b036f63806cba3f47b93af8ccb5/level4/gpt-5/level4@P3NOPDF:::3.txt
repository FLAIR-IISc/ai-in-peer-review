The paper studies when a distribution can be recovered given knowledge of both the data-generating process and the missingness mechanism. It extends prior work (e.g., Mohan et al.) by considering a more general class of missingness processes, deriving a novel identification formula, and showing that certain causal queries can remain identifiable even when the observational joint distribution is not. The paper is generally clear and technically sound, with several points that would benefit from clarification.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution: a generalization of missingness processes and a new identification formula.
- Insightful result that causal effects may be identifiable even when the observational joint is not, which is important for causal inference under missingness.
- Writing is overall readable, with useful figures.

Main comments
1) Assumption of known censoring mechanism:
- The results require knowledge of the process by which observations are censored. Please discuss the realism of this assumption in typical machine learning and applied settings. Concrete examples (e.g., designed studies, administrative processes) would help.
- It would also help to include more detail from Figure 1 in the main text (potentially by making the figure smaller) so readers can assess the plausibility and use of this knowledge.

2) Scope of missing data problems considered:
- The framework appears to assume the full population is known. For instance, even if V_o is empty and R_v = 1 for all V in V_m (so no variables are observed for a particular unit), it is still assumed that the unit’s existence is known.
- Please make this assumption explicit and note that this excludes missing-data problems such as population size estimation (e.g., capture–recapture).

3) Ambiguity in “mgraph”:
- The term seems to be used inconsistently: sometimes V* (proxy/recorded) variables are included (e.g., Figure 1), and other times they are omitted (e.g., Figure 2).
- Please provide a single, precise definition of mgraph and state explicitly whether V* nodes are part of the graph. If V* nodes are omitted for readability in some figures, say so and clarify how to interpret those diagrams.

4) Restricting R as parents of V or U:
- In the current paper, R variables are not allowed to be parents of variables in V or U. Since the R variables are treated as real quantities rather than mere indicators, please justify this restriction. Is it a substantive assumption (e.g., ruling out mechanisms where the missingness process causally influences the underlying variables) or mainly for technical convenience?
- If possible, discuss whether the main results extend to models where R can be parents of V or U, and what new difficulties would arise.

5) Definition of “neighbor”:
- The term “neighbor” (bottom of p.4) is not defined. Please specify whether “neighbor” means any adjacent node (via directed or bidirected edge), only parents/children, or some other relation.

6) Relation to Garcia et al. and positivity:
- On p.8 (first paragraph), when discussing “rectification” by Garcia et al., please clarify how their assumptions align with yours. Recoverability here assumes positivity; do Garcia et al. also assume positivity? If not, indicate what stronger or alternative conditions their approach requires.

7) Maximum likelihood and MAR:
- p.8 (second paragraph): The statement “Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption” is too vague. Please be specific about:
  - Why standard ML approaches under MAR do not apply to (or fail in) the broader missingness mechanisms studied here.
  - Precisely which assumptions are violated, and provide at least one concrete example from your class where ML is inconsistent.
  - If any subclasses of your mgraphs admit consistent ML, state the conditions clearly.

Minor comments and edits
- p.1, line 33: “can bias outcomes” → “can bias inferences and outcomes.”
- p.2, Figure 1: The label “Qualifications” in the caption/text is unclear—please verify and clarify the wording.
- p.3, line 158 (Example 1): The equality should read P(Y | X, Z, R_y) = P(Y | X, Z), not P(X, Z).
- p.3, line 168: “Since Y independent of R given X, Z” should specify the relevant component: “Y independent of R_y given X, Z.”
- p.3, line 214: Condition (ii): R^(part) = {R^(1), R^(2), …, R^(N)} (use consistent superscripts).
- p.3, line 215: “R^j respectively” → “R^(j), respectively.”
- p.4, Corollary 1: “does not contain a partially observed variables” → “does not contain any partially observed variables.”
- p.6, Footnote 2: Please add a reference for the variable-level definition.
- Throughout: Maintain consistent terminology distinguishing R (missingness variables), V* (recorded/proxy variables), V (true variables), and U (unobserved variables). When conditioning on R, indicate the specific components (e.g., R_y) rather than the full vector R where appropriate.

Suggestions to strengthen the paper
- Provide a small, worked example where the joint distribution is not identified but a causal query is, and show exactly how knowledge of the missingness mechanism enables identification.
- Briefly discuss robustness: how sensitive are the identification results to misspecification of the missingness graph? A short remark on practical diagnostics or partial knowledge would be valuable.
- If feasible, sketch how the identification formula translates into estimation procedures, even if only at a high level (e.g., weighting, augmentation, or g-formula analogs), and note any computational implications.

Overall assessment
An interesting paper that builds on earlier work, extends the class of missingness mechanisms for which recoverability is possible, and delivers a novel identification formula with meaningful implications for causal inference. While the practicality of requiring a known censoring mechanism may limit immediate applicability in some ML settings, the theoretical contributions are clear and sound. Addressing the clarifications above would improve readability and broaden the paper’s impact. Not clear how broad the application is, but technically sound.
