- Studies when probabilistic and causal queries are recoverable from data with missing entries using missingness graphs (m-graphs).
- Generalizes sequential factorization to allow missingness indicators (R-nodes) in the ordering, enabling recovery when missingness mechanisms interact.
- Provides algorithms that recover joint distributions via partitions of R variables and their Markov blankets when no admissible sequence exists.
- Introduces new graphical conditions that certify non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions.
- Gives sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects P(y|do(x)) even when outcome Y is inseparable from its missingness mechanism Ry (using do-calculus, d*-separation, dormant independence).
- Applies results to attrition and derives necessary and sufficient graphical conditions and transparent estimands for simple attrition settings.
- Builds on Mohan et al. (2013) and Pearl & Mohan (2013) and connects to established causal identification literature.
- Complements likelihood, IPW, and imputation approaches by providing graphical criteria that determine when those procedures can succeed.
- Clarifies and corrects prior claims (e.g., Garcia 2013) in the attrition context.
- Technical strength: clear generalization of ordered factorizations and constructive recovery formulas beyond admissible-sequence regimes.
- Novel insight: shows causal effects can be recoverable even when related probabilistic quantities (e.g., P(y|x)) are not.
- Practical relevance: provides actionable estimands for randomized studies with dropout (|Vm| = 1).
- Well-structured paper with motivating examples and theorems; proofs provided in the appendix.
- Needs more guidance on eliciting and validating m-graph assumptions in practice (e.g., checklists or testable implications).
- Theorem 2 (partition-based recovery) needs more intuition and a worked example in the main text illustrating collider paths and Markov blankets.
- Should discuss scope and limitations, especially gaps between necessary and sufficient conditions in general MNAR beyond simple attrition.
- Recommend adding high-level pseudocode or a decision flowchart for practitioners to apply the methods.
- Lacks empirical illustration — suggest simulations for finite-sample behavior, robustness to misspecification, and sensitivity to positivity violations.
- Notation can be dense (e.g., minimality in Definition 2); recommend a symbol table and fixing reference formatting inconsistencies.
- Quality: results appear technically sound with precise theorems and proofs.
- Clarity: generally clear but could benefit from improved exposition and streamlined notation.
- Originality: introduces new recoverability and non-recoverability criteria and extends sequential factorization to interacting missingness.
- Significance: tackles MNAR with actionable graphical tests; attrition results likely to influence practice and methodology.
- Pros for acceptance: substantive theoretical advances, important insight on causal recoverability, and relevance to multiple communities.
- Cons for acceptance: limited practical guidance and empirical demonstration, expository density, and incomplete discussion of general completeness.
- Overall assessment: focused, well-written, and original contribution to MNAR recoverability; recommend acceptance after minor improvements to exposition, guidance, and references.
