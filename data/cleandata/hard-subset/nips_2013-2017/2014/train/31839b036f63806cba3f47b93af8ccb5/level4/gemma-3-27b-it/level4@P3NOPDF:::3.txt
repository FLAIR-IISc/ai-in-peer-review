This is an interesting and technically sound paper that builds on earlier work in the field of missing data and causal inference. It addresses the fundamental problem of recovering a distribution given knowledge of the generative process and the mechanism leading to missing data. The authors expand upon previous work, notably that of Mohan et al., by considering a more general class of missingness processes, resulting in a novel identification formula. A particularly strong contribution lies in demonstrating that certain causal queries can be identified even when the observational joint distribution itself is not. While the paper is generally well-written, several points require clarification and could benefit from further elaboration.

**Major Comments:**

(1) A central requirement of the presented results is knowledge of the process by which observations are censored. This raises a practical concern: is such knowledge realistically available in typical machine learning applications? The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of scenarios where this assumption holds, or by acknowledging the limitations in practical deployment. Additionally, the detail within Figure 1—which appears crucial for understanding the notation and setup—could be improved by potentially reducing its size to allow for more explanatory text within the main body of the paper.

(2) Section 2 assumes knowledge of the full population, even in cases where no variables are observed for an individual (Vo = emptyset, Rv = 1 for all V in V_m). This implies we know *that* an individual exists, even if we observe nothing about them. This assumption effectively excludes certain types of missing data problems, such as estimating population sizes (e.g., wildlife estimation), where the very existence of individuals is uncertain. Explicitly stating this limitation and its implications would improve the clarity of the paper’s scope.

(3) The term “mgraph” appears to be used ambiguously. The graphs sometimes include variables from the set V* (as in Figure 1), while at other times they do not (as in Figure 2). If the omission of V* variables is intentional for simplification, this should be explicitly stated and explained. Consistency in the presentation of these graphs is essential for avoiding confusion.

(4) The authors restrict mgraphs by disallowing R variables from being parents of variables in V or U. While they treat the R variables as real quantities rather than simple indicators, the justification for this restriction remains unclear. A detailed explanation of why this constraint is necessary – whether for identifiability, computational tractability, or another reason – is crucial. Alternatively, the authors should acknowledge if this restriction is primarily for convenience and discuss potential avenues for relaxing it in future work.

(5) The term "neighbor" appearing at the bottom of page 4 is undefined. Providing a clear definition of this term is necessary for readers to fully understand the subsequent arguments.

(6) On page 8, the first paragraph refers to the "rectification" of the observation by Garcia et al. The authors’ definition of recoverability relies on the positivity assumption. Does the work of Garcia et al. also require a positivity assumption, or does it operate under a stronger condition? Clarifying this comparison is important.

(7) The statement on page 8, second paragraph, that “Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption” is overly broad. The authors should elaborate on why Maximum Likelihood cannot be directly applied to the missingness processes described in this paper. A more specific explanation of the challenges posed by these processes in the context of standard Maximum Likelihood estimation would be helpful.

**Minor Comments:**

*   Page 1, line 33: "can bias outcomes" – consider revising to “may bias outcomes” or “can introduce bias.”
*   Page 2, Figure 1: “Qualifications” – please clarify what is meant by this label.
*   Page 2, Example 1 on page 3, line 158: The equation P(Y |X,Z,R_y) = P(X,Z) appears incorrect. The right-hand side should be P(Y|X,Z).
*   Page 3, line 168: "Since Y || R | X,Z" should be "Since Y || R_y | X,Z" (using the subscript to denote the specific missingness variable).
*   Page 3, line 214: "Condition (ii) R^{part} ={R^{(1)},R^{(2)}, \ldots , R^N}" should be "Condition (ii) R^{part} ={R^{(1)},R^{(2)}, \ldots , R^{(N)}".
*   Page 3, line 215: “R^j respectively” should be “R^{(j)} respectively”.
*   Page 4, Corollary 1: The statement that it "does not contain a partially observed variables" is unclear. Please rephrase for clarity.
*   Page 6, Footnote 2: Please provide a reference for the variable-level definition mentioned.



In summary, this is a valuable contribution to the literature on missing data and causal inference. Addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding the assumptions, clarity of notation, and practical applicability, will significantly enhance the paper’s impact and accessibility.
