The paper addresses the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data corrupted by missing entries, given a model of the missingness process. It extends the results of Mohan et al. [2013] by presenting more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries of the form P(y|x) and P(y, x) as well as causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)). The authors show that causal queries may be recoverable even when the factors in their identifying estimands are not recoverable.

The paper's technical quality seems high, and the results are very involved but appear to be correct. The authors provide some nice examples, such as the identifiability of causal distributions even when conditional distributions are unavailable. However, some of the results are incremental improvements upon existing ones and do not contribute to a bigger picture of what is really going on.

The paper's main weakness is its clarity. There is probably too much material for a conference paper, as evidenced by the density of the text and the 13-page technical appendix. The article does not flow clearly and is hard to follow as a result. The notation could be improved, and the use of the same capital 'V' to denote different objects makes it hard work for the reader.

The paper deals with an important problem and contains results that seem genuinely interesting and useful. Sections 5 and 6 are clear and potentially quite significant. However, it would be better to step back and work out what is going on in the general case and try to come up with a complete criterion for identifiability in this framework.

The paper's results have implications for handling missing data in various fields, including machine learning and epidemiology. The authors provide a useful approach to recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data corrupted by missing entries. However, the paper's poor presentation and notation may limit its impact.

In terms of the NIPS criteria, the paper's quality is high, but its clarity is a major weakness. The paper's originality and significance are clear, but its impact may be limited by its poor presentation. The paper's relevance to the NIPS community is high, as it addresses an important problem in machine learning and provides a useful approach to handling missing data.
