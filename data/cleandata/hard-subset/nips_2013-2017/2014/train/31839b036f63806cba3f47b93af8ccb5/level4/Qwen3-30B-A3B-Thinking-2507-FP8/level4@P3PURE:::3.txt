

This is an interesting paper addressing the problem of when a distribution can be recovered from knowledge of the generating process and the process leading to missing data. The authors consider a more general class of missingness processes than in earlier work by Mohan et al., leading to a novel identification formula. Additionally, they demonstrate that causal queries can be identified even when the (observational) joint distribution is not identified. The paper is generally well-written, though several points require clarification or correction, as detailed below.

MAIN COMMENTS:  
(1) The results require knowledge of the censoring process. Is this knowledge typically available in machine learning applications? It would be helpful to include more detail about Figure 1 in the main text (e.g., by reducing its size).  
(2) Section 2 assumes the full population is known: even when \(V_o = \emptyset\) and \(R_v = 1\) for all \(v \in V_m\) (i.e., no variables are observed for an individual), the model assumes knowledge of the individual’s existence. This excludes settings like estimating animal population counts.  
(3) The term "m-graph" is used ambiguously. In Figure 1, the graph includes \(V^*\) variables, but in Figure 2, it does not. If \(V^*\) variables are omitted in Figure 2, this should be explicitly stated.  
(4) The paper restricts m-graphs from having \(R\)-variables as parents of \(V\) or \(U\) variables. Given the authors treat \(R\)-variables as real quantities (not merely indicators), they should justify this restriction or clarify its purpose (e.g., convenience).  
(5) The term "neighbor" (bottom of p.4) is undefined.  
(6) On p.8, first paragraph: The authors reference Garcia’s "rectification" of observations. Recoverability as defined here assumes positivity. Do Garcia et al. also assume positivity? If not, their approach may require stronger conditions.  
(7) On p.8, second paragraph: "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption" is overly vague. Why cannot Maximum Likelihood be applied to the missingness processes described here?  

MINOR COMMENTS:  
- p.1, line 33: "can bias outcomes" → "can bias outcomes" (typo).  
- p.2, Figure 1: Add qualifications to the caption.  
- p.3, Example 1, line 158: \(P(Y \mid X,Z,R_y) = P(X,Z)\) → \(P(Y \mid X,Z,R_y) = P(Y \mid X,Z)\).  
- p.3, line 168: "Since \(Y \perp\!\!\!\perp R \mid X,Z\)" → "Since \(Y \perp\!\!\!\perp R_y \mid X,Z\)".  
- p.3, line 214: Condition (ii) \(R^{part} = \{R^{(1)}, R^{(2)}, \ldots, R^N\}\) → \(R^{(part)} = \{R^{(1)}, R^{(2)}, \ldots, R^{(N)}\}\).  
- p.3, line 215: "\(R^j\) respectively" → "\(R^{(j)}\) respectively".  
- p.4, Corollary 1: "does not contain a partially observed variables" → "does not contain partially observed variables".  
- p.6, Footnote 2: Provide a reference for the variable-level definition.  

The paper builds on earlier work with technically sound contributions. However, the scope of practical applications is not clearly established.
