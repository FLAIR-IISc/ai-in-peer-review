

The paper addresses identifiability in missing data mechanisms using Mohan et al.'s framework, presenting improved results and illuminating examples (e.g., causal distribution identifiability without conditional distributions).  

**Quality**  
Technical quality is strong; results are sophisticated yet appear correct. Theorem 3 offers clear, interpretable insights. However, several contributions feel incremental rather than advancing a broader conceptual framework. Section 3, which introduces Theorem 1, appears disconnected from the paper’s core focus and is difficult to parse. Its content resembles Richardson (2009) on Markov blankets, yet the exposition lacks clarity. Quality improves significantly in Sections 5 and 6, which deliver crisp answers and compelling results on causal queries.  

**Clarity**  
Clarity is the paper’s primary weakness. The content exceeds typical conference paper scope (evidenced by dense text and a 13-page appendix), suggesting it would better suit a journal. The presentation suffers from poor notation: the overuse of capital 'V' for distinct objects (e.g., vertex vs. vertex set) creates confusion, and subscripts become illegible. The inconsistency between *Vi* (a vertex) and *Vo* (a vertex set) is particularly jarring. Corollary 1’s set equation exemplifies this poor presentation. Notation should be streamlined to prioritize readability without sacrificing precision.  

**Originality and Significance**  
Sections 5 and 6 present genuinely interesting, potentially significant results on an important problem. However, the paper would benefit from stepping back to develop a unifying criterion for identifiability—mirroring the completeness achieved for causal effects without missing data. It is unrealistic to expect statisticians to sift through multiple papers to locate a specific identifiability result (e.g., as complex as Theorem 1). Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to employ inverse probability weighting, likely because conditioning sets function as Markov blankets for *R(i)* in the mixed graphs. Richardson (2003, 2009) offers relevant context here.  

**Other Comments**  
- p. 4, l. 214: *R^part* is a partition of *R*, not "a set containing partitions." The condition should state that *R_x* and *R_y* "belong to distinct elements of the partition."  
- p. 4, l. 213: Use `\leftrightarrow` instead of `<-->`.  
- Garcia (2013): Is the cited work incorrect, or merely framed differently?  
- Definition 4 and Theorem 4 use an undefined graph notation for "severing edges."  
- *Vo* and *Vm* appear before definition (and may be unnecessary).  
- Language occasionally lacks precision: "oftentimes" → "often."  

**Summary**  
This work tackles a vital problem with useful technical contributions, particularly in Sections 5–6. However, poor notation, excessive density, and a lack of conceptual synthesis undermine its impact. The paper would be significantly stronger with clearer notation, tighter organization, and a more cohesive narrative that illuminates the broader identifiability landscape. While the results are technically sound, their presentation obscures their potential significance. A journal-length revision addressing these issues would be highly valuable.
