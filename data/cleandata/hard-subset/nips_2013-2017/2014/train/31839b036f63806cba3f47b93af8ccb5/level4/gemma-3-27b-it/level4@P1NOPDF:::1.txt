The paper addresses the problem of identifiability in the presence of various missing data mechanisms, building on the framework introduced by Mohan and others. It improves upon existing results and provides illustrative examples, such as demonstrating identifiability of causal distributions even when conditional distributions are unavailable.

**Quality**

The technical quality appears high; the results are complex but seem correct. Results like Theorem 3 are clear and interpretable. However, some contributions feel incremental and, as far as I can tell, do not contribute to a broader understanding of the underlying principles. 

Section 3 feels somewhat disconnected from the rest of the paper, and its main result (Theorem 1) is difficult to follow. It appears closely related to the Markov blanket results presented in Richardson (2009). The quality improves in later sections, with Section 5 offering clear and insightful answers, and Section 6 presenting interesting results regarding causal queries.

---
**Clarity**

Clarity is the paper’s primary weakness. The sheer volume of material, coupled with its density and a 13-page technical appendix, suggests this work might be better suited for a longer, more comprehensive journal publication than a conference paper. The article lacks a clear flow, making it challenging to follow the arguments.

While finding suitable notation for numerous quantities is inherently difficult, the notation could be significantly improved. The repeated use of the capital letter ‘V’ to represent different objects creates confusion, and the subscripts occasionally become illegible. The inconsistency in representing *V<sub>i</sub>* as a vertex and *V<sub>o</sub>* as a set of vertices is particularly problematic. The set equation in Corollary 1 exemplifies poor presentation.

---
**Originality and Significance**

Sections 5 and 6 are particularly clear and interesting, and potentially represent a significant contribution. The paper tackles an important problem and contains results that appear genuinely useful. 

However, a more holistic approach to understanding the general case would be beneficial. Ideally, the authors could develop a comprehensive criterion for identifiability within this framework, mirroring the success achieved in the area of causal effect estimation without missing data. It is unlikely a statistician would meticulously search through a series of papers to find the specific result addressing the identifiability of a particular causal effect, especially when that result is as intricate as Theorem 1.

Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to leverage inverse probability weighting. The underlying reason for their effectiveness likely stems from the conditioning sets functioning as Markov blankets for *R<sub>i</sub>* within the mixed graphs used by the authors. Richardson (2003) and (2009) may offer relevant insights.
