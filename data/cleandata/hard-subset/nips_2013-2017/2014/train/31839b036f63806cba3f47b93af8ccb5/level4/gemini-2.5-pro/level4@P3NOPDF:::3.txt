### **Summary**

This is an interesting and technically sound paper that addresses the problem of recovering a probability distribution from data with missing values. The authors' primary contribution is to generalize previous work (e.g., Mohan et al.) by considering a broader class of missingness processes, which are represented graphically. This generalization leads to a novel identification formula for the true underlying distribution. A particularly significant result is that the paper demonstrates the ability to identify certain causal queries even in cases where the full joint observational distribution is not identifiable. The paper is generally well-written, but there are several areas where clarity could be improved and key assumptions could be further justified.

### **Main Comments**

The paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the literature on missing data. The strengths lie in the formal graphical representation of complex missingness mechanisms and the resulting identification criteria. The extension to causal queries is especially noteworthy. However, the following points should be addressed to strengthen the paper.

**(1) Practicality of Assumptions:** The proposed method hinges on knowing the graph that describes the missingness process. The authors should comment on the practical applicability of this assumption. In which typical machine learning applications or data-collection scenarios would a researcher have access to this detailed causal knowledge about why data is missing? A discussion of this point would help ground the theoretical contributions in real-world contexts.

**(2) Scope of the Missing Data Problem:** The framework presented appears to assume that the existence of every individual in the population is known, even if all variables for that individual are missing (e.g., if Vo = ∅ and Rv = 1 for all V in V_m). This assumption should be stated explicitly in Section 2. It would be helpful for the authors to clarify that this formulation excludes problems where the population size itself is an unknown quantity to be estimated (such as in capture-recapture studies), thereby better defining the scope of their method.

**(3) Ambiguity of "m-graph" Definition:** The term "m-graph" seems to be used inconsistently. For example, the graph in Figure 1 includes the surrogate variables V*, while the graph in Figure 2 does not. If V* variables are considered a formal part of the m-graph definition and are simply omitted in some diagrams for visual clarity, this should be explicitly stated. A precise and consistently applied definition is needed to avoid confusion.

**(4) Justification for Structural Restrictions:** The paper states that m-graphs do not permit R variables to be parents of variables in V or U. Given that the R variables are treated as real quantities and not merely as abstract indicators, this is a significant structural constraint. The authors should explain the justification for this restriction. Is it a necessary condition for the theoretical results to hold, or a simplifying assumption made for convenience? Clarifying this will help readers understand the fundamental limitations and boundaries of the proposed framework.

**(5) Undefined Terminology:** The term "neighbor," used at the bottom of page 4, is not defined. Please provide a formal definition in the context of the graphs used in the paper.

**(6) Comparison to Prior Work:** On page 8, the paper discusses the "rectification" of observations by Garcia et al. The authors' definition of recoverability relies on a positivity assumption. To provide a clearer comparison, could the authors clarify whether Garcia et al. also assume positivity? If their method does not, it may rely on stronger conditions, and a discussion of this difference would be insightful.

**(7) Specificity on Maximum Likelihood Estimation:** The statement on page 8 that the "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption" is too vague. To better motivate the authors' contribution, it would be more effective to explain why standard MLE fails for the more general MNAR processes considered in this paper. Please elaborate on the specific challenges that your proposed method overcomes that standard MLE cannot address in this expanded setting.
