This paper proposes novel necessary and sufficient conditions to guarantee whether a causal query can be inferred from data in the presence of missing observations. It extends previous works that explicitly model the missing data generating mechanism into the causal diagram.

The authors provide a comprehensive overview of the problem of deciding whether a causal or probabilistic query is estimable from data corrupted by missing entries, given a model of missingness process. They introduce the concept of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to explicitly portray the missingness process and dependencies among variables in the available dataset.

The paper is clearly written, and the authors do an excellent job of explaining complex concepts and providing illustrative examples. The organization of the paper is logical, and the notation is well-defined.

One of the significant contributions of this paper is the extension of the results of Mohan et al. [2013] by presenting more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries of the form P(y|x) and P(y, x) as well as causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)). The authors also derive graphical conditions for recovering causal effects of the form P(y|do(x)) when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable.

The authors apply their results to problems of attrition and characterize the recovery of causal effects from data corrupted by attrition. They also discuss related work and provide a comprehensive list of references.

Strengths of the paper include:

* The paper provides a comprehensive overview of the problem of deciding whether a causal or probabilistic query is estimable from data corrupted by missing entries.
* The authors introduce the concept of Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to explicitly portray the missingness process and dependencies among variables in the available dataset.
* The paper is clearly written, and the authors do an excellent job of explaining complex concepts and providing illustrative examples.
* The authors derive novel necessary and sufficient conditions to guarantee whether a causal query can be inferred from data in the presence of missing observations.

Weaknesses of the paper include:

* The paper assumes a high level of technical expertise in the field of causal inference and missing data. Some readers may find the notation and concepts challenging to follow.
* The paper could benefit from more discussion on the limitations of the proposed approach and potential avenues for future research.

Overall, this is a well-written and technically sound paper that makes a valuable contribution to the state of the art in the analysis of causal mechanisms in the presence of missing data. I strongly recommend its acceptance for publication.
