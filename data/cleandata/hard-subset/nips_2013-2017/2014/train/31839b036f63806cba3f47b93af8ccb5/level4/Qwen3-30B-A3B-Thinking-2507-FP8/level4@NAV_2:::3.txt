

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) by establishing more general graphical conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (P(y|x) and P(y,x)) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))) from data corrupted by missing entries. The authors introduce the concept of "missingness graphs" (m-graphs) to model the missingness process and demonstrate that causal queries may be recoverable even when the factors in their identifying estimands (such as P(y,x) and P(y|x)) are not recoverable. Specifically, they derive conditions for recovering causal effects when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable, and apply their results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by addressing an important gap in the literature: while prior work established conditions for recovering probabilistic queries, this paper shows that causal queries can sometimes be recovered even when the corresponding probabilistic queries cannot. This is a novel and important insight that advances our understanding of missing data mechanisms. The results are technically sound, with rigorous proofs provided in the appendix, and the paper is well-organized with clear exposition of the key concepts.

Strengths:
1. Originality: The paper provides a substantial extension of Mohan et al. (2013) to a more general class of missingness processes, including cases where missingness mechanisms interact with one another. The demonstration that causal queries may be recoverable even when Y and Ry are not d-separable is particularly novel and represents a significant theoretical contribution.
2. Quality: The theoretical results are well-supported by rigorous proofs and examples. The paper carefully addresses the limitations of previous work and provides clear graphical conditions for recoverability.
3. Significance: The results have important implications for causal inference in longitudinal studies where attrition is common. The paper provides necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for recovering causal effects under simple attrition, which rectifies previous misunderstandings in the literature (e.g., Garcia, 2013).
4. Clarity: The paper is generally well-written with clear explanations of technical concepts. The structure is logical, moving from foundational concepts to more complex results and applications.

Weaknesses:
1. Clarity: The definition of m-graphs is ambiguous in some places. The authors sometimes include V* variables in the graphs (e.g., Figure 1) and sometimes omit them (e.g., Figure 2), without explaining this inconsistency. The term "neighbor" appears on page 4 without definition.
2. Significance: The paper does not adequately address whether the knowledge of the missingness process (required for the approach) is typically available in machine learning applications. The authors should discuss the practical applicability of their methods in real-world settings.
3. Quality: The paper assumes positivity (P(Vo, Vm, R=0) > 0) without discussing whether this is a restrictive assumption in practice. The claim that "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption" is too vague and needs clarification.
4. Clarity: The assumption that the full population is known (even when Vo is emptyset) is not explicitly stated. This excludes certain missing data problems like estimating animal populations, which should be acknowledged.

Pros for acceptance:
- Significant theoretical contribution extending Mohan et al. (2013)
- Novel insight that causal queries can be recoverable when probabilistic queries are not
- Rigorous proofs and clear graphical conditions
- Important application to attrition problems in longitudinal studies

Cons for acceptance:
- Ambiguous definition of m-graphs (inconsistent inclusion of V* variables)
- Insufficient discussion of practical applicability in machine learning contexts
- Unexplained assumption about the full population being known
- Some technical terms not properly defined

Overall, this is a technically sound paper that makes an important theoretical contribution to the field of missing data analysis. The results are significant for causal inference in the presence of missing data, particularly in longitudinal studies with attrition. With some clarification of the m-graph definition and discussion of practical applicability, this paper would be an excellent contribution to the field.
