This paper addresses the problem of recovering a distribution from knowledge of the generating process and the process leading to missing data, which is an interesting and relevant topic. The authors consider a more general class of missingness processes than previously studied, leading to a novel identification formula. Furthermore, they investigate the identification of causal queries and demonstrate that these can be identified even when the observational joint distribution is not identified.

The paper is well-written, but there are some areas that require improvement, which are outlined below.

MAIN COMMENTS:

(1) The results presented in this paper rely on knowledge of the process by which observations are censored. However, it is unclear whether this knowledge is readily available in typical machine learning applications. To enhance the paper, it would be beneficial to include more details regarding Figure 1 in the main text. This could be achieved by reducing the size of the figure.

(2) In Section 2, it should be noted that the missing data processes considered here assume that the full population is known. This is an important assumption, as it excludes missing data problems such as estimating the number of animals in a population, even if no variables are observed for a particular individual.

(3) The term "mgraph" appears to be used ambiguously throughout the paper. In some instances, the graph includes the V* variables (Figure 1), while in others, it does not (Figure 2). If the V* variables are being omitted in these graphs, this should be explicitly stated.

(4) The paper does not allow mgraphs to have R variables as parents of variables in V or U. Given that the authors treat the R variables as real quantities rather than simply indicators, they should provide a justification for this restriction or acknowledge that it is mainly a matter of convenience.

(5) The term "neighbor" is introduced at the bottom of page 4 without a clear definition. It would be helpful to provide a definition or explanation for this term.

(6) On page 8, the first paragraph mentions the "rectification" of the observation by Garcia. However, the authors' definition of recoverability assumes positivity. It is unclear whether Garcia et al. also assume positivity, and if not, they may require a stronger condition.

(7) The statement on page 8, second paragraph, that the "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption" is too vague. The authors should provide a clearer explanation of why maximum likelihood cannot be applied to any of the missingness processes described in the paper.

MINOR COMMENTS:

* Page 1, line 33: "can bias outcomes" should be rephrased for clarity.
* Page 2, Figure 1: The qualifications for the figure could be included in the main text.
* Page 2, Example 1, line 158: The equation P(Y | X, Z, R_y) = P(X, Z) should be corrected to P(Y | X, Z).
* Page 3, line 168: The equation should be corrected to Y || R_y | X, Z.
* Page 3, line 214: Condition (ii) should be corrected to R^(N).
* Page 3, line 215: R^j should be corrected to R^(j).
* Page 4, Corollary 1: The statement does not contain partially observed variables.
* Page 6, Footnote 2: A reference for the variable-level definition should be provided.

Overall, this is an interesting paper that builds upon earlier work. While the technical soundness of the paper is clear, the breadth of its application is less apparent.
