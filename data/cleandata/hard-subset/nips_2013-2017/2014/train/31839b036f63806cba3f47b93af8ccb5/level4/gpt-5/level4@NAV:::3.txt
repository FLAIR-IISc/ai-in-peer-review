Review- This is a compelling manuscript. It tackles the question of when a distribution can be recovered given knowledge of the data-generating mechanism and the process that induces missingness. The authors consider a broader class of missingness mechanisms than in prior work by Mohan et al., which yields a new identification formula. They also address identification of causal queries, showing these can be identified even when the observational joint distribution itself is not. The paper is generally well written, though there are a few issues and areas for improvement outlined below.

MAIN COMMENTS:
(1) The results depend on knowing the censoring mechanism. Is such knowledge realistic in typical machine learning applications? It would help to bring more of the detail associated with Figure 1 into the main text (this could be done by reducing the figure’s size).
(2) Section 2. It should be stated that the missing-data frameworks here presume the entire population is known: even if Vo = emptyset, and Rv = 1 for all V in V_m (so no variables are observed for this individual), it is still assumed we know this individual exists. This rules out problems like estimating the size of an animal population.
(3) The term mgraph appears to be used ambiguously. At times the graph includes the V* variables (Figure 1), while elsewhere it does not (Figure 2). If the V* nodes are simply being omitted for readability, please make this explicit.
(4) In this paper, mgraphs do not allow R variables to be parents of variables in V or U. Since the R variables are treated as substantive quantities rather than mere indicators, please justify this restriction (or clarify if it is primarily for convenience).
(5) The term “neighbor,” which appears at the bottom of p.4, is not defined.
(6) p.8, first paragraph. “Rectification” of the observation by Garcia. Recoverability as defined by the authors assumes positivity. Do Garcia et al. also assume positivity? If not, their condition may be stronger.
(7) p.8, second paragraph. “Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption.” This is too vague. Why can’t maximum likelihood be applied to any of the missingness processes described here?
