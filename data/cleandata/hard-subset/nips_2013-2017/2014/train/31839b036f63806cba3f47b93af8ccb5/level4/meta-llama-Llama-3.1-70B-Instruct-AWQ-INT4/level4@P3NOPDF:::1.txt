This paper addresses the problem of identifiability in the presence of various data generation mechanisms for missing data, utilizing a framework introduced by Mohan and others. The authors build upon existing results and provide insightful examples, such as the identifiability of causal distributions even when conditional distributions are unavailable.

Quality
--------

The technical quality of the paper appears to be high, with the results being complex but seemingly correct. Certain results, such as Theorem 3, are clear and interpretable. However, some of the results are incremental improvements upon existing ones and do not contribute to a broader understanding of the subject matter.

Section 3 seems unrelated to the rest of the paper, and its primary result, Theorem 1, is challenging to comprehend. This section appears to be closely related to the Markov blanket results in Richardson (2009). The quality of the paper improves in later sections, with Section 5 providing clear answers and Section 6 presenting interesting results about causal queries.

Clarity
--------

This is the paper's primary weakness. The sheer amount of material presented may be too much for a conference paper, as evidenced by the dense text and the 13-page technical appendix. The article lacks a clear flow, making it difficult to follow. This work might be better suited as a longer, more in-depth journal paper.

The notation used in the paper could be improved. Utilizing the same capital 'V' to denote multiple objects can be confusing for the reader, and in some cases, the subscripts become illegible. Specifically, the use of 'Vi' to represent a vertex and 'Vo' to represent a set of vertices is particularly perplexing. The set equation in Corollary 1 is an extreme example of poor presentation.

Originality and Significance
-----------------------------

Sections 5 and 6 are clear, interesting, and potentially significant. This paper addresses an important problem and contains results that seem genuinely interesting and useful. However, it might be beneficial to take a step back and work towards developing a complete criterion for identifiability in this framework, similar to what was achieved in the case of finding causal effects without missingness.

Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to be a form of inverse probability weighting. The reason these results work is likely related to the conditioning sets being Markov blankets for R(i) in the mixed graphs used by the author. Richardson (2003) and (2009) might be relevant in this context.

Other Comments
--------------

* p4, l214: R^part is a partition of R, not "a set containing partitions." The condition on line 214 should then be that Rx and Ry "belong to distinct elements of the partition."
* The language used is sometimes casual, such as the use of 'oftentimes' (p4, l213). Consider using more formal language throughout the paper.
* Instead of '<-->', use '\leftrightarrow' (p4, l213).
* It is unclear whether Garcia (2013) is actually incorrect or simply uses a different framework. Clarification on this point would be beneficial.
* A notation for graphs constructed by severing edges is used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4 but is not defined anywhere in the paper.
* Vo and Vm are used in the sentence before they are defined, which appears to be unnecessary.
