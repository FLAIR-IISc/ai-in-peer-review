

This is an interesting paper addressing the problem of recovering a distribution given knowledge of the generating process and the missing data mechanism. The authors consider a more general class of missingness processes than in earlier work by Mohan et al., yielding a novel identification formula. Additionally, they establish identification of causal queries even when the (observational) joint distribution is not identifiable. The paper is generally well-written, though it contains minor errors and areas requiring clarification, as detailed below.

MAIN COMMENTS:
(1) The results require knowledge of the censoring process. Is this typically available in machine learning applications? It would be beneficial to include more detail about Figure 1 in the main text (e.g., by reducing its size).
(2) Section 2 should clarify that the missing data processes assume the full population is known: even when no variables are observed for an individual (V₀ = ∅, Rᵥ = 1 for all V ∈ Vₘ), the individual's existence is assumed. This excludes problems like estimating animal population sizes.
(3) The term "mgraph" is used ambiguously: it includes V* variables in Figure 1 but omits them in Figure 2. If V* variables are omitted in some graphs, this must be explicitly stated.
(4) The paper restricts mgraphs from having R variables as parents of V or U variables. Given R variables are treated as real quantities (not indicators), the justification for this restriction (or its convenience) should be explained.
(5) The term "neighbor" (bottom of p.4) is undefined.
(6) On p.8, first paragraph: "rectification" by Garcia. The authors' recoverability definition assumes positivity; it is unclear if Garcia et al. make this assumption. If not, their approach may require a stronger condition.
(7) On p.8, second paragraph: "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption" is overly vague. Why cannot Maximum Likelihood be applied to any missingness process described here?

An interesting paper building on prior work. Not clear how broadly applicable the results are, but technically sound.
