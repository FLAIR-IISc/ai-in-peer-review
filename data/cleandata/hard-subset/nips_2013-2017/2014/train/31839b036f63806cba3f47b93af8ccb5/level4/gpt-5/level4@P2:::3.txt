Summary
The paper studies when probabilistic and causal queries are recoverable from data with missing entries, given an explicit model of the missingness mechanism encoded by missingness graphs (m-graphs). It extends Mohan et al. (2013) in three main ways: (i) it generalizes the sequential factorization approach by allowing R-variables (missingness indicators/mechanisms) to appear in admissible ordered factorizations; (ii) it provides algorithms and graphical conditions to recover joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists; and (iii) it supplies non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions. Importantly, it shows that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when related probabilistic quantities (e.g., P(y,x) or P(y|x)) are not, including cases where Y is not d-separated from its missingness mechanism Ry. The paper also analyzes attrition and offers necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects in that setting. These contributions connect the MNAR literature with causal identification (via do-calculus and d*/dormant independences), building directly on Mohan et al. (2013) and related work by Pearl, Shpitser, and others.

Strengths
- Clear and substantive technical advancements beyond Mohan et al. (2013), including more general factorization criteria (Theorem 1) and recovery formulas (Theorem 2; corollaries).
- Novel non-recoverability conditions (Theorem 3; Corollary 2) that clarify limits of inference.
- Insightful result that some causal effects are recoverable even when key probabilistic relations are not (Theorem 4), with a concrete attrition application (Theorems 5–6).
- The work is well-motivated, relevant to MNAR scenarios, and rigorous (proofs in appendix).

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- The approach assumes a correctly specified missingness model. Practical availability and elicitation of such models in typical ML applications are not discussed in depth.
- Some definitional/notation issues (e.g., “neighbor,” inclusion of V* in m-graphs) could be clarified.
- Restrictions on parents of R-variables are asserted but not fully justified.
- Statements about maximum likelihood under MAR are too general and may mislead.

Quality
The results appear technically sound and carefully stated, with clear use of graphical separation, Markov blankets, collider paths, and do-calculus. Proofs are deferred but the claims are plausible and consistent with prior causal/missing data theory.

Clarity
Generally well-written, but several definitions and notational choices should be tightened, and some examples/figures need small corrections for precision.

Originality
The generalization of ordered factorization to include R-variables, the broader recoverability algorithm for semi-Markovian models without admissible sequences, and the non-recoverability criteria are original contributions. The demonstration that causal effects can be recoverable despite inseparability of Y and Ry is insightful.

Significance
If adopted, the graphical criteria can guide analysts in MNAR settings, especially in longitudinal studies and attrition. The results may influence both methodological development and applied practice.

Arguments for acceptance
- Meaningful theoretical advances on a core problem (MNAR recoverability).
- Clear connection to and extension of established causal identification tools.
- Practical value through explicit criteria and examples (attrition).

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on a known, correct missingness graph; practical guidance on model assessment/elicitation is limited.
- Some presentation issues (definitions, notation, minor errors) hinder readability.
- Scope of empirical applicability could be further illustrated.

Main comments
1) Assumed knowledge of the missingness process. The results require specifying the m-graph, including interactions among R-variables. Please discuss how such knowledge is obtained in typical ML workflows, how sensitive recovery is to misspecification, and whether graphical tests (e.g., Mohan & Pearl, 2014) can be leveraged here. Consider bringing more detail from Figure 1 into the main text (possibly by resizing) to exemplify elicitation.

2) Population frame assumption. The framework assumes the full population is known even when all substantive variables for some units are missing (Vo = ∅ and Rv = 1 for all V ∈ Vm). Please state this explicitly and clarify that problems like unknown-population-size estimation (e.g., capture–recapture) are out of scope.

3) Definition of m-graph and inclusion of V*. The paper alternates between m-graphs that include V* (Figure 1) and those that omit them (Figure 2). If omission is for clarity and V* are proxies deterministically defined by Equation (1), please state the convention up front.

4) Restricting parents of R. You disallow R-variables from being parents of V or U. Since R is treated as a substantive causal mechanism, please justify this restriction (e.g., interpretational, identifiability, or convenience), and discuss how results might extend if R→V or R→U edges were permitted.

5) Define “neighbor.” The term appears (p.4) without a formal definition. Please define neighbors/adjacency in the semi-Markovian setting you use.

6) Positivity and comparison to Garcia (2013). Your recoverability definition assumes positivity on complete cases. When you “rectify” Garcia’s observation, clarify whether Garcia also assumes positivity; if not, please specify whether your condition is strictly stronger and how conclusions depend on it.

7) Maximum likelihood under MAR. The statement “ML yields consistent estimates under MAR” is too broad. ML can be applied under a variety of missingness mechanisms, but consistency depends on correct likelihood specification (including the missingness model under MNAR). Please refine this discussion and contrast with your graphical approach, which targets recoverability under specific MNAR structures.

Minor comments and typos
- p.1, line 33: “can bias outcomes” → “can bias estimates and conclusions.”
- p.2, Figure 1: “Qualifications” likely needs clarification or re-labeling.
- p.3, Example 1 (line ~158): “P(Y|X,Z,Ry) = P(X,Z)” → should be “P(Y|X,Z,Ry) = P(Y|X,Z).”
- p.3, line ~168: “Since Y ⟂⟂ R | X,Z” → should be “Since Y ⟂⟂ Ry | X,Z.”
- p.3, line ~214: “Rpart = {R(1), R(2), …, R^N}” → use consistent notation “R(N).”
- p.3, line ~215: “R^j” → “R(j).”
- p.4, Corollary 1: “does not contain a partially observed variables” → “does not contain a partially observed variable.”
- p.6, Footnote 2: Please provide a citation for the variable-level definition.

Overall evaluation
A strong and technically solid paper that advances the theory of recoverability under MNAR, with important insights for causal inference. With clarifications on assumptions, definitions, and practical elicitation of m-graphs, the paper would be a valuable contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
