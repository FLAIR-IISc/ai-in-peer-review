This is an interesting paper that addresses the problem of recovering a distribution given knowledge of both the data-generating process and the process that causes data to be missing. The authors consider a more general class of missingness processes than in earlier work by Mohan et al., leading to a novel identification formula. Furthermore, the paper examines the identification of causal queries, showing they can be identified even when the full observational joint distribution is not.

The paper is generally well-written, but there are several areas that could be improved for clarity.

**MAIN COMMENTS:**

1.  The proposed results require knowledge of the process by which observations are censored. The authors should comment on how readily this knowledge is available in typical machine learning applications. It would also be helpful to include more of the technical detail regarding Figure 1 in the main text, which could be accomplished by reducing the figure's size to create space.

2.  In Section 2, it should be clarified that the missing data processes considered assume the full population size is known. That is, even if no variables are observed for an individual (i.e., V_o = ∅ and R_v = 1 for all V in V_m), their existence is still assumed. This implicitly excludes certain classes of missing data problems, such as population size estimation.

3.  The term "m-graph" appears to be used ambiguously. Specifically, the graph in Figure 1 includes the V* variables, whereas the graph in Figure 2 does not. If the V* variables are simply being omitted for presentation in some figures, this convention should be explicitly stated.

4.  The m-graph framework presented here does not allow R variables to be parents of variables in V or U. Given that the authors treat the R variables as real-world quantities and not simply as indicators, they should explain the justification for this structural restriction or clarify if it is a simplifying assumption made for convenience.

5.  The term "neighbor," which appears at the bottom of page 4, is not formally defined.

6.  On page 8, the paper discusses the "rectification" of observations by Garcia et al. The authors' definition of recoverability assumes positivity. The review would be strengthened by a discussion of whether Garcia et al. also assume positivity. If not, their method might require a stronger condition, and this distinction should be noted.

7.  The statement on page 8 that the "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption" is too vague. The authors should elaborate on why standard maximum likelihood estimation cannot be applied to the more general missingness processes described in this work.

**MINOR COMMENTS:**

*   **p. 1, line 33:** "can bias outcomes" should be revised for clarity (e.g., "can introduce bias into outcomes").
*   **p. 2, Figure 1 caption:** "Qualifications" seems out of place; please clarify its meaning.
*   **p. 3, line 158 (Example 1):** The equation `P(Y |X,Z,R_y) = P(X,Z)` appears to have a typo on the right-hand side, which should likely be `P(Y|X,Z)`.
*   **p. 3, line 168:** "Since Y || R | X,Z" should probably be "Since Y || R_y | X,Z".
*   **p. 3, line 214:** In Condition (ii), `R^{part} ={R^{(1)},R^{(2)}, \ldots , R^N}` should be `R^{(N)}`.
*   **p. 3, line 215:** `R^j` should be `R^{(j)}`.
*   **p. 4, Corollary 1:** The phrase "does not contain a partially observed variables" is grammatically incorrect.
*   **p. 6, Footnote 2:** Please provide a reference for the variable-level definition of MAR.

In summary, this is a technically sound paper that builds on important earlier work. While its immediate breadth of application could be better articulated, the theoretical contributions are solid.
