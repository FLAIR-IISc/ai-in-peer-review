

Quality  
The paper addresses identifiability under various missing data mechanisms using Mohan et al.'s framework, improving some existing results and providing illustrative examples (e.g., causal distribution identifiability without conditional distributions). Technical quality is high; results are intricate yet appear correct, with Theorem 3 being clear and interpretable. However, several contributions represent incremental advances without advancing the broader conceptual understanding. Section 3 appears disconnected from the core narrative, and Theorem 1 is difficult to grasp, likely relating to Richardson (2009) on Markov blankets. Later sections (5 and 6) demonstrate stronger quality, offering clear insights and interesting causal query results.  

Clarity  
This is the paper’s primary weakness. The content is overly dense for a conference paper (evidenced by 13-page appendix and text density), hindering flow and readability—this would better suit a journal. Notation requires significant improvement: overuse of capital 'V' for distinct objects complicates reading, subscripts become illegible, and the inconsistent use of Vi (vertex) vs. Vo (set of vertices) is particularly confusing. Corollary 1 exemplifies poor presentation.  

Originality and Significance  
Sections 5 and 6 present clear, significant contributions to an important problem. The results are genuinely interesting and useful. However, the work would benefit from synthesizing a general identifiability criterion within this framework (analogous to causal effect identification without missingness). The current approach risks requiring statisticians to navigate multiple complex theorems (e.g., Theorem 1) to determine identifiability for their specific query. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 likely reflect inverse probability weighting, potentially linked to Markov blankets for R(i) in mixed graphs; Richardson (2003, 2009) warrants consideration.
