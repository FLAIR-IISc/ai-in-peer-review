Summary
The paper studies identifiability under a variety of missing-data mechanisms within the graphical framework introduced by Mohan and colleagues. It strengthens several existing results and provides instructive examples, including cases where causal effects are identifiable even when certain conditional distributions are unavailable.

Quality
The technical development appears sound. The arguments are intricate but, on inspection, seem correct, and results such as Theorem 3 are both clear and interpretable. However, some contributions feel incremental relative to prior work, and their role in a broader, unifying picture is not fully articulated. Section 3 appears orthogonal to the main thrust; its central result (Theorem 1) is difficult to parse and to relate to the rest of the paper. By contrast, the later parts are stronger: Section 5 presents crisp results, and Section 6 offers interesting insights on causal queries.

Clarity
Clarity is the main weakness. The manuscript likely attempts to cover too much for a conference paper, as reflected in dense prose and a 13-page technical appendix. The narrative flow suffers as a result, making the paper harder to follow than necessary. The notation, while challenging to manage in this setting, could be improved materially:
- The repeated use of the capital V for multiple objects (individual vertices, sets of vertices, observed/missing subsets) creates confusion; subscripts and superscripts sometimes become illegible in complex expressions.
- The coexistence of Vi as a vertex and Vo as a set of vertices is especially confusing.
- The set expression in Corollary 1 is a particularly opaque instance of the notation problem.

Concrete suggestions to improve readability:
- Reduce scope for the conference version. Consider moving Section 3 and some proofs to the appendix or a journal version, and tighten the connection between sections.
- Adopt distinct symbols (e.g., different letters or font styles) for individual variables, sets of variables, and observed/missing partitions. Avoid overloading V.
- Define all graph operations (e.g., edge severing) when first introduced, and ensure all sets (Vo, Vm) are defined before use.
- Add a running example to illustrate key definitions and results as they appear.

Originality and Significance
Sections 5 and 6 are novel, clear, and potentially significant. The paper addresses an important problem and contains results that seem genuinely useful. At the same time, the presentation would benefit from a more explicit attempt to synthesize a general criterion for identifiability within this framework—analogous to the complete criteria available for causal effects without missingness—so that practitioners are not forced to search across multiple papers for case-specific results. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 read as instances of inverse probability weighting; the intuition likely turns on conditioning on Markov blankets of R(i) in the relevant mixed graphs, suggesting connections to Richardson (2003, 2009). Making these connections explicit would both clarify the logic and situate the contribution within the broader literature.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Section 3/Theorem 1: Clarify the conceptual purpose of this section and its relationship to later results. If it is primarily technical scaffolding, streamline or defer to the appendix. If it provides a core insight, motivate it with an example and intuitive explanation.
- Theorem 3: This is a highlight—keep and perhaps foreground it earlier. Consider adding a brief intuition paragraph.
- Sections 5–6: These are the clearest and most impactful parts. If space is tight, prioritize these sections and the main identifiability examples in the conference version.
- Relationship to prior work: Elaborate on how your conditions relate to Markov blanket-based separation in acyclic directed mixed graphs (ADMGs) and to results of Richardson (2003, 2009). If Theorem 2/Corollary 1 instantiate IPW, say so explicitly and discuss when these forms are necessary/sufficient.
- Scope and generality: Discuss prospects for a complete identifiability criterion in this missing-data graphical setting. Even a partial roadmap would be valuable to readers.

Minor and editorial
- Page 4, line 214: R^part is a partition of R, not “a set containing partitions.” The condition should read that Rx and Ry “belong to distinct elements of the partition.”
- Tone: Replace casual expressions such as “oftentimes” with more formal alternatives (e.g., “often”).
- Page 4, line 213: Use \leftrightarrow rather than the ASCII <-->.
- Clarify whether Garcia (2013) is incorrect under your assumptions or simply addresses a different framework; specify the exact points of divergence.
- Define the notation for graphs constructed by severing edges when it first appears (used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4).
- Ensure Vo and Vm are defined before they are first mentioned; avoid forward references where unnecessary.
- Proof presentation: Where algebraic displays become dense (e.g., Corollary 1), consider breaking into smaller steps or moving long set-theoretic expressions to the appendix with a more reader-friendly summary in the main text.

Overall assessment
An important problem tackled with a solid and promising approach, with several results of clear interest—especially in Sections 5 and 6. However, the current presentation and notation substantially impede accessibility. Tightening the scope for the conference version, clarifying the role of Section 3, cleaning up notation, and making explicit connections to related work (e.g., Richardson’s ADMG results and IPW) would markedly strengthen the paper.
