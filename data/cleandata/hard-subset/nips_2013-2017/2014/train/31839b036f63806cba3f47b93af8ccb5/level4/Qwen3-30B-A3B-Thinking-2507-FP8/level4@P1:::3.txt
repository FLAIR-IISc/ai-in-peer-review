

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) to develop more general conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries. While Mohan et al. focused on cases where missingness mechanisms do not interact, the authors generalize to scenarios where missingness mechanisms can influence each other. The key contributions include: (1) novel graphical conditions for recovering joint and conditional distributions; (2) the important insight that causal queries like P(y|do(x)) may be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations (such as P(y,x)) are not; and (3) specific applications to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.

The paper is technically sound and makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of missing data analysis. The authors correctly build upon Mohan et al. (2013) while extending their framework to handle more complex missingness mechanisms. The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries is particularly novel and important, as it reveals a fundamental difference in how missingness affects different types of queries. The graphical criteria presented are well-motivated and clearly articulated, with theorems supported by detailed examples and proofs in the appendix.

The paper is generally well-written, though some sections could be clarified for better accessibility. The authors effectively demonstrate that causal effects can be recoverable even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable, which challenges prevailing assumptions in the literature. For example, in Figure 3, the paper shows that P(y|do(z)) can be recovered despite the collider path between Y and Ry, which would prevent recovery of the joint distribution P(y,z,w).

However, I have several concerns about the paper's assumptions and presentation:

First, the paper assumes knowledge of the missingness process, which may not be realistic in many machine learning applications. It would strengthen the paper to discuss when such knowledge might be available in practice and how to handle cases where it's not. The authors briefly mention this in the draft, but it deserves more thorough discussion.

Second, the paper's restriction that R variables cannot be parents of V or U variables needs better justification. The authors describe R variables as "real quantities," but it's unclear why this restriction is theoretically necessary rather than merely convenient. This limitation significantly narrows the applicability of their framework.

Third, the term "m-graph" is used inconsistently. Sometimes the graph includes the V* variables (as in Figure 1), while other times it does not (as in Figure 2). The authors should clarify whether V* variables are omitted in certain figures or if they're part of the m-graph definition, and explain the implications.

Fourth, the term "neighbor" appears without definition on page 4, which may confuse readers unfamiliar with graph theory terminology. A brief definition would improve clarity.

Fifth, the paper assumes positivity (P(Vo, Vm, R=0) > 0) for recoverability, while Garcia (2013) does not. This discrepancy should be addressed, as it could affect the practical applicability of their results.

The paper's application to attrition problems is well-motivated and addresses an important practical concern. However, the claim that "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption" (p.8) is too vague. The authors should specify why ML methods cannot be applied to the missingness processes described in their framework.

Minor issues include:
- On page 3, line 158: "P(Y|X,Z,R_y) = P(X,Z)" should be "P(Y|X,Z,R_y) = P(Y|X,Z)"
- On page 3, line 168: "Since Y || R | X,Z" should be "Since Y || R_y | X,Z"
- On page 3, line 214: "R^{part} = {R^{(1)}, R^{(2)}, \ldots, R^N}" should be "R^{(N)}"
- On page 4, Corollary 1: "does not contain a partially observed variables" should be "does not contain a partially observed variable"

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of missing data analysis, with clear graphical criteria for recoverability that advance the state of the art. The distinction between recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries is particularly noteworthy. However, the assumptions about the missingness process being fully known and the restriction on R variables as parents of V or U variables limit the paper's practical applicability. Addressing these limitations would strengthen the paper significantly.

Overall, the paper is technically sound, makes an important theoretical contribution, and advances our understanding of recoverability in missing data problems. While the assumptions may limit immediate practical application, the theoretical framework provides valuable insights for future work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity issues and better justify the technical assumptions.
