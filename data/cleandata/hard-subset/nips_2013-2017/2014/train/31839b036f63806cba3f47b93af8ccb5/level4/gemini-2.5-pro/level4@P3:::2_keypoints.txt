- Generalization of sequential factorization (Theorem 1) to allow dependencies between missingness mechanisms, handling common longitudinal-study scenarios.

- Novel method for recovering joint distributions (Theorem 2) via partitioning missingness variables and their Markov blankets when sequential factorization fails.

- First formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3, Corollary 2), giving necessary conditions to identify unestimable queries.

- Demonstration that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when corresponding probabilistic queries (P(y,x), P(y|x)) are not, using d*-separation (Theorem 4).

- Application to attrition in experiments with necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects, correcting prior claims.

- High significance and impact: advances fundamental understanding of missing data identifiability and practical value of non-recoverability criteria.

- High originality: non-trivial extensions of Mohan et al. [2013] and novel results linking causality and missing data.

- Strong technical quality: precise theorems, clear logical progression, and proofs provided; application demonstrates utility.

- Excellent clarity and organization: motivated theorems illustrated with clear graphical examples.

- Accessibility concern: assumes substantial background in graphical models and causality; needs a more detailed background for broader audience.

- Lack of empirical validation: entirely theoretical paper would be strengthened by small simulation studies demonstrating key results (e.g., Theorem 4).

- Practical model-specification issue: framework depends on correctness of the assumed m-graph; discuss sensitivity to misspecification and how to construct/validate graphs.

- Overall assessment: strong, original, technically deep work that merits acceptance and is a good fit for NIPS.

- Minor formatting issue: corrupted reference formatting (escape characters/question marks) should be fixed in the final version.
