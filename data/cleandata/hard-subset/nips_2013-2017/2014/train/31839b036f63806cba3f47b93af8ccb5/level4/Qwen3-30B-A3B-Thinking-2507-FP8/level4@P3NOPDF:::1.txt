

Review  

This paper addresses identifiability in missing data mechanisms using Mohan et al.’s framework, offering incremental improvements to existing results and presenting insightful examples (e.g., causal distribution identifiability without conditional distributions). While the technical quality is high and proofs appear correct, the paper’s contribution lacks a unifying theoretical perspective.  

**Quality**  
The technical content is sophisticated and largely sound, with Theorem 3 providing a clear, interpretable result. However, several contributions appear incremental rather than transformative. Section 3, which introduces Theorem 1, feels disconnected from the core narrative and is difficult to follow due to its complexity. Its relationship to Richardson (2009)’s Markov blanket results is evident but not leveraged to clarify the argument. The quality improves significantly in Sections 5 and 6, which deliver clear, meaningful results on causal queries. The paper would benefit from a sharper focus on synthesizing these insights into a broader theoretical framework rather than accumulating isolated results.  

**Clarity**  
Clarity is the paper’s most significant weakness. The density of content, coupled with an excessive technical appendix (13 pages), suggests the material is better suited for a journal than a conference venue. The notation is poorly designed: the repeated use of capital 'V' for distinct concepts (e.g., vertex sets, observation sets) and inconsistent subscripting (e.g., `Vi` as a vertex vs. `Vo` as a set) creates confusion. The set equation in Corollary 1 exemplifies this poor presentation. While notation for complex quantities is inherently challenging, the current choices hinder comprehension. A more systematic notation scheme would substantially improve accessibility.  

**Originality and Significance**  
Sections 5 and 6 present genuinely interesting and potentially impactful results, addressing a critical problem in causal inference under missing data. The paper’s core significance lies here, though it falls short of delivering a complete, general criterion for identifiability—unlike the established framework for causal effects without missingness. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 suggest inverse probability weighting principles, likely tied to Markov blankets in the mixed graphs used (see Richardson 2003, 2009). The paper misses an opportunity to explicitly connect to these foundational works or derive a cohesive criterion. As stated, it risks becoming a reference list for specific cases rather than a guiding framework—untenable for practitioners needing to navigate complex identifiability conditions.  

**Other Comments**  
- p. 4, l. 214: Correct "R^part" to denote a partition of `R` (not "a set containing partitions"). The condition should state that `Rx` and `Ry` "belong to distinct elements of the partition."  
- p. 4, l. 213: Use `\leftrightarrow` instead of `<-->`.  
- Garcia (2013): Clarify whether the framework differs fundamentally or is merely complementary.  
- Definition 4 and Theorem 4: The notation for "severing edges" lacks definition.  
- `Vo` and `Vm`: Defined after first use (unnecessarily).  
- Language: Avoid casual phrasing (e.g., "oftentimes").  

**Conclusion**  
This work tackles an important problem with technically sound results in Sections 5–6, but its presentation and scope undermine its impact. The notation issues and lack of a unifying narrative prevent it from being a compelling contribution to the field. While the core insights are valuable, the paper would require substantial revision to merit acceptance. It is recommended for weak acceptance *only* if the authors address the clarity and structural shortcomings, particularly by streamlining the presentation, resolving notation conflicts, and articulating a broader theoretical vision that connects Sections 3, 5, and 6. Without these changes, the paper risks being a collection of technical results rather than a cohesive advancement.
