This paper presents an interesting and technically sound contribution to the problem of distribution recovery under missing data, specifically investigating scenarios where knowledge of the generating process and the missingness mechanism are available. The work extends previous results by Mohan et al. by considering a more general class of missingness processes, leading to a novel identification formula. Notably, the authors demonstrate the identifiability of certain causal queries even when the observational joint distribution remains unidentified. While generally well-written, the paper would benefit from addressing several points regarding clarity and detail.

**MAIN COMMENTS:**

(1) A key requirement of the presented results is knowledge of the process governing the missing data (censoring mechanism). It is unclear how frequently this level of knowledge is attainable in typical machine learning applications. Discussing the practical limitations regarding knowledge of the censoring process would strengthen the paper. Additionally, elaborating on the details of Figure 1 within the main text—perhaps by reducing its size—would improve readability and understanding.

(2) Section 2 implicitly assumes knowledge of the full population. Even in cases where no variables are observed for a given individual (Vo = emptyset, Rv = 1 for all V in V_m), the framework assumes the existence of that individual *a priori*. This constraint excludes scenarios common in missing data problems, such as estimating population sizes (e.g., wildlife populations). Explicitly acknowledging this limitation would be valuable.

(3) The term “mgraph” appears to be used inconsistently. Figure 1 includes V* variables, while Figure 2 omits them. If the omission is intentional, this should be clearly stated and explained. A consistent definition of the mgraph is crucial for clarity.

(4) The paper imposes a restriction preventing R variables (representing the missingness process) from being parents of variables in V or U within the mgraph. Given the authors treat R variables as continuous quantities rather than simple indicators, a justification for this restriction is needed. Is it a simplifying assumption for mathematical convenience, or does it reflect a fundamental limitation?

(5) The term “neighbor” on the bottom of page 4 is undefined. Providing a clear definition is essential for readers to understand the subsequent arguments.

(6) On page 8, the first paragraph references “rectification” of the observation by Garcia et al. and the authors’ definition of recoverability which relies on positivity. Does the work of Garcia et al. also assume positivity? If not, their approach may require a more stringent condition for identifiability than the one presented here.

(7) The statement on page 8, second paragraph, that “Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption” is overly broad. The paper should explain *why* standard maximum likelihood cannot be applied to the missingness processes considered in this work. A more specific explanation is necessary to justify the contribution of the presented identification formula.

**MINOR COMMENTS:**

*   P.1 line 33: “can bias outcomes” – Consider rephrasing for improved clarity and grammatical correctness.
*   P.2 Figure 1: The word “Qualifications” appears within the figure. This is unclear; clarify its meaning or remove it.
*   P.2 Example 1 on p.3 line 158:  The equation P(Y |X,Z,R_y) = P(X,Z) appears incorrect. The correct equation should be P(Y |X,Z).
*   P.3 line 168: “Since Y || R | X,Z” should be “Since Y || R_y | X,Z” (correcting notation for variable-specific missingness).
*   P.3 line 214: “Condition (ii) R^{part} ={R^{(1)},R^{(2)}, \ldots , R^N}” should be “Condition (ii) R^{part} ={R^{(1)},R^{(2)}, \ldots , R^{(N)}}” (correcting superscript notation).
*   P.3 line 215: “R^j respectively” should be “R^{(j)} respectively” (correcting superscript notation).
*   P.4 Corollary 1: The corollary states it applies to scenarios without partially observed variables, which seems counterintuitive given the paper's focus on missing data. Clarify the intent.
*   P.6 Footnote 2: Please provide a reference for the variable-level definition.

In summary, this is a technically sound paper building on prior work in the area of missing data identification. While the theoretical contributions are promising, clarifying the practical applicability and addressing the points raised above will significantly improve the paper’s clarity and impact.
