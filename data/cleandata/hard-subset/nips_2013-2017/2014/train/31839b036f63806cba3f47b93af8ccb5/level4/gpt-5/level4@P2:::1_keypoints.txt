- Paper generalizes sequential factorization for m-graphs to allow interdependent missingness mechanisms (Theorem 1).  
- Provides recoverability procedures for joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists (Theorem 2, Corollary 1).  
- Introduces graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3, Corollary 2).  
- Shows some causal effects (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when related probabilistic factors are not, including non-d-separable Y–Ry cases.  
- Applies results to attrition in longitudinal studies and gives necessary and sufficient conditions for causal recoverability under simple attrition.  
- Technical development appears sound and careful, with clear, interpretable results like Theorem 3.  
- Some contributions are incremental extensions of Mohan et al. (2013) rather than a unifying theory; broader organizing principle is not fully articulated.  
- Theorem 1 (Section 3) is hard to parse, feels disconnected from later sections, and its practical scope is under-illustrated.  
- Presentation is dense for a conference format; heavy notation and many edge cases reduce readability.  
- Notation is confusing/overloaded (e.g., capital V used for multiple roles; Vi vs. Vo distinction unclear).  
- Some graph operators and constructions are used before being defined (e.g., edge-severing notation in Definition 4/Theorem 4).  
- Corollary 1’s set equation is hard to parse and should be simplified or broken into steps.  
- Novelty: non-recoverability criteria and examples where causal effects are recoverable despite non-recoverable probabilistic factors are original and potentially influential.  
- Theorem 2/Corollary 1 resemble IPW and Markov-blanket ideas; paper should clarify connections to ADMG factorization and IPW (Richardson 2003, 2009).  
- Pros for acceptance: addresses an important MNAR inference problem, gives explicit non-recoverability conditions, compelling causal examples, and a useful attrition case study.  
- Cons for acceptance: readability/notation issues, Theorem 1’s unclear role, some incremental elements, and limited practical guidance (algorithms, worked examples, software).  
- Suggested revisions: streamline scope (emphasize Sections 5–6 and attrition), move technical generalities to a journal version, and present a unifying recoverability perspective.  
- Suggested revisions: explicitly relate results to IPW and ADMG/Markov blanket literature to highlight general principles.  
- Suggested revisions: improve notation (avoid overloading V, introduce Vo and Vm before use), define all graph operators, and use standard bidirected notation (↔).  
- Suggested revisions: add intuition, small running examples for Theorem 1, explain minimality in factorization, and provide practitioner guidance (checklist/decision flow and computational notes).  
- Specific comments to address: clarify R^part as a partition, replace “<-->” with ↔, avoid informal language like “oftentimes,” clarify the Garcia (2013) critique, and define edge-severing notation.  
- Overall: promising and important work with useful results, but needs much improved organization, notation clarity, and clearer positioning relative to prior work.
