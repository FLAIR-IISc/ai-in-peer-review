This is an interesting paper. It discusses the problem of how to recover from a process that generates a distribution and processes missing data.

This paper considers a more general class of missing data processes compared to the previous work of Mohan and others.

It leads to a new identification formula.

Additionally, the authors consider the identification of causal reasoning and show that although (observational) joint distributions are not identifiable, they are identifiable.

The paper is generally well-written, although there are some flaws and areas for improvement that I describe below.

Key Comments:

(1) The results here require knowledge of the process by which the observations were censored.
Is this knowledge available in common machine learning applications?
It would be helpful to include more detailed information related to Figure 1 in the main paper. (This figure can be reduced.)
(2) Section 2. It should be mentioned here that missing data processes always assume that the complete population is known:
Even if Vo = emptyset, and Rv = 1 V_m for all V (so no variable is observed for this person), it is still assumed that we know this person exists.
(This excludes missing data problems such as estimating the number of animals in the population.)
(3) The term "mgraphs" seems to be used ambiguously. In particular, sometimes the V* variables are included in the graph (Figure 1) and sometimes they are not (Figure 2). If the V* variables are simply omitted in these graphs, it should be explained.
(4) In this paper, mgraphs are not allowed to have R variables as parents of V or U.
Since the authors describe considering the R variable not only as an index but also as a real quantity, they should explain why this limitation is justified (or whether it is primarily for convenience).
(5) The term 'neighbor', which appears below p.4, is not defined.
(6) p.8 first paragraph. "Correction" of observations by Garcia.
The recoverability positivity defined by the authors assumes positivity. Do Garcia and others also assume positivity?
If not, they may need more stringent conditions.
(7) p.8 second paragraph. "The maximum likelihood method is known to give consistent estimates under MAR assumptions".
This is very vague. Why cannot the maximum likelihood be applied to any of the missing data processes described here?
