The paper addresses the challenging problem of determining identifiability of probabilistic and causal queries in the presence of missing data, building upon the missingness graph framework introduced by Mohan et al. The authors extend existing results, providing more general conditions for query recovery and demonstrating that causal queries can sometimes be identified even when their constituent probabilistic relations are not. Several illustrative examples, particularly relating to attrition, are included.

**Quality**

The technical quality of the paper is generally high. The derivations appear rigorous and the theoretical results, such as Theorem 3, are clearly stated and interpretable. However, the contributions feel somewhat incremental; while improvements are made, it's not immediately clear how these advances fit into a broader, unifying understanding of missing data identifiability. Section 3, in particular, feels somewhat disconnected from the core narrative, and the presentation of Theorem 1 is dense and difficult to follow. The subsequent sections demonstrate stronger technical merit, with Section 5 offering useful non-recoverability criteria and Section 6 presenting intriguing results concerning causal query identification. The extensive technical appendix (13 pages) suggests the potential for overly detailed exposition within the main body of the paper. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations and potential issues.

**Clarity**

Clarity is the paper's primary weakness. The density of mathematical notation and the sheer volume of material make the paper challenging to follow. While representing complex concepts with concise notation is necessary, the repeated use of 'V' to denote different sets is particularly confusing, and the subscripts occasionally become illegible. The set equation in Corollary 1 is a prime example of notationally dense and difficult-to-parse presentation. The paper lacks a clear, guiding narrative, and the organization feels somewhat disjointed, hindering comprehension. This complexity might be better suited for a more expansive journal publication allowing for detailed development.

**Originality and Significance**

Sections 5 and 6 represent the most original and potentially significant contributions of the paper. The results presented in these sections are interesting and address an important problem in causal inference with missing data. The demonstration that causal effects can be identified even when conditional distributions are unrecoverable is particularly noteworthy. However, the paper could benefit from a more holistic approach. Aiming for a complete criterion for identifiability within this framework, analogous to the work on causal effect identification without missingness, would significantly enhance the paper's impact. The current approach of presenting a series of theorems and corollaries feels somewhat piecemeal. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 bear resemblance to inverse probability weighting techniques, and it would be beneficial to explicitly connect these results to the existing literature and highlight the underlying principles, such as Markov blankets.

**Arguments for Acceptance**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in causal inference.
*   Extends existing theory on identifiability with missing data.
*   Presents novel results regarding the identifiability of causal queries under challenging conditions (e.g., when Y and its missingness mechanism are inseparable).
*   Includes illustrative examples, particularly related to attrition.
*   Demonstrates technical rigor in its derivations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance**

*   Clarity and organization are significant weaknesses, hindering comprehension.
*   The presentation is dense and notationally complex.
*   The contributions feel somewhat incremental and lack a unifying framework.
*   Section 3 is difficult to follow and its relevance is unclear.
*   The length and level of detail may be better suited for a journal publication.

**Specific Comments**

*   p4, l214: "R^part is a partition of R" should be "R^part is a partition *of* R". The condition on line 214 should state that Rx and Ry "belong to distinct elements of the partition."
*   p4, l213: Use ↔ rather than <-->
*   Consider whether Garcia (2013) is genuinely incorrect, or simply operating within a different framework.
*   Define the notation for graphs constructed by severing edges before using it in Definition 4 and Theorem 4.
*   Avoid using Vo and Vm before defining them.



Overall, this is a technically sound paper addressing an important problem. However, its clarity and organization need significant improvement to make it accessible to a broader audience and fully realize its potential impact. I recommend acceptance with major revisions focusing on improving clarity, streamlining the presentation, and providing a more unifying perspective on the results.
