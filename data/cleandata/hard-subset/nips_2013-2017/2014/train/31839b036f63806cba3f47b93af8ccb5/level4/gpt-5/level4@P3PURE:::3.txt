This is an interesting and technically sound paper. It addresses the problem of when a distribution can be recovered from knowledge of both the data-generating process and the missingness (censoring) process. The paper considers a more general class of missingness processes than prior work by Mohan et al., leading to a novel identification formula. In addition, the authors consider the identification of causal queries and show that these can be identified even when the (observational) joint distribution is not identified. The paper is generally well written, though there are some glitches and areas for improvement noted below. Overall, this is an interesting paper that builds on earlier work. It is not entirely clear how broad the application is, but the contributions appear technically sound.

Main comments
1) Assumed knowledge of the censoring process
- The results require knowledge of the process by which observations are censored. Is such knowledge typically available in machine learning applications? Please discuss practical scenarios and limits of this assumption.
- It would be helpful to include more of the detail currently relegated to Figure 1 in the main text (you could make the figure smaller to accommodate this).

2) Clarify population-level assumptions in Section 2
- The missing data setup implicitly assumes that the full population is known. Even if V_o = empty set and R_v = 1 for all V in V_m (so no variables are observed for this individual), it is still assumed we know this individual exists.
- This excludes missing data problems such as estimating the number of animals in a population (capture–recapture–type settings). Please state this assumption explicitly.

3) Ambiguity in the use of “mgraph”
- The term “mgraph” seems to be used ambiguously. Sometimes the graph includes the V* variables (Figure 1), whereas at other times it does not (Figure 2).
- If the V* variables are simply being omitted in some graphs for readability, please say so explicitly and establish a consistent convention.

4) Restrictions on parents of R variables
- In this paper, mgraphs are not allowed to have R variables as parents of variables in V or U. Given that the R variables are treated as real quantities rather than mere indicators, please explain why this restriction is justified (or clarify if it is mainly for convenience or identifiability).

5) Undefined terminology
- The term “neighbor,” which appears at the bottom of p.4, is not defined. Please define it precisely when first introduced.

6) p.8, first paragraph: “rectification” by García et al.
- Recoverability, as defined by the authors, assumes positivity. Do García et al. also assume positivity? If not, they might require a stronger condition. Please clarify the relationship.

7) p.8, second paragraph: maximum likelihood under MAR
- The statement “Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption” is too vague. Why could maximum likelihood not be applied to any of the missingness processes described here? Please be precise about when and why ML works (or fails), relative to your generalized missingness processes.

Minor comments
- p.1, line 33: “can bias outcomes” — please check phrasing for clarity in context.
- p.2, Figure 1: “Qualifications” — please verify the caption/labeling and intended meaning.
- p.2, Example 1 (p.3, line 158): P(Y | X, Z, R_y) = P(X, Z). The RHS should be P(Y | X, Z).
- p.3, line 168: “Since Y || R | X, Z” should be “Y || R_y | X, Z.”
- p.3, line 214: Condition (ii) R^{part} = {R^{(1)}, R^{(2)}, …, R^N} should be R^{(N)}.
- p.3, line 215: “R^j respectively” should be “R^{(j)}.”
- p.4, Corollary 1: “does not contain a partially observed variables” — grammatical error; please correct (e.g., “does not contain any partially observed variables”).
- p.6, Footnote 2: Please provide a reference for the variable-level definition.

Overall assessment
- Strengths: Extends prior work by Mohan et al. to a broader class of missingness processes, derives a novel identification formula, and shows identification of causal queries even when the observational joint is not identified. The contributions are clearly motivated and technically careful.
- Weaknesses/clarifications needed: Practical availability of the censoring mechanism, clarity/consistency of the mgraph formalism (especially treatment of V*), justification for structural restrictions on R, and precision in comparisons to prior work (e.g., positivity and ML under MAR).

An interesting paper that builds on earlier work. Not clear how broad the application is, but technically sound.
