- Paper extends Mohan et al. to a more general class of missingness processes and derives novel identification formulas.  
- Key contribution: shows causal queries can be identified even when the observational joint distribution is not.  
- Paper is generally well-written and technically rigorous, with clear articulation of assumptions and limitations.  
- Results rely on knowing the censoring/missingness process; practicality of this assumption in ML applications should be discussed.  
- Figure 1 needs more detail or resizing to improve accessibility and context for qualifications.  
- Framework assumes a known full population, excluding problems like estimating population size when existence is unknown.  
- The term “m-graph” is used ambiguously (sometimes includes V* variables, sometimes not); explicit statement about omissions is needed.  
- Paper restricts m-graphs to prevent R variables from being parents of V or U; a justification or discussion of this limitation is required.  
- The term “neighbor” is used without a formal definition and should be defined.  
- Clarify whether Garcia et al.’s results assume positivity, since recoverability here depends on that assumption.  
- The claim that maximum likelihood is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR is too vague; explain why ML may not apply to the missingness processes here.  
- Minor corrections: rephrase “can bias outcomes” (p.1); clarify Figure 1 qualifications (p.2).  
- Typographical/formula corrections: Example 1 P(Y|X,Z,R_y)=P(X,Z) → P(Y|X,Z) (p.3); Y || R | X,Z → Y || R_y | X,Z (p.3); correct indexing/parentheses in R^part and R^(j) (p.3); grammar fix in Corollary 1 (p.4).  
- Overall assessment: technically sound and interesting, but broader applicability is unclear.  
- Arguments for acceptance: extends theoretical missing-data frameworks, significant identification result for causal queries, technically rigorous and well-motivated, assumptions and limitations clearly stated.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on known missingness process limits practical applicability, notation/explanations need clarity, connection to broader ML practice should be strengthened.
