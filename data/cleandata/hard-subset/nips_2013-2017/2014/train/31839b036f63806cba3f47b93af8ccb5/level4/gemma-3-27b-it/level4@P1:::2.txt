This paper proposes novel necessary and sufficient conditions to guarantee whether a causal query can be estimated from data in the presence of missing observations. It extends previous work that explicitly models the missing data generating mechanism within a causal diagram. The paper is clearly written and, while building upon existing contributions in this area, the additional insights provided represent a valuable contribution to the state of the art in causal inference with missing data. 

The central problem addressed is the estimability of causal and probabilistic queries from data corrupted by missing entries, given a model of the missingness process. The authors extend the results of Mohan et al. [2013] by presenting more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries – joint and conditional distributions – as well as causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)). A key finding is that causal queries may be recoverable even when the factors in their identifying estimands are not recoverable themselves. Specifically, the authors derive graphical conditions for recovering causal effects when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable. Finally, they apply these results to the problem of attrition, characterizing the recovery of causal effects from data affected by attrition.

The paper is logically organized, beginning with an introduction to missingness graphs and the concept of recoverability (Mohan et al. [2013]). It then refines the sequential factorization theorem (Mohan et al. [2013]) and provides algorithms for cases where this theorem doesn't apply. Further, the paper introduces graphical criteria for identifying non-recoverability, a crucial addition to existing literature.  The subsequent sections focus on the recoverability of causal queries, demonstrating that P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when Y and its missingness mechanism are inseparable, a result the authors highlight as particularly noteworthy. The paper concludes with applications to attrition and a thorough review of related work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of recoverability conditions to causal queries, particularly the demonstration that P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable, is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents clear definitions and theorems supported by logical derivations.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the concepts are explained in a relatively accessible manner, given the complexity of the topic.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition, a common problem in observational studies, underscores the practical importance of the work.
*   **Completeness:** The authors address both sufficient conditions for recoverability and necessary conditions for non-recoverability, providing a more comprehensive framework.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** The mathematical notation and theoretical arguments can be challenging for readers not already familiar with causal inference and graphical models. While the authors do a good job of explaining the concepts, a steeper learning curve is inherent in the topic.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The paper primarily focuses on theoretical results. While the example in Section 7 is helpful, more extensive empirical validation, potentially through simulations, would strengthen the findings.
*   **Assumptions:** The reliance on correctly specifying the missingness graph (m-graph) is a significant assumption. The paper acknowledges this, but a discussion of sensitivity analysis or methods for assessing the robustness of the results to misspecification of the m-graph could be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in causal inference.
*   The theoretical results are novel and contribute to a deeper understanding of recoverability in the presence of missing data.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.
*   The application to attrition highlights the practical relevance of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical nature of the work and lack of extensive empirical evaluation may limit its immediate impact.
*   The strong assumption of a correctly specified m-graph could be a limitation in practice.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution to the field of causal inference with missing data. The novelty of the results, the clarity of the presentation, and the practical relevance of the application to attrition outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that future work should explore empirical validation and robustness to misspecification of the missingness graph.
