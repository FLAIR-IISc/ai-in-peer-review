This paper presents a formal graphical analysis for deciding the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values. The work extends the missingness graph (m-graph) framework of Mohan et al. [2013] to establish more general conditions for recoverability. The primary contributions include: (1) a generalized sequential factorization theorem that handles dependencies between missingness mechanisms; (2) novel graphical criteria that are sufficient for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions; and (3) a significant result showing that causal queries, P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the associated joint distribution is not, particularly when a variable and its missingness indicator are not d-separable. These theoretical results are then applied to the practical problem of attrition.

This is a well-executed and important theoretical contribution to a problem of broad relevance. The paper's strengths are numerous:

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Significance:** The paper provides powerful and practical tools for a fundamental problem that plagues nearly all empirical science. The key finding—that recoverability for causal queries has weaker requirements than for probabilistic queries—is a profound insight that could significantly impact how researchers approach causal inference with incomplete data. The development of formal non-recoverability criteria is also highly valuable, as it allows researchers to identify when an estimate is impossible to obtain from the given data and model, saving them from fruitless analysis.
*   **Originality and Quality:** The paper makes several novel contributions that substantially advance the state of the art. While it builds on a recent line of work, the generalized theorems (e.g., Theorem 1 and 2), the introduction of necessary conditions for recoverability (Theorem 3), and the specific analysis of causal effects under inseparability (Theorem 4) are all new and non-trivial extensions. The claims are technically sound, well-argued, and supported by proofs.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of defining the problem, introducing the necessary theoretical machinery, and motivating each result with concise and illustrative examples. The logical progression from probabilistic recovery to causal recovery makes the paper easy to follow. The writing is precise, and the graphical illustrations are effective.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The work is primarily theoretical, and I could not identify any significant weaknesses. A minor point for future consideration could be a discussion on the algorithmic complexity of checking the proposed graphical conditions, which would be relevant for researchers looking to implement these checks in software. However, this does not detract from the paper's core contribution.

Overall, this is a strong paper that is technically solid, original, and addresses a significant problem. It provides valuable new insights and tools for the causal inference and missing data literature.
