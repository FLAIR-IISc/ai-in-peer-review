This paper addresses the problem of recovering probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing entries. The authors build upon the work of Mohan et al. [2013], which introduced "missingness graphs" (m-graphs) to model the data generation and missingness processes. The key contributions of this paper are: (1) generalizing the conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y|x), P(y,x)) to a broader class of missingness processes, including those where missingness mechanisms can influence each other; (2) developing novel graphical criteria for determining when such queries are *not* recoverable; and (3) demonstrating, critically, that causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) can be recoverable even when the corresponding observational joint distribution is not. The results are applied to the specific problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

The paper is technically sound and tackles an important problem. The extensions to previous work are significant, particularly the results concerning the recoverability of causal effects when the corresponding probabilistic queries are unidentifiable. The writing is generally clear, though several points could be clarified to improve the manuscript.

**Main Comments:**

1.  **Practical Applicability:** A central assumption of this work is that the graphical model for the missingness process is known a priori. It would significantly strengthen the paper's impact to include a discussion on the practical applicability of this assumption. For instance, in what typical machine learning applications can practitioners be expected to have this level of detailed knowledge about the censoring mechanism? Providing examples would help ground the theoretical contributions in practical settings.

2.  **Scope of the Framework:** The framework appears to assume that while variable values may be missing for an individual, the existence of that individual in the population is always known (i.e., we have a complete set of records, some with missing entries). This is an important distinction, as it excludes problems like capture-recapture where the goal is to estimate the population size itself. It would be helpful to state this assumption explicitly in Section 2 to better define the scope of the work.

3.  **Clarity on 'm-graph' Notation:** There appears to be an inconsistency in the visualization of 'm-graphs'. The graph in Figure 1 explicitly includes the proxy variables V*, whereas the graphs in Figure 2 do not. If the V* variables are simply omitted for clarity in later figures, this convention should be explicitly stated to avoid potential confusion for the reader.

4.  **Justification of Model Restrictions:** The definition of an m-graph restricts R variables (missingness mechanisms) from being parents of substantive variables in V or U. While this may simplify the analysis, it is a strong assumption. The authors should provide a justification for this restriction. Does it reflect a common class of real-world problems, or is it made primarily for analytical convenience?

5.  **Undefined Terminology:** The term 'neighbor' is used in Theorem 3 and elsewhere but does not appear to be formally defined in the context of the graphs used in the paper. Please provide a precise definition.

6.  **Comparison with Prior Work:** On page 8, the authors claim to 'rectify' a result from Garcia [2013]. However, the definition of recoverability in this paper relies on a positivity assumption (P(V, R=0) > 0). Does the work by Garcia et al. make the same assumption? If not, their condition for non-recoverability might be necessary under a different set of assumptions, and the authors' claim of a 'rectification' should be qualified accordingly.

7.  **Clarity on Maximum Likelihood:** The statement on page 8 that the "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption" is overly general. Maximum likelihood is a principle, not a single method. It would be more precise to state that for parametric models, likelihood-based inference is generally consistent under MAR. Furthermore, it would be helpful to clarify why standard ML approaches cannot be directly applied to the MNAR models considered in this paper (e.g., because the observed-data likelihood is unidentifiable without the structural assumptions encoded in the m-graph).

**Minor Comments and Typos:**

*   **p. 1, abstract, line 3:** "missingness process" should be "the missingness process".
*   **p. 2, Figure 1 caption:** "Qualifications" is misspelled.
*   **p. 3, Example 1, line 158:** The equation `P(Y |X,Z,Ry) = P(X,Z)` appears to have a typo on the right-hand side. It should likely be `P(Y |X,Z)`.
*   **p. 3, line 168:** "Since Y⊥⊥(Rx, Rz)|X,Z, Y⊥⊥Ry|X,Z, Z⊥⊥Rz" seems to be missing from the provided text, but based on the review draft, it seems there was a typo in the original text. `Y || R | X,Z` should probably be `Y || Ry | X,Z`. The authors should double-check all independence statements for correctness.
*   **p. 3, line 214:** In the definition of `Rpart`, `R^N` should be `R^(N)`.
*   **p. 3, line 215:** `R^j` should be `R^(j)`.
*   **p. 4, Corollary 1:** "does not contain a partially observed variables" should be "does not contain partially observed variables".
*   **p. 6, Footnote 2:** A reference for the variable-level definition of d*-separation would be helpful for readers not deeply familiar with this concept.

Overall, this is a strong paper with valuable theoretical contributions to the challenging problem of inference with missing data. Addressing the points above, particularly those related to assumptions, scope, and clarity, would further improve its quality and impact.
