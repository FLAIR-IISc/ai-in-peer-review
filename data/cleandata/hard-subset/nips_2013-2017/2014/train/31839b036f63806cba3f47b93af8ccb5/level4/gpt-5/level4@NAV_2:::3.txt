This paper tackles the question of when one can recover a distribution given knowledge of both the data-generating mechanism and the process that induces missingness. It broadens the class of missingness processes considered relative to Mohan et al. [2013], yielding a new identification formula. The authors also treat causal queries, showing these can be identified even when the observational joint is not. The manuscript is generally clear and well-organized, with a few issues and suggested improvements noted below.

MAIN COMMENTS:
1) The results assume knowledge of the censoring/missingness process. How realistic is this in typical machine learning applications? It would help to bring more of the Figure 1 details into the main text (potentially by reducing the figure size).
2) Section 2: The missingness framework implicitly assumes the full population roster is known. Even if Vo = ∅ and Rv = 1 for all V ∈ Vm (so an individual has no observed variables), the individual’s existence is still assumed known. This excludes settings such as estimating the size of an animal population. Please state this explicitly.
3) The term “m-graph” appears to be used inconsistently: sometimes V* nodes are included (Figure 1), other times they are not (Figure 2). If V* nodes are simply omitted for brevity in some figures, please clarify.
4) In this paper m-graphs disallow R variables as parents of nodes in V or U. Since R variables are treated as actual mechanisms rather than mere indicators, please justify this restriction (or state if it is a convenience assumption).
5) The term “neighbor” (bottom of p.4) is not defined. Please provide a definition.
6) p.8, first paragraph: Regarding the “rectification” of Garcia’s observation—recoverability here assumes positivity. Do Garcia et al. also impose positivity? If not, their claim may require a stronger condition.
7) p.8, second paragraph: “Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR” is too vague. Why can’t ML be applied to the missingness processes studied here? Please clarify the scope and limitations.

MINOR COMMENTS:
- p.1, line 33: “can bias outcomes.”
- p.2, Figure 1: “Qualifications.”
- p.2 / p.3, line 158 (Example 1): P(Y|X,Z,Ry) = P(X,Z) — right-hand side should be P(Y|X,Z).
- p.3, line 168: “Since Y ⟂⟂ R | X,Z” should be “Since Y ⟂⟂ Ry | X,Z.”
- p.3, line 214: Condition (ii) Rpart = {R(1), R(2), …, R(N)} (not R^N).
- p.3, line 215: “R^j” should be “R(j).”
- p.4, Corollary 1: “does not contain a partially observed variables” → grammar needs correction.
- p.6, Footnote 2: Please provide a reference for the variable-level definition.

Overall, this is a strong paper that extends prior work. The breadth of practical applicability remains somewhat unclear, but the technical contributions are sound.
