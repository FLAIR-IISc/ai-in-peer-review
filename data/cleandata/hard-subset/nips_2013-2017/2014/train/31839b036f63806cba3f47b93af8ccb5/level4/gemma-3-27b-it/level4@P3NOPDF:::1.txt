## Review of NIPS 2014 Submission

This paper addresses the challenging problem of identifiability of causal effects in the presence of missing data generated by various mechanisms, building upon the framework introduced by Mohan and colleagues. The work improves upon existing results and provides illustrative examples – notably, demonstrating identifiability of causal distributions even when full conditional distributions are unavailable. While the technical quality is generally high and the results appear correct, the presentation significantly hinders the reader’s ability to grasp the core contributions.

**Quality:**

The technical quality of the paper is strong. Results, such as Theorem 3, are clearly stated and interpretable. However, a significant portion of the results feels incremental, lacking a unifying narrative or overarching principle. While mathematically sound, the cumulative impact feels less than the sum of its parts. 

Section 3, concerning Theorem 1, feels somewhat disconnected from the remainder of the paper. The theorem itself is difficult to understand and appears closely related to existing Markov blanket results, specifically those presented in Richardson (2009). The quality improves noticeably in Sections 5 and 6, which offer clearer insights into identifiability and present interesting results regarding causal queries.

**Clarity:**

Clarity is the primary weakness of this submission. The paper attempts to cover a substantial amount of material, potentially exceeding the appropriate scope for a conference paper. This breadth contributes to a dense text and a lengthy 13-page technical appendix. The lack of a cohesive flow makes the paper difficult to follow; it would likely benefit from the more expansive treatment afforded by a journal publication.

The notation employed further exacerbates these issues. While developing notation for such a complex problem is inherently challenging, the repeated use of ‘V’ to represent different objects creates significant confusion. The inconsistency of ‘Vi’ representing a vertex while ‘Vo’ denotes a *set* of vertices is particularly problematic. The set equation in Corollary 1 serves as a prime example of unclear presentation. Overall, the notation requires substantial effort from the reader and frequently obscures the underlying concepts.

**Originality and Significance:**

Sections 5 and 6 demonstrate the potential for significant contributions. The paper addresses an important problem and contains genuinely interesting and useful results. However, the current presentation hinders a full appreciation of this potential. 

A more strategic approach might involve stepping back to identify a more general criterion for identifiability within this framework – analogous to the complete criterion established for causal effect identification without missing data. The current presentation, comprised of a series of specialized results, is unlikely to be easily navigated by practitioners seeking to determine the identifiability of a specific causal effect. The complexity of results like Theorem 1 makes this issue particularly acute.

Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to leverage principles of inverse probability weighting. The underlying reason for their success likely relates to the conditioning sets functioning as Markov blankets for R(i) within the mixed graphs used by the authors. Exploring the connection to existing literature, such as Richardson (2003) and (2009), would strengthen the argument.

**Other Comments:**

*   p4, l214: “R^part is a partition of R, not “a set containing partitions.” The condition on line 214 should state that Rx and Ry “belong to distinct elements of the partition.”
*   The language is occasionally informal (e.g., ‘oftentimes’).
*   p4 l213: Use  \leftrightarrow  rather than <--> .
*   It is unclear whether Garcia (2013) is incorrect or operates within a different framework. This distinction should be clarified.
*   Definition 4 and Theorem 4 introduce notation for graphs constructed by severing edges without providing a prior definition.
*   The variables Vo and Vm are used before their definitions (and their introduction appears unnecessary).

**References:**

Richardson, T.S., Markov properties for acyclic directed mixed graphs, SJS 30(1) 145-157, 2003
Richardson, T.S., A factorization criterion for acyclic directed mixed graphs, UAI 25, 2009.

**Overall:**

This paper tackles an important problem with a potentially useful approach. However, it is significantly hampered by poor presentation and notation. I recommend rejection in its current form, but encourage the authors to revise and resubmit, focusing on clarity, a more unifying narrative, and a careful review of the notation used throughout. Addressing these issues would greatly enhance the impact and accessibility of this valuable work.
