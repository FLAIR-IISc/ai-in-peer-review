This is an interesting paper that addresses the problem of deciding whether a causal or probabilistic query is estimable from data corrupted by missing entries, given a model of the missingness process. The authors extend the results of Mohan et al. [2013] by presenting more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries of the form P(y|x) and P(y,x) as well as causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)). They show that causal queries may be recoverable even when the factors in their identifying estimands are not recoverable.

The paper is generally well-written, and the results are novel and technically sound. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem of missing data and the concept of recoverability. They also provide a thorough review of related work in the field.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a general framework for recovering probabilistic and causal queries from missing data. The authors present a novel identification formula that can be used to determine whether a query is recoverable, and they provide several examples to illustrate the application of this formula.

However, there are some areas for improvement. One of the main limitations of the paper is that it assumes knowledge of the process by which observations are censored. This knowledge may not be available in typical machine learning applications, and it would be helpful to discuss the implications of this assumption.

Additionally, the paper could benefit from more detail regarding the figures. For example, Figure 1 is not fully explained in the main text, and it would be helpful to include more information about the graph and its components.

There are also some minor errors and inconsistencies in the paper. For example, the term "m-graph" is used ambiguously, and it is not clear whether the graph includes the V* variables. Additionally, the term "neighbor" is not defined, and the notation could be improved in some places.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of missing data analysis. With some revisions to address the areas mentioned above, it has the potential to be an excellent paper.
