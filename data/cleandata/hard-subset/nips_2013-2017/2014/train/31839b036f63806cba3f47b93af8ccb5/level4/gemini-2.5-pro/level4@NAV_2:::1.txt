This paper addresses the problem of determining whether probabilistic and causal queries can be recovered from datasets with missing entries. The authors build upon the missingness graph (m-graph) framework introduced by Mohan et al. [2013], extending previous results by providing more general graphical conditions for the recoverability of queries such as P(y|x), P(y, x), and the causal effect P(y|do(x)). A key contribution is showing that causal queries may be recoverable even when the underlying joint or conditional distributions are not, a result demonstrated with insightful examples. The proposed methods are then applied to the specific problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

**Quality**
The technical quality of the paper appears to be high. The theoretical results are involved but seem sound. In particular, the non-recoverability criteria presented in Theorem 3 are clear, interpretable, and provide a valuable contribution by establishing necessary conditions. Similarly, the results in Section 6 concerning the recoverability of causal queries even when the corresponding probabilistic terms are not separable are novel and interesting.

However, some parts of the work feel more like incremental improvements. For instance, Section 3, which introduces a "General Ordered Factorization," feels somewhat disconnected from the rest of the narrative. Its main result, Theorem 1, is dense and challenging to comprehend, which may limit its practical applicability. The paper's quality strengthens in the later sections, which offer clearer and more impactful results.

**Clarity**
Clarity is the paper's primary weakness. The manuscript attempts to cover a substantial amount of material, which makes the presentation very dense and difficult to follow. The inclusion of a 13-page appendix suggests that the work might be better suited for a longer journal article, which would allow for a more thorough and paced explanation of the concepts.

The notation, while addressing a complex topic, could be significantly improved to aid the reader. The symbol 'V' is used to denote several different objects (a set of nodes, a set of substantive variables, etc.), and the subscripts can become difficult to read. The distinction between a single variable `Vi` and a set of variables `Vo` is also a source of confusion. The presentation of the set equation in Corollary 1 is a prime example of where the notational complexity hinders understanding.

**Originality and Significance**
The paper tackles an important and highly relevant problem in many scientific domains. The contributions in Sections 5 and 6 are particularly original and significant. Providing graphical criteria for non-recoverability is a novel step, and demonstrating the unique recovery properties of causal queries is a genuinely useful insight.

The overall significance could be enhanced if the authors aimed for a more unified theory. Rather than presenting a series of specific sufficient or necessary conditions, the field would greatly benefit from a complete criterion for recoverability within this framework, akin to the completeness results established for causal identification in the absence of missing data. Such a result would provide a more fundamental understanding of the problem.

Furthermore, the connection between the proposed methods and existing statistical concepts could be made more explicit. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1, for example, appear to be related to inverse probability weighting, where the conditioning sets function as Markov blankets for the missingness indicators. Drawing this connection and citing relevant work (e.g., Richardson [2003]) would help situate the contribution within the broader literature.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**
*   The paper's overall narrative could be improved. Section 3 feels somewhat isolated and could be better integrated or perhaps streamlined to focus on the more impactful results in later sections.
*   Regarding the critique of Garcia [2013], it would be helpful to clarify if that work is incorrect under its own assumptions, or if the discrepancy noted by the authors arises from a different modeling framework.
*   The notation for graph manipulation (e.g., `G_X` and `G^X`) is used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4 but does not appear to be formally defined in the text.
*   On page 2, the terms `Vo` and `Vm` are used in a sentence before they are defined. Reordering this would improve readability.
*   On page 4, the description of `R(part)` as "partitions of R variables" could be clarified. It seems to be *a partition* of the set R, and the subsequent condition should state that `Rx` and `Ry` belong to distinct elements of this partition.
*   The writing is occasionally informal (e.g., "oftentimes"). A pass to ensure a consistently formal scientific tone would be beneficial.
*   A minor notational suggestion: consider using a standard LaTeX command like `\leftrightarrow` for bi-directed edges for better rendering.
