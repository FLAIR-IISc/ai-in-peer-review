The paper addresses the problem of identifiability in the presence of various mechanisms for missing data, using a framework introduced by Mohan et al. It improves upon some existing results and provides interesting examples, such as the identifiability of causal distributions even when conditional distributions are unavailable.

Quality
The technical quality of the paper appears to be high, with involved but seemingly correct results. Theorem 3, for instance, is clear and interpretable. However, some results are incremental improvements upon existing ones and do not contribute to a broader understanding of the subject. Section 3 seems unrelated to the rest of the paper, and its main result, Theorem 1, is difficult to comprehend. It appears to be closely related to the Markov blanket results in Richardson (2009). The quality of the paper improves later, with Section 5 providing clear answers and Section 6 presenting interesting results about causal queries.

Clarity
The paper's main weakness is its clarity. The sheer amount of material presented makes it challenging to follow, and the density of the text, combined with the 13-page technical appendix, suggests that it might be better suited as a longer, more in-depth journal paper. The notation could be improved, as the use of the same capital 'V' to denote different objects can be confusing. For example, the distinction between Vi as a vertex and Vo as a set of vertices is not immediately clear. The set equation in Corollary 1 is a notable example of poor presentation.

Originality and Significance
Sections 5 and 6 are clear and interesting, and potentially significant. The paper addresses an important problem and contains genuinely interesting and useful results. However, it might be beneficial to take a step back and work towards a complete criterion for identifiability in this framework, similar to what has been achieved in the case of finding causal effects without missingness. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to be a form of inverse probability weighting, likely due to the conditioning sets being Markov blankets for R(i) in the mixed graphs used. Richardson (2003) and (2009) may be relevant in this context.

Other Comments
On page 4, line 214, R^part is a partition of R, not "a set containing partitions." The condition on line 214 should be revised to state that Rx and Ry "belong to distinct elements of the partition." The language used is sometimes informal, such as the use of 'oftentimes' on page 4, line 213. It would be more suitable to use \leftrightarrow instead of <--> in the notation. It is unclear whether Garcia (2013) is incorrect or simply uses a different framework. The notation for graphs constructed by severing edges, used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4, is not defined anywhere in the paper. The terms Vo and Vm are used before they are defined, which appears to be unnecessary.
