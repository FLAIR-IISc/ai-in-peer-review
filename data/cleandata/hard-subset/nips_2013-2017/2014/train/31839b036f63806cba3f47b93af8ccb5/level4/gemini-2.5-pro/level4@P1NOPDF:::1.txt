This paper addresses the problem of identifiability in the presence of various missing data generating mechanisms, building on a framework introduced by Mohan et al. The authors improve upon some existing results and provide several insightful examples, such as establishing the identifiability of causal distributions even when certain conditional distributions are unavailable.

### Quality

The technical quality of the paper appears high; the results are highly involved but seem correct. Some results, such as Theorem 3, are clear and easily interpretable. However, other results are incremental improvements on existing work, and their contribution to a broader understanding of the underlying principles is not immediately apparent.

Section 3 feels orthogonal to the rest of the paper, and its main result, Theorem 1, is particularly difficult to comprehend. It appears to be closely related to the Markov blanket results in Richardson (2009). The paper's quality improves significantly in the later sections. Section 5 provides clear answers to specific problems, and Section 6 presents interesting results on causal queries.

### Clarity

Clarity is the paper's main weakness. The authors attempt to cover too much material for a conference publication, as evidenced by the density of the text and the 13-page technical appendix. The presentation lacks a clear narrative flow, making the paper difficult to follow. The material would likely be better suited to a longer journal article, which would allow for a more thorough and considered presentation.

While acknowledging the difficulty of devising notation for the many quantities involved, the chosen notation could be significantly improved. The overloaded use of the capital letter 'V' to denote several different objects makes the paper challenging for the reader, and in some places, the subscripts become illegible. For instance, the convention where Vᵢ denotes a single vertex while Vₒ denotes a set of vertices is particularly confusing. The set equation in Corollary 1 is a striking example of this lack of clarity.

### Originality and Significance

The paper addresses an important problem and contains several genuinely interesting and useful results. The contributions in Sections 5 and 6 are especially clear, interesting, and potentially significant.

However, the paper would be strengthened by taking a step back to develop a more general, complete criterion for identifiability within this framework, similar to what has been achieved for causal effect identification in the absence of missing data. As it stands, a practitioner would find it difficult to navigate this line of work to find a specific result applicable to their problem, especially given the complexity of theorems like Theorem 1. A unifying, general criterion would be far more valuable.

On a technical note, Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to be a form of inverse probability weighting. The underlying reason for these results is likely related to the conditioning sets acting as Markov blankets for R(i) in the authors' mixed graph representation. The work of Richardson (2003, 2009) may be relevant here.

### Other Comments

*   p4, l214: R^part should be defined as a partition of R, not "a set containing partitions". The condition on line 214 should then state that Rₓ and Rᵧ "belong to distinct elements of the partition".
*   p4, l213: Please use `\leftrightarrow` instead of `<-->` for the bidirected edge symbol.
*   The authors should clarify whether the results in Garcia (2013) are incorrect or simply derived under a different framework.
*   The notation for graphs constructed by severing edges, used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4, is not defined.
*   The variables Vₒ and Vₘ are used (seemingly unnecessarily) in the sentence preceding their definition.
*   The language is occasionally too casual (e.g., 'oftentimes').

### Summary

This paper presents a useful approach to an important problem, but its contributions are marred by poor presentation and confusing notation.
