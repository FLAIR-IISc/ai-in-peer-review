The paper addresses the problem of identifiability in the presence of various mechanisms for missing data, using a framework introduced by Mohan and others. The authors build upon existing results and provide insightful examples, such as the identifiability of causal distributions even when conditional distributions are unavailable.

Quality
The technical quality of the paper appears to be high, with involved but seemingly correct results. Theorem 3, for instance, is clear and interpretable. However, some results are incremental improvements upon existing ones and do not contribute to a broader understanding of the problem. Section 3 seems disconnected from the rest of the paper, and its main result, Theorem 1, is challenging to comprehend. This section appears to be closely related to the Markov blanket results in Richardson (2009). The quality of the paper improves in later sections, with Section 5 providing clear answers and Section 6 presenting interesting results on causal queries.

Clarity
The paper's main weakness lies in its clarity. The sheer amount of material presented, coupled with the density of the text and the 13-page technical appendix, suggests that this work may be better suited for a longer, more considered journal paper. The article's flow is disrupted, making it difficult to follow. The notation could be improved, particularly with regards to the use of the same capital 'V' to denote different objects. The subscripts are often illegible, and the distinction between Vi as a vertex and Vo as a set of vertices is confusing. The set equation in Corollary 1 is a notable example of poor presentation.

Originality and Significance
Sections 5 and 6 are clear, interesting, and potentially significant. The paper tackles an important problem and presents genuinely interesting and useful results. However, it may be beneficial to step back and work towards a complete criterion for identifiability in this framework. The complexity of the results, such as Theorem 1, makes it challenging to imagine a statistician navigating the literature to find the precise result needed. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to be related to inverse probability weighting, possibly due to the conditioning sets being Markov blankets for R(i) in the mixed graphs used. Richardson (2003) and (2009) may be relevant in this context.

Other Comments
Page 4, line 214, should be revised to indicate that R^part is a partition of R, not "a set containing partitions." The condition on line 214 should then be that Rx and Ry "belong to distinct elements of the partition." The language is sometimes casual, such as the use of 'oftentimes' on page 4, line 213. It would be better to use \leftrightarrow instead of <--> . The notation for graphs constructed by severing edges, used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4, is not defined anywhere. Vo and Vm are used before they are defined, which appears unnecessary. It is also unclear whether Garcia (2013) is incorrect or simply uses a different framework.

The paper addresses an important problem and presents a useful approach, but its poor presentation and notation detract from its overall quality.
