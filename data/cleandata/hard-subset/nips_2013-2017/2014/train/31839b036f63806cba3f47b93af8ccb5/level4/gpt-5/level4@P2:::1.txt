Summary
This paper studies when probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y, x), P(y|x)) and causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))) are estimable from data with missing entries, given a graphical model of the missingness process (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. (2013). It generalizes prior results on sequential factorization to settings where missingness mechanisms can influence each other (Theorem 1), provides recoverability procedures for joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists (Theorem 2 and Corollary 1), and introduces graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2). A central contribution is showing that certain causal effects are recoverable even when related probabilistic factors are not, including cases where Y is not d-separable from its missingness mechanism Ry. The paper applies these ideas to attrition in longitudinal studies and gives necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability of causal effects under simple attrition.

Quality
The technical development appears sound and careful. Results such as Theorem 3 are clear and interpretable, and Sections 5–6 provide useful, actionable insights (e.g., non-recoverability criteria; causal recoverability despite non-separable Y–Ry). Some contributions feel incremental extensions of Mohan et al. (2013) rather than a unifying theory, and the connection to a broader organizing principle is not fully articulated. Section 3 in particular feels somewhat disconnected from later sections; Theorem 1 is difficult to parse and its practical scope is not well illustrated. Later sections are clearer and more compelling.

Clarity
Presentation is the main weakness. The paper is dense for a conference format (a long technical appendix plus many definitions and edge cases), and the narrative flow is hard to follow. Notation is heavy and sometimes confusing:
- The same capital V is used for multiple roles (vertices, observed sets, partially observed sets), and subscripts/superscripts become hard to read. In particular, Vi is a vertex while Vo denotes a set, which is confusing.
- The set equation in Corollary 1 is especially hard to parse.
- Some notation (e.g., graphs formed by severing edges used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4) is used before being defined or not defined at all.
Overall, the paper would benefit from a more streamlined presentation, clearer notation choices (possibly distinct symbols for vertices vs. sets), and more intuition and examples ahead of formal statements. As written, the material might be better suited to a longer journal article.

Originality and Significance
The paper addresses an important and timely problem: principled inference with MNAR data using causal graphs. The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) and the demonstration that P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable when P(y|x) is not are novel and potentially influential. The attrition case study is useful. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 resemble inverse probability weighting, with conditioning sets that appear to be Markov blankets for R(i) in the relevant mixed graphs; clarifying the relationship to known factorization and Markov blanket results for ADMGs (e.g., Richardson 2003, 2009) would strengthen the contribution and help readers see the general principle. A more explicit positioning relative to existing IPW and MAR/MNAR approaches would also be helpful.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a clearly important problem; extends graphical methods for MNAR data.
- Provides explicit non-recoverability conditions, which are valuable in practice.
- Offers compelling examples where causal effects are recoverable despite non-recoverable probabilistic factors.
- Includes an application to attrition with precise conditions and procedures.

Cons for acceptance
- Presentation and notation significantly hinder readability and adoption.
- Section 3’s main result (Theorem 1) is hard to interpret and feels orthogonal to later sections.
- Some contributions are incremental extensions without an overarching unifying criterion for recoverability.
- Limited practical guidance on applying the results (e.g., algorithmic steps, worked examples, or software).

Suggestions for improvement
- Streamline and refocus: consider narrowing the scope for a conference paper, emphasizing Sections 5–6 and the attrition results, and postponing some technical generalities to a journal version.
- Provide a unifying perspective: outline a roadmap that situates Theorems 1–3 within a broader criterion for recoverability, analogous to identifiability algorithms for causal effects without missingness.
- Clarify connections to prior work: explicitly relate Theorem 2/Corollary 1 to IPW and to Markov blanket/factorization results in ADMGs (Richardson 2003; Richardson 2009).
- Improve notation and definitions:
  - Avoid overloading V; use distinct symbols for vertices vs. observed/partially observed sets.
  - Define all graph operators (e.g., edge-severing constructions) before use.
  - Ensure Vo and Vm are introduced before first use.
- Expand intuition and examples: add small, running examples for Theorem 1; explain why minimality is needed in the general ordered factorization.
- Address the broader practical picture: provide guidance for practitioners on checking recoverability (e.g., a checklist or decision flow), and discuss computational aspects.

Specific comments
- p4, l214: R^part is a partition of R, not “a set containing partitions.” State that Rx and Ry belong to distinct elements of the partition.
- Use a standard bidirected-arrow notation (↔) rather than “<-->”.
- Language: prefer more formal alternatives to “oftentimes.”
- Clarify whether the critique of Garcia (2013) reflects genuine contradiction or different modeling assumptions/frameworks.
- The notation for graphs constructed by severing edges appears in Definition 4 and Theorem 4 but is not defined; please define it explicitly.
- The set equation in Corollary 1 could be simplified or broken into steps for readability.

References
- Richardson, T.S. (2003). Markov properties for acyclic directed mixed graphs. Scandinavian Journal of Statistics 30(1).
- Richardson, T.S. (2009). A factorization criterion for acyclic directed mixed graphs. UAI 25.

Overall
An important problem and promising approach with several useful results, but the paper would benefit greatly from improved organization and notation, and from clarifying the unifying principles behind the various theorems.
