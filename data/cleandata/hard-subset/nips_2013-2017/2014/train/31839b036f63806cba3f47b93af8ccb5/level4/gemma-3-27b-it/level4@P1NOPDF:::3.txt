This is an interesting paper addressing the problem of distribution recovery when given knowledge of the generating process and the process leading to missing data. The paper considers a more general class of missingness processes than previous work, such as that by Mohan et al., leading to a novel identification formula. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that causal queries can be identified even when the observational joint distribution itself is not. The paper is generally well-written, though there are some areas for improvement, detailed below.

MAIN COMMENTS:

(1) The results presented require knowledge of the process by which observations are censored. Is this knowledge readily available in typical machine learning applications? It would be helpful to include more detail regarding Figure 1 within the main paper; this could potentially be achieved by reducing the figure’s size.

(2) Section 2 should explicitly state that the missing data processes here assume knowledge of the full population. Even if *V<sub>o</sub>* = ∅, and *R<sub>v</sub>* = 1 for all *V* in *V<sub>m</sub>* (meaning no variables are observed for a given individual), the framework still assumes knowledge of that individual’s existence. This excludes missing data problems involving estimation of population size, such as estimating the number of animals in a population.

(3) The term “mgraph” appears to be used ambiguously. Specifically, sometimes the graph includes the *V*** variables (Figure 1), while at other times it does not (Figure 2). If the *V*** variables are simply being omitted in the latter case, this should be clarified.

(4) The paper restricts mgraphs from having *R* variables as parents of variables in *V* or *U*. Given that the authors treat the *R* variables as real quantities rather than simple indicators, they should justify this restriction, or explain whether it’s primarily for convenience.

(5) The term “neighbor,” appearing at the bottom of page 4, is not defined.

(6) Page 8, first paragraph: the text references “rectification” of the observation by Garcia. The authors’ definition of recoverability assumes positivity. Do Garcia et al. also assume positivity? If not, they may require a stronger condition.

(7) Page 8, second paragraph: “Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption.” This statement is too vague. The paper should explain why maximum likelihood cannot be applied to any of the missingness processes described here.

MINOR COMMENTS:

P.1 line 33: “can bias outcomes” could be rephrased for clarity.

P.2 Figure 1: Check the labeling for “Qualifications”.

P.2 Example 1 on p.3 line 158: *P(Y | X, Z, R<sub>y</sub>) = P(X, Z)* should be *P(Y | X, Z)*.

P.3 line 168: “Since Y || R | X,Z” should be “Since Y || R<sub>y</sub> | X,Z”.

P.3 line 214: “Condition (ii) R<sup>part</sup> ={R<sup>(1)</sup>,R<sup>(2)</sup>, \ldots , R<sup>N</sup>}” should be R<sup>(N)</sup>.

P.3 line 215: “R<sup>j</sup> respectively” should be R<sup>(j)</sup>.

P.4 Corollary 1: The statement “does not contain a partially observed variables” is unclear; please revise.

P.6 Footnote 2: Please provide a reference for the variable-level definition.

Overall, this is an interesting paper that builds on earlier work. While the breadth of application isn’t immediately clear, the technical work appears sound.
