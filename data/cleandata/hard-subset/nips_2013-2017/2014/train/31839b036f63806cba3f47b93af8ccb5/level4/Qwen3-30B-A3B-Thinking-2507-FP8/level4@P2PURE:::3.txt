

This paper addresses an interesting problem: when a distribution can be recovered from knowledge of the generating process and the process leading to missing data. It considers a more general class of missingness processes than in earlier work by Mohan et al., leading to a novel identification formula. Additionally, the authors investigate the identification of causal queries, demonstrating these can be identified even when the (observational) joint distribution is not identified. The paper is generally well-written, though it contains minor issues and areas for improvement, detailed below.

MAIN COMMENTS:
(1) The results require knowledge of the censoring process. Is this knowledge typically available in machine learning applications? Including more detail about Figure 1 in the main paper would be helpful (e.g., by reducing its size).
(2) Section 2 should clarify that the missing data processes assume the full population is known. Even when $V_o = \emptyset$ and $R_v = 1$ for all $v \in V_m$ (so no variables are observed for an individual), the model assumes this individual's existence is known. This excludes problems like estimating animal population sizes.
(3) The term "mgraph" is used ambiguously. It sometimes includes $V^*$ variables (Figure 1) and sometimes omits them (Figure 2). If $V^*$ variables are omitted in some graphs, this should be explicitly stated.
(4) The paper prohibits $R$ variables from being parents of variables in $V$ or $U$. Given the authors treat $R$ variables as real quantities (not merely indicators), they should justify this restriction (or clarify it is primarily for convenience).
(5) The term "neighbor" (bottom of p. 4) is undefined.
(6) On p. 8, first paragraph: "rectification" of the observation by Garcia. The authors' definition of recoverability assumes positivity. Do Garcia et al. also assume positivity? If not, their approach may require a stronger condition.
(7) On p. 8, second paragraph: "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption." This is overly vague. Why cannot Maximum Likelihood be applied to any missingness process described here?

MINOR COMMENTS:
p. 1, line 33: "can bias outcomes" → "can bias outcomes" (correct as is, but phrasing is slightly awkward; no change needed).
p. 2, Figure 1: Add qualifications.
p. 3, Example 1, line 158: $P(Y \mid X,Z,R_y) = P(X,Z)$ → should be $P(Y \mid X,Z)$.
p. 3, line 168: "Since $Y \perp\!\!\!\perp R \mid X,Z$" → should be $Y \perp\!\!\!\perp R_y \mid X,Z$.
p. 3, line 214: Condition (ii) $R^{part} = \{R^{(1)}, R^{(2)}, \ldots, R^N\}$ → should be $R^{(part)} = \{R^{(1)}, \ldots, R^{(N)}\}$.
p. 3, line 215: "$R^j$ respectively" → $R^{(j)}$ respectively.
p. 4, Corollary 1: "does not contain a partially observed variables" → "does not contain partially observed variables."
p. 6, Footnote 2: "Please give a reference for the variable-level definition." An interesting paper building on earlier work, but the scope of application is not clearly established; technically sound.
