This paper presents an interesting and technically sound contribution to the field of missing data, focusing on the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries given a model of the missingness process. The authors extend prior work by Mohan et al. (2013) to encompass more general conditions for recovery, notably demonstrating that causal queries can be identifiable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not. This is a valuable result with potentially significant implications. The paper is generally well-written, though clarity could be improved in several areas, as detailed below.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The core problem addressed is determining whether a causal or probabilistic query is estimable from data corrupted by missing entries, given a model of the missingness process represented as a ‘Missingness Graph’ (m-graph). Building upon the foundation laid by Mohan et al. (2013), the authors provide broader conditions for recovering joint and conditional probabilities [P(y|x), P(y,x)] and causal effects [P(y|do(x))]. A key finding is that causal effects can be recoverable even when the factors comprising their identifying estimands are not. This is particularly relevant when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable. Finally, the authors apply their framework to problems of attrition, characterizing the recovery of causal effects in such scenarios.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical results appear rigorous and well-motivated, building logically upon the existing literature (Mohan et al., 2013; Pearl & Mohan, 2014). The mathematical derivations seem carefully considered.
* **Novelty:** The demonstration that causal queries can be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not is a significant contribution. This expands the scope of identifiable effects in the presence of missing data.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition problems, a common challenge in observational studies, highlights the potential real-world impact of the work.
* **Clear Framework:** The use of m-graphs provides a formal and intuitive way to represent the missingness process and reason about recoverability.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions & Applicability:** The approach heavily relies on knowledge of the missing data process. It is crucial to acknowledge and discuss the limitations of this assumption in practical machine learning applications where the missingness mechanism is often unknown or difficult to accurately model. A more detailed discussion of the scenarios where this knowledge *is* realistically available would be beneficial.
* **Clarification of M-graph Representation:** The representation of m-graphs is sometimes inconsistent. The inclusion or exclusion of proxy variables (V*) varies between figures (Figure 1 includes them, Figure 2 does not).  The paper should clearly state when and why V* variables are included or omitted, and maintain consistency in the graphical representations.
* **Restriction on R Variable Parents:** The constraint that R variables cannot be parents of variables in V or U (the substantive variables) requires justification. The authors should explain the rationale behind this restriction or discuss the potential implications of relaxing it.
* **Vagueness and Missing Definitions:** The term "neighbor" is used without definition (p.4). Similarly, the statement regarding Maximum Likelihood estimation on p.8 is too vague. A more precise explanation of why ML could or could not be applied to the described scenarios is needed.
* **Positivity Assumption:** The discussion of Garcia (2013) on p.8 needs clarification regarding the positivity assumption. It should be explicitly stated whether Garcia et al. also assume positivity, and if not, what stronger conditions they require.

**Specific Comments/Suggestions:**

* **p.1, line 33:** "can bias outcomes" – Rephrase to “can introduce bias into results”.
* **p.2, Figure 1:** Consider reducing the size of the figure and adding more descriptive labels directly on the graph to improve readability.
* **p.2, Section 2:** Explicitly mention that the current framework assumes knowledge of the entire population, even if some variables are never observed for any individual (e.g., Vo = empty set).  This clarifies a potentially subtle but important assumption.
* **p.3, Example 1, line 158:** Correct the equation to P(Y |X,Z).
* **p.3, line 168:** Change "Y || R | X,Z" to "Y || R<sub>y</sub> | X,Z".
* **p.3, line 214 & 215:** Correct the superscripts to R<sup>(1)</sup>, R<sup>(2)</sup>, ..., R<sup>(N)</sup> and R<sup>(j)</sup>.
* **p.4, Corollary 1:** Change "does not contain a partially observed variables" to "does not contain any partially observed variables".
* **p.6, Footnote 2:** Include a citation for the variable-level definition mentioned.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles an important problem in causal inference and missing data.
* The theoretical results are novel and expand upon existing work in a meaningful way.
* The technical presentation is generally rigorous.
* The application to attrition is relevant and demonstrates the practical value of the framework.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The strong assumption of knowing the missing data process may limit the applicability of the method.
* Several areas of the paper could benefit from greater clarity and precision.
* The justification for certain restrictions (e.g., R variable parents) is lacking.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with the potential to make a significant contribution to the field. The technical soundness and novelty of the results are commendable. However, addressing the weaknesses identified above – particularly clarifying the assumptions, improving the clarity of the presentation, and justifying the restrictions – would significantly strengthen the paper.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions** contingent upon addressing these points. The authors should focus on making the paper more accessible and clearly delineating the practical limitations of their approach.
