The paper studies identifiability under various generated missing-data mechanisms, using the framework introduced by Mohan and colleagues. It strengthens some existing results and provides several illustrative examples (e.g., identifiability of causal distributions even when conditional distributions are unavailable).

Quality
Technical quality appears high; the results are intricate but seem correct. Results such as Theorem 3 are clear and interpretable. Several contributions feel incremental relative to prior work and, as far as I can tell, do not yet coalesce into a broader picture of what is fundamentally driving the phenomena here.
Section 3 seems orthogonal to the rest of the paper, and its main result (Theorem 1) is difficult to parse. It appears closely related to Markov blanket results in Richardson (2009). The presentation improves later: Section 5 provides clear, concrete answers, and Section 6 presents interesting findings about causal queries.

Clarity
This is the paper’s primary weakness. There is likely too much material for a conference paper (and the importance of some parts is unclear), as indicated by the density of the text and the 13-page technical appendix. The paper’s flow is not smooth, which makes it difficult to follow; it would likely be better as a longer, more carefully organized journal article.
The notation could be substantially improved. Reusing the capital V for multiple distinct objects places a heavy burden on the reader, and some subscripts are hard to read. It is particularly confusing that Vi is a vertex while Vo denotes a set of vertices. The set equation in Corollary 1 is an extreme example of poor presentation.

Originality and Significance
Sections 5 and 6 are clear, interesting, and potentially significant. The paper addresses an important problem and offers results that are genuinely interesting and useful.
It might be better to step back and clarify the general case, aiming for a complete identifiability criterion within this framework (analogous to what has been achieved for identifying causal effects without missingness). It is hard to imagine a statistician combing through a sequence of papers to locate the precise result establishing (non-)identifiability for a given causal effect, especially if that result is as intricate as Theorem 1.
Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to instantiate inverse probability weighting. Presumably, these work because the conditioning sets are Markov blankets for R(i) in the mixed graphs the author uses; see Richardson (2003, 2009) for potentially relevant connections.

Other Comments
- p4, l214: R^part is a partition of R, not “a set containing partitions.” The condition should state that Rx and Ry belong to distinct elements of the partition.
- Language is occasionally casual (e.g., “oftentimes”).
- p4, l213: Use \leftrightarrow rather than <-->.
- Is Garcia (2013) actually incorrect, or simply operating under a different framework?
- A notation for graphs obtained by severing edges is used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4 but is never defined.
- Vo and Vm are used before they are defined (apparently unnecessarily).
