Summary
The paper addresses identifiability under a range of missing-data mechanisms within the framework introduced by Mohan and collaborators. It refines some existing results and provides several illustrative examples, including identifiability of causal distributions even when certain conditional distributions are unavailable.

Quality
The technical quality appears high. Although the results are intricate, they seem correct, and some—such as Theorem 3—are clear and interpretable. However, several contributions read as incremental extensions of existing work and do not obviously advance a unifying perspective on the core issues.
Section 3 feels largely orthogonal to the rest of the paper, and its main result (Theorem 1) is hard to parse. It appears closely related to Markov blanket results in Richardson (2009). The exposition and contributions strengthen later in the paper: Section 5 delivers clear, concrete answers, and Section 6 contains interesting results about causal queries.

Clarity
Clarity is the paper’s main weakness. There is likely too much material for a conference paper, as suggested by the density of the exposition and a 13-page technical appendix. The overall narrative does not flow well and is difficult to follow; the work would likely be better suited to a longer, carefully structured journal article.
Notation is a particular pain point. Reusing the same capital V to denote multiple distinct objects makes reading needlessly difficult, and subscripts are sometimes hard to decipher. The fact that Vi denotes a vertex while Vo denotes a set of vertices is especially confusing. The set equation in Corollary 1 exemplifies these presentation problems.

Originality and Significance
Sections 5 and 6 are clear, interesting, and potentially significant. The paper tackles an important problem and contains results that appear genuinely useful.
That said, it may be more impactful to step back and aim for a general characterization—ideally, a complete criterion for identifiability in this framework—analogous to what has been achieved for causal effect identification without missingness. It is hard to envision practitioners navigating a sequence of narrowly targeted papers to locate the specific (non-)identifiability result they need, particularly when results are as complex as Theorem 1.
Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to instantiate a form of inverse probability weighting. The underlying reason these results work is presumably related to the conditioning sets functioning as Markov blankets for R(i) in the mixed graphs under consideration; Richardson (2003, 2009) seem relevant here.

Other Comments
- p4, l214: R^part is a partition of R, not “a set containing partitions.” The condition should state that Rx and Ry “belong to distinct elements of the partition.”
- The language is occasionally too casual (e.g., “oftentimes”).
- p4, l213: Use ↔ rather than <-->.
- Is Garcia (2013) actually incorrect, or does it simply adopt a different framework?
- Notation for graphs obtained by severing edges is used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4 but is never defined.
- Vo and Vm are referenced before they are defined (and seemingly without necessity).

Overall: Important problem and useful approach, though marred by poor presentation and notation.
