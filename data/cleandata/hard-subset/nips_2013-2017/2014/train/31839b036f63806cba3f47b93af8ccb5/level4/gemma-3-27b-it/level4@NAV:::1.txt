The paper investigates identifiability under various missing data mechanisms within the framework established by Mohan et al. It builds upon existing results and provides illustrative examples, notably demonstrating identifiability of causal distributions even when full conditional distributions are unavailable.

**Quality:**

The technical quality is generally high; the results are complex but appear to be correct. Theorems like Theorem 3 are clearly stated and interpretable. However, some results represent incremental advancements and, to my assessment, do not contribute to a broader understanding of the underlying principles. 

Section 3 feels somewhat disconnected from the rest of the paper, and its primary result (Theorem 1) is difficult to follow. It bears a strong resemblance to Markov blanket results presented in Richardson (2009). The paper’s quality improves in subsequent sections, with Section 5 offering clear and insightful findings, and Section 6 presenting interesting results regarding causal queries.

---

**Clarity:**

Clarity is the paper’s primary weakness. The scope of the material—potentially excessive for a conference format—contributes to a dense text and a substantial 13-page technical appendix. The article lacks a cohesive flow and is therefore challenging to follow; it might be better suited for a more extensive journal publication.

While developing suitable notation for a large number of quantities is inherently difficult, improvements are needed. The repeated use of the capital letter ‘V’ to represent different objects creates confusion, and the subscripts occasionally become illegible. The inconsistency of ‘Vi’ denoting a vertex while ‘Vo’ represents a set of vertices is particularly problematic. The set equation in Corollary 1 exemplifies poor presentation.

---

**Originality and Significance:**

Sections 5 and 6 are particularly clear, interesting, and potentially significant. The paper addresses an important problem and contains genuinely valuable and useful results. 

A more holistic approach—stepping back to analyze the general case and attempting to formulate a comprehensive criterion for identifiability within this framework (similar to advancements in identifying causal effects without missingness)—would be beneficial.  It seems impractical for a statistician to navigate numerous papers to locate the specific result concerning the identifiability of a causal effect, especially given the complexity of results like Theorem 1.

Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to leverage inverse probability weighting, and their effectiveness likely stems from the conditioning sets functioning as Markov blankets for R(i) within the author’s mixed graphs. Richardson (2003) and (2009) may offer relevant background.
