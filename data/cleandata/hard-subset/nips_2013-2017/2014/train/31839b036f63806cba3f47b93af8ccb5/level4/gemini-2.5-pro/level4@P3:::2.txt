### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of determining whether a probabilistic or causal query is recoverable from a dataset with missing values, given a graphical model of the missingness process (an "m-graph"). It significantly extends the work of Mohan et al. [2013] by providing more general and powerful conditions for recoverability. The main contributions are:

1.  A generalization of the sequential factorization theorem (Theorem 1) that allows for dependencies between missingness mechanisms, a common scenario in longitudinal studies.
2.  A novel method for recovering joint distributions (Theorem 2) for a class of problems where the sequential factorization approach fails, based on partitioning the missingness variables and their Markov blankets.
3.  The first formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability* (Theorem 3, Corollary 2). These provide necessary conditions, allowing researchers to quickly identify when a query cannot be estimated from the available data under the given model assumptions.
4.  A key finding that causal queries, such as P(y|do(x)), can be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries, like P(y,x) or P(y|x), are not (Section 6). This is demonstrated with a new theorem (Theorem 4) that leverages the concept of d*-separation.
5.  An application of these theoretical results to the problem of attrition in experimental studies, where the paper provides necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering causal effects, correcting some claims in prior literature.

In essence, the paper provides a more complete theoretical toolkit for reasoning about identifiability and estimability in the presence of missing data, moving beyond standard MAR/MCAR assumptions to handle complex "Missing Not At Random" (MNAR) scenarios.

---

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The problem of missing data is ubiquitous and a critical challenge in almost all data-driven fields. This paper provides fundamental theoretical results that advance our understanding of what can and cannot be learned in such settings. The distinction between the recoverability of causal and probabilistic queries is a particularly profound and important insight. The non-recoverability criteria are also of high practical value, as they can prevent researchers from attempting to estimate quantities that are fundamentally unidentifiable under their stated assumptions.

2.  **Originality:** The contributions are highly original. While building directly on Mohan et al. [2013], the extensions are non-trivial and substantially broaden the scope of the framework. The introduction of formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability is a novel and welcome addition to the literature. Theorem 4, which shows causal effects can be recoverable when conditional probabilities are not, is a surprising and powerful result that deepens the connection between the fields of causality and missing data.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The theorems are stated precisely, and the logical progression from prior work is clear. The derivations and arguments presented in the main text are convincing, and proofs are provided in the appendix. The application of the general theory to the specific, practical problem of attrition effectively demonstrates the utility and correctness of the framework.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. Each theoretical contribution is motivated, presented formally as a theorem, and then immediately illustrated with a clear graphical example. This structure makes the dense, technical material much more accessible and easy to follow. The relationship to and extension of previous work is stated explicitly.

---

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Accessibility for a Broader Audience:** The paper assumes significant familiarity with the graphical modeling and causality literature (e.g., d-separation, do-calculus, m-graphs, inducing paths, d*-separation). While appropriate for a specialized audience, a slightly more detailed overview in the background section could broaden the paper's accessibility to the wider NIPS community, which includes many practitioners who may not be deeply versed in this specific formalism.

2.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong and stand on their own, the claims would be further bolstered by a small simulation study. For instance, demonstrating the recovery of a causal effect using the method from Theorem 4 on synthetic data—where the ground truth is known—would provide a compelling empirical confirmation of the paper's central and most surprising result.

3.  **Practical Application and Model Specification:** The entire framework relies on the correctness of the assumed m-graph. The paper rightly focuses on deriving results given a model, but a brief discussion on the practical challenges of specifying this graph would be beneficial. Mentioning the sensitivity of the conclusions to model misspecification, or how one might go about constructing and validating such a graph in practice (perhaps with more prominence for the Mohan and Pearl [2014] citation), would strengthen the connection to real-world applications.

---

**Overall Assessment**

This is a strong, well-written, and technically deep paper that makes significant and original contributions to the important problem of inference with missing data. The strengths—particularly the novelty of the non-recoverability criteria and the foundational insights into causal query recovery—far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides results that are likely to be built upon by other researchers. It is an excellent fit for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
