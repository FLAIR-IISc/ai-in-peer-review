Summary
The paper develops necessary and sufficient conditions for determining when a causal query is identifiable from data in the presence of missing observations. The approach augments causal diagrams with an explicit representation of the missingness mechanism, thereby unifying treatment of MCAR, MAR, and MNAR scenarios within a single graphical framework. The contribution extends earlier work that used missingness indicators in causal graphs and offers additional insights into when causal quantities can or cannot be recovered from observed data. The paper is focused and clearly written.

Strengths
- Theoretical significance: Providing necessary and sufficient conditions is a strong contribution, offering a complete characterization of identifiability for the class of problems considered. This moves the literature beyond sufficient or case-specific criteria and helps delineate the exact boundary between identifiable and non-identifiable queries under missingness.
- Conceptual clarity: By explicitly modeling the data-generating and missingness mechanisms in the causal graph, the paper delivers clear intuition about why certain queries are recoverable and others are not, especially under MNAR.
- Practical relevance: The results can guide analysts on whether causal questions are answerable from available data, what additional measurements might be needed, and when imputation/weighting strategies are principled versus futile.
- Presentation: The exposition is clear, the problem is well-motivated, and the scope and positioning with respect to the causal modeling paradigm are easy to follow. The paper stays focused on its main message.

Weaknesses and questions
- Dependence on the causal graph: The conditions require a correctly specified causal diagram, including the structure of the missingness process. In practice, such graphs are often uncertain. It would help to discuss how sensitive the results are to graph misspecification and whether partial identification or sensitivity analyses can be used when the missingness mechanism is only partially known.
- Scope of causal queries: Please clarify the breadth of queries covered. Do the conditions apply to interventional effects (e.g., P(Y|do(X))) and counterfactuals, or primarily to associational queries? If the results vary by query type, make this explicit and provide guidance.
- Constructiveness and computation: If the conditions are satisfied, is there a constructive procedure for obtaining an estimand in terms of observed quantities? Conversely, when conditions fail, can non-identifiability be certified systematically? Providing an algorithmic description and commenting on computational complexity would increase practical utility.
- Empirical illustration: More substantive examples (beyond small didactic graphs) or simulation studies would help readers appreciate the magnitude of the gains over MAR-based methods and the kinds of settings where the proposed conditions unlock identifiability.
- Relation to prior work: The paper would benefit from a crisper contrast with earlier graphical approaches to missing data. Identify concrete cases where prior sufficient conditions were inconclusive or failed but the proposed necessary-and-sufficient conditions succeed, and conversely, show boundaries where both approaches agree.
- Guidance for practice: Many practitioners struggle to elicit or justify the missingness mechanism. Suggestions for minimal assumptions that enable identifiability, or diagnostic checks that distinguish competing mechanisms, would make the contribution more actionable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a step-by-step worked example that starts from a commonly encountered missingness scenario, constructs the augmented graph, applies the conditions, and derives an estimand when possible.
- Discuss robustness: outline how to proceed when some edges or dependencies in the missingness graph are ambiguous, including partial-identification bounds or sensitivity analysis templates.
- Clarify notation and definitions (e.g., missingness indicators, proxy variables) early and keep them consistent throughout to streamline application of the conditions.
- Comment on extensions to longitudinal or time-varying settings, where missingness can be dynamic and history-dependent, and on the interplay with selection bias or measurement error.
- If available, provide or reference software that implements the graphical checks; even a prototype would enhance adoption.

Overall assessment
This is a focused, well-written paper that makes a meaningful theoretical contribution by delivering necessary and sufficient conditions for identifiability with missing data in a causal-graphical framework. The work extends and consolidates prior insights while offering clear benefits for both theory and practice. Addressing the points above—especially clarifying scope, sharpening the contrast to existing results, and adding more illustrative examples—would further strengthen the paper. I view this as a valuable addition to the literature and lean toward acceptance.
