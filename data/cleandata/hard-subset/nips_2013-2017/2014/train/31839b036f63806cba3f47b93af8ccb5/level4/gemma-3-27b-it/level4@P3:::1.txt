This paper addresses the challenging problem of identifying probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values, leveraging and extending the framework of “Missingness Graphs” introduced by Mohan et al. The core contribution lies in providing more general conditions for recoverability, particularly demonstrating that causal queries can be identified even when underlying probabilistic relationships are not. The paper builds upon previous work in causal inference and missing data, offering refinements to existing theorems and introducing new graphical criteria for non-recoverability.

**Quality:**

The technical quality of the paper is generally high. The results are intricate, yet the proofs (as assessed through the provided outline and a cursory examination of the appendix) appear rigorous. Theorems 3 and 4 are particularly clear and provide valuable insights into conditions for non-recoverability and causal query identification. However, some sections feel incremental and lack a unifying narrative. Specifically, Section 3, introducing Theorem 1, feels somewhat detached from the rest of the paper and the theorem itself is conceptually dense. The improvement in quality later in the paper, with the more accessible results in Sections 5 and 6, suggests a potential restructuring could enhance clarity. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations and potential issues, which is commendable.

**Clarity:**

Clarity is the most significant weakness of this submission. The paper attempts to cover a substantial amount of material, resulting in dense prose and a 13-page appendix. This density hinders readability and makes it difficult to follow the logical progression of ideas. The notation, while aiming for conciseness, is often overloaded and inconsistent. The repeated use of ‘V’ for different sets (vertices, observed variables, etc.) is particularly confusing, and subscripts become difficult to parse in complex equations. For example, the equation in Corollary 1 is exceptionally cumbersome. The organization could be improved to better highlight the key contributions. A more focused presentation, potentially sacrificing some breadth for depth, would greatly benefit the paper.

**Originality and Significance:**

Sections 5 and 6 represent the most original and potentially significant contributions of this work. The identification of conditions for recovering causal queries, even when probabilistic relationships are unidentifiable, is a noteworthy result. The paper tackles an important problem in causal inference – dealing with missing data – and provides tools for researchers to assess the identifiability of their queries. However, the paper falls short of providing a comprehensive, unified criterion for identifiability in this framework. While the incremental improvements are valuable, a broader theoretical framework would significantly enhance the impact of the work. The connection to inverse probability weighting, as hinted at in the discussion of Theorem 2 and Corollary 1, could be more explicitly explored, potentially linking the work to a wider body of literature. Relevant works by Richardson (2003, 2009) on Markov blankets in mixed graphs might provide valuable context and potentially streamline the analysis.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in causal inference.
*   Sections 5 and 6 present original and potentially significant results regarding the identifiability of causal queries.
*   The authors demonstrate a strong technical understanding of the field and provide rigorous proofs.
*   The framework of Missingness Graphs provides a valuable tool for reasoning about missing data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper suffers from significant clarity issues due to density, overloaded notation, and inconsistent organization.
*   Some sections, such as Section 3, feel detached and contribute less to the overall narrative.
*   The lack of a unified criterion for identifiability limits the overall impact of the work.
*   The length and complexity may make it difficult for researchers to apply the results in practice.

**Specific Comments & Typos:**

*   p4, l214: “R^part is a partition of R, not “a set containing partitions”. The condition on line 214 should then be that Rx and Ry “belong to distinct elements of the partition”.
*   p4, l213: Use ↔ rather than <-->
*   The claim regarding Garcia (2013) requires further clarification – is it a disagreement in framework or an error in Garcia’s analysis?
*   A notation for graphs constructed by severing edges is used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4, but is not formally defined.
*   The introduction of Vo and Vm *before* their definition is slightly awkward.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference with missing data, particularly in its exploration of causal query identifiability. However, the significant clarity issues and the lack of a unifying theoretical framework prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection**, but with a strong encouragement for the authors to revise and resubmit, focusing on improving clarity, streamlining the presentation, and potentially exploring a more unified theoretical framework. A more focused and accessible presentation will significantly enhance the impact of this potentially important work.
