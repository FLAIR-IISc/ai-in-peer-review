- Paper extends Mohan et al. (2013) to broader recoverability conditions for probabilistic and causal queries under missingness graphs (m-graphs).  
- Key result: causal queries can be recoverable even when underlying probabilistic relations are not, with implications for attrition problems.  
- Strength: Theoretical results are technically sound and build logically on existing literature.  
- Strength: Novel demonstration that causal effects may be identifiable when constituent probabilistic estimands are not.  
- Strength: Practical relevance demonstrated via application to attrition in observational studies.  
- Strength: m-graphs offer a clear, formal framework to represent the missingness process.  
- Weakness: Method heavily relies on knowing the missingness mechanism; practicality and applicability of this assumption need discussion.  
- Weakness: Inconsistent representation of m-graphs (presence/absence of proxy V* variables) across figures; clarify and standardize.  
- Weakness: Restriction that R variables cannot parent V or U needs justification or discussion of consequences if relaxed.  
- Weakness: Some terms and claims are vague or undefined (e.g., "neighbor") and ML estimation discussion lacks precision.  
- Weakness: Positivity assumption and its relation to Garcia (2013) are unclear; specify whether positivity is assumed or if stronger conditions apply.  
- Specific: Rephrase "can bias outcomes" to "can introduce bias into results."  
- Specific: Reduce Figure 1 size and add descriptive labels for readability.  
- Specific: Explicitly state the assumption of knowing the entire population, even if some variables are never observed.  
- Specific: Correct Example 1 equation to P(Y|X,Z) and adjust notation Y || R to Y || R_y | X,Z.  
- Specific: Fix superscript notation for R variables (R^(1), R^(2), …, R^(N); R^(j)).  
- Specific: Corollary 1 wording: "does not contain any partially observed variables."  
- Specific: Add citation for the variable-level definition mentioned in Footnote 2.  
- Argument for acceptance: Tackles an important problem with novel, rigorous theoretical contributions and relevant application.  
- Argument against acceptance: Strong missingness-process knowledge assumption, clarity issues, and unjustified modeling restrictions limit applicability.  
- Overall: Strong paper with recommendation to accept with minor revisions focused on clarifying assumptions, notational consistency, and practical limitations.
