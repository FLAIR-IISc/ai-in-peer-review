This paper addresses the challenging problem of determining identifiability of probabilistic and causal queries in the presence of missing data, building upon the framework of “Missingness Graphs” introduced by Mohan et al. The authors extend existing results, providing more general conditions for recovery and demonstrating that causal queries can be identifiable even when underlying probabilistic relationships are not. Several examples, including applications to attrition, are presented to illustrate the theoretical findings.

**Quality:**

The technical quality of the paper is generally high. The results, while complex, appear to be rigorously derived. Theorems 3 and 4 are particularly clear and provide valuable insights. However, some sections feel incremental and their contribution to a broader understanding of the field is less apparent. Section 3, specifically Theorem 1, is dense and challenging to follow, potentially benefiting from a more intuitive presentation or closer connection to related work (e.g., Markov blanket concepts). The later sections demonstrate improved clarity, with Section 5 offering useful non-recoverability criteria and Section 6 presenting intriguing results concerning causal queries. The reliance on a 13-page appendix suggests the material might be better suited for a journal publication where more extensive development is feasible.

**Clarity:**

Clarity is the primary weakness of this submission. The paper attempts to cover a substantial amount of material, leading to a dense writing style and hindering readability. The extensive use of the variable ‘V’ to represent different sets and concepts creates considerable confusion, as do the frequently overloaded subscripts. The equation in Corollary 1 is a particularly egregious example of notationally dense presentation. While finding concise notation for complex graphical models is challenging, improvements are necessary to facilitate comprehension. The organization could also be refined to improve the flow of ideas.

**Originality and Significance:**

Sections 5 and 6 stand out as particularly original and potentially significant contributions. The paper tackles an important and practically relevant problem, and the results obtained demonstrate genuine value. However, the overall presentation could be strengthened by a more holistic approach. Rather than a series of theorems and corollaries, a more unified criterion for identifiability within this framework would be highly desirable. This could potentially streamline the process for practitioners seeking to determine identifiability for specific causal effects, avoiding the need to navigate a collection of complex results. The connection to inverse probability weighting in Theorems 2 and Corollary 1 is worth noting and could be explicitly highlighted, potentially drawing on related work by Richardson (2003, 2009) on Markov blankets and graphical models.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   Rigorous theoretical development of conditions for recovering probabilistic and causal queries.
*   Demonstration of causal query identifiability even when probabilistic relationships are non-recoverable.
*   Clear and insightful results in Sections 5 and 6.
*   Application of the framework to the practical problem of attrition.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   Poor clarity and readability due to dense writing and overloaded notation.
*   Section 3 (Theorem 1) is particularly difficult to understand.
*   Presentation feels fragmented rather than a cohesive, unified framework.
*   Reliance on a lengthy appendix.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a challenging and important problem in causal inference.
*   The results, particularly in Sections 5 and 6, are novel and potentially impactful.
*   The framework of Missingness Graphs provides a valuable tool for reasoning about missing data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of clarity significantly hinders understanding and accessibility.
*   The incremental nature of some results raises questions about overall significance.
*   The length and complexity suggest the work might be better suited for a journal publication.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The paper presents valuable technical contributions but is currently hampered by significant clarity issues. Addressing these concerns—through improved notation, a more streamlined presentation, and potentially a more unified theoretical framework—would substantially enhance the paper’s impact and accessibility. A shorter, more focused paper concentrating on the core contributions (particularly those in Sections 5 & 6) might also be beneficial.
