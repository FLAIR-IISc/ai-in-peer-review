The paper tackles identifiability in the presence of multiple missingness mechanisms using the m-graph framework of Mohan et al. It strengthens prior results and offers instructive examples (e.g., identifying causal effects even when the corresponding conditional distributions are not recoverable).

Quality
The technical work appears solid; while intricate, the arguments seem correct. Results such as Theorem 3 are crisp and easy to interpret. Some contributions feel incremental and do not yet cohere into a broader unifying picture. Section 3 seems somewhat detached from the rest; its main contribution (Theorem 1) is difficult to parse and appears closely related to Markov blanket–type results (cf. Richardson, 2009). The presentation tightens later: Section 5 provides clear, actionable criteria, and Section 6 contains engaging results on causal queries.

Clarity
This is the main weakness. There is likely too much content for a conference submission (reflected in the dense exposition and a 13-page technical appendix), and the narrative flow suffers; this might be better suited to a longer journal article. The notation could be streamlined. Overloading V for multiple roles makes reading laborious, and some subscripts are hard to parse. It is particularly confusing that Vi denotes a vertex while Vo denotes a set of vertices. The set expression in Corollary 1 exemplifies the presentation issues most strongly.

Originality and Significance
Sections 5 and 6 are clear, interesting, and potentially impactful. The paper addresses an important problem and contains results that appear genuinely useful. It may be more effective to step back and articulate what holds in the general case, aiming for a comprehensive identifiability criterion within this framework (as has been achieved for causal effect identification without missingness). It is difficult to envision practitioners searching across multiple papers for a bespoke (non-)identifiability result, particularly when the statement is as intricate as Theorem 1. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 read as forms of inverse probability weighting; their validity seems tied to the conditioning sets being Markov blankets for R(i) in the mixed graphs used here. Richardson (2003, 2009) appear relevant.

Other Comments
- p4, l214: R^part is a partition of R, not “a set containing partitions.” The condition should state that Rx and Ry “belong to distinct parts of the partition.”
- The prose is occasionally informal (e.g., “oftentimes”).
- p4, l213: Use ↔ rather than <-->.
- Please clarify whether Garcia (2013) is actually contradicted by your results or whether the conclusions arise from a different modeling framework.
- A notation for graphs obtained by severing edges is used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4 but not defined.
- Vo and Vm are referenced before being defined (apparently unnecessarily).
