

This paper addresses the problem of determining whether probabilistic and causal queries can be recovered from data corrupted by missing entries, given a model of the missingness process. The authors build upon Mohan et al. (2013) by extending their results to provide more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (P(y|x) and P(y,x)) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))). The paper introduces "missingness graphs" (m-graphs) to explicitly represent the missingness process and dependencies among variables. A key theoretical contribution is demonstrating that causal queries may be recoverable even when the associated probabilistic relations (such as P(y,x) and P(y|x)) are not recoverable, particularly when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable.

The paper's main contributions include: (1) refining the sequential factorization theorem to handle cases where missingness mechanisms influence each other; (2) providing new graphical criteria for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions; (3) establishing conditions under which causal effects can be recovered despite inseparability between Y and Ry; and (4) applying these results to attrition problems in longitudinal studies.

Strengths: The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The theoretical results are carefully developed with clear proofs in the appendix. The authors make an important distinction between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries, which addresses a significant gap in the literature. The paper demonstrates how to recover causal effects even when the underlying probabilistic distributions are not recoverable—a novel insight with substantial implications. The application to attrition problems provides concrete value for practitioners. The related work section thoroughly contextualizes the contributions within the broader literature on missing data.

Weaknesses: The paper could better address the practical applicability of the m-graph framework. While the authors acknowledge that the approach requires knowledge of the missingness process, they do not sufficiently discuss whether this assumption is realistic in typical machine learning applications (as noted in the draft review). The paper also uses "m-graph" ambiguously—sometimes including V* variables (Figure 1) and sometimes not (Figure 2), which could confuse readers. The restriction that R variables cannot be parents of V or U variables needs better justification, particularly since the authors treat R variables as real quantities rather than simple indicators. The definition of "neighbor" (p.4) is missing, and the paper's assumption of positivity (for recoverability) should be explicitly compared with previous work like Garcia (2013).

Clarity issues include minor but distracting errors: "P(Y |X,Z,R_y) = P(X,Z)" (p.3) should be "P(Y|X,Z)", "Y || R | X,Z" should be "Y ⊥⊥ R_y | X,Z", and several notational inconsistencies (e.g., R^j vs. R^(j)). The paper's claim about maximum likelihood (p.8) is overly vague and could be clarified with specific references to how ML methods might apply to the missingness processes described.

Originality is strong, as the paper significantly extends Mohan et al. (2013) and addresses the specific gap regarding causal queries. The significance is high for researchers working with missing data, particularly in causal inference contexts. The paper advances the state of the art by providing necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for recoverability that are applicable to a broader class of missingness processes than previous work.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Novel theoretical contributions that distinguish between recoverability of probabilistic versus causal queries
- Comprehensive treatment of missingness mechanisms beyond previous work
- Clear graphical criteria for recoverability and non-recoverability
- Practical application to attrition problems in longitudinal studies
- Well-structured presentation with detailed proofs

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Ambiguous use of m-graph terminology (with/without V* variables)
- Insufficient discussion of practical applicability and assumptions
- Unjustified restriction on R variables as parents of V or U
- Minor but distracting notational errors throughout
- Limited comparison of the proposed approach with practical ML methods

Overall, this is a technically strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theory of missing data. The main weakness is insufficient attention to the practical applicability of the framework, which would be valuable for the NIPS community. With minor revisions addressing the notational issues, clarifying the m-graph definition, and expanding the discussion of practical applicability, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.

The paper's contribution is important for the field, as it provides a formal framework for determining recoverability of causal effects when standard probabilistic recovery fails. This advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, particularly for applications involving complex missingness mechanisms. The authors should address the minor issues noted above to strengthen the paper's clarity and practical relevance.
