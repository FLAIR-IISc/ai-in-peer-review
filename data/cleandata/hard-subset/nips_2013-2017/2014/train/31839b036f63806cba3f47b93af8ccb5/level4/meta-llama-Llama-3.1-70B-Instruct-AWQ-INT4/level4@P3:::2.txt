This paper proposes novel necessary and sufficient conditions to guarantee whether a causal query can be inferred from data in the presence of missing observations. It extends previous works that explicitly model the missing data generating mechanism into the causal diagram.

The authors provide a thorough review of the existing literature on handling missing data, including deletion-based methods, maximum likelihood estimation, inverse probability weighting, and imputation-based methods. They also discuss the use of graphical models for handling missing data, which is a relatively new development in this field.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of a new graphical representation called "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs), which is used to model the missing data generating mechanism. The authors show how m-graphs can be used to determine whether a causal query is recoverable from data with missing observations. They also provide a set of necessary and sufficient conditions for recoverability, which are based on the concept of d-separation in causal Bayesian networks.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow for readers familiar with causal Bayesian networks and graphical models. The authors provide several examples to illustrate the application of their method, including a discussion of attrition in longitudinal studies.

The paper's main strength is its ability to provide a clear and concise framework for analyzing causal mechanisms in the presence of missing data. The authors' use of m-graphs and d-separation provides a powerful tool for determining recoverability, and their conditions for recoverability are easy to understand and apply.

One potential weakness of the paper is that it assumes a high degree of familiarity with causal Bayesian networks and graphical models. Readers without a strong background in these areas may find some of the concepts and notation difficult to follow. However, the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the necessary concepts, which should be sufficient for most readers.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the state of the art in the analysis of causal mechanisms in the presence of missing data. The authors' use of m-graphs and d-separation provides a powerful tool for determining recoverability, and their conditions for recoverability are easy to understand and apply. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it a pleasure to read.
