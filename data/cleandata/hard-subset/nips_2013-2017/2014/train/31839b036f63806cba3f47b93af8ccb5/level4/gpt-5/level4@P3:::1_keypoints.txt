- Studies recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries under missingness using m-graphs, extending Mohan et al. (2013) and applying results to attrition.  
- Theorem 1: generalizes ordered factorization to allow missingness indicators R in separating sets, enabling recovery when R variables interact.  
- Theorem 2 & Corollary 1: give recoverability of joint distributions via partitions of R and their Markov blankets; corollary handles models without latent common causes between substantive vars and R.  
- Theorem 3 & Corollary 2: provide graphical criteria for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions.  
- Theorem 4: shows some causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when corresponding probabilistic quantities are not, using d*-separation and dormant independencies.  
- Theorems 5 & 6 (attrition): give necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering joint distributions and causal effects when |Vm| = 1, and correct/clarify Garcia (2013).  
- Technical quality is high; non-recoverability criteria are clear, interpretable, and fill a gap.  
- Theorem 1 is useful but difficult to parse; needs stronger intuitive explanation and examples for the minimality requirement.  
- Theorem 2/Corollary 1 read like principled inverse-probability weighting; a short derivation tying Markov blankets to ADMG separation would help.  
- Causal recovery results are interesting and broadly correct; must emphasize that identifiability in the substantive model is a prerequisite for recoverability.  
- Main weakness is clarity: manuscript is dense for conference format and would benefit from fewer running examples and deferring generality to the appendix.  
- Organization issues: Section 3 feels orthogonal and opaque; move main intuitions/examples earlier and proofs/generalizations to appendix.  
- Notation problems: overloading V and mixing single-variable vs set subscripts (Vi vs Vo) make reading difficult; recommend distinct styles for sets vs variables.  
- Define and use consistently the graph-surgery/edge-removal/manipulated-graph notation where first introduced; clarify R-partition (Rpart) definition.  
- Language and style: tighten phrasing, avoid colloquialisms, and fix typographical conventions (e.g., use ↔ for bidirected edges).  
- Originality: novel non-recoverability criteria and the finding that causal recoverability can hold without probabilistic recoverability; factorization generalization is incremental but practical.  
- Significance: important and relevant to NIPS, with strong ties to graphical models, causal inference, and missing-data practice.  
- Usability concern: lacks a unified, near-complete “recipe” for practitioners to decide recoverability.  
- Pros: tackles an important practical problem, provides clear non-recoverability criteria, novel causal-recoverability results, and useful attrition theorems.  
- Cons: presentation and notation impede understanding, some results are incremental relative to prior work, and no unified completeness criterion is provided.  
- Specific suggestions: clarify Rpart conditions, introduce Pasub and Mb with succinct definitions and an illustrative figure, and improve Corollary 1’s readability.  
- Notation fixes: avoid reusing V, define Vo and Vm before use, and use consistent subscripting conventions.  
- Clarify whether the disagreement with Garcia (2013) stems from differing assumptions or a concrete error and state assumptions for the counterexample.  
- References need cleanup and consistent formatting; include and cite Richardson (2009) UAI factorization formally if relied upon.  
- Overall evaluation: technically solid and timely with important contributions, but readability and presentation issues make it borderline for acceptance without substantial editorial revision.
