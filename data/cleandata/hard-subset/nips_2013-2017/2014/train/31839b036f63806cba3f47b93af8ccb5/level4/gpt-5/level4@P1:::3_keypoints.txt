- Paper extends recoverability from m-graphs: generalized ordered factorization including R variables to handle interacting missingness mechanisms.  
- Introduces a theorem for joint distribution recovery without an admissible sequence using collider paths and partitions of R.  
- Presents graphical non-recoverability criteria for joint and conditional distributions.  
- Shows causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when Y and Ry are not d-separable, using do-calculus and dormant independencies.  
- Applies results to attrition, giving necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for “simple attrition” (|Vm|=1).  
- Builds on Mohan et al. (2013), Mohan & Pearl (2014), and Shpitser & Pearl (2006,2008), and contrasts with MAR/MCAR and standard ML/EM approaches.  
- Quality: technically sound with proofs; some notational/definitional glitches affect readability.  
- Clarity: generally well written and structured, but missing/inconsistent definitions (e.g., “neighbor,” V* usage) and vague statements in places.  
- Originality: high — novel inclusion of R in factorization, general recovery theorem, explicit non-recoverability criteria, and causal-recoverability under inseparability.  
- Significance: potentially important for MNAR and attrition causal inference; practical impact depends on realism of m-graph assumptions.  
- Strength: clear generalization allowing interactions among missingness mechanisms and useful non-recoverability bounds.  
- Strength: demonstrates causal recoverability can succeed even when probabilistic recovery fails.  
- Main concern 1: Need discussion on realism of specifying the m-graph in ML applications and guidance on elicitation and sensitivity analysis.  
- Main concern 2: Clarify population/observation assumptions (existence of sampling units recorded) and note capture–recapture/population-size problems are out of scope.  
- Main concern 3: Inconsistent use of V* in m-graphs — state explicitly whether V* are always part of formal m-graph and when omitted in figures.  
- Main concern 4: Justify prohibiting edges R→V or R→U, or clarify it’s for convenience and discuss extensions if such edges are allowed.  
- Main concern 5: Define “neighbor” formally (e.g., adjacency in the mixed graph).  
- Main concern 6: Clarify positivity assumptions and how they compare to Garcia (2013); state that results assume positivity over complete cases.  
- Main concern 7: Tighten the Maximum Likelihood discussion — specify conditions under which ML/EM yields consistent estimates and why MNAR settings here need the presented methods.  
- Minor issues: several typos/inconsistent notations (examples listed by reviewer) and a missing reference for variable-level definition in Footnote 2.  
- Pros: solid theoretical advances, clear impossibility results, useful MNAR causal results with attrition application.  
- Cons: heavy reliance on accurate m-graph specification, definitional inconsistencies, and a few imprecise statements to be tightened.  
- Overall: technically sound, original, and valuable work; recommend addressing clarity and assumption-justification issues before publication.
