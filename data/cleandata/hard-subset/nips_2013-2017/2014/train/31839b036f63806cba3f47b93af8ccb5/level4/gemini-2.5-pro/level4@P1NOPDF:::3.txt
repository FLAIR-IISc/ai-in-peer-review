This paper addresses the interesting and important problem of recovering a probability distribution given knowledge of both the data-generating process and the mechanism causing data to be missing. The authors consider a more general class of missingness processes than that addressed in earlier work by Mohan et al., which leads to a novel identification formula. Furthermore, they explore the identification of causal queries, showing that these can be identified even when the full observational joint distribution cannot be. The paper is generally well-written, though there are some minor issues and areas for improvement, which I describe below.

**MAIN COMMENTS**

(1) The presented results require knowledge of the censoring process. A key question is whether this knowledge is readily available in typical machine learning applications, and the paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this point. Separately, it would be helpful to include more of the detail regarding Figure 1 in the main body of the paper; this could likely be achieved by resizing the figure to create more space for text.

(2) In Section 2, it should be clarified that the missing data processes under consideration assume the full population is known. That is, even if no variables are observed for a given individual (i.e., V_o = ∅ and R_v = 1 for all V in V_m), it is still assumed that the existence of this individual is known. This assumption excludes certain types of missing data problems, such as population size estimation (e.g., estimating the number of animals in a species), and this scope should be made explicit.

(3) The term 'm-graph' appears to be used inconsistently. In some instances, the graph includes the V* variables (e.g., Figure 1), while in others, it does not (e.g., Figure 2). If the V* variables are merely omitted from certain diagrams for clarity, this convention should be explicitly stated.

(4) The proposed framework for m-graphs disallows R variables from being parents of variables in V or U. Given that the R variables are treated as substantive quantities and not just as indicators of missingness, the authors should justify this restriction. Is it a necessary theoretical constraint or one made primarily for convenience?

(5) The term 'neighbor', used at the bottom of page 4, is not defined.

(6) On page 8, the first paragraph discusses the "rectification" of an observation by Garcia et al. The definition of recoverability in this paper relies on a positivity assumption. It is unclear if Garcia et al. make the same assumption. If not, their results might depend on a stronger condition, and this connection should be clarified.

(7) The statement on page 8 that the "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption" is too vague. The authors should elaborate on why standard maximum likelihood estimation cannot be applied to the more general missingness processes described in this paper.

**MINOR COMMENTS**

*   p. 1, line 33: The phrase 'can bias outcomes' is slightly awkward; please consider rephrasing.
*   p. 2, Figure 1: The label 'Qualifications' appears to be a typo.
*   p. 3, line 158 (in Example 1): The equation P(Y | X, Z, R_y) = P(X, Z) seems incorrect. The right-hand side should likely be P(Y | X, Z).
*   p. 3, line 168: The independence statement should be Y ⊥⊥ R_y | X, Z, not Y ⊥⊥ R | X, Z.
*   p. 3, line 214: In condition (ii), R^N should be R^{(N)} for notational consistency.
*   p. 3, line 215: Similarly, R^j should be R^{(j)}.
*   p. 4, Corollary 1: The phrase 'does not contain a partially observed variables' is grammatically incorrect and its meaning is unclear. Please revise for clarity.
*   p. 6, Footnote 2: Please provide a reference for the variable-level definition of m-separability mentioned.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper that makes an interesting contribution by building on earlier work. While its immediate practical applicability could be discussed further, the theoretical results are valuable.
