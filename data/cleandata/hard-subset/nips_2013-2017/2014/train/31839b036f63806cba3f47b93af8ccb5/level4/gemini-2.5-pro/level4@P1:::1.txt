This paper addresses the problem of recoverability for probabilistic and causal queries from data with missing values. Building on the missingness graph framework of Mohan et al. (2013), the authors extend existing results and provide new graphical conditions for recoverability. A key contribution is showing that causal queries can sometimes be recovered even when the corresponding probabilistic queries are not, illustrated with several interesting examples.

### Quality
The technical quality of the work appears to be high; the theoretical results, while intricate, seem correct. In particular, results like Theorem 3 provide clear and interpretable non-recoverability criteria. However, some of the contributions feel incremental, extending prior work without necessarily contributing to a more unified, high-level understanding of the problem. For instance, Section 3 feels somewhat disconnected from the rest of the paper, and its central result, Theorem 1, is challenging to parse and apply. The paper's quality and clarity improve significantly in the later sections, with Section 5 providing useful non-recoverability conditions and Section 6 presenting interesting findings on causal queries.

### Clarity
Clarity is the paper's primary weakness. The manuscript attempts to cover a substantial amount of material, which may be too ambitious for a conference paper format. This results in a very dense presentation, supported by a 13-page appendix, which makes the paper difficult to follow. The overall narrative would benefit from a clearer structure, and the work might be better suited for a longer journal article where the concepts can be developed more thoroughly.

The notation presents a significant barrier to understanding. While developing a clear notation for this topic is undoubtedly difficult, the current system could be substantially improved. For example:
*   The overloading of the capital letter 'V' for many different objects (variables, sets of variables, etc.) requires the reader to constantly disambiguate from context.
*   The use of 'Vi' for a single vertex and 'Vo' for a set of vertices is particularly confusing.
*   In some places, subscripts are small and difficult to read.
The complex set equation in Corollary 1 is a prime example of these notational challenges.

### Originality and Significance
The paper addresses an important and practical problem. The results in Sections 5 and 6, which provide novel non-recoverability criteria and explore the recoverability of causal queries, are particularly interesting and potentially significant contributions.

The paper's long-term impact could be increased by shifting focus from a collection of specific conditions to a more general theory. A valuable contribution would be a complete algorithm or criterion for recoverability within this framework, analogous to the completeness results that exist for causal identification in the absence of missing data. Without such a general result, it is difficult to see how a practitioner would navigate the series of specialized theorems (some as complex as Theorem 1) to determine if a query is recoverable in their specific application.

On a technical note, Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to be related to inverse probability weighting. The mechanism likely relies on the conditioning sets being Markov blankets for the missingness indicators R(i). The authors may find the work of Richardson (2003, 2009) on Markov properties for mixed graphs relevant here.

### Minor Issues and Questions:
*   The variables Vo and Vm are used in the sentence before they are defined.
*   The notation for graphs with severed edges (e.g., GX) is used in Definition 4 and Theorem 4 but does not appear to be defined.
*   p. 4, l. 213: Consider using `\leftrightarrow` instead of `<-->` for the bi-directed edge.
*   p. 4, l. 214: R_part is a partition of R. The text describes it as "partitions of R variables". The condition on the following line should probably state that Rx and Ry "belong to distinct elements of the partition".
*   Regarding the discussion of Garcia (2013): Is that work incorrect, or does it simply use a different framework or set of assumptions that lead to a different conclusion? Please clarify.
