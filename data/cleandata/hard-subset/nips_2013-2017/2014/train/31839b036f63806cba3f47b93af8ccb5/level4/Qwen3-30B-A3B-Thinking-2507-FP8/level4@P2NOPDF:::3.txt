

This paper presents a novel approach to recovering distributions under missing data mechanisms, extending prior work by Mohan et al. by considering a broader class of missingness processes. The authors derive a new identification formula and demonstrate that causal queries can be identified even when the full observational joint distribution is not identifiable. The paper is technically sound and well-structured, though several points require clarification to strengthen its contribution and applicability.

**Main Comments:**  
(1) **Practical relevance of censoring process knowledge:** The results assume knowledge of the censoring process, but the paper does not address whether this is feasible in typical machine learning applications. Clarifying the availability of such knowledge (e.g., through domain expertise or auxiliary data) would significantly enhance the paper’s impact. Additionally, Figure 1’s key details (e.g., the role of *V* variables) should be integrated into the main text (e.g., by reducing the figure size) to avoid reliance on supplementary material.

(2) **Population assumption:** The framework implicitly assumes the full population is known (e.g., even when *V*<sub>o</sub> = ∅ and *R*<sub>*v*</sub> = 1 for all *v* ∈ *V*<sub>m</sub>, the individual’s existence is assumed). This excludes settings like population estimation (e.g., wildlife counts), where the total population size is unknown. The authors should explicitly state this limitation.

(3) **Ambiguity in *m*-graphs:** The *m*-graph notation inconsistently includes *V*<sup>*</sup> variables (Figure 1) versus excluding them (Figure 2). If *V*<sup>*</sup> are omitted in some graphs, this must be clarified to avoid confusion.

(4) **Restriction on *R* variables:** The paper prohibits *R* variables from being parents of *V* or *U* variables. Given *R* is treated as a real-valued quantity (not merely an indicator), the justification for this restriction (e.g., theoretical necessity vs. convenience) requires elaboration.

(5) **Undefined term:** The term "neighbor" (p. 4, bottom) is used without definition, creating ambiguity in the context of *m*-graphs.

(6) **Positivity assumption:** The authors’ definition of recoverability assumes positivity. It is unclear whether Garcia et al.’s "rectification" framework (cited on p. 8) also assumes positivity. If not, their approach may require stronger conditions, which should be addressed.

(7) **Clarification on MLE:** The claim that "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR" (p. 8) is misleading. The paper does not explain why MLE cannot be applied to the proposed missingness processes (e.g., MNAR mechanisms). A precise justification is needed.

**Minor Comments:**  
- p. 1, line 33: "can bias outcomes" → "can bias outcomes" (typo).  
- p. 2, Figure 1: Add qualifications to the caption (e.g., "Variables *V*<sup>*</sup> denote missingness indicators").  
- p. 3, Example 1, line 158: *P(Y | X, Z, R<sub>y</sub>) = P(X, Z)* → should be *P(Y | X, Z)*.  
- p. 3, line 168: *Y ⊥⊥ R | X, Z* → should be *Y ⊥⊥ R<sub>y</sub> | X, Z*.  
- p. 3, line 214: *R<sup>part</sup> = {R<sup>(1)</sup>, R<sup>(2)</sup>, …, R<sup>N</sup> }* → should be *R<sup>(N)</sup>*.  
- p. 3, line 215: *R<sup>j</sup> respectively* → *R<sup>(j)</sup>*.  
- p. 4, Corollary 1: "does not contain a partially observed variables" → "does not include partially observed variables."  
- p. 6, Footnote 2: Provide a reference for the variable-level definition.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to missing data identification, particularly for causal inference under non-MAR mechanisms. However, the scope of applications remains unclear—while the framework is technically rigorous, its practical utility hinges on the feasibility of obtaining the assumed censoring process knowledge. The authors should address the above points to strengthen the paper’s clarity, scope, and impact. The minor notational errors and ambiguities must be resolved for publication.
