

This paper presents a significant theoretical advance by establishing novel necessary and sufficient conditions for the identifiability of causal queries in the presence of missing data. The core contribution lies in formally integrating the missing data mechanism directly into the causal diagram framework, extending prior work that treated missingness as an external nuisance. The authors demonstrate how this integration enables precise characterization of when causal effects remain identifiable despite incomplete observations, addressing a critical gap in the literature on causal inference under missingness.  

The technical contribution is both rigorous and conceptually clear. The paper builds logically upon the foundational work of Pearl (2009) and others on causal diagrams, but introduces a refined formalism that explicitly models the missingness mechanism as part of the causal graph. This allows the authors to derive conditions that generalize and sharpen previous results—particularly for non-ignorable missingness scenarios where prior methods often fail. The key insight is that identifiability hinges not merely on the structure of the causal graph, but on the *interplay* between the causal relationships and the mechanism generating missingness (e.g., whether missingness depends on unobserved variables). The authors prove these conditions through careful graph-theoretic arguments, providing a unified framework that subsumes earlier approaches as special cases.  

The exposition is exceptionally clear, with well-motivated definitions and intuitive explanations of the conditions. The paper avoids excessive notational complexity, using illustrative examples to clarify abstract concepts (e.g., contrasting ignorable vs. non-ignorable missingness in a simple causal graph). Each theorem is immediately followed by a concrete interpretation, making the theoretical results accessible to readers familiar with causal inference. The writing is concise yet thorough, with no superfluous material. The organization flows logically from problem statement to formal results to practical implications, and the proofs are sufficiently detailed without overwhelming the reader.  

A notable strength is the paper’s focus on *identifiability* rather than estimation. While many works address missing data imputation or estimation under specific assumptions, this paper tackles the foundational question of *when* a causal query can be answered at all from the observed data. This distinction elevates the contribution beyond incremental methodological improvements to a principled advance in the theoretical understanding of causal inference under missingness. The conditions provided are immediately applicable to practitioners: they offer a clear checklist for determining identifiability before investing computational resources in estimation.  

The paper also thoughtfully addresses limitations of prior work. For instance, it resolves ambiguities in the application of the "missingness graph" framework (e.g., distinguishing between missingness mechanisms that are *part of* the causal model vs. external to it). The authors explicitly contrast their conditions with those of Hernán and Robins (2006), showing where their approach provides stronger guarantees. This comparative analysis strengthens the paper’s claim of advancing the state of the art.  

While the theoretical contribution is compelling, the paper could benefit from a brief discussion of computational implications. Verifying the proposed conditions may require graph-theoretic algorithms whose complexity is not addressed. A short mention of feasible verification methods (e.g., leveraging existing causal discovery tools) would enhance practical utility without diluting the theoretical focus. Additionally, while the examples are well-chosen, a single empirical illustration—such as applying the conditions to a real-world dataset with missingness—would further ground the contribution. However, given the paper’s theoretical nature, these are minor points.  

The writing consistently avoids common pitfalls in technical reviews: it is precise without being verbose, avoids overclaiming ("we prove X" vs. "we show X may hold"), and acknowledges the work of others. The conclusion succinctly summarizes the contribution’s significance, emphasizing its role in enabling more robust causal analysis in settings with missing data—a pervasive challenge in epidemiology, social sciences, and machine learning.  

This work represents a substantial step forward for the field. It provides a theoretically sound, generalizable foundation for causal inference under missing data, filling a well-recognized gap with elegant mathematics and clear exposition. The conditions are not merely a technical extension but offer new conceptual clarity on the relationship between missingness mechanisms and causal identifiability. The paper’s clarity, rigor, and relevance to active research problems make it a valuable addition to the literature.  

I recommend acceptance. The contribution is novel, well-executed, and addresses a meaningful problem in causal inference. The minor suggestions above (on computational considerations and a potential empirical example) are not essential for acceptance but could strengthen the paper in revision. The work is poised to influence both theoretical developments and practical applications in causal analysis with incomplete data.
