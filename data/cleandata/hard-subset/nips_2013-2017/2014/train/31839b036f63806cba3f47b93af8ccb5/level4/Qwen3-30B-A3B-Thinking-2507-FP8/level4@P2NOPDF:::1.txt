

The paper addresses identifiability in missing data mechanisms under Mohan et al.'s framework, presenting technical improvements and well-chosen examples (e.g., causal distribution identifiability without conditional distributions).  

**Quality**  
The technical content is sophisticated and largely correct, with Theorem 3 offering clear, interpretable results. However, several contributions appear incremental rather than transformative, failing to advance a cohesive theoretical understanding. Section 3 (Theorem 1) is notably disconnected from the paper’s core narrative and suffers from poor exposition, resembling Richardson (2009)’s Markov blanket results without clear integration. The quality improves significantly in Sections 5 and 6, which deliver compelling, well-motivated results on causal queries.  

**Clarity**  
This is the paper’s most serious flaw. The density of content—evidenced by the 13-page technical appendix—exceeds conference appropriateness, suggesting a journal-length work. The text lacks logical flow, making it difficult to follow. Critical notation issues exacerbate this: overuse of 'V' for distinct objects (e.g., vertex *Vi* vs. vertex set *Vo*), illegible subscripts, and inconsistent notation (e.g., *Vo* and *Vm* used before definition) create unnecessary confusion. Corollary 1’s set equation exemplifies poor presentation. A more consistent, reader-friendly notation system is essential.  

**Originality and Significance**  
Sections 5 and 6 present genuinely interesting and potentially significant results on causal identifiability. The problem is important, and the framework offers useful insights. However, the paper would gain substantially by synthesizing a unified identifiability criterion—mirroring the completeness achieved in causal effect estimation without missingness. As currently structured, the reader must navigate fragmented results (e.g., Theorem 1’s complexity) to find specific identifiability claims, which is impractical for applied statisticians. Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to leverage inverse probability weighting, likely due to conditioning sets acting as Markov blankets in the mixed graphs; Richardson (2003, 2009) provides relevant context.

**References**  
Richardson, T.S. (2003). Markov properties for acyclic directed mixed graphs. *Sankhyā: The Indian Journal of Statistics*, 65(1), 145–157.  
Richardson, T.S. (2009). A factorization criterion for acyclic directed mixed graphs. *Proceedings of the 25th Conference on Uncertainty in Artificial Intelligence (UAI)*.  

**Summary**  
This work tackles a critical problem with a promising framework, yielding valuable results in Sections 5–6. Yet, poor notation, disorganized structure, and excessive density undermine its impact. The paper’s core contribution—identifiability under missing data mechanisms—deserves a clearer, more unified presentation. With substantial revisions to notation, structure, and scope (e.g., consolidating Section 3, developing a general criterion), this could be a significant contribution. As submitted, it risks being overlooked due to accessibility issues. The technical quality is strong, but the presentation must improve to match the work’s potential.
