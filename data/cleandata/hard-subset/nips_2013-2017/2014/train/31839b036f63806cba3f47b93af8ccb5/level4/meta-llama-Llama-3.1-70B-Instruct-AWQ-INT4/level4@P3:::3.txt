This paper addresses the problem of deciding whether a causal or probabilistic query is estimable from data corrupted by missing entries, given a model of the missingness process. The authors extend the results of Mohan et al. (2013) by presenting more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries of the form P(y|x) and P(y, x) as well as causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)). The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem and their approach.

The main contribution of the paper is the development of graphical conditions for recovering joint and conditional distributions, as well as causal queries. The authors introduce the concept of "missingness graphs" (m-graphs), which provide a formal representation of the missingness process and the dependencies among variables in the available dataset. They then present several theorems that provide sufficient conditions for recoverability of different types of queries.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to handle more general classes of missingness processes than previous work. The authors consider cases where the missingness mechanisms interact with each other, and they provide novel identification formulas for these cases. They also consider the identification of causal queries and show that these can be identified even when the observational joint distribution is not identified.

The paper is generally well-written, but there are some areas for improvement. One of the main limitations of the paper is that it requires knowledge of the process by which observations are censored, which may not be available in typical machine learning applications. The authors could benefit from discussing the applicability of their results to real-world problems and the potential limitations of their approach.

Additionally, some of the notation and terminology used in the paper could be clarified. For example, the term "m-graph" is used ambiguously, and it is not clear whether the graph includes the V* variables or not. The authors could also provide more detail regarding Figure 1, which is an important illustration of their approach.

Overall, the paper is a solid contribution to the field of graphical models for missing data. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem and their approach, and they present several important results that extend previous work in this area.
