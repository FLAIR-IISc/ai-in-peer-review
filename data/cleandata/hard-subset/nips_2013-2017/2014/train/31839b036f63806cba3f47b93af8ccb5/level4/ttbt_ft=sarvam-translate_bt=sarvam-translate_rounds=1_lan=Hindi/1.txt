This research paper deals with the problem of identification in the presence of various generated mechanisms for missing data, using a framework presented by Mohan and others. Some existing results have been improved, and some good examples are given (such as identifying the cause effect even when a conditional distribution is not available).

**Quality**
The technical quality seems high, the results are very complex but seem correct. Results like Theorem 3 are clear and explainable. Some results are gradual improvements over existing results, and (as far as I can see) do not contribute to a larger picture of what is actually happening here.

Section 3 seems orthogonal to the rest of the paper, and its main result (Theorem 1) is quite difficult to understand. It seems closely related to the Markov blanket results in Richardson (2009). The quality improves later, Section 5 gives some good clear answers, and Section 6 gives some interesting results about the cause question.

**Clarity**
This is the main weakness of the paper. First of all, there is probably too much material for a conference paper (although the importance of some of it is not clear), as is evident from the density of the text and the 13-page technical appendix. The article does not flow clearly, and as a result, it is difficult to follow - perhaps it would work better as a longer, more considered, journal paper.

While it is not easy to find good notation for so much quantity, the notation can certainly be improved. Using the same capital 'V' to represent so many different objects makes it difficult for the reader, and the subscripts become unreadable in some places. Furthermore, the fact that Vi is a vertex but Vo is a set of vertices is particularly confusing. The set equation in Corollary 1 is perhaps the most extreme example of poor presentation.

**Originality and Importance**
Sections 5 and 6 are clear and interesting, and potentially quite important. This paper deals with an important problem, and it contains results that seem genuinely interesting and useful.

Perhaps it would be better to step back and find out what is happening in the general case, and (try) to come up with a complete criterion for identification in this framework (as was achieved in the case of finding the cause effect without the missing effect). It is difficult for a statistician to sift through a series of such papers to find the exact result that indicates the (non-) identification of the cause effect in which he is interested, especially if the result is as complex as Theorem 1.

Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 seem to be a form of inverse probability weighting. The reason for these results working is probably related to the conditioning set being a Markov blanket for R(i) in the mixed graph being used by the author. Richardson (2003) and (2009) may be relevant.

**Other Comments**

The language is sometimes a bit informal: for example 'often'.
Page 4, line 213. Use \leftrightarrow instead of <-->.
Is Garcia (2013) really wrong, or just a different framework?
A notation definition for graphs created by cutting edges is used in Theorem 4 and Theorem 4, but not defined anywhere.
Vo and Vm are used in the sentence before they are defined (apparently unnecessarily).
