This paper addresses the critical problem of determining whether a probabilistic or causal query can be consistently estimated from a dataset with missing entries. The authors build upon the framework of "Missingness Graphs" (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. [2013], which explicitly model the mechanisms responsible for missing data. The paper extends this prior work by providing more general graphical conditions for the recoverability of probabilistic queries like P(y|x) and P(y, x), and causal queries of the form P(y|do(x)).

A key contribution of this work is the demonstration that causal queries may be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic quantities are not. Specifically, the authors derive graphical conditions under which P(y|do(x)) can be estimated even when the outcome variable Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable in the m-graph—a condition that would typically preclude the recovery of joint or conditional distributions involving Y. The paper introduces several new theoretical results, including a generalized sequential factorization theorem, a recovery method for cases where factorization fails, and novel graphical criteria that are sufficient for *non-recoverability*. Finally, these theoretical tools are applied to the practical and common problem of attrition in experimental studies.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality and Significance:** This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the fields of causal inference and missing data analysis. The problem of missing data is ubiquitous, and formal methods for assessing the recoverability of scientific queries are of immense practical and theoretical importance. The paper's main results are technically sound, with proofs provided in the appendix.
    *   The insight that causal queries can be recovered under weaker conditions than probabilistic queries is particularly important. This could allow researchers to draw valid causal conclusions from imperfect datasets where they might have previously assumed it was impossible.
    *   The introduction of formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2) is a novel and valuable contribution. Knowing when a query is *not* recoverable is just as important as knowing when it is, as it prevents researchers from applying methods that would yield biased estimates and alerts them to the need for different assumptions or data collection strategies.

*   **Originality:** While the paper builds directly on the foundations laid by Mohan et al. [2013], its extensions are substantial and non-trivial.
    *   The "General Ordered Factorization" (Theorem 1) broadens the applicability of the factorization approach to more complex scenarios, such as longitudinal studies where missingness at one time point may influence missingness at another.
    *   The results on attrition (Section 7) not only demonstrate the utility of the proposed framework but also serve to correct and clarify prevailing heuristics in the existing literature, which is a valuable service to practitioners.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of first reviewing the necessary background on m-graphs and recoverability, then systematically presenting their new contributions. The flow from sufficient conditions for recovery (Theorems 1 & 2), to necessary conditions (Theorem 3), to the special case of causal queries (Theorem 4), and finally to a practical application (Section 7) is logical and easy to follow. The use of clear examples (e.g., Example 1 and 3) to illustrate the core concepts and theorems is highly effective.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is very strong, and the following points are minor suggestions for potential improvement:

*   The conditions in Theorem 2, which rely on concepts like "collider path" and partitions of R variables (R(part)), are quite technical. While the definitions are provided, a bit more intuition in the main text about *why* these specific graph structures permit or preclude recoverability could further enhance the reader's understanding.
*   The paper focuses exclusively on the theoretical question of recoverability (i.e., the existence of a consistent estimator). A brief discussion on the practical implications for estimation—for instance, the statistical efficiency or computational complexity of the resulting estimators—would be a welcome addition and could broaden the paper's impact for practitioners.
*   In Section 7.2, the paper states that its results rectify prevailing opinion, citing Garcia [2013]. This is an important point, and it could be strengthened by briefly explaining *why* the previous condition (X being an ancestor of Ry) was thought to be necessary and how the new collider-path-based condition provides a more complete picture.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that provides novel, important, and clearly-presented results on a fundamental problem in data analysis. It significantly advances the state-of-the-art in using graphical models to reason about missing data. The theoretical contributions are strong, and their practical utility is well-demonstrated through the analysis of attrition. The paper is a definite asset to the NIPS proceedings.
