- Investigates recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing data using Missingness Graphs, extending Mohan et al.  
- Presents general conditions for recovering P(y|x), P(y,x), and P(y|do(x)), emphasizing cases where causal queries are recoverable even when probabilistic relations are not.  
- Shows causal effects can be identified even when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable.  
- Extends sequential factorization to accommodate interactions between missingness mechanisms (Theorem 1).  
- Formalizes conditions where causal queries are recoverable despite non-recoverable probabilistic relationships (Example 3, Theorem 4).  
- Applies the theoretical results to attrition problems, providing a concrete practical illustration.  
- Paper is well-structured, clearly written, and builds effectively on prior work (Mohan et al., Pearl & Mohan).  
- Theorems and proofs are rigorous and provided in the appendix.  
- Numerous examples and theorems improve comprehension of complex concepts.  
- Novel theoretical contributions advance causal inference under missing data.  
- Practical relevance is demonstrated but primarily for “simple attrition” settings.  
- Mathematical notation and concepts can be challenging; more pedagogical explanation (e.g., d*-separation, inducing paths) is needed.  
- Limited empirical validation: no real or simulated experiments to assess practical performance and robustness.  
- Critique of Garcia [2013] is strong but would benefit from a more nuanced, thorough comparison.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound results that extend existing knowledge and are clearly presented.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of empirical validation, high technical complexity for broader audiences, and narrow attrition scope.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve accessibility and to discuss/plan empirical evaluation and extensions to more complex attrition mechanisms.
