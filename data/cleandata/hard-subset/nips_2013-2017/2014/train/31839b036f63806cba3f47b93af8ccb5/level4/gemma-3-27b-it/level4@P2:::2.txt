This paper investigates the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries from datasets with missing data, building upon the framework of “Missingness Graphs” introduced by Mohan et al. [2013]. The authors extend prior work by presenting more general conditions for recovering queries of the form P(y|x), P(y,x), and P(y|do(x)), with a particular focus on scenarios where causal queries are recoverable even when associated probabilistic relations are not. A key contribution is demonstrating that causal effects can be identified even when Y and its missingness mechanism are not d-separable. The work applies these results to the specific problem of attrition.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, effectively building upon the foundation laid by Mohan et al. [2013] and Pearl & Mohan [2013]. The authors provide a comprehensive overview of missingness graphs and recoverability, progressively refining existing theorems and introducing new graphical criteria. The extension of sequential factorization to accommodate interactions between missingness mechanisms (Theorem 1) is a significant step forward. Furthermore, the exploration of conditions under which causal queries are recoverable despite non-recoverable probabilistic relationships (demonstrated with Example 3 and formalized in Theorem 4) is novel and impactful. The application to attrition problems (Section 7) provides a concrete illustration of the methodology's practical relevance. The numerous examples and theorems are useful for comprehension.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents new theoretical results regarding the recoverability of causal queries under missing data, particularly in cases where traditional assumptions (e.g., d-separation) do not hold.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical framework is rigorous, and the proofs (provided in the appendix) appear sound.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains complex concepts, making it accessible to readers familiar with causal inference and graphical models.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The application to attrition problems demonstrates the potential of the methodology for addressing real-world challenges.
*   **Completeness:** The paper thoroughly reviews related work, acknowledging contributions from various areas like missing data analysis and causal inference.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** While the writing is clear, the mathematical notation and concepts can be challenging for readers not deeply familiar with causal inference and graphical models. Greater pedagogical explanation of concepts like d*-separation and inducing paths would be helpful.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses heavily on theoretical results. While examples are provided, the lack of empirical validation on real or simulated datasets limits the assessment of the method's practical performance and robustness.
*   **Scope of Attrition:** The discussion of attrition primarily focuses on “simple attrition”. Extending the analysis to more complex attrition mechanisms would strengthen the practical implications.
*   **Reference to Garcia [2013]:** The critique of Garcia [2013] is strong but could benefit from further nuance. A more thorough comparison of the conditions presented in both papers would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents novel and theoretically sound contributions to the field of causal inference under missing data.
*   The results extend existing knowledge and provide new insights into the conditions under which causal effects can be identified despite complex missingness patterns.
*   The clear writing and comprehensive analysis make the paper a valuable resource for researchers in this area.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical validation limits the assessment of the method’s practical utility.
*   The complexity of the material may make it challenging for a broad audience to fully grasp the contributions.
*   The focus on a specific type of attrition might limit the immediate applicability of the results.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the challenging problem of causal inference with missing data. While the lack of empirical validation and inherent complexity are limitations, the novelty, rigor, and clarity of the work strongly support acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve the accessibility of the material and to acknowledge the limitations of the current scope. Specifically, I suggest briefly discussing potential avenues for empirical evaluation and exploring extensions to more complex attrition mechanisms in future work.
