This paper addresses the problem of recovering a distribution from knowledge of the generating process and the process leading to missing data. The authors consider a more general class of missingness processes than earlier work by Mohan et al., leading to a novel identification formula. Additionally, they examine the identification of causal queries and demonstrate that these can be identified even when the observational joint distribution is not identified.

The paper is generally well-written, but there are some areas for improvement. The main comments are as follows:

(1) The results presented in this paper require knowledge of the process by which observations are censored. However, it is unclear whether this knowledge is typically available in machine learning applications. Furthermore, including more detail about Figure 1 in the main paper would be beneficial. This could be achieved by reducing the size of the figure.

(2) In Section 2, it should be noted that the missing data processes described always assume that the full population is known. Even in cases where no variables are observed for an individual, it is assumed that the existence of this individual is known. This excludes certain missing data problems, such as estimating the number of animals in a population.

(3) The term "mgraph" appears to be used ambiguously throughout the paper. In some instances, the graph includes V* variables (Figure 1), while in others, it does not (Figure 2). If the V* variables are being omitted in these graphs, this should be explicitly stated.

(4) The authors do not allow mgraphs to have R variables as parents of variables in V or U. Given that R variables are treated as real quantities rather than simply indicators, it would be beneficial to explain why this restriction is justified or whether it is primarily for convenience.

(5) The term "neighbor" is introduced at the bottom of page 4 without a clear definition.

(6) On page 8, the first paragraph mentions the "rectification" of the observation by Garcia. However, it is unclear whether Garcia et al. assume positivity, which is a condition required for recoverability as defined by the authors. If Garcia et al. do not assume positivity, they may require a stronger condition.

(7) The second paragraph on page 8 states that the "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under the MAR assumption." This statement is too vague, as it does not explain why maximum likelihood cannot be applied to any of the missingness processes described in the paper.

Minor comments:

* Page 1, line 33: "can bias outcomes" should be rephrased for clarity.
* Page 2, Figure 1: Consider adding qualifications to the figure.
* Page 2, Example 1, line 158: The equation P(Y | X, Z, R_y) = P(X, Z) should be corrected to P(Y | X, Z).
* Page 3, line 168: The equation Since Y || R | X, Z should be corrected to Y || R_y | X, Z.
* Page 3, line 214: Condition (ii) R^{part} ={R^{(1)},R^{(2)}, \ldots , R^N} should be corrected to R^{(N)}.
* Page 3, line 215: R^j should be corrected to R^{(j)}.
* Page 4, Corollary 1: The statement does not contain partially observed variables.
* Page 6, Footnote 2: A reference for the variable-level definition would be appreciated.

Overall, this is an interesting paper that builds upon earlier work. While the technical soundness of the paper is clear, the breadth of its application is less apparent.
