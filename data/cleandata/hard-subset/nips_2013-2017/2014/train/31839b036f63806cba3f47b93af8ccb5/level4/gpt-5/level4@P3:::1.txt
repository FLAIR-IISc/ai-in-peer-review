Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies when probabilistic and causal queries are recoverable from data subject to missingness, given a graphical model of the missingness process (m-graphs). Building on Mohan et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors broaden sufficient conditions for recovering joint and conditional distributions and introduce new non-recoverability criteria. They further show that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when the corresponding probabilistic relations (e.g., P(y|x), P(x, y)) are not recoverable, and apply their results to attrition in longitudinal studies.

Contributions include:
- A generalization of ordered factorization to include missingness indicators R in the separating sets (Theorem 1), enabling recovery in models where R variables interact.
- A recoverability result for joint distributions in semi-Markovian models via partitions of R and their Markov blankets (Theorem 2) with a corollary for models without latent common causes between substantive variables and R (Corollary 1).
- Graphical criteria for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2).
- Conditions under which causal effects are recoverable despite inseparability of Y and Ry, formalized via d*-separation and dormant independencies (Theorem 4), relating to Shpitser and Pearl (2006, 2008) and ADMG theory (Richardson, 2003).
- Application to attrition, with necessary and sufficient conditions for recovering joint distributions and causal effects when |Vm| = 1 (Theorems 5 and 6), clarifying and correcting claims in Garcia (2013).

Overall, the work situates within a line of research that uses causal graphical models to go beyond MAR/MCAR assumptions (Rubin, 1976; Little and Rubin, 2002), complementing likelihood-based, weighting, and imputation approaches, and connecting to ADMG Markov properties and factorization (Richardson, 2003, 2009).

Quality
- Technical soundness appears high. The non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3; Corollary 2) are clear, interpretable, and fill a gap.
- The extension in Theorem 1 is useful but difficult to parse; a stronger intuitive explanation and examples justifying the minimality requirement in the generalized factorization would help.
- Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 read as principled forms of inverse probability weighting; providing a brief derivation that highlights the role of Markov blankets (and their relation to ADMG separation) would aid understanding.
- The causal recovery results (Section 6) are interesting and correct in spirit; the connection to d*-separation and do-calculus is appropriate. It would help to emphasize early that identifiability in the substantive model is a necessary prerequisite for recoverability.

Clarity
This is the paper’s main weakness.
- Scope and density: The manuscript is very dense for a conference format, with many definitions and results (plus a lengthy appendix). The narrative would benefit from consolidation around a few running examples, deferring some generality to the appendix.
- Organization: Section 3 feels orthogonal and hard to digest on first reading; later sections (5 and 6) are clearer and more impactful. Consider moving the main intuitions and examples forward, with proofs and generalizations in the appendix.
- Notation: The overloading of V for several different sets and variables, and the mixing of single-variable and set subscripts (e.g., Vi versus Vo) make reading difficult. Consider using different font styles or diacritics for sets versus variables, and avoid reusing the same letter for multiple roles. The set equation in Corollary 1 is particularly hard to parse.
- Definitions and graph surgery notation: Define precisely the notation for edge removal and manipulated graphs where first used and be consistent. Ensure the definition of R-partitions (Rpart) is correct and unambiguous.
- Language: Tighten informal phrasing and avoid colloquialisms.

Originality
- The non-recoverability criteria and the demonstration that causal effects may be recoverable even when corresponding probabilistic quantities are not are novel and important.
- The generalization of sequential factorization to include R variables is incremental but useful in practice.
- The attrition results (Theorems 5 and 6) provide clean necessary and sufficient conditions in a widely encountered setting.

Significance
- The topic is important and relevant for NIPS, with clear connections to graphical models, causal inference, and learning under missing data.
- The results will be valuable to practitioners who can encode missingness assumptions graphically and then diagnose recoverability.
- That said, usability would increase with a clearer narrative and a more unified “recipe” for deciding recoverability, ideally moving toward completeness results analogous to identification without missingness.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Addresses an important problem with practical relevance.
- Clear, useful non-recoverability criteria.
- Conceptually interesting demonstration of causal recoverability when probabilistic recovery fails.
- Attrition results provide necessary and sufficient conditions and correct misunderstandings in prior work.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Presentation and notation make the paper hard to follow; Section 3 is especially opaque.
- Some contributions are incremental relative to Mohan et al. (2013).
- Lacks a unifying, near-complete criterion for recoverability; practitioners may struggle to locate and apply the right result.

Specific suggestions and minor comments
- Clarify Rpart: it is a partition of R; state that Rx and Ry in different parts must not be neighbors or connected by a collider path. Update the text accordingly.
- Define, early and consistently, the graph notation used for removing incoming/outgoing edges and for manipulated graphs; ensure consistency with the do-calculus notation and with GX-type definitions.
- Improve the statement and readability of Corollary 1’s set conditions; consider introducing Pasub and Mb with succinct definitions and an illustrative figure.
- Notation: avoid overloading V; distinguish vertices (e.g., uppercase) from sets (e.g., script letters) and use consistent subscripts for sets vs elements. Ensure Vi (vertex) versus Vo (set) is not conflated.
- Language: avoid “oftentimes”; prefer “often”.
- Typographical: use ↔ instead of <-> for bidirected edges.
- Vo and Vm appear before being defined in at least one place; define before use.
- Clarify whether the disagreement with Garcia (2013) stems from different assumptions/frameworks or a concrete error; state the assumptions under which your counterexample applies.
- References need cleanup: ensure complete bibliographic information and consistent formatting; add the Richardson (2009) UAI factorization citation formally if you rely on it.

Overall evaluation
Important and timely paper with solid technical contributions, notably the non-recoverability criteria and the causal recoverability results. However, the current presentation and notation substantially impede readability. With significant editorial improvements and a stronger unifying narrative (potentially shortening or deferring some generality to the appendix), this would be a strong contribution. As is, I view it as borderline for a conference but leaning positive due to significance and technical merit.
