Summary
This paper studies when probabilistic and causal queries can be recovered from data with missing entries, given a graphical model of the missingness mechanism (m-graphs). It extends Mohan et al. (2013) by: (i) generalizing sequential factorization to include missingness indicators R, enabling recovery in settings where missingness mechanisms interact; (ii) providing algorithms for recovering joint distributions when no admissible sequence exists; (iii) introducing graphical criteria for non-recoverability of joint and conditional distributions; and (iv) showing that certain causal effects P(y|do(x)) are recoverable even when related probabilistic quantities (e.g., P(y|x), P(y,x)) are not, including cases where Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable. The paper also analyzes “simple attrition,” giving necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for recovering joint distributions and causal effects in that setting. The work connects to prior results on identifiability via do-calculus (Shpitser & Pearl, 2006; Pearl, 2009) and to ADMG theory (Richardson, 2003), and builds directly on Mohan et al. (2013).

Quality
- Technical soundness appears high. Results such as Theorem 3 (non-recoverability of P(V)) are clear and interpretable. The later sections (5–6) are particularly convincing and include informative examples (e.g., recoverability of P(y|do(z)) even when P(y|z,w) is not recoverable).
- Some results seem incremental relative to Mohan et al. (2013), and the paper as a whole does not yet yield a unifying or complete criterion for recoverability in this framework.
- Section 3 feels orthogonal to the rest. Theorem 1 (general ordered factorization) is hard to parse and appears closely related to Markov blanket/factorization properties in ADMGs (cf. Richardson, 2003, 2009). The technical quality improves thereafter; Sections 5 and 6 provide crisp statements and useful insights about causal queries.

Clarity
This is the main weakness.
- Scope and density: There is likely too much material for a conference paper, as evidenced by dense prose and a 13-page technical appendix. The narrative does not flow smoothly; a more streamlined structure (or a journal-length treatment) would help.
- Notation: Reuse of V for multiple objects is taxing; subscripts are often hard to read. It is especially confusing that Vi denotes a vertex while Vo denotes a set of vertices. The set expression in Corollary 1 is an extreme case of difficult presentation. Consider:
  - Using distinct symbols for vertices vs sets (e.g., boldface for sets).
  - Avoiding Vo and Vm before they are defined.
  - Defining the notation for graphs with “severed” edges when first used (it appears in Definition 4 and Theorem 4 without prior definition).
  - Simplifying conditioning sets where possible and providing a running example to anchor each theorem.

Originality
- Sections 5 and 6 contain novel, interesting results—especially the graphical non-recoverability criteria and the demonstration that certain causal effects are recoverable despite inseparability between Y and Ry.
- However, the contribution in Section 3 seems close in spirit to existing ADMG factorization/Markov blanket results; clarifying the precise relationship to Richardson (2003, 2009) would strengthen claims of novelty.

Significance
- The problem is important. The results in Sections 5 and 6 are potentially impactful for practice.
- That said, the field would benefit from a more unified characterization of recoverability in m-graphs, analogous to complete identifiability results for causal effects without missingness. As is, it is hard to imagine practitioners searching across multiple papers for bespoke criteria, especially when statements like Theorem 1 are complex.
- Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 look like inverse probability weighting (IPW): the conditioning sets resemble Markov blankets of R(i) in the mixed graph. Please make this connection explicit and explain when/why the IPW interpretation holds.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with clear connections to ongoing work in causal inference and missing data.
- New, interpretable non-recoverability criteria (Theorem 3; Corollary 2).
- Insightful results showing causal recoverability beyond probabilistic recoverability.
- Useful analysis of attrition with necessary and sufficient conditions (Theorems 5–6).

Cons for acceptance
- Presentation and notation significantly hinder readability.
- Section 3’s main result is hard to understand and its novelty relative to ADMG factorization is unclear.
- The paper feels like a set of extensions rather than a unified theory; lack of a complete criterion limits practical uptake.

Specific comments and suggestions
- p.4, l.214: R^part is a partition of R, not “a set containing partitions.” State that Rx and Ry belong to distinct elements of the partition.
- p.4, l.213: Use ↔ (or bidirected-edge notation) rather than “<-->”.
- Language: avoid casual terms such as “oftentimes.”
- Clarify whether Garcia (2013) is actually wrong under the same assumptions, or whether the discrepancy arises from different frameworks/assumptions.
- Define the notation for graphs constructed by severing edges before using it (appears in Definition 4 and Theorem 4).
- Avoid using Vo and Vm prior to their definitions.
- Strengthen the discussion linking Theorem 2/Corollary 1 to IPW and to Markov blankets in ADMGs; cite and contrast with Richardson (2003, 2009).

Overall
An important and promising line of work with several valuable results (especially in Sections 5–6), but currently marred by presentation and organizational issues. With clearer notation, a tighter narrative, and an explicit positioning relative to ADMG factorization and IPW, this could be a strong contribution.
