This paper presents an interesting contribution to the study of distribution recovery in the presence of missing data. The core problem addressed is determining when a distribution can be recovered from knowledge of both the data-generating process and the missingness mechanism.

A key contribution is the consideration of a broader class of missingness processes than in previous work, such as that by Mohan et al. This generalization results in a novel identification formula. Furthermore, the authors explore the identification of causal queries, demonstrating that they can be identified even in cases where the full observational joint distribution is not.

While the paper is generally well-written, there are several points that require clarification and areas for improvement, as detailed below.

MAIN COMMENTS:
(1) The presented results hinge on the assumption that the process by which observations are censored is known. The authors should comment on the practicality of this assumption, particularly its availability in typical machine learning applications. Additionally, to improve clarity, it would be beneficial to incorporate more of the details related to Figure 1 directly into the main text, perhaps by resizing the figure to create space.

(2) In Section 2, it should be explicitly stated that the considered missing data processes presume the full population size is known. That is, even when an individual has no observed variables (e.g., V_o = ∅ and R_v = 1 for all V in V_m), their existence is still assumed to be recorded. This assumption distinguishes the work from missing data problems where the population size itself is an unknown to be estimated, such as in species population estimation.

(3) The usage of the term 'm-graph' appears to be inconsistent throughout the paper. Specifically, the m-graph in Figure 1 includes V* variables, whereas the one in Figure 2 does not. If the V* variables are intentionally omitted in some diagrams for simplicity, this convention should be explicitly explained to the reader.

(4) The proposed m-graph framework restricts R variables from being parents of variables in V or U. Since the R variables are treated as substantive quantities rather than mere indicators, the authors should provide a justification for this structural constraint, clarifying whether it is a fundamental requirement or a simplification for convenience.

(5) The term 'neighbor', used at the bottom of page 4, is not formally defined.

(6) On page 8, the authors discuss the "rectification" of observations by Garcia et al. The definition of recoverability in this paper relies on a positivity assumption. It is unclear if the work by Garcia et al. makes a similar assumption. If not, their method might require a different or stronger condition, and this distinction should be clarified.

(7) The statement in the second paragraph of page 8, "Maximum Likelihood method is known to yield consistent estimates under MAR assumption," is overly general. The authors should elaborate on why standard maximum likelihood estimation would not be applicable to the broader class of missingness processes they introduce in this work.

MINOR COMMENTS
*   Page 1, line 33: The phrase "can bias outcomes" appears to be an incomplete sentence.
*   Page 2, Figure 1 caption: "Qualifications" may be a typo.
*   Page 3, line 158 (Example 1): The equation `P(Y |X,Z,R_y) = P(X,Z)` seems to contain a typo. The right-hand side should likely be `P(Y|X,Z)`.
*   Page 3, line 168: The independence statement `Y || R | X,Z` should likely be more specific, for instance, `Y || R_y | X,Z`.
*   Page 3, line 214: In Condition (ii), for consistency, `R^N` should be `R^{(N)}`.
*   Page 3, line 215: For consistency, `R^j` should be `R^{(j)}`.
*   Page 4, Corollary 1: The phrase "does not contain a partially observed variables" is grammatically incorrect.
*   Page 6, Footnote 2: A citation should be provided for the variable-level definition of m-separability.
