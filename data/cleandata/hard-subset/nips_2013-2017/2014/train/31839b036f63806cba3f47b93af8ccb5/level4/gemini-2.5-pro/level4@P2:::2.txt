This paper addresses the critical and ubiquitous problem of determining whether a probabilistic or causal query can be consistently estimated from data with missing entries. The authors build upon the formal framework of "missingness graphs" (m-graphs) introduced by Mohan et al. [2013] to represent the data-generating and missingness processes in a unified graphical model. The work extends previous results by providing more general conditions for the recoverability of probabilistic queries (e.g., P(y,x), P(y|x)) and, more significantly, delves into the recoverability of causal queries (e.g., P(y|do(x))). The central and most striking contribution is the demonstration that causal queries may be recoverable even when the underlying joint or conditional distributions are not, particularly in cases where a variable is not separable from its own missingness mechanism. The paper develops novel graphical criteria for both recoverability and non-recoverability and applies these theoretical results to the practical problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper's most important contribution is establishing a clear and theoretically grounded distinction between the recoverability of probabilistic and causal queries. The finding that P(y|do(x)) can be recoverable while P(y) is not (as shown in Section 6) is a powerful and non-obvious insight. This significantly advances the state-of-the-art by showing that problems previously deemed intractable due to missing data might still permit valid causal conclusions.

2.  **Novelty of Non-Recoverability Criteria:** A major strength is the introduction of formal graphical criteria for *non-recoverability* (Theorem 3 and Corollary 2). Most literature focuses on sufficient conditions for when recovery is possible. By providing necessary conditions, the authors give researchers a powerful tool to quickly diagnose when their assumptions are insufficient and a query is fundamentally inestimable without further knowledge or data. This is of immense practical value.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a rigorous extension of prior work. The generalization of the sequential factorization theorem to handle dependencies among missingness mechanisms (Theorem 1) is a valuable addition. The development of theorems for cases where no admissible sequence exists (Theorem 2) broadens the applicability of the m-graph framework. The proofs are provided in the appendix for verification.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and logically structured. The authors make excellent use of illustrative examples (e.g., Example 1, Example 3) to build intuition and demonstrate the application of their theorems. The progression from probabilistic queries to causal queries and finally to the specific case of attrition is clear and easy to follow.

5.  **Practical Relevance:** By applying the theoretical framework to attrition (Section 7), a common and challenging issue in experimental science, the authors demonstrate the practical utility of their findings. The resulting theorems (Theorems 5 and 6) provide clear, graphical conditions for recoverability in this specific but important context, correcting some stated beliefs in existing literature.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for Improvement):**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the contributions stand on their own as a formal piece of work, the paper's impact could be significantly strengthened by including a small simulation study. For instance, the authors could generate data from a known m-graph (like the one in Figure 3), introduce missingness, and show that their proposed estimator for P(y|do(z)) converges to the true value, while a naive approach (e.g., complete-case analysis) produces a biased estimate. This would provide a concrete demonstration of the theory's power.

2.  **Presentation of Theorem 2:** Theorem 2 introduces several new concepts (`collider path`, `R(part)`, `Mb(R(i))`) in a very dense fashion. While the definitions are formally correct, the intuition could be built up more gradually. A small, dedicated figure illustrating the partitioning of R variables and the corresponding Markov blankets might improve the accessibility of this otherwise powerful theorem.

3.  **Connection to Existing Work:** The connection to dormant independence and d*-separation in Section 6 is crucial for the main causal result. While correctly cited, a brief, intuitive explanation of these concepts in the main text could help readers who are not specialists in that specific area of causal inference to better appreciate the mechanism behind the result.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the theory and practice of handling missing data within the principled framework of graphical causal models. The distinction it draws between probabilistic and causal recoverability is a fundamental insight, and the provision of non-recoverability criteria is a highly valuable, practical addition to the field. While the paper could be improved with empirical validation, its theoretical contributions are solid and important. It is well-written, builds clearly on previous work, and is highly relevant to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
