

This paper extends the work of Mohan et al. (2013) on missingness graphs (m-graphs) to develop more general conditions for recovering probabilistic queries (P(y|x), P(y,x)) and causal queries (P(y|do(x))) from missing data. The authors demonstrate that causal queries may be recoverable even when the underlying probabilistic relations are not recoverable, specifically showing that P(y|do(x)) can be recovered even when Y and its missingness mechanism Ry are not d-separable. The paper applies these results to attrition problems, providing necessary and sufficient graphical conditions for recovering causal effects.

Quality: The technical quality is strong, with rigorous theoretical development. The proofs appear sound, and results like Theorem 3 are clearly interpretable. The paper makes a meaningful contribution by extending Mohan et al.'s framework to handle missingness mechanisms that interact with one another (Section 3), a significant limitation in prior work. However, some results appear incremental rather than transformative, particularly Theorem 1 which requires careful navigation of complex notation and concepts. The paper's most valuable contributions are in Sections 5-6, which establish clear non-recoverability criteria for joint/conditional distributions and demonstrate the surprising recoverability of causal effects even when probabilistic queries are not recoverable. The authors correctly note that causal queries may circumvent the separability requirement that blocks probabilistic query recovery (Example 3), which is a significant insight.

Clarity: This is the paper's primary weakness. The dense writing style, inconsistent notation, and lack of clear organization hinder comprehension. The use of the same capital "V" to denote multiple different objects (e.g., V, V*, Vm, Vo) creates significant confusion, particularly when Vi denotes a vertex while Vo denotes a set of vertices. The notation for missingness mechanisms (R, Rx, Ry, R(i)) is especially problematic, with subscripts becoming illegible in places. The presentation of Corollary 1 is particularly unclear, with a confusing set equation that obscures the main point. The paper would benefit from a more systematic notation scheme and better organization. Section 3, which presents Theorem 1, is particularly difficult to follow and seems disconnected from the rest of the paper. The authors might have improved clarity by focusing on the most significant contributions (Sections 5-6) and relegating some technical details to the appendix.

Originality: The paper makes a genuine contribution to an important problem in missing data analysis. The key insight that causal queries may be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not represents a meaningful advance over Mohan et al. (2013). The graphical conditions for recoverability in attrition problems (Theorem 6) also constitute a significant contribution. However, the paper could strengthen its contribution by providing a more unified framework for recoverability, rather than presenting a series of specific results. The authors would benefit from explicitly connecting their work to the broader goal of developing a complete criterion for identifiability in the missing data framework, as achieved for causal effects without missing data (Shpitser and Pearl, 2006). The connection to Richardson's Markov blanket work (2003, 2009) could also be more clearly articulated.

Significance: This is an important problem with clear practical implications for experimental sciences plagued by missing data. The paper's results on attrition (Section 7) directly address a common challenge in longitudinal studies. The demonstration that causal effects may be recoverable even when probabilistic queries are not (Example 3) is particularly valuable for practitioners. The paper provides a valuable framework for diagnosing recoverability and communicating assumptions about missingness processes. However, the complexity of the conditions (e.g., Theorem 1) may limit practical utility, as the authors acknowledge that it would be difficult for practitioners to navigate these results. A more streamlined framework would enhance the paper's significance.

The paper contains several minor issues that would improve readability: "R^part" should be defined as "a partition of R" rather than "a set containing partitions" (p. 4, l. 214); "R^part" should be used consistently to denote a partition; "↔" should replace "<-->" (p. 4, l. 213); "oftentimes" should be replaced with a more formal term; and the notation for graphs with severed edges (Definition 4, Theorem 4) should be defined earlier.

Overall, this is an important paper addressing a significant problem with sound technical contributions, particularly in Sections 5-6. However, the poor presentation and notation significantly hinder the paper's accessibility and impact. The authors should focus on clarifying their notation, improving organization, and emphasizing the most significant contributions to maximize the paper's value to the community. With these improvements, this work would be a strong contribution to the field.
