- Problem: Determine whether probabilistic or causal queries are estimable from data with missing entries given a missingness model.  
- Extension: Builds on Mohan et al. (2013) to provide more general recoverability conditions for P(y|x), P(y,x), and P(y|do(x)).  
- Main contribution: Shows causal queries can be recoverable even when underlying probabilistic relations are not, with attrition examples.  
- Framework: Uses Missingness Graphs (m-graphs) and leverages Pearl’s graphical models and do-calculus.  
- Advance: Extends sequential factorization to interacting missingness mechanisms, addressing prior limitations.  
- Non-recoverability focus: Provides practical criteria and guidance for when queries cannot be recovered.  
- Theoretical soundness: Rigorous definitions, theorems, and well-structured proofs (in appendix).  
- Novelty: Original insights about causal vs probabilistic recoverability and interacting missingness.  
- Clarity: Generally well-written and organized; motivating introduction and illustrative examples.  
- Practical relevance: Direct implications for observational/experimental studies; non-recoverability criteria are useful.  
- Complexity: Framework can be hard to grasp without strong causal inference/graphical models background.  
- Notation burden: Heavy use of notation makes derivations harder to follow; would benefit from gradual introduction.  
- Empirical evaluation: Lacks comprehensive simulations or real-data experiments to demonstrate practical utility.  
- Related work: Related work section could more deeply compare to multiple imputation and broader missing-data frameworks beyond MNAR.  
- For acceptance: Significant theoretical contribution, novel results, clear writing, and useful non-recoverability tools.  
- Against acceptance: Theoretical complexity limits accessibility; insufficient empirical validation; dense notation.  
- Recommendation: Strong paper; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Suggested revisions: Add intuitive explanations (e.g., d*-separation), discuss computational complexity, include small-scale simulations, and expand related-work comparisons.
