This paper addresses the problem of identifiability for probabilistic and causal queries when data is subject to a specified missingness mechanism. Building on the "m-graph" framework from Mohan et al. [2013], the authors extend previous work by providing more general graphical conditions for recovering quantities like P(y|x), P(y,x), and the causal effect P(y|do(x)). Key contributions include a generalized sequential factorization theorem, new criteria for non-recoverability, and a demonstration that causal queries may be recoverable even when the corresponding probabilistic queries are not. The results are applied to the problem of attrition in longitudinal studies.

**Quality**

The technical quality of the work appears high. The paper tackles a difficult and important problem with formal rigor, and the theoretical results seem sound. The non-recoverability criteria presented in Theorem 3 are a particularly valuable contribution, as they are clear, interpretable, and provide researchers with explicit graphical tests to determine when their assumptions are insufficient for estimation. The finding in Section 6 that causal queries can be recovered even when a key variable and its missingness mechanism are inseparable is insightful and potentially surprising to many practitioners.

However, some results feel more like incremental extensions than significant conceptual leaps. For example, Section 3 on sequential factorization feels somewhat disconnected from the main narrative and its central result, Theorem 1, is highly complex and difficult to parse. While technically sound, it is not clear how this result contributes to a broader, more unified understanding of recoverability. The paper's quality improves significantly in the later sections, which offer clearer and more impactful results.

**Clarity**

Clarity is the paper's main weakness. The manuscript attempts to cover a substantial amount of ground, resulting in an extremely dense presentation that is difficult to follow. The large number of theorems, combined with a 13-page appendix, suggests that the material might be better suited for a longer journal article where the concepts could be introduced and developed more carefully.

The notational choices significantly hinder readability and place a heavy burden on the reader.
*   The letter 'V' is overloaded to represent a vertex, a set of all vertices, and various subsets (Vo, Vm, V*), making expressions ambiguous. The convention that Vi denotes a single variable while Vo denotes a set of variables is particularly confusing.
*   Subscripts and superscripts can become dense and nearly illegible, as exemplified by the set equation in Corollary 1.
*   Some notation is used without a clear definition (e.g., the graph surgery notation Gx and Gx in Definition 4 and Theorem 4).

These issues make the paper challenging to read and may prevent the key ideas from reaching a wider audience.

**Originality and Significance**

The paper addresses a problem of high significance for machine learning and statistics. The results in Sections 5 and 6 are original, interesting, and have the potential for significant impact. Providing formal graphical criteria for non-recoverability (Section 5) is a novel contribution to this area. The analysis of causal queries (Section 6) clarifies important subtleties in identifiability that are often overlooked.

While the paper presents a valuable collection of tools, it stops short of providing a complete or unifying theory for recoverability in this framework, analogous to the completeness of do-calculus for causal effect identification. It is difficult to imagine a practitioner searching through a series of papers like this to find a specific, complex result (like Theorem 1) that matches their problem. A more impactful long-term contribution would be to work towards a complete graphical algorithm for determining recoverability.

The methods in Theorem 2 and Corollary 1 appear to be a form of inverse probability weighting. This connection could be made more explicit. The conditioning sets seem to function as Markov blankets for the missingness indicators R(i) in the corresponding mixed graph. The authors may benefit from discussing this connection and citing relevant work on factorizations for mixed graphs (e.g., Richardson, 2003; 2009).

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Notation:** I strongly urge the authors to reconsider their notational choices for a future version. Using different symbols for vertices, sets of vertices, and specific subsets would dramatically improve clarity.
2.  **Clarity on Garcia [2013]:** The paper claims to rectify an opinion from Garcia [2013]. Could the authors clarify if the result in that paper is incorrect, or if it applies to a different framework or a more limited set of assumptions?
3.  **Undefined Notation:** The notation for graphs with severed edges (GX, GX) should be formally defined when it is first introduced.
4.  **Minor Issues:**
    *   On page 2, the variables Vo and Vm are used in a sentence before they are defined.
    *   On page 4, line 214: Rpart is a partition of R, so the text should describe Rx and Ry as belonging to "distinct sets in the partition" rather than "distinct partitions."
    *   The language is occasionally informal (e.g., "oftentimes").
    *   The LaTeX for a bidirected edge should be `\leftrightarrow` instead of `<-->`.

In summary, this paper contains important and useful results for a significant problem. The originality and technical quality of Sections 5 and 6 are high. However, the paper is significantly marred by poor presentation and confusing notation, which limits its accessibility and impact in its current form. A major revision focused on improving clarity and perhaps narrowing the scope would turn this into a much stronger paper.
