

The paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a method for adapting object classifiers to detectors using only image-level classification labels for most categories, with bounding box annotations required for only a small subset. The key insight is to learn a generic transformation from classification to detection using a small set of categories with bounding box annotations, then apply this transformation to adapt classifiers for categories without bounding box data. This addresses a significant bottleneck in scaling object detection to large numbers of categories, as obtaining bounding box annotations for tens of thousands of categories is prohibitively expensive.

The work makes a valuable contribution to the field by bridging the gap between classification and detection. It builds on the R-CNN framework [1] but addresses its limitation of requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The paper demonstrates significant improvements (50% relative mAP) over a classification-only baseline on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge, showing the approach's viability for scaling detection to tens of thousands of categories. The 7.6K detector demonstration is particularly compelling, showcasing the practical impact of the method.

The paper excels in quality through its thorough experimental evaluation. The ablation study (Table 1) clearly demonstrates the importance of each component, with the category invariant adaptation (learning background and fine-tuning convolutional layers) providing the most significant improvement. The error analysis (Figure 5) effectively shows how LSDA reduces background confusion and localization errors compared to the classification-only baseline. The method is well-motivated as a domain adaptation problem between classification and detection tasks, with appropriate citations to related work on domain adaptation [12-16] and multiple instance learning [6,7].

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to evaluation. The technical details of the LSDA algorithm are sufficiently explained, though some implementation specifics could be clarified (e.g., exact CNN architecture details beyond "Krizhevsky et al. [4] architecture" and training time breakdowns). The comparison with R-CNN [1] is well-placed, highlighting the reduction in required bounding box annotations.

The originality is strong, as the paper identifies and solves a significant problem not addressed by prior work. While domain adaptation has been used for visual domain shifts, adapting between classification and detection tasks represents a novel application of these techniques. The paper correctly distinguishes its approach from related work, noting that previous domain adaptation methods [17-22] require detection data for all categories, whereas LSDA works for held-out categories without detection data. The comparison with MIL-based methods [6,7] is appropriate, noting they haven't been evaluated on ImageNet.

The significance is high, as the method enables detection for tens of thousands of categories with only image-level labels, potentially revolutionizing large-scale object detection. The 7.6K detector demonstration provides concrete evidence of the approach's scalability. The paper's potential to reduce annotation costs while maintaining detection performance addresses a critical bottleneck in the field.

Key weaknesses include the limited discussion of why background adaptation is so crucial (Figure 3 shows it provides the largest improvement), and the category-specific adaptation component (Equation 1) appears to provide only marginal improvement (16.15% vs. 15.85% mAP when k=10 vs. k=|B|). The paper could better justify the choice of nearest neighbors for category adaptation, including an analysis of how different similarity metrics affect performance. The "fast detector" implementation details (Section 4.4) are sparse, mentioning only "fast region proposals" and "spatial pyramid pooling" without sufficient technical detail to understand the performance gains.

The paper could strengthen its contribution by comparing with more recent domain adaptation methods specifically designed for detection tasks. While the authors cite domain adaptation work, a more direct comparison with methods like those in [20-22] would better establish the novelty. Additionally, the paper could more clearly articulate the theoretical guarantees of the adaptation process, beyond the empirical results.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, addressing a significant problem in computer vision with a technically sound solution that advances the state of the art. The method is both practical (7.6K detector) and theoretically grounded (domain adaptation framework). The evaluation is comprehensive, and the results are convincing. The proposed approach has clear potential for adoption by the computer vision community to scale detection systems to large numbers of categories.

Overall, the paper makes a strong contribution to the field with a novel, well-supported approach to scaling object detection. With minor clarifications to the methodology details and a more thorough comparison with relevant domain adaptation techniques, the paper would be even stronger. The results are compelling and the method has significant potential for practical applications in large-scale detection systems.
