

This paper introduces LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a method for transforming image classifiers into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The authors address a significant challenge in scaling object detection: the scarcity of bounding box annotations for large numbers of categories, while classification data (image-level labels) is more abundant. The key insight is to treat the transformation from classification to detection as a domain adaptation problem, learning a generic transformation from a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations and applying it to classifiers for categories without such annotations.

The paper makes a compelling contribution to the field by demonstrating that a 7,604 category detector can be created using only classification data for most categories, with bounding box annotations required for only a small subset. The method involves three key components: recognizing background as an important step in adaptation, transferring category-invariant information between classifier and detector feature representations, and applying category-specific adaptation using nearest neighbors in feature space. The authors provide a thorough ablation study showing that their full LSDA model achieves a 50% relative mAP improvement over a classification-only baseline on held-out categories.

Strengths of the paper include:
1. Clear problem statement addressing a genuine bottleneck in scaling detection systems.
2. Well-designed experiments with appropriate ablation studies to isolate the contributions of each component.
3. Practical demonstration of the method's scalability with a 7,604 category detector.
4. Good technical execution with clear implementation details and publicly available code.
5. Strong theoretical grounding in domain adaptation while addressing the unique aspects of the classification-to-detection task.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear figures that effectively illustrate key concepts. The methodology is technically sound, with appropriate statistical validation of results (p=0.017 for the k=10 category adaptation improvement). The comparison to the classification-only baseline is particularly convincing, demonstrating the substantial value of their adaptation approach.

Some weaknesses and suggestions for improvement:
1. The paper claims to be the first to adapt to held-out categories with no detection data, but could better distinguish from MIL-based approaches that also use weak labels. A more detailed comparison with MIL methods (e.g., Ali et al. [6] and [7]) would strengthen the positioning of the work.
2. The category-specific adaptation using nearest neighbors assumes similarity in classification features implies similarity in detection features. The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of when this assumption holds and when it might fail, including examples of categories where this approach might not work well.
3. While the paper shows the method reduces training time (from 3 days to 5.5 hours), it doesn't provide a detailed comparison of inference time versus standard R-CNN or other detection methods. This is important for practical adoption.
4. The error analysis in Figure 5 is insightful but could be expanded to include a more detailed discussion of failure cases, particularly those related to the Oth (other) error type where the detector localizes incorrect objects.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a critical scalability problem in object detection. The approach has clear potential for practical applications, as demonstrated by the 7,604 category detector. The method builds appropriately on previous work (R-CNN, domain adaptation) while making a novel contribution to the specific problem of scaling detection to thousands of categories.

The significance of this work is substantial, as it addresses a major bottleneck in deploying object detection systems at scale. The ability to leverage abundant classification data for detection could significantly reduce the annotation burden for large-scale detection systems. While the paper doesn't quite reach the performance of full detection training (as shown in Table 1), the 50% relative mAP improvement is impressive given the minimal supervision required.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The technical contribution is sound, the experiments are well-designed, and the approach addresses an important problem in computer vision. The authors have clearly demonstrated the value of their method with convincing results on a standard benchmark. The work is well-positioned to influence future research in large-scale object detection.
