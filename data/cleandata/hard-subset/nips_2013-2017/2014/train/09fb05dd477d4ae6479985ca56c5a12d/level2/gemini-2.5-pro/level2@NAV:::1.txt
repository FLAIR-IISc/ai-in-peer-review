This paper addresses the critical challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, where collecting bounding box annotations is prohibitively expensive. The authors propose Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a novel method for converting pre-trained image classifiers into object detectors, even for categories that lack any bounding box data. The core idea is to frame this as a domain adaptation problem, learning a generalizable transformation on a subset of categories with full supervision and applying it to others. This work builds upon the fine-tuning approach of R-CNN but crucially generalizes it, removing the requirement for bounding box labels for every target category.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a highly significant problem. The proposed method of learning a transferable mapping from the classification task to the detection task is novel and has the potential to dramatically scale up object detection capabilities by leveraging abundant image-level classification data. The demonstration of a 7.6K category detector is a compelling proof of concept.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The claims are well-supported by strong empirical evidence on the ILSVRC2013 dataset. The ablation study is particularly effective, clearly demonstrating that the category-invariant feature adaptation (fine-tuning layers 1-7 on the detection task) provides the largest performance boost (+50% relative mAP). The error analysis insightfully shows that the method primarily succeeds by reducing localization and background-confusion errors, which are the key differentiators between classification and detection.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the method is explained logically through its component parts (category-invariant and category-specific adaptation), and the figures effectively illustrate the core concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  The category-specific adaptation, which averages the classifier-to-detector weight changes (δ) from nearest neighbors, feels somewhat heuristic. While it provides a statistically significant improvement, the choice of L2 distance on classifier weights as the similarity metric could be better motivated or compared against other possibilities. Perhaps a small network could learn to predict this transformation more explicitly.

2.  While the relative improvement is large, the absolute performance on held-out categories (16.15% mAP) still trails the fully supervised upper bound (22.56% mAP) considerably. This highlights that the learned transformation, while effective, is not a perfect substitute for direct supervision, a point the authors rightly acknowledge.

**Recommendation:**

The strengths of this paper—addressing a major bottleneck in computer vision with a novel, well-executed, and clearly presented approach—far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It represents a significant step towards truly large-scale object detection. I strongly recommend acceptance.
