This paper proposes LSDA, a method for adapting image classifiers into object detectors, particularly addressing the scarcity of bounding box annotations for many categories. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification feature space to the detection feature space using categories with both classification and detection data, and then apply this transformation to classifiers trained on categories with only classification data. The paper demonstrates improvements on the ImageNet detection challenge and showcases a scalable 7.6K detector.

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work:**

The problem addressed – scaling object detection to a large number of categories with limited labeled data – is a crucial one. The paper rightly points to the success of deep CNNs in classification (fueled by datasets like ImageNet) and the bottleneck of obtaining bounding box annotations for detection. The approach builds upon the work of Girshick et al. (R-CNN), which showed the power of transferring features from pre-trained classifiers, but extends it by learning a domain adaptation specifically for the classification-to-detection task, rather than simply fine-tuning with bounding box data.  The paper also correctly situates itself relative to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approaches, noting the complementarity but lacking direct comparison on the same scale. Connections to domain adaptation literature are also well made, highlighting the novelty of adapting *both* the domain *and* the task. The references are comprehensive and relevant, demonstrating awareness of the state of the art.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The LSDA approach presents a novel way to leverage abundant classification data for object detection, particularly for categories lacking bounding box annotations. This is a significant contribution to addressing the scaling problem in detection.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline using raw classifier weights is substantial and demonstrates the effectiveness of the learned adaptation.
* **Scalability:** The successful deployment of a 7.6K detector using ImageNet leaf nodes demonstrates the potential for scaling to a massive number of categories.
* **Clear Explanation and Implementation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the LSDA algorithm. The availability of code and models is a significant benefit for reproducibility and further research.
* **Good Error Analysis:**  The error analysis, demonstrating a shift from localization and background confusion errors to more subtle classification errors, provides useful insight into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the baseline comparison to using raw classifier weights is informative, the paper lacks a more thorough comparison to more recent and sophisticated detection frameworks (e.g., Faster R-CNN, YOLO) or more advanced domain adaptation techniques.  A comparison to MIL-based methods on a subset of the ImageNet dataset would also strengthen the evaluation.
* **Category Specific Adaptation (k value):** The choice of 'k' (number of nearest neighbors) for category-specific adaptation feels somewhat arbitrary. While the paper reports using k=10, a more extensive exploration of different 'k' values and a justification for the chosen value would be beneficial.
* **Performance on Challenging Categories:** The examples of errors in Section 4.3, showcasing misclassifications between similar categories (lemon vs. orange), suggest that the method may struggle with fine-grained distinctions, a common challenge in detection. Further discussion and potentially analysis of performance on such categories would be valuable.
* **Simplified Test Time:** The description of the test time implementation is brief. While the reduction in complexity by avoiding SVMs and bounding box regression is noted, the trade-off in performance should be explicitly quantified.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a significant problem in object detection – the scarcity of labeled data.
* The proposed LSDA method is novel and demonstrates a substantial improvement in performance.
* The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and accompanied by publicly available code and models.
* The scalability of the approach is demonstrated with a 7.6K detector.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The comparison to state-of-the-art detection frameworks and domain adaptation methods is limited.
* Some implementation details (e.g., choice of 'k' for category-specific adaptation) lack thorough justification.
*  Performance on fine-grained categories may be a limitation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution to the field of object detection. While the comparison to state-of-the-art methods could be more comprehensive, the significant improvement over the baseline, the scalability of the approach, and the clear explanation of the method make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I suggest adding a discussion of how LSDA compares to more recent detection frameworks and a more detailed justification for the choice of 'k' in the category-specific adaptation step.
