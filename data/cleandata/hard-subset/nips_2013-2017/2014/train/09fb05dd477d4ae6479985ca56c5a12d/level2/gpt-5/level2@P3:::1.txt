Summary
The paper addresses the scarcity of bounding-box annotations for large-scale object detection by proposing LSDA, a method that adapts image classifiers into object detectors. The approach treats classification-to-detection as a domain adaptation problem. A CNN is first fine-tuned on classification data for all K categories. Using detection data for only a subset B of categories, the network is further fine-tuned to learn (i) a background class and (ii) a category-invariant transformation of shared layers (1–7) from classification to detection. For categories without detection data (set A), the method transfers a category-specific output-layer shift by averaging the fine-tuning deltas of the k nearest neighbor categories in set B (neighbors are chosen by L2 distance between normalized fc8 classification weights). At test time, per-region scores are computed as category score minus background score.

On ILSVRC2013 detection (200 categories), the paper simulates a scenario with detection labels for 100 categories and only image-level labels for the other 100. On the held-out categories, LSDA improves mAP from 10.31% to 16.15% (+50% relative), with the majority of the gain due to feature-level adaptation and background modeling; the category-specific delta transfer provides a smaller but statistically significant improvement (p=0.017). Error analysis shows reduced localization and background confusions after adaptation. The paper also demonstrates a 7.6K-category detector by adapting classification-only categories, and provides an accelerated variant using SPP and fast proposals (~0.5 s/image) along with public code/models.

Relation to prior work
- R-CNN [1] showed the importance of fine-tuning classification nets for detection but required bounding boxes for all categories. LSDA differs by adapting to novel categories with no detection labels.
- MIL-based weakly supervised detection [6,7] trains detectors from image-level labels without bounding boxes, but has not been shown at ImageNet scale. LSDA is complementary, leveraging a small detection subset to learn a generic classification-to-detection transformation.
- Domain adaptation for detection (e.g., adaptive SVMs and related methods [17–22]) typically assumes detection labels exist in source and target categories. LSDA appears to be the first to adapt to held-out categories lacking any detection data.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated formulation of classification-to-detection as domain adaptation, decomposed into category-invariant and category-specific components, plus an explicit background class.
- Solid empirical evaluation with a realistic ILSVRC-2013 protocol; ablations disentangle contributions (background class, shared-layer fine-tuning, output-layer delta transfer).
- Statistical testing for the category-specific transfer adds credibility.
- Useful error analysis illustrating reduction in localization/background errors—consistent with the paper’s claims about task shift.
- Scalability demonstrated by the 7.6K detector and a practical faster variant; release of code/models increases impact.

Weaknesses and questions
- The category-specific transfer is heuristic: nearest neighbors are chosen via fc8 weight similarity in the classification network. Additional analysis of why this metric correlates with detection behavior would strengthen the claim. Alternatives (semantic/WordNet proximity, visual similarity on region features, or metric learning) are not explored.
- The large-scale 7.6K result is largely qualitative. Even partial quantitative evaluation (e.g., on the 200 overlapping detection categories, or a sampled subset with crowdsourced boxes) would better substantiate scalability.
- The baseline chosen for held-out categories is “classification-only network applied to proposals.” Comparisons to weakly supervised detection baselines (e.g., MIL on held-out categories) would contextualize the absolute mAP and show complementary benefits or trade-offs.
- The approach uses the softmax outputs without SVMs or bbox regression to maintain adaptability, incurring a “small” performance loss. A quantitative bound on this gap on the categories with detection labels would clarify the practical cost.
- Some implementation details that affect reproducibility and interpretation (fine-tuning schedules, hard-negative mining for background, proposal settings) are only sketched and could be expanded.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: Methodologically sound with appropriate ablations, significance testing, and error analysis. The transfer heuristic is simple and effective but under-theorized; nonetheless, empirical support is adequate for a conference contribution.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized; the three insights and training pipeline are clearly described. A small number of training details could be elaborated.
- Originality: Novel adaptation to categories with no detection labels using a learned classification-to-detection transformation and category-specific delta transfer. Distinct from R-CNN and prior adaptation/MIL work.
- Significance: Addresses an important bottleneck in large-scale detection; offers a practical path to thousands of detectors without exhaustive box annotation. Public release increases community value.

Pros for acceptance
- Demonstrates a principled and scalable solution to a central data bottleneck in detection.
- Strong empirical gains on held-out categories with careful ablations and error analysis.
- Practical impact via 7.6K detector and faster variant; code/models released.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited quantitative validation of the 7.6K-scale detector.
- No comparison to contemporary weakly supervised detection baselines on the same benchmark.
- Category-specific transfer mechanism could be better justified or compared to alternatives.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential (reasonable within rebuttal/camera-ready):
- Provide a comparison to at least one MIL-based weakly supervised baseline on the held-out categories (even if on a subset) to situate absolute mAP.
- Add a brief analysis of sensitivity to the neighbor metric (e.g., try WordNet similarity or cosine similarity on layer-7 features) and to k beyond what is reported, including a plot if possible.
- Quantify the performance delta between the softmax-only detector and SVM + bbox regression on the categories with detection labels to bound the trade-off.

Optional (nice-to-have):
- Include partial quantitative evaluation of the 7.6K detector on any overlapping annotated subset or via light additional annotation.
- Report per-class AP and a confusion analysis focusing on fine-grained confusions to illustrate when category-specific transfer helps most.
- Clarify training details (learning rates, epochs, background mining strategy, proposals used) in an appendix for full reproducibility.

Overall assessment
A timely and impactful contribution that enables large-scale detection by adapting classification models using limited detection data. Despite some heuristic components and missing comparisons, the method is well validated and clearly advances the state of practice. The contribution is appropriate for NeurIPS and merits acceptance after addressing the essential clarifications.
