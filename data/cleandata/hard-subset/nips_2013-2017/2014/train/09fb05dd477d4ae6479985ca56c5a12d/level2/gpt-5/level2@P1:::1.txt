Summary
The paper addresses the challenge of scaling object detection to thousands of categories when bounding box annotations are scarce, while image-level labels are plentiful. The proposed method, LSDA, learns a transformation from image classification to object detection using a deep CNN. The key idea is to exploit categories with both classification and detection labels to learn: (i) a category-invariant adaptation by fine-tuning shared layers for detection and learning an explicit background class, and (ii) a category-specific adaptation by estimating how per-class weights would have changed during detection fine-tuning and transferring this change to categories without detection data via nearest-neighbor averaging in the classification weight space. Experiments on ILSVRC2013 simulate 100 held-out categories with no detection labels and show a ~50% relative mAP gain over using classification weights directly on region proposals. The paper also demonstrates a 7.6K-class detector using only 200 categories with bounding boxes and classification data for the rest, plus a faster variant using SPP and fast proposals.

Relation to prior work
LSDA builds directly on CNN-based classification [Krizhevsky et al.] and detection (R-CNN), but differs from R-CNN in that it does not require detection labels for all categories. It contrasts with weakly supervised detection (MIL) and with domain adaptation methods that typically require labeled target data per category or do not modify deep features. It is complementary to OverFeat/SPPNet-style architectures and to fast proposal methods. To my knowledge, adapting detectors to unseen categories by learning a category-invariant detection transformation and transferring category-specific deltas is novel at the time of this work.

Quality
- Methodology: Sound and well-motivated. The decomposition into background modeling, category-invariant representation adaptation (layers 1–7), and a simple category-specific delta transfer is clear. The nearest-neighbor transfer in fc8 is simple, computationally light, and compatible with large K.
- Empirics: The ablation shows the dominant gain comes from feature adaptation plus learning a background class; category-specific transfer provides a smaller but statistically significant boost. The error analysis is informative and supports the claim that LSDA reduces localization and background confusion errors—key differences between classification and detection. A 7.6K-class demonstration underscores scalability.
- Missing comparisons: It would strengthen the evaluation to include: (1) a weakly supervised detector (MIL) trained on the held-out classes; (2) R-CNN-style SVMs and bounding box regression on LSDA features to quantify the lost accuracy from not using them; (3) alternative transfer schemes for δA (e.g., semantic similarity via WordNet or distributional embeddings, or sparse coding over δB rather than uniform k-NN averaging).
- Experimental protocol: The paper uses ILSVRC train for weak classification labels and val1 for detection fine-tuning on B, evaluated on val2. Please clarify hyperparameter selection (e.g., choice of k) and ensure no tuning on val2. The claim of 5.5 hours training versus ~3 days for R-CNN is compelling; please report hardware and per-stage times for reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The description of the δ layers (fcB, δB, fcA, background) could be streamlined with a concise algorithm box or schematic to depict how Wc → Wd is constructed for both B and A. Please specify:
- How k was selected and whether class weighting or similarity-based weighting was tried.
- Exact network/hyperparameters for fine-tuning (learning rates per layer group, schedule, data augmentation).
- Details of test-time scoring and calibration (softmax vs logit subtraction, per-class thresholds), and the cross-category NMS used in large-scale detection.

Originality
Moderate-to-high. While fine-tuning for detection is known, using a learned category-invariant transformation to turn arbitrary classifiers into detectors without any bounding box labels per held-out category is novel. The k-NN transfer of per-class deltas is simple but new in this context.

Significance
High practical impact. The approach enables detectors for thousands of categories with limited detection supervision, which is valuable for large-vocabulary applications. The relative mAP gain on held-out classes is meaningful, and the release of code/models is a strong community contribution. There remains a sizable gap to fully supervised detectors, suggesting room for future work (e.g., MIL to mine positives for A, class-agnostic box regression, better transfer metrics).

Pros
- Addresses an important scalability bottleneck; conceptually simple and scalable.
- Clear decomposition into background modeling, shared feature adaptation, and per-class transfer.
- Solid ablations and error analysis; statistically significant improvements on held-out classes.
- Large-scale 7.6K detector and faster variant show practicality.
- Code and models promised for release.

Cons
- Limited comparison to alternative transfer mechanisms and to weakly supervised detection baselines.
- The category-specific transfer is simplistic; gains are modest relative to the category-invariant part.
- Some experimental protocol details (k selection, hyperparameters, runtime breakdown) could be clearer.
- No analysis of negative transfer or per-class performance as a function of similarity to B.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Evaluate δA transfer using semantic similarity (WordNet distance, word embeddings) and/or visual similarity in a learned space; consider weighted averaging or sparse coding over δB.
- Add a baseline MIL method on held-out classes, and a variant that adds class-agnostic bounding box regression trained on B to all classes.
- Report sensitivity to k and to the similarity metric; provide per-class correlation between performance gain and similarity to nearest neighbors in B.
- Clarify whether any calibration is applied across 7.6K classes, and whether background subtraction suffices for long-tail categories.
- Consider joint multi-task training that explicitly regularizes Wd toward Wc + δ, potentially improving stability and transfer.
- Provide exact hardware and timings for the 5.5h training claim, and the throughput numbers (proposals per image, FPS) for both standard and fast versions.

Recommendation
Weak accept to accept. The problem is important, the approach is simple and effective, and the empirical gains on held-out categories are persuasive. With stronger baselines for δ transfer and weakly supervised detection, this could be a clear accept. As it stands, it is a valuable and timely contribution likely to influence large-scale detection research.
