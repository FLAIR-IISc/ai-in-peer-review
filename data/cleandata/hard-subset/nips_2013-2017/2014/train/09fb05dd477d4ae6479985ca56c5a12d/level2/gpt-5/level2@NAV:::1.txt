Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes LSDA, a method to adapt image classifiers into object detectors when bounding box annotations are available only for a subset of categories. The approach has two components: (1) category-invariant adaptation by fine-tuning shared CNN layers on detection data and learning an explicit background class, and (2) category-specific adaptation by transferring the average fine-tuning “delta” from nearest-neighbor categories (in fc8 space) that do have detection labels. Evaluated on ILSVRC13 with 100 trained and 100 held-out categories, LSDA yields a 50% relative mAP gain over using classifier weights on proposals, and demonstrates a 7.6K-category detector and a faster SPP-based variant. The work is well-situated with respect to R-CNN (which requires boxes for all classes), domain adaptation (feature/model adaptation), and weakly supervised/MIL methods.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound and supported by ablations isolating background modeling, shared-layer fine-tuning, and output-layer transfer, with a paired t-test for significance. The error analysis (Loc/BG/Oth) is informative and consistent with the hypothesized task shift from classification to detection. However, quantitative evaluation for the 7.6K model is qualitative only, and comparisons to strong weakly supervised baselines on this scale are absent (authors note MIL has not been evaluated on ImageNet detection).

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with implementation details and released code. Minor issues: clarify the definition of sets A/B (indices), report sensitivity to k in nearest-neighbor transfer more fully, and specify fine-tuning schedules/hyperparameters for reproducibility.

Originality: Adapting to unseen detection categories via shared-layer task transfer plus a learned background class is novel relative to prior domain adaptation and R-CNN, which assume detection data for all categories. The nearest-neighbor delta transfer is a simple, plausible heuristic.

Significance: The approach addresses a central bottleneck (box annotation scarcity) and is practically useful for scaling detectors. While absolute mAP on held-out classes remains modest, the relative gains and released resources make this a valuable contribution.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, effective formulation of classification-to-detection adaptation with strong ablations.
- Practical impact: large-scale detector and faster variant; code/models released.
- Solid error analysis demonstrating reduced localization/background confusions.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited quantitative validation for the 7.6K detector; no large-scale comparison to weakly supervised MIL.
- Category-specific transfer provides small gains; heuristic nearest-neighbor choice not deeply justified.
- Reliance on region proposals without bbox regression limits absolute performance.

Constructive suggestions:
- Provide quantitative evaluation of the 7.6K model on a measurable subset; include MIL-based training for held-out categories to compare.
- Explore learned meta-mapping from classifier to detector weights (e.g., regression over fc8 or semantic/WordNet features) instead of nearest neighbors.
- Reintroduce bbox regression or lightweight distillation to recover performance without losing adaptability.
- Evaluate generalization to other datasets or across domains.
