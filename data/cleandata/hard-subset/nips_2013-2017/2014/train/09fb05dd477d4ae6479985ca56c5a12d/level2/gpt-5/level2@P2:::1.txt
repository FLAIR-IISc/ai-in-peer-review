Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the scarcity of bounding-box annotations for large-scale object detection by learning to adapt image classifiers into object detectors. The proposed LSDA method: (1) fine-tunes a CNN (AlexNet-style) on detection data for a subset of categories (B) to learn a category-invariant transformation and an explicit background class; (2) transfers this transformation to held-out categories (A) that lack detection labels; and (3) performs a light category-specific adaptation for A by adding the average fine-tuning delta of the k nearest supervised categories (based on fc8 similarity). On ILSVRC13 detection, simulating 100 labeled and 100 unlabeled categories, LSDA improves mAP on held-out categories from 10.31% to 16.15% (+50% relative), with ablations showing the dominant gain comes from category-invariant adaptation and background modeling; the per-class delta averaging yields a small but statistically significant bump. They further scale to a 7.6K-category detector, report a faster SPP-based variant (~2 fps), and release code/models.

The work builds on R-CNN (classification-to-detection fine-tuning) but differs crucially by enabling adaptation to categories with no detection labels. It contrasts with weakly supervised MIL approaches and domain adaptation methods that typically require detection labels for all categories. To my knowledge (for 2014), the transfer to held-out categories at this scale is novel.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation with high practical relevance: turning plentiful classification labels into usable detectors.
- Simple, well-motivated decomposition: background modeling, category-invariant representation transfer, and lightweight category-specific transfer.
- Solid experimental protocol on ILSVRC13 with a realistic split; ablations isolate where the gains come from; significance testing is reported.
- Insightful error analysis showing reduced background and localization errors after adaptation.
- Scales to thousands of categories with an accompanying fast inference variant; code/models released, increasing impact.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited absolute performance: while relative gains are strong, mAP remains far from the fully supervised upper bound. The per-class delta adaptation yields only modest additional benefit over category-invariant fine-tuning.
- Evaluation gaps at scale: the 7.6K detector is only qualitatively evaluated. Even a small human-judged sample or proxy benchmark would substantiate the scaling claim.
- Baseline completeness and calibration: the decision to forgo SVMs and bounding-box regression simplifies training, but complicates fair comparison to R-CNN-like pipelines. A hybrid baseline (R-CNN extras for B, LSDA adaptation for A) would help quantify the unavoidable loss vs. flexibility.
- Similarity metric for category-specific transfer is ad hoc (fc8 Euclidean distance). Alternatives (taxonomy distance, attribute/word embeddings, or learned metrics) may outperform; the sensitivity to k and to distance choice is underexplored.
- Score computation details: subtracting background score (is it logits or softmax probabilities?) needs clarification; cross-class score calibration for multi-class NMS is not thoroughly addressed.
- Notation/clarity nits: the A/B set definitions could be cleaner (avoid inclusive index ranges that overlap at m); some training-set construction details (class balance, negatives) could be more explicit.

Assessment by review criteria
- Quality: Technically sound overall; claims are supported by controlled ablations, a principled held-out protocol, and significance testing. Some methodological choices (nearest-neighbor delta) are heuristic but empirically validated.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. The training pipeline and ablation factors are understandable. A few details (scoring, calibration, A/B definitions) need tightening.
- Originality: The central idea—learning a generic classification-to-detection adaptation and transferring it to unlabeled categories—is novel relative to prior detection/domain-adaptation work circa 2014.
- Significance: High practical significance. Even with moderate per-class accuracy, enabling thousands of detectors without box labels is impactful; the released resources amplify this.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative evaluation for the 7.6K model (e.g., human verification of top-N detections per class, or evaluation on a held-out subset with newly annotated boxes).
- Explore alternative similarity signals for category-specific transfer (WordNet distance, attributes, word vectors) and report sensitivity to k and the metric.
- Clarify whether background subtraction is applied to logits or probabilities; consider reporting calibration experiments (e.g., Platt scaling or temperature scaling) to improve cross-class comparability for NMS.
- Include a hybrid baseline that adds SVMs and bbox regression for categories in B while keeping LSDA for A, to quantify the cost-benefit of the simplified pipeline.
- Analyze negative transfer cases (e.g., when nearest neighbors are semantically close but visually dissimilar) and consider simple safeguards (e.g., similarity thresholding, weighted averaging).
- Minor: clarify the construction of the classification set (how negatives are handled when multiple objects are present); clean up notation and typos.

Questions for the authors
- Are score_i and score_background logits or softmax probabilities? Did you try log-odds or temperature scaling?
- How sensitive is performance on A to k and to the choice of distance metric? Do WordNet or attribute-based neighbors help?
- Can you report a small-scale quantitative evaluation of the 7.6K detector (e.g., precision@K via human rating)?
- Did you try adding bbox regression trained on B and applying it to A?

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper addresses an important problem with a clear, effective approach; demonstrates solid gains on a standard benchmark; and provides resources that will likely be used by the community. The main limitations are the modest absolute accuracy and the lack of quantitative validation at 7.6K scale, but these do not outweigh the novelty and practical utility.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and practically valuable adaptation to categories without detection labels
- Clear ablation-backed evidence; statistically significant gains
- Scales to thousands of categories; code/models released

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited absolute mAP; category-specific transfer adds small gains
- No quantitative results for the large-scale 7.6K detector
- Some heuristic choices and calibration details underexplored

Confidence: High (familiar with detection and domain adaptation literature of the period)
