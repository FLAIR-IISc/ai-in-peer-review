## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper proposes LSDA, an algorithm to leverage readily available image-level labels for object detection, addressing the bottleneck of requiring extensive bounding box annotations. The core idea is to learn a transformation from classifiers (trained on image-level data) to detectors, transferring knowledge to categories lacking bounding box data. This is framed as a domain adaptation problem, and the authors demonstrate its efficacy on the ImageNet detection challenge, achieving a significant performance boost on held-out categories and scaling to a 7.6K detector.

The paper is well-motivated, tackling a crucial problem in scaling object detection. The approach of adapting classifiers is a logical extension of recent work like R-CNN [1], and the formulation as a domain adaptation problem is sensible. The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating a substantial 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline using raw classifier weights. The ablation study clearly highlights the contribution of each component of the LSDA algorithm. The release of models and code is a significant positive aspect, promoting reproducibility and further research. 

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge the limitations, a deeper discussion of failure cases would be beneficial. The error analysis, while present, focuses primarily on *types* of errors rather than specific categories where the approach struggles. A more detailed analysis of *why* certain categories are difficult to adapt would be valuable. The comparison to related work, particularly Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) [6, 7], feels somewhat cursory. While the authors mention MIL, they don't delve into the specific advantages and disadvantages of their approach compared to these methods.  The reliance on the ImageNet hierarchy for scaling to 7.6K categories is a potential limitation – performance on less structured datasets isn’t addressed. Additionally, the discussion of the fast detector (2fps) is brief; more details on the architectural modifications would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant problem in scaling object detection.
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated approach.
*   Strong experimental results on a challenging dataset.
*   Publicly available code and models.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of failure cases and error analysis.
*   Superficial comparison to related work (MIL).
*   Potential limitations of relying on the ImageNet hierarchy.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a promising approach. While there are areas for improvement, the novelty, solid experimental results, and practical impact make it a worthwhile contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
