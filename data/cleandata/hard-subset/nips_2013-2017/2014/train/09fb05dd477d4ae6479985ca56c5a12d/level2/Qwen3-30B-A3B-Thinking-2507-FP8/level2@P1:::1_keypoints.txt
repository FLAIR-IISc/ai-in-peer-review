- Introduces LSDA, a method to convert image classifiers into object detectors without bounding box annotations for all categories.
- Frames the classification-to-detection transformation as a domain adaptation problem using a small set of categories with bounding boxes.
- Demonstrates scalability by building a 7,604-category detector using classification data for most categories.
- Key components: model background explicitly, transfer category-invariant features between classifier and detector, and apply category-specific adaptation via nearest neighbors in feature space.
- Ablation study shows full LSDA yields a 50% relative mAP improvement over a classification-only baseline on held-out categories.
- Experiments are well-designed with clear figures, public code, and solid implementation details; statistical validation included (p=0.017).
- Strengths: clear problem statement, strong experiments, practical scalability demonstration, good technical execution, and theoretical grounding in domain adaptation.
- Weakness: Claim of being first to adapt to held-out categories should better distinguish from MIL-based weakly supervised methods (e.g., Ali et al. [6],[7]).
- Weakness: Nearest-neighbor category-specific adaptation assumes classification-feature similarity implies detection-feature similarity; needs deeper analysis and failure cases.
- Weakness: Paper reports reduced training time but lacks detailed comparison of inference time versus R-CNN and other detectors.
- Weakness: Error analysis is useful but should be expanded, especially discussing failures tied to the "Other" localization error type.
- Significance: Addresses a major bottleneck, enabling large-scale detection with far fewer bounding box annotations and practical applicability.
- Limitation: Performance does not fully match detectors trained with full detection supervision, though improvements are notable given minimal supervision.
- Recommendation: Accept — technically sound, well-validated, and likely to influence future large-scale object detection research.
