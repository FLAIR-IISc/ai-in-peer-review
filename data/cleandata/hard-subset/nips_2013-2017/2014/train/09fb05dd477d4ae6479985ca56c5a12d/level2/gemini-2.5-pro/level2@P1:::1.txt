This paper addresses the critical and challenging problem of scaling object detection to a very large number of categories. The authors observe that while bounding box annotations for detection are scarce and expensive, image-level classification labels are abundant. They propose an algorithm, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), which leverages this asymmetry. The core idea is to treat the conversion of a classifier into a detector as a domain adaptation problem. The method learns a generic transformation from a small set of categories for which both classification and detection data are available, and then applies this learned transformation to turn classifiers for new categories (with only image-level labels) into detectors. This approach builds upon the R-CNN framework but crucially extends it to categories without any bounding box annotations.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is sound and well-motivated, breaking down the adaptation problem into two logical components: a category-invariant feature transformation and a category-specific classifier transformation. The claims are well-supported by a thorough experimental evaluation on the ILSVRC2013 detection dataset. The authors conduct a detailed ablation study that clearly demonstrates the contribution of each component of their algorithm, showing a significant 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories over a strong baseline. The error analysis is also insightful, demonstrating that the adaptation successfully reduces localization errors and confusion with background, which are key challenges that differentiate detection from classification. The authors are honest about the performance gap that remains compared to a fully supervised detector, appropriately framing their work as a significant step towards a solution rather than a complete one.

**Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly stated, and the proposed method is described in a logical and structured manner. Figure 2 provides an excellent, clear overview of the model and the adaptation process. The experimental setup is described with sufficient detail, and the results are presented clearly in tables and figures. The inclusion of qualitative results and the error analysis further enhances the reader's understanding of the method's behavior.

**Originality**

The work is highly original. While it leverages the successful R-CNN architecture, the core contribution is a novel training and adaptation paradigm. Framing the problem of creating detectors from classifiers as a domain adaptation task, specifically one that generalizes to held-out categories without target domain labels, is a new and insightful perspective. This work is clearly distinct from previous domain adaptation methods, which typically focus on adapting between visual domains for the same task, and from weakly-supervised MIL-based approaches. To my knowledge, this is the first method to explicitly learn and transfer the "task difference" between classification and detection to new categories.

**Significance**

The significance of this work is very high. It tackles a fundamental bottleneck that prevents the vision community from building object detectors at the same scale as object classifiers. By drastically reducing the need for expensive bounding box annotations, this method has the potential to enable the creation of detectors for tens of thousands of categories. This could have a major impact on numerous applications. The authors underscore this potential by building and releasing a 7604-category detector, a valuable contribution to the community in its own right. The ideas presented are likely to be built upon by many other researchers.

**Strengths:**
*   **Addresses a major, practical problem:** The paper tackles the crucial challenge of scaling object detection, a key bottleneck for the field.
*   **Novel and elegant formulation:** The idea of learning a transferable transformation from a classifier to a detector is both original and intuitive.
*   **Strong empirical validation:** The 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out classes is a compelling result, and the ablation study convincingly demonstrates the method's effectiveness.
*   **Insightful analysis:** The error analysis provides strong evidence that the method is learning the correct things (i.e., localization and background rejection).
*   **Demonstrated impact:** The creation and release of a 7.6K-category detector and associated code is a significant contribution that makes the work immediately useful to the community.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The method for category-specific adaptation, which relies on averaging the weight changes of nearest neighbors, is a reasonable heuristic but could be explored further. It would be interesting to see an analysis of the sensitivity to the number of neighbors (k) or a discussion of alternative approaches for transferring this category-specific knowledge.
*   The experimental split of the ILSVRC dataset (first 100 classes alphabetically vs. the next 100) could potentially introduce a bias. To further strengthen the claim of generality, it would be beneficial to report results averaged over several random splits of the 200 classes into the set with bounding boxes and the held-out set.
*   The paper appropriately positions itself with respect to MIL-based weakly supervised methods, but a more detailed discussion of the relative pros and cons would be helpful. For example, LSDA requires a seed set of fully annotated detection classes, whereas some MIL methods do not, which is an important distinction in the problem setup.

In summary, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel, significant, and well-executed contribution to the field of computer vision. It addresses a very important problem with an elegant and effective solution. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall strength of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
