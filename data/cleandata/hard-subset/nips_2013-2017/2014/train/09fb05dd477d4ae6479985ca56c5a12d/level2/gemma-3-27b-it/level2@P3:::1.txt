This paper proposes Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a method to leverage image-level labels for object detection, particularly in scenarios where bounding box annotations are scarce. The core idea is to learn a transformation from classifiers (trained on image-level data) to detectors (requiring bounding boxes), and then apply this transformation to classifiers for categories lacking bounding box annotations. The work builds on the R-CNN paradigm but crucially reduces the reliance on bounding box labels by transferring knowledge from categories where they *are* available. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of LSDA on the ImageNet detection challenge, achieving significant improvements over a baseline and scaling to a 7.6K detector.

The strengths of the paper are substantial. The problem addressed – scaling object detection to a large number of categories – is important and practical. The proposed solution is novel in its approach to domain adaptation, explicitly framing the classifier-to-detector conversion as a learning problem.  The experimental results on ImageNet are convincing, demonstrating a clear benefit of the LSDA approach, particularly on the held-out categories. The ablation study effectively dissects the contributions of the different components.  The release of models and code at lsda.berkeleyvision.org is commendable and facilitates reproducibility. The analysis of error types (localization, background confusion, other) provides valuable insight into the learned representations. The exploration of a faster detector variant (2fps) further enhances the practical relevance of the work. This work nicely complements recent efforts in weakly supervised learning and multiple instance learning for detection (e.g., [6, 7]).

However, several areas could benefit from further clarification and improvement. The motivation for choosing the specific network architecture (Krizhevsky et al. [4]) is not fully explained. While it's a standard choice, briefly discussing alternatives or the rationale for this selection would be helpful. The description of the nearest neighbor selection for category-specific adaptation (Equation 1) feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more thorough exploration of different similarity metrics and neighbor selection strategies, along with their impact on performance, would strengthen this aspect of the work.  The discussion of “statistical significance” in section 4.2 mentions a paired sample t-test, but doesn't provide the test statistic or p-value. Reporting these values would enhance the rigor of the analysis. The authors state they achieved "50% relative mAP boost" but do not specify the baseline mAP value, making it harder to assess the absolute magnitude of the improvement. The statement "To achieve R-CNN performance requires additionally learning SVMs..." is somewhat misleading, as the performance comparison is not directly between the full R-CNN pipeline and LSDA. A more precise comparison would be beneficial.  While the authors acknowledge a performance gap to “oracle” performance, quantifying this gap could provide a more concrete understanding of the potential for future improvement. The error analysis in Figure 5 could be made more informative by providing numerical data (e.g., percentages) along with the qualitative examples.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in scaling object detection.
*   Proposes a novel and well-motivated approach to domain adaptation.
*   Demonstrates significant empirical improvements on a standard benchmark.
*   Provides a clear and insightful ablation study.
*   Releases code and models for reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Some aspects of the method (e.g., nearest neighbor selection) lack detailed justification and exploration.
*   Statistical analysis could be more rigorous and complete.
*   Performance comparison to baselines and related work could be more precise.




Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and can be addressed through revisions. The potential impact of this work on scaling object detection is considerable.  A revision addressing the points raised above is recommended.
