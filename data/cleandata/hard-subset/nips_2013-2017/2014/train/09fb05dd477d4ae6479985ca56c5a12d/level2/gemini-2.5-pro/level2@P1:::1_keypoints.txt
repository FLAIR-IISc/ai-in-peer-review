- Tackles scaling object detection to many categories by leveraging abundant image-level labels and scarce bounding boxes.  
- Proposes LSDA: convert classifiers into detectors via domain adaptation learned from categories with both label types.  
- Learns a category-invariant feature transformation and a category-specific classifier transformation, applied to held-out classes.  
- Builds on the R-CNN framework but uniquely extends detection to categories without bounding-box annotations.  
- High technical quality: method is sound, well-motivated, and logically decomposed into two adaptation components.  
- Strong experimental validation on ILSVRC2013 with a detailed ablation study showing each component’s contribution.  
- Reports a significant 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories over a strong baseline.  
- Error analysis shows adaptation reduces localization errors and background confusion—key detection challenges.  
- Authors acknowledge remaining gap to fully supervised detectors and frame the work as an important step forward.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-written; Figure 2 gives a clear overview and experiments are described in detail.  
- Includes qualitative results and insightful error analysis that improve understanding of method behavior.  
- Highly original: novel framing of classifier→detector conversion as transferable domain adaptation across categories.  
- Distinct from prior domain adaptation and MIL approaches; first to explicitly transfer the “task difference” between classification and detection.  
- High significance: reduces need for bounding boxes, enabling detectors for thousands of categories and broad practical impact.  
- Demonstrated impact by building and releasing a 7,604-category detector and associated code.  
- Strengths summarized: addresses a major problem, presents an elegant transferable formulation, strong empirical results, insightful analysis, and tangible community resource.  
- Weakness: category-specific adaptation uses a k-NN averaging heuristic—needs sensitivity analysis and exploration of alternatives.  
- Weakness: dataset split (first 100 vs next 100 classes alphabetically) may introduce bias; recommend averaging over random splits.  
- Weakness: more detailed comparison/discussion with MIL-based weakly supervised methods would clarify trade-offs (e.g., need for seed fully annotated classes).  
- Overall conclusion: excellent, novel, significant work with minor weaknesses; strong recommendation for acceptance.
