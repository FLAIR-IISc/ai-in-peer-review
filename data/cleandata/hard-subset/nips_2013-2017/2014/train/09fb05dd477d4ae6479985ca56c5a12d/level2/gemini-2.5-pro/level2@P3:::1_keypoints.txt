- Tackles scaling object detection to many categories by converting image classifiers into object detectors using a learned transformation (LSDA).  
- Frames the task as domain adaptation from image classification to object detection and builds on deep CNNs and the R-CNN framework.  
- Learns a category-invariant feature transformation by fine-tuning shared network layers and a category-specific transformation for the final classification layer.  
- Trains the transformation on categories with bounding-box data and applies it to classifiers for new categories with only image-level labels.  
- Achieves a 50% relative improvement in mAP on held-out categories versus the baseline classification network.  
- Demonstrates a large-scale 7,604-category object detector, showing practical scalability.  
- Technically sound with thorough experiments and a detailed ablation study that isolates component contributions.  
- Ablation and error analysis show most gains come from category-invariant fine-tuning, which improves object/background separation and localization.  
- Omits R-CNN’s SVM and bounding-box regression steps to preserve adaptability, accepting a small performance trade-off.  
- Paper is very well written, clearly organized, and supported by informative figures and tables; experimental setup is well described.  
- Minor clarity improvement suggested: add a visual/diagram for the category-specific nearest-neighbor weight-averaging process.  
- Contribution is original: proposing a transferable “classifier-to-detector” mapping and framing task-level adaptation as domain adaptation.  
- Offers an alternative to weakly supervised methods by leveraging limited strong supervision to generalize detection.  
- Significant impact: reduces annotation bottleneck, enables detectors for tens of thousands of categories, and releases code/models.  
- Weaknesses: the category-specific adaptation (nearest-neighbor weight averaging) is heuristic and could be replaced by a more principled learning-based approach.  
- Evaluation limitation: does not fully assess generalization to held-out categories that are semantically distant from training adaptation categories.
