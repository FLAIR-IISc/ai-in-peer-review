- Proposes LSDA: adapt image classifiers into object detectors by treating classification-to-detection as domain adaptation with category-invariant and category-specific components.
- Method: fine-tune a CNN on classification for K categories; use detection data for subset B to learn a background class and transform shared layers; transfer category-specific output-layer shifts to held-out set A by averaging deltas from nearest neighbors in fc8 weight space; score regions as class minus background.
- Main result: On ILSVRC2013 with 100 held-out categories, LSDA raises mAP on held-out categories from 10.31% to 16.15%, with most gain from feature adaptation and background modeling and a smaller but significant category-specific improvement (p=0.017).
- Error analysis: adaptation reduces localization and background confusions, supporting the task-shift claims.
- Scalability: demonstrates a 7.6K-category detector and a faster ~0.5s/image variant; code and models are released.
- Relation to R-CNN: differs by enabling adaptation to novel categories without bounding-box training data, whereas R-CNN requires boxes for all categories.
- Relation to MIL/weakly supervised detection: complementary approach that leverages a small detection subset rather than training everything with MIL; MIL baselines at ImageNet scale are not compared.
- Relation to domain adaptation: appears novel in adapting to held-out categories lacking any detection labels, unlike prior adaptive-SVM-style work.
- Strength: clear, well-motivated formulation decomposing category-invariant and category-specific adaptation plus explicit background modeling.
- Strength: solid empirical evaluation with realistic protocol and ablation studies disentangling components.
- Strength: statistical significance testing for category-specific transfer increases credibility.
- Strength: useful error analysis that confirms reduced localization/background errors.
- Strength: practical impact via large-scale demonstration, faster variant, and public release of code/models.
- Weakness: category-specific transfer is heuristic (nearest neighbors via fc8 weight similarity) with limited justification and no exploration of alternatives (WordNet, visual similarity, metric learning).
- Weakness: 7.6K-scale results are mostly qualitative; lacking partial quantitative validation on overlapping categories or sampled annotations.
- Weakness: baseline for held-out categories is classification-on-proposals only; no comparison to weakly supervised (MIL) baselines to contextualize mAP.
- Weakness: choosing softmax-only outputs (no SVMs/bbox regression) for adaptability may incur a performance loss that is not quantitatively bounded.
- Weakness: some implementation/training details (fine-tuning schedules, hard-negative mining, proposal settings) are only sketched, affecting reproducibility and interpretation.
- Quality eval: methodologically sound with appropriate ablations and analysis, though the transfer heuristic is under-theorized.
- Clarity eval: generally clear and well organized; additional training details would help reproducibility.
- Originality eval: novel approach for adapting to categories without detection labels, distinct from R-CNN and prior adaptation/MIL work.
- Significance eval: addresses an important bottleneck for large-scale detection and offers a practical path to thousands of detectors.
- Pros for acceptance: principled, scalable solution with strong empirical gains, careful ablations/error analysis, and practical deliverables (7.6K detector, faster variant, released code).
- Cons against acceptance: limited quantitative validation of the 7.6K detector, lack of comparisons to weakly supervised baselines, and a category-transfer mechanism that needs stronger justification.
- Essential suggested fixes: compare to at least one MIL-based weakly supervised baseline (even on a subset), analyze sensitivity to neighbor metric and k, and quantify the performance gap between softmax-only and SVM + bbox regression on categories with detection labels.
- Optional suggested fixes: add partial quantitative evaluation of the 7.6K detector, report per-class AP and finer confusion analysis to show when transfer helps, and provide detailed training/hyperparameter settings in an appendix.
- Overall assessment: timely, impactful contribution that advances large-scale detection with limited box annotations and merits acceptance after addressing essential clarifications.
