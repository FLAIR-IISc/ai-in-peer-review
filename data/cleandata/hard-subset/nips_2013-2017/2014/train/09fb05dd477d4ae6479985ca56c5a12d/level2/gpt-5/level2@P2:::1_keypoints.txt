- Summary: Proposes LSDA to adapt image classifiers into detectors by category-invariant fine-tuning on labeled categories, transferring the transform to unlabeled categories, and applying a k-NN average delta for category-specific adaptation.  
- Novelty: Adapting classifiers to detection for held-out categories at this scale appears novel relative to prior work (circa 2014).  
- Main empirical result: On ILSVRC13 simulating 100 labeled and 100 unlabeled categories, LSDA raises mAP on held-out classes from 10.31% to 16.15% (+50% relative).  
- Dominant gains: Category-invariant representation adaptation and explicit background modeling provide the largest improvements.  
- Category-specific transfer: Averaging the fine-tuning delta from k nearest supervised categories yields a small but statistically significant extra boost.  
- Scaling: Authors claim a 7.6K-category detector and provide a faster SPP-based variant (~2 fps) with code/models released.  
- Strength — problem importance: Addresses a practical and impactful problem of converting abundant classification labels into detectors.  
- Strength — simplicity and decomposition: Clear, well-motivated split into background modeling, category-invariant transfer, and light per-class adaptation.  
- Strength — experiments: Realistic held-out protocol, ablation studies, and significance testing strengthen claims; error analysis is insightful.  
- Strength — impact: Scales to thousands of categories and releasing resources increases community value.  
- Weakness — absolute performance: Despite strong relative gains, absolute mAP is far below fully supervised upper bound.  
- Weakness — small per-class gain from delta: The category-specific averaging step provides only modest additional benefit.  
- Weakness — large-scale evaluation: 7.6K detector is only qualitatively evaluated; quantitative human or proxy evaluation is missing.  
- Weakness — baseline/comparison issues: Forgoing SVMs and bbox regression simplifies training but complicates fair comparison to R-CNN baselines; a hybrid baseline is needed.  
- Weakness — similarity metric: Using fc8 Euclidean distance for NN transfer is ad hoc; sensitivity to k and alternative metrics (WordNet, embeddings) is underexplored.  
- Weakness — score calibration: Details on subtracting background score (logits vs probabilities) and cross-class calibration for NMS are unclear.  
- Weakness — clarity nitpicks: A/B set notation, training-set construction, class balance, and negatives need clearer description.  
- Suggested improvement: Provide quantitative evaluation for the 7.6K model (e.g., human verification or held-out annotated subset).  
- Suggested improvement: Explore and report alternative similarity signals (WordNet, word vectors, attributes) and sensitivity to k.  
- Suggested improvement: Clarify whether background subtraction uses logits or softmax and evaluate calibration approaches (Platt/temperature scaling).  
- Suggested improvement: Add a hybrid baseline using SVMs and bbox regression for B while applying LSDA to A to quantify trade-offs.  
- Suggested improvement: Analyze negative transfer and consider safeguards like similarity thresholds or weighted averaging.  
- Suggested improvement: Clarify classification set construction, negatives handling, and clean up notation/typos.  
- Questions for authors: Are score_i and score_background logits or probabilities, and was log-odds or temperature scaling tried?  
- Questions for authors: How sensitive is performance on A to k and to the distance metric; do WordNet/attribute neighbors help?  
- Questions for authors: Can you provide a small quantitative evaluation of the 7.6K detector (precision@K via human rating)?  
- Questions for authors: Did you try training bbox regression on B and applying it to A?  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — novel, practically valuable, and experimentally supported despite moderate absolute accuracy and incomplete large-scale validation.  
- Confidence: High — reviewer is familiar with detection and domain-adaptation literature of the period.
