

This paper introduces LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a novel approach to scale object detection to thousands of categories by leveraging available classification data. The main claim is that the authors can transform image classifiers into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories, enabling detection for tens of thousands of categories that lack bounding box annotations but have classification data.

The paper makes a strong technical contribution by framing the classifier-to-detector transformation as a domain adaptation problem. The authors provide a well-structured methodology with three key insights: recognizing background is crucial, category-invariant information can be transferred, and category-specific differences exist between classifiers and detectors. Their experimental evaluation on ImageNet LSVRC-2013 demonstrates a 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline classifier-only approach on held-out categories (those without bounding box annotations), which is statistically significant (p=0.017). The 7.6K detector implementation further showcases the scalability of their approach.

The paper excels in technical quality and clarity. The methodology is well-explained, the experiments are comprehensive, and the implementation details are sufficient for reproducibility. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, noting their approach still requires bounding box data for a small subset of categories and falls short of oracle performance (using full bounding box annotations for all categories).

The contribution demonstrates significant originality in addressing a major practical challenge in computer vision. While domain adaptation techniques have been explored before, the authors' specific approach to adapting classifiers to detectors for held-out categories represents a meaningful advancement over R-CNN and related methods that require bounding box data for all categories.

The significance of this work is substantial, as it addresses a critical bottleneck in scaling object detection systems. The potential to leverage the vast amount of existing classification data for detection tasks could have broad practical implications for real-world computer vision applications.

The paper's main weakness is limited discussion of failure cases beyond the error analysis in Section 4.3. A more thorough examination of when LSDA fails would strengthen the paper. Additionally, the comparison to MIL-based approaches (mentioned in Related Work) could be expanded to better contextualize the contribution.

Overall, this is a high-quality, technically sound paper with clear significance and novel contribution. The methodology is well-supported by rigorous experiments, and the results demonstrate a meaningful advancement in the field. I recommend acceptance.
