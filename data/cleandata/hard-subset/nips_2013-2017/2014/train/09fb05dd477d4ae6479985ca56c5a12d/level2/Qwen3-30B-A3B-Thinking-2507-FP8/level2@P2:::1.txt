

This paper proposes LSDA, a method to adapt image classifiers into object detectors using only a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations. The core insight is treating the classifier-to-detector transformation as a domain adaptation problem, learning a generic feature transformation from a few categories and applying it to thousands of categories lacking bounding box data. The approach demonstrates significant improvements (50% relative mAP) on ImageNet held-out categories and produces a 7.6K detector.

**Strengths:** The problem is well-motivated, addressing a critical bottleneck in scaling detection to large category sets. The method is technically sound, with clear ablation studies showing the importance of category-invariant adaptation (feature transformation) over category-specific adaptation. The 7.6K detector result is impressive and demonstrates practical scalability. The implementation details and code release are commendable.

**Major Weaknesses:**  
1. **Inadequate comparison to MIL approaches:** The related work cites MIL methods (e.g., [6,7]) as "complimentary" but fails to demonstrate *how* LSDA outperforms them in the same weakly-supervised setting. The authors claim "MIL-based methods have not yet been evaluated on ImageNet," but this is misleading—their own experiment simulates weak supervision (using ImageNet classification data for all categories), making direct comparison possible. Without such a comparison, the claimed novelty ("first method to adapt to held-out categories") is unsubstantiated. MIL methods could potentially achieve similar results without the category-specific adaptation step.  

2. **Unjustified category-specific adaptation:** The nearest-neighbor approach for adapting categories without detection data (Eq. 1) lacks theoretical grounding. Why k=10? Why not k=5 or k=|B|? The paper reports k=10 as optimal but provides no analysis of sensitivity or error propagation. If the "nearest" categories are not semantically similar (e.g., due to poor feature representations), this could degrade performance. The authors should validate this with ablation studies on k or propose a more principled similarity metric.  

3. **Limited significance of results:** The 50% relative mAP improvement is on *held-out* categories only (the 100 categories without bounding box data). The absolute mAP on these categories is low (16.15% in Table 1), and the paper omits the baseline absolute mAP for fair comparison. The "7.6K detector" claim is impressive but lacks concrete examples of detections for novel categories (Figure 6 shows only a few examples, none from the 7.4K unannotated categories). Without demonstrating meaningful detection quality on these categories, the scalability claim is overstated.  

**Minor Issues:**  
- The "fast detector" (2fps) claim lacks comparison to state-of-the-art fast detectors (e.g., Fast R-CNN, SSD), making it hard to assess efficiency gains.  
- The background adaptation (Section 3.1) is well-motivated but could better clarify why the background class is critical for detection (vs. classification).  
- The error analysis (Section 4.3) shows reduced background errors but doesn't quantify *how much* detection quality improves in practice (e.g., precision-recall curves for held-out categories).  

**Recommendations for Improvement:**  
1. Compare LSDA directly to MIL-based methods on the same weakly-supervised setup (e.g., using only image-level labels for all categories). This is critical to establish novelty.  
2. Justify the choice of k in Eq. 1 with sensitivity analysis or a principled similarity metric (e.g., semantic distance from WordNet).  
3. Provide absolute mAP values for all baselines and include more detection examples for the 7.4K held-out categories.  
4. Discuss computational cost of adaptation relative to training a detector from scratch.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
LSDA addresses a meaningful problem with a technically plausible approach. However, the lack of comparison to MIL methods and the unvalidated category-specific adaptation undermine the claimed novelty and significance. The results on held-out categories are promising but insufficient to justify the scalability claim without higher-quality detections on the majority of categories. The paper would benefit from stronger empirical comparisons and more rigorous justification of its adaptation strategy. While the core idea has merit, the current evidence does not fully substantiate the impact claimed.  

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept. The work is promising but requires stronger evidence to support the central claims. With the suggested revisions (especially the MIL comparison and k-justification), it would be a strong contribution. As is, the weaknesses are critical to the main contribution.
