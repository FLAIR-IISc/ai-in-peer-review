This paper addresses the significant challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, where collecting bounding box annotations is prohibitively expensive. The central idea is to learn a generic transformation that converts an image classifier into an object detector. This transformation is learned on a subset of categories for which bounding box data is available and is then applied to classifiers for new categories that only have image-level labels. The problem is framed as a domain adaptation task, where the goal is to adapt from the source domain of image classification to the target domain of object detection. The proposed method, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), leverages deep convolutional neural networks and builds upon the R-CNN framework. It learns both a category-invariant feature transformation by fine-tuning shared network layers and a category-specific transformation for the final classification layer. Experiments on the ILSVRC2013 detection dataset show that LSDA achieves a 50% relative improvement in mean Average Precision (mAP) on held-out categories compared to a baseline using the original classification network. The work culminates in the creation of a 7,604-category object detector, demonstrating its potential for large-scale application.

**Quality**
The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and well-executed piece of research. The claims are substantiated by a thorough set of experiments, including a detailed ablation study that methodically evaluates the contribution of each component of the proposed system. This analysis clearly shows that the primary performance gain comes from the category-invariant adaptation of feature representations (fine-tuning the lower layers of the CNN), which effectively teaches the network to distinguish objects from background and improve localization. The error analysis further reinforces these findings by demonstrating a significant reduction in localization and background confusion errors after adaptation. The methodology is a logical extension of prior work like R-CNN, but tailored to a novel and important problem setting. The authors are candid about certain design choices, such as omitting the SVM and bounding box regression steps from the R-CNN pipeline to maintain the adaptability of the system, even at a small performance cost.

**Clarity**
The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to understand. The motivation for the problem is compellingly laid out in the introduction. The proposed LSDA algorithm is described systematically, broken down into its category-invariant and category-specific components. The figures and tables are informative and effectively support the main arguments. In particular, Figure 3 provides an excellent summary of the ablation study, and the error analysis in Figure 5 offers valuable insight into the method's behavior. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail to understand the evaluation protocol. A minor suggestion for improvement could be to provide a more visual illustration or diagram for the category-specific weight adaptation described in Section 3.2, which could make the nearest-neighbor averaging process even more intuitive.

**Originality**
The core contribution of this work is highly original. While it uses established tools like CNNs and the R-CNN detection pipeline, the central idea of learning a transferable "classifier-to-detector" mapping is novel. The formulation of this problem as a form of domain adaptation—where the adaptation is not just between visual domains but also between tasks (classification vs. detection)—is a key conceptual insight. This work is clearly differentiated from previous research. It extends fine-tuning approaches like R-CNN by removing the requirement for bounding box labels for all target categories. It also provides a distinct, and potentially complementary, alternative to weakly supervised methods like Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) by leveraging a small amount of strong supervision to learn a generalizable transformation. The paper does an excellent job of positioning itself within the context of related work in domain adaptation and weakly supervised learning.

**Significance**
The significance of this work is substantial. It directly tackles one of the most critical bottlenecks in building large-scale visual recognition systems: the cost of detailed annotation. By providing a principled way to leverage abundant, weakly-labeled classification data to create detectors, this approach could dramatically accelerate progress towards object detection for tens of thousands of categories. The successful demonstration of a 7.6K-category detector is not just a proof of concept but a valuable resource in itself. The ideas presented are likely to be influential and inspire further research into transfer learning across different vision tasks. The public release of models and code further enhances the potential impact of this contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a highly important and practical problem in computer vision.
*   Proposes a novel and elegant solution by framing the problem as learning a transferable transformation.
*   Provides strong empirical validation through a thorough ablation study and insightful error analysis.
*   Demonstrates impressive large-scale results with a 7.6K-category detector.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-structured.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The category-specific adaptation method (averaging weight changes from nearest neighbors) is a heuristic. While shown to be effective, a more principled learning-based approach could be explored.
*   The evaluation does not test the generality of the learned transformation on held-out categories that are semantically distant from the categories used for training the adaptation.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, significant, and well-executed contribution to the field.
