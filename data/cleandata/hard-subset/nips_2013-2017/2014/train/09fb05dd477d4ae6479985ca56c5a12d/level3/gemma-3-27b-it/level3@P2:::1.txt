This paper proposes Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), an algorithm for leveraging image-level classification data to train object detectors in scenarios where bounding box annotations are scarce. The core idea is to learn a transformation from classifiers to detectors, applying this learned transformation to categories lacking bounding box data. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of LSDA on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge, achieving a significant improvement over a baseline that simply uses classifier weights for detection.

The paper addresses a critical problem in scaling object detection – the costly and time-consuming process of acquiring bounding box annotations. The recent success of deep convolutional neural networks in classification, fueled by datasets like ImageNet with millions of labeled images, contrasts sharply with the limited availability of bounding box data for detection. LSDA cleverly bridges this gap by leveraging readily available classification data. The approach builds on existing ideas of adapting classifiers to detectors, most notably inspired by R-CNN [1], but importantly extends them to the setting of *held-out* categories – those without any bounding box annotations.

**Strengths:**

* **Technically Sound:** The method is logically well-motivated and the experimental setup appears sound. The ablation study in Figure 3 provides convincing evidence for the contributions of each component of the LSDA algorithm. The authors clearly outline the training procedure and the rationale behind their choices.
* **Clarity:** The paper is very well written and organized. The motivation is clearly stated, and the proposed method is described in a comprehensive and understandable manner. The figures and tables are helpful in illustrating the key concepts and results.
* **Originality:** While the core concepts of adapting classifiers for detection and domain adaptation are not novel, the combination of these ideas to address the specific problem of large-scale detection with limited bounding box annotations is a significant contribution. Applying adaptation specifically to held-out categories is a novel extension of existing work.
* **Significance:** The potential impact of this work is considerable. Enabling detection for a vast number of categories with only image-level labels could significantly expand the applicability of object detection systems. The results presented are encouraging, demonstrating a substantial improvement over a simple baseline. The release of the models and software is a valuable contribution to the community.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Contribution:** While the work is sound and valuable, the contribution is perhaps incremental rather than ground-breaking. The paper combines existing techniques in a clever way, but doesn’t introduce fundamentally new algorithms or theoretical insights.
* **Trade-off Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the trade-off between the reduction in annotation cost (using image-level labels instead of bounding boxes) and the resulting decrease in detection accuracy. While the improvement over the baseline is demonstrated, a more comprehensive analysis of this trade-off would be valuable.
* **Long-Term Relevance:** The authors acknowledge the possibility that the problem of bounding box scarcity may be mitigated by increased resource investment in annotation. While this is a valid point, a more nuanced discussion of the long-term relevance of the problem would be helpful.
* **Presentation of Results:** The claims of "78%" and "50% relative mAP" improvements (in the abstract and elsewhere) are potentially misleading. The key result is the improvement relative to the baseline on the held-out categories, which is more accurately represented by the DNN performance in Figure 3b. A clearer presentation of this core result is necessary.
* **Comparison to R-CNN:** A more detailed and dedicated section specifically outlining the differences between LSDA and R-CNN would be beneficial. While alluded to throughout the paper, a focused comparison would strengthen the contribution.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and practical problem in scaling object detection.
* The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.
* The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate a significant improvement over a reasonable baseline.
* The release of models and code promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The contribution is somewhat incremental, combining existing techniques rather than introducing fundamentally new ones.
* The trade-off between annotation cost and accuracy is not fully explored.
* The long-term relevance of the problem is open to debate.



**Specific Comments:**

* In Figure 3a, clarify which methods utilize category labels for detection to ensure a fair comparison.
* Focus the discussion on the DNN performance in Figure 3b, as it represents the core result. Revise the claims of “78%” and “50%” improvements accordingly.
* Add a dedicated section (or table) clearly highlighting the key differences between LSDA and R-CNN. Consider removing Figure 6 to accommodate this.
* Correct the typo on line 431 (“classi?ers”).
* Address potential inconsistencies in method naming (e.g., “DNN” vs. “DDA”).




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses a relevant problem with a well-designed and effectively implemented solution. The clarity of the writing and the comprehensive experimental evaluation are particularly commendable. While the contribution is perhaps not revolutionary, it is significant and has the potential to impact the field. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to clarify the presentation of results and strengthen the comparison to existing methods.
