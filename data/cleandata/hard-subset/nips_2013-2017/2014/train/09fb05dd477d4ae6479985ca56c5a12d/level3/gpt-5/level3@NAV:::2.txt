Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes LSDA, a framework that adapts CNNs trained for image classification into object detectors when bounding boxes are available for only a subset of categories. The approach combines (i) category-invariant adaptation via fine-tuning layers for detection and learning a background class, and (ii) category-specific adaptation by adding an “offset” to the classification fc8 weights, estimated by averaging the fine-tuning deltas of k nearest labeled neighbors. This builds on R-CNN’s insight that detection fine-tuning matters, while aiming to transfer from classification to detection without per-class boxes, and relates to domain adaptation (e.g., model/feature adaptation) and weakly supervised detection/MIL. On ILSVRC-2013, the method reports competitive performance (4th in detection), and demonstrates a 7.6K-category detector with a fast variant.

Quality: The methodology is sound at a high level and the empirical gains over a classification-only baseline are clear. However, the core adaptation rule Wj^d = Wj^c + average(δ) lacks a principled derivation. The “offset” δ is introduced operationally, but its physical/statistical meaning is not explained (e.g., first-order parameter update approximation to fine-tuning, relation to gradients/Hessian). The superiority of the kNN variant over other averaging choices is not analyzed; why fc8 Euclidean proximity should predict transferability is unclear. Some comparisons are not apples-to-apples: parts of val are used for training and val2 for testing, while competing methods often report on the official test set; the claim of parity with R-CNN on val2 is not statistically justified given val2’s small size.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The CNN architecture and training protocol are straightforward to implement. Section 3.2 would benefit from an explicit motivation/derivation for the offset, its interpretation, and a discussion of calibration effects introduced by the background softmax.

Originality: The idea of learning a generic classifier-to-detector transformation and transferring category-specific adjustments is novel relative to prior detector adaptation that assumes boxes for all classes.

Significance: The potential to scale detection to thousands of categories is important, and the reported challenge ranking underscores impact. That said, the evidential weight is weakened by mixed evaluation settings.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, practical framework; simple to implement; code/models released.
- Meaningful empirical gains and large-scale 7.6K demonstration.
- Novel angle on adapting classification to detection with limited boxes.

Cons for acceptance:
- Insufficient principled justification for the adaptation rule and the “offset.”
- Unclear why kNN averaging helps; no analysis of similarity metrics.
- Fairness of comparisons (val vs. test); weak statistical support for some claims.

Suggestions: Provide a derivation for Wj^d (e.g., first-order Taylor or meta-learning view), analyze δ semantics and calibration, compare neighbor metrics (semantic/visual), report sensitivity to k and class splits, and include results on the official test server or matched val protocols with significance tests.
