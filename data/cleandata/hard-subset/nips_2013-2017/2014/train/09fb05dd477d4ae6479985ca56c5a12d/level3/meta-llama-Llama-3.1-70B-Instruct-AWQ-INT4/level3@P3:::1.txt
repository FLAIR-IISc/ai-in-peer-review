This paper proposes a novel approach to object detection, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), which adapts object classifiers for detection without requiring bounding boxes at scale. The algorithm is simple and straightforward, making it an attractive solution for large-scale object detection tasks.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, approach, and experimental setup. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the results, including an ablation study and error analysis. The experiments are conducted on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge, and the results demonstrate significant improvement over a baseline of just using raw classifier weights on object proposal regions.

The paper's main contribution is the adaptation technique for modifying a convolutional neural network for detection. The authors propose a multi-stage algorithm that uses corresponding classification and detection data to learn the change from a classification CNN network to a detection CNN network and applies that difference to future classifiers for which there is no available detection data.

The results show that the proposed approach can increase performance of a classification network by 50% relative improvement using the adaptation algorithm. The authors also demonstrate the scalability of their approach by producing a 7604 category detector, which is a significant contribution to the field of object detection.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results. The writing is clear, and the figures and tables are well-annotated and easy to understand.

In terms of originality, the paper combines two existing adaptation ideas in a novel way, making it an interesting and significant contribution to the field. However, it is not ground-breaking, and the contribution is likely to be surpassed by future research.

The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses a difficult problem in object detection and provides a unique solution. The results are important, and other researchers are likely to build upon this work. However, the trade-off between fewer boxes and mAP is unclear and hard to judge, and the long-term importance of the problem (bounding-box scarcity) may be solved by resource investment.

One minor issue is the inconsistent method name ("DNN" vs "DDA"), and there is a typo on line 431 ("classi?ers"). Additionally, the authors should clarify in Figure 3a which methods (besides the authors' method and R-CNN) use category labels for detection for fair comparison.

The main result should be the DNN performance in Figure 3b (about one-third between baseline and oracle), and the 78% and 50% claims are misleading for reasons noted. The authors should provide a dedicated table/subsection clearly specifying differences from R-CNN (suggest removing Figure 6 to make space).

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of object detection. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the results, and the paper is well-written and clearly organized.
