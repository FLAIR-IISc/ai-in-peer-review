This paper proposes a novel approach to adapt object classifiers for detection, thereby avoiding the need for bounding boxes at scale. The authors introduce the Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA) algorithm, which learns the difference between classification and detection tasks and transfers this knowledge to classifiers for categories without bounding box annotated data.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, approach, and experimental results. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the results, including an ablation study to evaluate the importance of each component of the algorithm. The paper also presents a detailed error analysis, which provides insights into the types of errors made by the system.

The algorithm is simple and straightforward, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how it works. The experimental setup is also well-designed, with a clear evaluation protocol and a comparison to a strong baseline.

The results show that the LSDA algorithm achieves a significant improvement over the baseline, with a 50% relative mAP boost on the held-out categories. The authors also demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a large-scale detection task, producing a 7604 category detector.

The paper is original and interesting, and it combines two existing adaptation ideas in a novel way. The authors provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from previous work, and they provide a thorough analysis of the results.

However, the significance of the paper is somewhat limited by the fact that the approach is likely to be surpassed by future research. The trade-off between fewer boxes and mAP is also unclear and hard to judge. Additionally, the long-term importance of the problem of bounding-box scarcity may be mitigated by resource investment.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of the approach, and the experimental results are well-presented.

In terms of originality, the paper combines two existing adaptation ideas in a novel way, and it provides a new approach to adapting object classifiers for detection.

In terms of significance, the paper provides a significant improvement over the baseline, and it demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach on a large-scale detection task. However, the significance of the paper is somewhat limited by the fact that the approach is likely to be surpassed by future research.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with some minor revisions to address the issues mentioned above. Specifically, the authors should:

* Clarify in Figure 3a which methods (besides the authors’ method and R-CNN) use category labels for detection for fair comparison.
* Provide a dedicated table/subsection clearly specifying differences from R-CNN (suggest removing Figure 6 to make space).
* Address the minor issues mentioned in the review, such as inconsistent method name ("DNN" vs "DDA") and a typo on line 431 ("classi?ers").
