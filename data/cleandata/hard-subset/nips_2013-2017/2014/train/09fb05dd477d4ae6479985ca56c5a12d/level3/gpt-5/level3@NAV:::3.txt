This paper proposes LSDA, a pragmatic method to adapt an ImageNet-trained Krizhevsky (AlexNet) classifier into a detector when only a subset of categories have bounding boxes. The pipeline: (1) train a K-way classifier on image-level labels; (2) fine-tune on region proposals for m annotated classes while adding a background class, adapting shared layers (1–7) to a detection regime; and (3) for unannotated classes, transfer category-invariant changes via shared fine-tuning and approximate category-specific deltas by averaging fc8 weight shifts from nearest annotated neighbors. Detection is performed by scoring region proposals and subtracting the background logit. The approach relates to R-CNN-style fine-tuning for detection and to domain adaptation, and stands alongside weakly supervised detection via MIL as a complementary direction.

Quality: The paper is technically sound and the results are compelling: a reported ~50% relative mAP gain over a classification-only baseline on held-out classes, plus a 7.6K-category detector with released code. The ablations highlight the value of background modeling and feature adaptation. However, key baselines are missing, weakening causal attribution: (1) a “full-image positives + region negatives” baseline using the same AlexNet features; (2) an MIL-based region selection baseline trained with weak labels; and (3) a trivial center/mean-box classifier to gauge dataset difficulty. Without (1) and (2), it is unclear whether LSDA truly outperforms strong weak-supervision alternatives, and (3) would calibrate how much gain comes from nontrivial localization.

Clarity: Generally well written. The experiments section would benefit from explicitly mapping each row of Table 1 to the precise training configuration (layers updated, presence of background, value of k in neighbor averaging, and the scoring formula), to enable exact reproduction.

Originality: The combination—domain adaptation from classification to detection with nearest-neighbor transfer of category-specific deltas—is novel enough for NIPS, though the contribution leans on the strength of AlexNet and is specific to architectures with separable shared layers and linear heads.

Significance: High. Scaling detection with minimal box supervision is a central problem, and the demonstrated gains and released models are likely to be used.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, logical method with strong empirical gains and large-scale deployment.
- Clear analysis showing reduced background/localization errors.
- Practical relevance; released resources.

Cons against acceptance:
- Missing three critical baselines (full-image/region-negative, MIL, mean-box).
- Attribution ambiguity: improvements may primarily stem from AlexNet and background modeling, not the proposed weakly supervised transfer.
- Some experimental details need clearer exposition.

Overall: Leaning toward acceptance contingent on adding the missing baselines or, at minimum, providing strong justification for their absence.
