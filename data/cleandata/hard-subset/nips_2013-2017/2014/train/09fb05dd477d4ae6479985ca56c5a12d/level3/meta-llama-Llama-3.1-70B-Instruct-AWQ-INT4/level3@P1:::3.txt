This paper proposes a novel approach to large-scale object detection, called Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), which enables the transformation of a classifier into a detector without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge, achieving a 50% relative mAP boost over a baseline of just using raw classifier weights on object proposal regions.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method, which is an extension of the Krizhevsky CNN model. The approach is simple and logical, making it likely valuable to vision and machine learning researchers. The experiments are thorough, and the results are interesting and compelling, largely building on the strength of the Krizhevsky model rather than a radical new idea.

However, there are some concerns regarding the baselines used in the paper. The reviewer suggests that the paper lacks comparisons to three obvious baselines that could challenge its claims: full-image positives with region negatives, MIL-based region selection, and center/mean-box classifier baseline. The reviewer is unsure whether the proposed method outperforms these baselines, particularly the first two, and therefore believes that these comparisons are necessary.

Additionally, the reviewer notes that the paper could benefit from clearer explanations in the experiments section, particularly regarding what each method in Table 1 corresponds to (which equations/layers applied). The reviewer also suggests that including the simple mean-box baseline is important to show how easy/hard the dataset/problem is.

In terms of originality, the reviewer believes that the paper is sufficiently novel for acceptance but relatively specific to certain CNN architectures and not highly innovative. However, the problem and results are significant and valuable for the community, and the paper's contribution to the field of object detection is substantial.

One potential concern is that the results might be due to the strong CNN features rather than a superior weakly-supervised detector training method. The reviewer suggests that the authors should consider this possibility and provide additional analysis or experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.

Overall, the paper is well-written and presents a valuable contribution to the field of object detection. While there are some concerns regarding the baselines and originality, the reviewer believes that the paper is worthy of acceptance.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach to large-scale object detection that achieves significant improvements over a baseline.
* The approach is simple and logical, making it likely valuable to vision and machine learning researchers.
* The experiments are thorough, and the results are interesting and compelling.
* The problem and results are significant and valuable for the community.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper lacks comparisons to three obvious baselines that could challenge its claims.
* The reviewer is unsure whether the proposed method outperforms these baselines.
* The paper could benefit from clearer explanations in the experiments section.
* The results might be due to the strong CNN features rather than a superior weakly-supervised detector training method.

Recommendations for the authors:

* Include comparisons to the three obvious baselines mentioned above.
* Provide clearer explanations in the experiments section, particularly regarding what each method in Table 1 corresponds to (which equations/layers applied).
* Consider the possibility that the results might be due to the strong CNN features rather than a superior weakly-supervised detector training method and provide additional analysis or experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.
* Include the simple mean-box baseline to show how easy/hard the dataset/problem is.
