

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

This paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), an algorithm that transforms object classifiers into detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The authors frame the problem as domain adaptation between image classification (source domain) and object detection (target domain). The key insight is that the transformation from classification to detection can be learned on a subset of categories with bounding box annotations and then applied to adapt classifiers for categories without such annotations.

The work builds on several prior approaches: Girshick et al.'s R-CNN demonstrated that fine-tuning deep features improves detection performance, while MIL methods have shown promise for weakly supervised detection. LSDA combines these ideas by learning a domain adaptation transformation from classification to detection, which can be applied to any classifier without bounding box data. This approach differs from R-CNN in that it requires bounding box annotations for only a subset of categories (not all), significantly reducing annotation requirements.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The experimental setup is appropriate: training on 100 categories with bounding boxes and labels, testing on 100 held-out categories with only labels. The main result (Figure 3b) shows LSDA achieves about one-third of the way between the baseline (using raw classifier weights) and the oracle (full detection data), filling ~35% of the gap. The paper's claim of "50% relative mAP improvement" is misleading as it refers to relative improvement from the baseline (not absolute performance), and the "78%" claim in the abstract appears to refer to a different metric.

Strengths:
- The paper clearly explains its contribution and situates it within the literature
- The algorithm is simple and straightforward to implement
- The results demonstrate a meaningful improvement over the classification-only baseline
- The approach enables detection for tens of thousands of categories (demonstrated with 7.6K detector)
- The paper is high quality with clear writing and good experimental design

Weaknesses:
- The main result (Figure 3b) should be emphasized as the primary contribution, rather than the misleading "78%" and "50%" claims
- Figure 3a needs clarification on which methods (besides LSDA and R-CNN) use category labels for detection to ensure fair comparison
- A dedicated table should be added to clearly specify differences from R-CNN (Figure 6 could be removed to make space)
- Inconsistent terminology ("DNN" vs "DDA") and a typo on line 431 ("classi?ers") need correction
- The significance discussion should more explicitly address whether the problem (bounding box scarcity) is truly long-term, as the authors question

The paper's originality is moderate - it combines existing ideas in a novel way but isn't ground-breaking. The significance is somewhat limited by the fact that the trade-off between reduced bounding box requirements and mAP performance isn't clearly established, and the problem itself (bounding box scarcity) may be solved by resource investment rather than algorithmic innovation.

The authors make a compelling case that their approach enables detection for tens of thousands of categories using existing classification data, but the paper would be stronger with clearer presentation of results, better comparison to relevant methods, and more nuanced discussion of the problem's long-term importance.

Pro arguments for acceptance: The paper presents a clear, well-executed approach to an important problem with meaningful results. The method is simple and has potential impact for scaling detection to many categories. The writing is excellent and the experiments are thorough.

Con arguments against acceptance: The main result is presented confusingly, the significance is questionable, and the problem may be short-term as bounding box scarcity may be solved with sufficient resource investment.

Overall, this is a solid NIPS paper that makes a useful contribution to the field. The authors have demonstrated a meaningful improvement over the baseline, and the approach has potential for practical applications. With minor revisions to clarify the results and address the minor issues noted above, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
