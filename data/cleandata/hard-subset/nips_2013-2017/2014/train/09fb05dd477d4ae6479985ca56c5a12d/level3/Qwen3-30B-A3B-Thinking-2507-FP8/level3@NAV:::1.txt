

This paper proposes LSDA, a method to adapt object classifiers into detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for the target categories. The authors frame this as a domain adaptation problem, training on a subset of categories with bounding boxes while adapting classifiers for held-out categories using category-invariant and category-specific transformations. The approach builds on R-CNN and domain adaptation techniques, but focuses on enabling detection for categories lacking bounding box data.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with clear experimental setup: training on 100 categories with boxes+labels and testing on 100 held-out categories with labels only. The main contribution is demonstrating that their method fills approximately 35% of the gap between the oracle (full bounding box data) and baseline (raw classifier weights) on held-out categories. However, the claims about "78% relative improvement" and "50% relative mAP" are misleading; the actual performance is about one-third of the way between baseline and oracle (Figure 3b), not the stated figures.

Strengths include the clear exposition, high-quality writing, and practical demonstration of a 7.6K detector. The method is simple and straightforward, which is appealing for scalability. The paper adequately addresses related work, though it could better clarify which methods in Figure 3a use category labels for detection to ensure fair comparison.

Weaknesses include the unclear trade-off between reduced bounding box requirements and detection performance (mAP), and the questionable long-term importance of the problem (bounding box scarcity may be solved through resource investment). The paper's significance is limited as the approach likely won't be state-of-the-art for long. The inconsistent use of "DNN" and "DDA" and the typo on line 431 are minor but should be fixed.

The authors should replace Figure 6 with a dedicated table clearly specifying differences from R-CNN, as the current figure is redundant. While the method is novel in combining existing ideas, it's not groundbreaking. The paper makes a useful contribution to the field, but the significance is modest. The main result should be presented as achieving "one-third of the performance gap between baseline and oracle" rather than the misleading 50% claim.
