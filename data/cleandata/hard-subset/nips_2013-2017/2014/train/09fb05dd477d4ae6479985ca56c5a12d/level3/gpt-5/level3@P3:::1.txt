Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses the scarcity of bounding-box annotations for large-scale object detection. It proposes LSDA, a simple adaptation pipeline that learns the transformation from classification to detection on a subset of categories with boxes and transfers it to held-out categories that have only image-level labels. Concretely: (1) pretrain a CNN for classification over all K categories; (2) fine-tune layers 1–7 and add a background class using detection data for m categories (set B) to learn a category-invariant shift from classification to detection; (3) estimate category-specific deltas for held-out categories (set A) by averaging the fc8 parameter changes of the nearest neighbors in B. The approach builds on R-CNN (fine-tuning for detection) and classic model/domain adaptation ideas (category-invariant and category-specific adaptation), but removes the need for per-category boxes at scale. On ImageNet Detection (200 classes), the experimental protocol trains detection on 100 categories with boxes and evaluates on 100 held-out categories with labels only, showing clear gains over a classification-only baseline.

Quality
- Soundness: The method is technically straightforward and well-justified. The ablations isolate the contributions of background modeling, category-invariant fine-tuning, and category-specific fc8 deltas. The finding that background and shared layers (1–7) account for most of the gains is compelling and consistent with the diagnosis of errors (reduced localization and background confusion).
- Empirical setup: The held-out protocol (100 with boxes for training, 100 without) is appropriate. The “oracle” upper bound (access to boxes for all 200) is a reasonable reference. Using Caffe and standard proposals makes the study reproducible.
- Claims vs evidence: The central claim is supported by mAP on the held-out categories. However, the way improvements are framed can be misleading. The most relevant headline is that LSDA fills roughly one-third of the gap between the classification-only baseline and the oracle on the left-out categories (≈35%), not “50% relative mAP” or “78%” figures that mix different comparisons. Please center the narrative on the Figure 3b DNN results for the 100 held-out categories.
- Completeness: Additional analyses that would strengthen the paper include sensitivity to the number of categories with boxes (size of B), to k in the nearest-neighbor delta averaging, and to the choice of nearest-neighbor metric.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and easy to follow. Figures are generally informative.
- Please clarify in Figure 3a which competing methods, besides your own and R-CNN, actually use category labels for detection (and how), to ensure a fair apples-to-apples comparison.
- Consider adding a dedicated table or subsection that crisply enumerates the differences from R-CNN: data requirements per category, use of background class, whether SVMs and bbox regression are used, training time, and what parts are trained jointly. This will help the reader situate LSDA. You could remove or move Figure 6 (qualitative 7.6K detections) to supplementary to make room.

Originality
- The contribution combines two established ideas—shared feature adaptation (category-invariant fine-tuning with background) and model-parameter transfer (category-specific fc8 deltas via nearest neighbors)—in a new setting: transferring a learned classification-to-detection transformation to categories without boxes. The combination is novel and useful, though not conceptually ground-breaking.

Significance
- Practical significance: The approach offers a pragmatic path to scaling detection when bounding boxes are scarce, and the empirical gains on held-out categories are nontrivial.
- Longevity: This line of work is likely to be surpassed by future methods that use weaker supervision or self-supervision to reduce annotation needs. Moreover, the long-term importance of the “no boxes at scale” problem is uncertain; with sufficient investment, collecting boxes for many categories might be feasible. The paper would benefit from quantifying the trade-off: how many bounding boxes are “saved” per point of mAP on held-out categories, and how performance scales as m increases.

Results interpretation
- On the 100 held-out categories, LSDA improves mAP by about 5–6 points (e.g., from ~10.3% to ~16.1%), while the oracle is ~26%. Thus LSDA bridges roughly 35% of the baseline-to-oracle gap. This is the main result and should be emphasized. Reporting “50% relative mAP” (vs the baseline) and “78%” figures obscures the more meaningful gap-based view and conflates settings (with vs without boxes).
- It’s helpful that you analyze error types (Loc, BG, Oth). Consider adding class-wise improvements and PR curves to illustrate where LSDA helps most (e.g., classes with strong part bias).

Questions and clarifications
- Please specify precisely which baselines in Figure 3a do and do not use category labels for detection, and whether any use background modeling or fine-tuning.
- How sensitive is performance to k in the neighbor averaging, and to the size/composition of B? Do semantically close neighbors (WordNet, confusion matrices) outperform fc8-distance neighbors?
- Does adding a class-agnostic bounding-box regressor trained on B (shared across categories) further close the gap for A?
- What is the impact of different proposal methods on the held-out results?

Suggestions for improvement
- Reframe the abstract and conclusions so the principal quantitative message is the fraction of the baseline–oracle gap closed on the held-out categories (Figure 3b), not relative percentages that are easy to misinterpret.
- Add a short subsection/table clearly contrasting LSDA with R-CNN, including training/inference costs and data assumptions; consider removing Figure 6 to make space.
- Provide a cost–benefit view: mAP vs number of boxed categories (m), to help practitioners decide the “sweet spot.”

Pros
- Addresses a relevant bottleneck (few boxes) with a simple, effective adaptation pipeline.
- Clear ablations and error analysis; convincing demonstration that background and shared-layer adaptation matter.
- Practical, reproducible setup; useful release of large-scale detectors.

Cons
- Conceptual novelty is moderate (combination of existing ideas).
- Headline numbers are framed in a potentially misleading way; the most relevant metric is the fraction of the baseline–oracle gap on held-out categories (~35%).
- Unclear fairness of baselines in Figure 3a re: use of labels; comparison to R-CNN could be better structured.
- Long-term impact is uncertain; the annotation trade-off is not quantified.

Minor issues
- Inconsistent method naming: the text alternates between “DNN” and “DDA” in places; please standardize on “LSDA.”
- Typo on line 431: “classi?ers” → “classifiers.”
- Please ensure figure captions explicitly state which settings use boxes and which do not.

Overall assessment
High-quality and clearly written paper with a simple, well-executed idea that makes a real step toward scalable detection without per-category boxes. While the novelty is incremental and the long-term significance uncertain, the empirical study is careful and the contribution will be of interest to the community. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the comparisons and reframing the main quantitative message.
