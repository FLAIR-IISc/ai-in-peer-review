This paper proposes Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a method for transferring knowledge from image classification networks to object detection, particularly in scenarios where bounding box annotations are scarce. The core idea is to leverage the readily available wealth of image-level labels to train classifiers, and then adapt these classifiers into detectors, focusing adaptation on categories with limited or no bounding box data. The approach builds upon the recent success of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and addresses a critical bottleneck in scaling object detection to a large number of categories.

The authors appropriately position their work within the context of recent advances in both classification and detection, citing key papers such as Girshick et al. [1] and Krizhevsky et al. [3]. They correctly identify the data annotation bottleneck for detection and highlight the contrast with the relative ease of obtaining image-level labels. The paper draws connections to related work in Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) [6, 7] and domain adaptation [12, 13, 14, 15, 16], but clearly distinguishes LSDA’s focus on adapting to held-out categories *without* any bounding box data, a unique aspect of their approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant problem:** The scarcity of bounding box annotations is a major hurdle in scaling object detection. LSDA directly tackles this problem with a practical and potentially impactful solution.
* **Logical and intuitive approach:** The idea of transferring knowledge from classification to detection through adaptation is conceptually sound and well-motivated. The three-stage adaptation process (category invariant, category specific, and background modeling) is clearly articulated.
* **Strong empirical results:** The quantitative results on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge demonstrate a significant performance improvement (+50% relative mAP) over a baseline using raw classifier weights.
* **Large-scale demonstration:** The authors showcase the scalability of their approach by creating a 7.6K detector using ImageNet data, highlighting the potential to address detection for a vast number of categories.
* **Code and models available:**  Publicly releasing the models and software (lsda.berkeleyvision.org) promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Missing Baselines:** The paper lacks critical baseline comparisons. As highlighted in the key points, comparisons to (1) using full-image positives with region negatives, (2) MIL-based region selection methods [6, 7], and (3) a simple mean-box classifier baseline would significantly strengthen the evaluation. The reviewer is uncertain if the proposed method meaningfully outperforms baseline 1 and likely baseline 2. The simplicity of a mean-box baseline (taking the average bounding box coordinates from all instances of a class) would be particularly insightful for demonstrating the difficulty of the task and the benefits of LSDA.
* **Experiment Clarity:** The experiments section could benefit from more detailed explanations. Table 1 presents a comparison of different adaptation techniques, but the correspondence between these techniques and the specific equations/layers applied is not entirely clear. Providing a more granular description of each method would improve reproducibility and understanding.
* **Attribution of Performance Gains:** The paper demonstrates impressive results, but it's difficult to disentangle the contribution of LSDA’s adaptation techniques from the inherent power of the underlying Krizhevsky CNN. The performance gains may be largely attributed to the strong CNN features themselves, rather than a superior weakly-supervised detector training method.
* **Originality:** While the approach is novel, it builds heavily on existing CNN architectures and established techniques like fine-tuning. The degree of originality, while sufficient for acceptance, is not exceptionally high.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and claims are supported by experimental results. The experimental setup is well-defined, and the ablation study provides insights into the importance of different components of the algorithm. It's a complete piece of work, although the addition of the suggested baselines would significantly enhance its rigor. The authors are careful to present their results and acknowledge the limitations of their approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The core ideas are clearly explained, and the figures are helpful in illustrating the approach. However, as noted above, the experiments section could benefit from more detailed explanations.

**Originality:** The problem addressed is relevant and important, and the proposed solution offers a novel approach to tackling the annotation bottleneck in object detection. However, it's an incremental improvement built on existing foundations.

**Significance:** The results are significant and have the potential to impact the field of object detection. The ability to leverage image-level labels for training detectors could unlock detection capabilities for a vast number of categories, moving closer to human-level visual recognition.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and challenging problem in object detection.
* Demonstrates a substantial performance improvement over a reasonable baseline.
* Provides a practical and scalable solution for leveraging large-scale image-level data.
* Makes code and models publicly available, promoting reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lacks crucial baseline comparisons that would strengthen the evaluation.
* Difficulty in attributing performance gains solely to the proposed adaptation techniques.
* Relatively incremental novelty.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of object detection. The proposed LSDA algorithm offers a promising approach for scaling detection to a large number of categories by leveraging readily available image-level labels. While the inclusion of stronger baselines and a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup would further strengthen the paper, the current work is sufficiently novel, well-executed, and significant to warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns raised above, particularly the inclusion of the suggested baseline comparisons.
