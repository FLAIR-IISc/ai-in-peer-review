This paper proposes Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), an algorithm that adapts object classifiers into detectors without requiring bounding box annotations. The main idea is to learn a transformation from classification to detection using a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations and then apply this transformation to other classifiers. This approach is relevant to the NIPS community, as it addresses the challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories.

The authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their work, including a thorough review of related literature. The paper is well-written, and the authors have made an effort to provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce their results.

The LSDA algorithm is based on the idea of domain adaptation, where the classification data is considered as the source domain and the detection data is considered as the target domain. The authors use a convolutional neural network (CNN) to learn a transformation from the source domain to the target domain. The algorithm consists of three main components: category invariant adaptation, category specific adaptation, and detection.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the LSDA algorithm. The authors evaluate their approach on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge and show a significant improvement over a baseline that uses raw classifier weights on object proposal regions. The results also demonstrate that the LSDA algorithm can be used to adapt any ImageNet-trained classifier into a detector, whether or not there are corresponding detection labels for that class.

The paper makes a novel contribution to the field of object detection, as it addresses the challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories without requiring bounding box annotations. The LSDA algorithm is simple and straightforward, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness.

However, the originality of the paper is not groundbreaking, as it combines two existing adaptation ideas. The significance of the contribution is also unclear, as the trade-off between fewer boxes and mAP is hard to judge. Additionally, the long-term importance of the problem of bounding-box scarcity is questionable, as it may be solved by resource investment.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, but there are some minor issues. For example, the method name is inconsistent ("DNN" vs "DDA"), and there is a typo on line 431 ("classi?ers"). Additionally, Figure 3a is unclear, and it would be helpful to specify which methods use category labels for detection for fair comparison.

Overall, the paper is of high quality, and the authors have made a significant contribution to the field of object detection. However, the originality and significance of the contribution are not groundbreaking, and the paper could be improved with some minor revisions.
