- Paper proposes LSDA: convert image classifiers into object detectors by adapting a Krizhevsky CNN, enabling detection for categories without bounding-box annotations.  
- Main contribution: a three-stage adaptation (train classifier on all classes; fine-tune on region proposals with a background class for a subset; transfer category-specific parameters to others via nearest-neighbor).  
- Demonstration: produces a 7.6K-category detector on ImageNet and reports a 50% relative mAP gain over a classification-only baseline on held-out categories.  
- Strength: Addresses a significant scaling problem with a simple, logical method that builds on R-CNN and domain adaptation.  
- Strength: Results are compelling and practical impact is increased by releasing models and code.  
- Strength: Well-written with clear motivation and methodology.  
- Weakness: Missing critical baseline — full-image positives with region negatives to test whether adaptation outperforms naive region application of classification weights.  
- Weakness: Missing MIL-based region selection baseline to check if weakly-supervised methods could suffice instead of adaptation.  
- Weakness: Missing mean-box classifier baseline to assess dataset detection difficulty without bounding boxes.  
- Attribution concern: Unclear if improvements stem from the adaptation method or simply the strength of Krizhevsky features.  
- Experimental clarity issue: Table 1 lacks clear mapping between listed methods and the specific equations/layers applied, hindering assessment.  
- Comparison shortfall: Paper does not sufficiently demonstrate superiority over existing weakly-supervised approaches like MIL.  
- Quality assessment: Technically sound but experimental evaluation is incomplete due to missing baselines, weakening the novelty claims.  
- Originality & significance: Logical extension of prior work (not radical); addresses an important problem and shows promise but falls short of fully proving superiority for large-scale detection.  
- Overall verdict: Interesting and valuable approach with compelling results, but missing comparisons and attribution clarity limit the strength of the claims; additional baselines would make it a stronger contribution.
