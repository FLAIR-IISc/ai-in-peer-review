- Introduces LSDA, a method to convert classifiers into detectors without bounding boxes for all categories by learning transformation on a subset and transferring it.
- Frames the problem as domain adaptation from classification (source) to detection (target) and proposes a three-step adaptation: model background, transfer category-invariant features, and category-specific adaptation via nearest neighbors.
- Builds on R-CNN; requires bounding boxes for only a subset (100/200) yet enables detection for held-out categories, showing substantial mAP gains over a classification-only baseline.
- Demonstrates scalability with a 7,604-category detector and reports a 50% relative mAP improvement over the baseline on held-out categories.
- Strength: Technically sound with clear experimental design and effective ablation study showing each component’s contribution.
- Strength: Conceptually simple and easy to implement and understand.
- Strength: Clearly written, well-organized, and reproducible with thorough error analysis reducing localization and background errors.
- Weakness: The 50% relative mAP improvement is misleading; LSDA actually recovers roughly one-third of the gap to oracle, not a 50% improvement in absolute terms.
- Weakness: The long-term significance is questionable since bounding box scarcity could be solved via annotation efforts (e.g., crowdsourcing); paper should discuss when LSDA is preferable.
- Weakness: Figure 3a is unclear about which methods use category labels for detection; fair comparisons are difficult without a dedicated comparison table.
- Weakness: Figure 6 (7,604-category results) is not essential and could be removed to make room for a comparison table.
- Weakness: Inconsistent method naming (DNN vs DDA) and minor typos (e.g., "classi?ers").
- Originality: Novel combination of domain adaptation and classifier-to-detector transformation applied to detection; differs from prior DA work by changing both domain and task.
- Significance: Addresses an important practical scaling problem but overstates gains and doesn’t fully justify why it’s preferable to collecting more bounding boxes.
- Arguments for acceptance: Clear experiments, simple effective approach, strong presentation, and demonstration of large-scale detection capability.
- Arguments against acceptance: Misleading result presentation, questionable long-term importance, and minor presentation/terminology issues.
- Recommendation: Accept, with revisions to clarify the 50% claim and add a dedicated comparison table to R-CNN and related methods.
