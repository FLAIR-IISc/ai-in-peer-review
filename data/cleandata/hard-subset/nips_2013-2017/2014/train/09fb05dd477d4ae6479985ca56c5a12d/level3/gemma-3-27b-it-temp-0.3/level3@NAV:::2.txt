## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper proposes LSDA, a framework for adapting image classification-trained deep convolutional neural networks for object detection, specifically addressing the challenge of limited bounding box annotations for many object categories. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification feature space to the detection feature space using categories with both classification and detection labels, and then apply this transformation to classifiers trained on categories with only classification labels. The authors demonstrate promising results on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge, achieving 4th place ranking and showcasing the potential for scaling object detection to a large number of categories. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented. The architecture is relatively simple and should be straightforward to implement, a significant strength. The experimental setup is well-defined, and the ablation studies provide some insight into the contribution of different components. The release of models and software is also commendable. The work builds upon the success of R-CNN [1] by tackling the data scarcity problem, and relates to prior work in domain adaptation and multiple instance learning, appropriately citing relevant literature.

However, the primary weakness lies in the lack of principled justification for the proposed adaptation method. While the empirical results are encouraging, the motivation behind adding the adapted weights (Wj^c + W_avg) in Section 3.2 feels ad-hoc. The “offset” parameter lacks a clear physical interpretation, and a more detailed explanation of *why* this particular combination is effective would significantly strengthen the paper. Furthermore, the superior performance of the kNN variant over other variants isn't adequately explained. Why does averaging the weights of nearest neighbors lead to better adaptation? 

A significant concern is the use of part of the validation set (val1) for training the detector, effectively creating a data leakage issue. This makes the comparison to other methods unfair, as they likely evaluated on the full, untouched validation set. The claim of RCNN having similar performance on val2, given its small size, is not statistically justified. A more rigorous evaluation using a separate test set is crucial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Achieves competitive performance on a challenging benchmark.
*   Addresses a significant problem in scaling object detection.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Code and models are publicly available.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Adaptation method lacks theoretical justification.
*   Experimental setup is flawed due to validation set usage.
*   Key design choices (offset parameter, kNN variant) are not well explained.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a promising approach. However, the methodological concerns regarding the adaptation method’s justification and the experimental setup need to be addressed before it can be considered a significant contribution. I recommend acceptance with major revisions, specifically requesting a more principled explanation of the adaptation method and a re-evaluation using a properly separated test set.
