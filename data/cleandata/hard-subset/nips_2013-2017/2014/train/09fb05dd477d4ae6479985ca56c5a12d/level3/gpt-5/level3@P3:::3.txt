Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles large-scale object detection when only a subset of categories has bounding-box annotations. The authors adapt a Krizhevsky-style CNN trained for image classification into a detector through: (1) training a K-way classification network on image-level labels for all categories; (2) fine-tuning on region proposals for the subset with bounding boxes (set B), adding an explicit background class and adapting layers 1–7; and (3) transferring the learned “classification-to-detection” deltas to held-out classes (set A) by averaging the fc8 changes of nearest neighbors in B. At test time, the network is run on region proposals and produces per-class scores minus a background score. The approach is cast as domain adaptation between the classification (source) and detection (target) tasks.

This builds directly on R-CNN (Girshick et al.) and the success of deep CNNs for classification (Krizhevsky et al.), while differing from R-CNN by not requiring bounding boxes for all categories and by using a background-aware softmax detector rather than SVMs/regression. It is complementary to weakly supervised MIL-based detectors (e.g., Ali & Saenko; Song et al.), which learn instance labels from image-level supervision but had not, at the time, been shown at ImageNet scale. The adaptation idea is also related to prior domain adaptation methods, but here the adaptation is learned within the deep model and transferred to categories lacking detection labels.

Strengths
- Simple and logical approach that leverages strong CNN features and a well-motivated background class to bridge classification and detection.
- Clear articulation of category-invariant (layer 1–7) vs. category-specific (fc8) adaptations, with ablations showing the dominant gains come from the former.
- Compelling large-scale demonstration: a 7.6K-category detector and a faster variant using SPP/fast proposals.
- Practical impact: significantly reduces annotation burden; provides models and code.
- Solid error analysis indicating reduced localization and background confusions after adaptation.

Weaknesses and missing baselines
- Three obvious baselines are absent, and they could materially change the interpretation of the gains:
  1) Full-image positives with region negatives: Train the K-way classifier on full positive images and treat sampled region proposals from images without the class as negatives; evaluate on region proposals at test time. It is unclear that LSDA would outperform this, especially given the strong pretraining and background modeling options.
  2) MIL-based region selection: Use MIL to select positive instances from regions in weakly labeled images, then fine-tune the detector. Given contemporary work (e.g., Song et al.), this could match or exceed the proposed fc8 neighbor-transfer for set A.
  3) Center/mean-box baseline: Score only the image center or a learned class-agnostic mean box to calibrate dataset difficulty. Including this helps contextualize how often “easy” detections suffice on ImageNet detection.
- Attribution concern: Gains may stem primarily from the strength of CNN features plus explicit background modeling rather than from the proposed category-specific transfer (nearest-neighbor δ averaging). Ablations suggest the fc8 transfer contributes the least; more rigorous controls are needed.
- Clarity in the experiments: Table 1 entries are not self-contained; the paper should explicitly map each row to which layers are adapted, whether the background class is added, what k is used, and which loss is optimized.

Quality
- The method is technically sound and empirically validated on an appropriate benchmark with a well-defined held-out protocol. The ablation study is valuable and the error analysis is insightful.
- However, the absence of the three baselines above weakens the claim that LSDA is the preferable way to obtain detectors without boxes. In particular, it is plausible baseline (1) and likely baseline (2) could narrow or eliminate the margin over the “classification-only on proposals” baseline.
- Statistical significance is reported for one comparison; broader significance testing across ablations and per-class analyses would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The method and training pipeline are easy to follow.
- The experiments section would benefit from clearer, explicit descriptions of each variant in Table 1, including:
  - Which layers (conv1–5, fc6–7, fc8) are fine-tuned vs. frozen.
  - Whether/when the background class is included in the softmax.
  - The precise definition of the score combination (score_i − score_bg) and whether calibration is needed across categories.
  - How k is selected for δ transfer and sensitivity to k; whether “nearest” is in fc8 space only or alternatives (e.g., WordNet, confusion-based) were tried.

Originality
- The work is sufficiently novel for NIPS 2014: adapting a classifier-to-detector transformation learned on a subset and transferred to held-out categories is a useful and concrete idea.
- That said, it is a relatively incremental advance built atop R-CNN and ImageNet pretraining, and is somewhat specific to softmax-based CNN detectors with an explicit background class.

Significance
- High practical significance: reduces the need for bounding boxes and enables scaling to thousands of categories. The community is likely to use or build on the released models/code.
- The results on ImageNet detection and the 7.6K detector are valuable demonstrations.

Requested experiments/analyses
- Add the three baselines:
  - Full-image positives + region negatives classifier.
  - MIL-based region selection for set A (e.g., Song et al.), optionally combined with your category-invariant fine-tuning.
  - Mean/center-box baseline to quantify dataset difficulty.
- Attribution controls:
  - Freeze conv1–fc7 after classification training and only add a background class; compare to full fine-tuning to isolate the background effect.
  - Compare nearest-neighbor δ transfer to random δ transfer and to zero δ (no fc8 transfer) across varying k and alternative neighbor metrics (semantic, confusion-based).
- Clarify Table 1 and report per-class AP deltas to show where the method helps/hurts, especially for small/large objects and visually similar categories.
- Report runtime trade-offs more explicitly and include accuracy vs. speed for the SPP/fast-proposal variant.

Pros
- Simple, well-motivated method; strong empirical gains on held-out classes.
- Scales detection to thousands of categories with modest additional training.
- Good error analysis; code/models released; practical impact.

Cons
- Missing strong baselines (full-image+region negatives, MIL, mean-box).
- Improvements may largely reflect background modeling and deep features, not the proposed fc8 transfer.
- Experimental descriptions (Table 1) need more precision.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and timely contribution with clear practical value. While not a radical innovation, it is an elegant, effective adaptation of classification networks to detection with minimal additional labels. Assuming the authors address the missing baselines and clarify the experiments, I lean toward acceptance.
