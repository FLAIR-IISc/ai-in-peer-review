- Problem: scales object detection to many categories by converting image-level classifiers into detectors to overcome bounding-box annotation scarcity.  
- Method: LSDA learns a transformation from classifiers to detectors on categories with boxes, then applies it to categories without boxes.  
- Motivation: leverages abundant image-level labels (e.g., from search engines) to reduce annotation bottleneck.  
- Background: builds on CNN-based classification and detection (e.g., Krizhevsky et al., R-CNN) and relates to MIL and domain adaptation.  
- Strength: addresses an important, practical problem in object detection scalability.  
- Strength: conceptually simple and implementationally straightforward, aiding reproducibility.  
- Strength: clear presentation and helpful figures explaining the method and experiments.  
- Strength: positive empirical results — substantial improvement (+50% relative mAP) over a naive classifier baseline and gains on held-out categories.  
- Strength: authors release code and models publicly.  
- Weakness: originality seen as incremental; no fundamental architectural breakthrough.  
- Weakness: significance unclear — trade-off between fewer annotations and achieved mAP not fully quantified.  
- Weakness: some performance claims (e.g., “78%” and “50% relative mAP”) are potentially misleading due to comparison context.  
- Weakness: lacks a detailed, explicit comparison to R-CNN (annotation needs, training, cost).  
- Weakness: minor technical issues (terminology inconsistency “DNN” vs “DDA”, and a typo).  
- Suggestions: clarify which methods in Figure 3a use category labels for detection during training.  
- Suggestions: present DNN result in Figure 3b as primary, and better contextualize the 78%/50% claims.  
- Suggestions: add a detailed table comparing LSDA and R-CNN (annotation requirements, procedure, computational cost), possibly removing Figure 6 to make space.  
- Acceptance pro: relevant problem, sound/simple method, noticeable improvements, clear writing, reproducible code/models.  
- Rejection con: incremental novelty, non-dramatic gains, insufficient R-CNN comparison, and ambiguous performance claims.  
- Overall: solid, borderline-accept paper; addressing clarification and comparison concerns would strengthen the submission.
