## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper proposes Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a method for leveraging image-level classification data to train object detectors, particularly for categories lacking bounding box annotations. The core idea is to learn a transformation from classifiers to detectors on categories with both types of data, and then apply this transformation to classifiers trained on categories with only image-level labels. The method is evaluated on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge, achieving competitive results and demonstrating the potential for scaling detection to a large number of categories.

The problem addressed is highly relevant to the field of object detection. Obtaining bounding box annotations is a significant bottleneck for scaling detection systems to the vast number of object categories present in real-world scenarios. Recent successes in image classification, fueled by large datasets of image-level labels, present an opportunity to overcome this limitation. This work builds upon the observation made by Girshick et al. (R-CNN [1]) that ImageNet classifiers can be adapted for detection, but extends this idea to categories *without* bounding box annotations, a crucial step towards large-scale detection. The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature, referencing relevant work on Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) [6, 7], domain adaptation [12, 13, 14, 15, 16], and deep convolutional networks for visual recognition [4, 5, 8, 9, 10, 11].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed approach of adapting classifiers for categories lacking bounding box annotations is a novel contribution. The ability to leverage readily available image-level data to build detectors for a large number of categories is a significant step forward.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The method achieves competitive performance on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge, demonstrating its practical effectiveness. The reported 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline using raw classifier weights is substantial.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method. The architecture and training process are described in a straightforward manner, and the figures are helpful in visualizing the approach. The code and models being publicly available (lsda.berkeleyvision.org) is also a significant positive.
* **Scalability Potential:** The method’s ability to scale to 7604 categories demonstrates its potential for real-world applications where a large number of object categories need to be detected.  The inclusion of a fast detector implementation further enhances this potential.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The central adaptation method lacks a strong theoretical grounding or detailed motivation. While the three key insights (background recognition, category-invariant information transfer, and category-specific differences) are stated, the rationale behind the specific implementation choices is not fully explained.
* **Unclear Parameter Choice:** The mechanism for adapting category-specific parameters (Section 3.2, Equation 1) with the average offset `W_avg` is not adequately justified. The paper does not explain *why* averaging the offsets from nearest neighbors is effective, and the physical interpretation of this "offset" is unclear.  Further experimentation with different averaging strategies (e.g., weighted averaging) could be beneficial.
* **Evaluation Protocol Concerns:** The use of part of the validation set for training the detector is a concern. Using the full validation set for evaluation and comparing against established results on the test set would provide a more rigorous and fair comparison. The claim of similarity to R-CNN performance on val2 lacks statistical support given the limited size of the dataset.
* **Limited Ablation Study Discussion:** While an ablation study is presented, the discussion of the results is superficial. The reasons *why* category-invariant adaptation proved more important than category-specific adaptation are not thoroughly explored. A deeper analysis of the learned transformations could provide valuable insights.
* **Fairness of Comparisons:** The comparison to existing methods is not entirely fair. The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of computational resources (training time, hardware) and hyperparameter tuning effort.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant challenge in object detection – the scarcity of bounding box annotations.
* The proposed method achieves competitive results on a challenging benchmark dataset.
* The approach has the potential to significantly scale object detection to a large number of categories.
* The code and models are publicly available, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The adaptation method lacks a strong theoretical justification and detailed motivation.
* The evaluation protocol has potential biases due to the use of validation data for training.
* The paper could benefit from a more in-depth analysis of the experimental results.
* The lack of clarity regarding the parameter choices hinders understanding and potential improvements.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper that presents a novel and promising approach to large-scale object detection. While the method lacks a strong theoretical foundation and the evaluation protocol has some limitations, the empirical results are compelling, and the potential impact of the work is significant.  I believe that addressing the weaknesses regarding justification and evaluation would substantially strengthen the paper.

**Rating:**  Strong Accept (with minor revisions addressing the clarity and justification concerns)
