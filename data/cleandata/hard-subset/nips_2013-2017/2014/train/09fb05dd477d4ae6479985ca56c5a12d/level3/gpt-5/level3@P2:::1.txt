Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the challenge of scaling object detection to thousands of categories when bounding box annotations are scarce. It proposes LSDA, a method that adapts image classifiers into object detectors. The approach has two components: (1) a category-invariant adaptation learned by fine-tuning a CNN (layers 1–7) for detection on a subset of categories with bounding boxes, which also introduces an explicit background class; and (2) a category-specific adaptation that transfers the observed changes in the classifier’s final layer weights (δ learned for categories with boxes) to categories without boxes by averaging the δ of their nearest neighbors (defined in the space of fc8 classification weights). At test time, detections are scored by subtracting the background score from each class score, and the method avoids the SVM and bounding-box regression steps of R-CNN for efficiency.

This work builds on R-CNN [1], which pre-trains on ImageNet classification and fine-tunes for detection but requires bounding boxes for all categories. It also relates to domain adaptation methods (e.g., [12–16, 17–21]) and weakly supervised MIL approaches (e.g., [6,7]) but differs by explicitly learning a classifier-to-detector transformation that generalizes to held-out categories without any detection labels. The paper also provides a large-scale demonstration (7.6K categories) and a faster variant using SPP [28] and fast proposals [27].

Quality
- Technical soundness: The method is simple and well-motivated. The category-invariant fine-tuning and explicit background class address the task shift from classification to detection; the category-specific nearest-neighbor δ transfer is reasonable and analyzed with an ablation (varying k). The evaluation protocol simulating 100 seen and 100 held-out ILSVRC detection classes is appropriate.
- Empirical support: On held-out categories, LSDA substantially improves over applying a classification network to region proposals. The ablations clearly show that most of the gain comes from the shared-layer fine-tuning and background modeling; the δ transfer offers a smaller but statistically significant additional gain. Error analysis indicates reductions in background confusion and localization errors.
- Completeness: The paper reports upper-bound “oracle” performance with full detection data, a strong baseline, and ablations. However, some reporting choices and comparisons need clarification (see below), and a more explicit comparison to full R-CNN variants would help.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The high-level story, training pipeline, and ablations are easy to follow. Figures are generally informative, though some labels and comparisons need to be made explicit for fairness. Minor notational and naming inconsistencies should be fixed (see suggestions).

Originality
The work combines established ideas—CNN fine-tuning for detection, explicit background modeling, and nearest-neighbor parameter transfer—into a coherent adaptation framework for the classifier-to-detector shift with held-out categories. While the components are familiar, the combination and empirical demonstration at scale are novel and timely. The contribution is interesting but not a fundamental algorithmic breakthrough.

Significance
Adapting classifiers to detectors without per-class bounding boxes addresses a practical bottleneck and is likely to influence follow-up work. The demonstrated gains on held-out categories and the 7.6K detector are valuable. That said, the longer-term impact depends on how the community continues to reduce annotation cost (e.g., cheaper box labeling, weak supervision), and the trade-off between saved annotation and mAP remains hard to quantify from the current results. It is plausible that later techniques will surpass these results, but this paper’s framing and analysis are useful steps.

Pros
- Clear problem setup and simple, effective algorithm.
- Solid ablation demonstrating where the gains come from; compelling error analysis.
- Practical large-scale demonstration (7.6K categories) and a faster inference variant.
- Careful framing as task/domain adaptation, with results on a standard benchmark.

Cons and questions
- Reporting and framing: The “50% relative” and “78%” improvements are potentially misleading. The main figure to emphasize should be how much of the gap between the baseline and the oracle is closed on held-out categories; by my reading LSDA closes roughly one-third (~35%) of that gap. Please standardize on absolute mAP, and, if reporting “gap closed,” define the denominator and report both absolute and relative gains.
- Fair comparison in Figure 3a: Clearly mark which competing methods use category-level detection labels, region proposals, or background modeling so the reader can assess the fairness of comparisons to LSDA and to R-CNN.
- Relation to R-CNN: Provide a concise table or subsection that specifies training data requirements, adaptation steps, background modeling, use of SVMs/bbox regression, runtime, and mAP for R-CNN vs LSDA. This would be more informative than the qualitative Figure 6; consider removing Figure 6 to make space.
- Category-specific transfer: Nearest neighbors are defined in fc8 weight space with Euclidean distance. Have you tried alternative similarity measures (e.g., WordNet/Tree distance, confusion-based similarity, features from earlier layers)? Please report sensitivity to k and to the similarity metric beyond the average δ.
- Scope of evaluation: Results for the seen categories (B) after adaptation would be helpful to confirm no degradation and to quantify the net benefit across all K classes. Also, can LSDA’s adaptation be combined with the SVM/bbox regression steps without losing flexibility, and what is the resulting mAP?
- Weakly supervised baselines: While MIL methods have not been evaluated at ImageNet scale in your paper, a discussion or small-scale comparison on a subset would help position LSDA relative to that line of work.

Minor issues and editorial suggestions
- Naming: The method is referred to as LSDA; ensure consistent use throughout. Avoid mixing “DNN” vs “DDA.”
- Typo: “classi?ers” on line 431.
- Clarify hardware and exact settings for the “fast detector” results (fps, GPU/CPU, image resolution, proposal count).
- In Section 3.3, explicitly state that the background subtraction is applied to logits or softmax scores, and justify the choice.

Overall evaluation
This is a high-quality, clearly written paper that addresses an important practical problem with a simple and well-validated approach. The main technical novelty is moderate, but the framing, analysis, and large-scale demonstration make it a worthwhile contribution. With improved clarity in reporting, a more explicit comparison to R-CNN variants, and better positioning of the headline results, the paper would be stronger.

Recommendation: Weak accept.
