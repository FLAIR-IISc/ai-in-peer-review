This paper proposes a novel framework for adapting image-classification-trained deep neural networks for object detection tasks. The Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA) algorithm aims to bridge the gap between classification and detection by learning the transformation from classification to detection on a subset of categories and transferring this knowledge to other classifiers. The authors evaluate their method on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge and demonstrate a 50% relative improvement in mean Average Precision (mAP) over a baseline classification-only network.

The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and clearly presents the LSDA algorithm. The authors provide a thorough analysis of their method, including an ablation study to evaluate the importance of each component. The results show that adapting the feature representation is the most crucial step in the algorithm, while adapting the category-specific parameters has a smaller impact.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to address a significant challenge in object detection, namely the need for large amounts of labeled data. The authors demonstrate that their method can be used to produce a high-quality detector for tens of thousands of categories, which is a significant contribution to the field.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The main contribution of the algorithm, the adaptation method, lacks principled justification and detailed motivation. The authors do not provide a clear explanation for why the adapted detection weights are computed as Wj^c + W_avg, and the physical meaning of the "offset" parameter is unclear. Additionally, the authors do not provide a clear explanation for why the kNN variant outperforms other variants.

Furthermore, the comparisons in the paper are not entirely fair. The authors use part of the validation set instead of the test set, which may not accurately reflect the performance of the algorithm on unseen data. Additionally, the claim that R-CNN has similar performance on val2 is not statistically justified due to the small size of val2.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques. The authors build on the work of Girshick et al. (R-CNN) and adapt it to the object detection task. However, the paper does not provide a significant departure from previous work in terms of the underlying architecture or the adaptation method.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a significant challenge in object detection and demonstrates a significant improvement in performance. The results have the potential to impact the field of computer vision, particularly in applications where large amounts of labeled data are not available.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of object detection. While there are some limitations to the paper, the authors demonstrate a novel and effective method for adapting image-classification-trained deep neural networks for object detection tasks.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and effective method for adapting image-classification-trained deep neural networks for object detection tasks.
* The authors demonstrate a significant improvement in performance on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge.
* The paper addresses a significant challenge in object detection, namely the need for large amounts of labeled data.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The main contribution of the algorithm lacks principled justification and detailed motivation.
* The comparisons in the paper are not entirely fair.
* The paper does not provide a significant departure from previous work in terms of the underlying architecture or the adaptation method.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Provide a clear explanation for the adaptation method and the physical meaning of the "offset" parameter.
* Use the test set instead of the validation set for comparisons.
* Provide a more detailed analysis of the results, including a breakdown of the errors and a discussion of the limitations of the method.
* Consider providing additional experiments to demonstrate the robustness of the method to different architectures and datasets.
