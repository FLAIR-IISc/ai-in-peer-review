This paper tackles the problem of scaling object detection when only a small subset of categories have bounding-box annotations. The authors propose LSDA, a two-stage adaptation of a CNN trained for image-level classification into a detector: (1) a category-invariant adaptation learned by fine-tuning layers 1–7 on categories with detection labels (set B), including a learned background class; and (2) a category-specific adaptation for held-out categories (set A) by adding to each classifier’s output weights an “offset” equal to the average change observed for the k nearest neighbors in set B (nearest in fc8 space). Detection scores combine per-class logits with a background logit. On ILSVRC-2013 detection, the method yields a 50% relative mAP gain over a classification-only baseline for held-out categories, enables a 7.6K-category detector using only classification labels for most classes, and includes a faster variant using SPP with ~2 fps for the large model. Relative to prior work, LSDA complements MIL approaches for weak supervision and differs from supervised domain adaptation methods that require detection labels for all classes; compared to R-CNN, LSDA aims to transfer the classification-to-detection transformation learned on a subset to categories without boxes.

Quality
- Empirically, the ablations are informative: background modeling and category-invariant fine-tuning provide the largest gains; kNN-based category-specific adaptation adds a smaller but statistically significant improvement (reported p=0.017). Error analysis shows a reduction in localization and background confusions, consistent with the method’s goals.
- However, some core design choices lack principled justification. In Section 3.2, the adaptation Wj^d = Wj^c + Δj (approximated by averaging δ over k nearest neighbors) is presented without a theoretical rationale. The “offset” δ is not clearly interpreted in optimization terms (e.g., as the result of fine-tuning under a local linearization), nor is its distribution analyzed to support averaging.
- The superiority of kNN averaging over other variants is not explained beyond empirical observation. It is unclear why fc8-nearest neighbors should be the right similarity metric, how k is chosen, or how sensitive results are to the metric or k.
- Comparisons are not fully controlled. Results rely on a val1/val2 split; statements about ranking (e.g., 4th) and about R-CNN achieving similar performance on val2 are hard to validate without test-set evaluation and appropriate statistical tests. Given val2’s modest size, claims of parity with R-CNN and challenge rankings should be tempered or supported by confidence intervals or bootstrap tests.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and easy to follow, with a straightforward architecture that is simple to implement in Caffe. The contributions are enumerated and the experimental setup is described in detail.
- Section 3.2 would benefit from a clearer explanation of δ: what “physical” meaning does it have, why should it transfer across semantically/visually related classes, and why is additive adaptation well motivated? A brief derivation (e.g., first-order Taylor expansion of fine-tuning around W^c) or a regularization view (learning Δ with a prior toward zero) would help.
- The rationale for choosing fc8 as the neighbor space, the value of k, and the distance metric should be explicitly justified. An analysis of sensitivity to k and alternative neighbor definitions (e.g., semantic distance in WordNet, distances in earlier layers) would strengthen the story.

Originality
- Adapting a classifier to a detector by learning a category-invariant transformation plus a light category-specific correction for held-out categories is novel relative to supervised detector adaptation (which assumes detection labels for all classes) and complements weakly supervised MIL approaches. The explicit background modeling and the transfer of the classification-to-detection “delta” are interesting ideas.
- While the additive offset is a reasonable heuristic, the lack of a more principled derivation weakens the conceptual contribution.

Significance
- The approach is practically significant: it reduces the need for bounding boxes to a small subset of categories, scales to 7.6K detectors, and provides speedups suitable for large-scale deployment. The 50% relative mAP gain over a strong classification baseline on held-out classes is meaningful.
- Nonetheless, the gap to fully supervised detection remains large, and some comparative claims rely on validation splits rather than the test set, limiting the conclusiveness of state-of-the-art comparisons.

Pros
- Clear, well-organized paper; method is simple and practical; code/models released.
- Strong empirical gains on held-out categories; informative ablations and error diagnostics.
- Scales to thousands of categories; includes a faster variant.

Cons
- Insufficiently justified key design (W^d = W^c + Δ via kNN averaging); unclear interpretation of δ.
- Limited analysis of why kNN in fc8 space works and how k is chosen.
- Comparisons rely on val splits; claims about ranking and parity with R-CNN are not statistically substantiated on the official test set.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide a principled motivation for the additive offset: e.g., view fine-tuning as learning Δ under L2 regularization; show that Δ is approximately low-norm or low-rank; visualize Δ distributions and correlations with class similarity.
- Analyze neighbor selection: report performance vs k, try alternative distances (cosine, earlier-layer features), and semantic neighbors (WordNet). Is performance correlated with visual/semantic proximity?
- Calibrate statistical claims: include confidence intervals/bootstraps; if possible, evaluate on the official test set to support ranking/comparison statements.
- Clarify the effect of each component when controlling for training data size (to disentangle benefits of background modeling vs additional supervision).
- Consider reporting results on PASCAL VOC or a subset overlapping with prior MIL methods to facilitate cross-paradigm comparisons.

Overall evaluation
A solid and timely contribution with clear practical value and good empirical evidence on held-out categories. I recommend acceptance if the authors can better justify the category-specific adaptation (both conceptually and empirically) and temper or strengthen comparative claims with more rigorous evaluation.
