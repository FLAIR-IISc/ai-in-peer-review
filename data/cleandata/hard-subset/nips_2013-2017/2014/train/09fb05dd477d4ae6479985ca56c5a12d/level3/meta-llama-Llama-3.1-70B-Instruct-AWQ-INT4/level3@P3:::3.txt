The paper "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation" proposes a novel approach to adapt a Krizhevsky CNN trained for classification to detection tasks. The method involves three stages: (1) classification training on all classes, (2) training on region proposals for annotated classes with a background class, and (3) adding the background class and adapting some CNN weights. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge, achieving a 50% relative mAP boost over a baseline of just using raw classifier weights on object proposal regions.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' ideas and understand their approach. The authors provide a thorough analysis of their method, including an ablation study to evaluate the importance of each component of their algorithm. The results are impressive, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the types of errors that their system makes on the held-out object categories.

One of the strengths of the paper is its simplicity and logical approach. The authors build on the strength of the Krizhevsky model, which has been shown to be effective for image classification tasks. By adapting this model to detection tasks, the authors demonstrate the potential of their approach to enable detection for tens of thousands of categories that lack bounding box annotations.

However, the paper lacks comparisons to some obvious baselines that could challenge its claims. For example, the authors do not compare their method to a full-image positives with region negatives baseline, a MIL-based region selection baseline, or a center/mean-box classifier baseline. These comparisons would be useful to evaluate the effectiveness of the authors' approach and to demonstrate its advantages over other methods.

Additionally, the authors' attribution of the results to their method rather than to the strong CNN features is a concern. The authors acknowledge that the Krizhevsky model is a strong classifier, and it is possible that the results are due to the features learned by this model rather than the adaptation method itself. Further analysis would be necessary to fully understand the contributions of the adaptation method.

In terms of originality, the paper is sufficiently novel for acceptance, but it is relatively specific to certain CNN architectures. The authors build on existing work, but their approach is not highly innovative. However, the problem and results are significant and valuable for the community, and the paper has the potential to make a substantial impact in the field of object detection.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of their method. However, the lack of comparisons to some obvious baselines and the attribution concern are limitations that should be addressed. With some additional analysis and comparisons, the paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to adapt a Krizhevsky CNN trained for classification to detection tasks.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge, achieving a 50% relative mAP boost over a baseline.
* The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' ideas and understand their approach.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of their method, including an ablation study to evaluate the importance of each component of their algorithm.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper lacks comparisons to some obvious baselines that could challenge its claims.
* The authors' attribution of the results to their method rather than to the strong CNN features is a concern.
* The paper is relatively specific to certain CNN architectures, and the approach is not highly innovative.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Compare the authors' method to some obvious baselines, such as a full-image positives with region negatives baseline, a MIL-based region selection baseline, or a center/mean-box classifier baseline.
* Provide additional analysis to fully understand the contributions of the adaptation method and to address the attribution concern.
* Consider evaluating the authors' approach on other datasets or tasks to demonstrate its generalizability and robustness.
