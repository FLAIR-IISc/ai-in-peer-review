## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper proposes Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a method for adapting image classifiers to object detectors, leveraging the abundance of image-level labels while mitigating the scarcity of bounding box annotations. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain (image-level labels) to the detection domain (bounding box labels) using a CNN architecture, enabling detection for categories lacking bounding box data. The authors evaluate their approach on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge, demonstrating significant improvements over a baseline and showcasing a 7.6K detector.

The paper builds upon the recent success of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for both classification [4] and detection [1], recognizing the substantial imbalance in available labeled data.  Specifically, it acknowledges the strong performance achieved through pre-training on large classification datasets like ImageNet [3], but highlights the data bottleneck for object detection.  The work is related to, and differentiates itself from, several existing approaches.  It contrasts with the R-CNN approach [1] by requiring bounding box data for only a subset of categories, and explores connections to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) [6, 7] while emphasizing its evaluation on the large-scale ImageNet challenge.  Domain adaptation techniques [12, 13, 14, 15, 16] are also discussed, but the authors highlight the novelty of adapting both the visual domain and the task itself (classification to detection).

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical problem:** The scarcity of labeled bounding box data is a major obstacle to scaling object detection. LSDA directly addresses this challenge by effectively leveraging readily available image-level annotations.
* **Strong experimental results:** The reported 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline suggests a significant contribution. The demonstration of a 7.6K detector, even with limited data for many categories, is impressive.
* **Clear and logical approach:** The proposed three-stage adaptation process – category invariant adaptation, category specific adaptation, and detection – is well-motivated and conceptually sound.
* **Practical contribution:** The release of models and software (lsda.berkeleyvision.org) facilitates reproducibility and encourages further research.
* **Fast Detector option:** The ability to modify the architecture for fast detection (2fps) demonstrates a practical consideration for real-time applications.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited baseline comparisons:** The paper lacks comparisons to several crucial baselines. Specifically, the reviewer notes the absence of comparison with (1) using full-image positives and region proposals as negatives, (2) MIL-based region selection methods, and (3) a simple “mean-box” classifier baseline, which would clarify how difficult the task genuinely is. The results could potentially be attributed to the inherent power of the underlying CNN features rather than a uniquely effective adaptation strategy.
* **Insufficient clarity in experimental details:** The explanation of the different methods presented in Table 1 is unclear. The reviewer requests more details about which equations or layers are applied in each configuration, making it difficult to assess the specific contributions of each adaptation component.
* **Incremental originality:** While the approach is novel, it builds heavily on existing CNN architectures and adaptation techniques. It doesn’t represent a radical departure from existing work, but rather a clever application of existing tools to a specific problem.
* **Potential bias towards CNN features:** The strong dependence on the Krizhevsky et al. [4] CNN raises concerns that the results might be heavily influenced by the quality of these features, rather than the adaptation algorithm itself. A more thorough evaluation with alternative CNN architectures would strengthen the findings.


**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant challenge in scaling object detection, with potential impact on the wider computer vision community.
* The experimental results demonstrate a meaningful improvement over existing methods, and the 7.6K detector is a valuable contribution.
* The method is conceptually simple, well-motivated, and practical.
* The authors have made their code and models publicly available, promoting reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of comparison to key baselines weakens the claims of superiority.
* The lack of clarity in the experimental section hinders a complete understanding of the results.
* The strong reliance on pre-trained CNN features raises concerns about the independent contribution of the adaptation algorithm.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that addresses an important problem with a reasonable and promising approach. However, the lack of thorough baseline comparisons and clarity in the experimental details raise concerns about the strength of the claims.  I believe the paper is currently borderline. **I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.**  Specifically, the authors should include comparisons to the aforementioned baselines, provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup in Table 1, and discuss the potential influence of the pre-trained CNN features on the results.  Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field.
