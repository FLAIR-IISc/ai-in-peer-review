This paper proposes Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a method for adapting pre-trained CNN image classifiers into object detectors. The key idea is to learn a general transformation from classification to detection using a subset of categories that have bounding box annotations, and then apply this transformation to the many categories that only have image-level labels. This approach frames the problem as a domain adaptation task, building on prior work like R-CNN but aiming to circumvent the need for exhaustive bounding box annotation.

The paper’s primary strengths lie in its focus on a significant, practical problem and the simplicity of its proposed solution. Scaling object detection to tens of thousands of categories is a major challenge, and the authors present a logical, intuitive three-stage adaptation process. The results are compelling, demonstrating a significant mAP improvement over a simple classifier baseline, and the work will likely be valuable to the computer vision community. The paper is also well-written and easy to follow.

However, the experimental evaluation has several weaknesses that temper the significance of the results. The most critical issue is the absence of crucial baseline comparisons that would properly contextualize the method's performance. The authors should compare LSDA against at least three strong alternatives: (1) a model trained on full-image positives and region-proposal negatives, (2) a standard Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) method for weakly-supervised region selection, and (3) a simple center/mean-box baseline to gauge the dataset's intrinsic difficulty. Without these comparisons, it is difficult to ascertain whether the proposed adaptation scheme is superior to existing, simpler weakly-supervised techniques.

A related concern is that the strong results may be more attributable to the power of the underlying Krizhevsky CNN features than to the novelty of the adaptation method itself. Finally, while the paper is generally clear, the experiments section could be improved. Specifically, Table 1 should be clarified to explicitly link each result row to the specific components of the model (e.g., equations, adapted layers) being evaluated.

In summary, this paper presents a sufficiently novel and significant contribution for acceptance. It tackles an important problem with a simple method and promising results. However, the claims would be much stronger if the authors addressed the missing baselines in their evaluation.
