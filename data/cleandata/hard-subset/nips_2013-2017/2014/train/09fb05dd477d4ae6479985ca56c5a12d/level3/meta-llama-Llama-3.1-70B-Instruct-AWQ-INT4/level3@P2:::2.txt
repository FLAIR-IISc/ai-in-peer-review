This paper presents a novel approach to object detection, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), which adapts image-classification-trained deep neural networks for object detection. The authors address the challenge of scaling object detection to large numbers of categories by leveraging image-level labels, which are easier to obtain than bounding box annotations. The proposed method learns a transformation from classification to detection using a subset of categories with bounding box annotations and applies this transformation to adapt classifiers for categories without bounding box annotations.

The paper relates to previous work on object detection, including the R-CNN model, which also adapts an ImageNet classifier into a detector. However, LSDA differs in that it does not require bounding box data for all categories and can adapt classifiers for categories without any bounding box annotated data. The authors also draw connections to domain adaptation methods, which aim to reduce dataset bias caused by differences in statistical distributions between training and test domains.

The paper is well-written, and the CNN architecture is interesting and simple to implement. The overall quality of the paper is good, and the evaluation on ImageNet LSVRC-2013 demonstrates the efficacy of the approach. The authors achieve a 50% relative mAP boost over a baseline of just using raw classifier weights on object proposal regions.

However, the main contribution of the paper, the adaptation method, lacks principled justification and detailed motivation. The authors do not provide a clear explanation for why the adapted detection weights are computed as Wj^c + W_avg in Section 3.2, and the physical meaning of the "offset" parameter is unclear. Additionally, the authors do not provide a clear explanation for why the kNN variant outperforms other variants.

The comparisons presented in the paper are also unfair because the authors used part of the validation set instead of the test set. Furthermore, the claim that R-CNN has similar performance on val2 is not statistically justified due to val2's small size.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the adaptation method and its components. The authors could also provide more insights into why the method works and how it can be improved.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the architecture is easy to follow. However, some sections, such as Section 3.2, could benefit from more detailed explanations and justifications.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to object detection, and the adaptation method is a significant contribution to the field. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the method differs from previous work and how it can be applied to other tasks.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a significant challenge in object detection, and the proposed method has the potential to enable detection for tens of thousands of categories. The evaluation on ImageNet LSVRC-2013 demonstrates the efficacy of the approach, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the results.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach to object detection that addresses a significant challenge in the field.
* The proposed method achieves a 50% relative mAP boost over a baseline of just using raw classifier weights on object proposal regions.
* The paper is well-written, and the architecture is easy to follow.
* The authors provide a detailed analysis of the results and discuss the limitations of the method.

* The claim that R-CNN has similar performance on val2 is not statistically justified due to val2's small size.
