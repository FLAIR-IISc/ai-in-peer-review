Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes LSDA, a practical framework to adapt a deep CNN trained for image-level classification into an object detector, even for categories with no bounding-box annotations. The approach has three components: (1) learn a background class and fine-tune shared layers on the subset of categories with detection labels (category-invariant adaptation), (2) adjust category-specific output weights for categories with detection labels, and (3) approximate category-specific adaptation for categories without detection labels by adding to the classification weights an average “delta” computed from k nearest neighbor categories (based on fc8 weight similarity) that do have detection labels. At test time, detection scores are computed as class score minus background score. The method yields a 50% relative mAP boost over a classification-only baseline on held-out categories and is demonstrated at scale with a 7.6K-category detector and a faster SPP/proposal variant. The authors relate their work to R-CNN (which requires detection labels for all categories), weakly supervised MIL approaches, and supervised domain adaptation for detection, arguing novelty in adapting to categories with no detection data.

Relative to prior work at NIPS and elsewhere, the paper leverages the now-standard strategy of pretraining on ImageNet classification and fine-tuning for detection (R-CNN, OverFeat), but adds a specific adaptation mechanism to bridge classification-to-detection for unseen categories. The idea of learning background and shared feature adaptation is consistent with domain adaptation literature, while the kNN-based category-specific delta is a simple heuristic not previously evaluated at this scale.

Quality
Strengths:
- Solid empirical improvements on held-out categories (+50% relative mAP), clear ablations that isolate the impact of background modeling and feature-level fine-tuning versus category-specific adaptation.
- Sensible error analysis showing reductions in localization and background confusions, aligning with the paper’s motivation.
- Demonstration of large-scale feasibility (7.6K categories) and a faster variant; code/models released.

Weaknesses/concerns:
- The category-specific adaptation formulation Wd = Wc + δ lacks a principled justification. The “offset” δ and its interpretation are not well motivated beyond empirical utility. Why is additive transfer appropriate, and why at the final layer only?
- The choice of nearest neighbors via L2 distance on normalized fc8 weights and averaging their δ is heuristic. No analysis of alternative metrics (e.g., cosine on features, taxonomy/WordNet similarity, confusion-based similarity), different k, or robustness to k is provided. The observation that kNN outperforms other variants is not explained.
- Comparisons are not strictly apples-to-apples. Much of the analysis uses a train/val split (train + val1 for fine-tuning; val2 for evaluation). Claims of ranking “4th” on the challenge leaderboard and parity with R-CNN on val2 are hard to substantiate without test server results under the same training protocol as competing methods. The small size of val2 undermines strong statistical claims; only one paired t-test is reported, and not for the cross-method comparisons.
- The method omits SVMs and bbox regression for convenience; while understandable for scalability, it complicates fairness when comparing to systems that include them. An apples-to-apples R-CNN baseline that uses the same omissions would help.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, well-organized, and easy to follow. Section 3.1 is precise and the ablations are informative. However:
- Section 3.2 needs a clearer derivation and motivation for the additive δ parameterization, its “physical meaning,” and why adaptation should be linear at the classifier layer. A schematic or small theoretical argument (e.g., first-order Taylor approximation of fine-tuning around Wc) would help.
- The rationale for kNN averaging and the observed superiority of a particular k needs explanation, with sensitivity analysis.
- The discussion of leaderboard placement and “similar performance” claims should explicitly state the training splits used by all methods and avoid overstating conclusions drawn from val2.

Originality
The category-invariant adaptation via background learning and shared-layer fine-tuning is close to R-CNN but used here to enable transfer to unseen categories. The kNN δ-averaging to synthesize detector weights for categories lacking detection labels is a simple, pragmatic idea; while heuristic, adapting to held-out categories without detection data is novel among supervised adaptation methods cited. Overall this is an incremental but useful contribution.

Significance
Scaling detection to thousands of categories without bounding boxes is important. The demonstrated gains on held-out categories and the released large-scale models make the work potentially impactful for practitioners. However, the lack of rigorous comparative evaluation on a common test protocol and limited justification for the key adaptation step temper the strength of the claims.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Provide a principled justification for Wd = Wc + δ. For example, show that fine-tuning the last layer with small steps leads to an additive update approximable by averaging neighbor updates; or derive from a multi-task regularizer that encourages proximity to Wc.
- Analyze sensitivity to k, similarity metrics, and neighbor selection strategies (taxonomy, semantic embeddings, feature-space neighbors, confusion matrices). Report per-class improvements versus semantic proximity to neighbors.
- Report test server results using a training protocol comparable to baselines (or retrain baselines on your split). If not possible, temper leaderboard claims and emphasize val2-only comparisons with confidence intervals.
- Include an ablation that adds bbox regression and/or SVMs to LSDA to assess whether the adaptation still helps when stronger heads are used.
- Clarify whether background subtraction (scorei − scorebg) is calibrated across categories; consider temperature scaling or Platt scaling to mitigate label imbalance across seen/unseen categories.
- Discuss failure modes where Oth errors are confusions with near-synonyms; could hierarchical NMS or taxonomy-aware rescoring help?

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Addresses an important and underexplored problem: detection without bbox labels for most categories.
- Method is simple, scalable, and shows meaningful gains on held-out categories.
- Clear ablations and error analysis; code/models released; large-scale demo.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Key adaptation step (δ and kNN averaging) is heuristic and insufficiently justified.
- Limited analysis of why kNN works and how to choose k/metric; no alternative similarity criteria evaluated.
- Comparisons rely on val splits; leaderboard/test-set claims are not strictly comparable; statistical support is thin.

Recommendation
Borderline. I lean weak reject in its current form due to the lack of principled motivation for the core adaptation step and fairness/rigor of comparisons, but I find the problem and empirical gains compelling. Strengthening the justification and evaluation could move this to a clear accept.
