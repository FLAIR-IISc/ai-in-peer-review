## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper tackles the significant challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, acknowledging the bottleneck of bounding box annotation. The core idea – adapting classifiers trained on abundant image-level labels to perform detection – is well-motivated and directly addresses a practical limitation in the field. The approach, LSDA, learns a transformation between classification and detection tasks and leverages this knowledge to enable detection in categories lacking bounding box data. This builds upon previous work adapting classifiers for detection (Girshick et al. R-CNN) but explicitly focuses on zero-shot transfer to entirely unseen categories.

The algorithm itself is straightforward and elegantly presented. The experimental setup, training on 100 categories with boxes/labels and testing on 100 held-out categories with labels only, is a strong evaluation protocol. The results demonstrate a substantial improvement – filling approximately 35% of the gap between oracle performance and a baseline using raw classifier weights – showcasing the effectiveness of the adaptation strategy. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to understand the proposed method and experimental setup. The authors’ commitment to reproducibility, with released models and code, is commendable.

However, while novel and interesting, the originality isn’t groundbreaking. It combines established adaptation ideas in a focused manner, rather than presenting a fundamentally new technique. The significance is also somewhat unclear. While the contribution is valuable, the trade-off between reducing the annotation burden (fewer boxes) and the resulting decrease in mAP isn’t thoroughly investigated. It’s difficult to judge if the gains from reduced annotation cost outweigh the performance drop. Furthermore, the long-term importance of the problem is questionable, as the authors acknowledge; increased resource investment may eventually alleviate the bounding box scarcity issue.

Several points require clarification. Figure 3a needs to explicitly state which comparison methods utilize category labels for detection, ensuring a fair comparison. The main result should be highlighted as the DNN performance increase in Figure 3b (the ~35% gap filling), rather than the potentially misleading claims of 78% and 50% improvement. A dedicated table or section detailing the differences between LSDA and R-CNN would be highly beneficial, potentially replacing Figure 6 to save space. Minor issues include inconsistent terminology (“DNN” vs. “DDA”) and a typo on line 431 (“classi?ers”).

**Arguments for acceptance:** Addresses a real-world problem, presents a clear and effective solution, well-written and reproducible.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Incremental innovation, unclear trade-off between annotation cost and performance, potential for the problem to diminish with resource investment.

**Overall:** I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions addressing the points above. The paper represents a solid contribution with practical relevance and a high level of technical quality.
