- Paper addresses scaling object detection when most classes lack bounding-boxes by adapting a classifier to a detector via a category-invariant fine-tuning and a kNN-based category-specific offset (LSDA).  
- Empirically LSDA gives a ~50% relative mAP gain on held-out categories and enables a 7.6K-category detector; includes a faster SPP variant (~2 fps).  
- Ablations show background modeling and category-invariant fine-tuning provide the largest gains; kNN-based category-specific adaptation gives a smaller but statistically significant improvement (p=0.017).  
- Error analysis indicates reduced localization and background confusions, consistent with the method’s goals.  
- The additive adaptation W^d = W^c + Δ is presented without principled theoretical justification or an optimization interpretation.  
- The paper does not analyze the distribution or properties of Δ to support averaging or transferability across classes.  
- The choice of fc8 for nearest neighbors, the kNN averaging superiority, selection of k, and sensitivity to metric/k are not explained or analyzed.  
- Comparisons rely on val1/val2 splits and small val2 size; claims about ranking and parity with R-CNN lack test-set validation and statistical rigor.  
- Paper is clearly written, easy to follow, and straightforward to implement in Caffe; experimental setup is well described.  
- Section 3.2 needs clearer explanation of δ’s meaning and motivation; a derivation (e.g., linearization) or regularization view would help.  
- The rationale for choosing fc8 neighbor space and k should be justified and sensitivity analyses added.  
- The adaptation idea (category-invariant transform plus light category-specific correction) and explicit background modeling are novel and interesting.  
- The additive offset remains a heuristic without a principled derivation, weakening the conceptual contribution.  
- The approach is practically significant for reducing bounding-box annotation needs and scaling detection, but the gap to fully supervised detectors remains.  
- Some comparative claims are limited by validation-only evaluation, reducing conclusiveness about state-of-the-art standing.  
- Pros: clear organization, practical and simple method, released code/models, strong empirical gains, scalable to thousands of classes, and a faster variant.  
- Cons: insufficient justification for the kNN additive offset and Δ interpretation, limited analysis of neighbor selection, and reliance on validation splits for comparisons.  
- Suggested experiments: provide principled motivation for the additive offset, show Δ norms/structure or low-rank behavior, and visualize Δ distributions and correlations with class similarity.  
- Suggested analyses: report performance vs k, try alternative distances (cosine, earlier-layer features), and semantic neighbors (WordNet); test correlation with visual/semantic proximity.  
- Statistical suggestions: include confidence intervals or bootstrap tests and evaluate on the official test set if possible to substantiate ranking/comparison claims.  
- Clarify component effects while controlling for training data size to disentangle background modeling vs added supervision benefits.  
- Consider reporting results on PASCAL VOC or overlapping subsets for easier comparison with prior MIL and weakly supervised methods.  
- Overall: a solid, timely, and practically valuable contribution recommended for acceptance if the authors better justify the category-specific adaptation conceptually and empirically and strengthen comparative/statistical claims.
