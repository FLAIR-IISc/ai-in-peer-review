This paper addresses the important and practical problem of scaling object detection to a large number of categories for which bounding box annotations are unavailable. The core idea is to treat the transformation of an image classifier into an object detector as a domain adaptation problem. The proposed algorithm, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), leverages a set of categories with full bounding box supervision to learn a general transformation that can then be applied to classifiers for held-out categories that only have image-level labels. The method first fine-tunes the shared feature-extraction layers of a pre-trained CNN on the supervised categories to learn a category-invariant mapping from classification features to detection features. This step also learns a generic "background" model. Second, it learns a category-specific adaptation for the final classification layer by observing the changes in classifier weights for the supervised categories and transferring this change (via a nearest-neighbor approach) to the held-out categories. This work is clearly inspired by R-CNN, which demonstrated the power of fine-tuning for detection, but LSDA aims to remove the requirement of bounding box data for every target category.

**Strengths**

*   **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and highly relevant problem. The prohibitive cost of collecting bounding box annotations is a major bottleneck for scaling object detection to tens of thousands of categories, whereas image-level labels are far more abundant. This work offers a pragmatic approach to bridging this gap.
*   **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is of high quality, exceptionally well-written, and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the proposed method is described logically, and the experiments are well-designed. The core experimental setup—training on 100 categories with bounding boxes and evaluating on 100 held-out categories with only image-level labels—is a clean and convincing way to test the paper's central claim.
*   **Simplicity and Elegance:** The proposed algorithm is simple, intuitive, and straightforward. The decomposition into category-invariant and category-specific adaptation makes sense and leads to an effective, multi-stage training procedure.
*   **Strong Results:** The experiments demonstrate a clear and significant improvement over the baseline of using an unmodified classifier for detection. The main result, closing approximately 35% of the performance gap between the baseline and a fully supervised oracle on held-out categories, is a solid contribution and validates the effectiveness of the adaptation approach.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Originality:** The work presents a novel and interesting combination of familiar techniques. The idea of fine-tuning a CNN for detection is from R-CNN, and the concept of learning transformations between domains is central to domain adaptation. The main novelty lies in learning this transformation on one set of classes and applying it to another, held-out set of classes. While this is a clever and useful contribution, it feels more like an incremental step than a fundamentally new direction.
*   **Long-term Significance:** A key question is the long-term importance of the problem itself. While bounding-box scarcity is a major issue today, it is possible that with sufficient investment in data collection platforms (e.g., crowdsourcing, active learning, synthetic data), this problem may be largely solved in the future. The paper would be stronger if it discussed the practical trade-off more explicitly: how much annotation effort is saved for what quantifiable cost in mean Average Precision (mAP)? This analysis would help practitioners and researchers better judge the utility and applicability of this method.
*   **Clarity of Results and Comparisons:**
    *   The abstract and Section 4.2 highlight a "50% relative mAP boost". While technically correct, this can be slightly misleading as relative improvements on low baselines can overstate the practical impact. The most informative result is the absolute performance shown in Figure 3b and Table 1 (e.g., improving from ~10% to ~16% mAP), which corresponds to closing about one-third of the gap to the fully supervised oracle. I would recommend emphasizing this "gap-closing" perspective as it provides a more calibrated view of the method's performance.
    *   In Figure 3a, which compares LSDA to other methods, it is not immediately clear which of the baseline methods (besides R-CNN) also utilize the image-level category labels for the held-out categories during training. For the comparison to be fair, all methods should have access to the same sources of information. The authors should explicitly clarify this in the figure caption or text.
*   **Clarity of Method vs. R-CNN:** The relationship to R-CNN is fundamental to this work. While the text explains the differences, the paper would benefit immensely from a dedicated table or subsection that explicitly itemizes the distinctions between LSDA and R-CNN (e.g., direct use of softmax scores vs. training separate SVMs, no bounding box regression, and the core adaptation mechanism for unseen classes). This would provide much-needed clarity for readers. To make space, the authors might consider removing Figure 6, as the qualitative examples are less informative than a direct, quantitative comparison of the system architectures.

**Minor Issues:**

*   The naming for the method and its components appears inconsistent between the text, which uses "LSDA," and the figures/tables, which seem to use abbreviations like "DNN" and "DDA." Standardizing the terminology throughout the manuscript would improve readability.
*   There is a typo on line 431: "classi?ers" should be "classifiers".
