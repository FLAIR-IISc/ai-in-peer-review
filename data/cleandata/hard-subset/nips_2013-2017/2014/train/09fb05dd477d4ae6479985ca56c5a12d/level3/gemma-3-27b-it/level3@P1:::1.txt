## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper addresses the crucial challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, a task hindered by the scarcity of bounding box annotations. The authors propose Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a method for transforming classifiers trained on image-level labels into detectors. The core idea is to learn the transformation between the classification and detection tasks on a subset of categories with bounding box data and then apply this learned transformation to classifiers of categories lacking such annotations. This approach leverages the abundance of image-level labeled data available from sources like search engines.

The work builds upon previous research in domain adaptation and transfer learning, particularly the use of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for both classification [Krizhevsky et al., 2012] and detection [Girshick et al., 2014].  The authors correctly highlight the breakthrough made by R-CNN, while noting its dependence on bounding box annotations for *all* categories. LSDA can be viewed as a natural extension, seeking to minimize this annotation bottleneck. Related work in Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) [Ali et al., 2012; Song et al., 2014] is acknowledged, but the authors position their method as complementary and evaluated on a larger scale (ImageNet).  The paper also briefly discusses connections to broader domain adaptation techniques [Kulis et al., 2011; Duan et al., 2014], but emphasizes the novelty of adapting both across visual domains *and* tasks.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** The scarcity of bounding box annotations is a significant bottleneck in scaling object detection. LSDA offers a practical approach to mitigate this issue.
* **Simple and effective:** The core idea of learning a transformation from classifiers to detectors is straightforward and conceptually sound.  The implementation appears relatively uncomplicated, which is a benefit for reproducibility.
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The explanations of the method and experimental setup are easy to follow. The figures are helpful in illustrating the core concepts.
* **Positive empirical results:** The experiments on the ImageNet detection task demonstrate a significant improvement (+50% relative mAP) over a baseline using raw classifier weights, and a substantial gain on held-out categories.
* **Publicly available code and models:** Releasing the code and models is a commendable practice that encourages reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Originality is incremental:** While the combination of existing ideas is sensible, the paper doesn’t present a truly novel architectural breakthrough. It builds upon established techniques, rather than introducing fundamentally new concepts.
* **Significance is debatable:** The improvement on held-out categories, while statistically significant, is not enormous. The trade-off between the reduction in annotation effort and the resulting mAP performance is not clearly quantified.  The long-term significance of this work hinges on whether bounding box annotation costs remain prohibitive, as increased resource investment could lead to more fully annotated datasets.
* **Misleading performance claims:** The claims of "78%" and "50% relative mAP" in the abstract are based on misleading comparisons (discussed below).
* **Lack of detailed comparison with R-CNN:** The differences between LSDA and R-CNN are not comprehensively delineated. A dedicated table or section comparing the two methods more explicitly would be beneficial.  Removing Figure 6 would provide space for this.
* **Minor technical issues:** There's some inconsistency in terminology ("DNN" vs. "DDA") and a minor typo (line 431: "classi?ers").

**Specific concerns and suggestions:**

* **Figure 3a:** Clarify in Figure 3a which methods, beyond LSDA and R-CNN, utilize category labels for detection during training, to ensure a fair comparison.
* **Performance Claims:** The primary result should be the DNN performance in Figure 3b (showing approximately one-third of the gap between the baseline and oracle). The 78% and 50% claims are potentially misleading because they are not clearly presented within the context of the full performance spectrum.
* **R-CNN Comparison:**  Provide a detailed table outlining the differences between LSDA and R-CNN (e.g., annotation requirements, training procedure, computational cost).



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in the field of object detection.
* The proposed method is conceptually sound, relatively simple, and demonstrates a noticeable improvement over existing baselines.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and includes publicly available code and models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The originality of the work is incremental rather than groundbreaking.
* The reported performance gains, while statistically significant, are not dramatic.
* The paper lacks a detailed comparison with closely related work like R-CNN.
* Some of the performance claims are potentially misleading.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that presents a reasonable approach to a challenging problem. While the work doesn't represent a paradigm shift, it makes a valuable contribution to the field.  I believe the paper is **borderline accept**.  Addressing the concerns raised above, particularly clarifying the performance claims and providing a more comprehensive comparison with R-CNN, would significantly strengthen the submission.  If the authors can address these points, I would be inclined to recommend acceptance.
