This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, for which bounding box annotations are prohibitively expensive to collect. The authors propose an algorithm, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), which learns to transform an image classifier into an object detector. The core idea is to leverage a small set of categories with full bounding box supervision to learn a generic transformation that can then be applied to classifiers for which only image-level labels are available.

The method starts with a deep convolutional neural network (CNN), pre-trained for classification on ImageNet. This network is then fine-tuned on all target categories using image-level labels. The key step involves a second round of fine-tuning using region proposals for the subset of categories that have bounding box annotations. This step learns both a category-invariant transformation in the lower layers of the network (fc6, fc7) and a model for the "background" class. For categories without bounding box data, the adapted lower-layer features are used directly. The final classification layer is adapted by averaging the learned adaptations from its nearest neighbors in the annotated set. This approach builds upon the success of CNNs for detection, as seen in R-CNN, but crucially extends it to the weakly supervised setting for a majority of classes. Experiments on the ILSVRC 2013 detection dataset show a significant 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories compared to using the initial classifier without adaptation.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a problem of high practical and scientific importance. As the community moves towards ever-larger visual recognition systems, the bottleneck of acquiring detailed annotations like bounding boxes becomes increasingly severe. A method that can effectively leverage abundant image-level labels to create detectors is a very significant contribution.
*   **Novelty and Simplicity:** The approach of framing classifier-to-detector conversion as a domain adaptation problem is elegant and novel. The proposed three-step process is logical, simple to understand, and appears effective. The idea of learning a generic, category-invariant transformation is particularly insightful.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The 50% relative mAP boost on held-out categories is a compelling result that clearly demonstrates the value of the proposed adaptation technique. The error analysis in Figure 5 effectively shows that the method works as intended, primarily by reducing localization errors and confusion with the background—the key challenges in transitioning from classification to detection.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the core ideas are communicated clearly. The motivation is strong and the method is presented in a logical flow.

**Weaknesses**

The primary weakness of this paper lies in the experimental evaluation, which lacks several crucial baseline comparisons. This makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed adaptation method over simpler alternatives.

1.  **Missing Baselines:** The authors compare their method to a baseline of using the raw classifier network, but several other straightforward baselines could be strong competitors and are necessary for a complete evaluation:
    *   **Full-Image Positives + Region Negatives:** A simple approach would be to fine-tune the classifier by treating the full training images (for classes without bounding boxes) as positive examples and random region proposals from other images as negative examples. Given the power of the CNN features, this simple form of weak supervision might already perform very well.
    *   **MIL-based Region Selection:** The paper mentions Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) methods as related work but does not compare against them. A simple MIL baseline, where for each positive image the highest-scoring region proposal is treated as the positive instance during training, would be a highly relevant comparison. It is plausible that such a method could perform on par with or better than the proposed adaptation scheme.
    *   **Localization Context Baseline:** To better understand the difficulty of the dataset and the localization task, it would be useful to include a very simple baseline, such as classifying a single box at the image center or a box of average size/aspect ratio for each category. This helps contextualize the performance gains from localization.

2.  **Attribution of Performance:** The impressive results are built on the foundation of a very powerful pre-trained CNN (Krizhevsky et al.) and the R-CNN pipeline. A major concern is that the strong performance may be attributable more to the power of the deep features themselves than to the novelty of the adaptation method. Without the stronger baselines mentioned above, it is hard to disentangle the contribution of the LSDA adaptation from the baseline power of the underlying feature representation.

3.  **Clarity in Experiments:** While the paper is generally clear, the experimental results in Table 1 could be explained more thoroughly. It is not immediately obvious how each row (e.g., "Adapt fc6-7 + BG") maps to the specific architectural changes and training procedures described in Section 3. Explicitly linking each experiment to the components of the proposed model would improve reproducibility and understanding.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
*   The paper addresses a significant and timely problem in computer vision.
*   The proposed method is simple, elegant, and shows strong empirical performance gains.
*   The core idea of learning a transferable classifier-to-detector transformation is a valuable contribution that others are likely to build upon.
*   The work is sufficiently novel for a NIPS publication.

**Arguments against Acceptance**
*   The experimental validation is incomplete. The lack of stronger, obvious baselines makes it difficult to fairly evaluate the paper's central claim about the effectiveness of its adaptation method. The reported gains, while large, might be achievable with simpler weakly-supervised techniques.

**Recommendation**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution. The problem is important, the idea is clever, and the results are promising. However, the concerns about the missing baselines are significant. I lean towards acceptance but strongly urge the authors to include comparisons to the baselines mentioned above in the final version. Even if LSDA does not outperform all of them, the analysis would provide a much clearer picture of the method's strengths and weaknesses, making for a stronger scientific contribution. Addressing the clarity of Table 1 would also be a straightforward but useful improvement.
