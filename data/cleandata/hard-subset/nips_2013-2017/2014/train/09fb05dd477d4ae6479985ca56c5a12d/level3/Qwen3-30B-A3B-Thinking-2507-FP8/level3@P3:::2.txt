

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

This paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a method to transform image classification-trained CNNs into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The authors address the key challenge in scaling object detection: the scarcity of bounding box labeled data for large numbers of categories, while noting the abundance of image-level classification data. They frame the problem as domain adaptation from classification to detection, learning a transformation from the source domain (classification data) to the target domain (detection data) using a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations. The method has been evaluated on ImageNet LSVRC-2013, where it ranks 4th in the detection challenge, and enables a 7,600-category detector using only classification data for most categories.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates good technical soundness with strong experimental results showing a 50% relative mAP improvement over a classification-only baseline. The ablation study in Section 4.2 provides valuable insights into the importance of different adaptation components. However, the paper lacks principled justification for key methodological choices. The motivation for using Wj^c + W_avg in Section 3.2 is unclear, with no physical interpretation provided for the "offset" parameter. The authors also don't adequately explain why the kNN variant outperforms other variants, which weakens their claims about the method's effectiveness. Additionally, the evaluation protocol is problematic: the authors use part of the validation set (val2) for comparison rather than the official test set, and the val2 set is too small (20,000 images total, with half used for evaluation) to make statistically justified claims about RCNN performance.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors provide a good overview of related work, including domain adaptation techniques and previous approaches to weakly supervised object detection. The CNN architecture is simple to implement, and the method is described in sufficient detail for reproduction. The figures effectively illustrate key concepts, including the adaptation process and error analysis.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by addressing the problem of adapting classifiers to detectors for categories without bounding box annotations. While previous work (R-CNN) has shown how to adapt classification networks to detection, LSDA extends this to categories without any detection data. However, the approach is a straightforward application of domain adaptation concepts to a new problem, and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from related work on domain adaptation for detection (e.g., the methods by Aytar et al. cited in Section 2). The authors could have better positioned their contribution relative to existing domain adaptation literature.

**Significance:** The potential significance of the work is high, as it addresses a critical bottleneck in scaling object detection to large numbers of categories. The ability to produce a 7,600-category detector using only classification data represents a substantial step toward practical large-scale detection systems. However, the significance is somewhat diminished by the lack of fair comparison with state-of-the-art methods (using the official test set) and the absence of a thorough analysis of the method's limitations.

**Strengths:**
- Clear presentation and well-organized structure
- Practical approach with demonstrated success on ImageNet
- Simple architecture that's easy to implement
- Interesting extension of domain adaptation to the classification-to-detection task
- Validated on a large-scale benchmark with meaningful results

**Weaknesses:**
- Lack of principled justification for key methodological choices
- Unclear motivation for the weight adaptation formula Wj^c + W_avg
- Insufficient explanation for why kNN variant outperforms others
- Unfair evaluation protocol using validation set instead of test set
- Claims about RCNN performance not statistically justified
- Limited discussion of the method's limitations and failure modes

**Recommendation:** The paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable approach to large-scale object detection. While the core idea is sound and the results are promising, the lack of principled justification for key components and the unfair evaluation protocol significantly undermine the paper's claims. The authors should address these issues before the work can be considered for publication at NIPS.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Provide a theoretical justification for the weight adaptation formula in Section 3.2, including the physical meaning of the "offset" parameter.
2. Conduct fair comparisons using the official test set rather than a subset of the validation set.
3. Include a statistical analysis of the RCNN performance claim, acknowledging the small size of val2.
4. Expand the discussion of why the kNN variant outperforms other variants.
5. Add a thorough discussion of the method's limitations and potential failure cases.
6. Better position the work relative to domain adaptation literature.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of object detection, but requires significant revisions to address the methodological and evaluation concerns before it can be considered for publication at NIPS. The core idea has potential, but the current presentation and evaluation do not fully support the claims made.
