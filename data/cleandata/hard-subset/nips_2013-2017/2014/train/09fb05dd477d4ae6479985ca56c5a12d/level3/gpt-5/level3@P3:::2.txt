Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes LSDA, a framework for turning image classifiers into object detectors when bounding-box annotations are available for only a subset of categories. The method casts the problem as domain adaptation from classification to detection. It learns (1) a category-invariant transformation by fine-tuning layers 1–7 of an ImageNet-pretrained CNN on detection data for a subset B, including adding a background class, and (2) a category-specific transformation of the final layer: for seen classes i∈B, W_i^d = W_i^c + δ_i; for unseen classes j∈A, W_j^d = W_j^c + average of δ over the k nearest neighbors in B (defined in the fc8 weight space of the classification network). At test time, class scores are adjusted by the background score.

The work is closely related to R-CNN (Girshick et al.)—which pretrains on classification and fine-tunes on detection for all categories—and to domain adaptation approaches that adapt models across domains. Unlike standard supervised adaptation, LSDA transfers to categories with no detection data. It is complementary to weakly supervised MIL-based detection, which is not evaluated at ILSVRC scale here. Empirically, on ILSVRC-2013 detection, LSDA improves mAP on held-out categories by roughly 50% relative (10.3%→16.2%); the authors also report placing 4th in the detection challenge and demonstrate a 7.6K-category detector with a faster SPP-based variant.

Strengths
- Clear, well-organized presentation; easy to follow. The CNN architecture and training protocol are simple to implement and reproduce.
- Practical and compelling problem: scaling detection to thousands of categories with limited box annotations.
- Strong empirical improvements versus a classification-only baseline; good error analysis showing reduced background and localization errors.
- Clean, modular idea that many will find useful; released models/code enhance reproducibility and impact.
- The background class and category-invariant fine-tuning are well-motivated and effective.

Weaknesses
- The main contribution—category-specific adaptation via W_j^d = W_j^c + average(δ)—lacks principled justification. Why an additive offset, why averaging in fc8 weight space, and what is the physical meaning of δ as an “offset” are not explained beyond intuition. Alternatives (e.g., multiplicative scaling, learned meta-mapper, or feature-space adaptation) are not discussed.
- The choice of kNN over other variants is not explained; there is no analysis of why kNN outperforms full averaging or other similarity metrics, nor sensitivity to k or distance definitions beyond a brief table.
- Some comparisons are on non-standard splits. Using val1 for detection training and val2 for evaluation complicates comparisons to R-CNN and challenge entries that train on train+val and evaluate on the held-out test set. The claim that R-CNN has similar performance on val2 is not statistically supported given val2’s limited size; confidence intervals or bootstrap tests versus R-CNN are missing.
- The decision to omit SVM heads and bbox regression simplifies training but further blurs comparability with state-of-the-art detectors of the time.

Quality
The method is technically sound at the engineering level, and the ablations isolate the main gains (category-invariant fine-tuning with background). However, the category-specific adaptation is heuristic; there is no theoretical or empirical justification for additivity of the offset, for the choice of fc8 weight space to define neighbors, or for kNN averaging. The statistical treatment is partial: while a t-test is reported for one ablation, cross-method comparisons (e.g., to R-CNN) on val2 lack proper uncertainty estimates. The use of part of the validation set for training further limits the strength of claims about ranking.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and easy to reproduce. Figures and ablations are helpful. Section 3.2 would benefit from a more careful derivation: explicitly define δ as the learned change induced by fine-tuning, explain why addition is chosen, and interpret δ’s magnitude/direction (e.g., shifts in bias vs. class prototypes). Clarify the “offset” parameter’s physical meaning and calibrate scores after adding a background head.

Originality
Adapting classification networks to detection was contemporaneously explored (e.g., R-CNN), but LSDA’s transfer to unseen detection categories via averaged offsets from nearest neighbors appears novel for the time. Framing the shift as a learned, category-invariant transform plus a lightweight category-specific adjustment is a neat synthesis. That said, the approach is a simple combination of known components, with limited theoretical novelty.

Significance
The problem is important, and the demonstrated 7.6K detector and 2 fps variant underscore practical impact. Many practitioners can adopt the pipeline. Nonetheless, the lack of principled grounding for the adaptation step and the evaluation caveats reduce the strength of the state-of-the-art claim. With cleaner evaluation and a better-motivated adaptation mechanism, the contribution would be stronger.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a principled rationale for additivity: e.g., show that fine-tuning primarily induces a low-rank, approximately additive shift in the last-layer weights, or derive W^d ≈ W^c + J·Δθ via a first-order Taylor expansion of fine-tuning steps. Analyze δ’s components (norm, bias shifts) and correlate with class properties.
- Justify the neighbor space and metric. Compare kNN using cosine vs. L2, fc8 vs. intermediate features, and semantic/WordNet similarity. Report sensitivity to k and show why kNN beats global averaging.
- Strengthen evaluation fairness: train and report results on the standard train+val split and submit to the test server; or re-train baselines (R-CNN) under your exact data protocol. Report CIs/bootstraps for all cross-method comparisons; avoid strong claims from val2 alone.
- Explore simple learned mappers from W^c to W^d (e.g., ridge regression or MLP on W^c predicting δ) as stronger baselines.
- Calibrate scores when introducing the background head (temperature scaling or Platt-like calibration) and report its effect.
- Discuss the failure modes (e.g., confusions among similar classes) and whether neighborhood selection can exploit taxonomy.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely, impactful problem; practical method with public release.
- Clear paper; easy to implement; solid ablations and qualitative analysis.
- Demonstrates large-scale detection (7.6K classes) with good speed.
- Competitive results on ILSVRC-2013; reported 4th-place ranking.

Cons against acceptance
- Core adaptation step lacks principled justification and detailed motivation.
- Insufficient explanation/analysis of why kNN averaging works best.
- Fairness and statistical rigor issues in comparisons (val2 evaluation, differing training data; unsubstantiated claims about R-CNN parity).
- Limited exploration of alternative adaptation mechanisms.

Overall evaluation
A good, clearly written paper with a simple, useful idea and promising results that many will build upon. I recommend acceptance if the authors can clarify and better justify the category-specific adaptation and temper or strengthen their comparative claims with standardized evaluation; otherwise, this sits at a strong borderline.
