This paper introduces Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a framework for adapting deep neural networks trained for image classification to the task of object detection. The key idea is to leverage the vast amount of image-level classification data to create detectors for categories that lack expensive bounding box annotations. The authors frame this as a domain adaptation problem, learning a transformation from the classification task to the detection task using a subset of categories for which both label types are available. This approach builds on prior work like R-CNN, which demonstrated the power of fine-tuning, but extends it by proposing a method that does not require bounding box data for every target category.

The paper’s primary strengths are its clear presentation, the significance of the problem it tackles, and its strong empirical results. The method is well-explained and appears straightforward to implement. The achievement of ranking 4th in the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge is a significant result that demonstrates the practical viability of the proposed framework. The goal of scaling object detection to tens of thousands of categories is highly relevant and important to the community.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses. The main technical contribution—the category-specific adaptation method—lacks a principled justification. In Section 3.2, the adapted detection weights are computed by adding an averaged offset to the original classification weights (Wj^c + W_avg). The motivation for this specific additive formulation is not well-explained, nor is the physical meaning of this "offset" parameter. Similarly, there is no clear analysis of why the kNN variant for averaging this offset outperforms other approaches, which leaves the method feeling somewhat heuristic.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation raises concerns about fairness and comparability. The authors evaluate their method on a split of the validation set (`val2`) instead of the standard test set, which makes direct comparisons to other state-of-the-art methods difficult. The claim that LSDA achieves performance similar to R-CNN is not statistically robust, given the small size of the `val2` set.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and effective method for a significant problem. While it achieves performance comparable to the state-of-the-art, the contributions are weakened by the lack of theoretical grounding for the adaptation mechanism and an evaluation protocol that hinders fair comparison. The paper would be significantly improved with a clearer justification for its core technical approach and evaluation on standard benchmarks.
