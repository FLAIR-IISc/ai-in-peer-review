## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper addresses the critical challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, a problem hampered by the scarcity of bounding box annotations. The authors propose LSDA, an algorithm that adapts classifiers trained on image-level labels to perform detection, leveraging the abundance of classification data. The core idea – transferring knowledge from classification to detection – builds upon previous work like R-CNN [1] but crucially avoids the need for bounding boxes for all categories. 

The approach is conceptually simple and well-motivated. By framing the problem as domain adaptation, LSDA learns a transformation applicable to classifiers lacking bounding box data. The experimental setup, training on 100 categories with boxes and testing on 100 held-out categories with labels only, is a clever way to isolate the adaptation capability. The reported results, achieving a 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline and filling approximately one-third of the gap between baseline and oracle performance, are encouraging. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the methodology and understand the results. 

However, while the idea is sound and the execution competent, the significance of the contribution feels somewhat limited. The authors acknowledge this, noting the potential for future work to surpass their results. The trade-off between reducing annotation requirements (fewer bounding boxes) and the resulting mAP is not clearly articulated. It’s difficult to judge whether the gain in scalability justifies the performance drop. Furthermore, the long-term importance of the problem is debatable. The paper doesn’t fully address the possibility that increased investment in bounding box annotation, driven by the success of detection systems, could alleviate the scarcity issue.

Several specific points require attention. Figure 3a needs clarification regarding which methods, besides LSDA and R-CNN, utilize category labels for detection to ensure a fair comparison. The authors overstate their main result; the key takeaway is the DNN performance in Figure 3b (the one-third gap closure), not the potentially misleading 78% and 50% claims. A dedicated table or subsection detailing the differences between LSDA and R-CNN is essential, and Figure 6 could be removed to accommodate this. Finally, minor inconsistencies in method naming ("DNN" vs. "DDA") and a typo on line 431 ("classi?ers") should be corrected.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of domain adaptation to address a practical problem in large-scale detection.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the methodology and results.
*   Demonstrated improvement over a strong baseline in a challenging setting.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited significance of the contribution; potential for future work to surpass the results.
*   Unclear trade-off between annotation cost and detection performance.
*   Questionable long-term importance of the problem.



Overall, this is a solid paper with a clear contribution. Addressing the concerns outlined above would significantly strengthen the work and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
