This paper addresses the significant challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, where collecting bounding box annotations is prohibitively expensive. The core idea is to treat the transformation of an image classifier into an object detector as a domain adaptation problem. The proposed method, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), leverages a set of categories for which both classification and detection (bounding box) data are available to learn a generic transformation. This learned transformation is then applied to adapt classifiers for new categories that only have image-level labels. The method builds upon the R-CNN framework, but its key departure is the ability to generate detectors for categories without any bounding box supervision. The LSDA algorithm consists of two main adaptation steps: a category-invariant adaptation, where the shared convolutional layers (1-7) of a CNN are fine-tuned on the detection data, and a category-specific adaptation for the final layer, where the change in weights for a new category is estimated by averaging the changes from its nearest neighbors in the source detection set. The authors evaluate their approach on the ILSVRC 2013 detection dataset by holding out 100 of the 200 categories from detection training. The results show a significant improvement over a baseline that uses the raw classifier, and the paper demonstrates the scalability of the approach by training a detector for over 7,600 categories.

**Quality and Clarity**

The paper is of high quality and is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is strong, the proposed method is described in a straightforward and understandable manner, and the experiments are thoughtfully designed to validate the central claims. The ablation study in Table 1 and Figure 3 effectively demonstrates the contribution of each component of the LSDA model. The error analysis in Section 4.3 is particularly insightful, showing that the adaptation successfully reduces localization errors and confusion with background—precisely the key challenges that differentiate detection from classification. The authors are also transparent about the performance upper bound (a fully supervised R-CNN) and the trade-offs made for speed and simplicity (e.g., not training SVMs or bounding box regressors).

**Originality and Significance**

The originality of the work lies in its problem formulation: learning a transferable mapping from the classification domain to the detection domain to handle categories with no bounding box data. While the constituent techniques (CNN fine-tuning, nearest-neighbor adaptation) are not entirely new, their combination to solve this specific problem is novel and interesting. The work clearly distinguishes itself from prior work like R-CNN, which requires bounding boxes for all categories, and MIL-based approaches, which have not been demonstrated at this scale.

The significance of the contribution is more debatable. On one hand, the paper tackles a very real and important problem. Enabling detection for the long tail of object categories for which we only have classification data is a valuable goal, and the demonstration of a 7.6K category detector is impressive. On the other hand, the practical impact of the proposed method is moderated by two factors. First, the performance on held-out categories, while a 50% relative improvement over the baseline, only closes about one-third of the performance gap to a fully supervised model (mAP of 16.15% vs. a baseline of 10.31% and an oracle of 26.89%). The paper does not make a compelling case for when this trade-off between annotation cost and performance drop is acceptable. Second, the work’s long-term significance hinges on the assumption that bounding box scarcity will remain a fundamental bottleneck. As data collection efforts continue to scale, this assumption may become less critical, potentially limiting the long-term impact of this line of work. Nonetheless, it represents a solid and interesting contribution to the field of large-scale visual recognition.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant and practical problem: scaling object detection with limited bounding box supervision.
*   The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and clearly presented.
*   The paper is technically sound, with well-designed experiments, a thorough ablation study, and insightful error analysis.
*   The core idea of learning a transferable classifier-to-detector transformation is novel and interesting.
*   Demonstrates impressive scalability by producing a 7.6K category detector.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The performance improvement on held-out categories, while significant relative to the baseline, is still far from the fully supervised upper bound. The practical utility of the resulting detector is unclear.
*   The long-term significance of the problem itself is debatable; large-scale annotation efforts may eventually reduce the need for such adaptation methods.
*   The technical contribution is more of a novel combination of existing ideas than a fundamentally new technique.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Framing of Results:** The main result is presented as a "50% relative mAP boost". While technically correct, this is somewhat misleading as the baseline is low. It would be more informative to frame the result in terms of closing the gap to the oracle performance. For instance, stating that LSDA closes approximately 35% of the performance gap between a pure classification network and a fully supervised detection network on the held-out categories would provide better context. This should be the main takeaway in Figure 3b.

2.  **Explicit Comparison to R-CNN:** The method is heavily based on the R-CNN pipeline, but the differences are scattered throughout the text. A dedicated table or subsection explicitly detailing the differences in training stages (e.g., fine-tuning data, use of SVMs, use of bounding box regression) would be extremely valuable for the reader to precisely understand the trade-offs being made. To make space, the qualitative examples in Figure 6, while interesting, are less critical than a clear technical comparison and could potentially be removed or moved to an appendix.

3.  **Minor Issues:** The paper is well-written, but a final proofread is recommended to catch minor typos (e.g., "classi?ers" was noted in an earlier draft).
