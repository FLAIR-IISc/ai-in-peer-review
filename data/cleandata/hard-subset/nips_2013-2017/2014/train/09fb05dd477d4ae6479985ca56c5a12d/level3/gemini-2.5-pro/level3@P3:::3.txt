### **Summary**

This paper introduces Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a method for training object detectors for categories that lack bounding-box annotations by leveraging categories that do. The approach treats the problem as a domain adaptation task, transferring knowledge from image classification (source domain) to object detection (target domain). The method proceeds in three stages: (1) A standard CNN (based on Krizhevsky et al.) is pre-trained for classification on all available categories. (2) The network's feature layers (conv1-fc7) are fine-tuned for detection using region proposals from the subset of classes that have bounding-box annotations, crucially adding a "background" class. (3) For classes without bounding-box data, the classification weights are adapted by adding the average weight change observed in their nearest-neighbor classes during the detection fine-tuning stage. The resulting model can then be applied to region proposals at test time to detect objects from thousands of categories. The authors demonstrate a significant relative mAP improvement on held-out classes in the ILSVRC2013 dataset and showcase a detector for over 7,600 classes.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a critical and practical problem in computer vision: scaling object detection to a large number of categories where exhaustive bounding-box annotation is infeasible. The ability to leverage abundant image-level classification data to create detectors is highly valuable to both the research and practitioner communities. The results, particularly the demonstration of a 7.6K-category detector, are compelling and highlight the method's potential impact.

2.  **Simplicity and Soundness:** The proposed approach is logical, simple, and well-motivated. Casting the problem as one of learning a transferable transformation from a classifier to a detector is an elegant framing. The multi-stage training process is easy to understand and builds logically upon prior work like R-CNN. The technical quality of the work is solid.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The core ideas are communicated effectively, and the motivation for each step of the LSDA algorithm is clear.

### **Weaknesses**

The primary weakness of this paper is the lack of comparison against several strong and obvious baselines. This makes it difficult to ascertain the true contribution of the proposed adaptation technique versus the power of the underlying CNN features.

1.  **Missing Baselines:** The experimental evaluation would be substantially strengthened by including the following comparisons:
    *   **Baseline 1 (Weakly-Supervised Training):** A standard weakly-supervised approach would be to fine-tune the network for a held-out class using its full-image classification data as positives and region proposals from negative images as negatives. It is not clear that the proposed adaptation method (averaging weight deltas from other classes) would outperform this more direct, albeit simple, training strategy. This is a crucial baseline to properly situate the paper's contribution.
    *   **Baseline 2 (MIL-based Training):** Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) is a natural framework for this problem, where an image is a positive "bag" of region proposals. An MIL-based approach could be used to identify and train on the most promising positive regions within the weakly-labeled images. Given prior work on MIL for detection, this represents a significant missing comparison. It is plausible that an MIL approach could outperform the proposed method.
    *   **Baseline 3 (Sanity Check):** To better understand the difficulty of the task and the dataset, a simple "center-box" or "mean-box" baseline would be informative. This involves running the initial classifier on a fixed box at the image center or a box with the average size and aspect ratio of objects in the dataset. Including this would help quantify how much the proposed method improves localization over a trivial guess.

2.  **Attribution of Performance:** The impressive results are largely built on the strength of the pre-trained Krizhevsky CNN. While the adaptation method clearly provides a boost over the raw classifier (a 50% relative mAP increase), the paper does not fully disentangle the contribution of its specific adaptation strategy from the general power of fine-tuning a deep network. The comparisons against the baselines suggested above would help clarify whether the proposed method is a superior way to train a weakly-supervised detector, or if much of the gain comes simply from any form of fine-tuning that teaches the network about background patches and localization.

3.  **Experimental Clarity:** While the paper is well-written overall, the experiments section could be clearer. Specifically, Table 1 presents results for several variants of the method (e.g., "LSDA-BG," "LSDA-fc7"), but it is not immediately obvious which parts of the methodology (e.g., which equations, which layers were frozen/fine-tuned) correspond to each row. A more explicit mapping would improve the clarity and reproducibility of the experimental analysis.

### **Constructive Feedback & Questions for Authors**

*   Could you elaborate on why the proposed nearest-neighbor weight adaptation was chosen over more direct weakly-supervised methods like those mentioned in the baselines above (e.g., fine-tuning with full-image positives and region-proposal negatives)?
*   In Section 3.2, the distance metric for nearest neighbors is the Euclidean distance between `l2`-normalized `fc8` classifier weights. Have you explored other similarity metrics (e.g., based on the WordNet hierarchy, which is available for ImageNet classes) to see if a more semantic similarity could improve the transfer?
*   Please consider adding a paragraph to the experiments section that explicitly links each model variant in Table 1 to the specific training procedure used. This would greatly enhance the paper's clarity for readers trying to understand the ablation study.

### **Final Assessment**

**Originality:** The paper is sufficiently novel for acceptance. While it builds heavily on existing CNN architectures (Krizhevsky et al., R-CNN), the core idea of learning a general, transferable "classifier-to-detector" transformation to handle classes without bounding boxes is a novel and valuable contribution to the field of weakly-supervised detection.

**Quality:** The method is technically sound and the results are interesting. However, the quality of the experimental evaluation is hampered by the omission of key baselines, which makes the central claims less convincing than they could be.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant. It addresses a major bottleneck in scaling object detection and provides a practical solution that could enable detection for tens of thousands of categories. This work is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers.

In summary, this is a good paper addressing an important problem with a simple and effective solution. Its main flaw is an experimental evaluation that lacks the necessary baselines to robustly support its claims against strong alternatives. Despite this, the significance of the problem, the compelling nature of the large-scale results, and the novelty of the approach warrant acceptance. The authors are strongly encouraged to address the concerns regarding baselines and experimental clarity in the final version.
