- Problem & approach: LSDA adapts image classifiers into detectors by learning a category-invariant transformation from classes with detection data and transferring it to classes lacking bounding boxes, plus approximating category-specific deltas via nearest neighbors in parameter space.
- Evaluation summary: On ILSVRC-2013, withholding detection labels for 100 of 200 classes, LSDA raises held-out mAP from ~10.3% to ~16.1%, closing ~1/3 of the gap to the fully supervised oracle; also demonstrates a 7.6K-class detector and a faster SPP variant.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on R-CNN fine-tuning but differs by transferring a generic classification→detection transformation to held-out classes; related to domain adaptation and weakly supervised detection but does not solve MIL directly.
- Method quality: Technically straightforward and sound; training protocol, ablations, and error analysis are appropriate and informative.
- Quantitative caveat: Headline relative-improvement claims (e.g., “50%”, “78%”) risk misleading without reporting absolute mAP and the fraction of the gap-to-oracle with explicit denominators.
- Clarity: Paper is clearly written with a clear three-part decomposition; figures are useful but need clarifications about baselines and label usage.
- Figure/data clarity request: Clarify in Figure 3a which baselines use detection labels versus only classification labels for fair comparison.
- Reporting recommendation: Center main quantitative claims on absolute mAP and fraction of gap closed; report denominators explicitly.
- R-CNN comparison: Provide a dedicated table/subsection listing differences from R-CNN (data requirements, objectives, background class, SVMs/bbox regression, training time).
- Minor edits: Ensure consistent naming (“LSDA”) and fix typos (e.g., “classi?ers”).
- Originality: Combines known components (fine-tuning, background class, parameter-space transfer) in a new, practical application at scale; not radical but novel in this setting.
- Significance: Important problem and a useful first step toward scaling detection with less bounding-box annotation, but absolute performance on held-out classes remains substantially below fully supervised oracle.
- Missing cost–benefit analysis: No systematic quantification of how mAP varies with number of detection-labeled categories (m) or marginal return per labeled box.
- Suggested experiments: Report performance vs m (e.g., m ∈ {20,50,100,150}); analyze sensitivity to nearest-neighbor count k; show per-category gains vs semantic similarity.
- Ablation requests: Test necessity of the background class and varying degrees of fine-tuning (with/without background, partial fine-tuning).
- Weakly-supervised comparisons: Include or discuss comparisons/integration with MIL/weakly supervised detection baselines.
- Calibration question: Address whether class-wise score calibration is required for fair cross-class comparisons at 7.6K scale.
- Generalization check: Recommend a cross-dataset test (e.g., PASCAL) to assess transfer generality.
- Pros: Tackles a clear scaling bottleneck; solid held-out evaluations and ablations; practical impact with 7.6K demo and released code; clear writing and good positioning.
- Cons: Absolute gains modest, potential overstatement via relative metrics, lacks systematic cost–accuracy trade-off analysis, limited direct comparison to weakly supervised alternatives.
- Overall judgement: High-quality, well-executed contribution that merits acceptance pending requested clarifications and minor edits.
