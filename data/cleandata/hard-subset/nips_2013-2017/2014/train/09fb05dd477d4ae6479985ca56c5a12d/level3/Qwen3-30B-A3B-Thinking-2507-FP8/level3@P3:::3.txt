

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

This paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), an algorithm that transforms image classifiers into object detectors by adapting a Krizhevsky CNN trained for classification. The approach leverages abundant image-level classification data to enable detection for categories lacking bounding box annotations, addressing a critical bottleneck in scaling object detection to large numbers of categories. The method builds on R-CNN's insight that fine-tuning is crucial for adapting classification features to detection, but extends it to work with minimal bounding box data for only a subset of categories.

The paper's main contribution is a three-stage adaptation process: (1) training a classification network on all classes, (2) fine-tuning on region proposals for a subset of classes with a background class, and (3) adapting category-specific parameters for classes without bounding box data using nearest-neighbor transfer. This enables detection for thousands of categories using only classification data for the majority, as demonstrated by a 7.6K category detector on ImageNet.

**Strengths:**
The paper addresses a significant problem in computer vision with a simple, logical approach that builds effectively on prior work (R-CNN, domain adaptation). The results are compelling, showing a 50% relative mAP improvement over a classification-only baseline on held-out categories. The method's simplicity makes it highly valuable to the vision and ML communities, and the release of models and code at lsda.berkeleyvision.org enhances its practical impact. The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the motivation and methodology.

**Weaknesses:**
The most significant concern is the lack of comparisons to three obvious baselines that would better contextualize the claimed improvements:
1. A baseline using full-image positives with region negatives (which would test if the method truly outperforms simple application of classification weights to regions)
2. A MIL-based region selection baseline (which would test if the adaptation is necessary or if existing weakly-supervised methods suffice)
3. A mean-box classifier baseline (which would reveal how difficult the dataset is to detect without bounding box annotations)

The paper's attribution concern is valid - it's unclear whether the improvements are due to the adaptation technique or simply the strength of the Krizhevsky features. The experiments section lacks clarity about what each method in Table 1 corresponds to (which equations/layers were applied), making it difficult to fully assess the technical contributions. The paper also doesn't sufficiently demonstrate that the adaptation method is superior to existing weakly-supervised approaches like MIL, which have been used for similar problems.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound, but the experimental evaluation is incomplete without the missing baselines. The results are well-supported for the comparisons that were made, but the attribution concern weakens the claims about the method's novelty.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, but the experiments section would benefit from more detailed explanations of the adaptation techniques and clearer correspondence between methods in Table 1 and the technical implementation.

**Originality:** The method is a logical extension of R-CNN and domain adaptation concepts rather than a radical new idea. While it's sufficiently novel for acceptance, it's relatively specific to certain CNN architectures and not highly innovative.

**Significance:** The problem of scaling detection to large numbers of categories is significant, and the results are promising. The ability to produce a 7.6K category detector represents a meaningful step toward large-scale detection, though the paper doesn't fully demonstrate this is superior to other approaches for such large-scale detection.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**
*Pro:* Addresses a significant problem with a simple, effective approach; results are compelling for the evaluation conducted; releases models and code; provides valuable insights into adapting classification networks to detection.
*Con:* Missing critical baselines that undermine the claimed improvements; attribution concerns about whether results stem from the adaptation method or strong CNN features; lacks clear demonstration of superiority over existing weakly-supervised detection techniques.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a valuable approach to large-scale detection with compelling results, the lack of critical baselines and attribution concerns prevent it from fully demonstrating the novelty and significance of its contribution. The method is interesting and likely valuable to the community, but the experimental evaluation is insufficient to fully support the claims. With additional baselines and clearer attribution, this could be a strong contribution. As is, the paper's significance is somewhat limited by the missing comparisons, particularly the mean-box baseline which would help establish the difficulty of the task.
