This paper proposes a novel approach to object detection, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), which aims to adapt object classifiers for detection without requiring bounding box annotations at scale. The authors argue that collecting bounding box annotations for a large number of categories is impractical and propose an algorithm that learns to transform an image classifier into an object detector using a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, approach, and experimental setup. The authors provide a thorough analysis of their results, including an ablation study and error analysis, which helps to understand the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

The proposed algorithm is simple and straightforward, which is a significant advantage. The authors demonstrate that their approach can fill approximately 35% of the gap between the oracle and baseline performance on the held-out categories. This is a notable achievement, especially considering that the algorithm only requires bounding box annotations for a small subset of categories.

The originality of the paper lies in its combination of two existing adaptation ideas, which is novel and interesting. However, it is not ground-breaking, as it builds upon previous work in the field. The significance of the contribution is also notable, as it addresses a practical problem in object detection. However, it is unclear how this approach will be surpassed in the future, and the trade-off between using fewer bounding boxes and achieving lower mAP is not clearly established.

One question raised by the paper is the long-term importance of the problem of bounding box scarcity. With the increasing availability of annotated data and advancements in annotation tools, it is possible that the problem of bounding box scarcity may be alleviated in the future.

Some minor issues with the paper include inconsistent method naming ("DNN" vs "DDA") and a typo on line 431 ("classi?ers"). Additionally, Figure 3a could be clarified to indicate which methods, besides the authors' method and R-CNN, use category labels for detection to ensure a fair comparison.

The main result of the paper should be the DNN performance in Figure 3b, which shows that the approach fills approximately one-third of the gap between the baseline and oracle performance. The 78% and 50% claims are misleading and should be removed.

A dedicated table or subsection clearly specifying the differences between LSDA and R-CNN would be helpful. This could potentially replace Figure 6, which is not essential to the paper.

Overall, the paper is well-written and presents a novel approach to object detection. While it may not be ground-breaking, it addresses a practical problem and demonstrates promising results.
