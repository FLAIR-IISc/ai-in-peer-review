This paper addresses the challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, where collecting bounding box annotations is prohibitively expensive. The authors propose Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a method to transform a pre-trained image classifier into an object detector, even for categories that lack any bounding box training data. The core idea is to treat this as a domain adaptation problem. The method starts with a CNN (based on Krizhevsky et al.) pre-trained for classification on all target categories. This network is then fine-tuned for detection using a subset of categories for which bounding box annotations *are* available. This fine-tuning step learns a category-invariant transformation (including a generic background class) in the lower layers of the network. For the final, category-specific layer, the method learns the adaptation for the annotated classes and then transfers this knowledge to the unannotated classes by averaging the learned changes from their nearest neighbors in the classification feature space. The approach is evaluated on the ILSVRC 2013 detection dataset, demonstrating a significant performance boost over using the raw classifier for detection.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Ambition:** The paper tackles a highly significant and practical problem. The gap between the number of categories for which we have strong classifiers (tens of thousands) and strong detectors (hundreds) is a major bottleneck in computer vision. The goal of enabling detection for thousands of categories is ambitious and important, and the presented 7.6K detector is a compelling demonstration of the method's potential impact.

2.  **Simplicity and Elegance:** The proposed approach is simple, logical, and well-motivated. Casting the problem as one of domain adaptation and learning a transferable transformation from classifiers to detectors is an elegant framing. The multi-stage adaptation process—particularly the distinction between category-invariant and category-specific adaptation—is intuitive and easy to follow. This simplicity makes the method likely to be understood, adopted, and built upon by other researchers.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The quantitative results are compelling. A 50% relative improvement in mAP on held-out categories over the classification baseline is a substantial gain. The error analysis in Figure 5 effectively demonstrates that the adaptation process successfully reduces localization and background confusion errors, which are the key challenges in moving from classification to detection. The qualitative results in Figure 4, showing the shift from localizing discriminative parts to whole objects, further strengthen this claim.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Missing Critical Baselines:** The main weakness of the paper is the lack of comparison to several obvious and important baselines. The current baseline is a network trained purely on classification data, which may be a weak starting point. To properly evaluate the contribution of the proposed adaptation technique, the following comparisons are essential:
    *   **Full-image positives vs. region negatives:** A simple weakly supervised baseline would be to train the detector using the entire training images as positive examples and region proposals from negative images as negative examples. It is not clear that the proposed adaptation method significantly outperforms this simpler approach.
    *   **MIL-based region selection:** The related work section mentions Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) methods, which are a standard approach for weakly supervised object detection. These methods treat region proposals within a positive image as a "bag" and attempt to identify the true positive instances. A comparison against a modern MIL-based detector is necessary to position this work within the field of weakly supervised detection.
    *   **Center/Mean-Box Heuristic:** To better understand the difficulty of the localization task on this dataset, a simple baseline that places a mean-sized bounding box at the center of the image and runs the classifier on it would be informative. This helps calibrate the performance and understand how much of the gain is from sophisticated localization versus simply identifying that an object is present.

    Without these comparisons, it is difficult to ascertain whether the performance gains are due to a superior adaptation method or primarily due to the power of the underlying CNN features, which are known to be very strong. The central claim that LSDA is an effective way to *train* a detector from weak labels is not fully substantiated.

2.  **Clarity in Experimental Section:** While the paper is generally well-written, the experimental section could be clearer. Specifically, Table 1 presents results for various stages of the adaptation process, but it is not immediately obvious how each row ("CNN Class.", "CNN Class.+BG", "CNN Det. L6-7", etc.) maps to the specific architectural changes and training steps described in Section 3. A more explicit description linking each experimental condition in the table to the specific layers being fine-tuned and the data being used would improve reproducibility and reader understanding.

3.  **Limited Originality:** The work builds heavily on the R-CNN framework and the Krizhevsky et al. network. The core idea of fine-tuning a pre-trained network is now standard practice. The novelty lies in the specific adaptation strategy for classes without bounding boxes. While this is a valuable contribution, it is more of an incremental improvement on existing architectures rather than a radically new approach to detection.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:** The paper addresses a very important problem with a simple, elegant solution. The results are significant, and the potential impact on scaling up object detection is high. The work is well-presented and provides a valuable contribution to the community.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:** The experimental evaluation is weakened by the absence of several crucial baselines. This makes it difficult to judge the true effectiveness of the proposed adaptation technique compared to simpler or alternative weakly supervised methods. This is a significant omission that undermines the paper's core scientific claims.

In its current form, the paper presents an interesting idea with promising results, but the claims are not sufficiently supported due to the missing baseline comparisons. If the authors can provide these comparisons and show a clear advantage, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
