

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

This paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a method to adapt image classifiers into object detectors using only a small subset of bounding box annotated data. The authors address the critical challenge of scaling object detection to thousands of categories by leveraging abundant image-level classification data. The core idea is to treat the transformation from classification to detection as a domain adaptation problem, where the source domain is classification data (image labels) and the target domain is detection data (bounding boxes). The method fine-tunes a pre-trained CNN (Krizhevsky et al.) on a subset of categories with bounding box annotations, learning a generic transformation that can then be applied to adapt classifiers for categories without any bounding box data.

The work builds upon several key threads in the literature: Girshick et al.'s R-CNN for adapting classifiers to detectors, domain adaptation techniques for shifting between visual domains, and recent advances in deep CNNs for visual recognition. The authors position their work as novel in adapting to held-out categories with no detection data, which distinguishes it from prior domain adaptation work that requires detection data for all categories.

**Strengths:**
- The problem addressed is highly significant for scaling object detection to large numbers of categories, a major challenge in computer vision.
- The method is conceptually simple and logically constructed, making it accessible to the vision community.
- The results are compelling: the authors achieve a 50% relative mAP improvement over the baseline on held-out categories (from 10.31% to 16.15% mAP).
- The paper demonstrates the method's scalability by producing a 7,604 category detector using ImageNet classification data.
- The implementation details are well-described, and the authors have made code and models publicly available.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks critical baselines that could challenge the significance of the proposed adaptation technique. Specifically, the authors do not compare with:
  1) A simple baseline using full-image positives with region negatives (a natural baseline for weakly supervised detection)
  2) MIL-based region selection methods (which have been used for similar weakly supervised detection tasks)
  3) A center/mean-box classifier baseline (which would establish the difficulty of the task)
- Without these comparisons, it's difficult to determine whether the improvement is due to the adaptation method itself or simply the strength of the underlying CNN features.
- The experiments section would benefit from clearer explanations of what each method in Table 1 corresponds to (which equations/layers were applied).
- The paper doesn't sufficiently address the attribution concern: it's possible the improvement stems from the strong CNN features rather than a superior adaptation method.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments. The CNN architecture is carefully chosen and appropriately applied. The method is complete and well-evaluated on the ImageNet detection task. However, the lack of proper baselines weakens the claims about the efficacy of the adaptation technique.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The main ideas are clearly presented, though the experimental details could be more transparent. The authors would benefit from explicitly stating what each method in Table 1 corresponds to in terms of the algorithmic components.

**Originality:** The specific application of domain adaptation to transform classifiers into detectors for categories without bounding box data is novel. However, the approach is relatively specific to certain CNN architectures and doesn't introduce highly innovative techniques beyond adapting existing methods. The novelty is in the application rather than the core methodology.

**Significance:** The problem of scaling detection to large numbers of categories is important and has practical implications for real-world applications. The method demonstrates clear potential for enabling detection for tens of thousands of categories, which is a significant contribution to the field. The ability to leverage abundant classification data rather than expensive bounding box annotations represents a valuable practical advance.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**
- Pro: Addresses a significant problem with a simple, practical approach; compelling results on held-out categories; potential for large-scale impact; well-motivated and well-executed experiments.
- Con: Lacks critical baselines that would strengthen the claims; attribution concerns about whether the improvement comes from the adaptation technique or the underlying CNN; insufficient comparison with existing weakly-supervised detection methods.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant practical implications, but the lack of proper baselines weakens the contribution. The authors should address these concerns before publication. The method has the potential to be valuable for the community, but the claims need to be better substantiated with appropriate comparisons to simpler alternatives. With the suggested revisions, this would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
