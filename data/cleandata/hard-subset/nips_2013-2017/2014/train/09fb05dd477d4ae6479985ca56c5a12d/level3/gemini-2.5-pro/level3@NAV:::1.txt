This paper introduces Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), an algorithm for converting pre-trained image classifiers into object detectors, specifically for categories that lack bounding box annotations. The core idea is to treat this as a domain adaptation problem. By using a subset of categories that have both image-level labels and bounding boxes, LSDA learns a generic transformation from the classification task to the detection task. This learned adaptation is then applied to classifiers for held-out categories, enabling detection without corresponding bounding box data. This work builds directly on methods like R-CNN, which demonstrated the power of fine-tuning for detection, but cleverly addresses R-CNN's main limitation: the requirement of bounding boxes for every new category.

The paper is of high quality, clearly written, and presents a simple, well-motivated algorithm. The experimental setup is sound, evaluating the method on held-out categories to simulate a real-world scenario of data scarcity. The originality lies in the novel combination of fine-tuning for detection and domain adaptation principles to transfer knowledge across categories, which is an interesting and valuable contribution, even if not a groundbreaking paradigm shift.

However, the significance of the work is debatable. While the method closes approximately 35% of the performance gap between a simple classifier baseline and a fully supervised oracle, the practical utility of this trade-off—reduced annotation effort versus a significant drop in mAP—is not thoroughly discussed. Furthermore, the long-term relevance of the problem itself is questionable, as large-scale data collection efforts may eventually mitigate the scarcity of bounding box annotations. The abstract's claim of a "50% relative mAP boost" is also somewhat misleading; framing the result as closing one-third of the gap to the oracle, as shown in Figure 3b, would be more transparent and scientifically grounded.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should consider the following revisions:
1.  **Clarity in Comparisons:** In Figure 3a, it is unclear which of the compared methods also use category labels during training. This should be clarified for a fair assessment of the proposed contributions.
2.  **Direct Comparison to R-CNN:** A dedicated table or subsection explicitly outlining the architectural and training differences between LSDA and the R-CNN pipeline would be highly beneficial. Figure 6, which shows qualitative examples, could be removed to create space for this more critical comparison.
3.  **Minor Issues:** The paper would benefit from a final proofread to ensure consistency in method naming and to correct minor typos (e.g., "classi?ers" on line 431).
