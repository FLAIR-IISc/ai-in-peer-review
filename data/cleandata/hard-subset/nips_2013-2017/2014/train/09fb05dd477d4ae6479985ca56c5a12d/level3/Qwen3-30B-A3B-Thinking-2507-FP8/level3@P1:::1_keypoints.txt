- Proposes LSDA: adapt image-level classifiers into object detectors without bounding-box annotations for target categories.  
- Addresses scarcity of bounding-box annotations vs abundance of image-level labels (e.g., ImageNet).  
- Key insight: learn a transformation from classification to detection on a subset with boxes, then transfer it to categories without boxes.  
- Builds on R-CNN and domain adaptation, treating classification-to-detection as a domain adaptation problem.  
- Introduces three components: model background explicitly, transfer category-invariant features, and apply category-specific adaptation via nearest neighbors.  
- Training pipeline: pre-train CNN on classification data, fine-tune layers 1–7 on categories with boxes, then apply category-specific adaptation to remaining categories.  
- Experimental result on ImageNet LSVRC-2013: LSDA achieves 15.85% mAP on held-out 100 categories vs 10.31% baseline (region-classifier), with oracle at 22.3% mAP.  
- LSDA recovers ~35% of the gap between baseline and oracle; category-specific adaptation (k=10) yields 16.15% mAP.  
- Demonstrates scalability by producing a 7.6K category detector.  
- Ablation study shows category-invariant adaptation (fine-tuning layers 1–7) is the most important component; category-specific adaptation gives significant additional gains.  
- Error analysis shows LSDA reduces localization errors and background confusion.  
- Novelty is limited—combines existing adaptation techniques rather than introducing fundamentally new ideas.  
- Some claims are misleading: the "fills 50% of the gap" statement is incorrect; actual improvement is about one-third of the gap.  
- Significance concern: long-term scarcity of bounding-box annotations may diminish as annotation efforts scale.  
- Paper could be strengthened by a dedicated comparative table with R-CNN and other adaptation methods (replace Figure 6 to make space).  
- Minor issues: inconsistent method naming ("DNN" vs "DDA"), a typo ("classi?ers"), and Figure 3b (held-out performance) should be emphasized over the 78% claim.  
- Overall assessment: well-written, technically sound, thorough experiments, practical and scalable contribution; recommendation — Accept.
