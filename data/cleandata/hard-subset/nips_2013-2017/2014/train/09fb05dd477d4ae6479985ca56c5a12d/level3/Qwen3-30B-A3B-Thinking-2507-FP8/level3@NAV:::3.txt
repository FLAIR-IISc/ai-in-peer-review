

This paper proposes LSDA, a method to adapt image classifiers into detectors using weakly-supervised learning. The authors build on the Krizhevsky CNN architecture, training a classifier on all classes, then adapting it to detection by fine-tuning on region proposals for a subset of classes with bounding box annotations, adding a background class, and applying category-specific adaptation for classes without detection data. The approach demonstrates significant improvements on ImageNet detection (50% relative mAP gain over classification-only baseline) and scales to 7,600 categories using only classification data for most categories.

The paper's strengths include its clear problem formulation addressing the critical challenge of scaling object detection to large numbers of categories, a simple and logical approach that is likely to be valuable to the community, and strong empirical results on a challenging dataset. The significance is clear: enabling detection for tens of thousands of categories using readily available classification data represents a meaningful advance for the field.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The most critical issue is the lack of comparisons to three obvious baselines: (1) full-image positives with region negatives, (2) MIL-based region selection, and (3) a simple mean-box classifier baseline. Without these comparisons, it's unclear whether the 50% improvement is truly due to the proposed adaptation method or simply because the authors are using strong CNN features. The authors' attribution concern is well-founded—their results may primarily reflect the power of the underlying CNN rather than a novel adaptation technique.

The paper is well-written, but the experiments section lacks clarity regarding what each method in Table 1 corresponds to. The authors would benefit from more detailed explanations of their implementation choices and how they relate to the proposed framework.

While the method is sufficiently novel for acceptance (though not highly innovative), the missing baselines significantly undermine the paper's claims. The authors should address these concerns before publication. The significance of the problem and results is strong, but the paper's contribution would be much clearer with proper baseline comparisons, particularly the mean-box baseline which would establish the difficulty of the detection task.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field that addresses an important problem, but the lack of critical baselines prevents the authors from making a compelling case for their method's superiority. The paper would be significantly stronger with these additional comparisons.
