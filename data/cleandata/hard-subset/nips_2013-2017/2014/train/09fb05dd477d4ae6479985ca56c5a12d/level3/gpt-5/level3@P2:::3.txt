Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes LSDA, a simple and compelling strategy to scale object detection to many categories with only image-level labels for most classes. The key idea is to (1) train a Krizhevsky-style CNN on image-level labels for all K categories, (2) fine-tune layers 1–7 plus a new “background” class on detection data available for a subset B of m categories using region proposals, thereby learning a category-invariant transformation from classification to detection, and (3) adapt the category-specific output layer for held-out categories A by transferring the average change observed on their nearest neighbors in B (nearest in the classification fc8 space). At test time, detection scores are obtained by applying the network to object proposals and subtracting the background score.

The approach is closely related to R-CNN (Girshick et al.), which fine-tunes a classification network with bounding boxes for all categories and trains SVMs/regressors. LSDA differs by: (i) explicitly learning a background class and adapting shared layers using only a subset of categories, (ii) transferring the learned category-invariant “classification→detection” transformation to categories without any bounding boxes, and (iii) using a nearest-neighbor delta transfer for the final layer of held-out classes. The work is also related to domain adaptation methods (e.g., model adaptation and feature transformations) and weakly supervised detection via MIL; unlike most prior adaptation work, LSDA adapts the task (classification→detection) and scales to thousands of categories. The paper also connects to OverFeat/DeCAF lines showing transfer of CNN features.

Quality
Strengths:
- Clear, well-motivated problem: enabling detection for categories lacking bounding boxes.
- Solid empirical gains: ~50% relative mAP improvement over the “classification-only on proposals” baseline on held-out ImageNet detection categories; convincing error analysis showing reduced background and localization errors.
- Ablations indicate that adapting shared layers and learning a background class are the dominant contributors; category-specific delta transfer adds smaller but statistically significant gains.
- Demonstrates scalability by producing a 7.6K-category detector and a faster SPP-based variant; models and code are released.

Weaknesses and missing baselines:
- The paper omits three natural baselines that could materially affect conclusions:
  1) Full-image positives with region negatives: train a detector using full images as positives (or high-IoU proposals from positives) and proposals from negative images as negatives, with a background class. This would test whether the primary gain is due to background modeling and region-level fine-tuning rather than the specific LSDA transfer mechanism. It is not clear LSDA would outperform this.
  2) MIL-based region selection on the weakly labeled classes (A), coupled with the same CNN backbone: iterative selection of positive instances in positive images and fine-tuning, as in prior MIL detectors. Given strong CNN features, MIL may match or exceed LSDA on held-out categories.
  3) Center/mean-box classifier baseline: apply a classifier to a fixed central (or mean) box to establish task/dataset difficulty. This helps calibrate the absolute benefit of proposal-based detection and the added value of adaptation.
- Attribution concern: some of the reported gains may be largely due to the strength of the CNN features and the introduction of a background class, rather than the specific neighbor-based fc8 delta transfer. The ablation suggests the category-invariant adaptation dominates; more clarity and alternatives (e.g., semantic/WordNet neighbor selection, cosine vs L2) would help isolate what is uniquely effective in LSDA.
- Experimental clarity: Table 1 needs a precise mapping from each row to the training recipe (which layers updated, whether background is used, whether fc8 deltas are applied, value of k, and equation references). As written, it is hard to reproduce.
- Comparability to R-CNN: while the choice not to train SVMs/regressors is reasonable to enable transfer, it would be useful to include the performance of R-CNN-like training on the categories in B to contextualize the absolute gap and to quantify the cost of skipping SVM/regression.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The experiments section would benefit from:
- Explicit, line-by-line descriptions of each variant in Table 1 and corresponding equations/layer updates.
- Clearer description of the data splits and their implications (e.g., using train for classification with single image labels vs val1 for detection).
- Details and sensitivity of nearest-neighbor selection (feature space, normalization, k-sweep, stability across random seeds).
- A brief runtime/memory summary for the 7.6K detector variants.

Originality
The paper’s central framing—learning a generic classification→detection transformation on a subset of classes and transferring it—is a novel and useful twist on prior fine-tuning and adaptation work. The method itself is simple and somewhat specific to softmax CNN architectures, and builds on well-known components (pretraining, fine-tuning with background, proposal scoring), but the transfer mechanism and scale are new for the time.

Significance
The problem is important, the results are practically useful, and the potential impact on large-scale detection is high. Releasing a 7.6K detector and a faster variant is valuable to the community. With stronger baselines, this could be a definitive reference on adapting classifiers to detectors without per-class boxes.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely and important scalability problem.
- Simple, pragmatic method with clear empirical gains and error analysis.
- Demonstrates large-scale applicability and provides released models/code.

Cons against acceptance
- Missing strong baselines (especially MIL and simple weak detection with background) leave uncertainty about the source of gains.
- Some ambiguity in experimental descriptions hinders reproducibility.
- The main improvements may stem from generic fine-tuning with a background class rather than the proposed category-specific transfer.

Questions for the authors
- How does LSDA compare to a weakly supervised baseline that treats full images as positives and proposal boxes from negatives as negatives, with background modeling?
- Can you include a MIL-based baseline on the held-out classes using the same feature backbone?
- Sensitivity of the neighbor-based transfer: different distance metrics, features (e.g., fc7 vs fc8), and k values?
- What is the performance when only fc7/fc8 are fine-tuned vs all layers? Which layers matter most?
- Can you provide absolute runtime/memory for the 7.6K detector variants and typical per-image proposal counts?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add the three baselines above and a k-sweep for neighbor transfer.
- Clarify Table 1 and tie rows to explicit layer-update recipes and equations.
- Report per-class error breakdowns highlighting where LSDA helps (e.g., categories with fine-grained confusions).
- Include a brief discussion on when LSDA fails (e.g., visually similar confusions) and possible remedies (e.g., MIL mining for A).

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The idea is simple and valuable, results are promising and likely to be useful, but the paper should add the missing baselines and clarify the experimental design to strengthen the evidence and attribution of gains.
