This paper proposes Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), an algorithm that transforms image classifiers into object detectors. LSDA addresses the challenge of scaling object detection to large numbers of categories by leveraging image-level labels from search engines, which are cheaper and easier to collect than precise bounding box annotations. The authors cast the task as a domain adaptation problem, where the data used to train classifiers is the source domain, and the data used to train detectors is the target domain. They seek to find a general transformation from the source domain to the target domain that can be applied to any image classifier to adapt it into an object detector.

The authors' approach is an extension of the R-CNN model, which adapts an ImageNet classifier into a detector but requires bounding box data for all categories. LSDA uses image classification data to train strong classifiers and requires detection bounding box labeled data for only a small subset of the final detection categories. The algorithm learns a transformation of the classification network into a detection network using the classes labeled with both classification and detection labels. This transformation is then applied to adapt classifiers for categories without any bounding box annotated data into detectors.

The paper presents an extensive evaluation of LSDA on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge, demonstrating significant improvement (+50% relative mAP) over a baseline of just using raw classifier weights on object proposal regions. The authors also demonstrate how to modify their architecture to produce a fast detector (running at 2fps for the 7.6K detector). Models and software are available at lsda.berkeleyvision.org.

The strengths of this paper include its well-written and clear presentation, simplicity, and logical approach. The authors' use of CNN models to train both a classification and a detection network is well-motivated, and their multi-stage algorithm is easy to follow. The paper's significance lies in its potential to enable detection for tens of thousands of categories that lack bounding box annotations but have plenty of classification data.

However, there are some concerns regarding the paper's quality and originality. The approach is not radically new, and the results might be due to the strong CNN features rather than a superior weakly-supervised detector training method. Additionally, the paper lacks comparisons to three obvious baselines that could challenge its claims: full-image positives with region negatives, MIL-based region selection, and center/mean-box classifier baseline. Including these baselines would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of LSDA's performance.

The paper's clarity is generally good, but the experiments section needs clearer explanations, especially regarding what each method in Table 1 corresponds to (which equations/layers applied). Furthermore, the authors could provide more details on the implementation of their algorithm, such as the specific architecture of the CNN models used and the hyperparameters chosen.

In terms of originality, while the paper's approach is sufficiently novel for acceptance, it is relatively specific to certain CNN architectures and not highly innovative. The authors could discuss the potential limitations of their approach and how it could be extended to other architectures or domains.

In conclusion, this paper presents a well-motivated and logical approach to transforming image classifiers into object detectors. While there are some concerns regarding the paper's quality and originality, the results are significant and valuable for the community. With some additional comparisons to baselines and clearer explanations of the experiments, this paper has the potential to make a strong contribution to the field.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper presents a well-motivated and logical approach to transforming image classifiers into object detectors.
* The results are significant and valuable for the community, demonstrating the potential to enable detection for tens of thousands of categories.
* The paper is well-written and clear, making it easy to follow and understand.

* The paper lacks comparisons to three obvious baselines that could challenge its claims.
