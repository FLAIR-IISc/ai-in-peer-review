This paper proposes Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), an algorithm that adapts a Krizhevsky CNN trained for classification to detection by learning the difference between the two tasks and transferring this knowledge to classifiers for categories without bounding box annotated data. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their approach on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge, achieving a 50% relative mAP boost over a baseline of just using raw classifier weights on object proposal regions.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed algorithm and its components. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the results, including an ablation study that highlights the importance of each component of the algorithm. The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results.

The quality of the paper is high, and the results are interesting and compelling. The authors demonstrate a significant improvement in detection performance on the held-out categories, which is a challenging task. The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the errors made by the system, which is useful for understanding the limitations of the approach.

However, the paper lacks comparisons to some obvious baselines that could challenge its claims. For example, the authors do not compare their approach to a full-image positives with region negatives baseline, a MIL-based region selection baseline, or a center/mean-box classifier baseline. These comparisons would be useful for understanding the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

The originality of the paper is sufficient for acceptance, but the approach is relatively specific to certain CNN architectures. The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how their approach could be applied to other architectures or domains.

The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses a difficult problem in object detection and provides a novel solution. The results are important and could have a significant impact on the field. The paper also provides a unique contribution to the field, as it is the first method to adapt to held-out categories that have no detection data.

However, there is a concern about attribution, as the results might be due to the strong CNN features rather than the superior weakly-supervised detector training method. The authors should provide more analysis to address this concern.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel algorithm for adapting a Krizhevsky CNN trained for classification to detection.
* The authors demonstrate a significant improvement in detection performance on the held-out categories.
* The paper provides a thorough analysis of the results, including an ablation study.
* The paper addresses a difficult problem in object detection and provides a unique contribution to the field.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper lacks comparisons to some obvious baselines that could challenge its claims.
* The approach is relatively specific to certain CNN architectures.
* There is a concern about attribution, as the results might be due to the strong CNN features rather than the superior weakly-supervised detector training method.

Revisions:

* Add comparisons to some obvious baselines that could challenge the claims of the paper.
* Provide a clear explanation of how the approach could be applied to other architectures or domains.
* Address the concern about attribution by providing more analysis to demonstrate the superiority of the weakly-supervised detector training method.
