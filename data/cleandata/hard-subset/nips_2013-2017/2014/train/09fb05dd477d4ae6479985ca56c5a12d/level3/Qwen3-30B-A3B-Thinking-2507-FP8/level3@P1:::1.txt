

This paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a method to adapt object classifiers trained on image-level labels into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for the target categories. The authors recognize that while image-level classification data is abundant (e.g., ImageNet's 20,000+ categories), bounding box annotations for detection are scarce and expensive to collect. Their key insight is to learn a transformation from classification to detection using a subset of categories with bounding box data, then apply this transformation to categories lacking bounding box annotations.

The work builds on recent advances in deep learning for object detection (R-CNN) and domain adaptation, treating the classification-to-detection transfer as a domain adaptation problem. LSDA introduces three key components: (1) recognizing background as critical for detection adaptation, (2) transferring category-invariant information between classifier and detector feature representations, and (3) applying category-specific adaptations using nearest-neighbor techniques for categories without bounding box data. The method is straightforward: pre-train a CNN on classification data, fine-tune layers 1-7 on a subset of categories with bounding box annotations, then apply category-specific adaptation to the remaining categories.

The experimental evaluation on ImageNet LSVRC-2013 demonstrates LSDA's effectiveness. The authors train on 100 categories with bounding box annotations and evaluate on 100 held-out categories with only image-level labels. Their approach achieves a 50% relative mAP improvement over the baseline classifier applied directly to region proposals (15.85% vs 10.31% mAP on held-out categories). This fills approximately 35% of the gap between the baseline and the oracle (full detection training on all 200 categories). The paper also demonstrates a 7.6K category detector, showing the scalability of their approach.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a meaningful problem. The method is clearly explained and the experiments are thorough. The authors provide a detailed ablation study showing that category-invariant adaptation (fine-tuning layers 1-7) is the most important component, while category-specific adaptation (using nearest neighbors) provides additional, statistically significant gains (16.15% mAP with k=10). The error analysis demonstrates that LSDA successfully reduces localization errors and background confusion, which are critical for detection.

However, the significance of the problem requires careful consideration. While the authors correctly note that bounding box annotations are expensive to collect, it's worth questioning whether this scarcity will persist as the field advances. As more resources are invested in annotation efforts (e.g., through crowdsourcing or automated tools), the problem may become less pressing. Additionally, the trade-off between the number of bounding box annotations required and the resulting mAP improvement is not clearly quantified. The claim that their method "fills 50% of the gap" is misleading; the actual improvement is about one-third of the gap between baseline and oracle (15.85% vs 10.31% baseline, 22.3% oracle), not 50% relative to the oracle.

The paper's novelty is somewhat limited, as it combines existing adaptation techniques rather than introducing fundamentally new ideas. While the combination is clever and effective, it's not a major breakthrough. The authors could strengthen their contribution by providing a dedicated table comparing their method with R-CNN and other adaptation approaches, which would clarify the differences more effectively than the current Figure 3a (which doesn't specify which methods use category labels for detection). Figure 6 (showing top detections for the 7.6K model) could be removed to make space for this comparative table.

The paper contains a few minor issues that need attention: inconsistent use of "DNN" and "DDA" as method names, a typo on line 431 ("classi?ers"), and the main result in Figure 3b should be emphasized as the key finding (the performance on held-out categories, not the 78% claim which is misleading).

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of object detection. The method is simple, effective, and clearly presented. While not a ground-breaking contribution, it addresses a practical problem with significant implications for scaling detection to large numbers of categories. The results are well-supported by experiments, and the approach could be valuable for practitioners working with limited annotation resources.

Strengths: Clear problem statement, well-executed experiments, strong ablation study, practical scalability demonstration, high-quality writing.

Weaknesses: Limited novelty (combines existing ideas), somewhat misleading claims about performance improvement, unclear significance of the problem in the long term, minor formatting issues.

Recommendation: Accept. This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of object detection that is both technically sound and practically relevant. While not a breakthrough, it provides a useful method for scaling detection to large numbers of categories with limited annotation resources, and the results are robust and well-documented. The minor issues noted can be addressed in the camera-ready version.
