This paper proposes LSDA, an algorithm to adapt classifiers trained on large image datasets (with image-level labels) into object detectors, leveraging the power of deep convolutional neural networks. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain to the detection domain using categories with both classification and bounding box annotations, and then apply this transformation to categories with only classification data. This is a compelling approach to address the scarcity of labeled bounding box data for many object categories. The work builds on the success of R-CNN and the availability of large-scale classification datasets like ImageNet.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the experimental results are compelling, demonstrating a significant improvement over a baseline that uses raw classifier weights. The analysis, particularly the ablation study in Figure 3, supports the claims and provides insight into the contribution of each component of the LSDA algorithm. However, a crucial weakness lies in the lack of comparison to several key baselines, which limits the confidence in the superiority of the proposed method (see below). The paper feels largely driven by the power of the Krizhevsky CNN rather than a fundamentally new detection methodology. While this is not inherently negative, the extent to which the gains are due to the CNN itself versus the adaptation method needs further investigation.

**Clarity:** The paper is well written and generally easy to follow. However, the experiments section (specifically Table 1) lacks sufficient detail. It’s unclear exactly *what* each method corresponds to in terms of equations or specific layers modified. A more detailed explanation of the experimental setup and hyperparameter choices would also be beneficial. The motivation behind specific design choices (e.g., using k-nearest neighbors for adapting category-specific parameters) could be elaborated further.

**Originality:** The approach is sufficiently novel for acceptance. While building upon existing work (R-CNN, domain adaptation), the specific combination of adapting classifiers trained on large-scale classification data to detection in a weakly-supervised manner is a valuable contribution. However, the method is relatively specific to certain CNN architectures (e.g., Krizhevsky’s network) and doesn’t represent a highly innovative architectural change.

**Significance:** The problem addressed – scaling object detection to a large number of categories with limited labeled data – is highly significant. The results are valuable to the computer vision and machine learning communities, particularly those interested in weakly-supervised learning and large-scale recognition. The 7.6K detector demonstrates the potential for scaling detection to a much broader range of categories. The release of models and software is a positive aspect, encouraging further research and application.

**Strengths:**
* Addresses a critical problem: scaling object detection with limited labeled data.
* Demonstrates significant improvement over a simple baseline.
* Well-written and clearly motivated.
* Publicly available models and code.
* Thorough ablation study identifying key components.

**Weaknesses:**
* **Missing Baselines:** The paper lacks comparison to three obvious and crucial baselines: (1) Using full-image positives with region proposals as negatives, (2) a MIL-based region selection method (similar to Ali et al. [6] or Song et al. [7]), and (3) a simple mean-box classifier baseline (predicting the mean bounding box for a category when detected).  The absence of these baselines significantly weakens the conclusions.
* **Baseline Performance Concerns:** I am unconvinced that the proposed method outperforms baseline 1 (full-image positives). This comparison *must* be included.  I also suspect that baseline 2 (MIL-based selection) could be competitive, and its inclusion is essential.
* **Dataset Difficulty:** Including the mean-box baseline (baseline 3) is important for understanding how difficult the dataset/problem actually is. A strong performance on this baseline would indicate that the task is relatively easy, while a poor performance would highlight the challenges of accurate localization.
* **Attribution of Results:** The improvements observed might be largely attributable to the strong CNN features inherited from the pre-trained classifier, rather than the proposed adaptation method itself.  Further analysis is needed to disentangle these effects.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and challenging problem.
* Demonstrates a clear improvement over a reasonable baseline.
* Well-written and clearly explained.
* Provides publicly available resources.
* Offers a practical solution for scaling object detection.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Lack of crucial baseline comparisons weakens the claims.
* The extent to which the gains are due to the CNN versus the adaptation method is unclear.
* Experiments section lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper addresses a significant problem and presents a compelling approach. However, the authors *must* include the missing baseline comparisons to strengthen their claims and provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their method. More detail in the experiments section, particularly regarding Table 1, would also be beneficial. Addressing these points will significantly improve the quality and impact of the work.
