

# Review for NIPS 2014

This paper introduces LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a method to transform object classifiers into detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The key insight is that the transformation from classification to detection can be learned on a subset of categories and transferred to categories lacking bounding box annotations. The authors frame this as a domain adaptation problem between classification (source domain) and detection (target domain), and propose a simple yet effective three-step adaptation process: (1) recognizing background as an important step, (2) transferring category-invariant information between feature representations, and (3) applying category-specific adaptation using nearest neighbors.

The paper builds on R-CNN (Girshick et al.), which demonstrated that fine-tuning classification networks for detection significantly improves performance but requires bounding box data for all categories. LSDA addresses this limitation by requiring bounding box data for only a subset of categories (100 out of 200 in their experiments) while still enabling detection for all categories. The authors demonstrate their approach on ImageNet detection, achieving a 50% relative mAP improvement over a classification-only baseline on held-out categories.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is technically sound with a clear experimental design. The ablation study (Figure 3) effectively demonstrates the contribution of each component of the method.
- The approach is conceptually simple and straightforward, making it easy to implement and understand.
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with sufficient detail for reproducibility.
- The results are significant for enabling detection for tens of thousands of categories (demonstrated with a 7,604 category detector), which addresses an important practical limitation in scaling object detection.
- The authors provide a thorough error analysis showing that LSDA successfully reduces localization errors and background confusion compared to the baseline.

**Weaknesses:**
- The claimed 50% relative mAP improvement is misleading. The baseline (classification-only network applied to region proposals) is not directly comparable to the oracle (full detection data) performance. The main result should be that LSDA achieves approximately one-third of the way between baseline and oracle performance (as shown in Figure 3b), not a 50% improvement relative to baseline. This misrepresentation needs clarification.
- The significance of the problem is questionable. The authors note that bounding box scarcity may be solved through resource investment (e.g., crowdsourcing), which could diminish the long-term importance of this work. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of when LSDA would be the optimal approach compared to simply collecting more bounding box data.
- Figure 3a lacks clarity on which methods use category labels for detection, making fair comparison difficult. A dedicated table comparing LSDA to R-CNN and other methods would improve clarity.
- Figure 6 (showing 7,604 category detections) is not essential to the main contribution and could be removed to make space for the proposed comparison table.
- The paper inconsistently uses "DNN" and "DDA" for the method name, and contains a typo on line 431 ("classi?ers").

**Originality:** LSDA combines two existing techniques (domain adaptation and classifier-to-detector transformation) in a novel way for the detection task. While the individual components are not groundbreaking, their combination for this specific application is novel and well-motivated. The approach differs significantly from previous domain adaptation methods, which typically adapt between visual domains while keeping the task fixed, whereas LSDA adapts both from a large visual domain to a smaller one and from classification to detection.

**Significance:** The problem addressed (scaling detection to thousands of categories) is important, but the paper doesn't sufficiently argue why this is more valuable than simply collecting more bounding box data. The 50% relative improvement claim is overstated, and the performance gap between LSDA and oracle (full detection data) is still significant. The contribution is valuable for enabling detection for categories without bounding box data, but its long-term importance may be limited as bounding box annotation methods improve.

**Arguments for acceptance:** 
- Clear, well-executed experiments demonstrating a practical solution to a meaningful problem
- Simple and effective approach with significant potential applications
- High-quality writing and clear presentation
- Demonstrates a real-world capability (7,604 category detector)

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Misleading presentation of results (50% relative improvement vs. one-third of the gap to oracle)
- Questionable long-term significance of the problem addressed
- Minor issues with presentation and terminology that need correction

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper presents a clear, well-executed solution to an important practical problem in object detection. While the claimed improvements need clearer presentation and the significance could be better justified, the core contribution is valuable, technically sound, and likely to be useful to the community. With minor revisions to clarify the results and improve the presentation of the comparison to R-CNN, this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS. The authors should revise the results section to clarify the 50% claim and add a dedicated comparison table with R-CNN.
