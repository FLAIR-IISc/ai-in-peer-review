Summary: The paper proposes LSDA, a simple adaptation strategy that learns a classifier-to-detector transformation from a subset of categories with bounding boxes and transfers it to held-out categories with only image-level labels. Concretely, a CNN pre-trained for classification is fine-tuned on detection for m categories to learn background modeling and a category-invariant shift; category-specific deltas are then approximated for held-out classes via nearest-neighbor averaging in the classifier weight space. The experimental protocol is clean: train with boxes+labels for 100 ImageNet detection categories and evaluate on 100 held-out categories with labels only. Relative to prior work (R-CNN fine-tuning for detection; weakly supervised MIL; shallow domain adaptation), LSDA combines ideas from fine-tuning, background modeling, and simple parameter transfer to reduce bounding-box supervision.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound and the ablations support the claims that (i) background modeling and (ii) feature-level adaptation are the main drivers, with smaller gains from the category-specific delta. That said, the headline “78%/50% relative” improvements are potentially misleading; the main result should be reported as the fraction of the oracle gap closed on the held-out categories, which is approximately one-third (≈35%). Please report absolute mAP, gap-closed metrics, and confidence intervals consistently. Also clarify in Figure 3a which competing methods, besides the authors’ method and R-CNN, use category labels for detection to ensure a fair comparison.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. Minor issues: inconsistent method name (“DNN” vs “DDA”) and a typo on line 431 (“classi?ers”). I recommend adding a dedicated table/subsection that itemizes the differences from R-CNN (background class, removal of SVM/BBR, adaptation steps, training time), potentially removing Figure 6 to make space.

Originality: The contribution is a novel combination of existing adaptation ideas rather than a fundamentally new paradigm. Nonetheless, the formulation is crisp and interesting.

Significance: The contribution is practical and will likely influence follow-up work; however, it may be surpassed by stronger weak/semi/self-supervised methods. The trade-off between fewer boxes and mAP remains unclear—please provide a curve varying m and annotation cost. The long-term importance is debatable if bounding-box scarcity can be alleviated by annotation investment.

Pros (accept): simple, well-executed idea; clear experimental setup; closes ~35% of the oracle gap; code/models released.
Cons (reject): incremental originality; ambiguous headline numbers; unclear cost-performance trade-off; uncertain long-term impact.
