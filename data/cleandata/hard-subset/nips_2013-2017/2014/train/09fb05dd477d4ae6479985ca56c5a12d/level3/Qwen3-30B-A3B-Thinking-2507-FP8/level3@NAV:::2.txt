

This paper introduces LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a framework for adapting image classification CNNs into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The authors propose a domain adaptation approach that leverages classification data for all categories while using detection data for only a subset. The method demonstrates promising results on ImageNet LSVRC-2013, achieving 4th place in the detection challenge and enabling detection for 7,604 categories using only classification data for most categories.

The paper is clearly written with a well-structured presentation of the methodology, making it easy to follow. The CNN architecture is simple to implement, and the experimental results demonstrate significant improvements (50% relative mAP) over classification-only baselines. The authors effectively relate their work to previous methods like R-CNN and domain adaptation techniques, showing how LSDA differs by enabling adaptation to held-out categories without detection data.

However, several critical weaknesses undermine the paper's scientific contribution. The adaptation method lacks principled justification - the key equation Wj^c + W_avg in Section 3.2 appears arbitrary without explanation of its physical meaning or theoretical basis. The authors fail to explain why kNN adaptation outperforms other variants, and the comparison to R-CNN is misleading as they use val2 (a small validation subset) instead of the actual test set. The claim of R-CNN's similar performance on val2 is statistically unsound due to the small size of val2.

While the method achieves comparable performance to state-of-the-art, the experimental setup compromises the validity of the comparison. The paper would benefit from stronger theoretical motivation for the adaptation technique and more rigorous evaluation protocols. The significance of the work is limited by these methodological concerns, though the potential to scale detection to thousands of categories is noteworthy.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting approach with clear presentation and promising results, but lacks sufficient theoretical justification and has methodological flaws in its evaluation. The technical contribution is incremental rather than transformative, and the adaptation method requires better explanation to be considered a solid scientific contribution.
