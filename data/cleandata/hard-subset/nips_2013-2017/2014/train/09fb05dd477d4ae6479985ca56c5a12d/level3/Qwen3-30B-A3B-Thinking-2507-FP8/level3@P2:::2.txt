

# Review for NIPS 2014

This paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a method to transform image classifiers into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The key insight is that the transformation from classification to detection can be learned on a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations and then transferred to categories without such annotations. This addresses the major challenge of scaling object detection to large numbers of categories, where obtaining bounding box annotations is prohibitively expensive.

The paper builds upon R-CNN [1] which demonstrated that fine-tuning a classification network improves detection performance, but required bounding box data for all categories. LSDA instead treats the classification-to-detection transformation as a domain adaptation problem, learning a generic transformation from the source domain (classification data) to the target domain (detection data). The authors also relate their work to MIL approaches [6,7] for weakly supervised detection, noting their complementary nature.

**Strengths:**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making the method easy to follow. The CNN architecture is straightforward and simple to implement. The 7.6K detector demonstration is impressive and showcases the practical potential of the approach. The ablation study effectively demonstrates the contribution of each component of the LSDA framework, with the category invariant adaptation proving most valuable (50% relative mAP boost over the classification-only baseline).

**Weaknesses:**
The main contribution, the adaptation method, lacks principled justification. The paper does not provide a clear theoretical foundation for why the adaptation technique works, nor does it thoroughly explain the physical meaning of the "offset" parameter used in the weight adaptation formula (Wj^d = Wj^c + 1/k Σ δB_NB(j,i)). The justification for using k-nearest neighbors for weight adaptation appears arbitrary, and the paper fails to explain why this variant outperforms others.

The evaluation methodology contains significant issues. The authors use part of the validation set (val1) for training detection data instead of the standard test set, which makes comparisons unfair. The claim that R-CNN achieves similar performance on val2 is not statistically justified due to val2's small size (20K images), and the paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing for their main results.

**Quality Assessment:**
The technical approach is sound, with appropriate use of domain adaptation concepts. The method is complete and well-evaluated on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 dataset, though the evaluation protocol is flawed. The authors are careful about evaluating strengths (the 50% mAP improvement) but less so about weaknesses (the lack of theoretical justification for the adaptation method).

**Clarity Assessment:**
The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The writing is precise, and the method is described in sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the results. The figures effectively illustrate the key concepts and results.

**Originality Assessment:**
The problem of scaling detection to large numbers of categories is novel and important, and the approach of adapting classifiers to detectors using a small subset of detection data represents a meaningful contribution. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from related work, particularly in the domain adaptation literature [12-16] and MIL-based detection [6,7]. The adaptation technique itself appears to be a practical engineering solution rather than a novel theoretical advance.

**Significance Assessment:**
The paper addresses an important practical challenge in computer vision: scaling detection to large numbers of categories. The demonstration of a 7.6K detector is significant, and the approach could potentially enable detection for tens of thousands of categories. However, the performance gains compared to state-of-the-art (ranking 4th in the detection challenge) are not sufficiently compelling given the methodological issues in the evaluation.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

*For acceptance:* The problem is important, the solution is practical and well-motivated, the paper is clearly written, and the results on the 7.6K detector demonstrate significant potential.

*Against acceptance:* The adaptation method lacks theoretical justification, the evaluation methodology is flawed (using validation data for training), and the comparison with R-CNN is not statistically sound. The performance gains compared to state-of-the-art are marginal, and the paper doesn't sufficiently establish how this work differs from previous domain adaptation approaches.

Overall, the paper presents a promising practical approach to scaling object detection, but the lack of principled justification for the adaptation method and the flawed evaluation protocol significantly undermine its scientific contribution. The method achieves comparable performance to state-of-the-art, but the comparisons use different settings and the adaptation method needs better explanation. I recommend rejection, but would be willing to accept if the authors could provide a more principled justification for their adaptation technique and re-evaluate using the standard test set.
