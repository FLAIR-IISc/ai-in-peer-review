- Proposal: LSDA adapts image classifiers into object detectors via domain adaptation to detect categories without bounding-box annotations.  
- Core idea: Frame classifier-to-detector adaptation as a domain shift problem, extending R-CNN by transferring detection knowledge from a subset of categories.  
- Results: On ImageNet LSVRC-2013, LSDA shows ~50% relative mAP improvement over classification-only baselines on 100 held-out categories and demonstrates a 7,604-category detector.  
- Strength — clarity and reproducibility: Paper is exceptionally clear, practical CNN architecture, and authors release code/models.  
- Strength — ablation: Ablation shows feature-level adaptation (fine-tuning layers 1–7) gives largest gain; category-specific adaptation adds incremental improvement.  
- Strength — scalability and speed: Demonstrates large-scale (7.6K) detection and a 2 fps speed optimization, showing real-world applicability.  
- Weakness — methodological justification: Lacks principled motivation for the nearest-neighbor averaging formula (Eq. 1) and the physical interpretation of the offset δB.  
- Weakness — hyperparameter choice: Choice of k=10 is empirically chosen without theoretical justification or explanation why it outperforms alternatives.  
- Weakness — statistical validity: Comparison to R-CNN uses the small val2 set (20K images), making claims of comparable performance statistically invalid.  
- Weakness — unsupported claims: Claim of ranking 4th in the detection challenge is not substantiated and lacks comparison to other SOTA methods (e.g., Fast R-CNN).  
- Originality: Novel application of domain adaptation to enable detection for unseen categories, addressing a key scalability limitation.  
- Limitations in novelty: Core technique draws on existing domain adaptation ideas and should be better contrasted with MIL-based weakly-supervised detectors and prior adaptation work.  
- Quality & clarity: Experiments are well-executed and writing is clear, but insufficient statistical rigor and justification for key design choices weaken the contribution.  
- Recommendation & required revisions: Borderline accept with major revisions — authors must (1) justify Eq.1 and k=10, (2) re-run comparisons on a held-out test set, and (3) clarify relations to prior weakly-supervised and adaptation work.
