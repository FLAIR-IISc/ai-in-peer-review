## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper presents LSDA, an algorithm for adapting classifiers trained on large datasets (like ImageNet classification data) into object detectors, particularly focusing on scenarios where bounding box annotations are scarce. The core idea is to learn a transformation between the classification and detection tasks and then apply this transformation to classifiers lacking detection data, effectively creating detectors for a large number of categories. This is a compelling approach addressing a key bottleneck in scaling object detection – the cost of annotation. The work builds logically on the success of CNNs for both classification and detection (e.g., Krizhevsky et al., Girshick et al.) and recognizes the potential of leveraging abundant classification data.

The strengths of the paper lie in its simplicity and potential significance. The proposed method is conceptually straightforward and easy to understand. The results demonstrate a substantial improvement over a baseline relying solely on classifier weights, suggesting the adaptation process is beneficial.  The ability to create a 7.6K detector, even with limited bounding box data, is impressive and has clear value to the community.  The code release is also a positive aspect, enabling reproducibility and further research. The paper is well-written and clearly presents the motivation, method, and experiments. 

However, the paper lacks crucial baseline comparisons that weaken the strength of its claims. The authors do not compare against three obvious and important baselines: (1) using full-image positives with region negatives (a simple yet effective approach), (2) employing Multiple Instance Learning (MIL)-based region selection methods (as mentioned in the related work), and (3) a simple mean-box classifier baseline which would quickly establish how challenging the dataset/problem is.  It's unclear if LSDA significantly outperforms these approaches, especially baseline 1 and 2.  Furthermore, the experiments section needs more clarity.  Specifically, Table 1 should explicitly state which equations or layers are applied for each method variation, making it easier to follow the ablation study.

The originality of the work is sufficient for acceptance, offering a novel combination of techniques.  However, it's somewhat specific to CNN architectures like Krizhevsky’s, and the innovation isn’t radical; it’s more of a practical and effective application of existing principles. A key concern is that the observed improvements might be primarily attributable to the strong features learned by the underlying CNN, rather than the proposed adaptation method itself. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** Addresses a significant problem, presents a logical and reasonably effective solution, and has clear potential for impact.
**Arguments against acceptance:**  Lacks key baseline comparisons, requires clarification in the experiments section, and the results may be heavily reliant on the strength of the underlying CNN.



Overall, this is a solid contribution with good potential, but the lack of baseline comparisons raises concerns. I recommend acceptance with the request that the authors include the suggested baselines in their evaluation.
