This paper proposes Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a framework to adapt deep neural networks trained for image classification into object detectors. The key motivation is to overcome the scarcity of bounding box annotations, which severely limits the number of categories for which modern detectors can be trained. In contrast, image-level classification labels are available for tens of thousands of categories. LSDA frames this as a domain adaptation problem, learning a transformation from a "classification network" to a "detection network" using a subset of categories for which both classification and detection data are available. This learned transformation is then applied to classifiers for new categories that lack any bounding box data.

The method consists of two main adaptation steps. First, a category-invariant adaptation is performed by fine-tuning the shared feature-learning layers (conv1-fc7) of a pre-trained classification network on the detection task, using the available bounding box data. This step also learns a generic background class, which is crucial for detection. Second, a category-specific adaptation adjusts the final classification layer's weights. For categories without detection data, this is done by averaging the weight adjustments (offsets) learned from their k-nearest neighbors in the original classification weight space. This approach builds upon the R-CNN framework but crucially extends it to categories without detection labels, a significant departure from prior work that typically requires per-category bounding box supervision for fine-tuning.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a highly significant and practical problem in computer vision: scaling object detection beyond the few hundred categories with available bounding box data. The core idea of learning a transferable mapping from a classifier to a detector is novel and presents a promising direction for leveraging large-scale, weakly-labeled classification datasets for the detection task.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is well-established, and the proposed method is described with sufficient detail. The figures, particularly the architectural diagram (Fig. 2) and the ablation study results (Fig. 3), are effective at illustrating the core concepts and contributions.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates a substantial +50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories compared to a classification-only baseline. The ablation study in Table 1 effectively dissects the contribution of each component of the LSDA framework, showing that both category-invariant and category-specific adaptations are beneficial. The release of a 7604-category detector is a valuable contribution to the community and showcases the practical utility of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Principled Justification for Adaptation:** The main weakness of the paper is the heuristic nature of the proposed category-specific adaptation method. The paper posits that the transformation can be modeled as an additive offset (`W_d = W_c + δ`), but provides little theoretical or empirical justification for this choice over other potential transformations. It is unclear what this "offset" represents conceptually. Furthermore, the strategy of averaging the offsets from k-nearest neighbors is intuitive but lacks a deeper motivation. The paper reports that k=10 works best but offers no analysis as to why this local average is superior to a global average (k=|B|) or other potential aggregation schemes. A more principled formulation or at least a more detailed discussion of the design choices would significantly strengthen the paper.

2.  **Non-Standard Evaluation Protocol:** The experimental setup raises concerns about the fairness and generalizability of the results. The authors split the official ILSVRC 2013 validation set into `val1` (used for training) and `val2` (used for evaluation). This is a non-standard protocol, as most published results on this dataset use the entire official training set for training and the entire validation set for evaluation. Using part of the validation set for training makes direct and fair comparison to other state-of-the-art methods difficult. The authors should provide a strong justification for this choice or, ideally, report results using the standard `train`/`val` split.

3.  **Potentially Unjustified Statistical Claims:** The paper claims that the difference in performance between using the kNN adaptation and the full average is statistically significant. However, a similar level of rigor is not applied when comparing to other methods. For instance, any implicit or explicit claims of being "close to" the performance of a fully-supervised R-CNN on the `val2` set should be treated with caution. Given the small size of `val2`, performance differences may not be statistically meaningful, and comparisons to results from other papers which used a different training/testing protocol are problematic.

In summary, the paper introduces a novel and promising method for a very important problem. The approach is simple, effective, and achieves strong results on a challenging benchmark. However, the work is weakened by the lack of a principled justification for the core adaptation mechanism and the use of a non-standard evaluation protocol that complicates fair comparison with prior art. Despite these weaknesses, the paper's contribution is significant enough to be of broad interest to the NIPS community. Addressing the concerns about the adaptation model's motivation and the evaluation setup would make this a much stronger paper.
