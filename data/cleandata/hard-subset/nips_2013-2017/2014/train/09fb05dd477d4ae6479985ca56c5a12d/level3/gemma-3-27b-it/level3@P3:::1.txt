LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation is a compelling paper addressing the crucial problem of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, hampered by the scarcity of bounding box annotations. The core idea – adapting classifiers trained on abundant image-level labels into detectors – is intuitively sound and well-motivated. The authors present a straightforward algorithm, LSDA, that leverages domain adaptation techniques to transfer knowledge from classification to detection. The experimental setup, training on 100 categories with boxes+labels and testing on 100 held-out categories with labels only, is a clever way to isolate the adaptation capability of the method. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear, making the approach easily understandable.

However, while technically sound and presenting a valuable contribution, the paper falls slightly short of being groundbreaking. The method cleverly combines existing adaptation ideas – fine-tuning and nearest neighbor weighting – rather than introducing entirely novel concepts. The significance of the work is also somewhat muted by the trade-off between reduced annotation effort and mAP performance, which isn’t fully elucidated. Furthermore, the long-term importance of the problem itself is questionable, given the potential for resource investment to overcome bounding-box scarcity.

**Quality:** The paper is technically strong. The ablation study (Figure 3) provides evidence supporting the effectiveness of each component. The experimental design is rigorous, and the results are presented clearly. The authors acknowledge limitations and potential avenues for future work.

**Clarity:** The paper excels in clarity. The algorithm is explained in a logical and concise manner. Figures 1 and 2 effectively illustrate the adaptation process. The writing is precise and avoids unnecessary jargon.

**Originality:** The originality lies in the specific combination of existing techniques—domain adaptation applied to transfer knowledge from classifiers to detectors, coupled with nearest-neighbor weighting for categories lacking bounding box data. While not a revolutionary breakthrough, this combination is novel and demonstrates a practical approach to a significant challenge. Related work is adequately referenced, although a more thorough discussion of recent advancements in weakly supervised detection might be beneficial.

**Significance:** The contribution is significant in demonstrating a viable path towards large-scale detection with minimal bounding box annotation. However, the overall performance improvement (filling ~35% of the gap between oracle and baseline, as highlighted in Figure 3b) suggests that substantial performance gains are still needed to match fully supervised approaches. The practical utility depends on the acceptable mAP trade-off for a given application.

**Specific Concerns and Suggestions:**

* **Clarification of Comparison in Figure 3a:** The paper should explicitly state which methods, besides LSDA and R-CNN, utilize category labels for detection in Figure 3a, to ensure a fair comparison. Are these methods using MIL or other techniques exploiting image-level labels?
* **Focus on Figure 3b:** The authors should emphasize the DNN performance presented in Figure 3b (~35% gap filling) as the main result. The claims of 78% and 50% improvement are misleading because they refer to relative improvements over a weak baseline (classifier-only), which doesn't accurately reflect the true performance gain in absolute terms.
* **Dedicated Comparison to R-CNN:** A dedicated table or subsection comparing LSDA to R-CNN, outlining the key differences in training data requirements, computational cost, and performance characteristics, would significantly enhance the paper. Consider removing Figure 6 to create space for this comparison. Specifically, detail the differences in fine-tuning procedures, output layer handling, and the use of SVMs/bounding box regression.
* **Minor Edits:** Correct the typo on line 431 ("classi?ers") and ensure consistent usage of the method name ("DNN" vs. "LSDA").

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a critical problem in scaling object detection.
* Presents a simple and effective algorithm.
* Demonstrates a measurable improvement in performance on held-out categories.
* Exceptionally well-written and clear.

**Con:**

* Incremental advance rather than a groundbreaking innovation.
* Significance is limited by the mAP trade-off.
* Long-term relevance may diminish with increased resources for bounding box annotation.
* Overstated performance claims based on relative improvements.



**Overall Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of object detection, offering a practical approach to address the scarcity of bounding box annotations. Addressing the points raised above will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
