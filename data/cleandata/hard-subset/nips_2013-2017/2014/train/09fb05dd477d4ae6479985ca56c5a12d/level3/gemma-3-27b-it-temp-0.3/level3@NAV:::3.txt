## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper proposes LSDA, an algorithm to adapt classifiers trained on large, weakly-labeled datasets (like ImageNet) into object detectors, particularly for categories lacking bounding box annotations. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain to the detection domain using categories with both types of labels, and then apply this transformation to classifiers for unseen categories. This is a compelling approach to address the significant bottleneck of obtaining labeled bounding box data for scaling object detection to a large number of categories. The work builds upon the success of deep CNNs for classification (Krizhevsky et al.) and the initial adaptation work of R-CNN (Girshick et al.), framing the problem as a domain adaptation task. 

The strengths of this paper lie in its simplicity and logical flow. The three-stage adaptation process – category invariant, category specific, and test-time scoring – is well-defined and easy to understand. The experimental results on ImageNet demonstrate a significant improvement over a baseline using raw classifier weights, and the ability to scale to a 7.6K detector is impressive. The release of models and code is a valuable contribution to the community. The problem addressed is significant, and the results suggest a valuable contribution to enabling detection for a vast number of categories.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of comparison to crucial baselines. The authors acknowledge MIL-based region selection and a center/mean-box classifier baseline, but do not include them in their evaluation. It’s unclear whether LSDA outperforms a simple full-image positive with region negatives baseline, and this comparison is essential to establish the value of the proposed adaptation method. The authors’ claim of a 50% relative mAP boost is less convincing without these comparisons. Furthermore, the experiments section lacks clarity. Table 1 presents results for different methods, but the correspondence between these methods and the equations/layers described in the text is not clearly explained. 

A key concern is the attribution of results. While the authors demonstrate improved detection performance, it’s difficult to disentangle whether this is due to the adaptation method itself or simply the strong features learned by the underlying Krizhevsky CNN. The paper doesn’t sufficiently address this point. The originality is sufficient for acceptance, but the approach is relatively specific to CNN architectures and isn’t a radical departure from existing techniques.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Addresses a significant problem, demonstrates a clear improvement over a basic baseline, well-written and logically structured.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Lacks crucial baseline comparisons, clarity issues in the experimental section, concern about attribution of results to the adaptation method versus the underlying CNN features.

**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the missing baseline comparisons and clarify the experimental section to improve the paper's rigor and impact.
