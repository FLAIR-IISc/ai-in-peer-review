## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper proposes Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a method for transferring knowledge from image classification networks to object detectors, particularly in scenarios where bounding box annotations are scarce. The core idea is to learn the transformation between the classification and detection tasks and apply this learned transformation to classifiers lacking bounding box data, effectively turning them into detectors. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their approach on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge, achieving competitive performance and enabling detection across a large number of categories (7.6K).

The problem addressed is highly relevant. Scaling object detection to a large number of categories is hampered by the expensive and time-consuming process of acquiring bounding box annotations. Leveraging the abundance of image-level labels available through search engines presents a compelling alternative. This work builds upon the recent success of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for both classification and detection, notably drawing inspiration from R-CNN [1]’s adaptation of ImageNet classifiers.  Related work in Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) [6, 7] and domain adaptation [12-16] is appropriately discussed, though the authors clearly position their work as distinct, focusing on adaptation to *held-out* categories without any detection data.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The presentation is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The method is described in a straightforward manner, and the architecture is relatively simple, making it potentially accessible for implementation and extension. The experimental setup is clearly defined, and the authors have released their models and code, which is a commendable practice. The results on ImageNet are strong, demonstrating a significant improvement (+50% relative mAP) over a baseline and competitive performance within the challenge. The large-scale detector (7.6K categories) is a notable achievement, showcasing the potential of the approach to address the challenge of detecting a vast number of object categories.  The speed improvement achieved through spatial pyramid pooling [28] is also a valuable contribution.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The core contribution, the adaptation method, lacks a strong principled justification. While the authors state the intuition behind their three key insights (background recognition, category invariant information transfer, and category-specific differences), the method itself feels somewhat *ad hoc*. The choice of adapting layers 1-7 and the specific implementation of category-specific adaptation could benefit from a more rigorous theoretical grounding. Specifically, the equation in Section 3.2 (Wdj = Wcj + 1/k ∑ δBNB(j,i)) is not sufficiently explained; the rationale for averaging the changes from k-nearest neighbors and the physical meaning of the resulting “offset” parameter are unclear.  Why does this specific approach work better than other possible aggregation methods?

Furthermore, the experiments, while comprehensive, contain some potential issues. The authors acknowledge that they used a portion of the validation set for training, which isn’t ideal and limits the generalizability of the reported performance. The comparison to R-CNN performance on val2 seems weakly justified, given the small size of that dataset, making statistical significance difficult to establish. While the ablation study provides insights into the importance of each adaptation component, the relatively small performance gains from category-specific adaptation raise questions about its overall contribution. The error analysis is informative, but the examples shown (Figure 5) don't give a holistic view of the error distribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates a practical approach to scaling object detection by leveraging abundant image-level labels.
*   Achieves competitive performance on the challenging ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection benchmark.
*   Clear presentation, well-organized, and includes publicly available code and models.
*   Successfully constructs a large-scale detector (7.6K categories).

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The adaptation method lacks strong theoretical justification and feels somewhat empirically driven.
*   Experimental setup includes using a portion of the validation set for training.
*   The explanation for the kNN approach in category-specific adaptation is unclear.
*   Some comparisons lack statistical rigor due to dataset size limitations.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of object detection, tackling the critical problem of scaling to a large number of categories. While the proposed adaptation method lacks a strong theoretical foundation and the experiments have some limitations, the strong empirical results, clear presentation, and availability of code make this a worthwhile submission. I believe that with minor revisions addressing the justification of the adaptation method and clarifying the experimental setup, this paper could be a strong addition to the NIPS proceedings. I currently rate this as a **weak accept**. Addressing the concerns regarding the theoretical justification and experimental details would significantly strengthen the paper.
