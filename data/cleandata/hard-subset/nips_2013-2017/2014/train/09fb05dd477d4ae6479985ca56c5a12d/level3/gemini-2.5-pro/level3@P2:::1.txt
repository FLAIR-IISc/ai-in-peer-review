This paper addresses the significant challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, for which bounding box annotations are expensive and difficult to obtain. The authors propose Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), an algorithm that learns to transform an image classifier into an object detector. The core idea is to treat this transformation as a domain adaptation problem. Using a set of "source" categories that have both image-level labels and bounding box annotations, LSDA learns a generic, category-invariant mapping in the lower layers of a CNN and a category-specific mapping in the final classification layer. This learned transformation can then be applied to "target" classifiers for which no bounding box data is available. The approach builds upon the R-CNN framework [1] but crucially removes the requirement for bounding box annotations for every category one wishes to detect. The method is evaluated on the ILSVRC 2013 detection dataset, where it is trained on 100 categories with full supervision and evaluated on 100 held-out categories that only used image-level labels during training.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a very practical and important problem. As the community moves towards ever-larger visual recognition systems, the bottleneck of annotation becomes increasingly severe. The idea of learning a general "classifier-to-detector" transformation is novel and provides an interesting new direction for scaling up detection without requiring exhaustive annotation for every single class.

2.  **Quality and Clarity:** The paper is of high quality. It is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and well-motivated by three key insights. The experiments are sound, and the ablation study in Figure 3 and Table 1 effectively demonstrates the contribution of each component of the LSDA algorithm. The public release of code and a 7.6K-category model is a valuable contribution to the community.

3.  **Experimental Results:** The results clearly demonstrate that the proposed adaptation method works. LSDA achieves a substantial improvement over the baseline of using a raw classifier for detection on the held-out categories. The error analysis in Section 4.3 provides valuable insight, showing that the adaptation primarily helps by reducing localization errors and confusion with background, which are the key challenges that distinguish detection from classification.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Positioning of Results:** While the results are positive, their presentation could be more balanced. The paper highlights a "50% relative mAP boost" over the baseline. A more informative and grounded way to frame the main result would be in the context of the performance gap between the baseline (10.31 mAP) and the fully-supervised oracle (approx. 27 mAP, based on Table 1). LSDA achieves 16.15 mAP, closing roughly one-third of this gap. This is a solid contribution, but framing it this way gives a clearer picture of where the method stands and how much work remains.

2.  **Significance and Practical Trade-off:** The long-term significance of the problem is worth discussing. It is possible that large-scale annotation projects may one day mitigate the scarcity of bounding boxes. More importantly, the paper does not analyze the practical trade-off the method offers. For a practitioner, the key question is: "How much mAP do I sacrifice to avoid annotating N bounding boxes?" A more explicit discussion of this cost-benefit analysis would significantly strengthen the paper's impact and help readers judge its practical utility.

3.  **Clarity of Comparison to R-CNN:** The relationship to R-CNN is fundamental, but the differences are scattered throughout the text. A dedicated subsection or table clearly outlining the differences would be very beneficial. This could compare data requirements (boxes for all vs. a subset of categories), training stages (e.g., LSDA omits the final SVM training and bounding box regression steps used in R-CNN), and training time. To make space for this, I would suggest removing Figure 6. While visually appealing, the qualitative results for the 7.6K detector are anecdotal, and a clearer technical comparison to the main prior work would be more valuable.

4.  **Experimental Clarity:** The comparison in Figure 3a needs clarification. For a fair comparison, it should be made explicit whether competing methods like Overfeat [5] were also trained under the same constraints (i.e., without bounding box data for the held-out categories). If they were not, the comparison is not on equal footing.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **For Acceptance:** The paper addresses a significant and timely problem. The proposed method is novel, intuitive, and technically sound. The work is of high quality, clearly presented, and supported by solid experimental evidence and a valuable code release.
*   **Against Acceptance:** The practical significance of the results is somewhat unclear due to a lack of analysis of the annotation-vs-performance trade-off. The performance, while a clear improvement over the baseline, still leaves a large gap to the fully supervised model, suggesting the contribution is an interesting but perhaps incremental step.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a novel and interesting contribution to an important problem. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the framing of the results and the clarity of comparison to prior work, which can be addressed in a revision.
