This paper addresses the scarcity of bounding-box annotations for large-scale object detection by learning how to adapt image classifiers into detectors. The core idea, LSDA, is to treat classification-to-detection as a domain/task adaptation problem: learn a category-invariant transformation (via fine-tuning shared layers and a background class) from categories that have detection data, then transfer that transformation to categories lacking bounding boxes; additionally, approximate category-specific deltas for held-out classes by averaging changes from their nearest neighbor categories in parameter space. The authors evaluate on ILSVRC-2013 detection by withholding detection labels for 100 categories (while using classification data for all 200), showing that adapted detectors substantially outperform applying classification nets to region proposals. They also scale to a 7.6K-category detector using only classification labels for most categories and provide a faster SPP-based variant.

Relation to prior work: The approach is closely related to R-CNN (Girshick et al.), which fine-tunes a classification network for detection but requires detection labels for all categories. LSDA differs by learning a generic classification→detection transformation from a subset and transferring it to held-out categories. It also connects to domain adaptation (feature/model adaptation) and weakly supervised detection/MIL, though LSDA does not solve MIL directly; rather, it exploits supervised detection data for only part of the label space. The work is well-situated within the deep feature transfer literature (e.g., DeCAF) and object detection pipelines using region proposals.

Quality: The method is technically straightforward and sound. The training protocol and ablations are appropriate: (i) classification-only baseline on region proposals; (ii) category-invariant adaptation by fine-tuning layers 1–7 and adding a background class using only the categories with detection data; (iii) category-specific adaptation via nearest neighbors in fc8. The held-out evaluation is well-designed and the error analysis (localization vs background vs other) is informative. The main quantitative result on the 100 held-out categories moves mAP from roughly 10.3% (classification-only) to about 16.1% with full LSDA, with an “oracle” upper bound (detection labels for all 200 classes) higher still; i.e., LSDA fills about one-third of the gap between baseline and oracle. The large-scale 7.6K demonstration is compelling qualitatively, and the released code/models increase credibility. One caveat is that some headline numbers (e.g., 50% relative improvement; 78% figures) can be read as overstating impact compared to the absolute mAP and gap-to-oracle; see clarity suggestions below. The trade-off between annotation cost and accuracy is not quantified beyond one operating point (m = 100).

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and organized. The three-part decomposition (background, category-invariant, category-specific adaptation) is easy to follow. Figures are generally helpful. That said:
- Please clarify in Figure 3a which baselines (besides your method and R-CNN) use category labels for detection versus only classification labels, to ensure fair interpretation.
- The main quantitative result should be centered on the DNN performance in Figure 3b (absolute mAP and fraction of gap to oracle). The “50% relative mAP” and “78%” claims are potentially misleading without context; please report both absolute gains and fraction of gap closed, and state the denominator explicitly.
- Provide a dedicated table/subsection that clearly itemizes differences from R-CNN (data requirements, training objectives, use of background class, need for SVMs/bbox regression, training time), possibly replacing Figure 6 if space is tight.
- Minor nits: ensure consistent method naming (“LSDA” throughout; avoid mixing “DNN”/“DDA”); fix the typo “classi?ers” (line 431).

Originality: The approach combines known ingredients (deep fine-tuning, background class, parameter-space similarity for transfer) in a new way to address classification→detection transfer for held-out categories. It is not a radical algorithmic departure but is a neat and practical instantiation of task/domain adaptation in deep nets that, to my knowledge, had not been demonstrated at this scale or in this precise setting.

Significance: The problem—reducing dependence on bounding boxes to scale detection to thousands of categories—is important and timely. The paper demonstrates a solid first step with measurable gains on held-out categories and a large-scale rollout. That said, the absolute performance on held-out classes remains well below the fully supervised oracle, and it is hard to judge the ultimate impact without a cost–accuracy analysis: how does mAP vary with the number of categories with detection labels (m), and what is the marginal return per labeled box? It is also fair to question the long-term importance if bounding-box scarcity is mitigated by annotation investment; however, even then, LSDA-like transfer could remain valuable for long-tail categories and rapid expansion.

Suggestions and questions:
- Report performance as a function of m (e.g., m ∈ {20, 50, 100, 150}) to characterize the cost–accuracy trade-off and support claims about scalability.
- Analyze sensitivity to the number of nearest neighbors k for the category-specific delta; show per-category improvements vs semantic similarity.
- Clarify whether the background class is essential: include ablations with/without background and with only partial fine-tuning.
- Provide comparisons to weakly supervised/MIL baselines on this setup, if feasible, or discuss integration (your conclusion hints at this).
- Discuss calibration: do adapted detectors require class-wise score calibration for fair cross-class comparisons at 7.6K scale?
- Consider a cross-dataset test (e.g., PASCAL) to check generalization of the learned transformation.

Pros:
- Addresses a clear bottleneck in scaling detection with a simple, elegant adaptation strategy.
- Solid empirical evaluation on held-out categories with informative ablations and error analysis.
- Practical impact: 7.6K-class detector and released code/models; faster SPP variant.
- Clear writing and good positioning w.r.t. prior work.

Cons:
- Absolute mAP on held-out categories remains low; gains are modest in absolute terms.
- Headline relative improvements risk overstating impact; clarity on baselines and denominators needed.
- No systematic cost–benefit analysis vs amount of bounding-box supervision.
- Limited comparison to alternative weakly supervised approaches in this exact setting.

Overall: A high-quality and clearly written paper that makes a useful and well-executed contribution to scaling detection via adaptation. While not ground-breaking, it opens a path toward large-scale detection with reduced annotation cost and is likely to be built upon. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on the clarifications and minor edits above.
