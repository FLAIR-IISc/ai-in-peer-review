The paper "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation" proposes a method to adapt image classifiers into object detectors, addressing the challenge of limited bounding box annotations for many categories. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain (image-level labels) to the detection domain (bounding boxes and labels) using supervised CNNs and a domain adaptation approach. This transformation is learned on a subset of categories with bounding box data and then applied to adapt classifiers for categories lacking such annotations. The authors demonstrate improved performance on the ImageNet detection task, achieving a 50% relative mAP gain over a baseline.

This work builds on the foundational R-CNN approach [1] by moving beyond simply fine-tuning pre-trained classifiers with bounding box data. It's related to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) methods [6, 7] which use weak labels for detection, but distinguishes itself by focusing on adaptation from a pre-trained classification network and evaluating performance on the large-scale ImageNet detection challenge. The paper also connects to domain adaptation literature [12-16, 17-22], but highlights its novelty in adapting *both* domain and task simultaneously, and, crucially, in enabling detection for categories with *no* bounding box annotations.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a significant problem:** The scarcity of bounding box annotations is a major bottleneck in scaling object detection to a large number of categories. This work offers a promising approach to alleviate this issue.
*   **Clear methodology:** The LSDA algorithm is well-defined and the three-step approach (category invariant adaptation, category specific adaptation, and detection) is logically presented.
*   **Strong experimental results:** The 50% relative mAP improvement on the ImageNet detection task demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation studies effectively dissect the contribution of each component.
*   **Thorough analysis:** The paper includes a detailed error analysis, identifying localization and background confusion as key areas for improvement, and provides insights into the types of errors made by the model.
*   **Availability of resources:** The authors have released the model and code, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Reliance on pre-trained classifiers:** The performance heavily depends on the quality of the initial image classifiers. While the authors acknowledge this, a more detailed discussion of how classifier performance impacts the adaptation process could be beneficial.
*   **Nearest neighbor selection:** The category-specific adaptation relies on finding nearest neighbors in feature space. The sensitivity of the results to different distance metrics or neighbor selection strategies is not explored.
*   **Limited comparison to other approaches:** While MIL methods are mentioned, a direct comparison on ImageNet with methods like [6, 7] would strengthen the paper. The authors state MIL methods have not been evaluated on ImageNet, but checking if any recent works have addressed this would be useful.
*   **The “oracle” performance comparison is somewhat misleading:** The final row in Table 1 showing the performance if the detector *had* access to detection data for all categories is a somewhat idealized upper bound. It doesn’t highlight the core contribution of the paper, which is achieving reasonable performance *without* that data.
*   **Figure 5's 'Other' category is rather broad:** While providing insight, the 'Other' error type (incorrect class) lacks specificity. More detailed categorization could illuminate further areas for improvement.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a critical problem in large-scale object detection.
*   The proposed approach is novel and well-motivated.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvement over a strong baseline.
*   The paper is well-written, clear, and includes thorough analysis.
*   The code and models are publicly available.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The reliance on pre-trained classifiers could be a limitation.
*   The lack of direct comparison to other relevant approaches (e.g., recent MIL methods on ImageNet).
*   Some aspects of the methodology (e.g., nearest neighbor selection) could benefit from further investigation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of object detection. The proposed LSDA algorithm is a practical and effective solution to the problem of limited bounding box annotations. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, including a discussion of the sensitivity of the nearest neighbor selection and potentially a comparison to more recent MIL-based methods if available.
