Summary
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to adapt an image classifier into an object detector when bounding box annotations are available for only a subset of categories. The approach: (1) pre-train a CNN for classification, (2) replace the final layer with K category classifiers and fine-tune on image-level labels, (3) fine-tune layers 1–7 and introduce a background class using detection data for m categories (set B), and (4) for held-out categories (set A), approximate category-specific detector weights by adding the average “delta” learned for the k nearest neighbors in B (nearest in L2-normalized fc8 space). At test time, per-region scores for K classes plus background are produced; predictions subtract the background score. On ILSVRC13 detection (using val1 + augmented train boxes for 100 classes as detection data, val2 for evaluation), LSDA improves mAP on held-out categories from 10.31% (classification-only baseline on proposals) to 16.15% (+50% relative). Ablations indicate most gains come from learning background and adapting shared layers; the fc8 neighbor transfer yields a small but statistically significant boost. The authors also present qualitative results for a 7.6K-category detector and release code/models.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and practical setting: scaling detection when only a subset of categories have boxes.
- Methodologically simple and easy to reproduce; code and models are reportedly released.
- Ablation study decomposes contributions (background class, shared-layer fine-tuning, fc8 transfer).
- Sensible error analysis showing reductions in background and localization errors after adaptation.
- Reasonable use of the R-CNN pipeline and ILSVRC13 protocol; training time reduction is pragmatic.

Weaknesses
- Limited comparative evaluation. The main baseline is the classification-only network on proposals. Missing are stronger baselines that leverage the available detection data for transfer, e.g.:
  - Fine-tune on B and use those adapted shared features to train class-specific linear SVMs for A (no neighbor delta).
  - MIL/WSOD baselines on A seeded by the classification network, or self-training/pseudo-box mining.
  - Alternative transfer schemes (e.g., hierarchy/semantic similarity or metric learning) beyond fc8 L2 proximity.
- The key novelty (neighbor-averaged fc8 deltas) yields only a small gain (+0.3 mAP); most gains come from standard fine-tuning with a background class, which is established practice post R-CNN.
- Calibration details are under-specified. “score_i – score_background” is ambiguous (pre-softmax logits vs probabilities); cross-class calibration and across-category NMS may be brittle, especially for 7.6K classes.
- The 7.6K-category result is purely qualitative; no quantitative checks (even proxy ones) are provided to substantiate scalability claims.
- Sensitivity to the choice of B, to k in neighbor averaging, and to the similarity metric is not explored thoroughly. WordNet/semantic similarity vs fc8-distance comparisons are absent.
- No report on the effect of adaptation on categories in B (does detection performance on known classes hold steady/improve?).
- Reference formatting is incomplete, and some implementation specifics (e.g., exact fine-tuning schedules, data balancing during detection fine-tuning, proposal settings) are sparse for full reproducibility.

Quality
Technically straightforward and generally sound; the empirical evidence shows a clear relative improvement on held-out classes. However, the lack of strong baselines and broader comparisons makes it difficult to assess how much of the gain is attributable to the proposed transfer versus generic fine-tuning and background modeling. The statistical test is provided only for the small fc8 transfer gain.

Clarity
Overall clear and readable, with a coherent pipeline and ablation narrative. Some notation/terminology could be tightened (scores vs logits; “softmax detector” vs score subtraction). References need attention and several details (training schedules, proposal parameters, calibration) should be clarified.

Originality
Moderate. Casting classification→detection as domain adaptation is reasonable, and learning a background class plus shared-layer fine-tuning is now established in detection transfer. The category-specific neighbor-delta idea is a minor novelty with small impact in the experiments.

Significance
The problem is important. If robust, such transfer could reduce supervision burden for large-scale detection. That said, absolute mAP remains low, the improvements beyond standard fine-tuning are modest, and the evaluation lacks comparisons to alternative transfer/weakly supervised methods. The impact is thus promising but not yet convincingly demonstrated against strong baselines.

Questions/Suggestions for Authors
- Please clarify whether “score_i – score_background” uses logits or probabilities. How are scores calibrated across classes for the 7.6K case?
- Provide results for several k and for alternative similarity measures (WordNet distance, attribute/word embeddings), and sensitivity to which classes are in B.
- Add baselines: (i) fine-tune on B, freeze shared layers, and train linear classifiers for A on proposals; (ii) MIL/WSOD on A initialized with the classification network; (iii) simple averaging of B deltas without nearest-neighbor selection.
- Report performance on B categories after adaptation, and per-class AP for A to assess where transfer helps/hurts.
- Quantify the 7.6K detector on a subset with available boxes, or via proxy metrics (e.g., CorLoc), and provide runtime/memory scaling characteristics.
- Compare with adding SVMs and bbox regression to your adapted features to bound the potential of the approach.

Pros (for acceptance)
- Addresses a timely and practically relevant problem.
- Simple, reproducible method with code release.
- Clear ablation showing substantial gains over a naive baseline.
- Insightful error analysis.

Cons (against acceptance)
- Missing strong baselines; unclear advantage over established fine-tuning practices.
- Limited novelty in the main technical contribution; the novel component yields small gains.
- Scalability claims are qualitative, lacking quantitative support.
- Some clarity and reproducibility gaps.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper tackles an important problem and shows encouraging gains, but the empirical validation against established alternatives is insufficient, and the novelty beyond standard fine-tuning with background is limited. Strengthening the evaluation with the suggested baselines and quantitative large-scale evidence would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
