Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to adapt image classifiers into object detectors when only a subset of categories has bounding box annotations. The approach treats the shift from classification (image-level labels) to detection (region-level labels plus background) as a domain adaptation problem. It learns (1) a category-invariant transformation by fine-tuning shared CNN layers and a background class using detection data for m categories (set B), and (2) a simple category-specific transformation of the final layer for held-out categories (set A) by transferring the average delta of the nearest neighbors in B (in fc8 space). At test time, per-region class scores are adjusted by subtracting the background score.

Relative to R-CNN, which requires detection labels for all categories, LSDA shows that most of the “classification→detection” shift can be learned once and transferred, enabling detectors for classes without bounding boxes. It contrasts with MIL-based weakly supervised detection by relying on a small set of fully annotated detection categories to learn a transferable representation. It also differs from prior domain adaptation that assumes all categories are labeled in both domains; here, adaptation is across task and across categories, with no detection labels for A.

On ILSVRC13 detection, using detection annotations for 100 categories and holding out 100, LSDA improves mAP on the held-out categories from 10.31% (classification-only applied to proposals) to 16.15% (+50% relative). Ablations indicate the dominant gain comes from learning background and fine-tuning shared layers; the category-specific fc8 transfer adds a smaller but statistically significant boost. The authors also demonstrate a 7.6K-category detector using LSDA.

Quality:
- Soundness: The method is technically simple and well-motivated. The experimental design follows established practice (R-CNN pipeline, selective search proposals, Caffe). The ablation study is informative: it isolates the value of background modeling and feature adaptation versus the category-specific transfer. A paired t-test supports the significance of the final layer transfer.
- Evidence: Results on ILSVRC13 with a clear split (100 B / 100 A) and a comparison to an upper bound (all categories with detection data) substantiate claims. Error analysis (Hoiem et al. tool) shows LSDA reduces localization and background confusions—exactly the failure modes expected from using classification features on proposals—adding causal credibility to the approach.
- Limitations: The split is alphabetical; random splits and variance across multiple splits would make the conclusions more robust. The category-specific transfer is evaluated but only with a few k values; further sensitivity analyses (e.g., using semantic/WordNet similarity or visual feature similarity beyond fc8) would be helpful. The large-scale 7.6K result is qualitative; a small quantitative probe (e.g., precision on a curated subset) would strengthen the scaling claim. There is no head-to-head comparison to contemporary weakly supervised detectors trained without any bounding boxes for A; while the setting differs, a proxy comparison would be informative.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The three insights guiding the method map directly to implementation choices. The training/evaluation protocol is described in enough detail to reproduce (architecture, initialization, datasets, fine-tuning strategy, background modeling, scoring rule).
- Minor clarity issues: the neighbor selection for fc8 transfer could specify whether cosine and L2 normalized fc8 are equivalent in practice (they use L2-normalized fc8+Euclidean), and report the exact k used in the main result (k=10 is mentioned). Cleaning up some reference formatting would help.

Originality:
- The framing of classification→detection as a domain adaptation across categories with missing target labels is novel at the time, and distinct from R-CNN fine-tuning or standard domain adaptation that assumes full category coverage. The idea to learn a transferable background model and shared feature shift from a subset and apply it to all categories is simple but original and practically important.
- The nearest-neighbor delta transfer for fc8 is a modest novelty, but it is positioned correctly as a smaller component.

Significance:
- High practical impact: Being able to bootstrap detectors for many categories without bounding boxes is a clear step toward large-scale detection. The 50% relative mAP gain on held-out categories is meaningful, and the large-scale demo suggests immediate utility.
- Conceptual impact: The result that most of the benefit comes from a category-invariant representation shift plus background modeling is a useful insight that others can build on. The released models/code further increase potential adoption.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-motivated problem formulation with a simple, effective solution.
- Solid experimental evidence with ablations and error analysis supporting the mechanism.
- Demonstrated scalability and reduced training time compared to full R-CNN training.
- Code/models promised, increasing reproducibility and impact.

Weaknesses:
- Single deterministic split (alphabetical) may bias conclusions; no variance across splits.
- Category-specific transfer mechanism is heuristic; alternative similarity metrics or learned transfer are not explored.
- Large-scale (7.6K) evaluation is qualitative only.
- No comparison to weakly supervised MIL-style methods on a shared subset (even if settings differ).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Report results across multiple random B/A splits, including mean and variance.
- Explore alternative or learned category-specific transfers (e.g., semantic distances, joint learning of δ predictors, or metric learning for fc8 similarity).
- Add a small quantitative evaluation for a subset of the 7.6K categories (e.g., human-verified precision at top-K).
- Consider training bounding box regression on B and test its transfer to A.
- Include a runtime/memory analysis for the 7.6K model and the SPPNet variant.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important bottleneck (bounding box annotation) with a practical, scalable method.
- Strong empirical gains on a challenging benchmark with careful analysis.
- Clear insight into what aspects of adaptation matter (background + shared features).
- Likely to influence subsequent large-scale detection work; code/models available.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited robustness analysis across data splits; category-specific transfer is simplistic.
- Lacks quantitative evaluation of the 7.6K detector and comparisons to weakly supervised alternatives.

Overall recommendation:
Accept. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and offers a meaningful, novel contribution to large-scale object detection with convincing empirical support. The limitations are addressable and do not diminish the core contribution.
