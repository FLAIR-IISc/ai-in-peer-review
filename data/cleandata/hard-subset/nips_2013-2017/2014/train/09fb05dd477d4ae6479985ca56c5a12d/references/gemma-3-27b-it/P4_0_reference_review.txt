The paper "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation" proposes a method to adapt image classifiers into object detectors, leveraging the abundance of image-level annotations and scarcity of bounding box annotations. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain (images with category labels) to the detection domain (images with bounding boxes and category labels) using supervised CNNs. This transformation is learned on a subset of categories with bounding box data and then applied to adapt classifiers for categories without such data. The paper demonstrates a 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline using raw classifier weights on the ImageNet detection task and showcases the ability to detect objects in 7604 categories, leveraging transfer learning to those without bounding box annotations.

This work builds upon the established trend of utilizing pre-trained classifiers for object detection, notably R-CNN [1], but addresses the key limitation of requiring bounding box annotations for all classes. It also connects with multiple instance learning (MIL) approaches [6, 7], offering a complementary strategy. The proposed approach can be viewed as a domain adaptation problem [12, 13, 14], however, the authors emphasize that their adaptation differs by addressing both domain and task shifts simultaneously. Previous supervised domain adaptation methods [17, 18, 19, 20, 21, 22] typically require labeled detection data in both source and target domains, which LSDA avoids.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant problem:** The scarcity of bounding box annotations is a major bottleneck for scaling object detection to a large number of categories. LSDA directly tackles this challenge.
* **Clear and well-motivated approach:** The idea of learning a transformation between classification and detection domains is intuitive and well-explained.
* **Strong experimental results:** The 50% mAP improvement on the held-out categories is substantial and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The demonstration with 7604 categories is impressive.
* **Ablation study:**  The ablation study effectively highlights the importance of each component of the algorithm (category invariant adaptation, category specific adaptation).
* **Error Analysis:** The analysis of false positive errors helps understand the strengths and limitations of the model.
* **Publicly available code and models:** Making the code and models available fosters reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited comparison to other weakly-supervised detection methods:** While the paper acknowledges MIL methods [6, 7], a direct comparison on a common dataset (e.g., ImageNet detection) would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Simplifications in the experimental setup:** The use of val1 for training the detector and val2 for testing, while standard in some prior works [1], could introduce bias. A more rigorous cross-validation scheme might be beneficial.
* **Dependency on pre-trained classification network:** While leveraging pre-trained networks is a common practice, the performance is inherently tied to the quality of the initial classifier.
* **Complexity of the approach:** The multi-stage training process (pre-training, fine-tuning, category-specific adaptation) can be computationally expensive. 
* **Choice of k for nearest neighbor adaptation:** The sensitivity to the value of 'k' in the category-specific adaptation step is not thoroughly investigated. A more detailed analysis of the impact of different 'k' values would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a practically important and challenging problem.
* The proposed approach is novel and demonstrates significant performance improvements.
* The experimental results are convincing and well-supported.
* The code and models are publicly available, promoting reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of direct comparison to other state-of-the-art weakly-supervised detection methods.
* The potential bias in the experimental setup due to the data split.
* The computational complexity of the algorithm.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of object detection, offering a practical solution to the problem of limited bounding box annotations. The proposed LSDA algorithm is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates strong performance. While some weaknesses exist, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more comprehensive comparison to related weakly-supervised detection methods and a discussion of the sensitivity of the 'k' parameter in the nearest neighbor adaptation step.
