## Peer Review for LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

**Summary:**

This paper presents LSDA, a novel approach to large-scale object detection that leverages the abundance of image-level labeled data alongside a limited amount of bounding box annotations. The core idea is to learn a transformation from image classifiers (trained on image-level data) to object detectors, effectively transferring knowledge from the classification domain to the detection domain. The authors frame this as a domain adaptation problem and propose a method that learns both category-invariant and category-specific transformations to achieve this adaptation. They demonstrate significant improvements over a baseline on the ImageNet detection challenge, particularly for categories without bounding box annotations, and release a large-scale detector for 7604 categories.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the context of recent advances in object detection, highlighting the limitations of requiring large amounts of bounding box annotations. They acknowledge the work of Girshick et al. (R-CNN) as a precursor, noting that LSDA aims to reduce the bounding box annotation requirement.  The paper also accurately references related work on Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) for weak supervision and domain adaptation techniques, highlighting how LSDA differs (e.g., focusing on adapting to *held-out* categories without any detection data). The connection to deep feature learning and the use of pre-trained CNNs is well-established.  The paper appropriately cites relevant domain adaptation methods, although it could benefit from a slightly more nuanced discussion of how LSDA differs from more recent advances in adversarial domain adaptation.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical bottleneck:** The limited availability of bounding box annotations is a major obstacle to scaling object detection to a large number of categories. LSDA directly tackles this problem.
* **Novel Approach:**  The idea of learning a transformation *between* classification and detection networks is insightful and a valuable contribution.  Specifically, disentangling category-invariant and category-specific adaptations is a strong aspect.
* **Strong Experimental Results:** The 50% relative mAP improvement over the baseline is significant, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach. The results on held-out categories are particularly impressive.
* **Large-Scale Demonstration:**  The release of a 7604-category detector is a notable achievement and facilitates further research.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the key ideas.
* **Ablation Study:** The ablation study provides useful insight into the contribution of each component of the algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the baseline (raw classifier weights) is a reasonable starting point, it’s not a particularly strong one.  A comparison against state-of-the-art detectors trained with limited bounding boxes (e.g., using MIL-based methods) would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Justification for Nearest Neighbor Selection:** The method for approximating the category-specific adaptation (using nearest neighbors) feels somewhat ad-hoc. While it works, the rationale could be strengthened with a more theoretical justification or a more thorough exploration of different neighbor selection strategies.
* **Computational Cost:**  The paper mentions reducing detection time with spatial pyramid pooling, but details on the computational cost of training and inference (especially for the 7604-category detector) are lacking.  This is a crucial consideration for large-scale applications.
* **Error Analysis Depth:**  While the error analysis in Figure 5 is helpful, it would be strengthened by a more quantitative analysis of the types of errors and a deeper dive into *why* certain categories are more prone to errors.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper glosses over the limitations of the approach. What types of categories *don’t* benefit from LSDA, and why?



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):**  The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. It feels like a complete piece of work. The authors are careful to present both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.
* **Clarity (9/10):** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The diagrams (especially Figure 2) are helpful.
* **Originality (8/10):** The approach of learning a transformation between classification and detection networks is novel and a significant contribution. However, it builds upon existing ideas in domain adaptation and MIL.
* **Significance (8/10):** The results are important and have the potential to impact the field of large-scale object detection. The release of the 7604-category detector is a significant contribution.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical limitation in object detection.
* Presents a novel and effective approach.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results.
* Contributes a valuable resource to the research community (the 7604-category detector).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Comparison to stronger baselines would be beneficial.
* Justification for nearest neighbor selection could be improved.
* Lacks detailed discussion of computational cost and failure cases.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of object detection. The approach is novel, the results are impressive, and the release of the large-scale detector is a significant achievement. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors should consider:

1.  Adding a comparison to stronger baselines (e.g., MIL-based methods).
2.  Providing a more thorough justification for the nearest neighbor selection strategy.
3.  Including more detailed information on the computational cost of training and inference.
4.  Expanding the error analysis to include a more quantitative assessment and a discussion of failure cases.
