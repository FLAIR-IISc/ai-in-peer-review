## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper tackles a crucial bottleneck in scaling object detection to a truly large number of categories: the prohibitive cost of bounding box annotation. The core idea – learning a transformation from classifiers (trained on readily available image-level labels) to detectors – is genuinely novel and compelling. It frames detection as a domain adaptation problem, cleverly leveraging the abundance of classification data. The approach, LSDA, appears to successfully transfer knowledge from labeled to unlabeled detection categories, demonstrating a significant performance boost over a strong baseline.

The strengths of this work are considerable. The problem is well-motivated and clearly articulated. The proposed solution, while conceptually straightforward, is insightful and effectively addresses the core challenge. The experimental results on ImageNet are promising, showing a 50% relative mAP improvement. The ablation study convincingly demonstrates the contribution of each component of LSDA. Furthermore, the authors thoughtfully analyze the types of errors the adapted detectors still make, revealing valuable insights into the limitations and potential future directions. The release of the 7.6k category detector and code is commendable and will undoubtedly facilitate further research in this area. 

However, there are areas where the paper could be strengthened. While the improvement over the baseline is substantial, the absolute mAP numbers for the held-out categories (around 16%) are still relatively modest. A more detailed comparison to existing weakly-supervised detection methods (mentioned in the Related Work, such as MIL-based approaches) with direct quantitative results would strengthen the contribution.  The analysis of the “nearest neighbor” strategy for adapting category-specific parameters feels somewhat lightweight; demonstrating the sensitivity to the choice of *k* or exploring alternative similarity metrics would be beneficial. Finally, while the authors acknowledge computational costs, a more thorough discussion of scalability and the efficiency of the adaptation process would be valuable. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of learning a classifier-to-detector transformation is a significant conceptual advance. This isn’t just another incremental improvement in detection accuracy; it's a different way of *thinking* about the problem.
* **Significance:**  Addressing the annotation bottleneck is paramount to scaling detection. This work provides a practical approach to achieving this goal.
* **Strong Experimental Validation:** The results on ImageNet, while not state-of-the-art overall, clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, particularly for categories lacking bounding box annotations.
* **Bold Approach:** The authors tackle a genuinely hard problem with a relatively simple and elegant solution, showing considerable promise.
* **Open Science:** The release of code and models is a strong positive signal.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Modest Absolute Performance:** While the relative improvement is impressive, the absolute mAP numbers on the held-out categories remain limited.
* **Limited Comparison to Existing Weakly-Supervised Methods:** A more thorough comparison to relevant prior work is needed to fully contextualize the contribution.
* **Superficial Analysis of Category-Specific Adaptation:** The nearest neighbor strategy warrants deeper investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite minor weaknesses, this paper represents a significant step forward in large-scale object detection. The novelty and potential impact of the proposed approach outweigh the limitations. The authors have identified a critical problem and offered a promising solution. I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field and **recommend acceptance**. I would encourage the authors to address the points raised above (particularly the comparison to other weakly-supervised methods and a more in-depth analysis of the category-specific adaptation) in a future version or as a response to reviewer queries. The potential to scale detection to a much larger number of categories makes this work exceptionally valuable.
