The paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a method to adapt classifiers trained on image-level labels to object detectors, leveraging a smaller set of bounding box annotations. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification feature space to the detection feature space, enabling the adaptation of classifiers for categories without bounding box annotations. The approach casts the problem as domain adaptation, fine-tuning layers of a CNN trained for classification on a dataset with image-level labels using bounding box annotations for a subset of classes. The key contributions are the category invariant and category specific adaptation components. Experiments on ImageNet demonstrate a 50% relative mAP improvement over using raw classifier weights.

This paper addresses a crucial challenge in scaling object detection to a large number of categories – the annotation bottleneck. Existing methods often require extensive bounding box annotations, which are expensive and time-consuming to acquire. The proposed LSDA algorithm offers a compelling solution by leveraging readily available image-level labels and a small amount of bounding box data. The approach builds upon the R-CNN framework, extending it to handle categories without labeled bounding boxes. The domain adaptation perspective is a natural fit for this problem, and the breakdown into category-invariant and category-specific adaptation is insightful. The reported improvement on the held-out categories is significant and suggests the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The ablation study clearly illustrates the contribution of each component.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** The paper tackles a significant limitation in scaling object detection – the need for extensive bounding box annotations.
*   **Novel approach:**  The idea of adapting classifiers trained on image-level labels to object detectors via domain adaptation is a novel and promising direction.
*   **Strong experimental results:** The 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories is substantial and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
*   **Clear and well-organized presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation, approach, and experiments.
*   **Ablation Study:** The ablation study provides valuable insights into the contribution of each component of the proposed method.
*   **Code Release:** The release of the code and model weights promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited comparison to MIL methods:** While the paper mentions Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approaches, a direct comparison on the ImageNet detection challenge is lacking.  MIL methods like [6, 7] are relevant baselines and a more thorough comparison would strengthen the paper.
*   **Assumptions about data split:** The method assumes availability of classification labels for all categories. While realistic given image search data, explicitly acknowledging and discussing the potential biases introduced by this assumption would be beneficial.  The quality of image-level labels obtained from search engines can vary significantly.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of ‘k’ for nearest neighbor averaging in category-specific adaptation could be sensitive. Further investigation of the impact of different ‘k’ values and a discussion of strategies for optimal selection would be valuable.
*   **Computational Cost:**  Although a faster version using spatial pyramid pooling is mentioned, the initial computational cost described seems relatively high (4 seconds per image).  Further optimization or discussion of computational efficiency could be included.
*   **Error Analysis depth:** While error analysis is provided, it is somewhat limited. A more detailed analysis of failure cases and the types of confusions made by the model could provide valuable insights.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and effective approach to address a critical problem in object detection – the annotation bottleneck.
*   The experimental results are strong and demonstrate significant improvement over existing methods.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed algorithm.
*   The code and model weights are publicly available, promoting reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of direct comparison with recent MIL-based methods is a weakness.
*   The assumptions about the quality and biases in image-level labels should be discussed more thoroughly.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong contribution to the field of object detection.  The proposed LSDA algorithm offers a practical and effective solution to the annotation bottleneck, enabling the training of detectors for a large number of categories.  While there are a few minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the comparison to MIL methods and a more thorough discussion of the data quality assumptions.
