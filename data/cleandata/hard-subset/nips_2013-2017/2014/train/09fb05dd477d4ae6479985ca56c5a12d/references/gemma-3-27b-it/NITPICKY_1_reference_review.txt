## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper proposes LSDA, an algorithm to adapt image classifiers into object detectors, mitigating the need for extensive bounding box annotations. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain (image-level labels) to the detection domain (bounding boxes and labels) using supervised CNNs and a domain adaptation framework. The authors demonstrate a significant improvement (+50% relative mAP) on the ImageNet detection task, particularly on categories without bounding box annotations.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical bottleneck:** The paper tackles the significant challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, where obtaining bounding box annotations is prohibitively expensive.
* **Novelty of approach:**  Adapting classifiers to detectors in a domain adaptation framework, specifically targeting held-out categories without any detection labels, is a novel contribution.  The strategy of transferring category-invariant features and then performing category-specific adaptation is conceptually sound.
* **Strong empirical results:** The reported 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline is substantial, and the ablation study provides evidence for the contribution of each component. 
* **Publicly available code and models:**  Releasing the code and models is highly commendable and facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of detailed hyperparameter tuning description:** While implementation details are mentioned, the paper lacks a detailed description of the hyperparameter tuning process, particularly for the fine-tuning stages.  Were learning rates, batch sizes, and regularization parameters systematically explored? This is a crucial aspect for reproducibility.
* **Insufficient comparison to strong baselines:**  The baseline of “raw classifier weights” is a weak comparison. While it establishes a lower bound, comparison against more sophisticated approaches leveraging weakly supervised learning (e.g., MIL-based methods like Ali et al. [6] and the related work [7] mentioned in the paper) is crucial, especially given the authors acknowledge their complementarity.  The authors state these haven't been evaluated on ImageNet, but even a preliminary comparison would strengthen the paper.
* **Ambiguous explanation of "nearest neighbors" selection:** The method for finding nearest neighbors for category-specific adaptation (Eq. 1) relies on Euclidean distance of normalized fc8 parameters. The justification for this metric isn't thoroughly explained and could benefit from a discussion of alternative distance metrics or neighbor selection strategies. A sensitivity analysis on the 'k' value would also be valuable.
* **Figure 3 is uninformative:** The visualization in Figure 3 depicting the category invariant adaptation is unclear and doesn't convincingly demonstrate the transformation occurring. It needs substantial improvement to be informative.
* **Minor inconsistencies in terminology and notation:** The paper occasionally uses inconsistent terminology (e.g., referring to 'm' and 'K' interchangeably) and lacks precise mathematical notation for certain operations, making the algorithm difficult to fully grasp. For example, the notation in Eq. 1 is a bit vague. 
* **Limited Error Analysis Depth:** While the error analysis identifies the types of errors, it lacks a quantitative breakdown and doesn't provide insight into why specific categories are frequently misclassified.
* **Testing setup discrepancy:** The training set is constructed as having fewer objects per image than the validation set. While the authors acknowledge this, they do not adequately justify this decision, as it impacts the generalization of the learned models.
* **The statement about "reducing training time from 3 days to roughly 5.5 hours" seems out of place.** It's a tangential detail that doesn't contribute to the core scientific contribution.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising approach to large-scale detection with limited annotation effort. The core idea is novel, and the experimental results are encouraging. However, the paper suffers from several shortcomings, including a lack of detailed experimental setup, insufficient comparison to relevant baselines, and clarity issues in certain explanations.  While the contributions are significant, the level of polish and rigor needed for NIPS is not fully met.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and important problem addressed.
* Promising empirical results demonstrating significant improvements.
* Publicly available code and models.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Lack of thorough hyperparameter tuning details.
* Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art baselines.
* Unclear visualizations and explanations in certain sections.
* Minor inconsistencies and lack of mathematical rigor.
* Weak baseline comparison.

**Recommendation:** **Reject.**

I believe the paper has potential, but the authors need to address the weaknesses outlined above to elevate its quality to the level expected for NIPS. Specifically, a more rigorous experimental evaluation, a clearer presentation of the methodology, and a more detailed discussion of related work are essential. Re-submission with these revisions would be worthwhile.
