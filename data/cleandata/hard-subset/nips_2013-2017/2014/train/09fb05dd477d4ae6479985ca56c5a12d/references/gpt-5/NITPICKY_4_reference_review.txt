Summary
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to convert image classifiers into object detectors when only a subset of categories have bounding-box annotations. The approach: (1) pretrain an AlexNet-style CNN on ImageNet classification, (2) replace the final layer with K class-specific classifiers for the 200 ILSVRC detection classes and fine-tune on image-level labels, (3) fine-tune layers 1–7 for detection using detection-labeled categories B (with a new background class), and (4) for categories without detection labels (A), approximate category-specific adaptation by adding the average fc8 “delta” learned for the k nearest neighbors in B (nearest in L2-normalized fc8 weight space). At test time, region proposals are scored with the CNN and class scores are adjusted by subtracting the background score. On ILSVRC13 with 100 held-out categories, LSDA reports a 50% relative mAP improvement over applying a classification network directly to proposals, and qualitative results for a 7.6K-category detector.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on R-CNN (fine-tuning for detection), selective search proposals, and AlexNet. It positions itself within domain adaptation (feature/model adaptation, e.g., Aytar & Zisserman; Hoffman et al.) but extends to task shift (classification to detection) and to categories without detection labels. It contrasts with weakly supervised detection/MIL (Ali et al.; Song et al.) but does not provide direct comparisons on ILSVRC.

Strengths
- Addresses a timely and significant problem (scaling detection without exhaustive bbox labels).
- Simple, practical adaptation pipeline that is easy to implement on top of existing CNNs.
- Clear decomposition into category-invariant (shared layers + background) and category-specific (fc8 delta transfer).
- Ablation suggests background modeling and feature adaptation are key; k-NN delta transfer gives additional gains.
- Error analysis indicates reductions in background and localization errors.
- Releasing models/code and demonstrating scalability to 7.6K categories is valuable to the community.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodology and technical details
- Ambiguity in scoring: “scorei − scorebackground.” Are these pre-softmax logits, post-softmax probabilities, or something else? Subtracting a background softmax probability is not theoretically calibrated; justification and empirical sensitivity are lacking. How are scores calibrated across classes?
- Background modeling and positives/negatives: missing IoU thresholds for positive/negative samples, mining strategy, and whether negatives include proposals from images without the class. This affects the central claim that background modeling is crucial.
- Fine-tuning specifics are under-specified: learning rates for conv/fc layers, batch construction, sampling ratios of positives/negatives, iterations/epochs, data augmentation, dropout, momentum, weight decay, and multi-scale details are not provided. Reproducibility suffers.
- Category-specific adaptation: the choice of L2 distance on L2-normalized fc8 weights as a semantic similarity proxy is ad hoc. No comparison against alternatives (e.g., wordnet/attribute similarity, cosine on features, confusion-based neighbors). Sensitivity to k is only minimally explored (only one result for k=10). No analysis whether negative transfer occurs for dissimilar classes.
- The “domain adaptation” framing is somewhat superficial; the method is essentially standard fine-tuning on detection for shared layers plus a nearest-neighbor weight delta transfer. No formal adaptation objective is optimized, and the method does not address covariate/conditional shift explicitly.

Experimental design
- Baselines are weak. The primary baseline applies a classification net to proposals. Missing stronger weakly supervised baselines (MIL on proposals using the same classification data, even for a subset), or simple heuristic adaptations (e.g., training per-class logistic regression on proposal features using image-level labels with MIL/EM). The assertion that MIL methods lack ILSVRC-scale evaluation is not a sufficient reason to omit comparison; a controlled subset evaluation would strengthen claims.
- No comparison to R-CNN variants on the subset of categories with detection data to quantify the loss from removing SVMs and bbox regression (claimed “small”). Quantify absolute mAP delta of skipping these steps on B and on A via transfer.
- Statistical testing: significance is reported for one configuration (k=10) over the category set using paired t-test on per-category APs. mAP is heavy-tailed; bootstrap over images or random splits would be more appropriate. Report variance across random initializations/seeds.
- The 7.6K detector results are purely qualitative. Even pseudo-quantitative sanity checks (e.g., on a few categories with bbox labels, or user studies) would make the scale claim more convincing.
- The data split is nonstandard (val1 used for training detection plus extra training images; val2 for evaluation). While this is acceptable, please clarify class/image counts, duplication checks, and any overlap with the classification-only set to avoid leakage.

Clarity and presentation
- Several typographical and formatting issues reduce clarity:
  - “m K” presumably means m << K; numerous hyphenation artifacts (“fea- ture”), and stray footnote placement mid-paragraph.
  - Figures/tables are referenced but not included; quantitative values (Figure 3, Table 1) are not visible here; ensure all numbers are in-text.
  - The description of background score combination and NMS across categories lacks thresholds and implementation specifics.
- References are incomplete/inconsistent: many entries miss years, venues, pages; duplicates (Aytar & Zisserman listed twice), “In In Proc.” repetitions, misspellings (“Hoeim” vs “Hoiem”), arXiv identifiers missing years, inconsistent capitalization.

Claims and interpretation
- The “50% relative mAP” improvement is from 10.31% to 16.15% on held-out categories, which remains far from the upper bound with full detection data. The practical utility needs stronger evidence via comparisons to competing weakly supervised methods.
- The statement that training SVMs “removes the adaptation capacity” is asserted without justification.

Questions for the authors
1. Precisely define the score used at test time and its calibration. Did you try logits vs probabilities vs log-odds? How sensitive is performance to this choice?
2. Provide full training hyperparameters, sampling ratios, and IoU thresholds for positives/negatives.
3. Report sensitivity to k for the nearest-neighbor delta transfer and compare to semantic neighbors (WordNet) or cosine similarity in feature space.
4. Provide a MIL baseline on a manageable subset for fairness, or justify convincingly why it is infeasible.
5. Quantify the impact of omitting SVMs and bbox regression (on B and on A through transfer).
6. Any evidence of negative transfer for some categories? Provide per-class deltas and analyze failure modes.

Pros
- Addresses an important scaling bottleneck.
- Simple, implementable idea with some empirical gains.
- Useful qualitative insights and code release.

Cons
- Under-specified methodology; several ad hoc choices without ablations.
- Weak baselines; missing comparisons to alternative weakly supervised detectors.
- Ambiguities in scoring/calibration and training setup undermine reproducibility.
- Presentation/reference quality issues.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline)
While the problem is important and the idea is promising, the paper in its current form contains numerous methodological ambiguities, missing baselines, and presentation issues. With stronger experimental comparisons, clearer technical details, and improved writing/references, this could become a solid contribution.
