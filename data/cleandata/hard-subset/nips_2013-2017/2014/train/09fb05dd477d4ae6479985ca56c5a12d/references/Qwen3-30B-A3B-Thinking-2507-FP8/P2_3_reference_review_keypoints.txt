- Proposes LSDA: transform image classifiers into object detectors by learning adaptation from classification to detection using few bounding-box categories.  
- Uses category-invariant adaptation (fine-tuning on bounding-box data) and category-specific adaptation (nearest-neighbor weight transfer) to adapt detectors.  
- Demonstrates ~50% relative mAP improvement on held-out ImageNet detection categories and builds a 7,604-category detector.  
- Ablation study shows category-invariant adaptation is the most important component; category-specific adaptation gives additional gains.  
- Compared against a strong baseline (raw classifier weights on region proposals) and includes error analysis showing reduced localization and background confusion.  
- Implementation details are thorough and code/models are publicly released for reproducibility.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and methodically explained with helpful visualizations.  
- LSDA is an original contribution: transfers a learned classification→detection transform across categories, enabling large-scale detection without full bounding-box annotation.  
- Significant practical impact: reduces bounding-box annotation burden and makes detection for thousands of categories feasible.  
- Results fall short of oracle (full bounding-box supervision) performance but represent a meaningful step toward scalable detection.  
- Weakness: no direct comparison to MIL methods on ImageNet, which could strengthen empirical claims.  
- Weakness: main improvement measured against a relatively weak baseline rather than stronger detectors like full R-CNN.  
- Weakness: category-specific adaptation via nearest neighbors is somewhat simplistic, though different k values were tested.  
- Weakness: paper lacks a thorough discussion of limitations and failure cases.  
- Recommendation: accept — technically sound, significant, well-evaluated, and practically useful contribution.
