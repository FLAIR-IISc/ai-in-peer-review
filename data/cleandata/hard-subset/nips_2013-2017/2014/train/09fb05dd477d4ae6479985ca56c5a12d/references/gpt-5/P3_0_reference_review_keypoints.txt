- Proposes LSDA: fine-tune a CNN on detection data for m base classes to learn a category-invariant detector and a background class, then transfer category-specific output-layer changes to K−m held-out classes via averaged nearest-neighbor weight deltas.
- On ILSVRC13 (100 detection, 100 held-out), LSDA raises held-out mAP from 10.31% to 16.15% (+50% relative), with most gains from feature adaptation and background modeling.
- Uses region proposals and CNN fine-tuning like R-CNN but uniquely transfers classifier→detector knowledge to classes without detection labels.
- Differs from MIL-based weakly supervised detection by leveraging a supervised detection subset to learn a background model and generic detection features without MIL.
- Strength: addresses scalability of obtaining bounding boxes and provides a practical path to large-scale detection.
- Strength: clean decomposition into category-invariant (feature) and category-specific (output) transfer, with ablation showing dominant effect of feature adaptation and background learning.
- Strength: solid ILSVRC13 experimental setup, informative error analysis, reproducibility (Caffe code/models) and a 7.6K-class demo.
- Weakness: classification-only baseline on proposals is weak; stronger baselines (SVMs on fc7, MIL baselines) are needed to contextualize LSDA’s gains.
- Weakness: category-specific transfer via averaged fc8 weight deltas is heuristic and ad hoc; alternatives (learned linear mapping, semantic similarity, metric learning) are not explored.
- Weakness: alphabetical split may introduce semantic bias; sensitivity to random splits and semantic/visual distance is not reported.
- Weakness: unclear calibration — whether scorei−scorebackground uses logits or softmax probabilities, and whether more principled calibration was tried.
- Weakness: omitted components (bbox regression, SVMs) leave a large gap to fully supervised detectors; hybrid approaches using these for base classes could clarify trade-offs.
- Weakness: method likely depends on base classes covering visual modes of held-out classes; lack of analysis of performance vs neighbor distance or supercategory overlap.
- Weakness: dependence on proposal method not fully analyzed; sensitivity to different proposal algorithms and recall is not quantified.
- Quality: technically sound with supportive ablations and error analysis, but some experimental choices limit conclusiveness.
- Clarity: generally clear and organized, but missing implementation details (learning rates/schedules, exact definition of score subtraction, nearest-neighbor computation specifics).
- Originality: novel and timely idea to learn and transfer a classifier→detector transformation across categories, though transfer rule itself is simple.
- Significance: addresses an important problem with practical gains and a scalable demo likely to spur follow-up work.
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to k and similarity measures; results on random splits; whether scores are logits or probabilities and if calibration was tried; per-class gains vs neighbor distance; whether a learned linear mapping would outperform k-NN deltas.
- Pros: practical solution to scalability, clear decomposition and ablations, reproducible with large-scale demo.
- Cons: weak baselines, heuristic category transfer, potential split bias and limited generalization analysis.
- Recommendation: Weak accept (high reviewer confidence).
