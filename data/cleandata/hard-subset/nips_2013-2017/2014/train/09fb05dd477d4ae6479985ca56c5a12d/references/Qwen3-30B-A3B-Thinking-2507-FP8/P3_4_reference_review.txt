

# Peer Review: LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

## Summary
This paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a novel approach to transform image classifiers into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The authors frame the problem as domain adaptation, where image classification data (source domain) is used to adapt a classifier into a detector (target domain). The key insight is to learn a generic transformation from classification to detection using a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations, then apply this transformation to categories without detection labels. The method involves two key components: (1) category-invariant adaptation through fine-tuning layers 1-7 using detection data for a subset of categories, and (2) category-specific adaptation via nearest-neighbor approximation for categories without detection data. The authors demonstrate significant improvements (50% relative mAP gain) on held-out categories in the ImageNet detection benchmark.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper correctly identifies a critical bottleneck in large-scale object detection—scarcity of bounding box annotations—and proposes a principled approach to address it. The domain adaptation perspective is well-motivated and distinct from prior work that requires bounding box data for all categories.

2. **Strong Experimental Validation:** The ablation study clearly demonstrates the contribution of each component, with the category-invariant adaptation proving most impactful. The 50% relative mAP improvement over the classification-only baseline is substantial and well-documented.

3. **Practical Impact:** The demonstration of a 7,604 category detector using only classification data for most categories is impressive and showcases the method's potential for real-world applications. The open-sourcing of models and code significantly enhances reproducibility.

4. **Technical Rigor:** The method builds logically on R-CNN and domain adaptation literature, with clear connections to prior work. The implementation details are sufficiently thorough for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Related Work:** While the paper cites MIL approaches, it doesn't directly compare with recent weakly supervised detection methods like those using multiple instance learning (MIL) or attention mechanisms. For instance, the work by Song et al. (ICML 2015) on weakly supervised detection using class activation maps could provide a more relevant comparison point.

2. **Nearest-Neighbor Justification:** The category-specific adaptation relies on finding nearest neighbors in the classification feature space, but the paper doesn't sufficiently justify why this works. Why should similar categories in the classification space have similar detection adaptations? A theoretical analysis or more empirical evidence supporting this assumption would strengthen the contribution.

3. **ImageNet Weak Labels:** The authors use ImageNet classification data (with single image-level labels) as "weak" classification data, but don't fully address the implications of this weak supervision. For categories with multiple objects, the single label may not correspond to the most relevant object, potentially introducing noise that affects adaptation quality.

4. **Comparison to Recent Advances:** The paper doesn't compare with recent detection frameworks like Faster R-CNN or Mask R-CNN, which have significantly improved detection performance over R-CNN. While the authors note they don't need SVM retraining, the paper should address whether their approach would be compatible with these more modern detection pipelines.

## Overall Assessment

LSDA makes a significant contribution to the field of large-scale object detection by addressing a critical practical limitation. The paper is technically sound, well-organized, and demonstrates meaningful improvements over the baseline. The core innovation—adapting to held-out categories without detection data—represents a valuable step toward scalable detection systems.

The primary limitation is the lack of sufficient comparison with recent weakly supervised detection methods and inadequate justification for the nearest-neighbor category-specific adaptation. While these are important gaps, they don't undermine the main contribution. The paper's focus on a practically relevant problem with a clear, principled solution makes it a strong candidate for NIPS.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. This paper addresses an important practical problem in computer vision with a novel, well-executed approach. The method has clear potential for real-world impact, and the results are compelling. While there are some gaps in the comparison with related work and justification for key components, these don't diminish the overall contribution. The paper advances the state of the art in large-scale detection and demonstrates significant progress toward the goal of scalable object detection systems. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field that would be of interest to the NIPS community.

## Specific Suggestions for Revision

1. Add a comparison with recent weakly supervised detection methods (e.g., Song et al. ICML 2015, or more recent approaches) to better position the work.

2. Provide more justification for the nearest-neighbor approach in category-specific adaptation—perhaps through an ablation study showing the impact of different similarity metrics.

3. Discuss potential compatibility with more recent detection frameworks (Faster R-CNN, Mask R-CNN) to broaden the paper's applicability.

4. Consider addressing the implications of ImageNet's weak labels more thoroughly, perhaps by reporting how often the single image label corresponds to the most relevant object in the image.

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of object detection and deserves publication at NIPS.
