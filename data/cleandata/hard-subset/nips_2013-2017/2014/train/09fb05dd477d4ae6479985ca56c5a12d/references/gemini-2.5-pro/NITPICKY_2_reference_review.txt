**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a method for converting image classifiers into object detectors, with the primary goal of enabling detection for categories that lack bounding box annotations. The authors frame this as a domain adaptation problem, learning a transformation from a "classifier domain" (image-level labels) to a "detector domain" (bounding box labels). The method begins with a standard CNN classifier pre-trained on ImageNet. This network is then fine-tuned in two stages using a small subset of categories for which bounding box data is available. First, a category-invariant adaptation fine-tunes the shared feature extraction layers (conv1-fc7) to learn a generic transformation from classification-centric features to detection-centric features, including the introduction of a background class. Second, a category-specific adaptation is learned for the final classifier layer (fc8). For categories with bounding box data, this adaptation is learned directly. For the held-out categories (without bounding boxes), the adaptation is approximated by finding the k-nearest neighbors among the annotated categories (based on classifier weight similarity) and applying the average of their learned parameter changes. Experiments on a simulated 200-class ILSVRC2013 setup (100 classes with boxes, 100 without) show a 50% relative mAP improvement over a classification-only baseline for the held-out categories.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper's primary strength lies in its attempt to tackle a significant and practical problem: scaling object detection beyond the limited number of fully annotated categories. The proposed method is intuitive, and the experimental results demonstrate a substantial improvement over the baseline, suggesting the core idea of learning a transferable adaptation has merit. The error analysis in Section 4.3 provides compelling evidence that the method successfully learns to mitigate key detection challenges like localization and background confusion, which are not central to the classification task.

However, the paper exhibits several technical and methodological weaknesses.
1.  The core mechanism for the category-specific adaptation is heuristic and lacks rigorous justification. The assumption that similarity in the classification weight space implies similarity in the required classifier-to-detector transformation is strong and not well-supported. The choice of `l2`-normalized Euclidean distance as the similarity metric and the simple averaging of parameter updates are presented without exploring or justifying alternatives (e.g., weighted averaging).
2.  The experimental setup is concerning. The use of a custom split of the ILSVRC2013 validation set (val1/val2) combined with parts of the training set complicates reproducibility. More critically, the decision to omit the SVM fine-tuning and bounding box regression steps from the R-CNN pipeline, while acknowledged, makes the absolute performance numbers difficult to contextualize against the state-of-the-art. The claim of a "small performance loss" is not substantiated and may downplay the importance of these components, which were shown to be critical in the original R-CNN work.
3.  The statistical significance test reported for the final 0.3 mAP gain is weak. A single paired t-test on a single experimental run is insufficient to robustly claim significance for such a small margin, which could easily be due to initialization or training noise.

**Clarity:** The paper's overall structure is logical, but the manuscript is marred by a substantial number of formatting inconsistencies and errors that detract from its professionalism and credibility.
1.  The bibliography is exceptionally sloppy. There is no consistent formatting for author names, conference/journal titles, or publication details. For instance, Girshick et al. [1] is cited again as [18], and Aytar et al. [14] is cited again as [18]. Many entries are incomplete.
2.  Typographical errors are present (e.g., "Hoeim et al." [26] should be "Hoiem et al.").
3.  Figure captions are inconsistent. Figure 2 is referenced without a corresponding figure in the text provided. Figure 5(a) has an extremely long, poorly formatted caption containing undefined terms (e.g., `1-r`), while other figures are only referenced in the main text.
4.  The footnote marker `*` in the introduction is oddly placed. The bullet points in Section 3 use a non-standard format (`.`). These numerous minor flaws suggest a hasty preparation of the manuscript.

**Originality:** The idea of transferring knowledge from data-rich to data-poor classes is not new, but its application to learning a generic *transformation* from a classifier to a detector is a novel and interesting contribution. The work is well-situated with respect to prior domain adaptation literature and R-CNN. However, the paper could have engaged more deeply with the highly relevant area of weakly-supervised object detection using Multiple Instance Learning (MIL). Dismissing a comparison because MIL methods have not been evaluated on the same large scale feels inadequate; a conceptual comparison of the approaches would have strengthened the paper.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is of high significance. If successful and robust, a method like LSDA could dramatically reduce the annotation bottleneck and enable the creation of detectors for tens of thousands of categories. The reported 50% relative improvement is promising. However, the significance of the results is tempered by the use of a potentially weak baseline (raw classifier on proposals) and the deviation from the full R-CNN pipeline. The demonstration of a 7604-category detector is interesting, but without any quantitative evaluation, it remains anecdotal.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a highly important and practical problem in computer vision: large-scale object detection with limited supervision.
*   The core idea of learning a transferable classifier-to-detector adaptation is novel and conceptually appealing.
*   The experimental results show a significant relative improvement over a baseline, and the error analysis provides good insight into what the model learns.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The manuscript suffers from a very high number of formatting errors, typos, and inconsistencies, particularly in the bibliography, which undermines the perceived quality and rigor of the work.
*   The central mechanism for adapting held-out categories is a heuristic with insufficient justification and analysis.
*   The experimental setup has issues with non-standard data splits and the omission of key components from the comparison pipeline (SVMs, BBox regression), making the results difficult to interpret in the context of other state-of-the-art methods.
*   The engagement with closely related work in weakly-supervised learning is superficial.

**Recommendation:** Rejection.

While the paper tackles an important problem with a novel idea and shows promising initial results, the significant number of minor flaws in its presentation, combined with the heuristic nature of a core component and weaknesses in the experimental methodology, prevent me from recommending acceptance. The sheer volume of formatting and citation errors is far below the standard expected for a top-tier conference like NIPS and raises concerns about the level of care taken in the research itself. A thorough revision addressing the technical justifications, strengthening the experimental protocol, and meticulously correcting the manuscript's presentation is required.
