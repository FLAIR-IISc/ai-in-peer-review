

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

This paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a novel approach to transforming image classifiers into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The core insight is that the transformation from classification to detection can be learned on a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations and then generalized to categories without such annotations. This addresses a critical bottleneck in scaling object detection systems to large numbers of categories (e.g., tens of thousands), where collecting bounding box annotations is prohibitively expensive.

The paper frames the problem as domain adaptation between the source domain (image-level classification data) and target domain (bounding box detection data). LSDA makes three key contributions: (1) recognizing background modeling as critical for adaptation, (2) transferring category-invariant information between classifier and detector representations, and (3) learning category-specific transformations via nearest-neighbor approximation for categories without detection labels. The approach is conceptually simple but potentially impactful, as it enables detection for categories without any bounding box annotations.

The authors demonstrate their approach on the ILSVRC2013 detection dataset, showing a 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline of applying raw classifier weights to object proposals. Their most significant result is the creation of a 7,604 category detector using only 200 categories with bounding box annotations, with the remaining categories adapted from classification data.

**Strengths:**

This paper excels in originality and significance. The core idea—adapting classifiers to detectors using only a small subset of annotated categories—is novel and addresses a fundamental scalability challenge in computer vision. While previous work (R-CNN, MIL-based methods) has explored weakly supervised detection, LSDA's approach of learning a generic transformation to adapt classifiers to detectors represents a significant conceptual leap. The paper's bold claim—enabling detection for tens of thousands of categories with minimal bounding box annotations—has the potential to transform how detection systems are built at scale.

The experimental results are compelling, particularly the 50% relative mAP improvement and the successful demonstration of a 7,600 category detector. The error analysis showing reduced localization and background confusion errors after adaptation provides strong evidence for the method's effectiveness. The paper also makes a valuable contribution by releasing their model and code.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons to state-of-the-art weakly supervised detection methods (MIL-based approaches mentioned in related work). While the authors note these methods haven't been evaluated on ImageNet, a direct comparison would strengthen their claims. The evaluation on 100/200 categories might not fully demonstrate the method's scalability to extremely large numbers of categories.

The paper could better clarify the limitations of their approach. For instance, how does the performance degrade when the number of categories with bounding box annotations (m) is very small (e.g., m=1)? The nearest-neighbor approximation for category-specific adaptation seems potentially fragile for categories with no similar neighbors in set B.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The core idea is sufficiently novel and bold to justify acceptance despite minor weaknesses in the experimental comparison and scalability analysis. The authors have made a significant conceptual contribution that addresses a critical challenge in scaling object detection systems. While the approach might not represent the absolute state-of-the-art for detection on standard benchmarks, it opens up a new direction for large-scale detection systems that could be highly influential.

The paper's significance is clear: it provides a practical path toward detection systems with tens of thousands of categories, which is currently infeasible due to annotation costs. This aligns perfectly with NIPS' interest in novel, impactful approaches that push the boundaries of the field. The authors' claim that "all that would be needed is to train a classification layer for the new categories" is provocative and potentially transformative.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a strong technical contribution that should be valuable to the computer vision community. While the methodological details could be expanded, the novelty and potential impact outweigh these concerns. The authors have demonstrated a clear path toward solving a major practical problem in computer vision.

In summary, this paper presents a bold, novel approach to a fundamental problem in object detection. It makes a significant conceptual contribution that could enable detection systems at unprecedented scale, and the experimental results support this claim. Despite minor limitations in the evaluation scope, the paper's novelty and significance make it an excellent fit for NIPS.
