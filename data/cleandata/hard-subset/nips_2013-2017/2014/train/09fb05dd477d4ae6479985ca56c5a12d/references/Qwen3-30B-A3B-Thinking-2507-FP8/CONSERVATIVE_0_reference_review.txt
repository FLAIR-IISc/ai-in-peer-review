

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

This paper proposes LSDA, an approach to adapt image classifiers into object detectors by learning a transformation from classification to detection that can be applied to categories without bounding box annotations. The core idea is to use a small set of categories with bounding box labels to learn a generic transformation that can be applied to classifiers for categories without detection data. The method involves pre-training on classification data, fine-tuning layers 1-7 on a small subset of categories with detection labels, and then applying category-specific adaptation to transform the output layer for the remaining categories.

The paper makes a reasonable contribution to the problem of scaling object detection to large numbers of categories, which is a significant practical challenge. The authors correctly position their work in relation to existing domain adaptation and transfer learning approaches, particularly citing R-CNN and MIL-based methods. The technical approach builds logically on established techniques, and the methodology is clearly described.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's scientific rigor and the evidence supporting its claims. The most troubling issue is the baseline used for comparison: the paper compares against "raw classifier weights on object proposal regions," which is a very weak baseline that does not represent state-of-the-art detection methods. The R-CNN paper itself demonstrated that fine-tuning on bounding box data significantly improves over such a baseline. The claimed 50% relative mAP improvement is thus misleading without comparison to established detection methods like R-CNN or Fast R-CNN, which would be the natural baseline for a detection paper.

The experimental setup is also problematic. The authors simulate having bounding box annotations for only 100 out of 200 categories, which is an unrealistically large proportion of the dataset. Real-world scaling would require adapting to a tiny fraction of categories with bounding box labels (e.g., 1 out of 10,000). The paper does not demonstrate this more challenging scenario. The 7,604 category detector mentioned in Section 4.4 is not evaluated on a meaningful scale - it's simply an extension of the 200-category experiment without demonstrating the claimed scalability.

The paper's claim of being "the first method to adapt to held-out categories that have no detection data" is questionable. Multiple instance learning approaches (cited in Section 2) have been proposed for exactly this problem, though they haven't been evaluated on ImageNet. The paper doesn't compare against these methods or explain why their approach is superior.

The ablation study (Figure 3) shows that the most important component is adapting the feature representation, which aligns with intuition but doesn't provide evidence that this is a novel contribution. The paper claims "category invariant information can be transferred," but doesn't provide theoretical justification or evidence that this transfer is fundamentally different from established domain adaptation techniques.

While the paper demonstrates some improvement over a weak baseline, the evidence is insufficient to support the claim that this method "enables detection of tens of thousands of categories." Without rigorous comparison to established detection methods and without demonstration on a more realistic scaling scenario, the paper fails to demonstrate significant progress beyond existing approaches.

The paper's strength lies in its clear description of a method that builds on established techniques, but it fails to provide the rigorous validation required to claim a meaningful advance. The method appears to be a reasonable extension of existing transfer learning approaches, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that it offers significant advantages over the state-of-the-art.

In summary, while the problem addressed is important, the paper lacks the rigorous experimental validation required for a NIPS submission. The baseline is inadequate, the scaling demonstration is unrealistic, and the comparison to existing methods is insufficient. The authors should compare against established detection methods (R-CNN, Fast R-CNN, Faster R-CNN), demonstrate the method on a more realistic scaling scenario (e.g., adapting to 10 out of 200 categories), and provide absolute mAP numbers rather than just relative improvements.

The paper represents a reasonable incremental contribution but falls short of the scientific rigor expected for NIPS. I recommend rejection due to insufficient evidence of significant improvement over established methods and inadequate experimental validation of the scalability claim.
