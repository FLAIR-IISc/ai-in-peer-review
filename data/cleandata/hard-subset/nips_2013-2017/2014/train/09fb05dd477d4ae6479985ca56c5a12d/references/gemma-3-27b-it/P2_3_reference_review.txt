## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

**Summary:**

This paper presents LSDA, an algorithm for adapting image classifiers into object detectors, addressing the challenge of limited bounding box annotations for large-scale detection tasks. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain (image-level labels) to the detection domain (bounding boxes and labels) using a subset of categories with both types of annotation. This transformation is then applied to classifiers trained on categories *without* bounding box annotations, effectively enabling detection for a large number of categories with minimal labeled data. The authors demonstrate a 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline that directly uses classifier weights for detection on the ImageNet detection challenge.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical problem:** The paper tackles a key bottleneck in scaling object detection to a large number of categories – the expensive and time-consuming process of collecting bounding box annotations.
* **Novel Approach:**  The idea of framing detection as a domain adaptation problem from classification is insightful and reasonably well-executed. Adapting classifiers to detectors, rather than training detectors from scratch, is a compelling strategy.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The 50% relative mAP improvement is significant, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed adaptation technique, especially on the held-out categories. The detailed ablation study clearly isolates the contribution of each component.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the LSDA algorithm. Figures 2 and 3 are particularly helpful in understanding the workflow and the effect of adaptation.
* **Practical Contribution:** The release of the 7.6K category detector and code is a valuable contribution to the community. The faster implementation with spatial pyramid pooling further enhances its usability.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to Related Work:** While the authors acknowledge related work (MIL, Domain Adaptation), the comparison feels somewhat superficial. The authors state that MIL methods haven’t been evaluated on ImageNet, but a more in-depth discussion of the trade-offs and complementary aspects would be beneficial. Similarly, the domain adaptation discussion could be more nuanced.
* **Reliance on Pre-trained Features:** The algorithm heavily relies on pre-trained ImageNet classification features. While this is common practice, it would be valuable to analyze the sensitivity of the approach to the quality and domain of the pre-trained model.  How would LSDA perform with features pre-trained on a different dataset?
* **Error Analysis Focus:** The error analysis, while insightful, primarily focuses on localization and background confusion. A deeper investigation into *why* the network misclassifies objects (as shown in Figure 5a) could provide further avenues for improvement.
* **Complexity of Implementation:** The method appears relatively complex, involving multiple fine-tuning stages and nearest neighbor searches.  While the results justify the complexity, a discussion of the computational cost and scalability to even larger datasets would be helpful.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The experimental setup is well-defined, and the results are convincingly presented. The ablation study provides strong support for the claims.  However, the reliance on pre-trained features and limited comparison to other domain adaptation approaches slightly lower the overall quality score.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The figures are clear and informative. The mathematical notation is relatively simple and well-explained.
* **Originality:** The approach of framing detection as a domain adaptation problem and adapting classification networks is novel. The specific implementation and the application to large-scale detection are also original contributions.
* **Significance:** The results are significant, demonstrating a practical approach to scaling object detection to a very large number of categories. The released code and 7.6K category detector are valuable resources for the community. The work has the potential to impact research in areas such as visual search and robotics.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant practical challenge in large-scale object detection.
* Presents a novel and effective approach based on domain adaptation.
* Achieves strong empirical results on a challenging benchmark dataset.
* Offers a valuable resource to the research community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited comparative analysis against other domain adaptation methods.
* Strong reliance on pre-trained features.
* The complexity of the implementation may limit its widespread adoption.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**.  The novelty and significance of the work outweigh the minor weaknesses. The LSDA algorithm offers a compelling solution to the problem of limited bounding box annotations for large-scale object detection, and the results are very promising.  While the comparison to related work could be more comprehensive, the authors have demonstrated a clear improvement over existing methods and provided a valuable contribution to the field.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the comments regarding the related work discussion and the analysis of pre-trained feature sensitivity.
