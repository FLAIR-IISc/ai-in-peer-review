### Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

**Summary**

This paper addresses the critical challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, for which exhaustive bounding box annotations are prohibitively expensive to collect. The authors propose an algorithm, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), which learns to transform an image classifier into an object detector. The core idea is to treat this as a domain adaptation problem. By leveraging a small set of categories with both image-level and bounding box labels, LSDA learns a generic, category-invariant transformation within the deep feature layers of a CNN. For categories lacking bounding box data, this learned transformation is applied to the shared feature layers, and a category-specific adaptation for the final classification layer is approximated by averaging the adaptations learned from visually similar source categories. Experiments on the ILSVRC2013 dataset show that this method provides a significant relative improvement (+50% mAP) on held-out categories compared to using a raw classifier baseline.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and difficult problem. The goal of building detectors for thousands of categories without requiring bounding box annotations for every class is a crucial step towards human-level visual understanding. The formulation of this problem as one of learning a transferable transformation from classification to detection is novel and insightful, moving beyond prior work like R-CNN that requires full detection supervision for all target classes.

2.  **Sound Methodology:** The proposed LSDA algorithm is well-motivated and logically structured. The decomposition of the adaptation into category-invariant (feature representation) and category-specific (classifier weights) components is sensible. The insight that learning to recognize "background" is a key part of the adaptation is particularly important and well-handled.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental setup is well-designed to test the central hypothesis. The ablation study in Figure 3 and Table 1 clearly demonstrates the value of each component of the LSDA pipeline, convincingly showing that the category-invariant feature adaptation provides the largest performance boost. The error analysis in Section 4.3 provides compelling evidence that the method succeeds by reducing localization and background confusion errors, which are the primary challenges when naively applying a classifier to detection.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising direction, the paper suffers from a considerable number of minor flaws in its technical details, presentation, and experimental clarity. These issues, in aggregate, undermine the confidence in the rigor of the work.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper exhibits a general lack of polish and attention to detail.
    *   **Referencing:** The bibliography is poorly formatted and contains numerous errors. For example, several entries have "In In Proc.", extraneous commas, or are incomplete (e.g., [10], [17]). The same paper appears to be cited twice with different formatting ([14] and [18]). This sloppiness is concerning in a top-tier conference submission.
    *   **Notation and Figures:** The notation in Equation (1), `δBNB(j,i)`, is clumsy and difficult to parse. A clearer formulation would improve readability. The caption for Figure 5(a) is not self-contained; terms like "sim", "ov", and "1-r" are used without any definition. The use of non-standard notation like `m K` instead of `m << K` is jarring.
    *   **Vague Statements:** Footnote 1 claims that using SVMs "removes the adaptation capacity of the system." This is a strong claim that is left entirely unsubstantiated and unclear. Does it mean the authors' specific adaptation method for the final layer cannot be applied to SVM weights? This needs to be explained precisely.

2.  **Methodological Concerns:**
    *   **Heuristic for Category-Specific Adaptation:** The core of the adaptation for held-out categories relies on a heuristic: averaging the classifier weight changes (`δB`) from the `k` nearest neighbors in the classification weight space (Section 3.2). The justification that similarity in classifier weights implies similarity in the required classifier-to-detector transformation is an assumption, not a proven fact. The choice of `L2` distance is not justified against other potential similarity metrics (e.g., cosine similarity). While empirically it shows a small gain, this critical step feels ad-hoc and lacks a solid theoretical or empirical foundation.
    *   **Omission of Key Detector Components:** The authors explicitly state they omit the standard steps of training class-specific SVMs and performing bounding box regression for speed and "flexibility." While this simplifies their framework, it also means they are not comparing against a state-of-the-art detector pipeline. This omission introduces a significant confounding variable; it is unclear how much of the performance gap to the "upper bound" is due to the lack of bbox data versus the simplified detection pipeline. The estimated 2-4 mAP points lost is substantial.

3.  **Experimental Ambiguity:**
    *   **Data Partitioning:** The description of the data split in Section 4.1 is ambiguous. It states that classification data is drawn from the ILSVRC2013 training set, and then that the detection set is augmented with 1000 images per category from the *same* training set. It is not specified whether these image sets are disjoint. Any overlap would compromise the integrity of the experiment, as the network would have seen bounding boxes for images it was also trained on with only image-level labels. This lack of clarity is a serious issue for reproducibility.
    *   **Upper Bound Comparison:** Table 1 reports an "All Data FT" mAP of 20.25%. This is presented as an upper bound. However, this is the average performance across all 200 categories. A more informative comparison would be the performance on the 100 held-out categories *if they had been trained with bounding box data*. This would provide a true "oracle" performance for the set of classes being evaluated and contextualize the 16.15% mAP achieved by LSDA more effectively.

**Recommendation: Reject**

This paper presents a novel and important idea for large-scale object detection. The core concept of learning a transferable adaptation from classifiers to detectors is valuable, and the empirical results show promise.

However, the manuscript in its current form is not ready for publication at NIPS. The work is undermined by a significant number of minor but collectively important issues, including a poorly prepared reference list, unclear methodological justifications for key components (especially the nearest-neighbor adaptation), and critical ambiguities in the experimental setup that impact reproducibility and the interpretation of results. While the high-level idea is strong, the execution and presentation lack the scientific rigor and meticulousness expected for a top-tier publication. The paper reads more like a promising progress report than a complete, polished piece of work. I would encourage the authors to address these numerous issues and resubmit to a future conference.
