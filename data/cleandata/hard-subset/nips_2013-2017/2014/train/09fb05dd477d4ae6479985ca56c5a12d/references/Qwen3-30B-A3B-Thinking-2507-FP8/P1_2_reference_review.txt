

# Peer Review: LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

## Summary

This paper introduces LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a novel approach to transform image classifiers into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The authors address the critical bottleneck in scaling object detection to large numbers of categories: the scarcity of bounding box labeled data. Instead of requiring bounding box annotations for all categories, LSDA uses image-level classification data (abundant) and only a small subset of bounding box labeled data to learn a transformation from classification to detection. The approach treats the problem as a domain adaptation task, where the source domain is image classification data and the target domain is detection data. LSDA employs three key insights: (1) recognizing background is crucial for adaptation, (2) category-invariant information can be transferred between classifier and detector feature representations, and (3) category-specific differences exist between classifiers and detectors. The authors demonstrate their method on the ImageNet detection dataset, achieving a 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline of using raw classifier weights on object proposal regions, and successfully creating a 7,604 category detector.

This work builds upon but significantly extends previous approaches like R-CNN, which required bounding box annotations for all categories, and MIL-based methods, which have not been evaluated on large-scale datasets like ImageNet. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of object detection by addressing a fundamental limitation in scaling detection systems.

## Evaluation

### Quality

The paper is technically sound with a well-designed methodology. The authors provide a clear ablation study (Figure 3, Table 1) demonstrating the importance of each component of their approach. The experiments are well-structured, using the standard ILSVRC2013 detection dataset with a realistic simulation of having bounding box labels for only a subset of categories. The implementation details are sufficient for reproducibility, and the authors have made their model definitions and weights publicly available.

The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately, noting that performance still lags behind oracle performance (where bounding box data is available for all categories). The statistical significance of their improvements is properly assessed with a paired sample t-test. The method's technical soundness is further validated by the clear demonstration of how their approach reduces localization errors (Figure 4).

*Weaknesses*: The paper doesn't provide enough detail on how the nearest neighbor approximation for category-specific adaptation performs when the nearest neighbors aren't actually similar in detection characteristics. It would be valuable to see how often the nearest neighbor approach leads to suboptimal adaptations.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and the authors' approach. The methodology is explained thoroughly with good visualizations (Figures 1-3) that aid understanding. The experimental setup is described with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The figures effectively illustrate key concepts (e.g., Figure 4 showing improved localization after adaptation).

*Minor weakness*: The paper states they "reduce training time from 3 days to roughly 5.5 hours" but doesn't specify what the baseline is that they're comparing to (e.g., is this compared to R-CNN training time or just the classification network training time?).

### Originality

This work makes a significant original contribution to the field. While previous work like R-CNN demonstrated that classification networks could be adapted to detection through fine-tuning, LSDA is the first method to adapt to *held-out categories* that have no detection data at all. This addresses a critical limitation in the field that has prevented scaling detection to thousands of categories. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from related work, including MIL-based methods and domain adaptation techniques, by emphasizing their ability to handle categories without any bounding box annotations.

The paper's core insight—treating the classification-to-detection transformation as a domain adaptation problem with specific techniques for handling held-out categories—is novel and well-motivated. The method represents a meaningful advancement over previous approaches.

### Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. The authors directly address a major bottleneck in scaling object detection systems to large numbers of categories, which is a critical step toward human-level detection capabilities. The paper's claim that "all that would be needed is to train a classification layer for the new categories and use our fine-tuned detection model" is a compelling argument for the method's practical utility.

The demonstration of a 7,604 category detector (with only 200 categories having bounding box annotations) is impressive and shows the potential scale of the approach. The paper's contribution is particularly significant given the scarcity of bounding box annotations for most categories in real-world applications. This work could substantially reduce the annotation burden for large-scale detection systems.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

### Strengths
- Addresses a critical bottleneck in scaling object detection (bounding box annotation scarcity)
- Novel approach to adapting classifiers to detectors for held-out categories
- Strong experimental results with a 50% relative mAP improvement
- Clear ablation study demonstrating the importance of each component
- Practical demonstration of a 7,604 category detector
- Well-written, clear organization, and good visualizations
- Public release of models and code

### Weaknesses
- The nearest neighbor approach for category-specific adaptation could fail if nearest neighbors aren't actually similar in detection characteristics
- Lacks comparison to other recent state-of-the-art detection methods beyond the baseline
- The "50% relative mAP improvement" needs clarification on whether it's relative to baseline or absolute (though the paper states it's relative)
- Doesn't thoroughly address how the method would perform on small objects or objects with significant occlusion
- Limited discussion of computational cost of the adaptation process

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. The work makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a fundamental limitation in scaling object detection systems. The technical soundness, clear presentation, and substantial experimental results demonstrate a strong scientific contribution that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Solves a meaningful and important problem in object detection (scaling to many categories)
- Novel approach that significantly extends previous work (R-CNN, MIL-based methods)
- Strong experimental results with clear ablation study
- Practical demonstration of a 7,604 category detector
- Well-written and clearly organized paper
- Public release of models and code enhances reproducibility

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- Some details about the nearest neighbor approach could be more thoroughly analyzed
- Limited comparison to other recent detection methods beyond the baseline
- Could provide more detail on how the method handles challenging detection scenarios

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Clarify the exact meaning of "50% relative mAP improvement" in the abstract and results sections to avoid confusion.

2. Provide more details about the nearest neighbor approach—specifically, how often the nearest neighbors are actually similar in detection characteristics, and what happens when they aren't. A small analysis on the correlation between classification similarity and detection adaptation quality would strengthen the paper.

3. Include a comparison to at least one recent state-of-the-art detection method (beyond R-CNN) to better contextualize the performance improvement.

4. Add a discussion about how the method might handle challenging detection scenarios like small objects, occlusions, or objects with significant viewpoint variations.

5. Clarify the baseline for the "reduced training time" claim (3 days to 5.5 hours) by specifying what the baseline method is.

## Conclusion

LSDA presents a significant advance in the field of object detection by addressing the critical problem of scaling detection to large numbers of categories with minimal bounding box annotations. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a compelling contribution that advances the state of the art. While there are minor areas for improvement, these don't detract from the paper's overall quality and significance. I believe this work would be of great interest to the NIPS community and would make an excellent contribution to the conference.
