

# Peer Review: LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a novel approach to transform image classifiers into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The core problem addressed is the significant challenge of obtaining bounding box annotations for large-scale object detection (tens or hundreds of thousands of categories), which is prohibitively expensive compared to acquiring image-level labels. The authors frame the problem as a domain adaptation task, where the source domain is classification data (image-level labels) and the target domain is detection data (bounding box annotations).

LSDA makes three key contributions: (1) recognizing background as an important step in adaptation, (2) transferring category-invariant information between classifier and detector feature representations, and (3) handling category-specific differences through nearest-neighbor approximation. The method fine-tunes a pre-trained classification network using a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations, then applies the learned transformation to adapt classifiers for categories without bounding box data.

This work relates to several previous contributions: it builds on R-CNN's insight that fine-tuning deep features is important for detection [1], but addresses the limitation of requiring bounding box data for all categories. It also connects to domain adaptation literature [12-16] and weakly-supervised detection methods using Multiple Instance Learning [6,7], though LSDA uniquely addresses adaptation to held-out categories with no detection data.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Practical Impact**: The paper addresses a genuinely important problem in computer vision - the scalability of object detection to large numbers of categories. The authors demonstrate a 50% relative mAP improvement over the baseline on held-out categories, which is a substantial improvement in the detection community.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The methodology is well-founded in the literature on domain adaptation and CNN fine-tuning. The ablation study (Figure 3, Table 1) provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of each component of the proposed approach.

3. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The authors clearly articulate the problem of scaling detection to large numbers of categories and the mismatch between classification and detection data requirements.

4. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The experiments on ImageNet detection dataset are well-designed, with appropriate baselines (classification-only network) and thorough analysis of error types (Figure 5). The demonstration of a 7,604 category detector showcases the scalability of the approach.

5. **Practical Implementation**: The authors provide publicly available code and models, which is valuable for reproducibility and adoption by the community.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art Weakly-Supervised Methods**: While the paper mentions MIL-based methods [6,7] as related work, it does not directly compare LSDA to these approaches on the ImageNet dataset. Given that MIL methods also aim to train detectors from weakly labeled data, this comparison would strengthen the paper's claims.

2. **Ambiguity in "Held-Out" Category Definition**: The paper states they use "the first 100 categories (alphabetically sorted)" for detection data, but does not explain why this particular subset was chosen. This selection could potentially bias results if these categories are easier to detect than the held-out ones.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: While the approach is well-motivated, the paper lacks a theoretical analysis of why the nearest-neighbor approximation (Equation 1) would work well for adaptation. This is particularly important since the paper claims to be the first method to adapt to held-out categories with no detection data.

4. **Potential Overfitting Concerns**: The paper mentions using "≈1000 images per class" for classification data, but ImageNet has 1.2M images for 1000 classes. The 1,000 images per class for 200 classes (200,000 total) might be insufficient to train strong classifiers, potentially making the baseline classification-only network weaker than it would be with more data.

5. **Evaluation Metric Ambiguity**: The paper states "50% relative mAP improvement" but does not clarify whether this is relative to the classification-only baseline or some other baseline. The exact numbers from Table 1 would help clarify this.

## Evaluation

### Quality
The paper is technically sound and well-supported by experiments. The methodology is carefully designed, and the ablation study provides convincing evidence for the importance of each component. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., "There is still a large gap to reach oracle (known bounding box labels) performance") and provide a clear analysis of error types. However, the lack of theoretical justification for the nearest-neighbor adaptation technique is a notable weakness.

### Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The figures (Figures 1-6) effectively illustrate key concepts. The experimental setup is described with sufficient detail to allow reproduction. Some minor improvements could be made to clarify the distinction between the "category invariant" and "category specific" adaptation components.

### Originality
The core idea of adapting classifiers to detectors using domain adaptation is novel in the context of scaling to large numbers of categories. While domain adaptation has been used in other contexts, LSDA's specific application to transfer from classification to detection for held-out categories appears to be novel. The paper properly situates itself relative to related work, particularly R-CNN and MIL-based approaches.

### Significance
This work addresses a critical bottleneck in scaling object detection to large numbers of categories. The 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories is significant, and the demonstration of a 7,604 category detector shows the potential impact of the approach. If the method can be applied to other large-scale detection tasks, it could substantially reduce the annotation burden for building detection systems.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The work addresses an important problem with a well-designed solution that shows significant improvements over baselines. The technical contribution is sound, and the results are compelling. While there are some limitations in the comparison to weakly-supervised methods and theoretical justification, these do not undermine the core contribution. The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a comparison to MIL-based approaches [6,7] on the ImageNet detection dataset, as these methods also aim to train detectors from weakly labeled data.

2. Clarify the "50% relative mAP" improvement by providing the absolute mAP values from Table 1.

3. Provide more details about the selection of the first 100 categories (alphabetically sorted) and whether this selection was randomized or potentially biased.

4. Include a brief theoretical discussion of why the nearest-neighbor approximation (Equation 1) is expected to work well for adaptation.

5. Consider adding a comparison to the state-of-the-art weakly-supervised detection method [7] on the ImageNet dataset.

## Final Assessment

This paper presents a novel and practical approach to scaling object detection to large numbers of categories by leveraging image-level classification data and a small subset of bounding box annotations. The results are impressive, with a 50% relative mAP improvement over the classification-only baseline on held-out categories. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and has clear practical significance for computer vision research and applications. While there are some limitations in comparison to related weakly-supervised methods, these do not significantly detract from the paper's contribution. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
