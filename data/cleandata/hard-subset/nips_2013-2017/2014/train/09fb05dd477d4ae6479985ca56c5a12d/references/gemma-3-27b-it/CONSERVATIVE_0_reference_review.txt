## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper proposes LSDA, an algorithm for adapting image classifiers to object detectors, leveraging the abundance of image-level labels and the scarcity of bounding box annotations. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain (image-level labels) to the detection domain (bounding boxes) using supervised CNNs, and then apply this transformation to adapt classifiers for categories without bounding box annotations. The motivation – addressing the bottleneck of bounding box annotation for large-scale detection – is certainly valid.

The paper is generally well-written and organized, though somewhat verbose. The introduction clearly lays out the problem and proposed solution. The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, placing the work within the context of MIL-based approaches and domain adaptation techniques. However, the discussion of related work feels somewhat cursory; for example, the connection to transfer learning beyond simple fine-tuning could be strengthened.  The methodological details are presented clearly, though Figure 2 could benefit from clearer visual separation of the different adaptation stages.

My primary concern with this work is the lack of a convincingly rigorous evaluation against strong baselines. While the paper demonstrates a 50% relative mAP improvement over a “classification only” baseline, this baseline is quite weak. Simply using raw classifier weights for detection is a naive approach, and a much more compelling comparison would be against more sophisticated techniques like fine-tuning the entire network with limited bounding box data (even if it's just on the 'm' categories) or employing more established domain adaptation methods *specifically designed* for object detection (as cited in the related work) – even if they require labeled data for all categories.  The authors acknowledge that R-CNN requires bounding box data for all categories, but do not convincingly demonstrate superiority over, say, a standard fine-tuning approach *within* the constraints they've imposed. The claim of adapting to “held-out categories that have no detection data” is interesting, but the mAP gains, while statistically significant, are modest (around 6%) and their practical impact is not fully explored.

The ablation study provides some insight into the importance of each component, but again lacks crucial baselines.  For example, how much performance is lost by *not* learning category-specific transformations (k=1)? The error analysis, while useful, is descriptive rather than diagnostic. Showing *why* the adaptation improves localization (Figure 4) is helpful, but doesn’t quantify the improvement. The presentation of the large-scale 7604 category detector is somewhat underwhelming without more detailed quantitative results.

Furthermore, the paper introduces several components (category invariant adaptation, category specific adaptation) without sufficient justification for the chosen approach. The nearest neighbor selection for category specific adaptation feels rather ad-hoc and lacks theoretical grounding. Why is the Euclidean distance in fc8 parameter space a good metric for determining similarity between categories? The claim that layers 1-7 induce a “generic, category invariant transformation” requires more justification than simply observing improved performance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in large-scale detection.
* Presents a novel approach to transferring knowledge from classification to detection.
* Demonstrates statistically significant improvements over a weak baseline.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Lacks a rigorous comparison to stronger and more established baselines.
* The proposed method’s components are not thoroughly justified.
* Modest performance gains on held-out categories warrant skepticism.
* Insufficient quantitative evaluation of the large-scale detector.



**Overall Assessment:**

I find this paper to be a reasonably well-executed but ultimately incremental contribution. While the idea of adapting classifiers to detectors is promising, the lack of a compelling comparison against existing methods and the modest performance gains raise concerns about its practical significance. I recommend **rejection** due to insufficient novelty and lack of rigorous evaluation.  The authors should significantly strengthen the experimental evaluation by comparing against stronger baselines and providing a more thorough justification for their methodological choices before resubmission.
