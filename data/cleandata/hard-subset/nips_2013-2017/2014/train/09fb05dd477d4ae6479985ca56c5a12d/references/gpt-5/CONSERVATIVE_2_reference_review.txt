Summary
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to adapt an image classification CNN into an object detection system when bounding box annotations are available for only a subset of categories. The approach has two components: (1) category-invariant adaptation by fine-tuning shared layers (conv1–fc7) and learning a background class using detection data for m categories, and (2) category-specific adaptation by estimating per-class output-layer deltas for held-out categories via nearest neighbors in fc8 weight space. At test time, region proposals are scored by the adapted network and a background score is subtracted. Experiments on ILSVRC13 detection simulate the setting with detection data for 100 categories and only image-level labels for the remaining 100. LSDA reports an improvement from 10.31% mAP (classification-only baseline) to 16.15% on held-out categories (+50% relative), and offers qualitative results for a 7.6K-category detector.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important problem (scaling detection beyond limited bbox-annotated categories) using established CNN architectures.
- Methodologically simple and computationally efficient; avoids additional SVMs and bbox regression to keep training time low.
- Clear decomposition into category-invariant and category-specific adaptation with an informative ablation. Background modeling and shared-layer fine-tuning are shown to be the main contributors.
- Solid experimental setup on ILSVRC with a standard split; includes error analysis and statistical significance for a key ablation (k = 10 vs no category-specific adaptation).
- Reproducibility is supported by using Caffe and releasing models/code.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: the main gains come from standard fine-tuning for detection with a background class; the category-specific transfer via nearest-neighbor fc8 deltas is a heuristic with small effect and minimal justification.
- Baselines are weak. The key comparison is to “classification-only on proposals,” which is known to localize poorly. There is no direct comparison to contemporary weakly supervised/MIL-based detectors on ILSVRC, nor to stronger internal baselines (e.g., fine-tuning with background only, or training per-class SVMs on adapted features even for held-out categories).
- Absolute performance on held-out categories remains low (16.15% mAP), and the 7.6K-category demonstration is qualitative; no quantitative evaluation or random-category audits are provided.
- The “domain adaptation” framing is somewhat stretched: the adaptation is not cross-domain but cross-task; there is no theoretical analysis or principled transfer criterion beyond fc8 proximity.
- Sensitivity analyses are limited. The choice of nearest-neighbor metric (fc8 Euclidean), number of neighbors k, and number of available detection categories m are not deeply probed. Gains from category-specific adaptation are marginal and only one p-value is reported.
- Some training choices complicate interpretation. The decision to forgo bbox regression and SVMs sacrifices performance; it would be useful to quantify how much of the gap to the full detector is due to these omissions versus the lack of bbox data.

Quality
Technically sound as an engineering contribution; the category-invariant fine-tuning with background is careful and the ablation supports the main claim that it is the dominant factor. However, the category-specific adaptation lacks rigorous justification and provides minor gains. The evaluation largely compares against internal baselines; absence of stronger external baselines undermines the strength of the claims. Error analysis is a plus, but quantitative breadth is limited (e.g., no per-class breakdowns or calibration analysis).

Clarity
The paper is readable and organized. The training pipeline, data splits, and model components are described at a level that should permit reproduction. Some details could be clearer: learning rates/schedules, exact proposal settings, IoU thresholds and NMS configuration, and sensitivity to k and distance metric for neighbor selection. The domain adaptation positioning could be tempered to avoid overstatement.

Originality
Moderate. The use of CNN fine-tuning for detection and adding a background class are established. The contribution is the attempt to generalize the classification-to-detection transformation learned on a subset of categories and to approximate per-class deltas via nearest neighbors. This is a straightforward heuristic rather than a principled transfer, and the novelty is incremental relative to R-CNN and prior adaptation work.

Significance
Pragmatic significance is moderate: showing that shared-layer fine-tuning with a background class can improve detection for held-out categories is useful, and releasing a large-scale model has potential practical impact. However, absent stronger baselines and more convincing quantitative results beyond a +5.8 mAP absolute gain from a weak baseline, it is difficult to conclude that LSDA materially advances the state of the art in weakly supervised detection. The 7.6K demonstration is interesting but anecdotal.

Questions for authors
- Can you compare to a weakly supervised/MIL baseline on a common subset (e.g., PASCAL VOC) or a scaled-down ILSVRC setting to establish relative gains?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of neighbor metric and k? Have you tried semantic (WordNet) or feature-space (fc7) distances?
- How does performance vary with m (number of categories with bbox)? Please provide a curve to validate scalability.
- What is the effect of including bbox regression and/or per-class linear SVMs trained on adapted features for held-out categories?
- Can you provide per-class AP changes to verify that improvements are not concentrated in a few categories?

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen baselines (MIL, WSD-like weakly supervised approaches where feasible, or at least per-class SVMs on adapted features).
- Provide sensitivity analyses for k, neighbor metrics, and m; include per-class AP tables and calibration plots.
- Quantify the contribution of the background class separately from other fine-tuning changes; e.g., fine-tuning with background only vs full fine-tuning.
- Add quantitative evaluation for a random subset of the 7.6K categories using human annotation to substantiate scalability claims.

Overall score: 5 (weak reject). The engineering insight that background-aware shared-layer fine-tuning transfers to held-out categories is useful, but the methodological novelty is limited, key comparisons are missing, and the quantitative evidence is not yet compelling.

Confidence: 0.7
