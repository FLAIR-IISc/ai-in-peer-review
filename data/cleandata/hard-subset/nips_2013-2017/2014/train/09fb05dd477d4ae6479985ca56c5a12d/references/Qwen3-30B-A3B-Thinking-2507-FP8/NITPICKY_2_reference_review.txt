

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

This paper proposes LSDA, an algorithm to transform image classifiers into object detectors using domain adaptation techniques, with the key innovation being the ability to adapt detectors for categories without bounding box annotations. The approach leverages category-invariant transformations and category-specific adaptations to achieve significant improvements over baseline classification-only models on held-out categories.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in scaling object detection to large numbers of categories, which is an important practical challenge. The core idea of adapting classifiers to detectors using limited bounding box annotations is conceptually sound and has potential practical value. The 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories (compared to classification-only baseline) is notable, and the demonstration of a 7604-category detector is impressive.

## Critical Weaknesses

### Technical and Methodological Flaws
1. **Ambiguous Performance Claims**: The paper states "significant improvement (+50% relative mAP)" without providing absolute mAP values for the baseline or LSDA. The claim of "50% relative improvement" is ambiguous - is it 50% of the baseline value or a 50% increase? The paper should clearly state both absolute and relative improvements.

2. **Missing Methodological Details**: In Section 3.2, the paper defines "δB" but never explains how these parameters are computed during training. The formula for category-specific adaptation (equation 1) lacks critical details about how δB is derived from the fine-tuning process.

3. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison**: The paper claims "we find empirically that fine-tuning induces a generic, category invariant transformation" but doesn't provide statistical evidence or comparative experiments to support this claim. The ablation study (Figure 3) is insufficient to prove the generality of the transformation.

4. **Unverified Claims**: The paper states "we find that one of the biggest reasons our algorithm improves is from reducing localization error" but provides no quantitative evidence or analysis to support this specific claim (beyond the qualitative examples in Figure 4).

### Experimental Design and Reproducibility Issues
1. **Hardware and Training Time Claims**: The paper claims "reduced training time from 3 days to roughly 5.5 hours" without specifying the hardware used for these measurements, making the claim difficult to verify or reproduce.

2. **Data Splitting Ambiguity**: The paper states they "split the ILSVRC2013 validation set in half as [1] did" but doesn't clarify how this differs from the standard split used in previous work, making it hard to compare results.

3. **Missing Model Availability**: While the paper claims "we have made our model definitions weights publicly available," no URL or DOI is provided, violating NIPS's reproducibility standards.

### Formatting and Presentation Issues
1. **Citation Inconsistencies**: The bibliography contains multiple formatting errors, including "In In Proc. CVPR" and inconsistent use of commas. The reference for R-CNN (cited as [1]) is incorrectly formatted.

2. **Figure References Without Figures**: The paper references "Figure 1," "Figure 2," "Figure 3," and "Figure 5" multiple times without providing the figures, making it impossible to evaluate the visual evidence.

3. **Ambiguous Terminology**: The paper states "we have m K" (line 11, Section 3) which is unclear and appears to be a typo (should be "m << K" or "m << K").

4. **Incomplete Dataset Description**: The paper claims "the ILSVRC2013 detection dataset offers a 200-category detection challenge" but doesn't clarify how this differs from the standard ImageNet detection challenge.

## Conclusion
While the paper addresses an important problem with a potentially valuable approach, the numerous technical and presentation flaws significantly undermine the contribution. The ambiguous performance claims, missing methodological details, and unverified assertions make it impossible to properly evaluate the significance of the results. The paper's inability to provide sufficient experimental details for reproducibility and the inconsistent presentation of results are particularly concerning for a NIPS submission.

The paper's core idea has merit, but the execution lacks the technical rigor required for publication at NIPS. The authors should address these issues before resubmitting, particularly by providing clear performance metrics, complete methodological details, and proper statistical evidence for their claims.

**Recommendation: Reject**
