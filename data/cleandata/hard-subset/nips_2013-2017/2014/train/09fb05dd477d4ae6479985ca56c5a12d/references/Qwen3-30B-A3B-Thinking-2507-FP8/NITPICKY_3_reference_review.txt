

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

## Summary
This paper proposes LSDA, a method for adapting image classifiers into object detectors using domain adaptation techniques. The core idea is to leverage image-level classification data for all categories while requiring bounding box annotations for only a subset of categories. The authors frame this as a domain adaptation problem between classification (source domain) and detection (target domain). They introduce three key insights: (1) recognizing background is crucial, (2) category-invariant information can be transferred, and (3) category-specific differences exist between classifiers and detectors. The method fine-tunes a pre-trained CNN on bounding box data for a subset of categories, then uses nearest neighbor search to adapt classifiers for categories without detection labels.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important problem in scaling object detection to large numbers of categories. The approach is conceptually simple and builds on established techniques (domain adaptation, fine-tuning), which is a strength. The experiments on ILSVRC2013 demonstrate a 50% relative mAP improvement over a classification-only baseline, which is significant. The idea of adapting classifiers for held-out categories with no bounding box data has practical implications for large-scale detection systems.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Errors and Inconsistencies
The paper contains several significant technical errors that undermine its credibility. Most notably, it incorrectly states that "R-CNN [1] trains SVMs on the extracted features from layer 7" when R-CNN actually uses SVMs on layer 5 features (Section 3.3). This is a fundamental misunderstanding of the baseline method they're comparing against.

The paper also contains inconsistent notation throughout. For example, in Section 3.2, the output layer is described as "fcA, fcB, δB, and fc − BG," which is confusingly written (the "fc − BG" notation should be "fc_background" or similar). Equation (1) has poor formatting with "1 k k∑ i=1" instead of proper mathematical notation.

### Methodological Issues
The paper claims a "50% relative mAP boost" but fails to specify the baseline mAP value. Without this, the claimed improvement is meaningless (a 50% relative improvement from 10% mAP is only a 5% absolute improvement, which may not be significant). The paper should provide both absolute and relative metrics.

The authors state "we find empirically that fine-tuning induces a generic, category invariant transformation" but provide no statistical evidence for this claim. The paper should include statistical significance tests for the improvements they report.

The paper claims "we have made our model definitions weights publicly available" but fails to provide a URL or link to these resources, making reproduction impossible. The reference to "lsda.berkeleyvision.org" is not included in the paper, which is a critical omission.

### Experimental Limitations
The experiment setup contains errors. The paper states "we split the ILSVRC2013 validation set in half as [1] did" but [1] (R-CNN) used PASCAL VOC, not ILSVRC2013. This is a citation error that undermines the experimental protocol.

The paper claims "we have released the 7.6K model and code" but provides no details about the model architecture or training process. The claim that "detection time is reduced to half a second per image" lacks comparison to other methods (e.g., R-CNN's 4s per image) or a proper ablation study.

### Writing and Formatting Issues
The paper contains multiple grammatical errors (e.g., "large of amount" in Introduction) and inconsistent terminology. The references have formatting issues, including "In In Proc. ECCV" in reference [26].

The paper states "the majority of false positive errors are confusion with background and localization errors" but fails to provide statistical evidence for this claim in the error analysis section. The paper also lacks a proper discussion of limitations.

### Originality Concerns
While the problem is important, the approach is not sufficiently novel. The paper cites "Tabula rasa: Model transfer for object category detection" [22] but fails to adequately differentiate their work from this prior work. The nearest-neighbor approach for category-specific adaptation is a standard transfer learning technique and not novel.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an important problem with potential practical significance, it contains numerous technical errors, methodological weaknesses, and formatting issues that significantly undermine its credibility. The paper's claims about performance improvements are not properly supported by the data, and the experimental setup contains critical errors that invalidate the comparison to the R-CNN baseline. The writing is inconsistent and contains multiple grammatical errors that detract from the paper's quality.

The paper's core idea has merit, but the execution is poor. The authors should address the technical errors, provide clearer explanations of their methodology, and properly evaluate their approach against the baseline they're comparing to before submitting to NIPS. The current version does not meet the standards of technical rigor expected at NIPS.
