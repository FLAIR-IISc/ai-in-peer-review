This paper proposes LSDA, a method for adapting classifiers trained on image-level labels to object detectors, leveraging a smaller set of bounding box annotations. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain to the detection domain and apply this transformation to classifiers for categories without bounding box labels. The approach is framed as a domain adaptation problem, and the authors demonstrate improved performance on the ImageNet detection task, achieving a 50% relative mAP gain over a baseline.

While the problem addressed – scaling detection to large numbers of categories with limited annotation – is undoubtedly important, and the idea of transferring knowledge from classification to detection is appealing, this work feels incremental and insufficiently validated given its claims. The approach relies heavily on the established R-CNN paradigm, and the adaptation strategy, while conceptually reasonable, lacks substantial novelty. The method's reliance on pre-trained CNNs (Krizhevsky et al. [4]) and fine-tuning, while standard, limits the perceived originality. 

The paper’s primary weakness lies in the limited scope of the experimental evaluation. While a 50% relative mAP improvement is reported, it’s unclear how this translates to absolute performance. The baseline (raw classifier weights) is weak, making the improvement less convincing. A comparison against more established detection methods, especially those that *also* leverage transfer learning or weak supervision (e.g., methods building on MIL as mentioned in the related work) is crucial. The authors acknowledge the lack of comparison to MIL-based methods due to evaluation on the ImageNet detection challenge being missing, but this is a significant oversight, as these methods represent a direct alternative. Furthermore, the ablation study, while helpful, doesn’t fully disentangle the contributions of each adaptation component. The impact of the nearest neighbor approach for categories without bounding boxes needs more thorough investigation – is k=10 truly optimal, or is it sensitive to the choice of k? 

The error analysis is a positive aspect, offering insights into the types of errors made by the adapted detectors. However, the observation that errors often involve similar categories is not surprising and doesn't fully explain the limitations of the approach. The claim of reducing localization error, supported by qualitative examples, is also somewhat weak; better visualization or quantitative metrics would strengthen this argument. The release of a 7604-category detector is ambitious, but the description lacks detail. Performance metrics on this large-scale detector are absent, rendering the achievement difficult to assess.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in object detection.
*   The idea of transferring knowledge from classification to detection is intuitively appealing.
*   Demonstrates a performance improvement over a weak baseline.
*   Includes error analysis providing some insights into the method's limitations.
*   Code and models are made publicly available.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty – builds heavily on established methods (R-CNN).
*   Insufficiently rigorous experimental evaluation – lacks comparison to strong baselines (especially MIL-based methods).
*   Baseline is weak, making the reported improvement less convincing.
*   Ablation study could be more comprehensive.
*   Limited details and performance metrics for the large-scale 7604-category detector.
*   Claims of improved localization are supported by limited evidence.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in scaling object detection.
*   Demonstrates a measurable improvement in performance, albeit on a weak baseline.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks substantial novelty compared to existing work.
*   Insufficiently validated against strong baselines.
*   Experimental results are not presented with sufficient detail.
*   The impact and significance of the work are overstated given the current evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

Reject. While the paper presents a reasonable approach to a challenging problem, it falls short of the standards expected for NIPS acceptance. The lack of a strong comparison to related work and the limited depth of the experimental evaluation raise concerns about the robustness and significance of the findings. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons to state-of-the-art methods and a more detailed analysis of the results. The current presentation feels more like a proof-of-concept than a significant contribution to the field.
