Summary
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to adapt image classifiers into object detectors when only a subset of categories has bounding box (detection) annotations. The approach treats the problem as domain adaptation from classification (source) to detection (target). It has two main components: (1) category-invariant adaptation by fine-tuning layers 1–7 of a CNN (AlexNet) for detection using only the m categories with detection labels, which also learns a background class; and (2) category-specific adaptation for the held-out categories by transferring the change observed in the fine-tuned detection output layer (δB) from nearest neighbor categories (in fc8 weight space) to categories without detection labels (A), averaging over k neighbors. At test time, they score region proposals with K+1 softmax outputs and use class score minus background score. On ILSVRC13 detection, with 100 categories held out from detection supervision, LSDA improves mAP on these held-out categories from 10.31% (classification-only baseline on proposals) to 15.85% via invariant adaptation and to 16.15% with category-specific transfer (about 50% relative). They also report a 7.6K-category detector demo and a faster SPP-based implementation.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on R-CNN, which showed the importance of fine-tuning for detection but requires bounding boxes for all categories. LSDA’s novelty is adapting to categories with no boxes by learning a generic “classification-to-detection” transformation and transferring category-specific output changes via nearest neighbors. It contrasts with weakly supervised MIL approaches, and with prior domain adaptation for detection that assume detection labels for all categories. The references cover these lines adequately; the positioning—adapting to unseen categories in the detection domain—is clear.

Strengths
- Addresses a high-impact problem: scaling detection to thousands of categories without collecting boxes for all.
- Clear, simple method that leverages existing CNN fine-tuning and adds an interpretable background class and a lightweight output-layer transfer.
- Solid ablations isolate contributions: the category-invariant fine-tuning and background modeling account for most gains; output-layer transfer gives a smaller but significant boost (p=0.017).
- Sensible evaluation protocol on ILSVRC13 with held-out categories; error analysis indicates reduced background and localization errors, matching the intended adaptation effect.
- Practical contribution: public code/models and a 7.6K-category detector demo; discussion of runtime trade-offs.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in the core mechanism: most gains stem from standard fine-tuning for detection plus adding a background class. The category-specific transfer via nearest-neighbor averaging of δ is heuristic and contributes marginally to mAP.
- Missing comparisons to strong weakly supervised detection methods on ILSVRC13 (authors note the lack of published results, but at least some smaller-scale WSOD baselines or MIL on their split would strengthen claims).
- Sensitivity analysis is limited. The method’s dependence on m (number of categories with boxes) and on k (neighbors) is only partially explored; no study of performance versus amount of detection supervision.
- The nearest-neighbor metric (L2 distance in fc8 classification weights) is ad hoc. Alternatives (visual feature similarity, semantic/WordNet, attribute sharing, learned metrics) are not evaluated.
- Absolute performance remains far from the oracle with detection labels for all categories; while expected, more discussion on closing the gap (e.g., combining with MIL mining) would be useful.
- Some evaluation choices could be clarified: training on val1 + sampled train boxes and evaluating on val2 is common in R-CNN, but details on possible overlap with classification-only images, calibration across classes, and cross-category NMS for the 7.6K demo are sparse.

Quality
Technically sound and empirically supported. The ablations justify the main claims. The statistical test for the output-layer transfer is a nice touch. However, the paper would benefit from broader baselines (even if weaker) and a sensitivity study to better characterize when LSDA helps most. The method is complete and reproducible (Caffe models released).

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The three “insights” map cleanly to implementation steps. Some figures/tables referenced are not present here, but the described results are understandable. Minor typographical issues aside, the exposition is adequate. Adding pseudocode or a compact algorithm box and more explicit training details (learning rates, batch composition for background vs foreground) would further aid reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. The framing—adapting classification to detection with partial category supervision—and learning a category-invariant transformation that transfers to unseen categories is a useful idea. The specific mechanisms (fine-tuning with background; neighbor-averaged δ transfer) are relatively simple and close to existing practice. The contribution is more in problem setup and empirical validation than in algorithmic novelty.

Significance
High practical significance. Many practitioners want to scale detection beyond the limited set with boxes; LSDA offers a straightforward path with measurable gains and released models. It is likely to be used/built upon, and it helped motivate later work on large-scale detection and weakly/partially supervised detection. Scientific significance is solid but not groundbreaking; nonetheless, the demonstrated 50% relative improvement on held-out categories is meaningful.

Questions/clarifications for authors
- How does performance vary with m (e.g., m=25, 50, 150)? Is there a diminishing return curve?
- How sensitive is the category-specific transfer to k and to the neighbor metric? Did you try semantic (WordNet) or feature-based neighbors?
- Can MIL-based mining on categories in A, initialized from LSDA, further narrow the gap to the oracle?
- Are classification and detection training image sets disjoint per category? Please clarify to avoid potential leakage.
- Any quantitative evaluation for the 7.6K detector beyond qualitative examples (e.g., on subsets with boxes)?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a practical, easy-to-apply method.
- Clear empirical improvements with ablations and error analysis.
- Public release and scalability demonstration increase impact.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited algorithmic novelty; strongest gains come from standard fine-tuning with background.
- Missing comparisons to alternative partial/weak supervision strategies and limited sensitivity analysis.
- Absolute accuracy still low; limited analysis of when/where it helps most.

Recommendation
Lean accept. Despite modest novelty, the problem is significant, the method is simple and effective, and the empirical evidence is convincing. The paper should be valuable to the community and likely to spur follow-on work. Constructive revisions to add sensitivity analyses and stronger baselines would further strengthen it.
