Summary
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to convert image classifiers into object detectors using limited bounding box supervision. The approach treats classification-to-detection transfer as domain adaptation. Starting from a CNN pre-trained on ImageNet classification, the method: (1) fine-tunes layers 1–7 and a background class using bounding-box annotations for a subset B of categories to learn a category-invariant transformation from classification to detection; and (2) performs category-specific adaptation for held-out categories A by transferring the average change in the final-layer weights (δ) from their nearest neighbors in B (nearest in L2-normalized fc8 space). Detection scores subtract the learned background score. Experiments on ILSVRC 2013 simulate having detection labels for 100 categories and only image-level labels for the remaining 100. On held-out categories, LSDA improves mAP from 10.31% to 16.15% (+50% relative) with k=10 nearest neighbors, with most of the gain coming from the shared feature adaptation and background modeling. The authors also demonstrate a 7,604-class detector using only 200 detection-labeled categories and release models/code. This work builds on R-CNN for detection and the use of CNN “deep features” for transfer learning, while differing by explicitly adapting from classification to detection without bounding boxes for all categories.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The methodology is clearly defined and internally consistent. The category-invariant adaptation is implemented via standard fine-tuning with a background class on subset B, which is appropriate for task shift to detection. Category-specific transfer via nearest-neighbor averaging of fc8 deltas is a simple, well-defined heuristic. The detection pipeline (region proposals + CNN scoring) is standard for the period. No theoretical guarantees are claimed or required for the heuristic; empirical validation is provided.
- Empirical support: Experiments use a large, relevant benchmark (ILSVRC13 detection) with a realistic held-out-category protocol. An ablation study isolates contributions: background modeling and layers 1–7 fine-tuning account for most gains; fc8 adaptation adds a small but statistically significant improvement (p=0.017). Error analysis indicates reduced localization and background confusions, matching the paper’s hypothesis. An “oracle” upper bound using full detection supervision is reported.
- Completeness: The study lacks comparisons to alternative weakly supervised detection methods (e.g., MIL-based) under similar data constraints on ILSVRC; the authors note those methods had not been scaled to this benchmark. The baseline choices and training details are mostly adequate but some hyperparameters are under-specified.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Figures and the three-step decomposition help understanding. Minor notational issues: the definition of sets A and B could be clearer (A should be {m+1,…,K}); fcA/fcB notation is dense and could be simplified. More explicit training details (learning rates, schedule, proposal counts) would improve reproducibility, though code/models are released.

Originality
- The framing of classifier-to-detector transfer as domain adaptation with category-invariant CNN fine-tuning and category-specific delta transfer to categories lacking bounding boxes is novel at the time. Prior supervised adaptation methods typically require detection labels for all categories; this work adapts to held-out categories. The background-class addition for task shift is known, but its role in cross-category transfer and the specific fc8-delta nearest-neighbor transfer are new contributions.

Significance
- The problem—scaling detection to thousands of categories with limited bounding box annotations—is important. Absolute performance is modest on held-out categories (≈16% mAP), but the relative improvement and the ability to produce 7.6K-class detectors are practically significant and likely to influence subsequent work. Public release of models/tools increases impact.

Strengths
- Addresses a critical scalability bottleneck for detection using abundant classification data.
- Clear decomposition: category-invariant vs category-specific transfer; demonstrates the importance of background modeling and feature adaptation.
- Solid empirical evaluation on a large-scale benchmark with ablations and error analysis; significance testing for fc8 adaptation.
- Demonstration at 7.6K classes and code/model release.

Weaknesses
- Category-specific transfer via nearest neighbors in fc8 space is heuristic; sensitivity analysis beyond k selection is limited (e.g., semantic vs visual neighbors).
- No comparison to state-of-the-art weakly supervised detection approaches under the same data regime on ILSVRC; even a constrained or smaller-scale proxy would strengthen positioning.
- The baseline excludes bounding-box regression; exploring a category-invariant regressor trained on B and applied to A could further reduce localization errors.
- Some experimental details are under-specified; clarity on proposal settings and training schedules would help reproducibility.
- Absolute mAP remains low on held-out categories; analysis of performance as a function of the number of categories with boxes (m) would be informative for practical adoption.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Provide sensitivity to k and to the neighbor metric (e.g., cosine on features, semantic similarity via WordNet) and report whether combining visual and semantic neighbors helps.
- Evaluate a category-invariant bounding-box regressor trained on B and applied to A.
- Include calibration analysis of the background subtraction choice (logit difference vs softmax probabilities), and per-class bias calibration for the held-out classes.
- Add results showing performance vs number of detection-labeled categories (m), to guide annotation trade-offs.
- If feasible, include a comparison on a subset where MIL/WSOD baselines can be trained at scale, or cite the closest available large-scale results.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and practical approach to reduce detection annotation requirements.
- Demonstrated large relative gains on a challenging large-scale benchmark, with solid analysis.
- High potential impact for scaling detection; released resources.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heuristic category-specific transfer with limited analysis; small marginal gains over category-invariant adaptation.
- Missing comparisons to contemporary weakly supervised detectors on the same benchmark.
- Modest absolute performance on held-out categories; incomplete exploration of bounding-box regression transfer.

Recommendation
Leaning toward acceptance based on originality and practical significance, with sound methodology and convincing ablations, despite missing comparisons and modest absolute mAP on held-out categories.
