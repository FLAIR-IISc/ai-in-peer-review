This paper proposes LSDA, an algorithm for large-scale object detection that leverages image-level classification data to adapt classifiers into detectors, requiring bounding box annotations for only a subset of categories. The core idea is to treat the classification-to-detection adaptation as a domain adaptation problem, learning a transformation that can be applied to classifiers lacking bounding box labels. The approach demonstrates a significant improvement (50% relative mAP) on the ImageNet detection task compared to a baseline using raw classifier weights.

This work is well-motivated and addresses a critical challenge in scaling object detection to a large number of categories – the scarcity of bounding box annotations. The problem is clearly articulated, and the proposed approach is a reasonable and intuitive solution, building upon the recent success of R-CNN and domain adaptation techniques. The connection to existing work, including Multiple Instance Learning and domain adaptation methods, is adequately discussed. The experiments on ImageNet are substantial, and the ablation study effectively demonstrates the contribution of each component of the LSDA algorithm. The improvement in localization accuracy shown in Figure 4 is particularly compelling. The release of both the model and code is commendable and will foster reproducibility and further research.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The clarity of certain sections could be improved. For example, the explanation of the category-specific adaptation (section 3.2) feels somewhat convoluted and the rationale behind using nearest neighbors isn’t fully convincing. A more intuitive explanation, potentially with a visual aid, would be helpful. While the quantitative results are strong, the analysis lacks depth. A more detailed breakdown of performance across different categories – distinguishing between those with and without bounding box annotations – would provide valuable insights. The ablation study primarily focuses on the core adaptation components; exploring the impact of different network architectures or training parameters would further strengthen the analysis. The comparison to other weakly-supervised detection methods is limited. While MIL-based methods are mentioned, a direct comparison on the ImageNet detection task is missing, hindering a comprehensive evaluation of LSDA’s strengths and weaknesses. The discussion of the error analysis (section 4.3) is somewhat superficial. While identifying the types of errors is useful, a deeper investigation into the causes of these errors and potential mitigation strategies would be beneficial. Finally, the paper assumes access to already trained classification models. The impact of the initial classification model quality on the final detection performance is not explored.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant problem in scaling object detection.
*   Presents a novel and intuitive approach to domain adaptation for detection.
*   Demonstrates strong quantitative results on the challenging ImageNet detection task.
*   Includes a thorough ablation study.
*   Code and model are publicly available, promoting reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Clarity of certain sections could be improved.
*   Limited comparison to other weakly-supervised detection methods.
*   Analysis lacks depth, particularly regarding category-specific performance.
*   Superficial error analysis.
*   The influence of the initial classification model quality is unexplored.



**Overall assessment:**

While the paper has some weaknesses in terms of clarity and analysis depth, the strength of the proposed approach, the solid experimental results, and the release of code make it a valuable contribution to the field. I believe that addressing the concerns regarding clarity and analysis would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should focus on improving the explanation of the category-specific adaptation, providing a more detailed analysis of performance across categories, including a comparison to relevant MIL-based methods, and expanding the error analysis to identify potential areas for improvement.
