- Proposes LSDA, a method to convert image classifiers into object detectors using minimal bounding-box annotations by learning a transformation from classification to detection.  
- Combines category-invariant adaptation (fine-tuning convolutional layers on a subset with boxes) with category-specific adaptation (nearest-neighbor mapping in classification feature space).  
- Demonstrates ~50% relative mAP improvement over a classification-only baseline on ImageNet and builds a 7,604-category detector using mostly classification data.  
- Clearly motivates the practical scalability problem of obtaining bounding-box annotations for thousands of categories.  
- Novel technical contribution: identifies background modeling, category-invariant transfer, and category-specific differences, and introduces a nearest-neighbor adaptation for outputs.  
- Strong experimental validation with ablation studies and statistical significance testing.  
- High practical impact by substantially reducing annotation costs for large-scale detection.  
- Well-written, well-organized paper with reproducible methodological detail and helpful figures.  
- Comparison is limited mainly to R-CNN and a classification baseline, lacking evaluation against newer detectors (e.g., Faster R-CNN, SSD, Mask R-CNN).  
- Insufficient justification for using Euclidean distance in fc8 space for category-specific nearest-neighbor adaptation.  
- No analysis of limitations when target categories differ greatly from annotated categories (e.g., animals vs. vehicles).  
- Training-time claim ("3 days to 5.5 hours") lacks detailed computational-cost analysis of the adaptation process.  
- Error analysis and discussion of failure modes are limited and could be expanded.  
- Overall assessment: strong, novel, and practically significant contribution that advances large-scale detection.  
- Recommendation: accept (8/10 — Strong Accept).  
- Key arguments for acceptance: addresses a critical scalability bottleneck, extends existing work conceptually, shows strong empirical gains, and demonstrates real-world scalability with thousands of categories.  
- Key arguments for improvement: compare to recent detectors, justify or explore alternative similarity metrics for category adaptation, analyze nearest-neighbor limits across dissimilar categories, and provide a detailed computational-cost/failure-mode analysis.
