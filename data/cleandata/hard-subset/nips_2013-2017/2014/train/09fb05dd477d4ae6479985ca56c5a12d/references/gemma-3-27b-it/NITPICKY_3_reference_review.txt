## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper proposes LSDA, an algorithm to adapt image classifiers into object detectors, leveraging labeled classification data and a limited amount of bounding box annotations. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain to the detection domain and apply it to categories lacking bounding box data. The authors present experiments on ImageNet detection, demonstrating a 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline using raw classifier weights.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical problem:** The need for massive amounts of bounding box annotations is a significant bottleneck in scaling object detection to a large number of categories. LSDA directly tackles this issue.
* **Novelty of approach:** Casting the problem as a domain adaptation task between classification and detection is a reasonable and potentially fruitful direction. The category-specific adaptation via nearest neighbor weighting is a clever addition.
* **Solid experimental validation:** The results on ImageNet demonstrate a substantial improvement, suggesting the proposed method is effective.  The ablation study offers insights into the contribution of each component.
* **Publicly available code and models:** This is a crucial positive aspect, enabling reproducibility and further research.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of detailed ablation study justification:** While an ablation study is present, the rationale behind choosing specific components for ablation is not fully explained.  For example, why were only the convolutional layers fine-tuned in the “Category Invariant Adaptation” experiment?
* **Insufficient comparison to existing weak supervision methods:** The paper mentions MIL-based methods but lacks a direct comparison, stating they haven't been evaluated on ImageNet detection. This is a significant oversight, as MIL represents a viable alternative. A discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of LSDA *relative to* MIL is crucial.
* **Ambiguous description of nearest neighbor selection:** The criteria for "nearest neighbors" (Euclidean distance of normalized fc8 parameters) seem somewhat arbitrary. A more detailed justification or exploration of alternative distance metrics would be beneficial. Was the number of neighbors (k) optimized, or was k=10 chosen arbitrarily?
* **Overstated claims about simplicity:** The paper emphasizes the simplification achieved by avoiding SVM training and bounding box regression. However, the LSDA pipeline still involves multiple stages of fine-tuning and parameter selection, raising questions about the overall practical simplicity.  The reduction in training time, while mentioned, doesn’t fully offset the complexity.
* **Presentation of results:** The figures (e.g., Figure 3, error analysis in Figure 5) are difficult to interpret. Axis labels are unclear, and the visual impact is diminished by the overall aesthetic.
* **Minor technical details:** The use of “sim” in Table 1’s category names is puzzling and requires clarification.  The paper states “We modified the analysis software made available by Hoeim et al. [26]” – what modifications were made?

**Detailed Comments:**

* **Introduction:** The motivation is well-established. However, the statement about “scaling our systems to human-level category detection” feels hyperbolic.
* **Related Work:** The discussion of domain adaptation is somewhat superficial. It would be helpful to discuss the challenges of adapting *between tasks* (classification to detection) versus simply adapting *within* a task.
* **Section 3.1:** The choice of Krizhevsky et al. [4] architecture is reasonable, but the justification could be stronger.
* **Section 3.2:** The nearest neighbor approach is interesting, but the rationale for using normalized fc8 parameters isn't fully clear. Why does normalization matter?
* **Section 4:** The experimental setup is adequately described, but more detail on hyperparameter tuning would be helpful.  What learning rates were used? What regularization techniques were employed?
* **Section 4.2:** The statistical significance test (paired sample t-test) is good practice, but it would be more informative to report effect sizes.
* **Section 4.3:** The error analysis is a valuable addition, but the visualization in Figure 5 is not particularly informative.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel approach to a significant problem.
* Demonstrable improvement in ImageNet detection.
* Publicly available code and models.
* Well-written and generally clear (despite some minor issues).

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* Lack of thorough comparison to existing weak supervision techniques.
* Unjustified design choices and a superficial ablation study.
* Overstated claims about simplicity.
* Presentation issues (figures, clarity of certain descriptions).
* The somewhat arbitrary nature of the normalization and nearest neighbor selection.



**Overall Assessment:**

While LSDA presents a promising approach to large-scale detection, the paper suffers from several minor but significant flaws. The lack of a comprehensive comparison to related work, the insufficient justification for certain design choices, and the presentation issues detract from the overall impact.  I believe the paper is currently not ready for NIPS publication, as it fails to meet the stringent standards of technical rigor and clarity. **I recommend rejection.**  I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit a revised version in the future. Specifically, a more thorough comparison to MIL-based methods is crucial.
