## Peer Review: LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

**Summary:**

This paper introduces LSDA, a novel approach to large-scale object detection that tackles the problem of limited bounding box annotations. The core idea is to learn a transformation from image classifiers (trained on abundant image-level labels) to object detectors (requiring expensive bounding box labels). LSDA frames this as a domain adaptation problem, leveraging labeled data for a subset of categories to adapt classifiers for categories without bounding box annotations. The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements on the ImageNet detection challenge, achieving a 50% relative mAP boost over a baseline relying solely on classifier weights. The work is well-motivated, addressing a critical bottleneck in scaling object detection to a larger number of categories.

**Related Work:**

The paper appropriately positions itself within the existing literature. It acknowledges prior work on Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) for weakly supervised detection and highlights the differences – namely, the focus on large-scale adaptation and adaptation to *held-out* categories.  The discussion of domain adaptation is also relevant, although the authors clearly delineate how their work differs by adapting *both* the visual domain and the task (classification to detection).  The referencing is comprehensive and demonstrates an awareness of the current state-of-the-art.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of learning a general transformation to adapt classifiers into detectors is a significant contribution.  Prior work often requires bounding box annotations for *all* categories, limiting scalability. LSDA breaks this dependency.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The 50% relative mAP improvement on ImageNet is substantial and demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach. The ablation study effectively isolates the contributions of the different adaptation components.
* **Practical Implications:**  The potential for detecting a vast number of categories with minimal annotation effort is a highly valuable contribution, pushing the field closer to human-level object recognition.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The figures effectively illustrate the key ideas and the experimental setup.  The release of the code and model is commendable and will facilitate further research.
* **Error Analysis:** The error analysis, particularly the breakdown of false positive types (localization, background, other), provides valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Dependence on Pre-trained Classifier:** While a practical necessity, the reliance on a pre-trained ImageNet classifier creates a dependency. The performance is inherently limited by the quality of this initial classifier. It would be valuable to explore the sensitivity of the approach to different pre-training datasets or architectures.
* **Category Specific Adaptation – Nearest Neighbor Approach:**  The method for approximating the fine-tuning for categories *without* bounding box data (using nearest neighbors) feels somewhat ad-hoc. While it works, a more principled approach to parameter estimation could potentially improve performance. Further justification for this method would be useful.  The exploration of different *k* values in the ablation study is a step in this direction, but a more thorough investigation would be beneficial.
* **Comparison to MIL methods:** While the authors acknowledge MIL methods, a direct comparison on the ImageNet detection challenge would significantly strengthen the paper.  It is noted in the related work that direct comparison hasn't been done yet, but this seems like a crucial validation step.
* **Computational Cost:** The runtime is mentioned as being reduced, but a clear breakdown of the computational costs of each stage (pre-training, fine-tuning, detection) would be helpful.  The paper touches upon using faster region proposals as a solution, but a more detailed discussion of computational trade-offs would be valuable.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. The ablation study strengthens the validity of the findings. It’s a complete and well-executed work, going beyond a mere position paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The explanation of the LSDA algorithm is easy to follow, and the figures are helpful. 

**Originality:** The approach is novel and represents a significant advancement in large-scale object detection. The idea of learning a general adaptation transformation is innovative.

**Significance:** The results are important and have the potential to significantly impact the field. The ability to scale object detection to a large number of categories with minimal annotation effort is a major step forward.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and effective approach to a challenging problem.
* Strong experimental results demonstrating significant performance improvement.
* Clear presentation and well-written paper.
* Potential for significant impact on the field of object detection.
* Released code and models for reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Dependence on a pre-trained classifier.
* Ad-hoc nature of the category-specific adaptation using nearest neighbors.
* Lack of direct comparison to MIL methods.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept.**

This is a strong paper that addresses a significant problem in object detection. The proposed approach is novel, effective, and well-supported by experimental results. While there are some minor weaknesses, the overall contribution is significant and warrants acceptance to NIPS.  I encourage the authors to address the concerns regarding the nearest neighbor approach and potentially include a comparison to MIL methods in future work.
