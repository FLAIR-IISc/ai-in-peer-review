Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to adapt image classifiers into object detectors when only a subset of categories have bounding-box annotations. The approach frames the problem as domain adaptation from classification (source) to detection (target). Concretely: (1) pretrain an AlexNet-style CNN on ImageNet classification; (2) replace the final layer with K categories and fine-tune on image-level data; (3) fine-tune layers 1–7 and add a background class using detection data for m categories (set B), yielding a category-invariant transformation that benefits all classes, including those without boxes (set A); and (4) apply a category-specific adaptation for held-out categories by adding to each classification weight vector an average delta learned from its k nearest neighbors (in fc8 weight space) among the categories in B. Detection is performed by scoring region proposals with the adapted network and combining category and background scores. Experiments on ILSVRC2013 with 100/200 split show a relative +50% mAP improvement over a classification-only baseline; a 7.6K-category qualitative demo is reported.

The work is related to R-CNN (fine-tuning for detection with full boxes), weakly supervised detection via MIL, and domain adaptation (feature/model/joint methods, adaptive SVMs). The main novelty claimed is adapting to held-out categories with no detection labels by learning class-agnostic and class-specific transformations within a deep network.

Strengths
- Clear and timely problem: reducing dependence on bounding boxes to scale detection to many categories.
- Sensible decomposition into category-invariant (shared layers + background) and category-specific (fc8 deltas) adaptation; the former appears to drive most gains.
- Empirical improvement on held-out categories (+50% relative mAP) with a reasonable ablation, including a significance test.
- Practicality: no need for SVMs/bbox regression, and a fast variant with SPP and fast proposals is mentioned.
- The method is simple to implement in standard CNN toolchains and (in spirit) could be broadly useful.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Technical/methodological
- Ambiguous scoring: “scorei − scorebackground.” Are these logits (pre-softmax) or post-softmax probabilities? If softmax probabilities are used, subtracting background is ad hoc and not calibrated across categories; if logits, please state so. Calibration across K+1 classes is nontrivial without per-class SVMs; some evidence on the effect of this choice is needed.
- Loss definition during detection fine-tuning: The paper states layers 1–7 are fine-tuned and a background weight is learned; “except for the linear classifiers for set A.” Does the loss exclude set A outputs entirely (frozen) while training a K_B+1 softmax over B ∪ {bg}? Please specify precisely what heads are present in the loss and how gradients are handled for A.
- Nearest neighbors in fc8 weight space is a heuristic. No comparison to other similarity metrics (WordNet/semantic distance, visual co-occurrence, confusion statistics) or learned mappings is provided. The improvement from δA is very small (0.3 mAP), casting doubt on the necessity of this component as presented.
- Choice of k: Although the text mentions experimenting with various k (incl. |B|), only k=10 is highlighted. How was k selected without access to detection labels for A? If tuned on A, it leaks supervision; if tuned on B, report that protocol and results.
- Data protocol: Using val1 for training and val2 for testing follows R-CNN, but the paper also augments detection training with 1000 bbox images per category from the training set; ensure no image overlap with val2 and clarify whether classification data overlap with detection images. The classification set uses ≈1000 images per class from the ILSVRC training set; if these images contain annotated boxes for the 200 detection classes, please discuss potential leakage and steps taken to avoid it.
- Upper bound: The “last row” (full detection supervision for all 200 classes) is referenced as an upper bound, but the number is not visible here. For context, please report mAP for A and B under full detection training, and mAP for B under the proposed no-SVM/no-bbox-regressor setup.
- Error analysis: The Hoeim-style breakdown is informative, but the figures and quantitative distributions are not present in this version. Without numbers, claims about reduced localization/background errors are hard to verify.
- Bounding-box regression: While omitted for flexibility, showing results with bbox regression (trained only on B) would be informative, especially since localization is a key error mode. Even if it cannot be applied to A heads directly, regressing from shared features may still help.
- Generalization: All quantitative results are on ILSVRC. A small-scale test on PASCAL or cross-dataset would strengthen the domain adaptation claim.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typographical/formatting issues hinder readability: broken enumeration in the “three key insights” (e.g., “. Recognizing background … 2. Category invariant … fea- ture representations 3. …”), hyphenation artifacts (“mush oom”), stray figure text (“microphone (sim): ov=0.00 1−r=−3.00”), repeated “In In Proc.” in references, missing years and incomplete bibliographic entries, duplicate entries (e.g., “Tabula rasa…” appears twice), and inconsistent notation (A = {m, …, K} should be {m+1, …, K}; “m K” missing relation symbol, presumably m << K). Please carefully proofread.
- Important tables/figures (Table 1, Figs. 3–5) are referenced but not present here; ensure they are clear, labeled, and self-contained, and that the main text reports key numbers.
- Some claims are vague (“small performance loss” vs R-CNN); quantify.

Originality
- The category-invariant adaptation via fine-tuning with a background class is a reasonable extension of R-CNN; the class-specific nearest-neighbor delta is the main novel mechanism, though its empirical impact is small. Position the work more clearly against weakly supervised detection (MIL) and adaptation methods that transfer across categories, including any contemporaneous work on semi/weakly supervised detection with CNNs.

Significance
- The problem is important; any method that lifts classification models toward detection without boxes at scale is impactful. However, the absolute mAP remains low (≈16% on held-out categories), and the incremental benefit of the class-specific component is minor. The qualitative 7.6K demo is nice but lacks quantitative validation.

Pros
- Addresses an important scalability challenge for detection.
- Simple, practical method with measurable gains on a large benchmark.
- Clear separation of invariant vs. class-specific adaptation; ablation included.
- Released code/models (per text).

Cons
- Ambiguities in training loss and scoring; calibration concerns.
- Minimal gains from the claimed novel class-specific adaptation; limited analysis of alternatives.
- Missing/unclear experimental details and absent key numbers/figures in this version.
- Many presentation and reference issues; notation inconsistencies.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The idea is promising and practically relevant, but the paper in its current form has too many presentation issues and underspecified technical details, and the core novelty (class-specific adaptation) yields marginal gains without adequate analysis. With clearer exposition, stronger baselines/ablations (including scoring calibration and bbox regression), and polished writing, this could be much stronger.

Confidence: High (familiar with detection, domain adaptation, and R-CNN-era methods).
