Summary
The paper tackles a bold and timely question: can we learn the generic transformation needed to convert image classifiers into object detectors using bounding-box annotations from only a subset of categories, and then transfer that to thousands of categories that have only image-level labels? The proposed LSDA approach casts classification→detection as a domain adaptation problem. It (1) fine-tunes a classification CNN into a detection CNN on m categories with box labels to learn category-invariant changes and a background class, and (2) approximates category-specific adaptation for held-out categories by transferring the average residual change from nearest neighbor categories (in fc8 space). On ILSVRC13 detection with 100 held-out categories, LSDA achieves a 50% relative mAP gain over applying a classifier to region proposals (≈10.3%→15.9%, and ≈16.2% with the fc8 residual transfer). It presents ablations, error diagnosis, and a 7.6K-category detector demonstration with released models and code.

Relation to prior work
- R-CNN fine-tunes for detection but requires box labels for every category. LSDA differs by learning category-invariant detector features and transferring them to categories without boxes, plus a light category-specific residual averaging.
- MIL/weakly supervised detection can train from image-level labels but typically underperforms and had not been demonstrated at ILSVRC scale; LSDA is complementary and more scalable in practice.
- Classic domain adaptation (feature/model/joint) adapts across visual domains for the same task; LSDA adapts both domain and task (classification→detection) and modifies deep feature extraction via fine-tuning.
To my knowledge, this is the first method to adapt detectors to held-out categories without any detection annotations for them.

Strengths
- Novel and ambitious problem formulation with a simple, pragmatic solution that leverages deep fine-tuning.
- Clear decomposition into category-invariant adaptation (including learning a background class) and category-specific residual transfer.
- Strong empirical evidence that the category-invariant feature adaptation is the main driver; good ablations and error analysis showing reduced background and localization errors.
- Significant practical impact: 7.6K-category detector with released models/code and a faster SPP-based variant.
- Training/runtime efficiency (no SVMs/regression) and broad applicability to any ImageNet-trained classifier.

Weaknesses
- The category-specific transfer is heuristic and provides only a modest gain; nearest-neighbor averaging in fc8 space may be suboptimal. More analysis of why and when it helps would strengthen the story.
- Evaluation uses a single split (first 100 categories alphabetically) for the “with boxes” set. Multiple random splits would make the claims more robust and avoid accidental bias in the chosen subset.
- Absolute mAP on held-out categories remains relatively low; the “50% relative” framing can overstate the practical improvement.
- Missing head-to-head comparisons with representative weakly supervised/MIL baselines on a reasonable subset (even if not at full ImageNet scale) to contextualize gains.
- No exploration of varying the amount of detection supervision (e.g., m = 25/50/100) or sensitivity to k in nearest-neighbor transfer beyond a brief mention.
- Excludes bounding box regression and SVMs; while the choice eases transfer, an upper-bound with those components on held-out classes would help quantify the tradeoff.

Quality
Technically sound and thoughtfully evaluated with ablations, statistical significance on a key comparison, and error diagnostics. Some design choices are heuristic, and a few baselines are missing, but overall the empirical support is convincing for the claimed setting.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The three insights are helpful. Minor issues: details on nearest-neighbor k selection and sensitivity, and a clearer description of data splits could improve reproducibility.

Originality
High. The idea of learning a generic classifier→detector transformation and transferring it to categories without detection supervision is fresh and impactful at the time of writing. Distinct from R-CNN and classical domain adaptation.

Significance
High practical significance: enables scaling detectors to thousands of categories without exhaustive box annotations, with released resources. Even if absolute mAP is modest, the direction is important and likely to spur follow-up work.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance to the particular set of m categories with boxes? Results over multiple random splits would help.
2) How does mAP vary as m decreases (e.g., 25/50/100)? Where are diminishing returns?
3) What is the sensitivity to k and to the metric for nearest neighbors? Have you tried semantic/WordNet distances or learned metrics?
4) Can you report results with lightweight bbox regression applied to adapted features for held-out categories?
5) Could you compare against a representative weakly supervised baseline on a 100-class subset?

Pros
- Novel and bold problem setting with a simple, scalable solution.
- Strong ablations show clear gains from category-invariant adaptation and background modeling.
- Practical impact: large-scale detector (7.6K), code/models released, faster variant.
- Good error analysis and diagnostics.

Cons
- Category-specific transfer is heuristic and offers small gains.
- Single fixed split; limited analysis of supervision tradeoffs.
- Missing comparisons to WSD/MIL on a reasonable subset.
- Absolute performance remains modest.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant new direction—learning a generic classifier-to-detector transformation transferable to unseen categories—with compelling empirical evidence and high practical impact. Despite some missing baselines and heuristic elements, the novelty and potential are strong.

Confidence
High.
