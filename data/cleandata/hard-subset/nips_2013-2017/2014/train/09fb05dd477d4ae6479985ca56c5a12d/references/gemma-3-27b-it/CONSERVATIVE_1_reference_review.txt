## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper proposes LSDA, an algorithm for adapting image classifiers to object detectors, leveraging the disparity in annotation requirements between the two tasks. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the source domain (classification data) to the target domain (detection data) using a subset of categories with bounding box annotations and then transferring this transformation to categories lacking such annotations. The authors frame this as a domain adaptation problem and present experimental results on the ImageNet detection task, claiming a 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline of directly applying classifier weights.

While the problem addressed – scaling object detection to a large number of categories with limited annotation resources – is undoubtedly important, I find the presented work to be somewhat lacking in rigor and justification for a NIPS publication. The approach feels intuitively plausible, building on the established success of transfer learning with R-CNN, but the validation and comparison to existing methods are insufficient to confidently endorse its novelty and significance. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** The challenge of scaling object detection to a massive number of categories is a genuine bottleneck in the field. Reducing the reliance on expensive bounding box annotations is a worthwhile goal.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation behind the work and the advantages of leveraging readily available image-level labels.
*   **Reasonable Approach:** Casting the problem as domain adaptation and leveraging the pre-trained classification network is a sensible starting point. 
*   **Initial Results:** The reported 50% improvement over the baseline is encouraging, though the baseline itself is rather weak (see weaknesses).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Weak Baseline:** The baseline comparison – directly using raw classifier weights on region proposals – is extremely weak. It doesn’t reflect the current state of the art in detection, even before considering fine-tuning or more sophisticated techniques. A comparison to a fully-trained R-CNN model (fine-tuned on the 100 annotated categories) would provide a much more meaningful benchmark. The paper mentions R-CNN but fails to adequately compare against it.
*   **Limited Comparison to Related Work:** While the related work section mentions MIL and domain adaptation, the comparisons are superficial. The authors acknowledge that MIL-based methods haven't been evaluated on ImageNet, but offer no explanation for *why* a direct comparison isn’t present. The differences between LSDA and existing domain adaptation techniques, while discussed, don’t convincingly demonstrate the novelty of the proposed approach. Simply adapting both domain and task is not sufficient to establish novelty without demonstrable performance benefits.
*   **Heuristic Category Specific Adaptation:** The category-specific adaptation using nearest neighbors feels ad-hoc and lacks theoretical justification. The choice of k=10 is empirically determined without a clear explanation of why this value performs best.  A more systematic evaluation of different nearest neighbor strategies and ablation studies are necessary.
*   **Insufficient Ablation Study:** The ablation study presented (Figure 3) is useful but incomplete. While it demonstrates the importance of each component, it doesn’t provide enough insight into the interplay between them.
*   **Lack of Generalization Analysis:** The paper demonstrates improvement on the held-out categories within the ImageNet dataset. However, there’s no discussion of how well this approach generalizes to *completely* novel datasets or categories with significantly different characteristics.
*   **Overstated Claims:**  The conclusion claims the potential to enable detection of "tens of thousands of categories." This is a significant claim that isn’t fully supported by the presented experiments. The performance on the held-out 100 categories, while positive, doesn't guarantee scalability to 7404 categories as demonstrated in the large-scale detection section.
* **Missing Details:** The performance of the 'fast' version of the algorithm (reducing detection time to half a second) is mentioned, but details on the performance trade-offs (e.g., mAP reduction) are absent.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem.
*   Presents a plausible approach based on sound principles.
*   Shows a positive, albeit modest, improvement over a very weak baseline.

**Against:**

*   Lacks a rigorous comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
*   Contains heuristic components with limited justification.
*   Generalization ability is unproven.
*   Claims are overstated relative to the experimental evidence.
*   Baseline is too weak to support strong conclusions.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is currently not suitable for acceptance to NIPS. While the problem is relevant and the initial results are promising, the lack of a thorough evaluation against strong baselines, the heuristic nature of some components, and the overstated claims raise concerns about the robustness and significance of the work. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit to a future conference. Specifically, a detailed comparison to a fine-tuned R-CNN baseline, a more systematic investigation of the category-specific adaptation strategy, and an analysis of generalization performance are crucial for strengthening the paper.
