## Peer Review: LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

**Summary:**

This paper presents LSDA, a novel approach to large-scale object detection that leverages the abundance of image-level labeled data and addresses the scarcity of bounding box annotations. The core idea is to learn a transformation from image classification networks to object detectors, utilizing a domain adaptation framework. Specifically, LSDA fine-tunes a pre-trained classifier on categories *with* bounding box annotations and then transfers this learned transformation to adapt classifiers for categories *without* bounding box annotations. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over a baseline using raw classifier weights on the ImageNet detection task, showcasing the effectiveness of their approach in adapting classifiers to detectors.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper positions itself within the growing body of work attempting to alleviate the annotation bottleneck in object detection. It acknowledges and builds upon the work of Girshick et al. (R-CNN) in adapting classifiers for detection, but moves beyond requiring bounding box data for all categories. The authors also connect their work to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approaches, highlighting them as complementary. They correctly identify their contribution as being the first to adapt classifiers to detect held-out categories with no detection labels, differentiating it from other domain adaptation techniques for object detection which generally require labeled data in both source and target domains.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a crucial problem:** The scarcity of bounding box annotations for large-scale detection is a major hurdle, and this paper offers a compelling solution.
* **Novel approach:** The idea of learning a generalizable transformation from classification to detection networks is innovative and allows for leveraging abundant image-level data.
* **Strong empirical results:** The reported 50% relative mAP improvement over the baseline on ImageNet is significant and demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach. The ablation study provides valuable insights into the contribution of each component.
* **Detailed analysis:** The error analysis, including the breakdown of false positives, provides a deeper understanding of the algorithm’s strengths and weaknesses. The visualization of localization improvement is particularly insightful.
* **Publicly available resources:**  Making the model definitions, weights, and code publicly available is commendable and facilitates reproducibility and further research.
* **Clear writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making it relatively easy to follow the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited comparison to recent methods:** While the paper mentions MIL, it lacks a direct comparison to more recent and competitive methods in weakly-supervised detection, especially those utilizing MIL on large datasets.  A comparison against those methods on ImageNet would significantly strengthen the evaluation.
* **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The effect of the *k* parameter in the category-specific adaptation (Equation 1) is briefly mentioned, but a more detailed analysis of its impact on performance would be beneficial.  How does performance vary with different *k* values, and what strategies can be used to select an optimal *k*?
* **Complexity of the adaptation process:** While the authors address the computational complexity, a more thorough discussion of the trade-offs between adaptation time, performance, and the size of the labeled detection dataset would be useful.
* **Over-reliance on R-CNN pipeline:**  The current implementation relies on the R-CNN pipeline, which is known to be slow. While they mention a faster version with SPPNet, the core contribution, the adaptation strategy, could be more readily transferable to more efficient detection frameworks (e.g., YOLO, SSD).
* **Weakness of Classification-Only Baseline:** The chosen baseline, using only raw classifier weights on object proposals, seems quite weak. A more competitive baseline, such as fine-tuning the entire network on the classification data and then applying it to proposals, would provide a more realistic benchmark.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in object detection.
* The proposed method is novel and demonstrates strong empirical results on a challenging benchmark.
* The paper is well-written, clearly explains the method, and provides a detailed analysis.
* The authors have made their code and models publicly available, promoting reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of comparison to more recent, competitive weakly-supervised detection methods limits the assessment of the method’s relative performance.
* The sensitivity to hyperparameters and the complexity of the adaptation process could be further explored.
* The reliance on the R-CNN pipeline might hinder the broader applicability of the proposed approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and effective approach to large-scale object detection. The authors address a critical bottleneck in the field and demonstrate promising results on a challenging dataset. While some weaknesses remain, particularly the limited comparison to recent methods and the need for a more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Include a comparison to more recent and competitive weakly-supervised object detection methods** (especially MIL-based approaches) on the ImageNet detection task.
2.  **Provide a more detailed analysis of the *k* parameter** in the category-specific adaptation step and discuss strategies for optimal selection.
3. **Discuss the adaptability of the method to more modern and faster detectors beyond the R-CNN pipeline.**
4. **Consider a stronger baseline comparison.**
