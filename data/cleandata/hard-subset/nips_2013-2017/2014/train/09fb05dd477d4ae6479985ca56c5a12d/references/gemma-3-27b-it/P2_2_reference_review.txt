## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, where obtaining bounding box annotations is prohibitively expensive. The authors propose LSDA, a method that learns to adapt image classifiers (trained with readily available image-level labels) into object detectors. They frame this as a domain adaptation problem, leveraging labeled detection data for a subset of categories to transfer knowledge to categories *without* bounding box annotations.  The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification feature space to the detection feature space, and then apply this transformation to adapt classifiers into detectors.  The paper demonstrates a significant performance improvement (50% relative mAP gain) on the ImageNet detection task, specifically on the held-out categories without detection labels.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a crucial problem:** The scarcity of bounding box annotations is a major bottleneck for scaling object detection. This work directly addresses this limitation with a practical and potentially scalable approach.
* **Strong experimental results:** The 50% mAP improvement on the held-out categories is substantial and clearly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed adaptation technique. The ablation study provides valuable insights into the contribution of each component.
* **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and methodology. The illustrations (Figures 1-3) are helpful in understanding the LSDA pipeline.
* **Novelty:** The combination of domain adaptation techniques with the specific goal of adapting classifiers to detectors for zero-shot detection categories is a novel contribution. While domain adaptation and MIL-based methods exist (as acknowledged by the authors), adapting to *held-out* categories without any detection data is a significant step forward.
* **Publicly available code and models:**  Making the implementation publicly available fosters reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited comparison to existing methods:** While the authors acknowledge related work like MIL-based methods, a direct quantitative comparison on ImageNet (or a similar large-scale dataset) is missing. This makes it harder to assess the relative advantages of LSDA. The statement that MIL methods haven’t been evaluated on the ImageNet challenge is not necessarily true and should be checked.
* **Dependence on pre-trained classifiers:** The method heavily relies on the quality of the initial ImageNet-trained classifiers.  Performance is likely sensitive to the choice of pre-trained model and the quality of the classification data. This dependency isn’t thoroughly explored.
* **Category-specific adaptation relies on nearest neighbors:** The approach for adapting category-specific parameters in the absence of detection data (using nearest neighbors) seems somewhat ad-hoc. The justification for using Euclidean distance on normalized fc8 weights could be strengthened. The sensitivity of the performance to the value of *k* (number of nearest neighbors) should be examined more thoroughly.
* **Simplification of the detection pipeline:**  The paper simplifies the detection pipeline by skipping the SVM training and bounding box regression steps typically used in R-CNN-style detectors. While this speeds up training, it might sacrifice some performance. This trade-off should be discussed more explicitly.
* **Error analysis depth:** The error analysis (Figure 5) is useful, but could be more in-depth.  A more detailed breakdown of the remaining errors, including specific examples, would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and effective approach. The experimental results are compelling, and the paper is generally well-written and clear. While there are some limitations regarding comparisons to existing work and the reliance on pre-trained classifiers, these do not overshadow the significant contribution of the work. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Solves an important and challenging problem in object detection.
* Demonstrates significant performance improvements on a large-scale dataset.
* Presents a novel and well-motivated approach based on domain adaptation.
* Code and models are publicly available, promoting reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of direct quantitative comparison to state-of-the-art related methods.
* Reliance on the quality of pre-trained classifiers.
* The nearest neighbor approach for category-specific adaptation feels slightly heuristic.




**Rating:**

**Strong Accept (7/10)**.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the need for a more thorough comparison to related methods and a deeper discussion of the limitations of the approach.
