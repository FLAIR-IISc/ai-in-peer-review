## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper proposes LSDA, an algorithm to adapt image classifiers into object detectors, leveraging labeled classification data and a limited amount of labeled detection data. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain (image-level labels) to the detection domain (bounding boxes), and then apply this transformation to adapt classifiers for categories lacking bounding box annotations. The authors cast this as a domain adaptation problem and demonstrate improvements on the ImageNet detection task.

While the problem addressed—scaling detection to a large number of categories—is highly relevant and significant, and the core idea of leveraging readily available classification data is promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor and several non-trivial issues that detract from its overall quality. The presentation, while generally clear, lacks precision and thoroughness in several key areas, hindering full assessment and raising concerns about the robustness of the results.

**Strengths:**

* **Relevant Problem:** The challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories is a crucial one, and the authors correctly identify the bottleneck of bounding box annotation.
* **Novel Idea:** The concept of learning a domain adaptation transformation from classification to detection, and then applying it to zero-shot detection categories, is novel.
* **Positive Results:** The reported 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline is encouraging, suggesting the approach has merit.
* **Publicly Available Code:** The release of code and models is a commendable practice for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Rigorous Ablation:** The ablation study (Figure 3) feels incomplete. While it shows the contribution of each component, the impact of the *order* of application isn't addressed. Does category-invariant adaptation *need* to precede category-specific adaptation? Are there synergistic effects that could be better quantified?
* **Nearest Neighbor Selection:** The approach for approximating the category-specific transformation (δA) using nearest neighbors in the classification space is ad-hoc and lacks justification. Why Euclidean distance on normalized fc8 weights? Have other distance metrics or neighbor selection strategies been considered? The influence of 'k' (number of neighbors) seems crucial, but the analysis in Table 1 is sparse and doesn't establish a clear optimal value.
* **Implementation Details:** The description of the implementation lacks crucial detail. For example, the specific hyperparameters used for fine-tuning (learning rate, weight decay, momentum) are not provided. The paper mentions a reduction in training time, but doesn't quantify it relative to the full R-CNN pipeline.  The choice to skip SVM training and bounding box regression, while potentially beneficial for speed, is presented as a minor tradeoff but could significantly affect performance, and should be analyzed more thoroughly.
* **Error Analysis:** The error analysis (Figure 5) is superficial. While showing types of errors is helpful, simply stating “most errors were localization errors” isn't informative.  A quantitative breakdown of error types *across* categories (particularly the held-out categories) would be much more useful.
* **Figure Quality & Consistency:** Figure 2 is visually cluttered and difficult to parse.  The notation isn't consistent throughout the paper. For instance, ‘B’ and ‘A’ are initially defined as sets, then later seem to be used as indices. Figure 3 lacks clear axis labels.
* **Related Work Gaps:** The related work section mentions MIL-based methods but dismisses them due to a lack of ImageNet evaluation. This is a weak justification. A brief discussion of their potential advantages and disadvantages relative to LSDA would be valuable. Furthermore, the discussion on domain adaptation is broad but doesn’t delve into techniques specifically tailored to adapting *features* for detection.
* **Statistical Significance:** The claim of statistical significance (p=0.017) for the effect of k=10 seems disproportionately low given the scope of the experiment and without detailed reporting of statistical tests used.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:** Addresses an important problem, proposes a novel idea, demonstrates positive results.  The availability of code encourages further research.

**Con:** Numerous methodological details are missing or poorly justified. The ablation study is incomplete. The error analysis is superficial. The statistical significance claim is weakly supported. The overall presentation lacks the rigor expected of a NIPS submission.  The reliance on ad-hoc choices (e.g., nearest neighbor selection) raises concerns about the generalizability of the approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the promising concept and positive initial results, I recommend **rejection**. While the core idea of LSDA is interesting, the paper feels underdeveloped and lacks the thoroughness and rigor expected of a NIPS publication. The numerous minor flaws, coupled with the significant concerns regarding methodology and analysis, outweigh the potential significance of the contribution. A major revision, addressing the points outlined above with more detailed experiments, robust analysis, and clearer presentation, would be necessary for reconsideration.
