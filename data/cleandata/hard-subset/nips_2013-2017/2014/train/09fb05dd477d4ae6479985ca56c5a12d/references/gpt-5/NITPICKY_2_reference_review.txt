Summary
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to adapt image classifiers into object detectors when bounding box annotations are available for only a subset of categories. The approach casts classification-to-detection transfer as domain adaptation: (i) fine-tune a pre-trained CNN on detection data for m categories to learn a category-invariant “detection” representation and a background class; (ii) adapt category-specific output weights for held-out categories by adding an average delta computed from nearest neighbor categories (by L2 distance on L2-normalized fc8 classification weights); (iii) use region proposals at test time and score a category by subtracting a background score. On ILSVRC13 (split), LSDA reports a 50% relative mAP improvement over a classification-only baseline on the 100 held-out categories (≈10.31→15.85% mAP, 16.15% with k=10 NN adaptation), with error analysis highlighting reduced localization and background confusions. The authors also present a 7.6K-class detector demo and a faster SPP-based variant.

Strengths
- Addresses a timely, important problem: scaling detection to many categories with minimal box supervision.
- Clear high-level idea: model background and adapt feature representation for detection, then approximate per-class adaptation for held-out categories via NN deltas.
- Sensible empirical protocol with held-out classes; ablations indicate the category-invariant adaptation (feature/background) is the main driver.
- Error analysis suggests meaningful reduction in localization and background mistakes; aligns with intuition on classification vs detection differences.
- Practicality: reduced training time relative to full R-CNN pipeline; demonstration on 7.6K classes.

Weaknesses and concerns
Major technical/experimental clarity issues
- Ambiguity about scores: you “produce a softmax detector” yet compute score_i − score_background. Is this subtraction applied to logits (pre-softmax) or probabilities? If logits, specify; if probabilities, the subtraction is hard to justify. Calibrations across classes without SVMs are not discussed.
- Fine-tuning scope: you say “fine-tuning layers 1–7” and “produces a softmax detector … including a background class,” but also state “all network weights (except for the linear classifiers for set A).” Please clearly specify which parameters are updated for A vs B during detection fine-tuning (conv1–5, fc6–7, fc8 for B and background, frozen fc8 for A).
- Nearest-neighbor adaptation: choice of distance (L2 on normalized fc8 weights) and k lack justification and sensitivity analysis. Why not semantic (WordNet), visual (feature) similarity, or a learned metric? Weighted averaging by similarity? Does k=|B| help/hurt? Only a single k=10 result is reported; the table/figures with k sweeps are not shown.
- Data splits and potential leakage: you use ILSVRC13 train as classification data (200k images) and also augment detection for the 100 B categories with 1000 bbox images per category from the same train set, in addition to val1. Please clarify overlap between classification and detection training, and argue why this does not confound the domain adaptation claim (source vs target domains are then partially the same images).
- Evaluation details: region proposal method/settings (# proposals), NMS strategy for evaluation (per-class vs cross-class), image scaling, and test-time details are missing. Speed claims (5.5 hours vs 3 days; 0.5s/image) lack hardware specs and exact configuration, limiting reproducibility.
- Statistical testing: the p=0.017 paired t-test is reported without specifying the sample size (100 AP values?), whether the distributional assumptions hold, and confidence intervals. The absolute mAP gain (+0.3) is small; provide per-class variance or bootstrapping.

Methodological limitations
- The absolute performance remains low on held-out categories (~16% mAP), and no comparison is provided to alternative weakly supervised/transfer baselines that could be adapted to ImageNet scale (e.g., MIL with deep features, or class-agnostic RPN-style transfer if applicable). Even if those works were not run at this scale, a discussion or scaled-down comparison would help.
- The per-class adaptation strategy is extremely simple; alternatives (e.g., learning a mapping from classification to detection weights conditioned on features/semantics) are not explored.
- The evaluation focuses on held-out categories. Please also report performance on categories in B after adaptation (to assess whether the shared adaptation degrades or improves supervised classes), and an oracle upper bound is shown but not fully contextualized.

Clarity, formatting, and anonymity
- Numerous typos and formatting issues reduce clarity: broken words (fea- ture), missing symbols (“m K” presumably m << K), inconsistent set definitions (A = {m, ..., K} should be {m+1, …, K}), dangling punctuation, and stray artifacts (e.g., “microphone (sim): ov=0.00 1−r=−3.00”). Equation (1) lacks clear definition of δ_B. Figures referenced but not shown in the submission text impede understanding of ablations and error analysis.
- References are severely incomplete/inconsistent: missing years/venues/pages, duplicates (e.g., “Tabula rasa” appears twice), inconsistent citation styles, and mismatch with numeric citations. This is below NIPS standards.
- Serious double-blind violation: the paper includes a project URL (lsda.berkeleyvision.org) and statement “we have released…” as well as acknowledgments; these reveal authorship/affiliation and are not allowed in a blind submission.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an impactful problem with a pragmatic, simple method.
- Demonstrates non-trivial gains on a challenging benchmark with reasonable analysis.
- Potentially useful to the community for scaling detection.

Cons for acceptance
- Double-blind violation and poor reference/formatting quality.
- Insufficient methodological clarity and ablation detail (scores, layers, k sensitivity, data splits).
- Limited absolute gains and missing comparisons; reproducibility gaps.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix anonymity and references; remove identifying URLs/acknowledgments.
- Clarify scoring (logits vs probabilities), training/fine-tuning details per layer, proposal pipeline, and evaluation/NMS settings; provide hardware and run-time settings.
- Provide detailed k-sensitivity, alternative similarity metrics, and a weighted NN variant; include per-class adaptation analysis.
- Report performance on both held-out and supervised categories; add comparisons to strong weakly supervised baselines where feasible.
- Provide per-class APs and confidence intervals; strengthen statistical analysis.

Recommendation
Score: 4 (reject)
Confidence: 0.8
Rationale: While the idea is promising and the empirical trend is encouraging, the submission falls short on clarity, rigor, and formatting, and violates anonymity. These issues overshadow the contribution in its current form.
