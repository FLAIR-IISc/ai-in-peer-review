Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles the long-standing bottleneck in detection: the need for bounding box annotations. It proposes LSDA, an approach to adapt image classifiers into object detectors when bounding boxes are available only for a subset of categories. The method casts classification-to-detection as domain adaptation. It learns (1) a category-invariant transformation by fine-tuning layers 1–7 on the detection task for the categories with boxes (including a learned background class), and (2) a simple category-specific adaptation by transferring the average parameter deltas (δ) from nearest neighbor categories (in fc8 space) that do have detection labels. At test time, detection scores are obtained directly from the softmax outputs by subtracting the background logit.

LSDA is well situated relative to R-CNN (which requires boxes for all categories) and to domain adaptation methods that assume labeled target data per class. It’s complementary to weakly supervised/MIL detectors, but aims at a different setting: transferring across tasks (classification→detection) with only partial box supervision. Experiments on ILSVRC13 simulate 100 seen-with-boxes vs 100 held-out categories, showing a +50% relative mAP gain over a classification-only baseline (≈10.31%→≈15.85%, and to 16.15% with δ transfer), and a qualitative 7.6K-class detector demo.

Strengths:
- Novel problem framing: learning a general, category-invariant transformation from classification to detection and transferring it to unseen categories. This is a bold step toward large-scale detection without exhaustive boxes.
- Simple, effective mechanism: fine-tune shared layers with a background class to capture task shift; lightweight category-specific transfer via nearest-neighbor averaging in classifier parameter space.
- Clear empirical signal: substantial relative improvement on held-out classes; ablation supports the claim that background modeling and shared-feature adaptation are the dominant factors.
- Scalability demonstrated: a 7.6K-class detector and a faster SPP+fast proposals variant suggest practical viability.
- Reproducibility: built on Caffe, models/code released.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Absolute performance on held-out categories remains modest (≈16% mAP) and far from the “oracle” all-classes-with-boxes upper bound; while expected, it limits immediate utility.
- The category-specific transfer is quite heuristic: L2 nearest neighbors in normalized fc8 space and averaging δ. It’s plausible but under-theorized; sensitivity to the metric, k, and class semantics could be explored more deeply.
- Comparisons: No direct comparison to strong weakly supervised detectors on the same split, nor to other adaptation strategies (e.g., attribute/semantic/graph propagation), beyond the ablation and an oracle bound. Even if not apples-to-apples, a baseline like training WSD on held-out classes would contextualize gains.
- Calibration and NMS: scorei − scorebackground is a simple calibration; cross-category NMS for 7.6K classes is heuristic. Some analysis of calibration across categories and the impact of not using per-class SVMs/box regression would be useful (they mention a small loss but don’t quantify on held-out).
- Evaluation protocol details: Using val1 for detection training and val2 for evaluation is standard, but it would help to clarify if any category imbalance or leakage in classification data could bias results.

Quality:
- Method is technically sound and well supported by experiments and ablations. The error analysis (localization vs background vs other) is informative and aligns with the claimed benefits of background modeling and localization capability. Statistical significance is reported for one comparison. Some aspects (δ transfer design, calibration) could be further justified or analyzed.

Clarity:
- Generally clear and well organized. The three insights and training stages are easy to follow. A bit more detail on the exact loss, optimization settings for fine-tuning, and sensitivity to k would improve reproducibility further, but given released code/models, this is acceptable.

Originality:
- High. Adapting classification networks into detectors for unseen categories via a learned, category-invariant transformation is a fresh idea at the time, distinct from R-CNN fine-tuning and from standard domain adaptation (which assumes per-class target labels). The δ transfer is simple but novel in this context.

Significance:
- High. The work opens a promising direction for scaling detection to thousands of categories without exhaustive box annotation. Even if absolute mAP is modest, the relative improvement and the scalability demonstration suggest broad impact and follow-on work (e.g., combining with MIL, better δ transfer, or meta-learning).

Pros (for acceptance):
- Bold, timely idea addressing a critical scalability bottleneck.
- Solid empirical gains on a realistic split; thorough ablation and error analysis.
- Simple, general method; practical speedups and a 7.6K-class demo.
- Code/models released.

Cons (against acceptance):
- Absolute performance gap to fully supervised detectors remains large.
- Category-specific transfer is heuristic; limited analysis of alternatives/semantics.
- Missing direct comparisons to contemporary weakly supervised detectors on the same setting.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Explore richer transfer for δA: graph propagation over WordNet/attributes, learned similarity metrics, or meta-learned deltas conditioned on classifier parameters.
- Report sensitivity to k, and try alternative similarity spaces (fc7 features, confusion matrices, semantic embeddings).
- Add a weakly supervised baseline on the held-out classes to contextualize LSDA’s gains.
- Quantify the calibration choice (scorei − scorebg) and analyze cross-class calibration and NMS at scale.
- Consider transferring bounding box regressors from B to A (e.g., class-agnostic regressor) to further reduce localization error.

Recommendation: Accept
This paper introduces a new and important direction with clear empirical benefits and strong potential for impact. Despite some heuristic components and incomplete comparisons, the novelty and ambition outweigh the limitations.

Confidence: High
