Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes LSDA, a method to convert image classifiers into object detectors when bounding-box annotations are available for only a subset of categories. The core idea is to treat the shift from classification to detection as a domain adaptation problem and to learn a transformation on a subset of “base” categories with detection labels that can be transferred to “held-out” categories that have only image-level labels. The method comprises: (1) category-invariant adaptation by fine-tuning shared layers (conv/fc1–fc7) for detection and learning an explicit background class; and (2) category-specific adaptation by estimating per-class output-layer changes for held-out classes via averaging the fine-tuning deltas of k nearest base classes in the classification weight space. At test time, proposals are scored by the network, and prediction uses a background-calibrated score.

The approach builds on CNN-based classification (Krizhevsky et al.) and region-based detection (R-CNN), while differing from R-CNN in not requiring detection labels for all classes. It is complementary to weakly supervised detection methods (MIL-based) that learn directly from image-level labels. Prior supervised domain adaptation for detection typically assumes labeled target data for all categories; LSDA is novel in adapting to categories with no detection labels.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation and practical impact: reduces the annotation burden for large-scale detection by leveraging abundant classification data plus limited detection labels.
- Simple, scalable method with strong empirical evidence: a 50% relative mAP gain on held-out classes (from 10.31% to 16.15% mAP) on ILSVRC13 val2, with ablations that isolate the contributions of background modeling, shared-layer fine-tuning, and output-layer adaptation.
- Insightful decomposition: convincingly shows that learning a background class and category-invariant feature adaptation are key to transfer from classification to detection (reducing background confusion and localization errors).
- Large-scale demonstration: qualitative 7.6K-class detector and a faster SPP-based variant show feasibility at scale.
- Reproducibility: uses Caffe and claims to release models/code.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Limited baselines and comparisons: While R-CNN is discussed, the paper lacks comparisons to alternative transfer strategies under the same supervision budget, such as: (a) using only the category-invariant fine-tuned network with background but no category-specific delta; (b) class-agnostic bounding-box regression; (c) alternative category-specific transfer functions (linear regression from Wc→Wd, low-rank sharing, or semantic/word-embedding neighbors). Some are partially addressed in ablations, but broader comparisons would strengthen claims.
- Category-specific adaptation heuristic: Averaging deltas from k nearest classes in fc8 weight space is intuitive but ad hoc; the choice of metric and k lacks deeper justification beyond a small k-sweep. It would be helpful to explore semantic similarity (WordNet distance), visual similarity in intermediate feature space, or learned mapping functions.
- Calibration details: The final score uses scorei − scorebackground. If scores are softmax probabilities, subtracting probabilities is not a principled calibration; if logits are used, clarify. Alternative calibrations (e.g., odds/logit differences, temperature scaling, or a shared calibration layer) could be evaluated.
- Quantitative scope: The 7.6K-class result is qualitative only. While understandable, even small-scale quantitative proxies (e.g., subset with boxes or human verification) would bolster the large-scale claim.
- Sensitivity to supervision size m: The paper uses m=100 of 200 classes; performance as a function of m (e.g., 25/50/100/150) would illuminate how much detection supervision is needed.
- Architecture choices: The method forgoes SVMs and bbox regression for simplicity; authors claim a 1–2 mAP loss but do not quantify on the same split, and class-agnostic bbox regression could help held-out categories without requiring per-class boxes.

Quality:
The empirical analysis on ILSVRC13 is solid and includes ablations, error breakdowns (Loc/BG/Other), and a significance test for the k-NN delta. Methodology and splits are described, though more details on scoring calibration and sensitivity to k would help. The approach is technically sound; however, the category-specific adaptation remains heuristic without theoretical backing.

Clarity:
Overall clearly written and well organized. The A/B notation and training pipeline are easy to follow. Suggestions:
- Clarify whether scorei − scorebackground uses logits or probabilities.
- Provide more explicit numbers for the effect of each adaptation component and for the claimed 1–2 mAP gap to R-CNN with SVMs/bbox regression on the same split.
- Minor typos/formatting in the provided version.

Originality:
The framing of classifier-to-detector transfer as domain adaptation coupled with explicit background modeling and transferable shared-layer fine-tuning is novel for adapting to unseen categories (no boxes). The output-layer delta averaging is a simple but new instantiation in this context. Related work is appropriately covered.

Significance:
High practical significance: enables scaling detection beyond the limited set of categories with bounding boxes. The relative mAP gains and qualitative large-scale demo indicate usefulness. The work is likely to inspire follow-ups on learning better class-specific transfer functions and integrating weak supervision.

Questions for authors / suggestions:
- How sensitive is performance to k and to the neighbor metric? Have you tried semantic embeddings (e.g., WordNet) or fc7-based similarities?
- Can you report mAP vs number of supervised detection classes m?
- What exactly is subtracted for background (logits vs probabilities), and have you tried alternative calibration schemes?
- Would a class-agnostic bbox regressor trained on B help A? Even if modest, it could reduce localization errors further.
- Any quantitative results for a subset of the 7.6K classes with boxes to validate the large-scale transfer?
- How does LSDA compare to training a class-agnostic objectness detector plus classification head?

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important and under-explored problem with clear motivation.
- Method is simple, computationally practical, and shows convincing gains on a strong benchmark.
- Releases large-scale models/code; likely to be used by the community.

Cons against acceptance:
- Category-specific adaptation is heuristic; limited exploration of alternatives and limited theoretical grounding.
- Missing some key baselines and sensitivity analyses (k, m, calibration, bbox regression).
- Large-scale results are qualitative.

Overall recommendation:
Lean accept. The paper makes a timely, practical contribution with clear empirical benefits and opens a promising direction for scaling detection with limited bounding-box supervision. Strengthening the evaluation along the suggested lines would further solidify the contribution. Confidence: high.
