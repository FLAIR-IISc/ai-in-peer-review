## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and practical approach to scaling up object detection to a large number of categories by leveraging readily available image-level classification data. The core idea of adapting a classifier to a detector via domain adaptation is sound, and the experimental results on ImageNet demonstrate a significant improvement over a strong baseline. While the method isn't a complete solution to the large-scale detection problem, it represents a valuable step forward and addresses a critical bottleneck – the scarcity of bounding box annotations. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the challenge of training detectors for a massive number of categories given the annotation bottleneck of bounding boxes. It proposes LSDA, a method to transform an image classifier into an object detector. This is framed as a domain adaptation problem: adapting from the source domain of image classification data to the target domain of object detection. The authors leverage pre-trained CNNs and fine-tune them using a limited amount of bounding box data, transferring this learning to categories without bounding box annotations. 

This work builds on the success of R-CNN [1] in utilizing pre-trained classifiers for detection. However, unlike R-CNN which requires bounding boxes for all categories, LSDA intelligently transfers knowledge from labeled categories to unlabeled ones. It also relates to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) [6, 7], acknowledging it as a complementary approach. The paper correctly positions itself within the broader context of domain adaptation [12-16], clarifying how its approach differs by adapting both the visual domain *and* the task (classification to detection), and crucially, by adapting to *held-out* categories with no detection data.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical problem:** The scarcity of bounding box annotations is a major impediment to large-scale object detection. LSDA directly addresses this issue.
* **Strong experimental results:** The 50% relative mAP improvement over the baseline is significant, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed adaptation strategy. The ablation study clearly highlights the contribution of each component.
* **Practical approach:** The method leverages existing pre-trained models and requires significantly less bounding box data than traditional approaches.
* **Clear formulation as domain adaptation:** Framing the problem as domain adaptation provides a solid theoretical foundation and allows for the application of existing techniques.
* **Insightful error analysis:** The analysis of false positives, particularly highlighting the reduction in localization and background confusion errors, provides valuable insights into the algorithm’s behavior.
* **Publicly available resources:** Releasing the model and code is a major plus, fostering reproducibility and further research.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Dependence on Nearest Neighbor Selection:** The method of adapting category-specific parameters for unseen classes relies on finding nearest neighbors in the classification space. This introduces a degree of arbitrariness and could be sensitive to the choice of distance metric and the number of neighbors (k). The impact of different 'k' values should be investigated more thoroughly.
* **Limited discussion of failure cases:** While the error analysis is helpful, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of *why* the model fails in certain scenarios (beyond “confusion with similar categories”).  A qualitative examination of misclassifications could provide valuable insights.
* **Comparison to state-of-the-art:** While comparisons to a baseline are provided, a more direct comparison to recent MIL-based approaches, even without a direct apples-to-apples comparison on ImageNet, would strengthen the paper. A discussion of the potential synergies between LSDA and MIL would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention optimization for speed, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost (training and inference time) would be valuable, especially considering the scale of the 7604-category detector.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 3.2:** The rationale behind averaging the changes in the output layer weights (δB) from nearest neighbors is reasonable. However, a more principled approach (e.g., weighted averaging based on similarity) might be explored.
* **Section 4.3:** The examples of false positives (Figure 5a) are insightful. Could you provide additional examples showcasing different types of errors?
* **Table 1:**  The "Oracle" performance provides a good upper bound. How close is the LSDA performance to the Oracle performance for the 100 categories *with* bounding box data?
* **Regarding the 7604-category detector:** What is the memory footprint of such a large model?  How does performance degrade as the number of categories increases?




**5. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a significant practical problem in object detection.
* Demonstrates a substantial performance improvement over a strong baseline.
* Presents a clear and well-motivated approach.
* Includes a thorough ablation study.
* Provides publicly available code and models.

**Con:**

* Relies on potentially sensitive nearest neighbor selection.
* Limited comparative analysis to related work (particularly MIL).
* Could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of failure cases and computational cost.

**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest:

* Conducting a more thorough investigation of the impact of the nearest neighbor selection (different 'k' values, distance metrics).
* Adding a discussion of the limitations of the approach and potential future directions (e.g., combining with MIL).
* Providing more detailed analysis of failure cases and computational costs.
* Expanding the related work discussion to include a more comprehensive comparison to MIL-based approaches.



I believe that addressing these minor points will further strengthen this already valuable contribution to the field of large-scale object detection.
