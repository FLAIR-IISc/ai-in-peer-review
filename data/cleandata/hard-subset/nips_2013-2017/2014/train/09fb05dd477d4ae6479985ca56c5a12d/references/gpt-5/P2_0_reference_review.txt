Summary
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to adapt image classifiers into object detectors at scale when bounding box annotations are available only for a small subset of categories. The core idea is to treat classification-to-detection as a domain/task adaptation problem and learn (i) a category-invariant transformation by fine-tuning shared CNN layers on detection data for m categories (including learning a background class), and (ii) a lightweight category-specific adaptation for held-out classes by transferring the average “delta” in the output layer from nearest annotated neighbors (based on fc8 weight similarity). Experiments on ILSVRC13 detection simulate access to detection boxes for 100 categories and only image-level labels for the other 100. On the held-out 100 categories, LSDA improves mAP from 10.31% (classification-only baseline applied to region proposals) to 16.15% (+50% relative), with most of the gain coming from the category-invariant fine-tuning and background modeling; the category-specific transfer adds a smaller but statistically significant improvement. The authors also demonstrate a 7.6K-category detector qualitatively and release code/models.

Relation to prior work
This builds on R-CNN by reusing region proposals and CNN fine-tuning, but differs by requiring detection boxes for only a subset of classes and explicitly modeling a transferable adaptation. It complements weakly supervised MIL approaches that learn detectors from image-level labels without boxes; unlike those, LSDA leverages some detection supervision to improve transfer to many classes. Compared to classic domain adaptation (feature transforms, adaptive SVMs), LSDA adapts both the representation (layers 1–7) and the task (background modeling), and uniquely targets held-out categories with no detection data.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: scaling detection to thousands of categories without exhaustive bounding box annotation.
- Simple, principled decomposition: category-invariant fine-tuning plus a small category-specific adjustment.
- Empirical support: ablation shows background modeling and shared-layer fine-tuning are key; nearest-neighbor delta transfer yields additional measurable gain (p=0.017).
- Sensible evaluation protocol on ILSVRC13 with held-out categories; error analysis indicates reductions in localization and background confusions.
- Practical impact: training time reduction (no SVMs or bbox regression) and release of a large-scale model and code.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Absolute performance remains modest (≈16% mAP on held-out categories) and far from the upper bound with full detection supervision; while expected, it limits immediate utility for high-precision applications.
- The category-specific adaptation is heuristic: nearest neighbors are defined via L2 distance of normalized fc8 weights. Alternatives (semantic distances, feature-space similarity, confusion-based neighbors) are not explored; sensitivity analysis beyond k is limited.
- Baseline comparisons could be stronger. For fairness, one would like to see: (a) features fine-tuned for detection using only the m categories and applied unchanged to the held-out categories without the background class subtraction; (b) a MIL baseline on the same 200-class setup; (c) R-CNN-style SVMs/bbox regression trained only for B but features reused for A, to isolate the contribution of end-to-end softmax vs post-hoc classifiers.
- Score computation uses score_i − score_background; calibration/normalization choices (e.g., logits vs probabilities) and their effect on mAP are not discussed.
- The 7.6K-category demonstration is qualitative; quantitative results beyond the 200 ILSVRC classes are understandably lacking but make it hard to assess generality at true scale.
- The split of categories (alphabetical) may inadvertently bias the “neighbor” pool; stratified splits based on semantic similarity would better stress-test transfer.

Quality
Technically sound overall and carefully executed. The adaptation mechanism for shared layers and background is well justified experimentally. The category-specific transfer is simple and effective, albeit heuristic with limited theoretical grounding. Ablations are informative; statistical testing is appreciated. Some missing baselines and sensitivity analyses reduce completeness.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The training procedure, data splits, and implementation details are described clearly enough to reproduce, especially given released code/models. Minor typographical issues aside, the narrative is easy to follow. Figures (referred to) appear to support claims, especially error analysis.

Originality
The framing of classification-to-detection as a transfer that is predominantly category-invariant, plus a small category-specific correction inferred from related classes without boxes, is novel at the time relative to R-CNN and classic domain adaptation. The introduction of an explicit background class as a key to task adaptation is also a valuable insight in this context.

Significance
Addresses an important bottleneck—bounding box annotation at scale. While absolute gains are modest, the relative improvement without any boxes for half the classes is meaningful and likely to spur follow-on work. The approach is simple enough to be adopted broadly and to combine with other advances (e.g., MIL for pseudo-boxes, better proposals), making it practically significant.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are results to the choice of distance metric and normalization for nearest-neighbor selection? Did you try semantic (WordNet) or joint metrics?
- Does using logits vs softmax probabilities for score_i − score_background change performance?
- Could you report a baseline that fine-tunes on B but omits the background class and/or subtractive scoring to isolate that component?
- Any quantitative results with SPP/fast proposals variant on the held-out split?

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen baselines and provide a sensitivity analysis on neighbor selection and k.
- Explore simple pseudo-box mining (MIL) for the held-out classes to complement LSDA.
- Calibrate scores across classes (e.g., temperature scaling) to potentially reduce Oth-type errors.
- Consider stratified category splits to evaluate robustness of transfer.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, impactful problem and practical method with code release.
- Solid empirical gains with insightful analysis showing why it works.
- Novel and useful decomposition of adaptation that others can build upon.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited absolute performance and some missing baselines/sensitivity studies.
- Category-specific transfer is heuristic with limited exploration of alternatives.
- Large-scale (7.6K) results are qualitative.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper presents a timely and practically valuable contribution with convincing evidence and clear insights, despite some missing baselines and the heuristic nature of the category-specific transfer. Potential impact on scaling detection is high. Confidence: high.
