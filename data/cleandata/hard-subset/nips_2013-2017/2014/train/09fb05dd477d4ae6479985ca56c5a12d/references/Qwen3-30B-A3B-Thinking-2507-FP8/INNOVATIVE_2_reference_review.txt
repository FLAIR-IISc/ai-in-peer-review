

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

This paper proposes a novel approach to object detection by transforming image classifiers into detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The authors frame the problem as domain adaptation, where the source domain consists of image-level classification data and the target domain consists of bounding box-labeled detection data. The key insight is that a general transformation from classification to detection can be learned from a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations and then applied to adapt classifiers for categories without any detection data.

The paper makes a compelling argument for the significance of the problem: collecting bounding box annotations for tens or hundreds of thousands of categories is impractical, while image-level annotations (e.g., from ImageNet) are readily available. The authors demonstrate that their LSDA approach can adapt any ImageNet-trained classifier into a detector with a 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline of using raw classifier weights on object proposal regions.

This work stands out as highly novel in the detection literature. While previous approaches like R-CNN [1] require bounding box data for all categories, and MIL methods [6,7] use weak labels but haven't been evaluated at scale, LSDA is the first to adapt to held-out categories with no detection data. The authors' three key insights—recognizing background is important, category invariant information can be transferred, and there are category-specific differences between classifiers and detectors—provide a clear conceptual framework for their approach.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with strong experimental validation on the ImageNet detection dataset. Their ablation study demonstrates that the most important component is adapting the feature representation (which reduces localization errors), while the least important is adapting the category-specific parameters. The 7,604 category detector they produce (using only bounding box data for the first 200 categories) is a compelling demonstration of the approach's scalability.

Strengths:
- Highly novel conceptual contribution that addresses a fundamental limitation in object detection
- Significant performance improvement (50% relative mAP) over strong baselines
- Clear framework for scaling detection to tens of thousands of categories
- Well-executed experiments on a large-scale dataset
- Practical demonstration of the method's capabilities

Weaknesses:
- The method still requires bounding box annotations for a subset of categories (100/200 in their experiments)
- The category-specific adaptation uses nearest neighbors, which may not generalize well to very dissimilar categories
- The paper doesn't fully address how the method would perform with more extreme category differences

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I believe these minor limitations are outweighed by the paper's significant conceptual contribution. The work represents a substantial shift in how we think about object detection, moving away from the paradigm of requiring bounding box annotations for every category toward a scalable approach that leverages the vast amount of available image-level annotation data.

The paper's significance is clear: it has the potential to enable detection for tens of thousands of categories, which is a critical step toward human-level detection capabilities. This is a major advance beyond the incremental improvements common in detection literature.

Compared to recent NIPS work, this paper stands out for its bold conceptual approach rather than technical refinements. It builds on but fundamentally extends R-CNN [1] and domain adaptation literature [12-16], making a clear contribution to the field.

The quality of the paper is strong, with well-supported claims and thorough experimental validation. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations and provide clear ablation studies.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of object detection and represents a novel direction that could have substantial impact. It successfully addresses a major practical limitation in scaling detection systems while maintaining strong performance. The conceptual simplicity of the approach, combined with its significant results, makes it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

Recommendation: Accept
