Summary
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to adapt image classification CNNs into object detectors when only a subset of categories has bounding-box annotations. The approach has two parts: (1) category-invariant adaptation by fine-tuning layers 1–7 and learning a background class using detection data for m categories (set B), and (2) category-specific adaptation for held-out categories (set A) by transferring the average change in the fc8 classifier weights from the nearest neighbors in B, where similarity is measured by L2 distance between normalized classification fc8 weights. At test time, scores are computed as class score minus background score. On ILSVRC13 detection with 200 classes, the authors simulate only 100 categories with detection labels, reporting a 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories (≈10.3% to ≈15.9–16.2 mAP). They provide an ablation study, an error analysis showing reduced localization/background confusions, and a qualitative demonstration of a 7.6K-class detector.

Relation to prior work
This builds on R-CNN, which fine-tunes a classifier for detection but needs bounding boxes for all categories. LSDA frames classification-to-detection as domain adaptation and transfers a learned “generic” detection transformation plus a heuristic category-specific delta. The paper references MIL-based weakly supervised detection and classic domain adaptation, but does not provide direct empirical comparisons to those on ILSVRC.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant and practically important setting: scaling detection when bounding boxes are scarce.
- Method is simple, computationally modest, and largely leverages established CNN fine-tuning practices.
- Empirical ablation supports the claim that background modeling and feature-level adaptation matter most.
- Error analysis is useful and consistent with expectations (reduced BG/Loc errors).
- Some statistical testing is provided; code/models are released.

Weaknesses
- Baselines are weak. The primary baseline is “classification-only applied to region proposals,” which is known to underperform. Missing comparisons to: (a) weakly supervised/MIL detectors using only image-level labels on held-out categories, (b) a simple transfer baseline that uses the detection-fine-tuned network (with background) without any fc8 adaptation for A (reported partially but not clearly isolated), (c) semantic transfer baselines (e.g., WordNet/attribute similarity), and (d) R-CNN with SVMs/bbox regression when feasible to demonstrate the performance gap.
- The category-specific transfer (nearest-neighbor delta averaging in fc8 space) is ad hoc with limited justification. No analysis of distance metric choices, k sensitivity, or what happens when A has no close neighbors in B. The gain from this component is reported to be the smallest; it is unclear whether it is worth the added complexity.
- Claims of “large-scale detection” (7.6K classes) are only qualitative. Without quantitative evaluation (even partial or proxy metrics), this does not substantiate scalability claims.
- Absolute performance on held-out categories remains low (~16% mAP), and the “50% relative mAP” figure risks being misleading. The small p-value reported is helpful but the practical impact remains modest.
- The experimental protocol is limited: only one split (alphabetical 100/100) is used; no sensitivity to m (how many detection-labeled categories are needed to achieve a given mAP); no cross-split variability or robustness study.
- Design choices lack ablations: e.g., justification of score_i − score_bg vs. softmax over K+1, effects of skipping SVMs/bbox regression on transferability, and whether treating A categories as “unknown” during detection fine-tuning harms them.
- Clarity and rigor could be improved: training details (negatives/background sampling, learning rates, iteration counts), precise composition of detection vs. classification batches during fine-tuning, and per-class results are largely absent.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive is performance to k in the nearest-neighbor averaging? What about other similarity measures (fc7 features, semantic/WordNet distance, metric learning)?
2. How does LSDA compare to a strong weakly supervised detector trained on A using MIL on proposals? Even a smaller-scale comparison would be informative.
3. Can you report results across multiple random B/A splits and as a function of m (e.g., m ∈ {25, 50, 100, 150})?
4. What is the performance if you only do category-invariant adaptation (fine-tune layers 1–7 + background) and leave fc8 unchanged for A? Please isolate this baseline clearly.
5. Any quantitative proxy for the 7.6K detector (e.g., on a subset with boxes, or pseudo-label validation)?
6. Why use score_i − score_bg instead of softmax? Did you evaluate calibration across classes?

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen baselines, especially weakly supervised detection on A and semantic-transfer variants. Include a robust “no fc8 adaptation” baseline.
- Provide sensitivity analyses (k, distance metric, m), multiple splits, and per-class breakdowns highlighting when transfer succeeds/fails (e.g., as a function of neighbor similarity).
- If possible, add a small-scale quantitative evaluation for the 7.6K model.
- Clarify training details to ensure reproducibility and justify the score computation choice.
- Consider simple alternatives to the delta transfer (e.g., linear regression from Wc to Wd trained on B, or a shared low-rank adapter) and compare.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound overall; however, several choices are heuristic with limited empirical justification. Missing strong baselines and robustness analyses limit the strength of the claims.
- Clarity: Generally readable, but some methodological and training details are underspecified. Figures/tables referenced but not shown here make it hard to fully assess.
- Originality: Incremental. The main novelty is the specific transfer mechanism for categories without boxes; category-invariant fine-tuning and background modeling are standard.
- Significance: Potentially useful in practice, but current evidence (absolute gains, breadth of baselines, and lack of quantitative large-scale validation) falls short of a strong impact claim.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem is important and the approach is pragmatic, but the empirical validation is not yet compelling enough against stronger baselines, and several design choices need clearer justification and robustness analysis.

Confidence
High.
