Summary and contributions
The paper addresses the practical challenge of scaling object detection to thousands of categories without requiring bounding box annotations for all classes. The core idea (LSDA) is to learn a generic transformation that turns an image classifier into a detector using detection data for a subset of categories, and then apply this transformation to held-out classes with only image-level labels. The approach has three components: (1) learn a background class and fine-tune layers 1–7 on detection data for the labeled subset (category-invariant adaptation), (2) keep classification weights for held-out classes but operate them on the adapted feature representation, and (3) perform a light category-specific adaptation by adding to the held-out class’s last-layer weights the average “delta” observed for its nearest neighbors among the labeled detection classes. Using an R-CNN-like pipeline, the method yields a reported 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out classes (roughly 10.3% to 15.9% mAP) on ILSVRC13 val2, with qualitative large-scale results for 7.6K categories.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned between weakly supervised detection (MIL) and supervised detector adaptation. Compared to MIL approaches that train detectors from image-level labels, LSDA leverages a small amount of bounding-box supervision on a subset to learn a transferable transformation across tasks (classification→detection). Compared to R-CNN/SPPnet that require box labels for all categories, LSDA uses detection labels for only a subset and transfers to held-out classes. It also connects to classic domain adaptation, but adapts across both visual domain and task, which prior methods generally do not. The paper appears to be the first to demonstrate adaptation to held-out detection categories at scale.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and practical significance: reducing per-category bounding box annotation requirements while enabling large-scale detection.
- Simple, coherent methodology with principled components: background modeling and category-invariant fine-tuning are well-motivated and empirically dominant.
- Solid empirical study on ILSVRC13 with a held-out/evaluation split, including ablations that disentangle background modeling, feature adaptation, and last-layer deltas.
- Error analysis reveals that the gain is largely from improved background rejection and localization, matching the stated motivation.
- Code/models released; the authors discuss speed-accuracy tradeoffs and provide a faster SPP-based variant.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modest absolute gains on held-out categories: while “+50% relative mAP” sounds large, the absolute held-out mAP remains low (~16%). The practical utility for many classes remains unclear without further improvements or additional weak/semi-supervised learning.
- Limited justification for the category-specific adaptation: averaging δ over nearest neighbors in fc8 space is heuristic. The choice of distance metric, nearest-neighbor cardinality k, and stability across categories is only lightly explored; negative transfer risks are not analyzed.
- Limited baselines: It would be useful to compare to a simple “background-only” adaptation baseline where only layers 1–7 are fine-tuned with a background class and fc8 for held-out classes stays unchanged (they partly do this, but deeper analysis per component/class would help); and to a naive calibration baseline (e.g., bias shift) without nearest-neighbor δ. Comparisons to weakly supervised MIL on the same data (even if not large-scale) would contextualize trade-offs.
- Evaluation scope: Quantitative results are only on the 100 held-out ILSVRC classes. The 7.6K detector is shown only qualitatively; without quantitative proxy metrics or human evaluation, it is hard to assess real scalability.
- Lack of analysis on how performance scales with the number of detection-labeled categories m: Do gains saturate quickly? How many labeled classes are needed to achieve most of the benefit?
- Architectural dependence and calibration: The decision to avoid SVMs and bbox regression is justified by flexibility and speed, but a hybrid evaluation (e.g., SVM/BBR for B, direct softmax for A) would help isolate if the method’s limitation is primarily model capacity or supervision scarcity.

Quality
Technically sound and carefully executed. The ablation shows where the gains come from; statistical testing is provided for the δA step. Some design choices (NN averaging) remain heuristic with limited theoretical or empirical justification beyond one heuristic distance and a single best k.

Clarity
Overall clear and well-organized. The three insights/components are well articulated. A few implementation details could be expanded (hyperparameters for fine-tuning, learning rates/schedules, sensitivity to proposal methods), but the release of code/models mitigates reproducibility concerns.

Originality
The central novelty is adapting across tasks (classification→detection) for held-out categories by learning category-invariant feature transformation and a light-weight category-specific adjustment from categories with boxes. This is a novel and timely contribution beyond standard domain adaptation (which keeps the task fixed) and beyond R-CNN style full per-class fine-tuning.

Significance
The work opens a practical path toward large-scale detection with limited box annotation, demonstrating measurable gains on a challenging benchmark and a proof-of-concept 7.6K-class detector. It is likely to be used and extended (e.g., combined with MIL, better meta-learning of the last-layer mapping, or open-vocabulary settings).

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the number of labeled detection categories m? Please include a curve vs. m.
- How robust is the nearest-neighbor δ transfer to the choice of feature space (fc7 vs. fc8) and metric? Any evidence of negative transfer on fine-grained classes?
- Did you try learning a regression from Wc to Wd on B (e.g., linear or shallow nonlinear mapping) instead of NN averaging? How does it compare?
- What is the effect of proposal quality (e.g., selective search vs. geodesic proposals) on held-out gains?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a stronger analysis of the category-specific transfer: explore alternative mappings (ridge regression, canonical correlation, or metric learning), different feature spaces, and per-class results to diagnose negative transfer.
- Add a scaling study with m and with k. Report variance across classes and group by semantic similarity or granularity.
- Include a hybrid detector baseline (SVM + bbox regression for B only) to separate architectural vs. supervision effects.
- Quantitatively evaluate a subset of the 7.6K categories via crowd-sourcing or cross-dataset transfer to substantiate scalability claims.

Pros
- Addresses an important bottleneck with a simple, scalable method.
- Clear empirical evidence that background modeling and feature adaptation matter.
- Good error analysis and public release of resources.

Cons
- Absolute mAP on held-out classes remains low.
- Category-specific adaptation is heuristic and under-analyzed.
- Evaluation limited to one benchmark and primarily to val splits; limited quantitative evidence for the 7.6K model.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper is well-motivated, technically solid, and introduces a useful and original idea with promising results, despite modest absolute performance and some heuristic components that could be further substantiated.
