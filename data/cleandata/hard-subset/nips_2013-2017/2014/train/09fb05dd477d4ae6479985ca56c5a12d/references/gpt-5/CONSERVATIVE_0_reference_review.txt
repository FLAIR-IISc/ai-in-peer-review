Summary
The paper tackles the problem of scaling object detection to many categories when bounding box annotations are available for only a subset. The proposed LSDA approach adapts a classification CNN into a detection CNN by (i) fine-tuning on the available detection categories to learn a background class and a “category-invariant” shift in the shared representation (layers 1–7), and (ii) estimating a “category-specific” shift for categories without boxes by averaging the fc8 deltas of nearest neighbor categories (in the classification weight space). On ILSVRC13 detection, with 100 categories held out from detection fine-tuning, LSDA improves mAP from ~10.3% (using classification network on proposals) to ~16.2% (+50% relative). The authors also demonstrate a 7,604-class detector by combining 200 detection-trained categories with 7,404 classification-only categories.

Relation to prior work is appropriately framed: R-CNN’s fine-tuning needs detection labels for all classes; LSDA uses detection labels for a subset and transfers to others. Prior domain adaptation and MIL work is cited; the contribution differs by addressing transfer to held-out categories with no detection labels.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant and practically important problem (reducing dependence on expensive bounding boxes).
- Clear decomposition into category-invariant (representation and background) and category-specific (last-layer) adaptations; ablations indicate that most gains come from the invariant part.
- Sensible, scalable method; simple to implement on top of standard CNN training.
- Empirical results show consistent improvements on the held-out categories; includes a significance test; error analysis indicates reductions in localization and background confusions.
- Public release of code/models and a large-scale (7.6K) demonstration increase potential impact.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: the main technical component is standard fine-tuning for detection on a subset of categories (very close to R-CNN), plus a simple nearest-neighbor averaging heuristic for fc8 deltas. The latter yields only a small incremental gain over the category-invariant adaptation.
- Evaluation is narrow: results are on a single split (first 100 alphabetical) of ILSVRC13; robustness across multiple random splits is missing. The 7.6K detector is shown qualitatively only.
- Baseline coverage is incomplete for a transfer/weak-supervision framing. Missing comparisons include:
  - Weakly supervised detectors (MIL/WSD) trained on held-out categories using only image-level labels (even on a smaller subset for feasibility).
  - Alternative transfer baselines such as calibrating classification scores with a learned background class only, parent/semantic sharing, or directly training one-vs-rest linear detectors on region features with mined positives.
  - R-CNN-style SVM and bbox regression trained on the detection categories and applied to held-out categories (e.g., class-agnostic bbox regression, or SVMs with background negatives only).
- Ad hoc choices are under-justified: using l2 distances in normalized fc8 space to define neighbors, the choice of k, and the sensitivity of results to these decisions are only lightly explored.
- Absolute performance remains low (16% mAP on held-out classes), and it is unclear how close LSDA gets to stronger, well-tuned weakly supervised alternatives on the same data.
- Some experimental details are sparse: training schedules/hyperparameters, hardware for the claimed 5.5h vs 3 days, proposal settings, and the precise data augmentation protocol.

Quality
Technically sound in the sense that the training and adaptation procedures are standard and correctly implemented. The ablation study is useful and supports the claim that category-invariant adaptation (feature shift + background) provides most of the benefit; the category-specific transfer adds a small but statistically significant improvement. However, the empirical validation is not sufficiently broad: there is only one held-out split and limited comparison to alternative approaches that also require no detection labels for the held-out classes. The 7.6K detector is not quantitatively evaluated.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The pipeline is described clearly, with high-level equations for the last-layer transfer. Some implementation details are missing (learning rates, number of fine-tuning iterations, proposal thresholds, NMS settings), and the neighbor-selection/sensitivity analysis could be elaborated. Minor typos are present but do not impede understanding.

Originality
Moderate. The core idea of fine-tuning for detection is established (R-CNN). Learning a background class and adapting shared layers using detection labels for a subset is a reasonable extension. The nearest-neighbor averaging in fc8 space for held-out classes is a simple heuristic; the novelty is limited and contributes a small fraction of the gains.

Significance
The problem is important; the approach is simple and practical; releasing large-scale models is valuable. However, the methodological contribution over strong baselines is modest, and the experimental evidence does not convincingly establish superiority over alternative weakly supervised or transfer approaches on the same setting. As presented, significance is constrained by limited evaluation and low absolute mAP.

Suggestions for improvement
- Evaluate on multiple random splits of held-out categories to assess robustness.
- Add baselines: (i) weakly supervised MIL on held-out classes; (ii) background-calibrated classification scores; (iii) semantic/attribute/WordNet-based parameter sharing; (iv) class-agnostic bbox regression applied to held-out classes; (v) SVMs trained with background negatives only on detection features.
- Provide sensitivity to k and to the neighbor distance metric; consider learned mappings from classification to detection weights.
- Quantify the effect of adding background alone vs full representation fine-tuning; report per-class gains and their correlation with neighbor similarity.
- Offer at least a small-scale quantitative evaluation for the 7.6K model (e.g., on a labeled subset).
- Include more training details to facilitate reproducibility.

Pros
- Addresses a meaningful, scalable detection problem.
- Simple, reproducible method with ablations and released code/models.
- Demonstrates consistent gains and error reductions.

Cons
- Limited novelty beyond established fine-tuning; heuristic category-specific transfer.
- Incomplete comparisons and a single held-out split.
- Modest absolute performance; qualitative-only large-scale results.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper tackles an important problem with a practical approach and shows some gains, but the novelty is limited and the empirical validation against competitive alternatives is insufficient for NeurIPS standards. Strengthening the experimental evaluation and broadening baselines could change this assessment.
