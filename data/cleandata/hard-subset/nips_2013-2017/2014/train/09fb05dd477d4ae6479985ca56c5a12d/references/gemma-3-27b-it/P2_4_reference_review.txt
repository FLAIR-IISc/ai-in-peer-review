## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

**Summary:**

This paper presents LSDA, a method for adapting image classifiers into object detectors, addressing the challenge of limited bounding box annotations for large-scale detection. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the image classification domain (with abundant image-level labels) to the object detection domain (requiring expensive bounding box annotations). The method fine-tunes a pre-trained CNN using available bounding box data for a subset of categories, and then transfers this learned transformation to adapt classifiers for categories *without* bounding box annotations. The authors demonstrate a significant performance improvement (+50% relative mAP) on the ImageNet detection task, particularly on categories without direct bounding box supervision.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of object detection and domain adaptation. It acknowledges the success of R-CNN and its reliance on bounding box annotations. It connects to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approaches for weak supervision but notes a lack of large-scale evaluation. The paper also references relevant domain adaptation techniques, highlighting its novelty in adapting *both* visual domains *and* tasks (classification to detection), and adapting to held-out categories with no detection data.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a critical problem:** The scarcity of bounding box annotations for large-scale object detection is a major bottleneck. LSDA directly tackles this issue with a practical and scalable approach.
*   **Strong experimental results:** The 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories is impressive and demonstrates the effectiveness of the adaptation technique.  The ablation study clearly highlights the contribution of each component.
*   **Clear and well-motivated approach:** The paper explains the intuition behind the three key insights (background recognition, category invariance, category specificity) and how they are incorporated into the algorithm.
*   **Comprehensive experiments:**  The authors conduct experiments on the challenging ImageNet detection task and provide a detailed analysis of error types, showing improved localization and reduced background confusion. The release of the 7.6k category detector and code is a valuable contribution.
*   **Good writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Dependence on Pre-trained Classifier:** The performance is heavily reliant on the quality of the pre-trained image classifier. While this is a common practice, it’s a potential limitation that isn’t fully explored. A sensitivity analysis on different pre-trained classifiers would be beneficial.
*   **Nearest Neighbor Averaging:** The category-specific adaptation using nearest neighbor averaging seems somewhat ad-hoc.  While it works, the rationale for choosing k=10 isn't fully explained.  Exploring alternative approaches for transferring category-specific knowledge could be valuable.
*   **Computational Cost:** The paper acknowledges a trade-off between performance and speed. While the faster version using spatial pyramid pooling is mentioned, a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the method would be useful.
*   **Limited Comparison to MIL Methods:** While the authors mention MIL methods, a more direct comparison, even if preliminary, would strengthen the paper. Evaluating against a recent, strong MIL-based approach would be valuable.

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound and well-supported by experimental results. The ablation study and error analysis provide convincing evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.  It is a complete piece of work, presenting a clear algorithm, thorough experiments, and a thoughtful discussion of its limitations.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized. The authors do a good job of explaining the technical details and motivating their design choices.  However, the explanation of the nearest neighbor averaging could be more detailed.

**Originality:**

The combination of domain adaptation and transfer learning to address the large-scale detection problem with limited annotations is novel. The approach of adapting classifiers to detectors, especially the ability to generalize to unseen categories, is a significant contribution.

**Significance:**

The results are important and address a key challenge in scaling up object detection systems. The ability to leverage readily available image-level annotations to improve detection performance is a valuable contribution. The released 7.6k category detector is a significant resource for the research community. This work is likely to be used by other researchers and practitioners looking to build large-scale detection systems.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and practical problem in object detection.
*   Demonstrates a substantial performance improvement on a challenging dataset.
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated approach.
*   Provides a valuable resource to the research community (code and model).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Reliance on the quality of the pre-trained classifier.
*   Ad-hoc nature of the nearest neighbor averaging approach.
*   Limited comparison to recent MIL methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong acceptance**. The authors present a novel and effective approach to large-scale object detection with limited annotations. The experimental results are compelling, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall contribution.  This paper has the potential to significantly impact the field of object detection.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
