

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

This paper proposes a method to adapt image classifiers into object detectors using domain adaptation techniques. The key idea is to use a small set of categories with bounding box annotations to learn a transformation from classification to detection, then apply this transformation to categories without bounding box data. The authors claim a 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline of using raw classifier weights on object proposals, and demonstrate a 7,604 category detector.

## Strengths

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to method to evaluation. The experimental setup is detailed, and the authors provide sufficient implementation details to reproduce their results. The problem of scaling object detection to large numbers of categories is indeed important, and the paper makes a reasonable attempt to address this challenge.

## Weaknesses

Despite these strengths, the paper falls short on several key criteria for NIPS publication.

First, the experimental validation is insufficiently rigorous. The claimed "50% relative mAP improvement" is based on a comparison to a very weak baseline (using raw classifier weights on region proposals without any adaptation or fine-tuning). This baseline is not comparable to standard detection methods like R-CNN, which requires additional SVM training and bounding box regression on top of the features. As the authors themselves note, "To achieve R-CNN performance requires additionally learning SVMs on the activations of layer 7 and bounding box regression on the activations of layer 5." The comparison to this weak baseline overstates the contribution of their method.

Second, the evaluation is limited in scope and scale. The authors evaluate on the ImageNet detection task with only 200 categories, using just 100 as the "labeled" set. The claimed ability to scale to "tens of thousands of categories" is not meaningfully demonstrated - the 7,604 category detector is only shown with example detections, not with quantitative evaluation. The paper fails to provide evidence that the adaptation technique generalizes effectively to truly large-scale problems.

Third, the proposed method appears to be a combination of established techniques rather than a novel contribution. The "category invariant adaptation" is essentially standard fine-tuning, while the "category specific adaptation" using nearest neighbors is an ad hoc heuristic without theoretical justification. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from existing weakly supervised detection methods like Multiple Instance Learning (MIL), which the authors acknowledge as "complimentary" but fail to compare against directly.

Fourth, the paper lacks proper ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component. The claimed importance of "category invariant adaptation" versus "category specific adaptation" is not convincingly established through controlled experiments. The paper also fails to address potential failure modes, such as how the nearest neighbor approach might fail when categories are dissimilar but have similar classification features.

Finally, the paper overstates its significance. The claim that this "enables detection of tens of thousands of categories" is misleading - the authors still require bounding box annotations for a small subset of categories, which is precisely what they claim to avoid. The paper does not demonstrate that this approach reduces annotation burden in practice, as the authors still need bounding box data for 100 categories out of 200.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses a meaningful problem, the contribution is incremental rather than innovative. The method builds on existing techniques without sufficient validation, and the experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the claimed advantages over established approaches. The paper's claims about scalability and performance improvement are overstated given the limited evaluation.

The paper would benefit from a more rigorous comparison to state-of-the-art weakly supervised detection methods, a more appropriate baseline (e.g., R-CNN without fine-tuning), and a more substantial evaluation of the scalability claim. The current evidence does not justify the paper's ambitious claims about enabling large-scale detection with minimal bounding box annotations.

This paper does not meet NIPS's standards for a significant contribution to the field. It presents an interesting combination of existing techniques but lacks the rigorous validation and theoretical grounding expected for a NIPS publication. The methodological claims are overstated relative to the evidence provided, and the paper would benefit from substantial revision before it could be considered for publication.
