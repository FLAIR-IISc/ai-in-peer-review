Summary
This paper tackles the fundamental bottleneck in scaling object detection: the scarcity of bounding box annotations compared to abundant image-level labels. The authors propose LSDA, a method to adapt image classifiers into object detectors with bounding boxes available for only a subset of categories. They cast classification-to-detection transfer as domain adaptation and learn (i) a category-invariant transformation by fine-tuning shared layers with detection data (including a learned background class), and (ii) a lightweight category-specific adjustment by transferring the average fine-tuning “delta” from nearest-neighbor categories (in fc8 weight space) that do have detection data. On ILSVRC2013, training on detection annotations for 100/200 categories and classification data for all 200, LSDA yields a 50% relative mAP boost on held-out categories (10.31% → 15.85% with invariant adaptation; 16.15% with category-specific adaptation). The paper also demonstrates a 7,604-category detector by adapting many classification categories without boxes.

Relation to prior work
R-CNN showed that fine-tuning on detection data is crucial but requires boxes for all categories. MIL-based weak supervision learns detectors from image labels only but hasn’t been shown at ImageNet scale here. Classical domain adaptation approaches typically require labeled target data for each class and do not modify deep features. LSDA is novel in transferring a learned classification→detection transformation to categories with no detection labels, leveraging a background class and nearest-neighbor transfer in output parameters, and demonstrating large-scale detection.

Strengths
- Novelty and direction: Introduces a pragmatic and conceptually clean path to scale detection to thousands of categories by adapting from classification. The idea of learning a generic detection transform (including background modeling) and applying it to held-out classes is bold and impactful.
- Clear decomposition: Separates category-invariant adaptation (shared layers + background) from category-specific adaptation (fc8 deltas via neighbors), with ablations that quantify each contribution.
- Empirical gains where it matters: On the held-out categories—precisely the challenging regime—the method delivers a substantial relative improvement, with error analysis showing reduced background and localization mistakes.
- Scalability: Demonstrates a 7.6K-category detector and a faster variant with SPP/fast proposals, pointing to practical deployment.
- Reproducibility: Implementation in Caffe, public models/code, and transparent training protocol.

Weaknesses
- Limited principled modeling of category-specific transfer: The nearest-neighbor averaging in fc8 is heuristic. Alternatives such as graph- or hierarchy-regularized multi-task learning, metric learning, or joint optimization over all categories could be more principled.
- Evaluation design: Using the “first 100” (alphabetical) categories as B may introduce bias. Randomized splits and varying m (number of categories with boxes) would better characterize robustness. Sensitivity to k (number of neighbors) is only briefly reported.
- Absolute performance remains modest: While relative gains are strong, absolute mAP on held-out categories is still low compared to the upper bound. It would help to quantify how much of the gap is due to missing SVM/bbox regression versus intrinsic limits of adaptation.
- Comparisons: No large-scale comparison to weakly supervised detectors (MIL variants) on the same benchmark. Even small-scale head-to-heads would contextualize the benefit of adaptation versus discovery-based learning.
- Background of novelty vs R-CNN: Some improvements (e.g., adding a background class, fine-tuning shared layers) are inherited from detection fine-tuning; the core novelty lies in transferring the learned transformation to unseen classes. The paper could be clearer in positioning that distinction.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound and well-supported by ablations, quantitative evaluation, and error analysis. The statistical test for category-specific gains (p=0.017) is appreciated. Some design choices are heuristic, but the empirical case is strong.

Clarity
Generally well-organized and readable. The three “insights” map clearly to components. A diagram of the parameter deltas and neighbor transfer helps. Minor notational inconsistencies aside, an expert could reproduce the pipeline given Caffe code.

Originality
The key contribution—adapting a learned classification→detection transform to categories without boxes—is novel and pushes beyond prior domain adaptation and R-CNN. The fc8 neighbor-delta transfer is simple but new in this context.

Significance
High. The approach directly addresses a major practical bottleneck and demonstrates large-scale applicability. Even if some components are heuristic, the direction is influential and likely to inspire more principled variants.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are results to the choice of B? Please report means/variances over multiple random splits and varying m (e.g., 25, 50, 100 categories with boxes).
- Does incorporating label hierarchy or semantic similarity (e.g., WordNet distances) to define neighbors outperform fc8 L2 nearest neighbors?
- Can you partially integrate MIL to mine pseudo boxes for A during fine-tuning? Any preliminary results?
- What fraction of the gap to the 200-class upper bound is closed by adding SVMs/bbox regression only for B (while keeping A adapted via LSDA)?

Suggestions
- Evaluate with randomized splits and report sensitivity to k and m.
- Explore graph-regularized or hierarchy-aware category-specific adaptation.
- Provide limited-scale comparisons to modern MIL approaches on a shared subset.
- Consider unsupervised domain adaptation losses that explicitly align classification and detection features (if computationally feasible).

Typos/clarity nits
- Some references are duplicated or incomplete; please standardize.
- Clarify training data composition to avoid confusion about using val1 for training and val2 for testing.

Recommendation
Accept (strong). Despite some heuristic components and incomplete comparisons, the paper introduces an important and original direction with compelling empirical evidence and clear potential for large impact.

Confidence
High.
