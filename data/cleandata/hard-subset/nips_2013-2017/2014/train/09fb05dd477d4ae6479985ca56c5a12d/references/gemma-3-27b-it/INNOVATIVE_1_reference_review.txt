## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper tackles the crucial problem of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, recognizing the annotation bottleneck associated with bounding boxes. The core idea – adapting a classifier trained on image-level labels to a detector – is compelling and represents a significant shift in thinking about how to approach large-scale detection. The authors frame this as a domain adaptation problem, which is a reasonable and insightful perspective. The experimental results on ImageNet demonstrate a substantial improvement (+50% mAP) over a baseline, validating the core concept.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The central idea of learning a transformation from classification to detection, enabling detection in categories *without* bounding box annotations, is genuinely novel. This is a bold approach, deviating from the standard paradigm of requiring fully annotated detection datasets.
* **Significance:** Successfully scaling detection to a massive number of categories is critical for real-world applications. Solving, or even making substantial progress on, the annotation bottleneck addressed by this work is highly significant. The potential impact is substantial.
* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of current detection approaches and convincingly argues for the need for a new paradigm.
* **Solid Experimental Validation:** The ImageNet results, while limited in scope, provide strong evidence that the proposed approach works and delivers a meaningful performance gain. The ablation study is helpful in understanding the contribution of each component.
* **Public Release of Code & Models:**  The authors’ commitment to releasing code and models is commendable and will facilitate further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The experiments are primarily focused on ImageNet detection. While a good starting point, it would be beneficial to see how the approach generalizes to other datasets or domains, particularly those with different characteristics (e.g., more cluttered scenes, different object types).
* **Simplifications & Potential Sub-optimality:** The decision to forgo the standard R-CNN post-processing steps (SVM & bounding box regression) for speed, while understandable, likely introduces a performance penalty.  The trade-off should be discussed more explicitly.
* **Nearest Neighbor Heuristic:** The method for transferring knowledge to categories lacking bounding box annotations (using nearest neighbors in feature space) feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more principled approach might yield better results. The sensitivity of performance to the 'k' parameter is not thoroughly investigated.
* **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:** While error types are categorized, a more in-depth analysis of *why* the model fails (e.g., specific object categories where adaptation is less effective) would be valuable.
* **Domain Adaptation Framing:** While framing as domain adaptation is reasonable, the paper doesn’t deeply engage with the domain adaptation literature beyond acknowledging related work.  Exploring more sophisticated adaptation techniques could further improve performance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a genuinely novel and significant idea that has the potential to reshape how we approach large-scale object detection.
* The experimental results are promising and support the core concept.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem and proposed solution.
* The release of code and models fosters reproducibility and further research.
* The authors address a key bottleneck in current detection systems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The evaluation is limited to a single dataset.
* The nearest neighbor heuristic for transferring knowledge is potentially suboptimal.
* The simplification of skipping SVM and regression stages introduces uncertainty about overall potential. 
* The connection to the broader domain adaptation literature could be strengthened.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a highly innovative idea. While the evaluation is limited, and some aspects of the implementation are simplified, the potential impact of this work is substantial. I believe the novelty and significance of the approach outweigh the current limitations. I am willing to overlook the relatively incomplete validation given the boldness of the approach and the promising results. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept** 

I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above in future work, particularly by expanding the evaluation to additional datasets and exploring more sophisticated knowledge transfer techniques.
