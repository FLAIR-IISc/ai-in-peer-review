**Summary**

This paper addresses the significant challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, where obtaining bounding box annotations for every category is prohibitively expensive. The authors propose Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a method that learns to transform an image classifier into an object detector. The core idea is to leverage a small set of categories with full bounding box supervision to learn a generic transformation that can then be applied to classifiers for categories that only have image-level labels. The method is framed as a domain adaptation problem, adapting from classification (source) to detection (target). The proposed transformation has two components: a category-invariant adaptation learned by fine-tuning the shared feature layers (conv1-fc7) of a CNN, and a category-specific adaptation for the final classification layer, which is approximated for new classes by averaging the adaptations learned on their nearest neighbors from the supervised set. Experiments on a 200-class subset of ImageNet show a 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories compared to using an unmodified classifier.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and practical problem. The "annotation bottleneck" is a well-known and critical barrier to building large-scale, real-world object detection systems. A method that can effectively leverage abundant classification data to create detectors is of great interest to the community.
2.  **Novel Formulation:** Framing the problem of classifier-to-detector conversion as a domain adaptation task is an interesting and novel perspective. The key insight—that the fine-tuning process on a subset of classes can learn a generalizable, category-invariant transformation for the detection task—is compelling and well-motivated.
3.  **Thorough Internal Evaluation:** The authors conduct a solid set of experiments to validate their approach. The ablation study in Table 1 and Figure 3 clearly demonstrates the contribution of each component of LSDA, highlighting that the category-invariant feature adaptation is the most critical step. The error analysis in Section 4.3 provides valuable insight, showing that the method successfully reduces localization errors and confusion with background, which are key challenges in adapting a classifier for detection.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Baselines and Comparison to a Weakened SOTA:** The primary weakness of this work lies in its experimental validation against the broader field.
    *   The main baseline is a classifier network applied directly to region proposals. While this is a necessary ablation, it is a very weak baseline. The more established line of work for training detectors from image-level labels is Multiple Instance Learning (MIL). The authors acknowledge MIL but do not compare against any such methods, stating they haven't been evaluated on ImageNet. This is not a sufficient justification; for a convincing contribution, the authors should have implemented or adapted a representative MIL-based detector as a more challenging baseline. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess the true advance provided by LSDA over existing paradigms for weakly-supervised detection.
    *   The paper builds upon the R-CNN framework but omits two key components for performance: training SVMs on top of CNN features and bounding box regression. The authors justify this by citing training time and flexibility. However, this decision means that the reported performance numbers, including the "upper bound," are not comparable to the state-of-the-art (e.g., R-CNN). The reported mAP of 16.15% on held-out categories, while a significant relative improvement over the weak baseline, may still be substantially lower than what a fully equipped R-CNN or a strong MIL method could achieve. A more rigorous evaluation would have included these components to situate the performance properly.

2.  **Ad-Hoc Category-Specific Adaptation:** The proposed method for adapting the category-specific layer (`fc8`) for held-out classes is a simple heuristic. It relies on the assumption that similarity in the classification weight space translates to a similar required adaptation for the detection task. While plausible, this is not rigorously justified. The empirical results show this step provides only a minor improvement (15.85% to 16.15% mAP), and its statistical significance, while noted, is marginal in effect size. This component feels underdeveloped and less principled than the category-invariant adaptation.

3.  **Modest Absolute Performance:** While the relative improvement is highlighted, the final absolute performance on the 100 held-out categories (16.15% mAP) is still quite low. The authors' own upper bound (training with full supervision on all classes in their simplified pipeline) is 21.89% mAP. This demonstrates a considerable performance gap remains between the proposed adaptation method and a fully supervised detector, questioning the practical utility of the resulting detectors for held-out classes in their current state.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a highly significant, practical problem in computer vision.
*   Proposes a novel and intuitive idea: learning a transferable transformation from a classifier to a detector.
*   The paper is clearly written, and the internal ablation studies are well-executed, providing clear support for the effectiveness of the core category-invariant adaptation.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental evaluation is incomplete. The lack of comparison to established weakly-supervised methods (e.g., MIL) makes it impossible to judge the method's contribution to the state of the art.
*   The comparison to the R-CNN framework is weakened by the omission of standard components (SVMs, bbox regression), making the reported absolute performance difficult to interpret.
*   The category-specific adaptation component is an ad-hoc heuristic with minimal performance contribution, weakening the overall methodological soundness.
*   The absolute performance on held-out classes remains low, suggesting the method is an interesting first step but may not yet be a practical solution.

**Conclusion:**

The paper presents an interesting new approach to a very important problem. The core idea of learning a generic feature transformation from classification to detection is promising. However, the work in its current form is not sufficiently mature for publication at NIPS. The primary concern is the lack of rigorous validation against established and relevant baselines, particularly from the MIL literature. Furthermore, the methodological contribution is mixed; while the category-invariant adaptation is a sound application of fine-tuning, the category-specific part is a weak heuristic. Before this work can be considered a solid contribution, it requires a much more thorough experimental evaluation that situates it properly within the existing literature on weakly-supervised object detection. Therefore, I recommend rejection.
