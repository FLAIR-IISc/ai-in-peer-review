This paper addresses the significant challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, where the primary bottleneck is the scarcity of bounding box annotations. The authors propose an algorithm, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), which learns to transform an image classifier into an object detector. The core idea is to frame this as a domain adaptation problem. Using a subset of categories for which both image-level labels and bounding box annotations are available, LSDA learns a generic transformation. This learned transformation is then applied to adapt classifiers for novel categories that only have image-level labels, thus enabling detection without corresponding bounding box data.

The proposed transformation has two main components. The first is a category-invariant adaptation, where the shared feature-extraction layers of a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) are fine-tuned on the detection task using the fully supervised categories. This process learns features better suited for localization and also learns a generic "background" model. The second component is a category-specific adaptation of the final classification layer. For categories without bounding box data, this adaptation is approximated by averaging the adaptations learned for their k-nearest neighbors among the fully supervised categories, where neighborhood is defined by the similarity of their original classifier weights. This approach builds upon the fine-tuning methodology of R-CNN [1] but critically generalizes it to categories with no detection-specific supervision, a problem R-CNN does not address. It differs from weakly-supervised methods based on Multiple Instance Learning (e.g., [6, 7]) by leveraging a fully supervised set of classes to learn a transferable model of adaptation.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The claims are well-supported by a thorough experimental evaluation on the ILSVRC2013 detection dataset. The experimental design, which simulates the target scenario by holding out 100 of the 200 classes from detection training, is appropriate and convincing. The ablation study (Figure 3, Table 1) is particularly effective, clearly isolating and quantifying the contribution of each component of the LSDA algorithm. The result is a significant 50% relative mAP improvement over a strong baseline. The inclusion of an error analysis (Figure 5) provides valuable insight into the method's effectiveness, demonstrating that the adaptation primarily succeeds by reducing localization errors and confusion with background—the key challenges that differentiate detection from classification. The authors are honest about the performance gap relative to a fully supervised oracle, which serves as a realistic upper bound.

A minor weakness lies in the heuristic nature of the category-specific adaptation. The assumption that categories with similar classification weight vectors should undergo a similar transformation for detection is plausible but not formally justified. While empirically shown to be beneficial, this component contributes less to the overall performance boost than the category-invariant feature adaptation.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the problem is framed precisely. The methodology is described logically, and Figures 1 and 2 provide excellent visual aids that make the core concept of the algorithm easy to grasp. The experimental section is clear, and the results are presented in an interpretable manner. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the work and potentially reproduce the results; the authors also state they have made code and models publicly available, which further strengthens the work's clarity and reproducibility.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel and significant contribution. While it utilizes established techniques like CNN fine-tuning from R-CNN, the central idea of learning a *transferable transformation* from a classifier to a detector is original. Framing this problem as a form of domain adaptation—adapting not just across visual domains but from a classification task to a detection task, and generalizing this adaptation to unseen classes—is a new and insightful perspective. To our knowledge, this is the first method to explicitly learn how to adapt classifiers into detectors for categories with zero bounding box annotations, moving beyond prior work that requires at least some detection data for all target classes.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a problem of high practical importance. The cost of collecting bounding box data is a major impediment to building large-scale, real-world object detection systems. LSDA offers a pragmatic and effective approach to mitigate this issue, potentially enabling the creation of detectors for tens of thousands of categories where only classification data is available. The demonstration of a 7,604-category detector, while qualitative, highlights the potential impact of this work. The ideas presented are likely to be influential and could inspire further research into transfer learning and weakly supervised methods for object detection. The principles of learning a category-invariant transformation could also be applied to more recent and advanced detector architectures.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a highly significant and practical problem in computer vision: scaling object detection beyond the limited set of classes with bounding box annotations.
*   Proposes a novel and elegant method that frames classifier-to-detector conversion as a learnable, transferable adaptation.
*   The method is technically sound, and the experimental validation is thorough and convincing, with strong ablation and error analyses.
*   The paper achieves a substantial performance improvement (+50% relative mAP) over a strong baseline on held-out categories.
*   The manuscript is exceptionally clear, well-written, and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The category-specific adaptation component relies on a plausible but heuristic nearest-neighbor approach.
*   A significant performance gap remains between the proposed method and a fully supervised upper bound, indicating that while the approach is promising, it is not a complete solution to the problem.
