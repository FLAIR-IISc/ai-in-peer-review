Summary
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to convert image classifiers into object detectors when only a subset of categories has bounding box annotations. The approach casts classification→detection as a domain adaptation problem. It has two components: (1) category-invariant adaptation by fine-tuning layers 1–7 of an AlexNet-style CNN on detection data for m “base” categories and introducing a background class; and (2) category-specific adaptation for held-out categories by transferring the average change in the output layer weights (δ) from their nearest neighbors (in fc8 space) among the base categories. At test time, region proposals are scored by the K+1-way softmax, using scorei − scorebackground as the final detection score. On ILSVRC13 detection, with detection labels for 100/200 classes, LSDA improves mAP on the held-out 100 classes from 10.31% (classification-only baseline on proposals) to 16.15% (+50% relative), with ablations showing background modeling and feature fine-tuning as the primary drivers, and a smaller gain from the category-specific transfer. The authors also demonstrate a 7.6K-category detector trained largely without box labels and release code/models.

Relation to prior work
The work is well positioned relative to R-CNN, which requires full box supervision for each category; MIL-based weakly supervised detection, which learns from image-level labels but was not validated at ImageNet scale; and domain adaptation methods, which mostly adapt across visual domains and require target labels for all categories. The key novelty is adapting from classification to detection for novel categories without boxes by learning a category-invariant representation shift plus a simple fc8 parameter transfer.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation that directly addresses the practical bottleneck of bounding-box annotation for large numbers of categories.
- Simple, effective architecture that leverages existing supervised CNNs and standard region proposals; minimal engineering beyond fine-tuning and a lightweight parameter transfer.
- Strong empirical gains on a challenging setup (held-out ImageNet detection classes), with ablations isolating the contributions of background modeling, shared feature adaptation, and fc8 transfer.
- Useful error analysis highlighting reductions in localization and background confusions—exactly the key gaps between classification and detection.
- Demonstration of scalability (7.6K-class detector) and release of models/code increase practical impact.

Weaknesses and concerns
- The category-specific adaptation is heuristic: nearest neighbors in fc8 weight space of the classification network may not be an ideal proxy for detection similarity. No analysis of alternative similarity metrics (e.g., cosine on intermediate features, semantic/WordNet distances) or learning δ via regression from base categories.
- Sensitivity to k (number of neighbors) is not deeply explored beyond a single reported value. It is unclear how robust performance is to k or to the choice of base set B.
- The split of categories (first 100 alphabetically) may introduce semantic skew; random or stratified splits and variance across multiple splits would strengthen claims.
- Comparisons to other weakly supervised/transfer baselines on the same data are limited. For example, how does LSDA compare to: (i) training R-CNN on base classes and using it as-is on held-out classes (without fc8 transfer); (ii) MIL-based detectors trained on held-out classes with the same proposals; (iii) training SVMs on deep features extracted from classification nets with a background class constructed from proposals?
- The evaluation omits bounding-box regression and SVM post-hoc training (as in R-CNN). While the choice simplifies adaptation, the gap to the fully supervised upper bound remains large; a controlled study quantifying how much of that gap is due to missing bbox regression vs. supervision would clarify limits.
- Some implementation details lack precision: number of fine-tuning iterations, learning rates per layer, negative sampling strategy, and proposal settings affect results and reproducibility.
- The statistical test is reported for a single comparison; broader uncertainty estimates (e.g., bootstrap CIs over classes) would be helpful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Replace or augment nearest-neighbor δ transfer with a learned mapping: train a regression from classification fc8 weights or intermediate representations to the observed δ on base classes (e.g., ridge regression or MLP), possibly with semantic features as inputs.
- Explore alternative similarity metrics and neighborhood selection (cosine, feature-space embeddings, metric learning), and report sensitivity to k and to the composition/size of B. Include results averaged over multiple random category splits.
- Add baselines: weakly supervised MIL on held-out classes using the same proposals and features; and “R-CNN base only” inference on held-out classes to isolate the benefit of fc8 transfer.
- Evaluate adding bbox regression trained only on base classes to test whether category-invariant localization improves held-out classes.
- Clarify the scoring rule: subtracting background logit vs. using softmax probabilities; report any calibration issues across categories.
- Provide more ablation on which layers to fine-tune for category-invariant adaptation and whether freezing early conv layers changes transfer quality.

Quality
Technically sound and experimentally supported. The core claims (category-invariant transfer and benefit of background modeling) are well evidenced. Some methodological choices for category-specific adaptation are heuristic, and broader comparisons/sensitivity analyses would solidify the conclusions.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. Minor issues: define A/B consistently early; fix the “three key insights” list formatting; specify training hyperparameters and data usage details for full reproducibility.

Originality
The decomposition into category-invariant feature adaptation plus category-specific parameter transfer to enable detection without box labels is novel relative to prior adaptation and detection work at the time.

Significance
High practical significance: reduces annotation burden and enables scaling detectors to thousands of classes. The released resources further amplify impact.

Recommendation
Accept (clear contribution with strong empirical evidence and high utility), with the caveat that additional sensitivity analyses and stronger baselines would further strengthen the paper.

Confidence
High.
