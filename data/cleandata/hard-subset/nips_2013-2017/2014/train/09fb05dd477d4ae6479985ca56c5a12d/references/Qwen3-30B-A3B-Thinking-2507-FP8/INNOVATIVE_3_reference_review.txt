

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

This paper presents LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a novel approach to object detection that transforms image classifiers into detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The core insight is that the transformation from classification to detection can be learned on a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations and then generalized to categories without such annotations. This addresses a fundamental limitation in scaling object detection to large numbers of categories, where collecting bounding box annotations for tens of thousands of categories is impractical.

The paper makes three key contributions: 1) Recognizing background as an important step in adaptation; 2) Transferring category invariant information between classifier and detector feature representations; and 3) Learning category-specific adaptations using nearest neighbors. The approach involves pre-training a CNN on classification data, fine-tuning on a small subset of detection data to learn a generic transformation, and then applying this transformation to adapt classifiers for categories without bounding box annotations.

The authors demonstrate their method on the ImageNet detection task, showing a 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline that simply uses raw classifier weights on object proposals. They also showcase scalability by producing a 7,604 category detector using only 200 categories with bounding box annotations.

**Originality**: The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to object detection that breaks from the traditional paradigm requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. While related to domain adaptation and MIL methods, LSDA specifically addresses the problem of adapting to held-out categories without detection data, which to the best of my knowledge has not been previously solved. The three key insights are compelling and the approach represents a significant conceptual shift from existing detection frameworks like R-CNN.

**Significance**: The problem of scaling detection to large numbers of categories is important for practical applications where classification data (e.g., from search engines) is abundant but bounding box annotations are scarce. The 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories is substantial, and the ability to produce a 7,604 category detector demonstrates the potential of this approach. This could significantly reduce the annotation burden for large-scale detection systems.

**Quality**: The methodology is technically sound and well-executed. The ablation study in Section 4.2 clearly demonstrates the importance of each component of the approach. The implementation details are sufficiently clear for reproduction. However, the paper could have provided more thorough analysis of limitations, particularly regarding the nearest neighbors approach for category-specific adaptation. For instance, how does the method perform when the nearest neighbor categories are not semantically similar? The paper also doesn't compare to recent MIL-based methods on the ImageNet detection dataset, though the authors acknowledge this as a limitation.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The figures (particularly Figure 3) effectively illustrate the key concepts. The explanation of the three key insights is particularly clear. The technical details are sufficiently described, though some implementation specifics could be expanded for full reproducibility.

**Strengths**: The paper's bold conceptual leap—using classification data to train detectors for categories without any bounding box annotations—is highly compelling. The 50% improvement on held-out categories is impressive, and the 7,604 category detector demonstration is a strong showcase of the approach's scalability. The method is relatively simple to implement while yielding significant gains.

**Weaknesses**: The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of when the nearest neighbors approach might fail (e.g., for categories with no similar neighbors) and how to address this. A comparison to MIL-based methods on the ImageNet detection task would strengthen the evaluation. The paper also doesn't explore how the method might interact with other detection techniques like region proposal networks.

**Conclusion**: This paper presents a highly novel and significant approach to scaling object detection. The core idea of adapting classifiers to detectors using only a small subset of bounding box annotations is bold and addresses a real practical limitation in the field. While the methodology could be strengthened with additional analysis of limitations and comparisons, the conceptual contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance. The 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories is compelling evidence of the approach's value, and the ability to scale detection to 7,604 categories represents a significant step toward practical large-scale detection systems.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, including R-CNN and domain adaptation methods, by focusing on the problem of adapting to held-out categories without detection data. This represents a meaningful conceptual advance in object detection. Given the high value placed on novelty and bold approaches, I recommend acceptance. The paper's significance and originality outweigh the minor methodological limitations.
