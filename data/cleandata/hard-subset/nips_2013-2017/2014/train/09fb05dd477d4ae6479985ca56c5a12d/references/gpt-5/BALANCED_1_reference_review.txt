Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to turn image classifiers into object detectors when only a small subset of categories has bounding-box annotations. The key idea is to view “classification → detection” as a domain/task adaptation problem and to learn (i) a category-invariant transformation via fine-tuning on detection data for a subset B of categories (including a background class), and (ii) a category-specific transformation for held-out categories A by transferring the average fine-tuning deltas from nearest neighbors in B (nearest in fc8 weight space). At test time, the detector uses per-region scores with a background score and simple score_i − score_bg calibration; no SVMs or bbox regression are used to preserve transferability.

The approach is built on CNNs (AlexNet) and R-CNN-style region proposals. Unlike R-CNN, which requires bounding boxes for all categories, LSDA uses detection annotations only for a subset and transfers to the remainder. The paper situates itself relative to MIL-based weakly supervised detection and classical domain adaptation (feature/model adaptation), noting that prior supervised adaptation methods typically require detection labels in the target categories. Empirically, on ILSVRC13 detection with 200 classes, they assume detection data for 100 and report +50% relative mAP on the 100 held-out categories over a “classification-only” baseline. They also demonstrate a 7.6K-class detector by adapting 7,404 categories without boxes.

Quality:
- Methodology: The category-invariant adaptation (fine-tuning layers 1–7, adding a background class) is technically sound and aligns with evidence from R-CNN that task-specific fine-tuning matters. The category-specific transfer via averaging δ vectors from nearest neighbors in fc8 space is simple and plausible, though heuristic.
- Experiments: The ablation is informative: background modeling and feature adaptation explain most gains; the category-specific fc8 transfer adds a smaller but statistically significant boost. Error analysis partitions false positives into localization, background, and other errors, showing the method reduces the first two, consistent with the hypothesis.
- Evaluation design: Using the first 100 categories alphabetically as the “B” split is potentially biased; multiple random splits would strengthen claims. Comparisons to stronger R-CNN variants (SVM heads, bbox regression) are not shown for the transfer setting; a class-agnostic bbox regressor trained on B and applied to A would be a valuable baseline. Hardware/training details are sufficient to reproduce the main setup via released code, but hyperparameters and training schedules could be more explicit.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a good high-level narrative and figures that support the claims. A few implementation details could use clarification (e.g., precise negative/background mining protocol; learning rates/schedules for different layers; how k is selected). The notation for Wc, Wd, and δ is clear.

Originality:
The framing of classification-to-detection as domain/task adaptation and the transfer to categories without any bounding boxes is novel and timely. The ingredients (fine-tuning, background class, nearest-neighbor parameter transfer) are individually known, but their combination to address “no box” categories is original and practically useful relative to R-CNN and prior domain adaptation work that assumes detection labels everywhere.

Significance:
The problem is important: scaling detection beyond a few hundred categories is bottlenecked by box annotation. Demonstrating consistent improvements on 100 held-out categories and a 7.6K-class detector underscores impact. While absolute mAP remains modest on the held-out set, the relative gain and the substantial reduction in annotation requirements are valuable. Given community interest in large-scale detection, this is likely to be used and built upon.

Strengths:
- Clear, simple method with a compelling motivation and solid empirical gains (+50% relative mAP on held-out classes).
- Good ablations and error analysis that align with the hypothesized mechanisms (benefits from background modeling and feature adaptation).
- Scalable demonstration to thousands of categories; code/model release and speedups with SPP/fast proposals increase practical relevance.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Single deterministic split (first 100 categories) risks bias; please report averages over multiple random splits of B/A, and variance.
- Category-specific transfer via fc8 nearest neighbors is plausible but ad hoc. Consider alternatives: semantic similarity (WordNet, word2vec) or learned metric over features; analyze sensitivity to k and to the distance metric; show per-class effects (who benefits/hurts).
- Calibration: score_i − score_bg is simple. Try temperature scaling or class-wise bias calibration using B to better align scores for A.
- Localization: explore class-agnostic bounding-box regression trained on B and applied to all categories—likely to further reduce localization errors without sacrificing transferability.
- Comparisons: show where LSDA sits relative to weakly supervised MIL baselines on the same protocol (even if scaled-down); or at least discuss expected differences. Also include an R-CNN “upper bound” with SVMs/bbox regression in the transfer setting if feasible.
- Generalization across architectures: results are on AlexNet; a brief check on a deeper backbone (if available) would strengthen the case that the adaptation principle is architecture-agnostic.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Addresses an important, underexplored setting (no boxes for many classes) with a practical, effective method.
- Solid empirical evidence, ablations, and error analysis; code/models released; large-scale demo.
- Conceptual clarity and compatibility with standard detection pipelines.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Methodological novelty is moderate; some steps are heuristic without theoretical grounding.
- Evaluation lacks multiple random splits and some key baselines (class-agnostic bbox regression; stronger R-CNN heads in transfer setting).
- Absolute performance on held-out categories remains low; scope of improvement may depend on backbone and proposals.

Recommendation:
Accept (borderline to clear accept). Despite its simplicity, LSDA provides a well-motivated, empirically validated approach to a practically significant problem—adapting classifiers into detectors at scale without box annotations for most categories. Addressing the suggested evaluation gaps (random splits, calibration, class-agnostic bbox regression) would further strengthen the paper.
