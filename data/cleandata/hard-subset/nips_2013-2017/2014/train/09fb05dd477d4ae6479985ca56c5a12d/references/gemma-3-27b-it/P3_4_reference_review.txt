## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the crucial problem of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, a bottleneck currently limited by the need for extensive bounding box annotations. The authors propose LSDA, a domain adaptation approach that learns to transform an ImageNet-trained classifier into an object detector. The key idea is to leverage readily available image-level labels for many categories and transfer knowledge from a subset of categories *with* bounding box annotations to those *without*. The method fine-tunes a CNN on labeled detection data, effectively learning a category-invariant transformation, and then applies this transformation, combined with a category-specific adjustment based on nearest neighbor relationships, to adapt classifiers for unseen categories. The results on the ImageNet detection task demonstrate a significant improvement (50% relative mAP) over a baseline using raw classifier weights, and the authors showcase a 7604-category detector.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of recent object detection techniques. It acknowledges the impact of R-CNN [1] in bridging classification and detection and highlights the limitations of relying solely on bounding box annotations. The connection to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) [6, 7] is appropriately noted, framing LSDA as a complementary approach. The discussion of domain adaptation methods [12-22] is thorough, clearly differentiating LSDA’s unique contribution: adapting to *held-out* categories without any detection labels.  The paper adequately references relevant work in deep learning, feature extraction, and object detection, demonstrating awareness of the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of learning a transformation from classification to detection and applying it to zero-shot detection categories is genuinely novel and addresses a significant practical challenge.  This is a clear step beyond simply fine-tuning pre-trained models.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The 50% relative mAP improvement on ImageNet detection is substantial. Demonstrating performance gains on a challenging dataset like ImageNet is a strong indicator of the method's effectiveness.
* **Scalability Potential:** The ability to build a 7604-category detector using primarily image-level labels is impressive and highlights the potential for scaling detection to a truly large number of categories, a critical step towards human-level object recognition.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the LSDA algorithm.  The diagrams (particularly Figure 2) are helpful in understanding the process.
* **Ablation Study:** The ablation study (Figure 3) provides valuable insights into the contribution of each component of the LSDA algorithm, confirming the importance of category-invariant adaptation.
* **Error Analysis:** The detailed error analysis, highlighting localization errors and background confusion, provides valuable insights into the limitations and potential future directions for the method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Reliance on Pre-trained Classifier:**  The performance is heavily reliant on the quality of the initial ImageNet-trained classifier. While this is a common practice, the paper doesn't sufficiently discuss the sensitivity of LSDA to different pre-training regimes or alternative architectures.
* **Nearest Neighbor Selection:** The use of Euclidean distance on fc8 weights for nearest neighbor selection seems somewhat arbitrary. A more sophisticated metric or a more principled approach to determining category similarity might improve performance. The sensitivity to the value of *k* (number of neighbors) should be explored more thoroughly.
* **Limited Comparison to MIL:** While acknowledging MIL [6, 7], the paper doesn’t provide a direct quantitative comparison. Given the relatedness, a comparison – even on a smaller subset of categories – would strengthen the paper.  The authors state that MIL methods haven’t been evaluated on ImageNet detection, but a more limited comparison might be feasible.
* **Detection Implementation Detail:** Skipping the SVM and bounding box regression steps, as mentioned in Section 3.3, feels like a compromise for speed rather than a principled design choice. The trade-off between speed and performance should be more clearly articulated.
* **Lack of Qualitative Results for the 7604 Category Detector:** While the authors demonstrate the ability to build a very large detector, they only provide limited visual examples (Figure 6). More comprehensive qualitative results, showcasing both successes and failures, would strengthen the claims of scalability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant, practical problem in object detection.
* Presents a novel and technically sound approach.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results on a challenging dataset.
* Offers a path towards scaling object detection to a large number of categories.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Reliance on a strong pre-trained classifier without thorough sensitivity analysis.
*  Somewhat arbitrary choice of metric for nearest neighbor selection.
* Lack of direct comparison to related techniques like MIL.
* The simplification in the detection pipeline (skipping SVM/regression) raises questions about optimality.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of object detection.  The proposed LSDA algorithm is a clever and effective approach to addressing the problem of scaling detection to a large number of categories. While some aspects could be further refined (sensitivity to pre-training, nearest neighbor selection, comparison to MIL), the novelty, strong empirical results, and potential impact of this work outweigh these concerns.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions.  I recommend the authors address the weaknesses regarding sensitivity analysis, nearest neighbor selection justification, and ideally, provide a limited comparison to a relevant MIL approach. Additionally, including more qualitative results for the 7604 category detector would further strengthen the paper.
