## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

**Summary:**

This paper proposes LSDA, a method for adapting image classifiers trained on large, image-level labeled datasets (like ImageNet) into object detectors, even for categories without bounding box annotations. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification feature space to the detection feature space using categories *with* bounding box annotations, and then apply this transformation to adapt classifiers for categories *without* bounding box annotations. The approach casts this as a domain adaptation problem and demonstrates a 50% relative mAP improvement on ImageNet detection compared to a baseline using raw classifier weights.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the context of recent work in object detection, particularly R-CNN and its successors which demonstrated the power of transfer learning from classification. It also acknowledges connections to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) as a weakly supervised detection technique and highlights the difference: MIL hasn't been evaluated on the large-scale ImageNet detection task. The authors also appropriately discuss the relevance of domain adaptation methods, emphasizing that their work differs by adapting both the domain *and* the task (from classification to detection) and by enabling detection in held-out categories.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical problem:** The scarcity of bounding box annotations is a major bottleneck in scaling object detection to a large number of categories. LSDA offers a promising solution by leveraging the abundance of image-level labels.
* **Clear and logical approach:** The idea of learning a transformation between classification and detection feature spaces is intuitive and well-motivated. The three key insights (background recognition, category invariant transfer, category-specific adaptation) provide a solid framework.
* **Strong empirical results:** The 50% relative mAP improvement over the baseline is significant, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach. The ablation study clearly shows the contribution of each component.
* **Detailed analysis & Visualization:** The error analysis and visualizations (e.g., Fig. 5, Fig. 6) are helpful in understanding the strengths and limitations of the method. The examples of improved localization are particularly compelling.
* **Publicly available resources:** Releasing the code and models is a major plus and facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Dependency on pre-trained classifier:** The method relies heavily on a strong pre-trained classifier. While this is common in transfer learning, it’s a point of vulnerability. Performance might degrade with a weaker base classifier.
* **Nearest Neighbor Approximation:** The category-specific adaptation via nearest neighbors (Equation 1) feels like a heuristic. While it seems to work in practice, the justification for using Euclidean distance on the fc8 parameters isn't strongly established.  A more principled approach might be desirable.
* **Limited comparison to other weakly supervised methods:** While the authors mention MIL, a direct empirical comparison on ImageNet is missing. This would strengthen the argument for LSDA’s advantages.
* **Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions speedup with spatial pyramid pooling, the initial training time (and even the 4s/image inference) might be a barrier to adoption.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The experimental setup is well-defined and the results are presented clearly. The ablation study provides good evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.  The claim of a 50% improvement is well-supported.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The diagrams (Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Fig. 3) are helpful in illustrating the method. However, Equation 1 could benefit from more explanation.
* **Originality:** The approach of learning a transformation between classification and detection spaces is novel, building upon existing ideas but offering a unique contribution.
* **Significance:** The problem addressed is highly significant, and the proposed solution has the potential to impact the field of object detection by enabling large-scale detection with limited annotation. The ability to detect objects in categories without bounding box labels is a substantial advance.

**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a key bottleneck in scaling object detection.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results on a challenging dataset.
* Offers a clear and well-motivated approach.
* Provides valuable resources (code and models).

**Con:**

* Relies on a strong pre-trained classifier.
* Nearest neighbor approximation lacks strong theoretical justification.
* Lacks a direct comparison to recent weakly supervised methods like MIL.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of object detection. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by compelling experimental results. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths outweigh them.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.**  I recommend the authors address the following points:

*   Provide a brief discussion of the sensitivity to the choice of pre-trained classifier.
*   Justify the use of Euclidean distance for nearest neighbor selection, or consider exploring alternative metrics.
*   Include a comparison to state-of-the-art weakly supervised detection methods (e.g., MIL) on the ImageNet dataset.
*   Clarify the computational complexity and potential optimizations for faster inference.
