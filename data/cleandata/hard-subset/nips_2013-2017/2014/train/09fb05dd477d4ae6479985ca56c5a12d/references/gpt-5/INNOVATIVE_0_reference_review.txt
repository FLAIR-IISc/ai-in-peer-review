Summary
This paper tackles the important problem of scaling object detection to thousands of categories without requiring bounding box annotations for all classes. The key idea (LSDA) is to view the shift from image-level classification to detection as a domain adaptation problem and to learn a generic, category-invariant transformation using only a subset of detection-labeled classes. The approach has three components: (1) introduce an explicit background class and fine-tune layers 1–7 on detection data for a subset of classes (B), learning a task shift from classification to detection; (2) transfer this category-invariant transformation to all classes, including those without boxes (A); and (3) perform a simple category-specific adaptation for A by adding the average delta learned on nearest-neighbor classes in B (measured in the fc8 weight space). On ILSVRC13, the method improves mAP on held-out categories from 10.31% to 16.15% (+50% relative), with most gains attributable to the category-invariant adaptation and background modeling. The paper also demonstrates a 7.6K-category detector built with this recipe and releases code/models.

Relation to prior work
Compared to R-CNN, which requires detection labels per class, LSDA explicitly learns and transfers a classification-to-detection transformation, allowing detectors for classes without boxes. This is distinct from MIL-based weakly supervised detection, which discovers boxes from image labels but has not been shown at ImageNet scale. It also differs from classic domain adaptation (feature/model adaptation) that assumes labeled data for each target class; here transfer happens across both task (classification→detection) and categories with no target labels. The use of CNNs for both classification and detection is in line with contemporary work, but the proposed adaptation framing and practical instantiation (background modeling + generic transformation + fc8 deltas) is novel.

Strengths
- Novel and bold problem framing: Treating classification-to-detection as a transferable, category-invariant transformation learned on a subset of classes is an impactful idea that opens a path to large-scale detection without exhaustive box annotations.
- Practical, simple method with clear insights: background class matters; most benefit comes from feature-level adaptation; category-specific tuning adds small but measurable gains.
- Solid empirical evidence: Ablations isolate contributions; error analysis shows reductions in localization and background confusions; statistical testing supports small fc8 gains.
- Scale and usability: Demonstration of a 7.6K detector, training time reduction versus R-CNN, and released code/models increase significance and likely community adoption.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Absolute performance remains modest on held-out categories (16.15% mAP), far from the detector trained with boxes for all classes. Some discussion on how close this is to practical utility would help.
- Category-specific adaptation is heuristic (nearest neighbors in fc8 weight space); alternatives (semantic/WordNet similarity, visual confusion matrices, learned meta-mapping) are not explored.
- Limited exploration of how performance scales with the number of detection-labeled classes m. A curve showing mAP on A as a function of m would strengthen the story.
- Evaluation protocol uses val2 as test with detection training on val1 plus additional training images; this is reasonable but somewhat nonstandard. It would be helpful to clarify any overlap and confirm no leakage and to report results on the official test server if feasible.
- Comparison to other weakly supervised approaches (MIL) at scale is largely qualitative; while such baselines may not be available on ILSVRC13, even a smaller-scale controlled comparison would contextualize LSDA’s gains.
- The choice to forgo SVMs and box regression trades off some accuracy for simplicity; a brief quantitative upper bound (e.g., adding bbox regression trained only on B and applied to A) could indicate the headroom.

Quality
Technically sound with a well-motivated design. The ablations and error analyses support the claims, and the training details are sufficient for reproduction (Caffe configs released). Some components are heuristic but justified by results. Statistical significance is reported for a small improvement; consider reporting confidence intervals for the main mAP gains as well.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The three insights and training pipeline are easy to follow. Minor editorial issues (typos, incomplete references) should be fixed. The notation around sets A/B and the fc8 delta construction is clear.

Originality
High. The core idea of learning a category-invariant mapping from classification to detection and transferring it to classes without boxes is, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial at the time. The background modeling and nearest-neighbor delta are simple but effective instantiations.

Significance
High. The problem is important, and the approach is practical and likely to influence follow-up work on large-scale detection and weak/partial supervision. The released 7.6K detector underscores impact.

Questions for authors
- How does performance on A scale with the number of detection-labeled classes m? Any evidence of diminishing returns?
- Did you try alternative similarity metrics for nearest neighbors (e.g., semantic distance, feature-space cosine of fc7 prototypes)?
- Could bbox regression trained on B help A via class-agnostic regressors?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of region proposals?
- Any qualitative failure modes where background hurts rare fine-grained classes?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, bold framing with clear, generalizable insights
- Demonstrable gains on a challenging benchmark, with careful ablations
- High potential impact for large-scale detection; practical release

Cons for acceptance
- Absolute accuracy on held-out classes is still low
- Some heuristics could be better justified or compared
- Evaluation limited to a specific protocol; missing test-server results

Overall recommendation
Accept. Despite some limitations, the paper introduces a compelling and original direction—adapting classifiers to detectors at scale with minimal box annotations—and backs it with convincing experiments and practical contributions. The novelty and potential impact outweigh the imperfections.
