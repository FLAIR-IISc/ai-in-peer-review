This paper proposes LSDA, a method for adapting image classifiers to object detectors, aiming to reduce the reliance on large, bounding-box annotated datasets. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain (image-level labels) to the detection domain (bounding boxes and labels), leveraging data where both are available and transferring this knowledge to categories with only image-level labels. While the problem addressed is relevant – scaling detection to many categories – the approach, while conceptually reasonable, feels incremental and lacks the extensive validation needed to warrant strong confidence.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is clearly stated, and the proposed method is explained in a relatively straightforward manner. The experiments are conducted on the ImageNet detection dataset, a standard benchmark, and the results are presented with some ablation studies. However, the paper suffers from a lack of depth in its experimental evaluation and comparison to established methods.

The main weakness lies in the limited comparison to existing approaches. While the authors mention Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) methods, they dismiss them by noting they haven’t been evaluated on ImageNet, rather than attempting a direct comparison (even a preliminary one). More importantly, they don’t adequately compare against simply *fine-tuning* a classifier directly on the detection data for the categories where it *is* available. The claim of a 50% relative mAP improvement over a “baseline of just using raw classifier weights” is vague and doesn’t specify whether this baseline included any fine-tuning, a crucial step highlighted by Girshick et al. (which the authors acknowledge).  Without a more thorough comparison to fine-tuning and a strong justification for why LSDA outperforms it, the contribution feels less significant.

The category-specific adaptation using nearest neighbors also feels somewhat ad-hoc. While the motivation is understandable given the lack of bounding box data, the choice of using Euclidean distance on fc8 weights feels arbitrary and potentially suboptimal.  There's no discussion of the sensitivity of the results to the number of neighbors 'k', beyond a brief mention of its impact, nor an attempt to justify the chosen value.

The error analysis, while including examples, is somewhat limited. Identifying localization and background confusion as common errors is not surprising for an adaptation from a classifier, and the analysis doesn’t provide much insight into *why* LSDA addresses these issues better than a direct fine-tuning approach.

Furthermore, the scale of the “large scale” detection (7604 categories) is presented but not thoroughly evaluated. Simply demonstrating detection on this many categories without a quantitative assessment of performance for a representative subset doesn't add substantial weight to the claims.  The mention of a faster implementation with spatial pyramid pooling is welcome but feels tacked on at the end.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a practically important problem – reducing the annotation burden for object detection.
* Provides a conceptually sound approach to domain adaptation.
* Demonstrates a performance improvement over a poorly defined baseline.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Limited comparison to strong baselines like fine-tuning.
* Ad-hoc nature of the category-specific adaptation.
* Insufficiently detailed experimental evaluation, particularly regarding the large-scale detection setup.
* Incremental contribution; doesn’t represent a substantial breakthrough.



Overall, this paper presents a reasonable approach to a relevant problem, but lacks the rigor and comprehensive evaluation expected of a NIPS publication. The results are promising, but require stronger justification and comparison to established methods. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit with a more thorough experimental evaluation, a clear comparison to fine-tuning, and a more principled approach to the nearest neighbor selection.
