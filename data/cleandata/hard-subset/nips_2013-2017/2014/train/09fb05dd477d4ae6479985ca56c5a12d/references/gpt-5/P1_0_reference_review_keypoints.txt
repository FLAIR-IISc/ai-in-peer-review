- Proposes LSDA to convert image classifiers into object detectors when only a subset of categories have bounding boxes, framing it as classification竊壇etection domain adaptation.  
- LSDA components: learn a background class, category-invariant adaptation by fine-tuning shared CNN layers on detection data for subset B, and category-specific adaptation by transferring per-class parameter deltas from B to A via nearest-neighbor averaging in fc8 space.  
- Reports ~50% relative mAP improvement on held-out ILSVRC13 categories (val2) versus using classifiers on region proposals; demonstrates a 7.6K-category qualitative detector and a faster SPP-based variant.  
- Builds on R-CNN fine-tuning but addresses transferring detection adaptation to unseen categories rather than requiring boxes for all classes.  
- Differs from MIL-based weakly supervised methods by leveraging a small fully-annotated set to learn a transferable classification竊壇etection transformation.  
- Unlike classic domain adaptation, LSDA jointly adapts deep features and the task and adapts to categories without target annotations.  
- Addresses an important practical problem: scaling detection to thousands of categories without bounding boxes for all.  
- Clear decomposition into background modeling, shared-layer adaptation, and output-layer transfer makes the approach interpretable and implementable.  
- Implementation is simple: standard CNN fine-tuning plus lightweight nearest-neighbor transfer for class-specific parameters.  
- Ablations show most gain comes from shared-layer fine-tuning and adding a background class, with per-class transfer giving a smaller but significant boost.  
- Error analysis shows reduced background confusion and localization errors after adaptation.  
- Efficient training/inference: avoids SVMs and bbox regressors to cut training time and provides a faster inference variant; code/models are released.  
- Limited baselines: lacks comparisons to plausible alternatives like class-agnostic bbox regressors, multi-task fine-tuning including A, or linear SVMs on region features.  
- Heuristic output-layer transfer: nearest-neighbor averaging in fc8 lacks sensitivity analysis (varying k, distance metric, alternative spaces like fc7 or semantics).  
- No analysis of how performance on held-out classes A scales with the number of annotated classes |B| (label-efficiency curve).  
- Limited statistical robustness: most results from a single split/run with only one p-value reported; needs variance across seeds and splits.  
- Large-scale 7.6K results are only qualitative; quantitative or partial human-verified evaluations would strengthen scalability claims.  
- Evaluation confined to ILSVRC val2 (with val1 used for fine-tuning); results on test server or other datasets (e.g., VOC) are missing for generalization.  
- Missing analysis of class-agnostic bbox regression trained on B and applied to A, which could reduce localization errors.  
- Some implementation details are missing (background sampling, IoU thresholds, minibatch composition, hard-negative mining, per-layer learning rates, proposal settings) which hurt reproducibility.  
- Overall technically sound with convincing evidence that shared-layer adaptation and background modeling drive gains, though some baselines and robustness checks are missing.  
- Presentation is clear and well-organized; figures and error analysis help, but A/B split protocol, neighbor metric, and more training details should be clarified.  
- Originality is moderate窶都trong: adapting classifiers into detectors for unseen categories via a learned transformation and explicit background modeling is a novel, practical combination.  
- High practical significance: reduces dependence on bounding boxes and enables scaling detection to many categories; insights likely to influence future work.  
- Key questions for authors: sensitivity to k/neighbor metric and cases where per-class adaptation hurts; performance vs |B|; value of class-agnostic bbox regressor; including classification loss on A during fine-tuning; exact background sampling/IoU protocol.  
- Pros for acceptance: important scaling problem, clear decomposition and ablations, good error analysis, released code/models, efficiency considerations.  
- Cons for acceptance: missing comparisons to plausible baselines, limited robustness analyses, and lack of quantitative large-scale validation.  
- Overall assessment: solid and impactful contribution with compelling empirical gains despite missing comparisons and robustness analyses.
