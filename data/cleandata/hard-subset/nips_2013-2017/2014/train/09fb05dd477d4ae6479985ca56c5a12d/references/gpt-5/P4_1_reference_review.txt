Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to transform image classifiers into object detectors with limited bounding box supervision. The core idea is to view classification-to-detection as domain adaptation: use detection data for a subset of categories (set B) to learn a generic, category-invariant transformation of a CNN from classification to detection, then transfer this transformation to categories without detection data (set A). Concretely: (1) pretrain an AlexNet-style CNN on ImageNet classification, (2) replace the final layer with K category classifiers and fine-tune on classification data, (3) fine-tune layers 1–7 plus a background class on detection data for categories in B, yielding a generic detection feature space and background modeling, and (4) for categories in A, approximate category-specific adaptation by adding to each classification weight vector the average “delta” learned for its k nearest neighbors in B (nearest neighbors in the L2-normalized fc8 classification space). At test time, the per-class score is the class logit minus the background logit; no SVMs or bbox regression are trained for speed and to enable transfer.

On ILSVRC13 detection, the authors simulate the low-annotation regime by holding out detection boxes for 100 of 200 categories. Compared to applying a classification network directly to proposals, LSDA improves mAP on held-out categories by about 50% relative (≈10.31% to 16.15% mAP), with most gains due to the category-invariant fine-tuning and background class; category-specific fc8 adaptation adds a smaller but statistically significant increment. The method is further demonstrated qualitatively by training a 7,604-category detector with detection labels for 200 classes and classification-only labels for the rest. The approach builds on R-CNN [1], extending it by transferring detection capability to unseen categories via domain adaptation; it also relates to MIL-based weakly supervised detection [6,7] and domain adaptation methods [12–22], but differs by adapting to categories with no detection labels.

Quality
- Soundness: The training procedure is clear and adheres to established practices (pretraining, fine-tuning, region proposals). The category-invariant and category-specific adaptation are well-defined. The subtraction of a learned background score is a reasonable approximation to detection likelihoods.
- Empirical support: The ablation study decomposes contributions: background class and feature adaptation (layers 1–7) provide the bulk of the gains; fc8 neighbor-averaged deltas (k=10) add a modest but statistically significant boost (p=0.017). Error analysis shows reductions in background and localization errors after adaptation, supporting the stated mechanism.
- Baselines and comparisons: The classification-only baseline is appropriate. However, additional baselines would strengthen the case: (a) training linear SVMs on fc7 features with hard negatives (R-CNN-style) for held-out classes to test whether background modeling alone explains gains; (b) pseudo-labeling or MIL baselines on the same 100/100 split; (c) simple calibration methods or background-subtraction ablations. Bounding box regression is omitted for speed; reporting its effect on B classes and whether it transfers to A (likely not) would contextualize the trade-off.
- Experimental design: The 100/100 alphabetical split is reasonable; sensitivity to m (number of classes with detection labels), to k (neighbors), and to the neighbor metric would be informative. Reporting performance on B classes after adaptation (to ensure no degradation) would complete the picture. Absolute mAP on held-out classes remains low, as expected for weak supervision, but the relative gains are meaningful.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and organized. The sets A and B are clearly defined; the training pipeline is described step-by-step; implementation details (Caffe, data splits, training times) are provided. Equation (1) is clear. Minor issues: a few typos and formatting glitches; a brief justification for subtracting background scores (e.g., softmax logit interpretation) would aid clarity. Figures (referenced) appear to support claims; ensuring reproducibility would benefit from hyperparameter details (learning rates, iterations, region proposal settings, NMS parameters).

Originality
- The key novelty lies in adapting a detection feature space learned from a subset of categories to unseen categories, and in the simple neighbor-averaged delta for category-specific outputs. While fine-tuning for detection (R-CNN) and domain adaptation are known, using them to transfer detection capability across categories without any detection labels is a new combination at the time. The emphasis on learning a background class and the finding that category-invariant adaptation dominates are useful insights.

Significance
- The approach addresses a practical bottleneck: scaling detection beyond a few hundred categories without massive bounding-box annotation. The relative mAP gains on held-out categories, reduced training time, and qualitative 7.6K-class results indicate utility. Although the absolute accuracy lags fully supervised detectors, the method offers a path to large-scale detection with limited annotation. This is likely to be of interest to both researchers and practitioners and can be built upon (e.g., with MIL or improved architectures).

Strengths
- Clear formulation of classification-to-detection as domain adaptation; principled use of background class.
- Careful ablation demonstrating where gains arise; statistical testing.
- Practical and scalable; code/models released; faster variant with SPP.
- Demonstrated large-scale applicability (7.6K categories).

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines; no direct comparison to contemporary weakly supervised detection methods on the same benchmark/split.
- Category-specific adaptation is heuristic (nearest neighbors in fc8 space); sensitivity analyses are limited.
- No bbox regression; absolute mAP remains modest.
- Evaluation restricted to one dataset/split; limited analysis of how performance scales with number of categories with detection labels.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines with SVMs on fc7 (with/without background negatives) and a MIL/weakly supervised method on the same split.
- Provide sensitivity to k and to neighbor metrics (cosine vs L2; using intermediate features).
- Report performance on B classes pre/post adaptation and with bbox regression (for context).
- Explore simple calibration or temperature scaling to improve score combination with background.
- Analyze dependence on m (e.g., 25/175, 50/150, 150/50 splits).

Pro/con for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses an important scalability problem with a simple, effective method.
- Solid empirical gains with clear ablations and error analysis.
- Breadth demonstrated via large-scale detector.
- Well-written and reproducible.

Cons:
- Comparisons to alternative weakly supervised/transfer baselines are limited.
- Heuristic category-specific transfer with modest gains and limited analysis.
- Absolute performance is low without bbox regression; single-dataset evaluation.

Recommendation
The paper makes a clear, technically sound contribution with practical significance and adequate novelty for NeurIPS. Despite some missing comparisons and analyses, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. Suitable for acceptance after addressing the suggested additions where feasible.
