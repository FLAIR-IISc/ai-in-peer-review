Summary
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to convert image classifiers into object detectors when bounding box annotations are available for only a subset of categories. The approach fine-tunes a CNN (AlexNet) on detection data for m “base” categories to learn (i) a category-invariant transformation of the feature extractor and (ii) a background class. For the remaining K−m “held-out” categories, LSDA transfers category-specific changes by averaging the output-layer weight deltas of the nearest neighbor base classes (measured by Euclidean distance between L2-normalized classifier weights). At test time, detections are produced by scoring region proposals using the adapted network and subtracting the background score. On ILSVRC13, with 100 categories having detection labels and 100 held out, LSDA improves mAP on the held-out set from 10.31% (classification-only baseline on proposals) to 16.15% (+50% relative), with most gains credited to the category-invariant adaptation and background modeling. The paper includes an error analysis (reduced localization and background confusions) and a demonstration of a 7.6K-category detector using only 200 detection classes plus 7.4K classification-only classes.

Relation to prior work
LSDA builds on R-CNN by using region proposals and CNN fine-tuning, but differs in that it does not require detection annotations for all classes; instead, it transfers knowledge to unseen classes. Compared to MIL-based weakly supervised detection, LSDA leverages a small supervised detection subset to learn a background model and a generic detection feature space, then transfers to others without MIL. In domain adaptation literature, prior supervised detector adaptation typically assumes labeled detection data for all categories; here, adaptation is across tasks (classification→detection) and across categories, with no detection labels for held-out categories. The method is simple and pragmatic rather than a formal DA objective.

Strengths
- Addresses an important scalability bottleneck: lack of bounding boxes for many classes; demonstrates a practical path to large-scale detection.
- Conceptually clean decomposition into category-invariant (feature) and category-specific (output-layer) transfer, with convincing ablation showing most gains come from the former and from explicitly learning a background class.
- Solid experimental setup on ILSVRC13 with a clear held-out protocol; statistically significant improvements; informative error analysis.
- Reproducibility and impact: implementation in Caffe, released models, demonstration at 7.6K classes, and a faster SPP-based variant.

Weaknesses and questions
- Baseline strength. The classification-only baseline on proposals is weak relative to alternatives that could be trained with the same supervision: e.g., train linear SVMs on fc7 features of the detection-finetuned network for base classes and transfer calibration to held-out ones; or apply weakly supervised/MIL baselines on held-out categories (even if not optimized for ImageNet scale, a representative comparison would contextualize LSDA’s gains). A stronger “upper-middle” baseline would clarify how much of the gain comes uniquely from LSDA vs generic feature adaptation.
- Category-specific transfer is heuristic. Averaging nearest-neighbor weight deltas in fc8 space is intuitive but ad hoc. Alternatives such as learning a linear mapping from Wc to Wd on base classes, using semantic similarity (WordNet/attributes), or metric learning might yield more robust transfer. The choice of k and the similarity metric’s effect are only lightly explored.
- Split bias. Using an alphabetical split of categories raises concerns about semantic bias. Please report sensitivity to different random splits, and analyze performance as a function of semantic/visual distance between held-out classes and their nearest base neighbors.
- Calibration and scoring. It is unclear whether “scorei − scorebackground” operates on logits or softmax probabilities; subtracting softmax probabilities is not equivalent to a log-odds difference and may distort calibration across classes. Some discussion or evidence that this simple calibration suffices (vs. Platt scaling or temperature scaling using base classes) would strengthen the claims.
- Upper bound and components. The gap to the fully supervised 200-class detector remains large. Bounding box regression and SVMs are omitted for speed and due to lack of labels on held-out classes; however, incorporating these for base classes while retaining end-to-end scoring for held-out classes could quantify the trade-off and the extent to which LSDA benefits from better localization.
- Generalization scope. The method’s success likely depends on base classes covering visual modes of held-out classes. An analysis of performance vs. neighbor distance or supercategory overlap would clarify how far LSDA can extrapolate.
- Proposal dependence. Results are shown with Selective Search; given the claimed fast variant, it would be useful to quantify LSDA’s sensitivity to different proposal methods and proposal recall.

Quality
The technical approach is sound and well-supported by ablations and error analysis. The category-invariant adaptation and background modeling are clearly important, and the small but significant gain from category-specific transfer is documented. Some experimental choices (baseline strength, split strategy, calibration details) limit conclusiveness but do not undermine the core claim.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. A few implementation details are missing or could be clarified: learning rates/schedules for the two stages, precise definition of “scorei − scorebackground,” and details on nearest-neighbor computation (distance on raw vs. normalized weights is mentioned, but not whether biases are included).

Originality
While built on R-CNN and standard fine-tuning, the idea of learning a classifier→detector transformation on a subset of categories and transferring it to unseen categories is novel and timely for large-scale detection. The nearest-neighbor delta transfer is simple rather than theoretically novel.

Significance
The problem is important and the demonstrated gains, though modest in absolute terms, are practically meaningful and scalable. The release of a 7.6K detector underscores potential impact and likely stimulates follow-up work.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are results to the choice of k and to different similarity measures (e.g., cosine in fc7 feature space, semantic distances)?
- Do the improvements persist with different random splits rather than alphabetical?
- Are scores subtracted as logits or probabilities? Did you try simple calibration on base classes?
- Can you report per-class gains vs. neighbor distance to base classes?
- Could a learned linear mapping from Wc to Wd outperform k-NN delta averaging?

Pros
- Addresses a key scalability bottleneck; practical and effective.
- Clear decomposition and ablations; error analysis is insightful.
- Reproducible; released models/code; large-scale demo.

Cons
- Baselines could be stronger; no MIL/WSD comparison on held-out classes.
- Category-specific transfer is heuristic; limited exploration of alternatives.
- Potential split bias; limited analysis of generalization breadth.
