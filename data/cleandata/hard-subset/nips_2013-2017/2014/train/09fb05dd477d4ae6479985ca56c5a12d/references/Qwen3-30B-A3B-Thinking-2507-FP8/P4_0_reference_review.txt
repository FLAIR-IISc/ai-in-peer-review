

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

## Summary
This paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), an algorithm that transforms image classifiers into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The authors frame the problem as domain adaptation, where the source domain consists of image classification data (with image-level labels) and the target domain consists of detection data (with bounding boxes). The key insight is that a transformation from classification to detection can be learned on a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations and then applied to all other categories. The method involves three components: category invariant adaptation (fine-tuning network layers using detection data for a subset of categories), category specific adaptation (using nearest neighbors to approximate fine-tuning for categories without detection data), and detection with LSDA (combining category scores with background scores). The authors evaluate their approach on the ImageNet detection dataset, demonstrating a 50% relative mAP improvement over a classification-only baseline and showing the feasibility of a 7,604-category detector.

The work relates to previous NIPS contributions in several ways. It builds on R-CNN [1], which demonstrated the importance of fine-tuning for adapting classification features to detection but required bounding box annotations for all categories. It also extends domain adaptation methods [12-16] by adapting not just between visual domains but also from classification to detection tasks. The paper distinguishes itself from MIL-based methods [6,7] that use weak labels but haven't been evaluated on large-scale detection challenges, and from other domain adaptation approaches [17-22] that require labeled detection data for all categories.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with thorough experimental evaluation. The ablation study (Figure 3, Table 1) clearly demonstrates the contribution of each component, and the statistical significance testing (p=0.017) adds rigor to the claims. The implementation details are sufficient for reproducibility, and the authors provide code and models publicly. The method effectively addresses the problem of scaling detection to large numbers of categories without requiring bounding box annotations for all.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The main ideas are explained logically, and the figures (particularly Figures 2-4) effectively illustrate the key concepts. The experimental setup is detailed in Section 4.1, and the results are presented in a way that makes it easy to understand the contributions of each component of the method.

**Originality:** The core idea of adapting classifiers to detectors for held-out categories with no bounding box annotations is novel. The authors clearly position their work against related approaches in the literature review, showing that their method is the first to address this specific problem. The combination of category invariant and category specific adaptation techniques represents a meaningful contribution to the field.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a critical practical limitation in object detection: the difficulty of collecting bounding box annotations for large numbers of categories. The 50% relative mAP improvement is significant, and the demonstration of a 7,604-category detector shows the potential impact of the method. The approach has clear practical implications for scaling detection systems to human-level category coverage.

## Weaknesses

**Quality:** While the paper is technically sound, it doesn't sufficiently explore the limitations of the approach. For example, the authors don't investigate how the method performs when the number of categories with bounding box annotations (m) is very small (e.g., m=1 or m=5). The nearest neighbor approach for category specific adaptation (Equation 1) could be sensitive to the choice of k, but the paper only tests a limited range of values without discussing how to select k optimally for different scenarios.

**Clarity:** The explanation of "category invariant adaptation" and "category specific adaptation" could be clearer. The distinction between these two components isn't immediately obvious from the text, and a more detailed explanation of why these two separate adaptations are necessary would strengthen the paper.

**Originality:** While the core idea is novel, the paper could better distinguish its approach from recent work on weakly supervised object detection. The authors mention MIL-based methods but don't fully address how their approach differs from these methods beyond the scale of the evaluation.

**Significance:** The paper demonstrates significant improvements on held-out categories, but it doesn't thoroughly address how the method would scale to even larger numbers of categories (e.g., 100,000+). The 7,604-category detector is impressive, but the paper doesn't discuss potential challenges with such a large-scale system.

## Recommendation

**Pro acceptance:** The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by solving a critical problem in scaling object detection to large numbers of categories. The method is technically sound, well-evaluated, and has clear practical implications. The 50% relative mAP improvement is substantial, and the demonstration of a 7,604-category detector represents a major step toward human-level detection capability.

**Con acceptance:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough exploration of the method's limitations, particularly regarding the sensitivity to the number of categories with bounding box annotations and the choice of k for nearest neighbors. The distinction between the two adaptation components could be clearer.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of object detection with clear technical merit, novelty, and significance. The method has the potential to significantly impact how large-scale detection systems are developed. I recommend acceptance.
