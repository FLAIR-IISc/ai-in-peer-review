This paper proposes LSDA, an algorithm for large-scale object detection that leverages classifiers trained on image-level labels to adapt to the detection task, requiring bounding box annotations for only a subset of categories. The core idea is to formulate detection as a domain adaptation problem, transforming classifiers into detectors. The approach fine-tunes layers of a CNN using labeled detection data and then utilizes nearest-neighbor relationships to transfer knowledge to categories without bounding box labels. The authors demonstrate a 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline using raw classifier weights on ImageNet, and showcase detection on a 7604-category detector.

This work builds upon the recent trend of leveraging pre-trained classification networks for detection (Girshick et al. [1]), and extends it to a significantly larger scale. It’s related to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approaches [6, 7] that utilize weak labels, but focuses on a different adaptation strategy. The paper also draws connections to domain adaptation methods [12-16], framing the classification-to-detection shift as a domain adaptation problem. Compared to supervised domain adaptation methods for detection [17-22], LSDA uniquely addresses the scenario of adapting to held-out categories *without* any detection data.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core idea of learning a general transformation from classification to detection, and applying it to unseen categories, is a novel contribution. Addressing the scarcity of bounding box annotations is a crucial problem for scaling up detection systems.
*   **Significant Improvement:** The reported 50% relative mAP improvement over the baseline is substantial and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   **Scalability:**  Demonstrating detection on 7604 categories is impressive and showcases the potential of LSDA for large-scale scenarios. The authors also provide an accelerated version of their pipeline, making it more practical.
*   **Thorough Experiments:** The ablation study in Section 4 provides clear insights into the contribution of each component of the algorithm. The error analysis helps understand the limitations and potential improvements.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the method is clearly explained, with a helpful diagram (Figure 2) illustrating the adaptation process. Code and models are publicly available.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the baseline of using raw classifier weights is a reasonable starting point, a more direct comparison to state-of-the-art weakly supervised detection methods (e.g., recent MIL approaches) on ImageNet detection would strengthen the paper. A detailed comparison table would be valuable.
*   **Nearest Neighbor Selection:** The method for selecting nearest neighbors in the output layer adaptation (Section 3.2) seems somewhat arbitrary. The impact of *k* (number of neighbors) is mentioned but not thoroughly analyzed. A more principled approach or sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
*   **Reliance on ImageNet:** The experiments are entirely based on ImageNet. While a strong benchmark, it would be valuable to demonstrate the generalizability of LSDA to other datasets or domains.
*   **Error Analysis Depth:** While the error analysis is a good start, a more detailed qualitative analysis of common failure cases (especially for the held-out categories) could provide further insights. The categorization of errors (Loc, BG, Oth) seems high-level.
*   **Ambiguity in "Sim" Label in Figure 5:** The "(sim)" label in Figure 5 is not explained and is confusing.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in object detection: the scarcity of bounding box annotations.
*   The proposed LSDA algorithm demonstrates a substantial improvement over a reasonable baseline.
*   The work is novel and makes a valuable contribution to the field.
*   The authors provide clear explanations, thorough experiments, and publicly available code.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The comparison to state-of-the-art weakly supervised detection methods is limited.
*   The nearest neighbor selection strategy could be improved.
*   The reliance on ImageNet limits the generalizability assessment.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to large-scale object detection. The core idea of adapting classifiers to detectors is well-motivated and the experimental results are promising. While the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to recent weakly supervised methods and a more detailed analysis of the nearest neighbor selection strategy, the significant improvement and scalability of LSDA make it a valuable contribution to the field.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Include a more detailed comparison to state-of-the-art weakly supervised detection methods on the ImageNet detection benchmark.
2.  Provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of the *k* parameter in the nearest neighbor selection strategy.
3.  Clarify the "(sim)" label in Figure 5.
4. Consider adding a discussion about the limitations of the approach, and potential future research directions.
