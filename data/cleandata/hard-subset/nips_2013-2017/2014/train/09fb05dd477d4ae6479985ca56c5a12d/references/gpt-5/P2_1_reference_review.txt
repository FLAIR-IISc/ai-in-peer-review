Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to convert image classifiers into object detectors when only a subset of categories have bounding box annotations. The approach treats classification-to-detection as domain adaptation. It comprises: (1) category-invariant adaptation by fine-tuning shared CNN layers (conv and fc) on detection data for m classes and learning a background class; and (2) category-specific adaptation by estimating how fc8 weights would have changed during detection fine-tuning and transferring this change (delta) to held-out classes via averaging deltas from nearest-neighbor classes (measured in the fc8 space of the classification network). At test time, region proposals are scored using the adapted network, combining category and background logits.

Experiments on ILSVRC2013 simulate 100 classes with detection data (B) and 100 held-out classes (A). Starting from a classifier applied to region proposals, category-invariant adaptation (fine-tuning layers 1–7 and adding a background class) improves held-out mAP from 10.31% to 15.85%; adding category-specific adaptation via k-NN deltas gives 16.15% (p=0.017). Error analysis shows reductions in background confusion and localization errors. The authors also demonstrate a 7,604-class detector (200 with boxes, 7,404 without) and a faster SPP-based variant.

This builds directly on R-CNN (which needs boxes for all categories) and complements weakly supervised/MIL methods, which the authors note had not been evaluated at ImageNet scale. Compared to prior domain adaptation work (feature/model adaptation), LSDA adapts across task (classification→detection) and to held-out categories without detection labels, which appears novel at the time.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The methodology is reasonable and well-supported empirically. The key claim—that most of the benefit comes from learning a background class and adapting shared representation, with smaller gains from category-specific transfer—is backed by an ablation and significance testing. The nearest-neighbor delta transfer is a simple, justifiable heuristic.
- Experimental design: The evaluation protocol mirrors R-CNN practice (val split into val1/val2, augmentation with training images). The baselines include classifier-only on proposals and variants of adaptation; an upper bound with detection labels for all classes is provided. However, some comparisons are missing: there is no head-to-head with weakly supervised detectors (even on PASCAL for overlapping classes), and no comparison to alternative category-specific mappings (e.g., learning a regression from classification fc8 weights to detection fc8 weights, or metric choices beyond L2 on normalized weights). It would also help to quantify sensitivity to k and to show per-class improvements, particularly for dissimilar held-out categories.
- Completeness: The paper is a complete empirical study for the stated goal, with error analysis and a large-scale demonstration. Some training details (learning rates, iteration schedules, precise choice of k, and calibration of background subtraction) could be more explicit for full reproducibility, though code/models are released.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The A/B notation and the two adaptation components are explained cleanly. Figures (ablation, error breakdown) and references are appropriate. Suggestions for clarity: explicitly state the selected k in the main text, detail hyperparameters for fine-tuning, and justify the decision to use score_i − score_background (e.g., log-odds interpretation) versus softmax probabilities or calibrated scores.

Originality:
Moderately high. While using CNN fine-tuning for detection (R-CNN) and domain adaptation are established, adapting from classification to detection with zero detection labels for held-out categories, and explicitly learning a background model plus a transferable delta in the classifier layer, is novel and practically motivated. The nearest-neighbor delta transfer is simple but new in this context.

Significance:
High practical significance. The approach addresses a key bottleneck—bounding box annotation scarcity—by enabling detectors for thousands of classes using detection labels for only a subset. The demonstrated 7.6K-class model and released code suggest real-world utility. Methodologically, the finding that category-invariant adaptation and background modeling drive most gains is informative for subsequent work. Absolute detection performance is not SOTA (no SVM/bbox regression), but the contribution is orthogonal: how to scale detectors when labels are scarce.

Strengths:
- Clear formulation of classification→detection adaptation with two complementary components.
- Strong ablation showing where the gains come from; statistically significant improvements on held-out classes (+50% relative mAP).
- Insightful error analysis showing reductions in background and localization errors.
- Scalable demonstration (7.6K categories) and faster variant with SPP; released models/code.
- Simple, efficient training (no SVMs/bbox regression), large reduction in training time.

Weaknesses:
- Category-specific adaptation is heuristic; limited exploration of alternative mappings/metrics or learned transfer functions.
- Missing comparisons to weakly supervised MIL methods on a shared benchmark (even if not at ImageNet scale).
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to k, to neighbor selection quality, and performance on fine-grained or dissimilar classes.
- Some reproducibility details are light (hyperparameters, calibration of scores).

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- How sensitive are results to k and to the neighbor metric? Does cosine similarity outperform L2 on normalized weights?
- Have you tried learning a linear mapping from W^c to W^d (e.g., ridge regression trained on B) instead of k-NN averaging?
- Can you report per-class gains and correlation with semantic/visual similarity to neighbors?
- Could simple calibration (temperature scaling or Platt scaling) further improve the combination with background?
- A small-scale comparison to a MIL baseline on PASCAL would strengthen the positioning.

Decision: Weak accept.
Rationale: The paper tackles an important problem with a simple, well-validated approach, demonstrates clear gains on held-out categories, and offers practical impact at scale. While some comparisons and deeper analyses are missing, the contribution is sufficiently novel and useful to merit acceptance.
