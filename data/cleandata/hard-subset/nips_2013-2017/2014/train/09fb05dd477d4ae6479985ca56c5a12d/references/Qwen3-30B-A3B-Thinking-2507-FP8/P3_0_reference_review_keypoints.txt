- Proposes LSDA to convert image classifiers into object detectors using bounding-box data from a subset of categories.  
- Frames the problem as domain adaptation and uses a three-stage process: category-invariant fine-tuning, category-specific nearest-neighbor adaptation, and detection.  
- Demonstrates large empirical gains (~50% relative mAP) on ImageNet and a 7,604-category detector, with a fast implementation (0.5s/image).  
- Tackles an important scalability problem by reducing the need for bounding-box annotations across many categories.  
- Methodology is clear, well-motivated, and distinguishes category-invariant vs. category-specific adaptation.  
- Strong experimental validation and ablation studies showing the contribution of each component.  
- Practical impact shown by a large-scale detector and publicly released code/models.  
- Well-written and organized, enabling reproducibility.  
- Compares primarily to R-CNN and an oracle; lacks comparisons to newer detectors (Faster R-CNN, YOLO, etc.).  
- Nearest-neighbor category-specific adaptation is ambiguously described and lacks practical details and edge-case handling.  
- Limited analysis of failure modes, especially when nearest neighbors are poor matches.  
- No discussion of computational scaling for nearest-neighbor search at extreme category counts (e.g., 100k).  
- Lacks theoretical justification for why classification feature-space nearest neighbors should transfer to detection.  
- Suggests adding comparisons to modern detectors, clarifying nearest-neighbor selection and k, and analyzing edge cases.  
- Recommends expanding error analysis, discussing scaling/approximate NN methods, and adding theoretical motivation.  
- Overall assessment: strong, novel, practically impactful work that advances transfer learning for detection.  
- Recommendation: accept, with weaknesses to address in the final version.  
- Final thought: LSDA is an important step toward scalable object detection by reducing annotation requirements and enabling large-category detectors.
