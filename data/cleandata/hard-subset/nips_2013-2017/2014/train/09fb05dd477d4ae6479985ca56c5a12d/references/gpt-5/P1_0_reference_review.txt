Summary
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to transform an image classifier into an object detector when only a subset of categories have bounding box annotations. The task is framed as domain adaptation from classification (source domain: image-level labels) to detection (target domain: region-level labels). The approach has three components: (1) learning to recognize background by fine-tuning a background class, (2) category-invariant adaptation by fine-tuning shared layers (1–7 of AlexNet) on detection data for a subset B of classes, and (3) category-specific adaptation by transferring per-class parameter changes learned on B to held-out classes A via nearest-neighbor averaging in the classification fc8 space. The method yields a 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories in ILSVRC13 detection (val2), compared to using a classifier directly on region proposals. The authors also demonstrate scalability by producing a 7.6K-category detector (qualitative results) and report a faster variant using SPP and fast proposals.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on R-CNN, which pretrains on classification and fine-tunes on detection but requires bounding boxes for all categories. Unlike MIL-based weakly supervised detection methods, LSDA leverages a small set of fully annotated detection categories to learn a generic classification-to-detection transformation that transfers to unseen categories, addressing a different regime. Compared to classic domain adaptation (adaptive SVMs, feature transforms), LSDA jointly adapts the deep feature extractor and the task (classification→detection), and crucially adapts to categories without target annotations, which most supervised DA methods do not cover.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important question: how to scale detection to thousands of categories without collecting bounding boxes for all of them.
- Clear decomposition of the adaptation problem: background modeling, category-invariant feature adaptation, and category-specific output adaptation.
- Simple and implementable: leverages standard CNN fine-tuning and a lightweight nearest-neighbor transfer at the output layer.
- Solid ablation: shows the dominant gain comes from shared-layer fine-tuning and background class; per-class adaptation adds a smaller but statistically significant improvement.
- Useful error analysis demonstrating reductions in background confusion and localization errors after adaptation.
- Efficiency: avoids SVM/bbox regression to reduce training time from days to hours; provides a faster inference variant; releases code/models.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Limited baselines. While R-CNN is used as an upper bound, there is no comparison against alternative transfer strategies for the held-out classes (e.g., class-agnostic bbox regressors trained on B and applied to all classes; multi-task fine-tuning that includes a classification loss on A during detection fine-tuning; or simple linear SVMs on region features). Including one or two such baselines would strengthen claims.
- Heuristic output-layer transfer. Nearest neighbors in fc8 parameter space is reasonable, but the paper lacks sensitivity analyses: k’s effect beyond a single best value, cosine vs Euclidean distance, or alternative neighbor spaces (fc7, semantic/WordNet similarity, or confusions). Reporting these would clarify robustness.
- Dependence on number of annotated categories. It would be informative to plot performance on A as a function of |B| to quantify label-efficiency and potential diminishing returns.
- Statistical robustness and variance. Most results appear from a single split/run; only one p-value is reported. Please include variance across multiple random seeds and, if feasible, across different A/B splits.
- Large-scale (7.6K) results are qualitative. Even a partial quantitative proxy (e.g., for categories overlapping with detection, few-shot evaluation, or human verification on a subset) would substantiate scalability claims.
- Evaluation scope. All quantitative results are on ILSVRC val2, with val1 used for detection fine-tuning. Reporting on the test server or another dataset (e.g., held-out VOC classes) would strengthen generalization claims.
- Missing bbox regression analysis. A class-agnostic regressor trained on B might further reduce localization errors for all classes without per-class labels; testing this would be valuable.
- Implementation details. More specifics would help reproducibility: background sampling strategy and IoU thresholds, positives/negatives per minibatch, hard-negative mining, fine-tuning hyperparameters (learning rates per layer group), and the exact region proposal settings.

Quality
Technically sound and well-supported by experiments and ablations given the scope. The main claims—substantial gains on held-out categories through shared-layer adaptation and background modeling—are convincingly demonstrated. Some baselines and robustness analyses are missing, and large-scale claims rely on qualitative evidence.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The three components of LSDA are explained crisply. Figures and the error analysis aid understanding. Please clarify the precise A/B split protocol, neighbor selection metric, and provide more training details as noted above.

Originality
Moderate to strong. While fine-tuning for detection is known (R-CNN), adapting to unseen categories via a learned classification-to-detection transformation is novel and practically motivated. The explicit background modeling as part of the task adaptation and the per-class delta transfer are new combinations in this context.

Significance
High practical significance. The method reduces dependence on bounding boxes and enables scaling to thousands of categories, a problem of broad interest. The insights—especially the importance of background modeling and category-invariant adaptation—are likely to influence subsequent work.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive is performance on A to k and the neighbor metric/space? Any cases where per-class adaptation hurts?
- How does performance on A change as |B| varies (e.g., 25/50/100 classes)?
- Can a class-agnostic bbox regressor trained on B help A?
- Did you try including a classification loss on A during detection fine-tuning to preserve classification performance while adapting features?
- What is the exact background sampling protocol and IoU thresholds?

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses an important scaling challenge with a simple, effective method.
- Clear decomposition and ablation demonstrating where gains come from.
- Good error analysis; code/models released; efficiency considerations.

Cons:
- Missing comparisons to a few plausible baselines/variants.
- Limited robustness analyses and quantitative validation at 7.6K scale.
- Evaluation confined to a single dataset split.

Overall assessment
A solid, impactful contribution that advances scalable object detection by adapting classifiers to detectors for unseen categories. Despite some missing comparisons and robustness analyses, the core idea and empirical gains are compelling.
