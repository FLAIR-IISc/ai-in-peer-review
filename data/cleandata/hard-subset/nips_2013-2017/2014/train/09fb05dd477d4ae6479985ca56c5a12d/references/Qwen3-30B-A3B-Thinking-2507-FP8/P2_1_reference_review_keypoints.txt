- Proposes LSDA: transform image classifiers into object detectors via domain adaptation from image-level labels (source) to bounding-box detection (target).  
- Uses a three-stage approach: train classifier, fine-tune layers 1–7 on detection data for subset B, and adapt category-specific parameters for remaining categories via nearest neighbors.  
- Demonstrates a 50% relative mAP improvement over a classification-only baseline on ImageNet detection.  
- Builds on R-CNN ideas but requires detection data for only a subset of categories.  
- Technically sound and well-executed with reproducible implementation; code and models released.  
- Thorough ablation study shows category-invariant adaptation (fine-tuning layers 1–7) gives the largest gain; category-specific nearest-neighbor adaptation gives a smaller but significant boost.  
- Error analysis shows LSDA reduces localization and background confusion errors for held-out categories.  
- Evaluation limited to ImageNet detection and lacks comparisons to more recent detectors (Fast/Faster/Mask R-CNN).  
- Does not discuss computational cost or scalability of the nearest-neighbor approach when the set B is large.  
- Limited analysis on performance for categories very dissimilar to those in set B.  
- Some technical details (e.g., handling ties in nearest-neighbor matching) are under-specified.  
- Connection to broader domain adaptation literature could be strengthened with explicit comparisons to related works (e.g., Hoffman et al.).  
- Novel application of domain adaptation to scale detection; nearest-neighbor transfer of category-specific adaptation is a fresh idea.  
- Method builds on existing techniques (R-CNN, domain adaptation) and could better distinguish itself from prior transfer approaches (e.g., Tabula Rasa).  
- Significant practical contribution: shows potential to enable detection for thousands of categories and demonstrates a 7,604-category detector.  
- Unclear whether LSDA will scale effectively to very large category counts (e.g., 100,000+) or match fully supervised state-of-the-art detectors.  
- Overall assessment: solid, impactful work with clear improvements and reproducibility — recommendation: accept.  
- Pro-acceptance: addresses a major annotation bottleneck, novel adaptation framing, strong empirical gains, thorough ablations, released code/models.  
- Con-acceptance: limited comparisons to recent SOTA, missing scalability and computational-cost analysis, insufficient distinction from some related transfer methods.
