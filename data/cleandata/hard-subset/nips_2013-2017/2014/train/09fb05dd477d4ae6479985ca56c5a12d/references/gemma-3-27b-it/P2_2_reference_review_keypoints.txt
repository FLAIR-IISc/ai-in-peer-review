- Summary: Proposes LSDA, a domain-adaptation method that converts image classifiers into object detectors to scale detection without bounding-box annotations, showing ~50% relative mAP gain on held-out ImageNet categories.

- Strength: Addresses a crucial bottleneck by reducing dependence on expensive bounding-box annotations for large-scale detection.

- Strength: Strong experimental results with substantial mAP improvement and a useful ablation study.

- Strength: Clear, well-structured presentation with helpful figures explaining the LSDA pipeline.

- Strength: Novel combination of domain adaptation to adapt classifiers into detectors for categories without detection data.

- Strength: Code and models are publicly released, aiding reproducibility.

- Weakness: Limited quantitative comparisons to related methods (e.g., MIL-based approaches) on large-scale datasets.

- Weakness: Heavy dependence on pre-trained ImageNet classifiers; sensitivity to the choice and quality of those models is not fully explored.

- Weakness: Category-specific adaptation via nearest neighbors (Euclidean distance on normalized fc8 weights) appears ad-hoc and lacks strong justification.

- Weakness: Sensitivity to the number of nearest neighbors (k) for adaptation is not thoroughly analyzed.

- Weakness: Simplified detection pipeline omits SVM training and bounding-box regression, which could trade off performance for speed and is not fully discussed.

- Weakness: Error analysis is useful but shallow; more detailed breakdowns and specific error examples are needed.

- Overall Assessment: Strong, well-motivated paper with compelling results that merit acceptance with minor revisions.

- Argument for acceptance: Solves an important problem, shows significant large-scale improvements, proposes a novel approach, and provides public code/models.

- Argument against acceptance: Lacks direct comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, relies on pre-trained classifiers, and uses a somewhat heuristic nearest-neighbor adaptation.

- Rating: Strong Accept (7/10); recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address comparisons and discuss limitations.
