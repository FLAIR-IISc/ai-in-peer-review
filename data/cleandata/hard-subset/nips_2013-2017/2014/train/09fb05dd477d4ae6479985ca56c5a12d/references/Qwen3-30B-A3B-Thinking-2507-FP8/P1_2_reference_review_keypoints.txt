- Introduces LSDA: adapts image classifiers into object detectors using few bounding-box labels and abundant image-level data.
- Frames classification-to-detection as a domain adaptation problem (classification domain → detection domain).
- Key technical insights: model background explicitly, transfer category-invariant feature changes, and account for category-specific differences.
- Uses nearest-neighbor approximation to transfer category-specific detector adjustments to held-out categories.
- Demonstrates a 50% relative mAP improvement over using raw classifier weights on object proposals.
- Builds a practical 7,604-category detector using bounding boxes for only 200 categories.
- Experiments on ILSVRC2013 with realistic simulated scarce-box settings and clear ablation studies (Figures/Tables).
- Shows reduced localization errors after adaptation and validates improvements with paired t-tests.
- Implementation details and model weights are publicly released for reproducibility.
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, and includes helpful visualizations aiding comprehension.
- Technically sound methodology with sufficient experimental rigor and analyses.
- Original contribution: first to adapt to held-out categories with no detection data, enabling large-scale detection scaling.
- Significant impact: reduces annotation burden and advances scalable detection toward thousands of categories.
- Weakness: nearest-neighbor adaptation may fail when neighbors don't share detection characteristics; lack of analysis on this failure rate.
- Weakness: limited comparisons to recent state-of-the-art detection methods beyond the baseline.
- Weakness: unclear phrasing about the "50% relative mAP improvement" could cause confusion about baseline vs. absolute improvement.
- Weakness: limited discussion/analysis of performance on small objects, heavy occlusion, or extreme viewpoint variation.
- Weakness: incomplete discussion of computational cost of the adaptation process.
- Minor clarity issue: ambiguous baseline for the claimed training time reduction (3 days → 5.5 hours).
- Recommendation: accept for NIPS due to technical novelty, experimental strength, and practical significance.
- Suggested improvements: clarify the 50% claim, analyze nearest-neighbor reliability, compare to at least one recent SOTA method, discuss challenging detection scenarios, and specify baseline for training-time claim.
