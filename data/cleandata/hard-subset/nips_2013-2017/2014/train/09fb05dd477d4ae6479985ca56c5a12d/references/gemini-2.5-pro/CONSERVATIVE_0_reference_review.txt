**Summary:**
This paper addresses the significant challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, for which bounding box annotations are prohibitively expensive to collect. The authors propose an algorithm, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), which learns to transform an image classifier into an object detector. The core idea is to leverage a small set of "source" categories with full bounding box supervision to learn a general, category-invariant transformation within a deep convolutional neural network. This learned transformation is then applied to adapt classifiers for "target" categories that have only image-level labels. The method involves three main steps: 1) fine-tuning the shared feature layers (1-7) of a pre-trained classification network on the source detection data to learn a generic shift from classification to detection (including a background model), 2) using this adapted feature representation for all categories, and 3) applying a category-specific adaptation to the final classifier layer, which for target categories is approximated by averaging the adaptations learned from their nearest neighbors in the source set. The approach is evaluated on the ILSVRC 2013 dataset, showing a 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories compared to using the original classifier weights directly for detection.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles a well-recognized and highly important bottleneck in computer vision: the scarcity of bounding box labels for training large-scale object detectors. The motivation to leverage abundant image-level classification data is strong and timely.
2.  **Clarity and Simplicity:** The proposed method is intuitive, clearly explained, and builds logically upon established work like R-CNN. The decomposition of the adaptation into category-invariant and category-specific components is sensible.
3.  **Thorough Ablation Study:** The authors provide a detailed ablation study (Table 1, Figure 3) that effectively demonstrates the contribution of each part of their method. The analysis showing that the category-invariant feature adaptation provides the largest performance boost is a valuable insight.
4.  **Insightful Error Analysis:** The error analysis in Figure 5 is a strong point of the paper. It compellingly shows that the proposed adaptation method specifically reduces localization and background confusion errors, which are precisely the key challenges that distinguish detection from classification. This provides good evidence that the method is learning the intended transformation.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Baselines:** The primary weakness of this work is the limited scope of its experimental comparison. The main baseline is a network trained only on classification data. While this is a necessary ablation to show the method's effectiveness, it is not a competitive baseline for the task. The paper explicitly mentions Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) methods as a promising complimentary approach for weakly supervised detection but fails to compare against them, stating they have not been evaluated on ImageNet. This is not a sufficient justification. For a contribution in this area, it is crucial to position the work relative to the established state-of-the-art in weakly-supervised or semi-supervised detection. Without such a comparison, the significance of the reported mAP is difficult to assess. A simple MIL baseline, even if not state-of-the-art, would have substantially strengthened the paper's claims.
2.  **Novelty of Components:** While the overall application is novel, the core technical components are largely based on existing techniques. The category-invariant adaptation is essentially the standard fine-tuning procedure introduced by R-CNN, with the key insight being its application to unseen classes. The category-specific adaptation relies on a simple k-NN heuristic based on Euclidean distance in the weight space of the final layer. This heuristic is not deeply justified and feels somewhat ad-hoc. Why is classifier weight similarity the correct proxy for adaptation similarity? The empirical gains from this specific step are also marginal (0.3 mAP). The contribution lies more in the clever recombination of existing ideas for a new problem setting rather than in a fundamental methodological innovation.
3.  **Remaining Performance Gap:** The paper demonstrates a significant *relative* improvement, but the *absolute* performance on held-out categories (16.15% mAP) remains far below the performance on fully supervised categories (23.32% mAP using the same pipeline, and even higher with a full R-CNN treatment). This substantial gap suggests that while the method is a promising step, the adapted detectors may not yet be of practical use for many applications, and the claim of "producing a high quality detector" is perhaps overstated.
4.  **Limited Generalization Study:** The experiments are conducted by splitting the 200 ILSVRC classes into two sets of 100. These classes come from the same domain and were curated for the same challenge. It is unclear how well the learned "generic transformation" would generalize if the source and target domains were more distinct (e.g., learning from PASCAL VOC and transferring to ImageNet categories). The current setup does not rigorously test the robustness of the category-invariant assumption.

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents a simple and intuitive method for a very important problem. The authors demonstrate that a generic transformation from a classifier to a detector can be learned and transferred to categories without bounding box labels, leading to significant improvements over a naive baseline. The strengths lie in the problem's significance, the clarity of the approach, and a solid set of ablation and error analyses that provide insight into the method's behavior.

However, the work is let down by a critical lack of comparison to established methods in the weakly-supervised detection literature. The methodological novelty is also limited, relying heavily on the standard fine-tuning paradigm of R-CNN combined with a simple heuristic for the final layer. While the results show promise, the large performance gap to fully supervised methods and the limited experimental scope raise questions about the immediate practical impact. The paper serves as a good proof-of-concept but lacks the rigorous comparison and methodological depth expected for a top-tier conference like NIPS. I am therefore leaning towards rejection but would reconsider if the authors could provide comparisons to stronger, relevant baselines.
