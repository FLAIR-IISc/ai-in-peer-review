Summary
The paper addresses the challenge of scaling object detection to thousands of categories when only a small subset has bounding box annotations. It proposes LSDA, a domain adaptation method that converts an image classifier into an object detector. The approach decomposes adaptation into: (1) learning a background class and fine-tuning shared layers (category-invariant adaptation) using detection data for a subset of categories B; (2) transferring category-specific changes from categories with detection labels to those without (A) by adding the average fc8 “delta” from k nearest neighbors in classifier weight space; and (3) using a background-adjusted score at test time (scorei − scorebg) over region proposals, with no SVMs or bbox regression. On ILSVRC13 detection, holding out detection labels for 100 of 200 categories, LSDA improves mAP on held-out classes from 10.31% to 16.15% (+50% relative), with ablations showing most gain comes from the category-invariant fine-tuning and learning a background class. The paper further demonstrates a 7,604-category detector by adapting 7,404 classifiers without boxes and provides a faster variant using SPP and fast proposals.

Relation to prior work
R-CNN showed fine-tuning from classification to detection yields large gains but requires bounding boxes for all categories. Weakly supervised MIL approaches learn detectors from image-level labels but had not been evaluated at ImageNet scale. Prior domain adaptation for detection typically adapts models across domains but assumes labeled detection data for each class. LSDA is distinct in adapting to categories with no detection annotations by leveraging the shared representation learned from a subset of classes and a simple category-specific transfer.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The methodology is straightforward, well-motivated, and consistent with known effects of fine-tuning for detection. The separation into category-invariant and category-specific adaptation is reasonable, and the nearest-neighbor delta transfer is a simple but plausible heuristic.
- Empirical support: The ablation study is informative: learning a background class and fine-tuning conv/fc layers (1–7) account for most gains; fc8 delta transfer adds a smaller but statistically significant boost. The error analysis is useful, showing reductions in localization and background confusions. The large-scale 7.6k demo is compelling, though mainly qualitative.
- Missing/unclear details: 
  - Sensitivity to k and the choice of distance for nearest neighbors (only k=10 reported; what about k=1, full average, cosine vs Euclidean on normalized weights?). 
  - Sensitivity to the number of labeled detection categories m (how do gains scale as m varies?). 
  - The background scoring scheme uses softmax and subtracts scorebg; a discussion of alternative calibrations (sigmoid per-class, Platt scaling) would help. 
  - No quantitative results for the 7.6k model (even proxy metrics or retrieval-style evaluation would be useful).
  - Comparison to a stronger baseline that adds SVMs and bbox regression only for B (and possibly applying bbox regression from B to A) would contextualize the trade-off between flexibility and accuracy.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The three insights and training pipeline are easy to follow. Figures illustrating qualitative improvements in localization and error breakdown are helpful. Some presentation issues could be improved: tighten Section 3 to more explicitly define the training losses, clarify how background ROIs are sampled, provide pseudo-code, and ensure equations are properly formatted. A table summarizing ablation settings and hyperparameters would aid reproducibility. Minor typos exist.

Originality
The core idea of adapting from classification to detection for unseen categories by learning a category-invariant transformation and transferring a category-specific delta is novel and was not present in prior domain adaptation or detection literature at the time. Leveraging classifier weight-space similarity to guide transfer is a simple, new design choice.

Significance
The problem is important: reducing dependence on bounding box labels is key to scaling detection. The demonstrated gains on held-out ImageNet classes and the 7.6k-category model suggest practical impact, and the released code/models increase utility. While absolute mAP remains far from fully supervised detection, the relative improvement is substantial in the weakly supervised regime, and the approach is likely to stimulate follow-up work on large-scale detection.

Pros
- Addresses a high-impact problem; practical path to large-scale detection.
- Clear decomposition into category-invariant and category-specific adaptation; strong empirical evidence that the former dominates.
- Statistically significant improvements over a well-defined baseline; informative error analysis.
- Simple, efficient method with released models and a large-scale demonstration.

Cons
- Category-specific transfer is heuristic (nearest neighbors in fc8 weight space) with limited analysis; no exploration of semantic similarity (e.g., WordNet) or learned mappings from Wc to Wd.
- Limited study of key sensitivities (k, distance metric, number of detection-labeled classes m).
- No quantitative evaluation of the 7.6k detector; cross-category NMS choices and calibration issues are underexplored.
- Does not investigate borrowing bbox regression or calibration from B to A.
- Comparison to stronger R-CNN variants (SVMs/bbox regression) is acknowledged but not quantified in a controlled setting.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide sensitivity analyses for k, neighbor metric, and semantic/attribute-based neighbors; consider learning a regression from Wc to Wd.
- Study performance as a function of m (e.g., m=25/50/100), including per-class variability and which categories benefit most.
- Explore transferring bbox regression from B to A and report its effect on localization errors.
- Calibrate scores for A vs B (sigmoids, temperature scaling) and analyze cross-category NMS.
- Add quantitative proxies for the 7.6k model (e.g., human-in-the-loop precision at top-K, or label transfer on a subset with boxes).

Overall recommendation: Accept (7/10)
The paper offers a clear, novel, and practically useful approach to scaling detection with limited box supervision, with solid empirical support and good clarity. While some analyses are missing and some components are heuristic, the contribution is significant and likely to influence subsequent work.
