Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to convert image classifiers into object detectors when bounding box annotations are available only for a subset of categories. The approach treats the transition from classification to detection as domain/task adaptation. It learns: (1) a category-invariant adaptation by fine-tuning all shared layers (conv and fc) on detection data for m “base” categories B and adding a background class, and (2) a category-specific adaptation by transferring the output-layer changes (delta between classifier and detector heads) from similar base categories to held-out categories A, using nearest neighbors in the classification weight space. At test time, per-region scores are computed with a background subtraction heuristic. Experiments on ILSVRC13 detection simulate the scenario with detection labels for 100 of 200 classes, showing a 50% relative mAP gain over applying a classification network to proposals; ablations attribute most gains to the category-invariant adaptation and background modeling. The authors further demonstrate a 7,604-class detector by adapting 7,404 classifiers with no boxes.

This work builds on R-CNN (fine-tuning with full detection supervision) and deep feature transfer. It differs from weakly supervised MIL-based detectors by leveraging a small set of detection labels to learn a generic classifier-to-detector transform, then transferring this transform to unseen classes. It also relates to model adaptation/domain adaptation literature, but unlike prior adaptive SVM/DPM approaches, it adapts both features and heads and transfers to categories with no detection data.

Strengths
- Problem importance: Acquiring boxes for thousands of categories is a major bottleneck; the proposal addresses a realistic and impactful setting.
- Conceptual simplicity and practicality: The two-part adaptation (background + feature fine-tuning; nearest-neighbor delta transfer) is easy to implement on top of standard CNNs.
- Empirical validation: Clear ablations isolate contributions: background class and shared-layer fine-tuning provide most of the gain; output-layer transfer adds a smaller but statistically significant improvement.
- Scale demonstration: The 7.6K detector showcases feasibility at scale and releases code/models.
- Insightful error analysis: The reduction in background and localization errors aligns with the hypothesized task shift from classification to detection.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Absolute performance is modest: Held-out class mAP (~16%) remains far from the fully supervised upper bound, and the choice to forego SVMs and bbox regression (to maintain compatibility for unseen categories) lowers the baseline. An analysis of how much of the gap stems from missing these components vs missing boxes would help.
- Baselines: The comparison set lacks strong weakly supervised detection baselines on the same split (e.g., MIL-style methods), even if not yet reported on ILSVRC at the time. A simple baseline of “freeze shared layers after B fine-tuning and train only fc8 for A with classification data + background” would clarify the marginal value of the delta-transfer step.
- Category-specific transfer is heuristic: Using Euclidean distance between L2-normalized fc8 classification weights as semantic/visual similarity is plausible but ad hoc. Comparisons to other similarity metrics (e.g., WordNet/attribute similarity, features from intermediate layers, or learned regression from Wc to Wd) are missing, and the sensitivity to k is only briefly mentioned.
- Limited analysis of data regime: How does performance scale with m (number of detection-labeled categories)? A curve would inform practitioners about the marginal utility of additional annotated classes.
- Clarity and completeness: Some implementation details are sparse (sampling strategy for positives/negatives, learning schedules during stage-wise fine-tuning, exact region proposal settings, and NMS details). The paper has minor notational inconsistencies and typos, and some references lack complete bibliographic info.

Quality
Overall technically sound. The methodology is consistent with modern CNN fine-tuning and domain adaptation intuitions. Claims are largely supported by ablations and error breakdown. However, the evaluation could be stronger with additional baselines (weakly supervised alternatives; learned output-layer mapping) and scaling analyses, and with a decoupling of “no SVM/bbox reg” vs “no detection labels for A” effects.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized; figures and the three “insights” structure help. Some missing details and minor typos/formatting issues reduce reproducibility. Providing more training hyperparameters and precise dataset splits would help an expert reproduce results.

Originality
The central idea—learning a generic classifier-to-detector transform on a subset of categories and transferring it to unseen categories—is novel and distinct from R-CNN and prior adaptive SVM/DPM work. The nearest-neighbor delta transfer is simple; more sophisticated mappings could be explored, but the contribution is still original at the time.

Significance
Moderate to high. The setting is practically important, and the approach is likely to be adopted, especially given the code/models. While absolute mAP is not state-of-the-art, the relative gain without boxes for held-out classes is meaningful. The 7.6K demonstration underscores potential impact.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to k in the nearest-neighbor transfer and to the choice of similarity metric? Have you tried learned linear mappings from Wc to Wd?
- Can you report the performance of adding SVMs and bbox regression only for B while keeping your direct scoring for A? Does this harm calibration across classes?
- How does mAP vary as m increases (e.g., m=25/50/100/150)?
- What is the effect of the background class alone (adding fc_bg but not fine-tuning all layers)?
- Could you provide more details on training hyperparameters, sampling ratios, and region proposal thresholds?

Suggestions for improvement
- Include stronger baselines and/or cite results on the same split for weakly supervised methods; at minimum, implement a simple MIL baseline on the held-out classes.
- Explore alternative or learned category-specific mappings (regression from Wc to δ, metric learning for neighbor selection, or semantic priors).
- Provide a scaling curve with respect to m and a study of robustness to noise in classification labels.
- Add a calibration analysis for cross-class NMS when mixing detected and adapted classes.
- Improve clarity by tightening notation (sets A and B), fixing typos, and expanding training details.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper addresses an important problem with a simple, effective method, presents clear ablations and a compelling large-scale demonstration. Strengths outweigh weaknesses, though additional baselines and analyses would strengthen the case.

Pros
- Addresses a realistic and impactful supervision regime
- Simple, practical method with released code/models
- Solid ablations and error analysis; 50% relative mAP gain on held-out classes
- Scales to thousands of categories

Cons
- Modest absolute mAP; missing comparisons to weakly supervised baselines
- Heuristic category-specific transfer; limited analysis of alternatives and sensitivity
- Some missing implementation details and minor clarity issues

Reviewer confidence: High (familiar with detection, CNN fine-tuning, and domain adaptation).
