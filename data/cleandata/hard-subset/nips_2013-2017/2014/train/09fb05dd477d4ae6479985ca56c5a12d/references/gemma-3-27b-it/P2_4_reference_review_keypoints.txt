- Paper proposes LSDA, a method to adapt image classifiers into object detectors to handle limited bounding-box annotations at large scale.  
- Core idea: learn a transformation from classification to detection by fine-tuning a CNN on categories with boxes and transfer that transform to categories without boxes.  
- Shows large gains: ~50% relative mAP improvement on ImageNet detection for held-out categories.  
- Positions work relative to R-CNN, MIL, and domain adaptation, and emphasizes novelty in adapting both domains and tasks.  
- Addresses a critical bottleneck: scarcity of bounding-box annotations for large-scale detection.  
- Strong experimental validation with ablation studies and detailed error analysis showing improved localization and reduced background confusion.  
- Clear motivation and explanation of three insights: background modeling, category invariance, and category specificity.  
- Releases code and a 7.6k-category detector, providing a valuable resource to the community.  
- Well-written and well-organized paper that is easy to follow.  
- Heavy reliance on quality of pre-trained image classifier; sensitivity to different pre-trained models is not explored.  
- Category-specific transfer via nearest-neighbor averaging (k=10) seems ad-hoc and lacks strong justification or alternatives.  
- Computational cost and scalability trade-offs are noted but not analyzed in depth; faster variants mentioned without full complexity discussion.  
- Limited empirical comparison to MIL-based weakly supervised detection methods.  
- Technically sound with convincing experiments, ablation, and error analysis.  
- Novel and significant contribution: adapting classifiers to detectors and generalizing to unseen categories.  
- Likely to have practical impact and be useful to researchers and practitioners building large-scale detectors.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important problem, strong results, novel method, and useful released resources.  
- Arguments against acceptance: dependence on pre-trained classifiers, somewhat ad-hoc nearest-neighbor step, and limited MIL comparisons.  
- Reviewer recommendation: strong accept; paper deemed impactful with minor weaknesses that do not outweigh contributions.
