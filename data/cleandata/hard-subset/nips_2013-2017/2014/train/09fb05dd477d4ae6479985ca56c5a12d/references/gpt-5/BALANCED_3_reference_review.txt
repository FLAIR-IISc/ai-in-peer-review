Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a method to convert image classifiers into object detectors when detection (bounding box) annotations are available for only a subset of categories. The core idea is to cast classification-to-detection as a domain adaptation problem and learn a generic transformation from classification to detection on categories with detection labels (set B), then transfer it to categories without detection labels (set A). Concretely: (1) pre-train a CNN for classification; (2) replace the final layer with K categories and fine-tune on image-level labels; (3) fine-tune layers 1–7 and add a background class using detection data for B, inducing a category-invariant change that helps all classes; and (4) approximate per-class detector parameters for A by adding the average “delta” observed for the k nearest categories in B (neighbors in the fc8 weight space). At test time, scores are adjusted by subtracting the background score; no SVMs or bbox regression are trained to keep training simple and fast.

The approach is positioned relative to R-CNN, which requires detection data for all categories, and to weakly supervised MIL methods that rely solely on image-level labels. It also relates to domain adaptation literature (feature/model adaptation) but differs in adapting across both domain (classification vs detection) and task. Experiments on ILSVRC2013 simulate 200 categories with detection data for 100. LSDA improves held-out mAP from ~10.3% to ~15.9% by category-invariant adaptation and to ~16.15% with the neighbor-based delta (k=10), a 50% relative gain over a classifier-only baseline. Error analysis shows LSDA reduces localization and background confusions. The authors also demonstrate a 7.6K-class detector qualitatively and report an implementation with faster region proposals/SPP.

Strengths:
- Problem importance and scope: Scaling detection beyond the limited set of categories with bounding boxes is impactful and fits NIPS topics (representation learning, domain adaptation, detection).
- Clear insight decomposition: Background modeling + category-invariant adaptation + category-specific adjustment are sensible, and ablations quantify their contributions.
- Practicality and efficiency: Training time is reduced compared to R-CNN’s full pipeline, and the method enables detectors for thousands of categories without box supervision.
- Empirical rigor: Uses ILSVRC2013 with a held-out split, provides ablations, significance testing (p=0.017), and detailed error analysis that explains where gains come from.
- Reproducibility: Implementation details via Caffe and release of models/code are noted.

Weaknesses:
- Absolute performance remains low for held-out categories (mAP ~16%), and the category-specific adaptation contributes modestly over the category-invariant part. This tempers the claimed importance of the fc8 delta transfer.
- Neighbor selection in fc8 weight space is heuristic. No exploration of alternative similarity metrics (e.g., semantic/WordNet distance, visual feature similarity, or learned metrics) or analysis of robustness to k beyond a few values.
- Baselines could be strengthened: comparisons to weakly supervised MIL on the same setting; semi-supervised/self-training approaches; or training a class-agnostic bounding box regressor that could further narrow the gap.
- Split sensitivity: Using the first 100 categories alphabetically for B risks bias; random splits and multiple seeds would better validate generality and report variance.
- Limited quantitative evaluation for large-scale (7.6K) setting; results are qualitative only. Any proxy quantitative test (e.g., a subset with boxes) would add evidence.
- Clarity issues: Some typos, missing words, figure/footnote glitches, and an incomplete reference list formatting detract from polish. The upper bound footnote is truncated.

Quality:
Technically sound overall; the training protocol is reasonable and the ablations support the central claims. The choice to forgo SVMs/regression is justified by training simplicity, though the resulting performance gap is acknowledged. Some design choices (neighboring in fc8 space) are under-motivated theoretically but are empirically validated to a limited extent.

Clarity:
Generally understandable, with a logical progression and helpful figures/ablation narratives. However, the manuscript would benefit from tighter notation, explicit algorithm pseudocode, hyperparameter details (learning rates, k sensitivity), and proofreading.

Originality:
Moderately novel. Prior work adapts classifiers to detectors (R-CNN) and studies domain adaptation, but LSDA’s partial-supervision across categories and the split between category-invariant and category-specific transfer is a useful conceptual and practical contribution.

Significance:
High practical significance for scaling detection when bounding boxes are scarce. The quantitative gains on held-out classes and the large-scale demo indicate potential to influence how practitioners extend detectors to many categories.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Evaluate multiple random B/A splits and report mean±std mAP; vary m to characterize sample efficiency curves.
- Explore alternative neighbor definitions: semantic (WordNet), visual (layer-7 embeddings), or learned metric; report sensitivity to k and the distance metric.
- Add a class-agnostic bounding box regression head trained on B and applied to A; quantify how much of the remaining localization error can be closed.
- Compare against a strong weakly supervised baseline (e.g., MIL with deep features) on the same split, and/or hybrid self-training where high-confidence detections for A bootstrap refinement.
- Calibrate per-class scores (e.g., via temperature scaling or class-specific bias transfer) to reduce inter-class confusion noted in Oth errors.
- Provide limited quantitative checks for the 7.6K model (e.g., on a subset with boxes or via human verification) and report throughput on the faster variant.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a simple, effective method.
- Solid empirical gains and insightful error analysis.
- Releases models/code; practical impact for large-scale detection.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Absolute performance is modest and category-specific transfer is weak.
- Baselines and robustness analyses could be more comprehensive.
- Some presentation polish issues.

Recommendation: Accept (clear accept)

Confidence: High (familiar with detection, domain adaptation, and deep fine-tuning literature)
