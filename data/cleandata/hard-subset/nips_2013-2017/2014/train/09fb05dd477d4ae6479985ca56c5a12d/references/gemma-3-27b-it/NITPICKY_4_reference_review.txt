## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper proposes LSDA, an algorithm for adapting image classifiers to object detectors, aiming to mitigate the annotation bottleneck in large-scale detection. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain (image-level labels) to the detection domain (bounding box annotations) using a subset of categories and then transfer this transformation to categories lacking bounding box labels. The authors cast this as a domain adaptation problem and demonstrate improvements on the ImageNet detection task.

While the problem addressed – scaling detection to a large number of categories – is undeniably important, and the core idea of adaptation is promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of shortcomings, ranging from superficial experimental validation to inconsistencies in the description of the methodology and a general lack of rigorous justification for design choices. 

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** The need for large-scale detection with limited bounding box annotations is a crucial challenge in computer vision.
* **Intuitive Core Idea:**  The concept of adapting classifiers to detectors is sensible and aligns with recent successes in transfer learning.
* **Demonstrated Improvement:** The reported 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline is encouraging, although the baseline's specifics are concerning (see Weaknesses).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Weak Baseline:** The baseline of using “raw classifier weights” on object proposals is exceptionally weak and doesn’t represent a competitive starting point. Comparing against a poorly performing baseline inflates the perceived impact of LSDA.  A more meaningful baseline would be fine-tuning the classifier on the available bounding box data *without* the LSDA adaptation.
* **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** The experimental setup lacks crucial details. For example, the exact number of images used for classification training per category is reported as “≈1000”, introducing ambiguity.  The augmentation strategy, beyond mentioning it follows Girshick et al., is not described.  The details of the nearest neighbor search for category-specific adaptation (k value justification, distance metric nuances) are also lacking.
* **Inconsistent Methodology Description:** The statement that the algorithm directly uses the final score vector *without* SVMs or bounding box regression (Section 3.3) is contradictory to the discussion in footnote 1, which acknowledges the performance gains from these steps while justifying their exclusion for speed. This inconsistency raises questions about the optimality of the chosen evaluation setup.
* **Superficial Error Analysis:** The error analysis (Figure 5) is presented in a qualitative manner and lacks quantitative backing. The visual examples, while illustrative, don't provide statistical evidence for the claims made about localization improvements.  Furthermore, the “Other” error category is too broad to be insightful.
* **Lack of Ablation Study Justification:** While an ablation study is presented (Figure 3), the rationale for prioritizing certain adaptation components (feature representation adaptation being “most important”) is based on empirical observation *without* statistical significance testing.
* **Unclear Novelty:** The adaptation technique builds upon existing domain adaptation methods (mentioned in Related Work) without clearly articulating how LSDA differs fundamentally. The contribution feels incremental rather than transformative.
* **Minor Technical Issues:** The description of equation (1) in Section 3.2 is somewhat unclear. The use of "NB(j, k)" feels unnecessarily complex and could be simplified. The phrase "statistically significant at p = 0.017 using a paired sample t-test" is missing the specification of *what* is being compared in the t-test.
* **Formatting & Presentation:** The references are not uniformly formatted. Some have journal names, others do not. Figures lack sufficient labeling and captions explaining key observations.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against Acceptance:**

* The weak baseline and insufficient experimental detail make it difficult to assess the true impact of LSDA.
* The lack of rigorous justification for design choices and the inconsistent methodology description raise concerns about the scientific rigor of the work.
* The paper, despite addressing an important problem, doesn't present a sufficiently novel or significant contribution to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
* The numerous minor flaws, while individually small, collectively detract from the overall quality of the paper.

**For Acceptance:**

* The core idea of adapting classifiers to detectors is interesting and potentially valuable.
* The reported improvement on the ImageNet detection task, even with the caveats regarding the baseline, is positive.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the authors identify a significant problem and propose a reasonable approach, the paper’s weaknesses, particularly the weak baseline, insufficient experimental detail, and lack of rigorous analysis, outweigh its strengths.  The paper feels rushed and incomplete, lacking the level of polish and thoroughness expected for a NIPS publication. I encourage the authors to address these issues through more comprehensive experimentation, clearer methodology description, and more robust statistical analysis before resubmitting.
