

# Peer Review: LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), an algorithm to transform image classifiers into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The core insight is to treat the transformation from classification to detection as a domain adaptation problem, where the source domain is image-level classification data and the target domain is bounding box-labeled detection data. The authors introduce three key components: 1) recognizing background as an important step in adaptation, 2) transferring category-invariant information between classifier and detector feature representations, and 3) accounting for category-specific differences. The method uses a small set of categories with bounding box annotations to learn a transformation that can be applied to classifiers for categories without bounding box data. The authors demonstrate a 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline on the ImageNet detection dataset, with the ability to produce detectors for 7,604 categories using only classification data for most of them.

The work builds on R-CNN's demonstration that fine-tuning deep features for detection significantly improves performance, but addresses the key limitation that R-CNN requires bounding box annotations for all categories. The authors position their work as complementary to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approaches that train detectors from weakly labeled data.

## Critical Review

### Quality

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a meaningful problem in computer vision: scaling object detection to large numbers of categories.
- The methodology is technically sound and well-motivated, with clear ablation studies demonstrating the contribution of each component.
- The experimental design is appropriate for the task, with careful comparison to baselines and analysis of error types.

**Weaknesses:**
- The claimed 50% relative mAP improvement is not sufficiently contextualized. The paper doesn't provide absolute mAP values for the baseline versus LSDA, making it difficult to assess the practical significance of the improvement. For example, an improvement from 10% to 15% mAP is substantial, but from 1% to 1.5% is less so.
- The paper uses a relatively small subset of categories (100 out of 200) for evaluation, which may not be representative of the true challenge of scaling to tens of thousands of categories. The authors should provide more analysis on how the method scales as the number of held-out categories increases.
- The nearest neighbor approach for category-specific adaptation (equation 1) lacks sufficient detail. What is the similarity metric used to find nearest neighbors? How does the performance vary with k (the number of neighbors)? The paper mentions testing "various values of k" but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of this hyperparameter's impact.
- The error analysis (Section 4.3) is somewhat superficial. While the paper shows that LSDA reduces background confusion and localization errors, it doesn't provide quantitative evidence of this claim across the entire dataset.

### Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to evaluation.
- The figures (particularly Figure 3) effectively illustrate the key insights of the method.
- The implementation details (Section 4.1) are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The distinction between "category invariant adaptation" and "category specific adaptation" is not clearly articulated. The paper states these as two separate components but doesn't explain why they need to be treated as distinct concepts or why they can't be handled by a unified approach.
- The paper mentions "we assume, we assume, the detection model should be most similar" without providing justification for this assumption. Why should classification model similarity predict detection model similarity?
- The paper states that "the main benefit of our approach is to transfer category invariant information" but doesn't provide evidence that the feature space transformation is actually capturing category-invariant information rather than some other effect.

### Originality

**Strengths:**
- The core idea of using a small set of categories with bounding box annotations to adapt classifiers for categories without bounding box annotations is novel and addresses a significant limitation of existing detection approaches.
- The paper makes a clear contribution to the domain adaptation literature by adapting from a classification task to a detection task, rather than just adapting between visual domains with the same task.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing domain adaptation methods. The authors cite several domain adaptation papers (e.g., [12-16]) but don't clearly explain how LSDA differs from them in terms of task adaptation versus domain adaptation.
- The relationship to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approaches is not adequately addressed. The paper mentions MIL as "complimentary" but doesn't explain how LSDA could be combined with or would differ from MIL-based methods.
- The paper doesn't sufficiently reference prior work on "transferring knowledge from classification to detection." While the R-CNN paper is cited, there's no discussion of other work that has attempted this, such as the work by Aytar and Zisserman on "Tabula Rasa" (which is cited but not properly contextualized).

### Significance

**Strengths:**
- The problem of scaling detection to large numbers of categories is important for practical applications in computer vision.
- The demonstration of a 7,604 category detector is impressive and shows the potential of the approach.
- The paper's claim that "any ImageNet-trained classifier can be adapted into a detector" has significant implications for large-scale visual recognition systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't adequately address the practical limitations of the approach. For example, how does the performance scale as the number of held-out categories increases? What happens when the held-out categories are semantically very different from the training categories?
- The paper states that "we can increase performance of a classification network by 50% relative improvement" but doesn't provide evidence that this improvement is consistent across different types of categories. For example, does the method work equally well for fine-grained categories versus broad categories?
- The paper doesn't discuss how LSDA would perform on datasets with different class distributions or with more challenging object categories. The ImageNet detection dataset is relatively easy compared to more recent benchmarks like COCO or OpenImages.

## Overall Assessment

LSDA presents an interesting approach to scaling object detection to large numbers of categories, addressing a meaningful problem in computer vision. The paper makes a clear contribution to the domain adaptation literature by framing the classification-to-detection transformation as a domain adaptation problem and demonstrating its practical utility on a standard benchmark.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that prevent it from being a strong NIPS submission. The most concerning issues are the lack of sufficient evidence to support the claimed improvements, the superficial treatment of key methodological details (particularly the nearest neighbor approach), and the limited scope of the experiments that don't adequately demonstrate scalability to the tens of thousands of categories the authors claim is possible.

## Recommendations

**Pro:**
- Addresses an important problem in computer vision: scaling detection to large numbers of categories
- Clear methodology with well-designed ablation studies
- Demonstrates significant improvement over a strong baseline (50% relative mAP boost)
- Shows promise for enabling detection on tens of thousands of categories

**Con:**
- Lack of sufficient detail on key components (nearest neighbor approach, similarity metric)
- Claims of significant improvement not sufficiently contextualized with absolute performance metrics
- Limited evaluation that doesn't adequately demonstrate scalability to large numbers of categories
- Insufficient differentiation from related work in domain adaptation and MIL

**Recommendation:** **Weak Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting approach to a meaningful problem, it lacks sufficient technical depth and experimental evidence to support its claims. The paper would benefit from more thorough analysis of the nearest neighbor approach, additional experiments demonstrating scalability to larger numbers of categories, and clearer differentiation from related work in domain adaptation and weakly supervised detection. The current version does not provide enough evidence to support the significant claims made about the method's potential impact on large-scale detection.
