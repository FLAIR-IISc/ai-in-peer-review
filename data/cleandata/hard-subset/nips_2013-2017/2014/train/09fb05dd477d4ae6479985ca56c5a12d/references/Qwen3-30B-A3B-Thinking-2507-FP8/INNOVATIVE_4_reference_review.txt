

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

This paper presents LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a novel approach to transform image classifiers into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The core insight is that the transformation from classification to detection can be learned on a subset of categories and then generalized to categories without bounding box annotations. This addresses a fundamental scalability challenge in object detection: the impracticality of collecting bounding box annotations for tens or hundreds of thousands of categories, while image-level annotations (e.g., from ImageNet) are readily available.

The paper makes a strong conceptual contribution by framing the classification-to-detection transformation as a domain adaptation problem. Unlike previous work that either requires bounding box data for all categories (R-CNN) or uses weak supervision (MIL methods), LSDA learns a generic transformation from classification to detection using only a subset of categories with bounding box annotations. The authors propose a three-stage approach: (1) category invariant adaptation (fine-tuning layers 1-7 using detection data for a subset of categories), (2) category specific adaptation (using nearest neighbors to approximate adaptation for categories without detection data), and (3) detection with LSDA (using the adapted network directly without additional SVM training).

The key strength of this paper is its bold conceptual leap. While previous work has focused on improving detection within the same category set, LSDA fundamentally changes the paradigm by enabling detection for categories without any bounding box annotations. This is a significant step toward scaling detection to the same scale as classification (tens of thousands of categories), which has been a major bottleneck in the field. The 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories is compelling evidence of the approach's effectiveness.

The paper's novelty is substantial. As the authors correctly note, "to our knowledge, ours is the first method to adapt to held-out categories that have no detection data." This represents a clear conceptual advance beyond both R-CNN (which requires bounding box data for all categories) and MIL-based methods (which have not been evaluated on large-scale detection challenges). The framing of the problem as a domain adaptation task between classification and detection, while keeping the task fixed, is particularly insightful.

While the methodological details are somewhat incremental (building on R-CNN and domain adaptation), the paper's bold approach to a significant problem outweighs this. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the need for some bounding box data for a subset of categories, but this is a necessary trade-off for the scalability they achieve.

The experimental evaluation is strong for the problem they're addressing. The ablation study clearly demonstrates the importance of each component, and the error analysis shows that LSDA effectively reduces localization errors and background confusion—key challenges in transitioning from classification to detection. The demonstration of a 7,600-category detector is particularly compelling for showcasing the method's scalability potential.

The paper's significance is clear: it opens the door to large-scale detection systems that were previously infeasible due to annotation constraints. This has direct practical implications for applications requiring detection across thousands of categories, such as web-scale image understanding. The authors' vision of "minimizing the gap between having strong large-scale classifiers and strong large-scale detectors" is exactly the kind of bold direction NIPS should champion.

Minor weaknesses include:
1. The nearest-neighbor approach for category-specific adaptation could be more sophisticated
2. The evaluation is limited to ImageNet detection dataset without broader validation
3. The paper doesn't fully address how the method would scale beyond the 7,600-category demonstration

However, these are minor concerns compared to the paper's significant conceptual contribution. The authors are appropriately cautious about their results, noting that "there is still a large gap to reach oracle (known bounding box labels) performance."

In summary, this paper presents a novel, bold approach to a fundamental problem in computer vision. The conceptual leap—transforming classifiers into detectors without bounding box data for all categories—is significant and addresses a critical scalability bottleneck. While the methodological details are somewhat incremental, the paper's core idea is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance. The authors have demonstrated substantial progress toward large-scale detection, which has clear potential to influence future work in the field.

I recommend acceptance for this paper. It exemplifies the kind of bold, novel research that advances the field in meaningful ways, rather than incremental improvements to existing approaches. The potential impact of enabling detection for tens of thousands of categories is substantial, and the paper makes a compelling case for this new direction.
