

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

This paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a method for transforming image classifiers into object detectors using domain adaptation techniques. The authors address the key challenge in scaling object detection to many categories: the need for expensive bounding box annotations for each category. They frame the problem as adapting from a classification task (source domain with image-level labels) to a detection task (target domain with bounding box annotations), requiring only a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations to train the adaptation. The method involves three key components: category invariant adaptation (fine-tuning shared layers using a subset of categories with bounding box data), category specific adaptation (approximating the transformation for categories without bounding box data using nearest neighbors), and detection at test time using the adapted network.

The paper builds on several relevant lines of work. It extends Girshick et al.'s R-CNN approach (which requires bounding box data for all categories) by using domain adaptation to transfer knowledge from categories with bounding box annotations to those without. It also relates to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) methods for weakly supervised detection, though the authors note these haven't been evaluated on large-scale ImageNet detection. The work also connects to domain adaptation literature, but the authors clearly differentiate their approach by adapting not just between visual domains but also between classification and detection tasks.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with appropriate experimental validation. The ablation study (Figure 3, Table 1) clearly demonstrates the contribution of each component of their algorithm, showing that category invariant adaptation is the most important step (50% relative mAP improvement over the baseline). The experiments on the ImageNet detection dataset are well-designed, with careful data splitting and comparison to a strong baseline. The authors acknowledge limitations, including the gap between their performance and the oracle performance (when detection data is available for all categories). The implementation details are sufficiently thorough for reproducibility, and the code/model are publicly available.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear, the methodology is described in sufficient detail, and the figures effectively illustrate key concepts (e.g., Figure 1 showing the domain adaptation concept, Figure 3 showing the ablation study). The explanation of how category specific adaptation works (Section 3.2) is particularly clear, with the mathematical formulation (Equation 1) being easy to follow. The authors also clearly explain their implementation choices, such as the decision to directly use the final score vector rather than training SVMs on layer 7 features as in R-CNN.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by addressing the scalability challenge of object detection through a domain adaptation framework that requires bounding box annotations for only a subset of categories. While related to domain adaptation and MIL work, the authors clearly differentiate their approach by focusing on adapting to held-out categories with no detection data. The paper's novelty is well-justified through a thorough discussion of related work, and the authors correctly note that this is "the first method to adapt to held-out categories that have no detection data." The extension to a 7,604 category detector demonstrates the practical significance of this contribution.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high, as it directly addresses a major bottleneck in scaling object detection to the thousands of categories needed for real-world applications. The 50% relative mAP improvement over the baseline is substantial, and the demonstration of a 7,604 category detector shows the practical impact of the approach. The paper makes a compelling case that this method could significantly reduce the annotation burden for large-scale detection systems, potentially bridging the gap between large-scale classification and detection. The authors also provide a clear path to future work, such as incorporating MIL techniques to further improve performance.

**Strengths:**
- Novel approach to scaling object detection through domain adaptation
- Clear problem statement addressing a real bottleneck in the field
- Strong experimental validation with ablation studies and quantitative results
- Well-structured, clearly written, and reproducible
- Demonstrated large-scale application (7,604 category detector)
- Public release of code and models

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could more explicitly discuss how their category-specific adaptation (Equation 1) relates to other techniques for transferring knowledge across categories (e.g., metric learning approaches)
- The evaluation focuses primarily on mAP, but a more detailed error analysis (beyond Figure 5) could further strengthen the claims
- The paper doesn't discuss computational complexity comparisons with other large-scale detection approaches beyond the claimed reduction in training time

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Addresses a significant problem in computer vision with a novel and well-justified solution
- Demonstrates substantial improvement over strong baselines on a standard benchmark
- Provides clear evidence of scalability to thousands of categories
- Well-written and technically sound with thorough experimental validation
- Publicly available code and models enhance reproducibility and potential impact

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The novelty is somewhat incremental over prior domain adaptation and R-CNN work, though the authors clearly distinguish their contribution
- The paper could provide more detailed comparisons to recent detection approaches that might also address scalability
- The category-specific adaptation approach using nearest neighbors is somewhat heuristic and could benefit from more theoretical justification

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution that addresses a meaningful challenge in computer vision. The paper is well-executed, clearly presented, and makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating how to scale object detection to large numbers of categories with minimal annotation effort. The results are compelling and the approach has clear potential for real-world impact. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission.
