

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

This paper proposes LSDA, an approach to adapt image classifiers into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The authors frame the problem as domain adaptation from classification (source domain) to detection (target domain), using a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations to learn a transformation that can be applied to categories without detection data. The key innovations are: 1) learning a category-invariant transformation through fine-tuning layers 1-7 on detection data for a subset of categories, and 2) approximating category-specific adaptation for held-out categories using nearest neighbors in the classification feature space.

The paper presents an ablation study showing a 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline of using raw classifier weights on region proposals. The authors claim this approach enables detection for tens of thousands of categories, demonstrated with a 7604-category detector.

**Quality:** While the paper demonstrates a reasonable improvement over a weak baseline (using raw classifier weights on region proposals), it fails to provide a meaningful comparison to state-of-the-art detection methods. The authors claim "significant improvement (+50% relative mAP)" but don't compare against established detection frameworks like R-CNN, Fast R-CNN, or Faster R-CNN, which are standard baselines for detection tasks. The paper also lacks a thorough analysis of how the "nearest neighbor" approximation for category-specific adaptation would scale to large numbers of categories. The authors note "we find empirically that fine-tuning induces a generic, category invariant transformation," but provide no statistical analysis to support this claim. The paper's evaluation on held-out categories is limited to the ImageNet detection dataset with only 200 categories, making it difficult to assess the approach's scalability to the tens of thousands of categories claimed.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good figures illustrating the approach. The methodology is explained with sufficient detail for reproduction. However, key implementation details are missing, such as the exact network architecture modifications beyond the output layer, the specific region proposal method used, and the hyperparameters for the nearest neighbor approach.

**Originality:** The core idea of adapting classifiers to detectors is related to R-CNN [1], which is properly cited. While the specific technique for adapting to held-out categories is novel, the paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from existing domain adaptation methods for detection. The claim that "to our knowledge, ours is the first method to adapt to held-out categories that have no detection data" is questionable, as multiple instance learning (MIL) approaches [6,7] also aim to learn detectors from weakly labeled data. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with these techniques.

**Significance:** The problem of scaling detection to many categories is important, but the paper fails to demonstrate meaningful progress toward this goal. The claimed 50% relative mAP improvement is against a weak baseline that doesn't represent a practical detection approach (R-CNN uses SVMs on extracted features, which the authors acknowledge improves performance). The paper doesn't demonstrate how LSDA would compare to a fully trained detector on the same categories. The 7604-category detector is presented as a showcase, but the paper lacks evidence that this system would be practically useful or outperform standard detection methods on those categories.

**Major Concerns:** The most significant issue is the lack of comparison against established detection baselines. Without showing that LSDA outperforms standard detection methods (even for the categories with bounding box data), the claimed "50% relative mAP improvement" is misleading. The paper also doesn't address how the nearest neighbor approximation would behave for categories with very different visual characteristics from those in the source set. The paper's claim that "we can adapt any ImageNet-trained classifier into a detector" is overstated, as the approach still requires bounding box data for a subset of categories.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an interesting problem, the lack of rigorous comparison to established detection methods, the weak baseline comparison, and the overstatement of the claimed improvements make the paper's contributions difficult to evaluate. The paper would require significant revision, including comprehensive comparisons to standard detection frameworks and a more thorough validation of the held-out category adaptation approach, before it could be considered for publication. The current evidence does not demonstrate that LSDA provides a meaningful advance over existing methods for object detection.
