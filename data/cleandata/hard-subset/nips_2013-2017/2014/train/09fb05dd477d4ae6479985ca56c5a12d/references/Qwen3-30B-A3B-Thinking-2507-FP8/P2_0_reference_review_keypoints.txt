- Proposes LSDA: transform image classifiers into object detectors by learning a transformation from a small set of annotated categories to adapt classifiers for unannotated categories.  
- Frames the problem as domain adaptation: classification data as source, detection data as target.  
- Two adaptation components: category-invariant (fine-tune on a subset with bounding boxes) and category-specific (nearest-neighbor transfer in feature space).  
- Demonstrates ~50% relative mAP improvement over a classifier-only baseline on ImageNet detection.  
- Scales up to a 7,604-category detector, showing practical large-scale applicability.  
- Methodology is technically sound with a clear distinction between invariant and specific adaptations.  
- Experimental setup is appropriate, including ablation studies that validate each component.  
- Provides quantitative metrics, error analysis, and visual examples to support results.  
- Paper is well-written, organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction (code and models available).  
- Novel contribution: adapting classifiers to detectors for held-out categories without bounding boxes, distinct from prior weakly supervised/detection methods.  
- Could strengthen originality claim by more detailed comparison to recent multi-class domain adaptation work (e.g., Hoffman et al.).  
- High practical significance: reduces bounding box annotation burden and has clear real-world impact potential.  
- Strengths: addresses an important scaling problem, novel and effective solution, strong results, scalability, public release of code/models, thorough ablation.  
- Weaknesses: sparse detail on nearest-neighbor selection (k, distance choice), evaluation limited to ImageNet, limited discussion of failure modes for dissimilar categories, and limited comparison to state-of-the-art detectors.  
- Recommendation: accept — the paper is novel, technically strong, clear, significant, and meets NIPS criteria (quality, clarity, originality, significance).
