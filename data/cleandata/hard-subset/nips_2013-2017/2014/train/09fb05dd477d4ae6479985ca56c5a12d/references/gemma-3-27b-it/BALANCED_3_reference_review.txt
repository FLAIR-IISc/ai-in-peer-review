This paper proposes LSDA, a method for adapting image classifiers into object detectors, particularly addressing the challenge of limited bounding box annotations for many categories. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain (image-level labels) to the detection domain (bounding boxes and labels) using supervised CNNs, effectively leveraging readily available classification data to boost detection performance on categories lacking bounding box annotations. The approach casts this as a domain adaptation problem, and fine-tunes layers of a pre-trained CNN.

This work builds upon the R-CNN paradigm [1] which first demonstrated transferring features learned for classification to detection, but LSDA aims to reduce the dependence on extensive bounding box annotations. It also relates to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approaches [6, 7] which utilize weak labels, but positions itself as complementary and evaluated on a larger scale (ImageNet). The paper also acknowledges the broader field of domain adaptation [12, 13, 14, 15, 16], highlighting its novelty in adapting both visual domain *and* task (classification to detection).

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical problem:** The scarcity of bounding box annotations is a major bottleneck in scaling object detection to a large number of categories. This work tackles this directly.
* **Novel approach:** LSDA presents a conceptually sound and novel approach to transfer learning for detection, framing it as a domain adaptation problem and focusing on learning a transformation.
* **Strong experimental results:** The reported 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline using raw classifier weights demonstrates the effectiveness of the method.  The ablation study (Figure 3) provides insightful analysis of the contributions of each component.
* **Large-scale experiment:**  Demonstrating the method on a 7604 category detector is impressive and highlights the potential for scaling.
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the diagrams (especially Figure 2) effectively illustrate the LSDA pipeline.  The release of code and models is a significant positive.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited comparison to strong baselines:** While the comparison to using raw classifier weights is a good starting point, the paper lacks a detailed comparison with more recent and state-of-the-art detection methods trained *with* bounding box data, even on the limited 100-category set.  How does LSDA compare to Faster R-CNN or YOLO, for example, when *both* have access to the same 100 categories of annotated data? This makes it difficult to assess the absolute performance of LSDA.
* **Category-specific adaptation (Section 3.2):** The nearest neighbor approach for adapting categories without bounding boxes seems somewhat ad-hoc.  The choice of *k* nearest neighbors could significantly impact performance, and more justification for the chosen value (and perhaps sensitivity analysis) would be beneficial.  The explanation of Euclidean distance on the fc8 parameters lacks intuition.
* **Error analysis depth:** The error analysis is a good start (Figure 5), but could be more comprehensive.  A quantitative breakdown of the error types (Loc, BG, Oth) would be more informative.  Further analysis of *why* the "Other" errors occur would be helpful – are there specific categories that are consistently confused?
* **Computational Cost:** The paper mentions that running the full pipeline takes 4 seconds per image. While a faster version is presented, it's important to clearly state the computational requirements and scalability limitations, especially given the ambition of a 7604-category detector.
* **Lack of negative results:** The paper focuses on successes. Are there categories where LSDA *doesn’t* work well? Discussing failure cases would strengthen the analysis.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and impactful approach to a significant problem.
* Strong experimental results on a challenging dataset.
* Clear and well-written presentation with publicly available code.
* Potential for scaling object detection to a large number of categories.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Limited comparison to state-of-the-art detection methods.
* Ad-hoc nature of the category-specific adaptation approach.
* Lack of detailed error analysis and discussion of failure cases.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a promising paper that addresses an important problem in object detection. The core idea of learning a transformation from classification to detection is novel and the experimental results are encouraging. However, the lack of comparison to stronger baselines and the somewhat ad-hoc category adaptation method prevent it from being a clear acceptance at this time.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection**, but with strong encouragement for the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art detectors, a more principled approach to category-specific adaptation, and a more detailed error analysis would significantly strengthen the paper. I believe with these revisions, this work has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
