**Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"**

**Summary**

This paper addresses the significant challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, for which bounding box annotations are prohibitively expensive to collect. The authors propose an algorithm, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), that learns to transform an image classifier into an object detector. The core idea is to treat this transformation as a domain adaptation problem. Using a set of "source" categories with both image-level labels and bounding box annotations, the method fine-tunes a pre-trained classification CNN for the detection task. This fine-tuning process is assumed to induce both a category-invariant transformation in the lower layers and a category-specific transformation in the final classification layer. For novel "target" categories that lack bounding box data, the category-invariant transformation is directly applied, and the category-specific transformation is approximated by averaging the transformations from visually similar source categories, where similarity is measured by the Euclidean distance between classifier weights. Experiments on the ILSVRC 2013 dataset show a significant relative mAP improvement on held-out categories compared to using the original classifier directly for detection.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles a crucial and highly relevant problem. Reducing the dependency on expensive bounding box annotations is a key step towards building truly large-scale visual recognition systems, and this work proposes a pragmatic approach to this issue.
2.  **Novelty of Approach:** The formulation of the problem as one of adapting a transformation is interesting. The central idea of transferring the learned adaptation from classifier weights to detector weights (`δB`) to new categories via a nearest-neighbor approach is a novel heuristic.
3.  **Empirical Demonstration:** The authors provide a clear experimental validation of their approach on a challenging large-scale benchmark. The ablation study (as described in the text) systematically evaluates the contribution of each component of LSDA, demonstrating that the feature representation adaptation provides the most significant boost.
4.  **Public Release:** The decision to release the code and the 7.6K category model is a valuable contribution to the community, encouraging reproducibility and future work.

**Weaknesses and Concerns**

While the paper presents a promising direction, it is marred by a significant number of flaws in its presentation, methodological justification, and experimental rigor.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript suffers from a general lack of polish that hinders readability and confidence in the work.
    *   **Formatting and Typos:** There are numerous typographical and grammatical errors (e.g., "a large of amount of images", "m K" instead of "m << K", "Hoeim" instead of "Hoai"). The formatting of the list in Section 3 is broken. Several references are incomplete or improperly formatted (e.g., repeated "In In Proc. CVPR,").
    *   **Missing Content:** Crucial visual aids and results are referenced but not provided. Figures 1 and 2 are mentioned in the introduction and methods section, respectively, but are absent. Table 1, which contains the detailed ablation study, is also missing, making it impossible to verify the quantitative claims in Section 4.2.
    *   **Unclear Explanations:** The explanation of the category-specific adaptation in Section 3.2 is convoluted. Stating the process is "equivalent to fixing fcB and learning a new layer, zero initialized, δB" is confusing. A more direct definition, such as defining `δB` as the difference between the fine-tuned detection weights and the original classification weights, would be much clearer. Furthermore, Footnote 1's claim that using SVMs "removes the adaptation capacity of the system" is unsubstantiated and requires significant clarification.

2.  **Methodological Justification:** The core mechanism for adapting novel classes is based on a strong, unsupported heuristic.
    *   **Nearest-Neighbor Assumption:** The paper assumes that proximity in the L2 space of classification weights (`Wc`) implies that the categories should undergo a similar adaptation to become detectors. The text states, "...we assume, the detection model should be most similar." This assumption is not theoretically or empirically justified. It is plausible that visually similar classes might require very different adaptations (e.g., distinguishing a "cat face" vs. a "whole cat"). The authors should have explored this critical assumption further, perhaps by analyzing the variance of `δB` among visually similar source classes or by considering alternative similarity metrics (e.g., based on semantic word embeddings).

3.  **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating a positive result, lacks the rigor expected for a top-tier publication.
    *   **Class Split:** The decision to use the first 100 alphabetically sorted categories as the source set and the remaining 100 as the target set is concerning. This non-random split could introduce systematic bias. A more robust evaluation would involve multiple random splits to ensure the results are not an artifact of this particular partition.
    *   **Incomplete Evaluation:** The absolute mAP on held-out categories (16.15%) is still quite low, limiting the immediate practical impact. The paper establishes an "upper bound" but defines it as the performance of their network trained on all 200 classes *without* the subsequent SVM and bounding box regression steps used by the original R-CNN. This presents a misleadingly low upper bound and understates the true performance gap compared to a fully trained detector.
    *   **Lack of Error Analysis:** Figure 3, the main result summary, lacks error bars, making it difficult to assess the statistical significance or variance of the results. While a single t-test is reported for one comparison, a more comprehensive statistical analysis is needed. The information presented in Figure 5(a) ("sim", "1-r") is not defined anywhere in the text.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a highly significant and practical problem.
*   The core idea of learning and transferring a classifier-to-detector transformation is novel and elegant.
*   The paper demonstrates a substantial relative performance gain on a challenging task.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The manuscript is poorly prepared, with numerous formatting errors, typos, and critical missing content (figures and tables).
*   The central methodological assumption (L2 distance of classifier weights as a proxy for adaptation similarity) is a strong heuristic that lacks adequate justification.
*   The experimental setup and evaluation lack rigor (non-random data split, lack of error bars, potentially misleading upper bound).
*   The clarity of key methodological descriptions is poor.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the core ideas in this paper are interesting and address an important problem, the execution and presentation fall well short of the standards expected at NIPS. The combination of missing figures and tables, numerous formatting and writing errors, and a central methodological assumption that is not rigorously justified makes it difficult to fully endorse the work in its current state. The experimental analysis could also be made significantly more robust. I believe the paper has potential, but it requires a major revision to address these substantial shortcomings before it is ready for publication.
