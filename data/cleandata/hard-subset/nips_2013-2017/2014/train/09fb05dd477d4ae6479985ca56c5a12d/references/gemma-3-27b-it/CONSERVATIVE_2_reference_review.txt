This paper proposes LSDA, an algorithm for adapting image classifiers into object detectors, leveraging the disparity in annotation cost between image-level and bounding box labels. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain (image labels) to the detection domain (bounding boxes) on a subset of categories and then transfer this transformation to unseen categories. The approach frames this as a domain adaptation problem, fine-tuning a CNN pre-trained on ImageNet classification data. 

While the problem addressed—scaling detection to a large number of categories—is undeniably important, and the motivation is clear, the paper presents a relatively incremental advance and relies heavily on established techniques. The core concept of adapting classifiers to detectors isn't novel (as acknowledged by the authors citing R-CNN), and the domain adaptation framing, while reasonable, doesn’t introduce a fundamentally new approach. The primary contribution appears to be demonstrating that this transfer *can* be effectively performed, even for categories without detection labels, and quantifying the improvement over a simple baseline of using classifier weights. However, the reported gains (+50% relative mAP) while significant, are presented without sufficient detail to assess their robustness.

The paper’s strengths lie in the clear problem definition and the generally sound methodology. The use of a well-established CNN architecture (Krizhevsky et al.) and the careful ablation study (though limited) contribute to the credibility of the work. The ablation study, showing the contribution of each adaptation component, is helpful. The release of the model and code is also commendable.

However, the weaknesses are more substantial. The evaluation is primarily limited to the ImageNet detection task, making generalization difficult to ascertain. The comparison to related work is somewhat superficial. While the authors mention Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approaches, they dismiss them without a direct comparison—a crucial omission, given that MIL represents a competing paradigm. Similarly, the discussion of domain adaptation methods feels cursory and doesn't adequately position LSDA within the broader landscape. The explanation of the nearest neighbor category selection for adapting the output layer (equation 1) feels ad-hoc and lacks justification. Why Euclidean distance on fc8 parameters? Why this specific averaging strategy? The analysis of error types, while insightful, doesn’t delve deeply into *why* certain errors persist. The claim that LSDA significantly reduces localization error is supported by qualitative examples, but lacks quantitative backing. The improved runtime reported (with spatial pyramid pooling) isn’t central to the core LSDA algorithm. Finally, the writing, while generally clear, occasionally lacks precision. For instance, the explanation of the category-specific adaptation (section 3.2) is somewhat convoluted.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant and important problem in scaling object detection.
* Demonstrates a practical approach to leveraging weakly-labeled data.
* Provides a reasonably well-executed empirical evaluation.
* Releases code and models for reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Incremental contribution; builds on established techniques.
* Limited scope of evaluation.
* Lack of direct comparison to competing methods (MIL).
* Insufficient justification for certain design choices.
* Qualitative error analysis not fully supported by quantitative results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a borderline submission. While the work is technically sound and addresses a relevant problem, it lacks the originality and significance to warrant strong acceptance. The lack of comparison to more recent and relevant work (like MIL methods) is a significant weakness. The paper feels like a solid engineering study, but not a groundbreaking research contribution. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly with a more thorough comparison to alternative methods and a more rigorous evaluation of generalization ability. Further justification and quantitative analysis of the key design choices would also improve the paper.
