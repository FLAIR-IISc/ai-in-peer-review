### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a method for training object detectors for a large number of categories when bounding box annotations are only available for a small subset of them. The core idea is to treat the problem as a form of domain adaptation, where the goal is to learn a general transformation from an image classifier into an object detector. This transformation is learned using the classes that have both image-level and bounding box labels. The method involves two main adaptation stages: 1) a category-invariant adaptation, where the lower layers of a CNN are fine-tuned for the detection task (including learning a background class) using the available bounding box data, and 2) a category-specific adaptation for the final classification layer, where the transformation learned for classes with bounding box data is transferred to classes without it by averaging the weight changes of their nearest neighbors in the original classifier's weight space. Experiments on the ILSVRC2013 dataset, simulating a scenario with 100 classes with bounding boxes and 100 without, show a 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline that uses the original classifier weights directly for detection.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Significant Problem:** The paper tackles a well-known and critical bottleneck in computer vision: the prohibitive cost of collecting bounding box annotations for scaling object detection to thousands or tens of thousands of categories. The proposed direction of leveraging abundant, cheap image-level labels is highly practical and important.

2.  **Clear and Methodical Approach:** The proposed LSDA algorithm is well-motivated and builds logically upon the successful R-CNN framework. The decomposition of the adaptation into category-invariant (feature representation) and category-specific (classifier weights) components is sensible and provides a clear structure to the method.

3.  **Solid Experimental Protocol:** The experimental design is a clear strength. Simulating the problem by holding out 100 of the 200 ILSVRC classes is a convincing way to provide a quantitative evaluation of the core claim. The inclusion of a detailed ablation study (Figure 3, Table 1) is crucial for understanding the contribution of each component of the proposed method, correctly identifying the category-invariant feature adaptation as the most impactful step. The error analysis further reinforces the claim that the adaptation successfully reduces localization and background confusion errors, which are key challenges in moving from classification to detection.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Baselines and Comparison to Related Work:** The primary weakness of this work is the lack of comparison against more established methods for weakly-supervised object detection. The paper's main baseline is a network trained purely on classification data, which is a natural starting point for an ablation study but is not a competitive baseline for the task itself. The authors mention Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) methods in the related work section but dismiss a direct comparison because they have not been evaluated on the ImageNet scale. This is an unsatisfying justification. For a rigorous evaluation, the authors should have implemented or adapted a representative MIL-based detector and evaluated it on their own experimental split. Without this comparison, it is difficult to ascertain whether the proposed adaptation scheme is superior to existing paradigms for learning from weak labels.

2.  **Heuristic Nature of Category-Specific Adaptation:** While the category-invariant adaptation is a straightforward application of fine-tuning, the category-specific adaptation is based on a rather simple and ad-hoc heuristic. The assumption that the classifier-to-detector transformation for a class can be approximated by averaging the transformations of its nearest neighbors in the L2-normalized classifier weight space is not well-justified. This feels more like an engineering trick than a principled approach. While the results show a statistically significant, albeit small, improvement from this step, the lack of deeper analysis or theoretical grounding makes this component less convincing.

3.  **Limited Absolute Performance and Significance:** While the 50% *relative* improvement is emphasized, the final absolute performance on the held-out categories is 16.15% mAP. This is still substantially lower than the 20.94% mAP achieved by the same network architecture when trained with full supervision on all classes (the "upper bound" in Table 1). This large performance gap raises questions about the practical utility of the resulting detectors and suggests that the adaptation method only partially solves the problem. It is unclear if a 16% mAP detector is sufficiently reliable for real-world applications.

4.  **Deviation from State-of-the-Art Practices:** The authors explicitly state that they omit the final SVM training and bounding box regression steps from the original R-CNN pipeline, which are known to provide significant performance gains. While they justify this for speed and simplicity, it complicates the interpretation of the results. This decision makes it difficult to compare their performance to established R-CNN results and to understand how the LSDA adaptation would function within a full, state-of-the-art detection system. This choice may artificially lower both their baseline and upper-bound results, but it leaves the reader wondering about the method's performance under a standard, high-performance configuration.

### Conclusion

The paper presents an interesting and clearly articulated approach to a very relevant problem. The experimental setup and ablation studies are well-conducted and provide good evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed components. However, the work is hampered by a lack of comparison to established weakly-supervised methods, the heuristic nature of the category-specific adaptation, and the significant performance gap that remains compared to fully supervised methods. While the idea of learning a transferable adaptation is promising, the current method feels more like a clever extension of an existing framework rather than a fundamentally new approach. The validation would be much stronger with comparisons to MIL-based baselines. Due to these concerns about methodological rigor and thoroughness of the evaluation, the paper falls short of the standards expected for publication at this conference.
