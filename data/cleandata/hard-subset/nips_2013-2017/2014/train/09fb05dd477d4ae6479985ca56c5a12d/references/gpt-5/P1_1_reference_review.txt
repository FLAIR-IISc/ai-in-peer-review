Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes LSDA, a method to convert image classifiers into object detectors at scale by learning a category-invariant transformation from classification to detection on a subset of categories with bounding boxes, and then transferring it to categories without any box annotations. Concretely: (1) pretrain a CNN for classification on all K categories; (2) fine-tune shared layers (1–7) for detection using bounding boxes for a subset B (and learn a background class), which induces a largely category-invariant “classification→detection” feature transformation; (3) perform a simple category-specific adaptation of the output layer for held-out categories A by adding the average fine-tuning delta from nearest neighbor categories in B (measured by fc8 weight similarity). At test time, scores are background-calibrated (score_i − score_background).

On ILSVRC13 detection with 200 categories, they simulate having detection labels for 100 (B) and only image-level labels for the other 100 (A). On the held-out A categories, LSDA improves mAP from 10.31% (classification-only network on proposals) to 15.85% via category-invariant adaptation, and to 16.15% with category-specific adaptation (k=10 neighbors), a ~50% relative gain. They report error analysis showing substantial reductions in localization and background confusions, and showcase a 7,604-class detector by applying the learned transformation to thousands of classification-only categories, with a faster SPP/fast-proposal implementation (0.5s/image). The approach builds on R-CNN (Girshick et al.) but removes the need for box labels for all categories; related domain adaptation/model transfer works (e.g., Aytar & Zisserman, Donahue et al.) are cited; weakly supervised MIL methods are discussed as complementary.

Strengths:
- Addresses a practically important problem: scaling detection beyond categories with box annotations.
- Simple, well-motivated decomposition into category-invariant (shared features, background modeling) and category-specific (fc8 deltas) adaptation.
- Sound experimental protocol on ILSVRC13 with held-out categories; thorough ablation shows which components matter (background + feature adaptation dominate).
- Clear evidence of error-mode shift (fewer localization/background errors), consistent with the task/domain shift.
- Computationally efficient training (hours vs days) and an engineering pathway to large-scale detectors; code/models released.

Weaknesses:
- Category-specific adaptation is heuristic (nearest-neighbor averaging in fc8 space); the gains are small relative to the category-invariant component and ablations on k/metrics are only briefly mentioned.
- Comparison to alternative weakly supervised baselines (MIL-based detectors) on ILSVRC is absent; while authors note those works haven’t been evaluated at this scale, even a smaller-scale comparison would be informative.
- The evaluation uses val splits; test-set results or comparison to state of the art (including SVM/bbox regression) are not provided. Absolute mAP remains low; the method trades accuracy for scalability, which is reasonable but should be more explicitly contextualized.
- Some choices (subtracting background logit, calibration across categories, cross-category NMS) could be better justified/quantified.

Quality:
Technically sound and carefully evaluated for the main claim: learning a generic classification→detection transformation on some classes helps held-out classes. The ablation isolating background modeling and feature fine-tuning is compelling; significance testing for the category-specific step is a plus. However, more sensitivity analyses would strengthen the case: varying |B| (how many detection categories are needed?), varying k and similarity metrics (fc7/fc8 cosine, semantic/WordNet distances), and an oracle bound vs. hybrid approaches (e.g., adding bbox regression only on B). A brief comparison to a weakly supervised MIL baseline on a common subset would also be helpful.

Clarity:
Generally clear and well organized; the main ideas and training pipeline are understandable and reproducible (Caffe models provided). Suggestions:
- Clarify the loss used during detection fine-tuning (softmax over K+1 with background) and the rationale for score_i − score_background vs. softmax probabilities or calibrated margins.
- Explicitly report results vs. k for the neighbor averaging and discuss other similarity metrics.
- State the “upper bound” mAP value (currently implied but not numeric in the text provided) and the cost/benefit of adding SVMs and bbox regression.
- Detail how cross-category NMS is used in quantitative evaluation (or only for qualitative 7.6k results).

Originality:
While building on R-CNN and standard fine-tuning, the core idea—learn a category-invariant feature/domain transformation from classification to detection and transfer it to unseen classes—is novel and timely. The category-specific delta transfer is a simple but new twist. The work is well positioned relative to domain adaptation and weakly supervised detection literature.

Significance:
High practical significance: enables thousands of detectors without box labels; demonstrates sizable relative gains on a major benchmark. The 7.6k model and speed improvements indicate potential for broad adoption. Scientifically, it provides evidence that most of the classification→detection gap can be closed by learning background modeling and shared feature adaptation, with smaller residual class-specific effects.

Suggestions/questions for authors:
- Provide sensitivity to number of labeled detection categories (e.g., 25/50/100) and to k/metric in Eq. (1).
- Try learning the fc8 delta via regression from classification weights or via a small hypernetwork, vs. neighbor averaging.
- Compare to a weakly supervised detector baseline on a shared subset (e.g., VOC or a 50-class ILSVRC slice).
- Report calibration experiments (e.g., per-class score normalization) and whether subtracting background is crucial.
- Consider adding bbox regression trained on B and transferring it generically to A to quantify localization gains vs. cost.

Pros:
- Important problem; scalable and simple solution
- Strong, well-controlled ablations; error analysis aligns with claims
- Open-source release; practical impact

Cons:
- Limited comparison to alternative weakly supervised/DA baselines
- Category-specific adaptation is heuristic with modest gains
- Absolute accuracy is still far from fully supervised detectors

Recommendation: Accept (clear contribution with practical impact and solid empirical evidence)
Confidence: High
