**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a method for creating object detectors for categories that lack bounding box annotations. The central idea is to leverage a small set of categories for which both image-level labels and bounding box annotations are available to learn a general transformation that converts an image classifier into an object detector. This transformation is then applied to classifiers for a large number of categories that only have image-level labels. The authors frame this as a domain adaptation problem, adapting from the classification task/domain to the detection task/domain. The method consists of two main adaptation steps: 1) a category-invariant adaptation, where the feature extraction layers of a CNN are fine-tuned for detection using the annotated categories, and 2) a category-specific adaptation, where the final classification layer for unannotated categories is adjusted by averaging the learned adjustments from its nearest neighbors among the annotated categories. Experiments on the ILSVRC2013 dataset show that LSDA improves performance on held-out categories by a relative 50% in mAP over a baseline that uses the raw classifier for detection.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses a critical and practical bottleneck in computer vision: the prohibitive cost of collecting bounding box annotations required to train large-scale object detectors. The goal of leveraging widely available image-level classification data is highly relevant and could have a significant impact on the field.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and the proposed method is clearly explained. The breakdown into category-invariant and category-specific adaptation is logical and easy to follow. The figures, particularly Figure 2 illustrating the architecture and Figure 5 showing the error analysis, are effective in communicating the core ideas and results.

3.  **Empirical Results:** The 50% relative mAP improvement over the "classification-only" baseline is substantial. The ablation study in Table 1 effectively demonstrates the contribution of each component of the proposed method. The error analysis in Section 4.3 provides compelling evidence that the adaptation successfully addresses key challenges of the detection task, namely reducing localization errors and confusion with background, which are less critical for whole-image classification.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Justification:** While the overall framework is intuitive, key components rely on heuristics that lack rigorous justification. The category-specific adaptation, in particular, is based on the strong assumption that classes with similar classifier weights (in L2-norm) require a similar transformation from classifier to detector. This is a plausible heuristic, but it is not validated or theoretically supported. Why should classifier similarity be a proxy for the required adaptation for localization? It is conceivable that visually similar objects (e.g., different but similarly-shaped bird species) might have similar classifiers but require different adaptations if one is typically seen perched on a wire and another on the ground. This core mechanism feels more like an engineering choice than a principled method.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Baselines:** The paper's primary baseline is a network trained only on classification data. While this is a necessary sanity check, it is a weak baseline. The problem of training detectors from image-level labels falls squarely into the category of weakly-supervised object detection. Established methods in this area, particularly those based on Multiple Instance Learning (MIL), are the most relevant point of comparison. The authors dismiss these methods by stating they "have not yet been evaluated on the large-scale ImageNet detection challenge," which is an insufficient reason to omit a comparison entirely. Without positioning LSDA against state-of-the-art weakly-supervised methods, it is impossible to assess its actual contribution and significance. The claims of novelty and performance are significantly weakened by this omission.

3.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The experimental setup, while reasonable, does not explore the robustness or limitations of the approach. The method's performance likely depends heavily on the number (`m`) and composition of the base categories with bounding box data. How does the performance on held-out classes degrade as `m` decreases from 100 to 50, or 20? This sensitivity analysis is critical for understanding the practical utility and data efficiency of the proposed method, but is not provided. Furthermore, the absolute performance on held-out classes (16.15% mAP) is still quite low compared to the fully supervised upper bound, which raises questions about the utility of the resulting detectors in practice.

4.  **Novelty:** The work is primarily a novel combination of existing techniques. The use of pre-trained CNNs and fine-tuning for detection is standard practice, established by R-CNN. The core contribution is the specific two-stage transfer learning strategy. However, as noted above, the category-specific transfer is a simple averaging heuristic. While the application to held-out categories is new, the technical novelty of the components themselves is limited.

**Conclusion and Recommendation**

The paper presents an interesting approach to a very important problem. The idea of learning a general classifier-to-detector transformation is appealing, and the empirical results show a clear improvement over a basic classification baseline.

However, the work is undermined by significant weaknesses in its evaluation and methodological justification. The category-specific adaptation is based on an unsubstantiated heuristic, and more importantly, the paper fails to compare its method against the most relevant body of work: weakly-supervised object detection. This makes it difficult to judge the paper's contribution to the state of the art. An established method might perform better, and without this comparison, the claims of the paper are not well-supported. Given my preference for rigorous validation against established methods, I find the experimental evaluation to be incomplete.

Therefore, while the problem is significant and the initial results are promising, the paper in its current form does not meet the standards for publication at NIPS. I would recommend rejecting the paper. It could be significantly strengthened by providing a more principled justification for the category-specific adaptation model and, crucially, by conducting a thorough comparison against relevant weakly-supervised detection baselines.
