

# Peer Review: LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes LSDA, a method to transform image classifiers into object detectors using domain adaptation. The core insight is that a small set of categories with bounding box annotations can be used to learn a generic transformation from classification to detection, enabling adaptation of classifiers for categories without any bounding box data. This addresses a key bottleneck in scaling object detection to large numbers of categories, where collecting bounding box annotations is prohibitively expensive.

The work builds on R-CNN's demonstration that fine-tuning deep features from classification to detection improves performance, but extends it by requiring detection annotations for only a subset of categories. It frames the problem as domain adaptation between classification (source domain) and detection (target domain), leveraging three key insights: (1) background recognition is critical, (2) category-invariant feature representation can be transferred, and (3) category-specific adaptation is needed.

## Strengths

The paper makes a compelling contribution to a significant problem in computer vision. The 50% relative mAP improvement over the classification-only baseline on held-out categories (Table 1) is impressive and well-supported by thorough ablation studies. The authors demonstrate that the most important component is category-invariant adaptation (learning background representation and fine-tuning layers 1-7), which aligns with their theoretical motivation.

The practical impact is substantial: the authors successfully create a 7,604-category detector using only image-level labels for the vast majority of categories, demonstrating the method's scalability. The approach is elegant in its simplicity—building on existing CNN architectures and adapting them through careful fine-tuning rather than introducing new complex components.

The experimental design is well-executed, with appropriate baselines and thorough analysis of error types (Figure 5). The analysis showing reduced localization errors after adaptation (Figure 4) provides compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness.

## Weaknesses

While the approach is promising, several limitations need addressing:

1. **Limited comparison to alternative approaches**: The paper mentions MIL-based methods as "complimentary" but doesn't provide a direct comparison. Given that MIL has been used for weakly supervised detection, a quantitative comparison would strengthen the claims. The paper states "MIL-based methods have not yet been evaluated on ImageNet detection," but this claim seems questionable given recent work like [7] on PASCAL VOC.

2. **Nearest neighbor assumption**: The category-specific adaptation relies on the assumption that semantically similar categories will have similar adaptation parameters. However, the paper doesn't thoroughly evaluate how this assumption holds when nearest neighbors are not semantically similar (e.g., when categories are from different semantic groups). This is particularly concerning for large-scale applications where categories may not cluster neatly.

3. **Computational efficiency claims**: The paper claims a "reduction from 3 days to 5.5 hours" in training time, but doesn't provide details about the hardware used or how this compares to standard R-CNN training. This makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the claimed efficiency gain.

4. **Scalability limits**: The method scales to 7,604 categories, but the paper doesn't address how performance would degrade as the number of categories increases further. The nearest neighbor approach may become less effective when the semantic space becomes more complex.

## Overall Assessment

LSDA makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of scaling object detection to large numbers of categories. The core idea of using domain adaptation to transfer detection capabilities from a few annotated categories to many unannotated ones is novel and well-motivated. The experimental results are strong, demonstrating significant improvements over the baseline.

However, the paper would benefit from more thorough comparison to existing weakly supervised detection approaches, deeper analysis of the nearest neighbor assumption's limitations, and more detailed discussion of computational efficiency claims. The method's scalability to truly massive category sets (e.g., 100,000+ categories) remains an open question.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

LSDA presents a significant contribution to the field with clear practical impact. The method addresses a critical bottleneck in computer vision—scaling detection to large numbers of categories—by cleverly leveraging existing classification data. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are well-designed, and the results are compelling. While the paper has some limitations in comparison to related work and scalability analysis, these do not undermine the core contribution. The method provides a practical path toward truly large-scale detection systems, which is an important direction for the field.

The paper meets NIPS standards for technical quality, significance, and originality. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, with clear potential for impact on both research and practical applications. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field that should be widely accessible to the community.

The paper would benefit from addressing the weaknesses mentioned above in a revision, but as it stands, it represents a strong contribution to the field of object detection.
