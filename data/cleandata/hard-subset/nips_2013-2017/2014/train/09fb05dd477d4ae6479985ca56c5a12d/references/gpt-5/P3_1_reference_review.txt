Summary
This paper tackles the problem of scaling object detection to many categories when bounding-box annotations are scarce. The proposed LSDA framework adapts an image classification CNN into a detector by: (1) adding a background class and fine-tuning shared layers on a subset of categories with detection labels (category-invariant adaptation); and (2) learning category-specific adjustments to the final layer for held-out classes by transferring the average “delta” learned on similar categories (nearest neighbors in the classification fc8 space). On ILSVRC13, with detection labels for 100 of 200 classes, LSDA improves mAP on the held-out 100 classes from ~10.3% to ~16.1% (+50% relative). The authors analyze error modes, show reduced localization and background confusions, and release a 7.6K-class detector and code.

Relation to prior work: R-CNN established fine-tuning for detection but requires boxes for all classes. WSOD/MIL methods train detectors from image-level labels but have not been shown at ImageNet scale; domain adaptation methods typically adapt between visual domains and need labels for all classes. LSDA’s novelty is adapting from classification to detection with only a subset of detection labels and transferring to held-out classes.

Strengths
- Clear and pragmatic formulation that separates category-invariant (shared feature) and category-specific (output layer) adaptation.
- Strong empirical evidence that most gains come from learning to model background and from fine-tuning shared layers; the simple category-specific transfer adds a smaller but statistically significant boost.
- Sensible and scalable evaluation on ILSVRC13 with a realistic split (100 with boxes, 100 without), including ablations and error diagnosis.
- Practical contribution: a 7.6K-category detector and a faster variant using SPP and fast proposals; code and models are released.
- The approach is architectural/simple and can be plugged into any ImageNet-trained classifier, which increases its potential impact.

Weaknesses
- The category-specific transfer is heuristic: using nearest neighbors in fc8 weight space is plausible but under-motivated; alternatives based on semantic hierarchies (WordNet), label embeddings, or feature-space similarity are not explored.
- Limited exploration of the adaptation frontier: no analysis across different sizes of B (fraction of classes with boxes), nor sensitivity to k in the neighbor averaging beyond a single best setting. Negative transfer risks are not quantified.
- Comparisons to WSOD/MIL are not provided, even on smaller-scale benchmarks (e.g., PASCAL) where they could be run; this limits the positioning against weakly supervised baselines when no boxes are available for A.
- The “upper bound” with boxes for all categories is referenced but the quantitative gap to LSDA is not fully detailed in the text; results without SVMs/bbox regression are compared, but it would be helpful to separate architectural choices from the adaptation contribution more rigorously.
- Notation and exposition around the output layer (fcA, fcB, δB, fc−BG) could be clearer; currently it’s easy to get lost in the bookkeeping.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The category-invariant adaptation via fine-tuning shared layers on detection data is well supported by the ablations and error analysis (reduced localization and background errors). The category-specific delta transfer is simple and gives a modest but statistically significant gain. The evaluation protocol on ILSVRC13 is reasonable, using region proposals and a standard split. However, the study would be stronger with:
- sensitivity to the number of adapted classes m and to k in Eq. (1),
- additional baselines for the category-specific transfer (e.g., semantic neighbors, random neighbors, full average, learned linear mapping from Wc to Wd),
- a head-to-head against a competitive WSOD baseline on a subset where feasible.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The high-level narrative and the experimental setup are easy to follow. Some notation can be streamlined: explicitly defining the output layer composition and avoiding overloading terms would help. Figures and tables (not shown here) are referenced appropriately; ensure all contain absolute numbers and confidence intervals where claims hinge on small deltas. Minor typos/formatting issues appear in the provided text.

Originality
The key idea—learning a generic classifier-to-detector transformation on a subset of classes and transferring to held-out classes—is novel relative to R-CNN and standard domain adaptation at the time. The delta-transfer mechanism is straightforward, bordering on heuristic; nevertheless, the setting and decomposition into invariant vs. specific components is new and practically relevant.

Significance
High practical significance: reduces annotation requirements for detection at scale and delivers a usable multi-thousand-class detector. Scientifically, it clarifies that most of the classifier-to-detector gap can be closed by learning background and fine-tuning shared features, while per-class deltas add smaller gains. The work is likely to influence subsequent research in large-scale detection and weakly/semi-supervised detection.

Questions and suggestions
- How does performance vary with m (e.g., 25, 50, 100, 150 adapted classes)? Is there a diminishing return?
- Sensitivity to k and neighbor definition: compare fc8-based neighbors to WordNet distance, attribute/word embeddings, or feature-space distances (e.g., fc7 class prototypes).
- Can you learn a linear mapping from Wc to Wd on B and apply it to A (instead of neighbor averaging)?
- Any evidence of negative transfer for A categories whose nearest B neighbors are semantically dissimilar?
- Could you train a class-agnostic bounding-box regressor and apply it to A to recover some of the loss from omitting bbox regression?
- Provide the numeric gap to the “oracle” detector with all boxes, and to R-CNN with SVMs/bbox regression on B, to contextualize the remaining headroom.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, practical problem with a simple, effective method.
- Solid empirical gains with careful analysis; code and large-scale models released.
- Clear message about what matters in classifier-to-detector adaptation.

Cons against acceptance
- Category-specific adaptation is heuristic and underexplored; limited analysis of sensitivity and alternatives.
- Missing comparisons to strong WSOD baselines (even on smaller datasets).
- Some exposition and quantitative details could be tightened.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning). Impactful and timely contribution with strong empirical evidence; could be further strengthened with additional analyses as suggested.

Reviewer confidence
High. Expertise in large-scale recognition, detection, and domain adaptation.
