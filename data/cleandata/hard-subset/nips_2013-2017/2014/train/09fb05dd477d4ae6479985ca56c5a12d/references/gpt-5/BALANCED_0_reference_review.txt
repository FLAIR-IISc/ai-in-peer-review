Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to convert image classifiers into object detectors when only a subset of categories has bounding-box annotations. The central idea is to view classification-to-detection as a domain adaptation problem: (1) learn a category-invariant transformation by fine-tuning an ImageNet-pretrained CNN on detection data for a subset B of classes, crucially including a background class; (2) learn category-specific adjustments by estimating the change from classifier to detector weights (δ) on B and transferring that change to held-out classes A via nearest neighbors in fc8 weight space; and (3) at test time, compute per-region scores for K classes plus background and use score_class − score_background as the detection score.

This builds on R-CNN (Girshick et al.), which adapts classification features to detection but requires bounding boxes for all categories, and on DeCAF (Donahue et al.) showing transferability of CNN features. Compared to weakly supervised detection via MIL (e.g., Song et al., Ali & Saenko), LSDA does not mine latent boxes for held-out categories; instead it transfers a learned generic “classification→detection” transformation. Unlike traditional domain adaptation (e.g., adaptive SVMs, Aytar & Zisserman), LSDA adapts across tasks (classification→detection) and to categories with no target labels.

On ILSVRC13 detection, with detection labels for 100 classes and only image-level labels for the other 100, LSDA improves mAP on held-out classes from 10.31% (classification-only baseline applied to proposals) to 16.15% (+50% relative), with most gains due to the category-invariant fine-tuning and learning a background class; the category-specific transfer adds a smaller but statistically significant improvement. Error analysis shows reduced localization and background confusions versus the baseline. They also demonstrate a 7.6K-category detector by combining 200 labeled detectors with 7.4K adapted classifiers, and a faster SPP/fast proposals variant.

Quality
- Soundness: The methodology is well motivated and reasonably executed. The ablation clearly supports the claim that category-invariant adaptation (including a learned background class) is the main driver of improvements; the simple category-specific transfer provides an incremental boost.
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments follow standard ILSVRC splits (val1/val2, with augmentation from training set). The main comparison is to a strong but simple baseline (classification-only on region proposals). An upper-bound “oracle” detector trained with all detection labels is reported.
- Missing comparisons: There is no quantitative comparison to contemporary weakly supervised detection methods (MIL variants) on ILSVRC; authors argue large-scale results were unavailable, but even smaller-scale comparisons (e.g., PASCAL) would be informative. The paper also omits comparisons to using semantic/WordNet similarity for δ transfer, or to more sophisticated transfer functions (e.g., learned regression from Wc to Wd).
- Analysis: The error breakdown (Loc/BG/Oth) is useful. More per-class or attribute-level analyses would strengthen the claims about which categories benefit most.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The three insights and training pipeline are explained succinctly.
- Some implementation details could be clearer: exact fine-tuning protocol (loss, learning rates, sampling ratios for positives/background), selection of k in nearest-neighbor transfer, and whether any calibration across classes was applied at test time.
- The decision to eschew SVMs and bbox regression is justified by flexibility/time, but a small study quantifying the trade-off on held-out categories would help.

Originality
- The idea of learning a generic classification→detection transformation from a subset of categories and transferring it to unseen categories appears novel and distinct from prior domain adaptation and weakly supervised detection work at the time. The nearest-neighbor δ transfer in classifier parameter space is a simple but original heuristic.

Significance
- The problem is important: scaling detectors without bounding boxes for every class. The gains on held-out categories are meaningful given the difficulty; the 7.6K demonstration highlights scalability and practical impact.
- Absolute mAP remains modest and far from the oracle; however, the approach is general, simple to implement, and likely to be adopted or extended. The background modeling insight and emphasis on category-invariant adaptation are valuable.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and practical, scalable solution.
- Strong ablation demonstrating where the gains come from.
- Insightful emphasis on learning background and category-invariant adaptation.
- Public release of models/code and large-scale 7.6K detector.
- Useful error analysis showing reduced localization/background mistakes.

Weaknesses
- Limited comparisons to alternative transfer strategies (semantic similarity, learned mappings) and to weakly supervised detectors.
- Category-specific adaptation yields relatively small gains; heuristic nature (fc8 NN in weight space) is under-justified.
- Evaluation focused on AlexNet-era backbone and a specific split; broader robustness experiments are limited.
- No per-class results or analysis of failure modes for visually fine-grained confusions, where Oth remains high.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive is performance to k in the δ averaging and to the choice of distance metric/space (fc8 vs earlier layers)?
- Did you try semantic neighbors (WordNet hierarchy or word embeddings) for δ transfer, or learning a regression from Wc to Wd?
- Can you report results with bbox regression trained only on B and shared across A? Similarly, any benefit from per-class calibration or score normalization?
- How does LSDA compare to a MIL baseline on a shared subset (e.g., PASCAL), even if not at 7.6K scale?

Suggestions for improvement
- Explore learned transfer functions (e.g., linear regression or MLP mapping Wc→Wd conditioned on category descriptors), and/or combine visual and semantic neighbors.
- Report per-class AP changes to identify which categories/types benefit most; analyze attributes (texture, deformability, size).
- Add experiments with more recent proposal methods and backbones (SPPNet results are a good start) and report the speed/accuracy trade-offs.
- Include an apples-to-apples comparison with a weakly supervised baseline on at least a subset to contextualize gains.

Decision
- Recommendation: Accept (weak).
- Justification: The paper addresses a timely and important problem with a simple, effective approach supported by solid ablations and a compelling large-scale demonstration. While some comparisons and deeper analysis are missing, the ideas are likely to influence follow-on work on scalable detection and task transfer.
