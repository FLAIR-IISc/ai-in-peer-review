Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to convert image classifiers into object detectors when only a subset of categories have bounding box annotations. The approach treats “classification-to-detection” as a form of task/domain adaptation. Concretely: (1) start with a CNN pretrained on ImageNet classification, (2) fine-tune all shared layers (conv and fc up to fc7) for detection using the categories with bounding boxes (set B), thereby learning a background class and a category-invariant transformation, and (3) perform a category-specific adaptation for held-out categories (set A) by adding an estimated delta to each class’s output weights, computed as the average change observed in its nearest neighbors (by fc8 similarity) within B. At test time, detection scores are class score minus background score, without SVMs or bbox regression to keep training time low. On ILSVRC13, with detection labels for 100/200 classes and only image-level labels for the remaining 100, LSDA improves held-out mAP from 10.31% (classification-only applied to proposals) to 16.15% (+50% relative), with ablations showing the shared feature adaptation and background class account for most of the gain; category-specific deltas add a small but significant boost. The paper also demonstrates a 7.6K-category detector by combining 200 detection-labeled categories with 7.4K classification-only categories.

This builds on R-CNN (Girshick et al.), which showed the value of fine-tuning for detection but required boxes for all categories. It relates to MIL-based weakly supervised detection (e.g., Ali & Saenko; Song et al.), and to domain adaptation for detection (Aytar & Zisserman; Xu et al.), but differs by adapting to categories with zero detection labels using deep features. It also touches on the trend of reusing CNN “deep features” across tasks (DeCAF).

Strengths
- Addresses a highly relevant scaling bottleneck: bounding box annotation cost for thousands of categories; offers a simple, practical recipe.
- Clear decomposition into category-invariant and category-specific components, with ablations quantifying their contributions; statistical testing included.
- Empirical evidence that learning a background class and fine-tuning shared layers is crucial to turn classifiers into detectors; good error analysis (Hoiem et al. protocol) showing reductions in background and localization errors.
- Demonstrates feasibility at scale (7.6K categories) and releases models/code; practical training-time advantages by avoiding SVM/bbox regression.
- Framed coherently within domain/task adaptation literature, with sensible use of CNN fine-tuning.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Absolute mAP on held-out categories remains low (16.15%); while the relative gain is meaningful, the gap to the oracle (fully supervised) is large and not narrowed by the simple category-specific adaptation.
- The nearest-neighbor delta transfer in fc8 is heuristic; limited exploration of alternatives (semantic/WordNet similarity, joint metric learning, or using post-fine-tuning features for similarity). The gain from this step is small compared to the invariant adaptation.
- No direct comparison to contemporary weakly supervised detection on a common dataset (e.g., PASCAL VOC) to contextualize performance when only image-level labels are available; even a smaller-scale comparison would help.
- The “fast” variant (SPP + fast proposals) is only briefly described; quantitative accuracy vs. speed trade-off is not reported.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized, but some details are missing or could be clarified: exact definition of background sampling; number of proposals per image; how negatives are constructed during fine-tuning; sensitivity to k (neighbors) beyond a single setting; and the performance “upper bound” details.
- Minor typographical issues and some repeated statements; figures referenced are essential for understanding but not all details are reproduced in text.

Originality
- The main novelty lies in using detection-labeled categories to learn a category-invariant, CNN-based adaptation and transferring that to held-out categories, plus a simple neighbor-averaged delta for class-specific adaptation. Conceptually incremental with respect to R-CNN fine-tuning and prior domain adaptation, but the specific setting (zero detection labels for some classes within a CNN) was underexplored at the time.

Significance
- Practical significance is solid: a workable path to large-scale detection with limited box annotations, with released resources likely to spur adoption. Scientific significance is moderate due to heuristic class-specific transfer and modest absolute accuracy; still, the result is a useful baseline and a step toward scalable detection.

Detailed suggestions
- Report performance as a function of the number of detection-labeled categories m (e.g., m ∈ {25, 50, 100, 200}) to quantify data efficiency and the scalability of category-invariant transfer.
- Explore stronger category-specific transfer: similarity in the fine-tuned feature space (fc7), learned similarity metrics, WordNet/semantic embeddings, or combinations thereof; consider a lightweight distillation or parameter regularization for fc8 of A.
- Incorporate weakly supervised mining for A (MIL on top-scoring proposals) initialized from LSDA to further close the gap to fully supervised performance; even small-scale results would be informative.
- Provide a quantitative comparison for the fast variant (mAP vs. runtime) and, where feasible, report the effect of adding bbox regression trained only on B but applied to A at test time.
- Expand error analysis: per-class AP for held-out categories, confusion matrices focusing on fine-grained confusions; measure how much of the remaining error is due to classification vs. localization after LSDA.
- Clarify training details: proposal generator and settings, background/negative sampling strategy, data augmentation, and the exact composition of detection vs. classification datasets to rule out leakage.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and timely problem with a simple, effective method.
- Clear empirical gains with ablations and error analysis.
- Demonstrates large-scale feasibility; resources released.

Cons for acceptance
- Modest absolute accuracy on held-out categories; gap to fully supervised remains large.
- Category-specific adaptation is heuristic with limited exploration.
- Missing comparisons to strong weakly supervised baselines on a common benchmark; limited quantification of fast variant.

Recommendation
Lean accept. While not a dramatic algorithmic leap, the paper provides a clear, well-executed approach to a problem of high practical importance, with convincing ablations and a compelling large-scale demonstration. Strengthening comparisons and category-specific adaptation would further improve the work, but the contribution is likely to be useful to the community.

Confidence
High: familiar with detection, domain adaptation, and R-CNN-era methodologies.
