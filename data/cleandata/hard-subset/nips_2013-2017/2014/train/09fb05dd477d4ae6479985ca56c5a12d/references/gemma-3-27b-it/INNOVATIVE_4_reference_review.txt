This paper presents LSDA, a compelling approach to large-scale object detection by adapting classifiers trained with image-level labels. The core idea – learning a transformation from classification to detection – is highly novel and addresses a critical bottleneck in scaling detection to a vast number of categories: the scarcity of bounding box annotations. While the methodology isn't flawlessly polished, the boldness of the concept and the significant experimental improvement (50% mAP) over a strong baseline earn it strong consideration. 

**Summary:** The authors tackle the problem of scaling object detection to a large number of categories given the annotation bottleneck of bounding boxes. They propose LSDA, which learns to adapt image classifiers (trained on readily available image-level labels) into object detectors, leveraging a smaller set of bounding box annotations for a subset of categories. This adaptation is framed as a domain adaptation problem, transferring knowledge from the classification domain to the detection domain. The approach involves category-invariant adaptation via fine-tuning shared layers and category-specific adaptation to refine outputs, even for categories lacking bounding box annotations.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The central idea of learning a transformation to adapt classifiers for detection is genuinely novel and addresses a crucial limitation in the field. The framework isn't simply fine-tuning; it’s *learning how to fine-tune* for detection.
* **Significance:** The potential impact is substantial. Scaling detection to tens or hundreds of thousands of categories is currently impractical with existing annotation requirements. This work offers a promising pathway toward that goal.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** A 50% relative mAP improvement is significant. The ablation study convincingly demonstrates the contribution of each component of LSDA. The demonstration of detectable performance on held-out categories with *no* bounding box data is particularly impressive.
* **Clear Conceptual Framework:** The paper presents a relatively clear description of the approach, framing it as a domain adaptation problem.
* **Publicly Available Code and Models:** Releasing code and models is a significant benefit for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors provide some qualitative error analysis, it’s somewhat superficial.  A more in-depth analysis of failure cases and the types of confusions made by the system would be valuable. The observation of confusion between similar categories (lemon/orange) suggests limitations in feature discrimination.
* **Simplifications and Assumptions:** The simplification of skipping SVM and regression training steps for faster testing, while understandable, sacrifices some performance. The reliance on nearest neighbor similarity for adapting categories *without* bounding box annotations is a reasonable heuristic but lacks rigorous justification.
* **Presentation Could Be Tighter:** Certain sections feel repetitive and could benefit from more concise writing.  Figure 2, while illustrating the process, could be visually clarified.
* **Comparison to MIL Methods:** While the paper acknowledges MIL approaches, it doesn't provide a direct empirical comparison on ImageNet detection. A head-to-head comparison would strengthen the claims.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The novelty and potential impact of LSDA are substantial. This work introduces a fundamentally new approach to large-scale detection.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate a significant improvement over a strong baseline.
* The paper addresses a critical practical challenge in the field.
* The authors have made their code and models publicly available, promoting reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The methodology is not flawless, and certain aspects (e.g., nearest neighbor adaptation) rely on heuristics.
* The error analysis is somewhat limited.
* A direct comparison to related MIL methods is lacking.




**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. While it isn’t a perfect piece of work, the boldness of the idea, the compelling experimental results, and the potential impact outweigh the minor methodological shortcomings. The authors have identified a fundamental challenge in scaling object detection and proposed a promising solution.  The paper pushes the boundaries of current research and introduces a novel direction for future exploration. I'd categorize this as a high-risk, high-reward submission that largely pays off.  Minor revisions addressing the error analysis and potentially adding a comparison to MIL methods would further strengthen the paper.
