

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

## Summary
This paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), an algorithm that transforms image classifiers into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The authors treat the problem as domain adaptation from classification (source domain) to detection (target domain), using a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations to learn a transformation applicable to categories without such annotations. Their method involves two key adaptations: category invariant adaptation (fine-tuning the network on categories with bounding box data) and category specific adaptation (using nearest neighbors to approximate category-specific parameters for categories without bounding box data). The paper demonstrates significant improvements (50% relative mAP) on ImageNet detection task with only 100 of 200 categories having bounding box annotations.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound and well-supported. The authors provide a clear ablation study (Figure 3, Table 1) showing that their category invariant adaptation is the most important component, while category specific adaptation provides additional gains. Their approach is carefully evaluated against a strong baseline (using raw classifier weights on region proposals), and they include error analysis (Figure 5) showing reduced localization errors and background confusion after adaptation. The implementation details are thorough, and they make their code and models publicly available, enhancing reproducibility. The paper is complete as a technical contribution rather than a position paper.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear from the outset, and the authors effectively position their work relative to R-CNN and other domain adaptation methods. The method is explained step-by-step with clear descriptions of both category invariant and category specific adaptation. The experimental setup and results are presented logically, with appropriate visualizations (Figures 3-6) that help illustrate their claims. The writing is concise yet sufficiently detailed for experts to reproduce the results.

## Originality
LSDA presents a novel approach to the problem of scaling object detection to thousands of categories. While previous work (R-CNN, MIL methods) has addressed related problems, LSDA's specific insight—that the transformation from classification to detection can be learned on a subset of categories and transferred to others—is original. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work: unlike R-CNN, it doesn't require bounding box data for all categories; unlike MIL methods, it's designed for large-scale detection; and unlike standard domain adaptation, it adapts both from a classification task to detection task and from a large visual domain to a smaller one. The authors properly cite relevant work and clearly articulate their contribution.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. Scaling object detection to tens of thousands of categories has been a major bottleneck in computer vision, as collecting bounding box annotations for all categories is prohibitively expensive. LSDA dramatically reduces this annotation burden, enabling detection for categories where bounding box data is unavailable. The 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories is impressive, and the authors demonstrate their approach on a 7,604 category detector, showcasing its practical utility. While the method still falls short of oracle performance (using full bounding box data for all categories), it represents a significant step toward enabling large-scale detection systems.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. Addresses a fundamental problem in computer vision with significant practical impact
2. Novel approach to domain adaptation for task transfer (classification to detection)
3. Strong empirical results with clear ablation study
4. Well-written and thoroughly evaluated
5. Code and models publicly available

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper doesn't directly compare to MIL methods on ImageNet (though they acknowledge these haven't been evaluated there)
2. The improvement is relative to a weak baseline (raw classifier weights on region proposals), not to R-CNN or other strong baselines
3. The category-specific adaptation using nearest neighbors is somewhat simplistic (though they test different k values)
4. The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss limitations or failure cases

## Recommendation

LSDA makes a significant contribution to the field of object detection by enabling detection for thousands of categories with minimal bounding box annotations. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with a novel approach. The results are compelling, and the authors have made their implementation publicly available.

While the paper could benefit from a direct comparison to MIL methods on ImageNet (which they acknowledge haven't been evaluated there) and a more thorough discussion of limitations, these do not significantly undermine the core contribution. The 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories is a substantial advancement in scaling detection, and the approach could enable practical applications that were previously infeasible.

I recommend acceptance to NIPS. This work advances the state of the art in a meaningful way, addresses a significant problem with practical impact, and is presented with clarity and technical rigor. It represents a valuable contribution to the field of computer vision and domain adaptation.
