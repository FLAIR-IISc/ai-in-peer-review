Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to transform an image classifier into an object detector when only a subset of categories has bounding-box annotations. The problem is framed as domain/task adaptation: the source domain is image-level classification data (available for all K categories), and the target is detection data (available for m ≪ K categories). The method proceeds in three steps: (1) start from an ImageNet-pretrained CNN (AlexNet), replace the final layer with K classifiers, and fine-tune on classification data; (2) perform category-invariant adaptation by fine-tuning layers 1–7 and learning an explicit background class using detection data for categories B, thereby learning a generic transformation from classification to detection; and (3) perform category-specific adaptation for categories A without bounding boxes by approximating the per-class parameter shift δ learned on B using nearest-neighbor averaging in fc8 weight space. At test time, region proposals are scored by the adapted network using scorei − scorebackground.

The approach is related to R-CNN (which requires detection data for all classes), to domain adaptation methods (which typically adapt between domains for the same tasks and require target labels for all classes), and to weakly supervised detection via MIL (which does not use any detection labels). LSDA occupies a distinct space: it uses detection labels for a subset of classes to learn a generic classifier-to-detector transformation, transferring that adaptation to held-out categories. Experiments on ILSVRC2013 show a 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories versus using classification-only weights on proposals. A 7.6K-class detector is demonstrated qualitatively, and a faster SPP-based variant is mentioned.

Quality
- Methodological soundness: The category-invariant adaptation via fine-tuning with a background class is technically sound and well motivated; it addresses the core gap between classification (no background) and detection (dominated by background) and the need for localization-sensitive features. The category-specific adaptation is simple (nearest-neighbor averaging of δ in fc8 space) and yields smaller yet statistically significant gains.
- Empirical support: Ablations indicate most gains come from the category-invariant adaptation and background modeling, with smaller gains from the class-specific δ transfer (k=10). Error analysis shows reduced localization and background confusions. Statistical testing (paired t-test) is reported for a key comparison.
- Missing comparisons: The paper does not compare against weakly supervised MIL methods on the same dataset (authors note such evaluations were not available), nor against other transfer baselines such as training linear SVMs on features from the detection-tuned shared layers for held-out classes, or using semantic/synset priors for class-specific transfer. A comparison to R-CNN with full pipeline (SVMs + bbox regression) is deferred due to training cost; an upper bound is reported, but a cost–accuracy trade-off analysis would strengthen the case.
- Reproducibility: The use of Caffe and release of models/code are positive. Some training details are light (sampling of positives/negatives, proposal settings, learning schedules), but the overall setup follows R-CNN conventions.

Clarity
The narrative and notation (sets A/B, Wc, Wd, δ) are clear. The key steps are described plainly and the intuition for each is explicit. The paper would benefit from tighter presentation (typos, truncated footnotes/references, clearer figure references) and more specifics on hyperparameters (k selection, distance metric normalization, mining strategy), but the central ideas are understandable.

Originality
The main novelty is in learning a general classifier-to-detector transformation (including an explicit background model and feature adaptation) from a subset of categories and transferring it to held-out categories—distinct from prior domain adaptation (same task) and prior detection transfer methods that require detection labels per class. The class-specific δ transfer via nearest neighbors in weight space is simple but novel in this context.

Significance
The problem is important: scaling detection to thousands of categories without bounding boxes for all. The reported gains on held-out classes are meaningful (relative +50% mAP) though absolute mAP remains modest. The demonstration of a 7.6K-class detector underscores applicability. Given the prevalence of pretraining and transfer, this approach could influence large-scale detection systems and spur follow-on work integrating weak supervision or semantics.

Pros
- Addresses a practical and impactful setting: detection with few annotated categories.
- Clear decomposition into category-invariant and category-specific adaptations; strong empirical evidence that background modeling and feature adaptation matter most.
- Ablation studies and error analysis are informative; statistical significance reported.
- Code/models released; demonstration at unprecedented scale (7.6K classes).

Cons
- Limited baselines: no direct comparison to strong weakly supervised detection on ILSVRC, to semantic transfer, or to simple feature-transfer baselines tailored for held-out classes.
- Class-specific adaptation is heuristic and yields small gains relative to the invariant component; alternatives are not explored.
- Evaluation confined to ILSVRC2013 val2 split; no results on PASCAL VOC or COCO; calibration and per-class breakdowns are limited.
- Some presentation issues (incomplete references, minor clarity gaps) and sparse training details.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines: (a) train linear SVMs for held-out classes on features from the detection-tuned shared layers; (b) compare to MIL-based weakly supervised detectors on ILSVRC if feasible; (c) incorporate semantic similarity (e.g., WordNet distances or label embeddings) for δ transfer and compare to fc8-neighbor averaging.
- Analyze k sensitivity and the choice of similarity metric; report per-class effects and whether visually/semantically similar neighbors help most.
- Provide more training details (sampling ratios, proposal settings, learning rates/schedules) and report calibration of the scorei − scorebackground heuristic versus softmax probabilities.
- Include results with bounding box regression or modern proposal methods to quantify the cost–accuracy trade-off.
- Evaluate on an additional benchmark or a held-out subset of ImageNet to test generality.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles a timely and important problem with a simple, effective approach that is well supported by ablations and analysis. While some comparisons and details are missing and the class-specific transfer is rudimentary, the category-invariant adaptation and background modeling contributions are clear and practically valuable. The work is within NeurIPS scope and makes a solid empirical contribution to large-scale detection through adaptation. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening baselines and clarifying details.
