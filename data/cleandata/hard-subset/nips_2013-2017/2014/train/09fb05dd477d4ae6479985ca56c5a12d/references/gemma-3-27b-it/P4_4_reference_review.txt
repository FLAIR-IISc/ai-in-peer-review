The paper proposes LSDA, an algorithm for adapting image classifiers into object detectors, addressing the challenge of limited bounding box annotations for large-scale detection. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain (image-level labels) to the detection domain (bounding boxes and labels) using supervised CNNs, effectively transferring knowledge from categories with abundant classification data to those lacking detection annotations. The approach frames this as a domain adaptation problem, focusing on category-invariant and category-specific transformations. Experiments on ImageNet demonstrate a 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline using raw classifier weights.

The paper is well-motivated, tackling a practical problem in scaling object detection to a large number of categories. The idea of leveraging readily available image-level annotations to boost detector performance is compelling. The authors appropriately connect their work to prior art in domain adaptation, MIL, and deep learning-based detection, citing relevant papers (e.g., Girshick et al. [1], Ali et al. [6], Krizhevsky et al. [4]). The experimental setup is clearly described, and the ablation studies provide insights into the contributions of different components of the LSDA algorithm. The quantitative results are significant, showcasing a substantial improvement in mAP. The release of the model and code is a positive contribution to the community.

However, several aspects could be improved. While the explanation of the three key insights (background recognition, category invariance, category specificity) is present, the technical details of *how* these insights are implemented in the algorithm could be more detailed. For example, the nearest neighbor selection for category-specific adaptation (Equation 1) feels somewhat ad-hoc; a more rigorous justification or exploration of alternative methods would strengthen the paper. The ablation study is helpful, but lacks statistical significance tests beyond the one mentioned for category-specific adaptation. Adding p-values for the other ablation results would improve rigor. The error analysis (Figure 5) is qualitatively interesting, but the quantitative metrics (e.g., percentages) associated with each error type would be beneficial. The comparisons to existing methods are somewhat limited. While the authors mention MIL-based approaches, a more direct quantitative comparison with state-of-the-art MIL methods on ImageNet (if feasible) would be valuable. Furthermore, the paper states the benefit of reducing localization error and demonstrates that qualitatively, but doesn't quantify the improvement in localization performance.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a significant problem in scaling object detection.
*   Novel approach leveraging classification data for detector adaptation.
*   Strong quantitative results on ImageNet.
*   Clear explanation of the core idea and experimental setup.
*   Code and models released for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Technical details of some implementation choices are lacking.
*   Limited statistical analysis in ablation studies.
*   Error analysis lacks quantitative metrics.
*   Comparison to related work could be more comprehensive.
*   Localization performance improvement not quantified.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper proposes a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a significant improvement over a strong baseline.
*   The release of code and models promotes reproducibility and further research.
*   The problem addressed is relevant to the NIPS audience.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Some aspects of the technical implementation could be more clearly explained and justified.
*   The statistical analysis could be more thorough.
*   The comparison to related work could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of object detection. While there are areas for improvement, the novelty, technical soundness, and significance of the results outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above. Specifically, more detail on implementation choices, additional statistical analysis, and a more thorough comparison to related work would strengthen the paper.
