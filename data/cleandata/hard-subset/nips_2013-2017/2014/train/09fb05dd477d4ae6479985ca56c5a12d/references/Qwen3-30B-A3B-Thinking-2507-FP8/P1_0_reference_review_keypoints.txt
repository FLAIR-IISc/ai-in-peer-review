- Summary: LSDA adapts image classifiers into object detectors via a three-stage process (category-invariant fine-tuning, background learning, category-specific nearest-neighbor transfer), improving mAP and scaling to 7,604 categories.

- Strength: Addresses a practical bottleneck by reducing need for bounding-box annotations and enabling large-scale detection.

- Strength: Clear, well-motivated three-stage technical approach with reproducible implementation details and ablation studies.

- Strength: Strong experimental validation showing a statistically significant ~50% relative mAP improvement over the baseline.

- Strength: Demonstrates scalability with a 7,604-category detector, illustrating real-world applicability.

- Strength: Well-written and organized paper with effective figures supporting the narrative.

- Weakness: Nearest-neighbor category adaptation is heuristic and lacks theoretical justification or exploration of alternatives (e.g., semantic similarity).

- Weakness: Evaluation is limited to a single held-out split (first 100 of 200 categories) with no analysis across semantic groups or per-category difficulty.

- Weakness: The “50% relative mAP improvement” claim can be misleading without explicit absolute numbers (10.31% → 15.85%, 5.54 absolute).

- Weakness: Lacks direct comparison to recent weakly supervised detection methods (e.g., MIL-based approaches).

- Weakness: Computational cost and training-time claims lack hardware/context details and clarity about which phases they refer to.

- Quality Assessment: Methodologically sound, experiments thorough, reproducible, and statistically validated.

- Clarity Assessment: Exceptionally clear and accessible writing; nearest-neighbor method could use a more detailed example.

- Originality Assessment: Novel framing of adapting to held-out categories without detection data; distinct from R-CNN, MIL, and prior domain-adaptation work.

- Significance Assessment: Meaningful advancement toward scaling detection to tens of thousands of categories with strong practical implications.

- Overall Assessment: Strong paper with significant contribution; minor weaknesses do not outweigh the strengths.

- Arguments for Acceptance: Important problem, novel scalable solution, strong experimental evidence, reproducible code/models, and clear presentation.

- Arguments Against Acceptance: Heuristic components, limited evaluation scope, ambiguous performance framing, and incomplete comparisons to recent weakly supervised methods.

- Recommendation: Accept.
