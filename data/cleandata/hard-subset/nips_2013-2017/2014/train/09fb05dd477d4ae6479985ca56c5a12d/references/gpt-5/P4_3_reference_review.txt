Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to adapt image classifiers into object detectors when only a subset of target categories has bounding box annotations. The authors cast the problem as domain/task adaptation: the source is image-level classification data across K categories, and the target is detection data (region-level, including a background class) available for only m categories (m << K). LSDA learns (1) a category-invariant transformation by fine-tuning layers 1–7 of a CNN (AlexNet) with detection data for the m classes, including learning a background class; and (2) a modest category-specific transformation of the output layer for classes without boxes by transferring the average weight delta from nearest neighbor categories (based on fc8 similarity) that do have detection data. At test time, the model outputs per-region scores for K categories plus background and uses score_i − score_background as the detection score, without additional SVMs or bounding box regression.

This work builds directly on R-CNN, which fine-tunes a classification CNN for detection using bounding boxes for all target categories. LSDA departs by requiring detection labels for only a subset, using those to learn category-invariant and category-specific changes that are then applied to held-out categories. The method is complementary to weakly supervised/MIL approaches that attempt to learn detectors without any boxes, and to prior domain-adaptation methods (adaptive SVMs, latent SVMs) that assume detection data for source and target categories. LSDA appears to be the first to adapt to “unseen-for-detection” categories at scale.

Empirically, on ILSVRC2013 detection: the authors simulate access to classification labels for all 200 classes and detection boxes for only the first 100. On the 100 held-out categories, LSDA improves mAP from 10.31% (classification-only baseline applied to region proposals) to 15.85% via category-invariant adaptation, and to 16.15% with nearest-neighbor output-layer adaptation (k=10), a 50% relative boost. Ablations show the dominant gain comes from adapting the shared feature representation and learning a background class; category-specific adaptation gives smaller, but statistically significant, gains. Error analysis indicates reduced localization and background confusions after adaptation. The authors also demonstrate a 7,604-category detector by combining 200 categories with boxes and 7,404 with only image-level labels and provide a faster SPP-based implementation.

Strengths
- Problem formulation: Tackles a practical and important setting—scaling detection to many categories with limited bounding box supervision—by leveraging abundant classification data.
- Method design: Clear decomposition into category-invariant (feature + background) and category-specific (output-layer) adaptations; nearest-neighbor transfer is simple and efficient.
- Empirical validation: Ablation analysis isolates the contributions of background class and shared-layer fine-tuning; provides a statistical significance test; includes error breakdowns that align with the proposed adaptation goals (improving localization and background rejection).
- Scope and impact: Demonstration of a 7.6K-category detector suggests scalability. Training time reductions and released code/models increase practical significance and reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Comparisons to alternative baselines: The main quantitative baseline is “classification-only on proposals.” It would strengthen the case to include: (a) fine-tune on the m categories and directly use those shared features for held-out categories without the output-layer transfer (partly covered), (b) use semantic/hierarchical similarity (e.g., WordNet) or visual fc7 embeddings instead of fc8 for nearest neighbors, (c) train image-level SVMs on held-out categories with region proposals to compare against the δ-transfer, and (d) compare to weakly supervised/MIL baselines (even if not at full ILSVRC scale, a subset study would be informative).
- Category-specific adaptation: The nearest-neighbor averaging of δ in fc8 is heuristic; the paper would benefit from a more thorough sensitivity analysis over k and similarity metrics, and a per-class analysis showing when transfer helps or hurts (especially for fine-grained/similar classes where “Oth” errors persist).
- Limited absolute performance: While the relative improvement is substantial, absolute mAP on held-out categories remains low (~16%). Discussion of failure modes beyond the error taxonomy (e.g., small object sizes, co-occurrence) and potential remedies (e.g., transferring bounding box regression, multi-scale testing) would be useful.
- Clarity of data splits: The text states the ILSVRC training set is used as “classification data” but later mentions augmenting detection training with 1,000 bbox-annotated images per category from the training set. Clarify whether there is overlap or how disjoint subsets are ensured, and provide exact counts per category used for detection fine-tuning.
- Calibration and post-processing: The decision to avoid SVMs/regression is motivated by flexibility and training time; still, a controlled experiment showing the net effect on the m supervised categories (and whether those learned components could be transferred) would quantify the trade-offs.

Quality
- The approach is technically sound and built on well-established components (CNN fine-tuning, region proposals). Ablations and statistical testing support the main claims. Some design choices (nearest-neighbor δ transfer) are heuristic and would benefit from deeper analysis or stronger baselines.

Clarity
- Generally clear, with a coherent narrative and figures that match claims. Notational details around fcA/fcB/δB and the background output could be streamlined. The description of dataset splits and augmentations should be made unambiguous.

Originality
- Adapting from classification to detection for categories without bounding boxes using a learned category-invariant transformation plus lightweight category-specific transfer is novel relative to R-CNN and prior adaptation/MIL literature.

Significance
- The setting is important, and demonstrated gains and scalability suggest that others can build on this. Code and models increase impact. The absolute performance gap to fully supervised detectors remains, indicating room for future work.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a timely, practically important problem (large-scale detection with limited boxes).
- Clear method with solid ablations; statistically significant improvements on a challenging benchmark.
- Scalable demonstration (7.6K classes) and released resources.
- Insightful error analysis aligning with the method’s intended effect.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing stronger baselines and comparisons to alternative transfer signals and weakly supervised methods.
- Category-specific adaptation is relatively weak and heuristic; limited analysis of when/why it helps.
- Absolute mAP remains low on held-out categories; some engineering choices (e.g., no bbox regression) may limit performance.

Overall, the paper presents a clear, technically sound approach with meaningful empirical gains in an important setting. Strengthening comparative baselines and clarifying data usage would further improve the contribution.
