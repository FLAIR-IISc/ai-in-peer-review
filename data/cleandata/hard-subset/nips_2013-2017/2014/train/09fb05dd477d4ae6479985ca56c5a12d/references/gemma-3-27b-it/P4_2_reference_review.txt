The paper "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation" proposes a method for adapting image classifiers into object detectors, leveraging the abundance of image-level labeled data and scarcity of bounding box annotations. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain to the detection domain using supervised CNNs, focusing on category-invariant and category-specific adaptations. The authors demonstrate significant improvements on the ImageNet detection task, particularly for categories without bounding box labels, achieving a 50% relative mAP gain over a baseline.

This work builds upon the established connection between classification and detection pioneered by R-CNN [1], but addresses a critical limitation: the need for bounding box annotations for all categories. It also relates to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approaches [6, 7] which utilize weak labels, but distinguishes itself by focusing on domain adaptation and explicitly transferring knowledge from classifiers to detectors.  The method also draws connections to domain adaptation literature [12, 13, 14, 15, 16], but extends it to adapt both the visual domain *and* the task (classification to detection).  The novelty lies in the targeted adaptation strategy, learning a transformation applicable to any pre-trained classifier, rather than requiring end-to-end training with limited bounding box data.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a significant problem:** The paper tackles the bottleneck of bounding box annotation in object detection, enabling scaling to a large number of categories.
*   **Novel approach:** The LSDA algorithm offers a fresh perspective on transferring knowledge from classification to detection, focusing on domain adaptation.
*   **Strong experimental results:** The reported 50% relative mAP improvement on the ImageNet detection task is substantial.
*   **Clear explanation:** The paper provides a relatively clear explanation of the algorithm, with accompanying diagrams.
*   **Ablation study:** The thorough ablation study (Figure 3, Table 1) effectively demonstrates the contribution of each component of the LSDA algorithm.
*   **Error Analysis:** The inclusion of an error analysis provides valuable insight into the behavior of the model and identifies areas for improvement.
*   **Publicly available resources:** The release of the 7.6K model and code encourages reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited comparison to recent detectors:** While comparisons are made to a classification-only baseline, the paper lacks a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art detectors trained with sufficient bounding box annotations, beyond establishing an upper bound. A comparison to more recent one-stage detectors like YOLO or SSD would be helpful.
*   **Sensitivity to nearest neighbor selection:** The category-specific adaptation relies on finding nearest neighbors in the feature space. The choice of *k* (number of neighbors) could significantly impact performance, and a more robust selection strategy or sensitivity analysis might be warranted.
*   **Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions optimizations for faster detection, the initial training and adaptation process might still be computationally expensive, especially for a very large number of categories.
*   **Assumptions about Classification Data Quality:** The experiments assume high-quality classification data. The analysis doesn't adequately address the impact of noisy or inaccurate classification labels.
*   **Lack of detail on ImageNet split:** The specific method of splitting ImageNet into classification and detection sets is not completely clear and could affect the fairness of the evaluation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a crucial problem in large-scale object detection.
*   The proposed approach is novel and demonstrates significant performance gains.
*   The experimental results are compelling and supported by a thorough ablation study.
*   The publicly available resources will foster further research in this area.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of comparison to more recent and competitive detectors limits the assessment of the practical significance of the results.
*   The sensitivity to the nearest neighbor selection in category-specific adaptation raises concerns about the robustness of the algorithm.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of object detection, offering a practical solution to the problem of limited bounding box annotations. The proposed LSDA algorithm is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong experimental results.  While some comparisons to state-of-the-art detectors are missing and the sensitivity of nearest neighbor selection is a concern, the overall quality of the work warrants acceptance. The publicly available resources further strengthen the paper’s impact and potential for future research.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
