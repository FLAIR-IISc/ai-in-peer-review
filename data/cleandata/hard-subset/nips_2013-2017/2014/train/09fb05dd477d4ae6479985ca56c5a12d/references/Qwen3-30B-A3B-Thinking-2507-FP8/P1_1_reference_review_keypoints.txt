- LSDA transforms image classifiers into object detectors without bounding box annotations for all categories by treating classification→detection as a domain adaptation problem.
- Key contributions: modeling background during adaptation, transferring category-invariant feature changes, and handling category-specific shifts via nearest-neighbor approximation.
- Method: fine-tune a classification CNN on a small set of detection-annotated categories and apply learned transformations to held-out categories.
- Builds on R-CNN insight about fine-tuning and connects to domain adaptation and weakly-supervised detection literature.
- Strong practical impact: addresses scalability of detection to thousands of categories and shows a 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories.
- Technical soundness: well-founded methodology with an informative ablation study demonstrating each component’s contribution.
- Clear problem formulation and effective experimental design, including error analysis and a 7,604-category detector demonstration.
- Practical implementation with publicly available code and models aids reproducibility and adoption.
- Limited direct comparison to MIL-based weakly-supervised methods on ImageNet weakens positioning against related approaches.
- Ambiguity in held-out category selection (first 100 alphabetically) raises potential selection-bias concerns.
- Lack of theoretical justification for the nearest-neighbor approximation used for category-specific adaptation.
- Potential overfitting or baseline weakness concerns due to limited per-class training data relative to full ImageNet.
- Ambiguous phrasing around the “50% relative mAP improvement” — unclear baseline and absolute numbers are not clearly stated.
- Quality: technically sound with convincing experiments, though theoretical analysis is lacking for some components.
- Clarity: generally well-written and organized, but could better distinguish category-invariant vs category-specific adaptation.
- Originality: novel application of domain adaptation for scaling detection to many held-out categories.
- Significance: addresses a critical bottleneck in large-scale detection and demonstrates substantial empirical gains and scalability.
- Recommendation: accept — important problem, solid solution, and compelling results despite some limitations.
- Suggested improvements: compare to MIL-based methods, report absolute mAP numbers, justify/clarify held-out category selection, add theoretical discussion for nearest-neighbor step, and compare to state-of-the-art weakly-supervised detection.
- Final assessment: a novel, practical, and well-validated approach that meaningfully advances large-scale object detection despite some comparative and theoretical gaps.
