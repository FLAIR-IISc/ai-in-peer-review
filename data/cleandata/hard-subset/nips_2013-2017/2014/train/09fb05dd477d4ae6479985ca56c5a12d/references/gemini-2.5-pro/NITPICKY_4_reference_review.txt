**Summary**

This paper addresses the challenge of scaling object detection to thousands of categories, for which bounding box annotations are prohibitively expensive to collect. The authors propose an algorithm, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), which learns to transform an image classifier into an object detector. The core idea is to leverage a small set of "source" categories with full bounding box annotation to learn a generic transformation. This transformation is then applied to adapt classifiers for "target" categories that only have image-level labels. The method consists of two main stages: 1) a category-invariant adaptation, where the feature extraction layers of a CNN are fine-tuned on the source detection data, and 2) a category-specific adaptation, where the final classification layer for target categories is adjusted based on the average adjustments learned for its nearest neighbors among the source categories. The authors demonstrate a 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories on a simulated ILSVRC2013 setup.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance and Motivation:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and important problem. The gap between the scalability of image classification and object detection is a major bottleneck in computer vision, and this work proposes a pragmatic approach to bridge it. The motivation is clear and compelling.
*   **Novelty:** The concept of learning a transferable mapping from a classifier to a detector is a novel contribution. Framing this as a domain adaptation problem where target categories have no direct supervision is an interesting perspective that distinguishes it from prior work in domain adaptation or weakly-supervised learning.
*   **Empirical Results:** The reported +50% relative improvement in mAP on held-out categories over a strong classification baseline is substantial. The error analysis in Section 4.3 effectively demonstrates that the adaptation successfully reduces localization and background confusion errors, which is the primary challenge in shifting from classification to detection.

**Weaknesses**

While the core idea is promising, the paper is undermined by a lack of methodological rigor, imprecise language, and a significant number of presentation and formatting flaws.

*   **Methodological Concerns:**
    1.  The term "transformation" is used loosely throughout the paper. In reality, the "category-invariant transformation" is simply standard fine-tuning of shared layers on a subset of classes. The claim that this induces a "generic" transformation is an empirical observation, not a well-defined or theoretically justified property of the process.
    2.  The category-specific adaptation proposed in Equation (1) is a purely heuristic method. The assumption that visually similar classifiers (by Euclidean distance of weights) should undergo similar adaptations for detection is not fundamentally justified. One could easily conceive of visually similar objects (e.g., different types of chairs) that require very different adaptations to distinguish their full extent from cluttered backgrounds. The choice of `k=10` neighbors is also presented without ablation or justification.
    3.  The description of learning `δB` in Section 3.2 is confusing and seems mathematically imprecise. The paper states learning a zero-initialized `δB` is "equivalent" to fine-tuning `fcB`. This is not strictly true; `δB` is simply the *result* of the fine-tuning (`W_d - W_c`), not a separately learned component with an equivalent loss. This lack of precision clouds the understanding of the method.
    4.  The detection scoring `score_i - score_background` (Section 3.3) is non-standard for a softmax output layer and lacks justification. Why is a simple subtraction the optimal way to combine these scores? This seems to be another ad-hoc choice that works empirically but lacks a principled foundation.

*   **Experimental Evaluation:**
    1.  The experimental setup relies on an arbitrary alphabetical split of the first 100 classes for training. This is not a robust evaluation protocol. The performance could be highly dependent on the specific classes chosen for adaptation. A more convincing evaluation would involve multiple random splits to demonstrate that the results are not an artifact of this particular partition.
    2.  The quantitative results in Table 1 are reported without any measure of variance (e.g., standard deviation across runs). The claim of statistical significance with a p-value is weak, as it is based on a paired test across categories within a single, arbitrary data split.
    3.  The comparison to the "upper bound" is somewhat misleading. The footnote clarifies that this bound omits the SVM and bounding box regression steps used by R-CNN, which are known to add several mAP points. This deflates the upper bound and makes the performance gap of LSDA appear smaller than it is relative to the true state-of-the-art oracle. Similarly, the training time comparison (5.5 hours vs. 3 days) is not apples-to-apples, as LSDA entirely skips the computationally expensive SVM training step.

*   **Clarity and Presentation:**
    1.  The paper suffers from a general lack of polish. There are typographical errors, such as `m K` which should likely be `m << K` (Section 3). The footnote on the first page containing acknowledgements is non-standard for this conference format.
    2.  The reference list is exceptionally poorly formatted. Multiple entries (e.g., [1], [3], [4], [10]) are incomplete, missing years, page numbers, or conference names, and simply end in ",, , ,". This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
    3.  The qualitative results for the "7604 category detector" in Figure 6 are purely anecdotal. While visually appealing, they provide no basis for judging the actual performance of the large-scale model.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and highly practical idea for scaling object detection.
*   The empirical results, despite the experimental design flaws, are strong and suggest the method is effective.
*   The work could inspire further research into learning transferable representations between related vision tasks.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The methodology relies on poorly justified heuristics, particularly the category-specific adaptation step.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks the necessary rigor to establish the generalizability of the results (e.g., using a single arbitrary data split).
*   The paper is marred by significant presentation issues, including unclear technical descriptions, typos, and a severely flawed reference section, which suggests a lack of careful preparation.

**Decision:**
I recommend **rejection**. While the paper is built on a strong and interesting premise, the execution is lacking. The combination of heuristic methodological choices, a non-robust experimental design, and a striking lack of polish in the presentation and references prevents me from recommending acceptance. The paper would require a major revision to address the methodological justifications, strengthen the experimental validation, and correct the numerous formatting and clarity issues before it meets the standards for NIPS.
