

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

This paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), an approach to transform image classifiers into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The authors frame the classification-to-detection transformation as a domain adaptation problem, where the source domain consists of image-level classification data and the target domain consists of bounding box-labeled detection data. The key insight is that a small subset of categories with both classification and detection labels can be used to learn a transformation that adapts classifiers for categories without bounding box annotations.

The paper relates well to previous work, particularly R-CNN (Girshick et al.) which demonstrated the importance of adaptation from classification to detection, but required bounding box data for all categories. It also connects to domain adaptation literature, though the authors could better distinguish their task (simultaneous domain and task adaptation) from previous domain adaptation approaches that only adapt between visual domains while keeping the task fixed.

**Strengths:**

1. The problem addressed is highly relevant and important - scaling detection to large numbers of categories is a major bottleneck in computer vision due to the high cost of bounding box annotations.

2. The approach is technically sound with a clear three-stage adaptation process (category invariant adaptation, category specific adaptation, and detection with LSDA).

3. The experimental results are impressive, showing a 50% relative mAP improvement over the baseline on held-out categories. The ablation study effectively demonstrates the importance of each component.

4. The demonstration of a 7,600-category detector is a significant contribution that showcases the scalability of the approach.

5. The paper is well-written with clear figures and provides code and model weights, enhancing reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The theoretical justification for the nearest-neighbor approach for category-specific adaptation is weak. The paper assumes categories with similar classification parameters will have similar detection parameters, but provides no evidence or analysis to support this assumption. The paper should either provide stronger justification or explore alternative approaches for category-specific adaptation.

2. The paper doesn't adequately distinguish its work from related weakly supervised detection methods. While it mentions Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approaches, it doesn't compare with or discuss how LSDA relates to these methods, which is crucial for establishing novelty.

3. The baseline comparison is somewhat vague - the paper states "50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline of just using raw classifier weights on object proposal regions" but doesn't provide a clear baseline for comparison to other methods in the literature.

4. The paper doesn't sufficiently address potential limitations of the approach. For example, how well does it perform for categories with very different visual characteristics from the source categories? How does it scale to truly massive category sets (e.g., 100,000+ categories)?

5. The paper claims "significant improvement" but doesn't provide statistical significance for all results, which is expected in NIPS papers.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Conduct a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art weakly supervised detection methods (e.g., MIL-based approaches) to better establish the novelty and advantages of LSDA.

2. Provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for the nearest-neighbor approach, or explore alternative methods for category-specific adaptation and compare their performance.

3. Clarify the baseline comparison by providing explicit mAP numbers for the baseline and showing comparison to other methods in the literature.

4. Include a discussion of the limitations of the approach and potential failure cases, particularly for categories with very different visual characteristics from the source categories.

5. Add statistical significance tests for all performance comparisons to meet NIPS standards.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper addresses an important problem with a technically sound approach that demonstrates impressive results, the lack of clear distinction from related work, weak theoretical justification for a key component, and insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art methods prevent it from meeting the high standards of NIPS. The significance of the work is clear, but the novelty and technical contribution need stronger justification.

I recommend rejection, but with the understanding that with significant revisions addressing the weaknesses above, the paper could be a strong candidate for future submission. The authors should focus on better situating their work within the existing literature on weakly supervised detection and domain adaptation, provide stronger justification for their key methodological choices, and conduct a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
