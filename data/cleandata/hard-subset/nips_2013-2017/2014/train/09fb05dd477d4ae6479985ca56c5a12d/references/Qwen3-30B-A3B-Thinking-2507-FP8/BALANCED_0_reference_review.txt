

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

## Summary
This paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), an approach to transform image classifiers into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The authors frame the problem as a domain adaptation task, where the source domain is image-level classification data and the target domain is bounding box detection data. They train on a subset of categories with bounding box annotations (set B) and adapt the classifier to categories without annotations (set A) using two key components: category invariant adaptation (fine-tuning shared layers using set B data) and category specific adaptation (using nearest neighbors in the classification feature space to approximate detection weights for set A categories). The method achieves a 50% relative mAP improvement over a classification-only baseline on held-out categories in the ImageNet detection benchmark.

The work relates to prior NIPS contributions on domain adaptation (e.g., [12-16]) and object detection (R-CNN [1]), but extends these by addressing the problem of adapting to categories with no bounding box data. While related to weakly supervised detection methods like MIL [6,7], LSDA is distinguished by its focus on adapting classifiers to detectors rather than training detectors directly from weak labels.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with thorough experimental validation. The ablation study effectively isolates the contribution of each component (category invariant vs. category specific adaptation). The statistical significance of the results (p=0.017) is appropriately reported, and the authors carefully acknowledge limitations (e.g., the gap to oracle performance). The implementation details are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The core insight of adapting classifiers to detectors for held-out categories represents a meaningful contribution to the literature. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly in the domain adaptation section where they note that previous methods adapt between visual domains with fixed tasks, while LSDA adapts both from classification to detection and from a large to small visual domain. The demonstration of a 7,604 category detector is a compelling practical demonstration of the approach's scalability.

**Significance:** The problem addressed (scaling detection to tens of thousands of categories) is highly relevant to the field, as bounding box annotation costs are prohibitive for large-scale detection. The 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories is substantial, and the method significantly reduces the annotation burden. The authors' demonstration of a practical 7,604 category detector shows clear potential for real-world impact.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and effectively uses figures to illustrate key concepts (Figures 1-6). The mathematical formulation is precise, and the algorithm description is accessible. The implementation details (using Caffe) and code availability further enhance clarity and reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

**Comparison to Related Work:** While the paper cites relevant work, it lacks a direct comparison to state-of-the-art weakly supervised detection methods (e.g., MIL-based approaches [6,7]) that could be directly compared to LSDA on the same benchmark. The authors state that MIL methods "have not yet been evaluated on the large-scale ImageNet detection challenge to allow for direct comparison," but this is an opportunity to include a side-by-side comparison of LSDA with existing weakly supervised methods on the same dataset.

**Absolute Performance:** The absolute mAP on held-out categories (16.15%) is relatively low compared to state-of-the-art detection methods (e.g., R-CNN achieves ~20-30% mAP on PASCAL VOC). The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how this performance compares to the state of the art for large-scale detection, rather than focusing solely on the relative improvement over the classification baseline.

**Category-Specific Adaptation Justification:** The nearest-neighbor approach for category-specific adaptation (Equation 1) is not thoroughly justified. Why k=10 is optimal is not explained, and the paper does not explore whether other similarity measures (beyond Euclidean distance on fc8 weights) might yield better results. The claim that "the classification model being most similar and hence, we assume, the detection model should be most similar" is a strong assumption that could be better supported.

**Evaluation Protocol:** The paper's evaluation protocol (using only 5,000 detection images for training) seems restrictive compared to standard detection benchmarks. The authors should clarify why they chose this particular split and how it compares to standard practice in the field.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with clear novelty and significant practical implications for scaling object detection to large numbers of categories. The technical contribution is sound, the experiments are well-designed, and the results demonstrate a meaningful improvement over the baseline. The main weaknesses are the limited comparison to state-of-the-art weakly supervised methods and the relatively low absolute performance numbers.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Including a direct comparison with weakly supervised detection methods (e.g., MIL approaches) on the same ImageNet detection benchmark
2. Providing a more detailed justification for the nearest-neighbor approach in category-specific adaptation
3. Discussing the absolute performance in the context of state-of-the-art detection methods
4. Explaining the evaluation protocol more thoroughly

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by addressing the critical problem of scaling object detection to large numbers of categories. While there are some weaknesses in the comparison to related work and the evaluation protocol, these are addressable with minor revisions. The technical quality is strong, the method is novel, and the significance of the problem is clear. The authors' demonstration of a 7,604 category detector is particularly compelling and shows the practical potential of their approach.

This paper would make a valuable contribution to the NeurIPS proceedings, and I believe it meets the high standards of the conference.
