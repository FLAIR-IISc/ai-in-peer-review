LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation is a well-written and motivated paper addressing the crucial problem of scaling object detection to a large number of categories. The core idea – adapting classifiers trained on image-level labels to detectors – is compelling and addresses a significant bottleneck in current detection pipelines: the need for extensive bounding box annotations. The paper clearly articulates the problem, establishes connections to relevant prior work (R-CNN, MIL, domain adaptation), and proposes a technically sound approach. The experimental results on ImageNet demonstrate a substantial improvement over a baseline, validating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a critical limitation in scaling object detection – the annotation bottleneck.
* **Novel Approach:** The idea of learning a transformation from image classifiers to detectors is a novel and promising direction. Casting it as a domain adaptation problem is a sensible choice.
* **Solid Technical Foundation:** The proposed method (LSDA) is well-defined and builds upon existing techniques (CNNs, R-CNN) in a logical manner. The breakdown into category-invariant and category-specific adaptation is well-motivated.
* **Strong Experimental Validation:** The results on ImageNet demonstrate a significant performance gain (+50% mAP) demonstrating practical effectiveness. The ablation study is helpful in understanding the contribution of each component.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is well-structured and easy to follow. The figures are informative and support the explanations.
* **Code Release:** Providing publicly available code and models (lsda.berkeleyvision.org) is a strong positive and promotes reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While a baseline using raw classifier weights is presented, a comparison to more recent and state-of-the-art detection methods (e.g., Faster R-CNN, YOLO, SSD) even with limited labeled data would strengthen the analysis. It’s unclear how LSDA compares to methods that actively leverage pseudo-labeling or semi-supervised learning approaches.
* **Dependence on Pre-trained Classifiers:** The method relies heavily on the quality of the initial ImageNet-trained classifiers. The performance may be sensitive to the specific classifier architecture and training procedure. This dependence should be discussed further.
* **Nearest Neighbor Selection:** The strategy for approximating category-specific adaptation using nearest neighbors seems somewhat ad-hoc.  A more principled approach (e.g., learning a mapping function) might be more robust. The sensitivity to the choice of 'k' in nearest neighbor selection should be explored in more detail.
* **Error Analysis Depth:** While the error analysis provides some insights, it’s relatively superficial. A more detailed analysis, perhaps categorizing errors based on object size, occlusion, or viewpoint, could reveal areas for further improvement.
* **Scalability Discussion:**  While a 7.6k category detector is built, a deeper discussion of the computational costs and practical limitations of scaling to even larger numbers of categories (e.g., tens or hundreds of thousands) would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant practical problem in object detection.
* Presents a novel and technically sound approach.
* Demonstrates strong experimental results on a challenging dataset.
* Well-written, clear, and organized.
* Code and models are publicly available.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lacks a comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art detection methods.
* Relies heavily on pre-trained classifiers, potentially limiting generalization.
*  The nearest neighbor approach for category-specific adaptation seems ad-hoc.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of object detection. The proposed LSDA method provides a practical and effective way to scale detection to a large number of categories, addressing a crucial bottleneck in current systems. While there are some weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them, and the publicly available code will further facilitate research in this area. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, specifically adding a comparison to stronger detection baselines and providing a more thorough analysis of the nearest neighbor selection strategy.  A discussion of the computational limitations when scaling to very large numbers of categories would also be beneficial.
