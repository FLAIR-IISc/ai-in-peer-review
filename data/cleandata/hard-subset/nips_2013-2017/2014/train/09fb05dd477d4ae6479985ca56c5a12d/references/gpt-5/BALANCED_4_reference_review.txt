Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to convert image classifiers into object detectors when only a subset of categories has bounding box annotations. The approach casts classification-to-detection as domain adaptation with two components: (1) a category-invariant adaptation learned by fine-tuning layers 1–7 of an ImageNet-pretrained CNN for detection on the subset with boxes, including learning a background class; and (2) a category-specific adaptation that estimates per-class output-layer shifts (δ) by averaging the fine-tuned changes from nearest neighbor classes (in fc8 weight space) that do have detection data. At test time, region proposals are scored by the adapted network, using score_i − score_background, without SVMs or bbox regression to keep training simple and scalable.

Experiments simulate the setting on ILSVRC13 detection with 200 classes: 100 “seen” with boxes and 100 held out. On held-out classes, mAP improves from 10.31% (classification-only baseline applied to region proposals) to 15.85% with category-invariant adaptation, and to 16.15% with the δ transfer (k=10 nearest neighbors), a ∼50% relative gain over baseline. Error analysis shows reductions in background and localization errors. The authors also demonstrate a 7,604-class detector by adapting the final layer for thousands of classes with only image-level labels and provide a faster SPP-based variant.

This work builds on R-CNN (Girshick et al.) for detection with CNNs, leverages domain adaptation ideas (e.g., adaptive SVMs, feature transforms), and relates to weakly supervised/MIL methods that learn detectors from image-level labels. Unlike prior adaptation methods that require detection data for all categories, LSDA explicitly transfers to held-out categories.

Quality
- Methodology is clearly specified and technically sound. The category-invariant component (fine-tuning with background) is well motivated and empirically dominant. The category-specific δ transfer is simple and plausible.
- Experimental setup on ILSVRC is appropriate; the ablation is informative. Reporting relative and absolute mAP and a significance test is appreciated.
- However, comparisons are limited. There is no head-to-head against other transfer strategies (e.g., using semantic/WordNet similarity, attribute embeddings) or weakly supervised detectors trained on the held-out set. It would also be useful to report whether adding bbox regression/SVMs on the seen classes helps the held-out classes (through better features/calibration).
- The absolute mAP on held-out classes remains low (∼16%), with a substantial gap to the oracle detector trained on all 200 categories. Sensitivity analyses are limited (e.g., effect of k, effect of number of seen categories m).

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a helpful decomposition into category-invariant vs. category-specific adaptation and an informative error analysis.
- Minor clarity issues: notation for A and B includes a small indexing typo (A = {m, …, K} presumably means {m+1, …, K}); some reference entries lack years; a few formatting artifacts remain.
- More detail on the nearest-neighbor selection (normalization, distance metric) and how k was chosen would improve reproducibility.

Originality
- The central idea—learning a generic classification-to-detection transformation on a subset of classes and transferring it to held-out classes—is novel relative to prior domain adaptation and detection work at the time. The explicit decomposition and the δ-transfer via neighbors in weight space are simple and new.
- While weakly supervised detection had been explored, those methods either do not scale or do not demonstrate cross-category adaptation of a detection head learned on other classes.

Significance
- The problem is important: reducing the need for bounding boxes to enable large-scale detection. Demonstrating a 7.6K-class detector and releasing models suggests practical impact.
- The relative gains are substantial; however, the absolute performance is still modest, and the transfer mechanism is heuristic. The paper is likely to stimulate follow-up work on better transfer mechanisms (e.g., meta-learning, semantic embeddings), making it a valuable contribution.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant, challenging problem (scaling detection with limited box annotations).
- Clear decomposition: background modeling and feature adaptation are shown to matter most.
- Solid ablation and error analysis; statistically significant gains on held-out categories.
- Scalable implementation; public release and a faster SPP variant.

Cons
- Limited baselines for category-specific transfer (no semantic/attribute/nearest-neighbor alternatives beyond fc8-L2).
- Absolute mAP remains low; gap to full-supervision is large.
- No exploration of how performance scales with the number of detection-annotated categories m or with different k values.
- Uses selective search; no analysis with modern proposals/RPNs (though a faster variant is mentioned).
- Trains on val1; while consistent with R-CNN, cleaner splits or test-set results would strengthen claims.

Questions for authors
- How was k selected? Please provide a sensitivity curve for k and for the number of seen categories m.
- Have you tried semantic similarity (WordNet/word embeddings) or attribute spaces for neighbor selection and/or a learned mapping from W^c to W^d?
- Does adding bbox regression and SVMs for seen classes improve held-out classes via better features/calibration?
- Can you report per-class APs and detailed confusion matrices for the held-out set?

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare δ-transfer against alternatives: semantic neighbors, metric learning, learned hypernetworks that predict δ from class embeddings, or linear regression from W^c to W^d.
- Incorporate weak supervision (MIL/self-training) to mine pseudo boxes for held-out classes and iterate adaptation.
- Provide scaling analyses over m and k; report results with modern proposals (e.g., RPN) and with bbox regression on seen classes.
- Clarify notation, fix reference metadata, and include more implementation details (normalization, calibration of scores vs. background).

Recommendation: Weak Accept
The paper introduces a simple, effective, and scalable approach to an important problem, with clear empirical gains and useful analysis. While some elements are heuristic and additional comparisons would strengthen the case, the contribution is sufficiently novel and promising to warrant acceptance.
