

# Peer Review: "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

## Summary

This paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a novel approach to transform image classifiers into object detectors without requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The key insight is that a transformation from classification to detection can be learned from a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations and then applied to categories without such annotations. The authors frame this as a domain adaptation problem between classification (source domain) and detection (target domain), and propose a three-stage adaptation process: 1) category invariant adaptation by fine-tuning convolutional layers on a subset of categories with bounding box annotations, 2) category specific adaptation using nearest neighbors in the classification feature space to approximate the detection transformation for categories without bounding box data, and 3) detection using the adapted network. The authors demonstrate significant improvements (50% relative mAP gain) on the ImageNet detection dataset and showcase a 7,604 category detector using their approach.

The paper builds on prior work in domain adaptation, R-CNN, and Multiple Instance Learning (MIL), but makes a distinctive contribution by enabling detection for categories without any bounding box annotations. This addresses a critical bottleneck in scaling object detection to large numbers of categories.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Problem Addressed**: The paper tackles a genuinely important problem in computer vision - the prohibitively expensive annotation requirement for large-scale object detection. The authors convincingly argue why scaling detection to thousands of categories is impractical with current methods.

2. **Clear Methodology**: The three-stage adaptation framework is well-motivated and logically presented. The distinction between category invariant and category specific adaptation is particularly insightful and well-justified.

3. **Strong Experimental Results**: The 50% relative mAP improvement over the classification-only baseline is impressive and well-documented. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the importance of each component of the approach.

4. **Practical Impact**: The demonstration of a 7,604 category detector is compelling and shows the real-world potential of the approach. The authors also provide a faster implementation (0.5s per image) that makes their approach more practical.

5. **Good Writing and Organization**: The paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility (code and models are publicly available).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison to Recent Detection Methods**: The paper only compares against the R-CNN baseline and the "oracle" (detection with all bounding box annotations). It does not compare against more recent state-of-the-art detection methods like Faster R-CNN, Mask R-CNN, or YOLO. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to gauge how LSDA performs relative to current detection approaches, which could have significantly better baselines than R-CNN.

2. **Ambiguity in Category-Specific Adaptation**: The nearest neighbors approach for category-specific adaptation is not clearly explained. The paper states "we find the nearest neighbors categories in set B for each category in set A" based on "l2-normalized fc8 parameters in the classification network," but it's unclear how this would work in practice. For example:
   - What happens if a category in set A has no close neighbors in set B (e.g., a category that's semantically very different)?
   - How does the method handle categories that are at the edge of the feature space?
   - What is the computational cost of finding nearest neighbors for thousands of categories?

3. **Limited Analysis of Failure Modes**: The error analysis in Section 4.3 is helpful, but it doesn't explore cases where the nearest neighbors approach fails. For example, the paper shows examples of false positives where the detector confuses similar categories, but doesn't discuss cases where the method fails completely due to poor nearest neighbors.

4. **Inadequate Discussion of Scaling Limitations**: The paper claims the method enables detection for "tens of thousands of categories," but doesn't address the practical limitations of the nearest neighbors approach for extremely large numbers of categories (e.g., 100,000+). The computational cost of finding nearest neighbors for each category could become prohibitive at this scale.

5. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper presents a strong empirical approach but lacks theoretical justification for why the nearest neighbors approach in the classification feature space would work for detection adaptation. A brief discussion of the theoretical underpinnings would strengthen the paper.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add Comparison to Recent Detection Methods**: Include comparisons against Faster R-CNN and other state-of-the-art detectors. Even if LSDA is applied as a post-processing step to these detectors, the comparison would provide important context for the results.

2. **Clarify Category-Specific Adaptation**: Provide more detail on the nearest neighbors approach, including:
   - A concrete example of how the nearest neighbors are selected
   - Analysis of how the number of nearest neighbors (k) affects performance
   - Discussion of edge cases where nearest neighbors might not be appropriate

3. **Expand Error Analysis**: Include more examples of failure cases, particularly those where the nearest neighbors approach fails. This would help readers understand the limitations of the method.

4. **Discuss Scaling Implications**: Add a section discussing the computational cost of the nearest neighbors approach as the number of categories increases. Consider proposing an approximate nearest neighbor search to scale to larger category sets.

5. **Add Theoretical Discussion**: Briefly discuss why the classification feature space might be appropriate for detecting category-specific adaptation, perhaps citing related work on feature space transfer.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in computer vision with a novel and well-executed approach. The methodology is sound, the results are compelling, and the practical implications are significant. While there are some limitations in the presentation and analysis that could be improved, these do not undermine the core contribution of the paper.

The paper advances the state of the art in transfer learning for object detection by enabling detection for categories without any bounding box annotations. This could have significant impact on large-scale vision applications where annotation is prohibitively expensive.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The problem is important, the approach is novel and well-executed, and the results are convincing. The weaknesses identified are important to address in the final version but do not detract from the paper's overall contribution.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of computer vision and domain adaptation, and it aligns well with the NIPS scope of advancing the state of the art in machine learning and its applications. The authors have made a valuable contribution that is likely to be widely adopted and built upon in future work.

## Final Thoughts

The LSDA approach represents an important step toward scaling object detection to large numbers of categories. The authors have demonstrated a practical method that significantly reduces the annotation burden while maintaining strong detection performance. This work has the potential to enable new applications in large-scale vision systems where manual bounding box annotation is impractical. The paper is well-written, well-executed, and makes a significant contribution to the field.
