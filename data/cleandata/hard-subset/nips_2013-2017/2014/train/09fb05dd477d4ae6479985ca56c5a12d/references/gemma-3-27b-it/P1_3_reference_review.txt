## Peer Review for LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and practical approach to scaling up object detection to a large number of categories, a significant bottleneck in the field. The core idea of adapting classifiers to detectors via domain adaptation is well-motivated, and the experimental results on ImageNet demonstrate a substantial improvement over a naive baseline. While not a groundbreaking theoretical advance, the work is solid, well-written, and addresses a critical real-world problem. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper tackles the challenge of training object detectors for a massive number of categories, where obtaining bounding box annotations is prohibitively expensive. The authors propose LSDA, a method to learn a transformation from image classifiers (trained on readily available image-level labels) to object detectors. This is framed as a domain adaptation problem, leveraging labeled data for a subset of categories to generalize to unseen categories.

The work builds upon the R-CNN approach [1] by moving beyond requiring bounding box data for *all* categories. It acknowledges and expands on the idea of adaptation from classification to detection. The paper positions itself well within the related work landscape, discussing Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) [6, 7] as a complementary approach and acknowledging the domain adaptation literature [12-22]. The authors correctly highlight that their work uniquely addresses adaptation to *held-out* categories with no detection data, distinguishing it from existing supervised domain adaptation methods.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Practical Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant and impactful. Scaling detection to large category counts is crucial for real-world applications.
* **Solid Methodology:** The LSDA approach is conceptually clear and well-defined. The decomposition into category-invariant and category-specific adaptation is sensible and allows for effective transfer learning.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The 50% relative mAP improvement over the baseline demonstrates the effectiveness of LSDA.  The ablation study (Figure 3) provides valuable insight into the contribution of each component.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The error analysis (Figure 5) is insightful, revealing that LSDA reduces localization and background confusion errors.  The visualization of false positives (Figure 6) offers a concrete understanding of the model’s strengths and weaknesses.
* **Code Release:** The availability of code and models at lsda.berkeleyvision.org is a major positive, promoting reproducibility and facilitating further research.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The figures are informative and contribute to understanding the approach.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** The paper lacks rigorous theoretical justification for the proposed adaptation techniques. While the intuition is sound, a more formal analysis would strengthen the work.
* **Dependence on Pre-trained Classifier:** The performance heavily relies on the quality of the pre-trained ImageNet classifier. The authors acknowledge this, but further discussion on the sensitivity to different pre-training datasets or architectures would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:**  The paper mentions using k=10 for nearest neighbor selection in category-specific adaptation, but the justification for this choice is limited. A more systematic exploration of the impact of ‘k’ would be valuable.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the baseline (raw classifier weights) is a sensible starting point, a comparison to more sophisticated zero-shot or few-shot detection methods would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of LSDA’s performance.



**4. Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. The methodology appears complete and the authors are careful about acknowledging limitations.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanations are clear, and the figures aid understanding.
* **Originality:** While the individual components (CNNs, domain adaptation) are not novel, the specific application of domain adaptation to transfer knowledge from classification to detection *across categories without detection labels* is a novel and valuable contribution.
* **Significance:**  The results are significant as they demonstrate a practical pathway towards scaling object detection to a much larger number of categories. This has the potential to impact a wide range of applications, including image search, robotics, and autonomous driving.

**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical problem in scaling object detection.
* Presents a practical and effective solution (LSDA) with strong empirical results.
* Clear writing and well-organized presentation.
* Publicly available code and models.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited theoretical analysis.
* Dependence on a strong pre-trained classifier.
* Could benefit from comparison to stronger baselines.

**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.**

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of object detection by addressing a significant practical challenge.  I suggest the following minor revisions:

* **Expand on theoretical justification:**  Add a brief discussion of the underlying principles that motivate the category-invariant and category-specific adaptation steps.
* **Sensitivity Analysis:** Include a short analysis of the impact of different pre-trained classifiers on LSDA’s performance.
* **Hyperparameter Discussion:** Provide a more thorough justification for the choice of k=10 in the category-specific adaptation step.  Perhaps include a plot of mAP vs. k.
* **Related Work Update:** Briefly mention more recent advancements in zero-shot or few-shot detection and discuss how LSDA differs.
