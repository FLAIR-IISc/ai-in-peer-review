## Peer Review for LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

**Summary:**

This paper introduces LSDA, a novel approach to large-scale object detection that leverages the abundance of image-level labeled data alongside limited bounding box annotations. The core idea is to learn a transformation from image classification networks to object detectors via domain adaptation.  LSDA fine-tunes a pre-trained classifier, utilizing labeled bounding boxes for a subset of categories to learn a generic transformation applicable to adapt classifiers for unseen categories (lacking bounding box annotations) into detectors. The authors demonstrate a significant performance improvement on the ImageNet detection task, particularly on held-out categories, showcasing the effectiveness of transferring knowledge from classification to detection.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper rightly positions itself within the growing body of work addressing the annotation bottleneck in object detection. It builds upon the success of R-CNN [1] in adapting classifiers for detection but addresses the scalability issue of requiring bounding box data for all categories. The authors acknowledge related work in Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) [6, 7] for weakly-supervised detection and domain adaptation techniques [12-16, 17-22], carefully distinguishing LSDA's novel contribution: adapting classifiers to *detectors* for *held-out categories* without any bounding box supervision. This combination is a key differentiator. The work also touches on the recent advances in deep CNNs for visual recognition [3, 4, 5] and highlights the benefit of transfer learning.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a critical problem:** The paper tackles the significant challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories given the cost of bounding box annotation.
* **Novel approach:** LSDA’s domain adaptation framework, specifically targeting the classifier-to-detector transformation, is a valuable contribution. The idea of leveraging category-invariant features and then applying category-specific adaptations is insightful.
* **Strong empirical results:** The reported 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories is substantial and convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach. The ablation study (Figure 3) clearly illustrates the contribution of each component.
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is well-organized and generally easy to follow. The figures are informative and aid in understanding the methodology.
* **Availability of code and models:** Releasing the code and pre-trained models is excellent for reproducibility and further research.
* **Error analysis:** The error analysis, particularly the breakdown of false positive types (Figure 5), provides valuable insight into the strengths and weaknesses of the model.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited comparison to other weakly-supervised methods:** While the authors acknowledge MIL-based approaches, a direct comparison on the ImageNet detection benchmark would strengthen the paper. It's unclear how LSDA performs relative to state-of-the-art MIL techniques.
* **Dependence on pre-trained classifier quality:** The performance is heavily reliant on the quality of the initial image classifier.  The paper could benefit from a discussion on the sensitivity of LSDA to variations in the pre-training data or architecture.
* **Heuristic for nearest neighbor selection:** The method for selecting nearest neighbors for category-specific adaptation (Equation 1) relies on Euclidean distance of fc8 weights, which feels somewhat arbitrary. A more principled approach or justification for this choice would be beneficial.
* **Lack of detailed analysis on large-scale detection:** While the 7604-category detector is mentioned, the results and analysis are limited. More details about the performance on these long-tail categories would be valuable. The statement about reducing detection time with faster proposals is useful but somewhat buried; it deserves more emphasis.
* **The "sim" label in Figure 5(a):** The meaning of "sim" next to category names in Figure 5(a) is not explained. This makes understanding the error analysis slightly difficult.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in object detection.
* The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong experimental results.
* The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides valuable insights into the challenges of large-scale detection.
* The authors have made their code and models publicly available, promoting reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a direct comparison to other state-of-the-art weakly-supervised detection methods.
* The heuristic nature of the nearest neighbor selection process.
* The limited analysis of the performance on the 7604-category detector.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and impactful contribution to the field of object detection.  The approach addresses a critical bottleneck – the need for large-scale bounding box annotations – and demonstrates significant improvements in performance, particularly on held-out categories.  While the paper could be strengthened by addressing the weaknesses outlined above (especially a more thorough comparison to other weakly-supervised methods), the overall quality and significance of the work warrant acceptance.

**Recommendation:** **Accept with minor revisions.**

I recommend the authors address the following points in a revision:

1. **Include a comparison to state-of-the-art MIL-based object detection methods on the ImageNet detection benchmark.**
2. **Discuss the sensitivity of LSDA to the quality of the pre-trained classifier.**
3. **Provide a more detailed justification for the nearest neighbor selection process or explore alternative approaches.**
4. **Expand the analysis of the 7604-category detector, including quantitative results and insights into performance on long-tail categories.**
5. **Clarify the meaning of “sim” in Figure 5(a).**
