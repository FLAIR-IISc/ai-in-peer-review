Summary
The paper tackles the problem of scaling object detection to many categories when only a subset has bounding box annotations. It proposes LSDA, a method to adapt a CNN trained for image classification into a detector for all K categories, using detection data for only m categories (B) and image-level labels for all (A ∪ B). The adaptation has two components: (1) a category-invariant transformation learned by fine-tuning layers 1–7 with detection data for B and adding a background class, which shifts features from a classification regime to a detection regime; (2) a category-specific adaptation for classes without boxes (A) that approximates the class-specific parameter changes observed in B by averaging the fc8 deltas of the k nearest neighbors in fc8 parameter space. At test time, region proposals are scored by the K+1 softmax and per-class scores are adjusted by subtracting the background score. On ILSVRC13 (200 classes), with detection labels restricted to 100 classes, LSDA improves mAP on the held-out 100 classes from 10.31% (classification-only baseline) to 15.85% via category-invariant adaptation, and to 16.15% with category-specific adaptation (p=0.017). The method reduces localization and background confusions and demonstrates a 7.6K-category detector (qualitative results), with a faster SPP-based implementation.

Quality
- Soundness: The approach is technically straightforward and correctly builds on established R-CNN fine-tuning. The category-invariant adaptation is well justified: detection requires background modeling and features that support localization; fine-tuning with a background class and region-level supervision naturally imparts this. The category-specific adaptation is a simple, defensible heuristic.
- Empirical support: The ablation cleanly isolates contributions: most of the gain comes from the shared feature adaptation and background modeling, with a smaller additional gain from the class-specific transfer. The use of an established benchmark (ILSVRC13 detection), a held-out protocol (100 categories without boxes), statistical testing for k-NN averaging, and an error analysis are all positives.
- Missing/weak baselines: The paper does not compare against alternative ways to transfer class-specific parameters (e.g., learning a regression mapping W^d = f(W^c) on B and applying to A, rather than k-NN averaging), or against weakly supervised MIL approaches run on the same setup (even if on a subset). It would also be informative to compare to a “background-augmented classifier” baseline: train a classifier with background negatives from proposals but without full detection fine-tuning, to isolate the effect of background vs feature adaptation.
- Completeness/reproducibility: Many details are provided (data splits, use of Caffe, proposal method). Some important details could be clearer (learning rates/schedules, k sensitivity beyond k=10, precise proposal settings, per-class APs). The 7.6K experiment is only qualitative.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and readable. The three “insights” and their operationalization are clear, and the training pipeline is easy to follow.
- Minor presentation issues likely due to formatting (e.g., list punctuation, missing table/figure content in this copy) hinder full assessment. Suggest clarifying notation for sets A/B, explicitly listing the exact mAP numbers for each ablation in the main text, and providing a brief algorithmic summary.

Originality
- The category-invariant adaptation via fine-tuning with a background class follows R-CNN, but the central idea—to learn a classification-to-detection transformation on some classes and transfer it to new classes without boxes—is novel and practically valuable. The class-specific transfer via nearest neighbors in parameter space is a simple but new twist in this context.
- Relative to prior domain adaptation work, this adapts across tasks (classification→detection) rather than visual domains, which is a meaningful shift. Related weakly supervised detection approaches are acknowledged; a direct comparison would strengthen claims.

Significance
- The problem is important: acquiring box annotations at scale is expensive. Demonstrating a 50% relative improvement over a classifier-only baseline on held-out categories shows clear progress toward large-scale detection with limited box supervision.
- The method is simple, scalable, and compatible with existing CNN detection pipelines. Code/models are released, and a faster variant is mentioned, increasing practical impact.
- Absolute mAP on held-out classes remains modest, and the demo of 7.6K categories lacks quantitative evaluation, leaving open how well the approach scales in practice. Nonetheless, the path it opens—adapting detectors for thousands of classes with few boxes—is compelling.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and timely problem with a simple, effective method.
- Clear ablations demonstrating where gains come from; statistically significant improvements.
- Insightful error analysis showing reduced localization/background mistakes.
- Practical contribution (released models/code; a fast variant) and a compelling large-scale demo.

Cons and suggestions
- Novelty is moderate; much of the gain comes from fine-tuning with background rather than a new learning objective.
- Class-specific adaptation is heuristic; compare to learned mappings from W^c to W^d.
- Missing baselines: weakly supervised MIL on the same data; classifier with explicit background negatives without full detection fine-tuning.
- Evaluate sensitivity to m (number of categories with boxes) and k (number of neighbors), and report per-class APs to understand which categories benefit most.
- Provide quantitative results for the SPP-based variant and (if possible) for a subset of the 7.6K categories with available boxes.

Overall assessment
- Quality: good; Clarity: good; Originality: moderate; Significance: high. I lean toward acceptance: the paper makes a meaningful, empirically supported step toward large-scale detection under weak supervision, with a method others can readily adopt and build upon.
