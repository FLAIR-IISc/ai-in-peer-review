Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a method to transform image classifiers into object detectors with minimal bounding box supervision. The core idea is to treat the classification-to-detection shift as a domain adaptation problem. The approach has two main components: (1) category-invariant adaptation by fine-tuning a CNN (layers 1–7) on detection data for a subset of categories B to learn a background class and adapt features for detection; and (2) category-specific adaptation that transfers per-class output-layer changes (δ) observed for categories in B to held-out categories A by averaging the deltas of nearest neighbors in the classification weight space. At test time, per-region scores for K categories and a background class are produced, and final scores are computed as class score minus background score.

Relative to prior work, LSDA goes beyond R-CNN [1] by not requiring bounding boxes for all classes; it uses detection data for only a subset and adapts the rest. It differs from weakly supervised MIL approaches [6,7] that learn detectors using only image-level labels, and from classic domain adaptation methods [12–16,17–22] that assume labeled data for all categories in the target domain. It builds on deep CNN pretraining for classification [4,8] and detection fine-tuning à la R-CNN, but introduces a mechanism to transfer the learned “classification-to-detection” transformation to categories without boxes.

Quality:
- Methodology: The training pipeline is clearly specified: pretrain on ILSVRC2012 classification, replace fc8 with K classes, fine-tune on classification data, then fine-tune layers 1–7 and background for detection on B, producing a softmax detector for B and adapted features. Category-specific deltas δ are computed for B and transferred to A via nearest neighbors in the classification fc8 weight space (L2 on normalized weights). The test-time decision rule subtracts the background score per region.
- Empirical validation: On ILSVRC2013, using detection labels for 100 of 200 categories and evaluating on the other 100 (val2), LSDA improves mAP on held-out categories from 10.31% (classification-only baseline applied to proposals) to 15.85% via category-invariant adaptation, and to 16.15% with category-specific adaptation (k=10), with a reported significance test (p=0.017). Ablations show the dominant gain comes from adapting shared features and adding a background class; category-specific transfer yields a smaller but measurable boost. Error analysis indicates reductions in background and localization errors relative to the classification-only baseline. A large-scale demonstration produces detectors for 7,604 categories, with qualitative results and a faster SPP-based implementation [28].
- Limitations: Absolute performance on held-out categories remains low compared to a fully supervised “upper bound” (not numerically reported in the text provided). The nearest-neighbor δ-transfer is heuristic and its sensitivity (choice of k, metric) is only briefly explored. No quantitative comparison against weakly supervised MIL baselines on the same benchmark is provided. The choice to avoid SVMs and bounding box regression (as in R-CNN) trades some accuracy for simplicity/speed; the impact is discussed but not fully quantified on held-out categories. Results appear to rely on a single alphabetic split; robustness to different splits or varying m (number of detection-labeled classes) is not reported.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized and conveys the method and experimental setup clearly. The definitions of sets A and B, the role of background, and Equation (1) are understandable. The ablation narrative is clear, as are the qualitative visualizations. Minor issues: A and B definitions use “{m,...,K}” where “{m+1,...,K}” may be intended; some references are duplicated or incomplete; explicit numerical upper-bound mAP would help. Providing more precise training details (learning rates, schedules, k selection rationale) would improve reproducibility, though the authors state code/models are released.

Originality:
The framing of classification-to-detection as domain adaptation with (i) a learned background class and shared feature adaptation from a subset of categories, and (ii) a neighbor-averaged δ transfer for held-out classes, appears novel relative to R-CNN and prior domain adaptation/detector transfer works that require labeled target data for all categories. The specific nearest-neighbor δ-transfer is a simple but original mechanism.

Significance:
The problem—scaling detection to thousands of categories with limited bounding box labels—is important. LSDA demonstrates a practical path: a 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories and a 7.6K-category detector. While absolute gains are modest, the approach reduces annotation requirements and can catalyze large-scale detection, making it likely to be used or extended. The concept of learning a generic classification-to-detection transformation is broadly applicable.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Addresses a significant scalability issue in detection with a clear, general approach.
- Sound empirical study with ablations showing where gains come from; statistical test included.
- Simple, efficient mechanism (background modeling, shared feature adaptation, δ-transfer) with released code/models and a large-scale demo.
- Novel adaptation setting: transferring to categories without any detection labels.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Absolute accuracy on held-out categories is low; gap to fully supervised detectors remains large.
- Category-specific adaptation is heuristic; limited analysis of alternatives (e.g., learning a mapping from Wc to Wd).
- No quantitative comparison to weakly supervised MIL methods on the same dataset; limited evaluation diversity (single split, fixed m).
- Omits standard components (SVMs, bbox regression) that could provide a stronger upper-bound or hybrid baseline.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Report results over multiple random splits and varying m to show robustness and data-efficiency trade-offs.
- Provide the numerical “oracle” mAP and, if feasible, a hybrid baseline that uses SVMs/bbox regression for B while keeping A adapted.
- Compare against a strong MIL-based weakly supervised detector on the same split, or include a simple MIL baseline.
- Explore learned category-specific mappings (e.g., regression from Wc to δ or metric-learning-based neighbor selection), and semantic neighbor metrics (WordNet/attributes) versus fc8-weight similarity.
- Clarify implementation details and correct minor notation/reference issues.

Overall recommendation:
The paper is technically sound, clear, and proposes a novel, practically significant approach to scaling detection with limited bounding box supervision. Despite modest absolute performance and some missing comparisons, the contribution is meaningful and timely. I lean toward acceptance.
