

# Peer Review: LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper proposes LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a novel approach to scale object detection to thousands of categories by leveraging available image-level classification data rather than requiring bounding box annotations for all categories. The authors frame the problem as a domain adaptation task, where the source domain consists of image classification data (with category labels) and the target domain consists of detection data (with bounding boxes and category labels). The key insight is that a transformation from classification to detection can be learned on a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations and then applied to classifiers for categories without bounding box data.

The paper builds on the R-CNN framework [1] and domain adaptation literature, but makes a significant contribution by enabling adaptation to held-out categories with no detection data. This differs from previous domain adaptation methods that require labeled target domain data for all categories. The approach also relates to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) methods [6,7] for weakly supervised detection, though the authors rightly note that MIL-based methods haven't been evaluated on the large-scale ImageNet detection challenge.

**Strengths:**
1. The problem addressed is highly relevant to the field - scaling detection to thousands of categories is a major practical challenge, and the authors correctly identify the bottleneck as the scarcity of bounding box annotations versus the abundance of image-level labels.

2. The methodology is sound and well-motivated, with three clear insights: background recognition importance, category-invariant information transfer, and category-specific differences. The ablation study (Table 1) effectively demonstrates the contribution of each component.

3. The empirical results are compelling: a 50% relative mAP improvement over the classification-only baseline on held-out categories, and the impressive demonstration of a 7,604-category detector. The error analysis (Figure 5) provides valuable insights into how the approach reduces localization errors and background confusion.

4. The paper is well-written with clear figures, and the authors have made code and models publicly available, which is highly commendable for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The comparison with state-of-the-art weakly supervised detection methods (like MIL approaches [6,7]) is insufficient. The authors cite these methods but don't provide a direct comparison on the ImageNet detection dataset, which would strengthen the claim of novelty.

2. The category-specific adaptation using nearest neighbors (Equation 1) is potentially limiting. The authors assume that classification parameters of similar categories will have similar detection parameter changes, but this may not hold for categories with significant visual differences (e.g., a "lemon" vs. "orange" might be similar, but "lemon" vs. "motorcycle" would not be). A more sophisticated approach to modeling category relationships could improve results.

3. The paper doesn't thoroughly address the computational cost of the approach. While they mention a "fast detector" running at 2fps, the comparison with other state-of-the-art detectors (like R-CNN, Fast R-CNN, Faster R-CNN) in terms of speed and accuracy tradeoffs would be valuable.

4. The evaluation is limited to the ImageNet detection challenge. It would strengthen the claim of generality to show results on another dataset or domain.

**Originality:** The approach is novel in its specific application to scaling detection to held-out categories with no bounding box data. While domain adaptation has been used for object detection before [17-22], these methods require labeled detection data for all categories. To our knowledge, this is the first method to adapt to categories without any detection data, making this a significant contribution to the field.

**Significance:** This work has high significance for the field, as it addresses a major bottleneck in scaling object detection to the thousands of categories needed for practical applications. The ability to leverage existing image classification data for detection could substantially reduce the annotation burden for large-scale visual recognition systems. The 7,604-category detector demonstrates the potential impact of this approach.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound, with appropriate ablation studies and statistical validation (p=0.017). The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., the performance gap compared to oracle performance with full bounding box annotations), demonstrating appropriate scientific rigor.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with helpful figures that illustrate the key concepts. The methodological details are sufficiently complete for replication.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to scaling object detection by leveraging domain adaptation to transfer knowledge from classification to detection. While there are some limitations in the comparison with related weakly supervised detection methods, the core idea is novel, the methodology is sound, and the results are compelling. This work has clear potential for impact in both academic research and practical applications.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Solves a major practical problem in computer vision (scaling detection to thousands of categories)
- Demonstrates clear empirical improvement (50% relative mAP gain)
- Provides a scalable solution (7,604-category detector)
- Well-written with clear methodology and sufficient ablation studies

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Comparison with weakly supervised detection methods is insufficient
- Category-specific adaptation method could be improved
- Limited evaluation to ImageNet dataset

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of object detection, with clear potential for both research impact and practical applications. The authors have addressed a significant bottleneck in scaling detection systems, and the approach is well-justified and rigorously evaluated.
