- Proposes LSDA: convert image classifiers into object detectors by learning category-invariant CNN fine-tuning on a subset with boxes and transferring class-specific weight deltas to held-out categories.  
- Introduces a background class and fine-tunes shared layers (1–7) on detection data to handle classification-to-detection shift.  
- Performs category-specific adaptation by averaging nearest-neighbor fc8 weight deltas from supervised categories and applying them to unsupervised categories.  
- Detection score = class score minus background score; no bounding-box regression used.  
- Evaluated on ILSVRC-2013 by simulating 100 supervised and 100 held-out classes, plus scaling to a 7.6K-category detector.  
- Strong baseline: using a classification network on region proposals is competitive.  
- Shared-layer fine-tuning with background yields the largest mAP gains for held-out classes (10.31% → 15.85%).  
- Category-specific fc-layer transfer gives a small but statistically significant additional gain (to 16.15% mAP, p=0.017).  
- Error analysis shows adaptation reduces localization and background false positives.  
- Method is clearly specified, computationally feasible, and builds on CNN/R-CNN and domain adaptation ideas.  
- Experiments include careful hold-out protocol, ablations isolating effects, and runtime/faster-variant discussion.  
- Limitations: sensitivity to number of supervised categories (m) not studied and no comparison to weakly supervised/MIL methods on the same benchmark.  
- Nearest-neighbor metric (L2 on normalized fc8 weights) is heuristic and not compared to semantic or feature-space alternatives.  
- Potential issue: background training may include unlabeled held-out objects, causing label leakage not quantified.  
- Omits bounding-box regression and SVM calibration, confounding comparisons to full-supervision upper bound.  
- Clarity is good overall, but some notation and sensitivity details (k selection) could be clarified.  
- Originality: novel framing for classification-to-detection transfer and simple, new nearest-neighbor delta transfer idea in this context.  
- Significance: addresses scalable detection with limited boxes, demonstrates practical gains and delivers a 7.6K detector.  
- Pros: pragmatic, effective, general method with thorough ablations, released models, and demonstrated scalability.  
- Cons: limited baselines vs. MIL, heuristic category-specific transfer with modest gains, no study of m, and potential training bias from unlabeled objects.  
- Suggested improvements: compare to weakly supervised baselines, analyze sensitivity to k and neighbor metrics, vary m and B composition, add bounding-box regression, and quantify background contamination effects.  
- Overall: technically sound, clearly presented, and a meaningful 2014 contribution despite some missing comparisons and limited analysis of the category-specific transfer.
