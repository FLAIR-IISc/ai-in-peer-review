## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper proposes LSDA, an algorithm to leverage the abundance of image-level labels for scaling object detection to a large number of categories. The core idea is to learn a transformation from classifiers (trained on image-level data) to detectors (requiring bounding box annotations) and apply this transformation to categories lacking bounding box data. This addresses a critical bottleneck in scaling detection – the cost of annotation. The approach builds upon recent successes in CNNs for both classification and detection (e.g., R-CNN [1]) and frames the problem as a domain adaptation task. Related work includes Multiple Instance Learning [6, 7] and domain adaptation techniques [12-22], but the authors correctly point out that their method uniquely tackles adapting to *held-out* categories with no detection data.

**Strengths:** The paper tackles a very important problem – scaling object detection. The proposed approach is conceptually sound and leverages readily available data. The experimental results on ImageNet demonstrate a significant improvement (+50% relative mAP) over a baseline using raw classifier weights. The ablation study (Figure 3) provides valuable insight into the contribution of each component. The authors also demonstrate the potential for a fast detector (2fps) and have released code and models, promoting reproducibility. The error analysis (Figure 5) is insightful, showing the algorithm improves localization and reduces background confusion. 

**Weaknesses:** The paper lacks a detailed comparison to recent MIL-based methods [6, 7]. While acknowledged, a direct quantitative comparison would strengthen the contribution. The discussion of the nearest neighbor approach for adapting categories without bounding box data (Section 3.2) feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more principled justification or exploration of alternative strategies would be beneficial. The improvement shown is on a limited subset of categories (100 with bounding boxes). While the 7.6K detector is impressive, a more detailed evaluation of its performance, beyond the example detections in Figure 6, would be valuable. The paper also doesn’t fully explore the limitations of the approach, such as its reliance on the quality of the initial classification models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Addresses a significant problem in scaling object detection.
* Presents a novel and conceptually sound approach.
* Demonstrates significant improvement on a challenging dataset.
* Provides a thorough ablation study and error analysis.
* Releases code and models for reproducibility.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
* Lacks a direct quantitative comparison to related MIL-based methods.
* The nearest neighbor adaptation strategy feels somewhat ad-hoc.
* Limited evaluation of the 7.6K detector’s performance.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a clear contribution. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly a more thorough comparison to related work and a more detailed evaluation of the large-scale detector.
