Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the important problem of scaling object detection when only a small subset of categories has bounding-box annotations, while many more categories have only image-level labels. The proposed LSDA approach learns how to transform an image classifier into a detector by: (1) learning a background class and adapting shared layers (category-invariant adaptation) using detection data for a subset B of classes, and (2) estimating category-specific output-layer deltas for classes without detection data (set A) by transferring the average “delta” learned for nearest neighbors in B (measured in fc8 space). At test time, per-region scores are computed for K classes plus background, and a simple combination scorei − scorebackground is used.

On ILSVRC-2013 detection with 200 classes, the authors simulate limited detection supervision by using detection labels for the first 100 classes and only image-level labels for the remaining 100. They show that LSDA improves held-out-class mAP from 10.31% (classification network applied to proposals) to 16.15% (+50% relative), with most of the gain from category-invariant adaptation (learning background and fine-tuning layers 1–7), and a smaller but statistically significant additional gain from category-specific output adaptation. Error analysis shows reduced localization and background confusions. They also build a 7.6K-category detector by combining 200 detection-supervised classes with 7.4K classification-only classes, and present a faster SPP-based variant running at ~0.5s/image.

Relative to prior work: R-CNN (Girshick et al.) adapts classification features to detection but requires bounding boxes for all classes; MIL-based weakly supervised detectors learn from image-level labels but had not (at the time) been demonstrated at ImageNet scale; classical domain adaptation typically presumes labeled target data per class. LSDA’s novelty is in learning a task adaptation (classification→detection) from a subset of categories and transferring it to categories without any detection labels.

Quality
Strengths:
- Careful experimental design on a standard benchmark with a realistic setup (partial detection supervision).
- Clear ablations isolating background modeling, shared-layer fine-tuning, and output-layer adaptation; the reported gains are consistent with the stated hypotheses.
- Statistical significance reported for the category-specific adaptation (p=0.017).
- Useful diagnostic error analysis (breakdown of Loc/BG/Other errors) that supports the claim that LSDA learns to localize and reject background better than the classification-only baseline.
- Practical contribution: 7.6K detector and a faster variant; code/models released.

Weaknesses / issues to clarify:
- Missing baselines: (a) comparison to training a single category-agnostic bounding-box regressor and/or calibration layer shared across all classes to reduce the gap to the oracle; (b) a stronger baseline that fine-tunes shared layers for detection using B and leaves A unchanged (they essentially provide this, but it would help to quantify the incremental gain of the background class alone vs. shared-layer fine-tuning separately—some of this is in Table 1 but could be more explicit).
- The category-specific transfer via nearest neighbors in fc8 is somewhat ad hoc and yields a small absolute gain. It would be informative to analyze sensitivity to k, the distance metric, and to compare to hierarchy/semantic neighbors (e.g., WordNet tree distance) or attribute-based neighbors.
- The scoring rule scorei − scorebackground is intuitive but departs from standard softmax calibration; clarifying whether scores are pre- or post-softmax and whether this is equivalent to a log-odds difference would improve reproducibility.
- How performance scales with the number m of detection-supervised categories is not explored. A curve m→mAP on held-out classes would strengthen the case for scalability.
- The 7.6K qualitative results are compelling but lack quantitative evaluation (understandably difficult). Even a small held-out annotated subset or human verification study would help.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The three key insights guide the method exposition; the training pipeline (pretraining, classification fine-tuning, detection fine-tuning on B, and output-layer transfer to A) is described in sufficient detail to reproduce, with Caffe references and data splits. Minor suggestions:
- Explicitly state whether score combination uses logits or softmax outputs.
- Consolidate the description of the ablation settings (background-only, layers 1–7 vs conv-only, etc.) to make Table 1 easier to interpret.
- Minor typographical issues (e.g., hyphenation artifacts) can be cleaned.

Originality
The core idea—learning a category-invariant task adaptation from classification to detection on a subset of categories and transferring it to unlabeled categories—is novel relative to contemporaneous work. Unlike prior domain adaptation methods, LSDA adapts across tasks and does not require detection labels for all categories. The nearest-neighbor delta transfer is simple but original in this context.

Significance
The paper addresses an important barrier for large-scale detection and demonstrates a tangible step forward: a 50% relative mAP gain on held-out categories without any bounding-box supervision. The practical release of a 7.6K detector and a faster variant increases impact. While the absolute mAP remains far from the fully supervised upper bound, the approach is likely to influence subsequent work on weak/partial supervision and scalable detectors.

Pros
- Addresses a timely, high-impact problem (scaling detection without per-class boxes).
- Solid empirical gains with clear ablations and diagnostics.
- Elegant, simple method that plugs into standard CNN training.
- Public release of large-scale models and code.

Cons
- Category-specific transfer provides modest gains; design is heuristic.
- Limited analysis of scalability with respect to the number of supervised detection classes.
- Missing comparisons to alternative transfer cues (semantic/hierarchical neighbors) and to shared regressors/calibration.
- Limited quantitative evaluation for the 7.6K detector.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide sensitivity analyses for k and neighbor metrics; compare to WordNet/semantic neighbors.
- Report results as m varies (e.g., 25/50/100 supervised categories).
- Clarify the score combination and calibration; consider adding a lightweight shared calibration layer.
- Explore a shared, category-agnostic bounding-box regressor for all classes to reduce localization error further.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper is technically sound, proposes an original and practical approach to a significant problem, and presents convincing empirical evidence with useful analysis. The limitations are reasonable and largely point to straightforward extensions.
