## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper proposes LSDA, an algorithm to adapt classifiers trained on image-level labels to object detectors, addressing the scarcity of bounding box annotations for many object categories. The core idea is to learn a transformation from the classification domain (image-level labels) to the detection domain (bounding boxes + labels) on a subset of categories and then apply this transformation to classifiers of categories lacking bounding box data. The method achieves promising results on ImageNet detection, demonstrating a significant improvement over a baseline that uses raw classifier weights and enabling detection for a very large number of categories (7.6K).

**Relation to Previous Work:** The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of object detection and domain adaptation. It builds upon the R-CNN work [1] by addressing the bounding box annotation bottleneck. It also relates to Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approaches [6, 7] which use weak labels, noting that LSDA hasn't been directly compared on ImageNet. The discussion of domain adaptation methods [12-16] and supervised domain adaptation for detection [17-22] is comprehensive, and the authors appropriately highlight the novelty of adapting to held-out categories without *any* detection data. The connection to deep feature learning [4, 5, 8-11] is also well-established. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The central idea of transferring knowledge from classification to detection for categories *without* bounding box annotations is genuinely novel and addresses a crucial problem in scaling object detection.
* **Significance:** The potential impact of this work is significant. Enabling detection for tens of thousands of categories with only image-level labels could unlock numerous applications. The demonstrated ability to create a 7.6K detector is impressive.
* **Technical Soundness:** The approach of learning a domain adaptation transformation seems well-motivated and logically sound. The ablation studies (Section 4.2) provide evidence that each component contributes to the overall performance.
* **Empirical Results:** The quantitative results on ImageNet are convincing, demonstrating a substantial improvement over the baseline. The error analysis (Section 4.3) and localization examples are helpful in understanding the strengths and limitations of the approach.
* **Code Release:** The public availability of models and software ([lsda.berkeleyvision.org]) is a major positive and facilitates reproducibility and further research.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the methodology and experiments.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to MIL:** While the authors mention MIL, a direct comparison on the ImageNet dataset would strengthen the paper. It is unclear how LSDA's performance compares to state-of-the-art MIL methods, especially in the context of large-scale detection.
* **Category-Specific Adaptation Heuristic:** The nearest neighbor approach for category-specific adaptation (Equation 1) seems somewhat ad-hoc. The justification relies on the assumption that similar classification models will have similar detection models, which may not always hold true. A more principled approach, or at least a more thorough evaluation of different nearest neighbor selection strategies, would be beneficial.
* **Performance Saturation:** The improvement from category-specific adaptation (k=10) is relatively small (+0.84 mAP). This suggests that the method may be approaching a performance saturation point, and further improvements might require exploring fundamentally different approaches.
* **Computational Cost:** While the faster detector version is mentioned, the computational cost of training and running the full 7.6K detector could be a barrier to adoption. A more detailed discussion of computational resources required for training and inference would be helpful.
* **Error Analysis Depth:** The error analysis is insightful, but could be expanded. For example, analyzing the types of categories where LSDA struggles could provide valuable guidance for future research.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical bottleneck in scaling object detection.
* Presents a novel and technically sound approach.
* Demonstrates significant empirical improvement on a challenging benchmark.
* Publicly available code and models promote reproducibility.
* Well-written and clearly explained.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lacks a direct comparison to strong MIL baselines.
* Category-specific adaptation relies on a heuristic.
* Performance improvements may be approaching saturation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of object detection. The proposed LSDA algorithm offers a promising solution to the problem of scaling detection to a large number of categories with limited bounding box annotations. While there are some minor weaknesses, the novelty, significance, and empirical results outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the comparison to MIL and providing more justification for the nearest neighbor heuristic. The authors should also consider briefly discussing the computational costs involved.
