- Introduces LSDA: converts image classifiers to object detectors by learning a classifierâ†’detector transformation using categories with bounding boxes.  
- Frames the problem as domain adaptation to avoid costly bounding-box annotation for many categories.  
- Uses a pre-trained CNN and R-CNN pipeline as the base and fine-tunes in three steps.  
- Step 1: fine-tune a baseline CNN for classification on all target categories using image-level labels.  
- Step 2 (category-invariant adaptation): fine-tune convolutional and FC layers on detection data to learn a generic shift and a background model.  
- Step 3 (category-specific adaptation): adapt final layer for held-out categories by averaging nearest-neighbor detection adaptations based on classifier-weight similarity.  
- Empirically rigorous evaluation on ILSVRC 2013 with held-out detection labels and thorough ablation study.  
- Category-invariant feature adaptation yields the largest performance gains; error analysis shows reduced localization and background confusion.  
- Achieves a 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories over a strong baseline; demonstrates a qualitative 7,604-category detector.  
- Novelty lies in learning and transferring the adaptation transformation rather than training detectors individually.  
- Technically sound, well-written, and clearly presented with helpful diagrams and detailed experimental protocol.  
- Weakness: category-specific adaptation is a simple heuristic (L2 on classifier weights) with marginal gains and limited justification.  
- Weakness: adapted detectors still lag fully supervised detectors (16.15% vs 22.58% mAP), leaving a performance gap.  
- Weakness: no direct empirical comparison to weakly-supervised methods and limited discussion of trade-offs.  
- Acceptance arguments: addresses an important scaling problem, proposes an elegant transfer idea, is well-validated, and likely to inspire follow-up work.  
- Rejection arguments: reliance on a heuristic for category-specific adaptation and remaining gap to fully supervised performance.
