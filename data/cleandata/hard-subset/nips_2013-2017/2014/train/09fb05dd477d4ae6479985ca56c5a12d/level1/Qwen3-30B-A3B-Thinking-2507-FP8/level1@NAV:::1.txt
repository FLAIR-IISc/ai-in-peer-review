

This paper introduces LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a method to enable object detection for tens of thousands of categories lacking bounding box annotations by adapting classifiers trained on abundant image-level classification data. The authors frame the problem as a domain adaptation task from classification (source domain) to detection (target domain), learning a generic transformation using a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations, then applying this transformation to adapt classifiers for categories without detection data. The approach involves three key components: category invariant adaptation (fine-tuning shared layers using detection data for a subset of categories), category specific adaptation (approximating transformation for new categories via nearest neighbors in the classification space), and detection inference using background-aware scoring.

The paper makes a strong contribution by addressing a critical bottleneck in scaling object detection—annotation costs. The experimental results on ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge demonstrate significant improvements (50% relative mAP gain on held-out categories) over baselines, with compelling ablation studies showing the importance of each component. The 7.6K detector demonstration effectively showcases the method's scalability.

Strengths include strong technical execution with clear methodology, thorough evaluation with statistical significance testing, and practical significance for real-world applications. The approach is well-positioned within the literature, building on R-CNN's adaptation concept while extending it to the large-scale setting where bounding box data is scarce.

Weaknesses include limited analysis of the nearest-neighbor approximation's failure modes for dissimilar categories, and the evaluation being restricted to ImageNet without broader domain validation. While the authors reference MIL methods, a direct comparison would strengthen the position against complementary approaches.

Quality: Strong experimental design with clear ablation studies and statistical validation. The method is technically sound and well-supported by results. (4/5)
Clarity: Well-structured, well-written with helpful figures. Some technical details about the nearest-neighbor implementation could be clarified. (4/5)
Originality: Novel approach to scaling detection to large category sets, significantly extending previous adaptation work. The framing as a domain adaptation problem for transfer to held-out categories is distinctive. (4/5)
Significance: Addresses a major practical challenge with clear potential impact on enabling large-scale detection systems. The 7.6K detector demonstration powerfully illustrates the significance. (4/5)

The paper represents a significant contribution to object detection, offering a practical solution to a major scalability bottleneck. It advances the state of the art by enabling detection for categories without bounding box annotations, with clear potential for real-world deployment. I recommend acceptance.
