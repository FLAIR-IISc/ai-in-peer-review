LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation is a compelling paper addressing a critical challenge in object detection – scaling to a large number of categories given the scarcity of bounding box annotations. The core idea of adapting classifiers trained on abundant image-level labels to function as detectors is well-motivated and tackles a practical bottleneck in the field. The paper builds upon the success of R-CNN-style architectures and cleverly frames the problem as a domain adaptation task. The experimental results on ImageNet LSVRC-2013 demonstrate a significant improvement over a naive baseline, and the ability to create a 7.6K detector is impressive. The authors also demonstrate a focus on practical impact by presenting a faster detector variant.

**Quality:** The technical soundness of the paper is generally high. The proposed LSDA algorithm is clearly presented and logically structured. The ablation studies are informative, effectively isolating the contributions of different components (background recognition, category-invariant adaptation, category-specific adaptation). The use of existing deep learning frameworks (Caffe) and the public release of code and models contribute to reproducibility. However, the theoretical justification for the nearest-neighbor averaging in category-specific adaptation feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more rigorous analysis or justification for this choice would strengthen the paper. Additionally, while the performance gains are substantial, the absolute mAP values achieved, particularly on the held-out categories, are still relatively low. Exploring strategies to further boost performance, potentially through more sophisticated adaptation techniques, would be valuable. The discussion of limitations is reasonable, acknowledging the remaining gap compared to fully supervised detection.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, making it easy to follow the proposed approach. The diagrams (Figures 1, 2, and 3) are helpful in visualizing the LSDA pipeline and the adaptation process. The mathematical notation is clear and concise. However, the description of the experimental setup could be more detailed. For example, providing the exact number of images used for training each category, and clarifying the augmentation strategy used, would enhance reproducibility. The explanation of the error analysis could also be more explicit – the distinction between "Loc," "BG," and "Oth" error types isn't immediately obvious and could benefit from illustrative examples.

**Originality:** The central contribution of LSDA lies in the novel application of domain adaptation to address the large-scale detection problem. While domain adaptation is a well-established field (as highlighted in the related work), applying it specifically to transfer knowledge *from* image classification *to* object detection, particularly for categories lacking bounding box annotations, is a significant and original contribution. The paper effectively leverages the abundance of classification data to overcome the limitations of scarce detection data. The related work section is comprehensive, accurately positioning LSDA within the context of existing methods like MIL and other domain adaptation techniques. The comparison to R-CNN and other recent advancements is well-articulated.

**Significance:** The potential impact of LSDA is substantial. The ability to create detectors for thousands of categories using readily available image-level data addresses a fundamental limitation in scaling object detection to real-world applications. The 7.6K detector demonstrates the feasibility of this approach and opens up opportunities for building more comprehensive visual recognition systems. The fast detector variant further enhances the practical applicability of the method. While the current performance isn’t state-of-the-art, the approach provides a solid foundation for future research and could significantly reduce the cost and effort required to deploy object detection in diverse scenarios. The release of code and models will facilitate further exploration and adoption by the broader research community.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical and practical challenge in object detection.
*   Novel application of domain adaptation for transferring knowledge from classification to detection.
*   Demonstrates significant performance improvement over a strong baseline.
*   Creates a 7.6K detector, showcasing the scalability of the approach.
*   Provides a faster detector variant.
*   Publicly released code and models enhance reproducibility and facilitate further research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical justification for the nearest-neighbor averaging in category-specific adaptation is somewhat weak.
*   Absolute mAP values remain relatively low, particularly for held-out categories.
*   Experimental setup details could be more comprehensive.
*   Error analysis could be more clearly explained with illustrative examples.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend acceptance. LSDA presents a significant and original contribution to the field of object detection. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and demonstrates a clear understanding of the relevant literature. While there are minor areas for improvement, the potential impact of this work is substantial, and it addresses a critical bottleneck in scaling object detection to a large number of categories. The public release of code and models further strengthens the value of this contribution.
