- Problem & contribution: Proposes LSDA to scale object detection to thousands of categories by adapting classifiers (image-level labels) into detectors using bounding-box data from a subset of categories.
- Key insight: Learn a transformation from classification to detection on labeled categories and transfer it to held-out categories without bounding boxes.
- Methodology: Builds on R-CNN and frames the task as domain adaptation, modeling background recognition, category-invariant transfer, and category-specific differences.
- Empirical result: Achieves a 50% relative mAP improvement over a classification-only baseline on held-out categories and demonstrates a 7,604-category detector.
- Ablation & analysis: Ablation study shows each component’s contribution; error analysis indicates reduced localization and background confusion.
- Reproducibility: Paper is well-written with clear figures and authors release code and models.
- Originality: Novel in adapting to categories with no detection data, differing from prior domain-adaptation and MIL methods that require labeled target data.
- Significance: High practical impact by reducing bounding-box annotation needs and enabling large-scale detection.
- Quality: Sound technical approach with appropriate ablations and statistical validation (p=0.017); acknowledges performance gap to oracle.
- Clarity: Organized and clearly written with sufficient methodological detail for replication.
- Weakness — comparisons: Lacks direct comparison to state-of-the-art weakly supervised (MIL) methods on ImageNet detection.
- Weakness — category adaptation: Nearest-neighbor-based category-specific adaptation may be limiting for visually dissimilar categories.
- Weakness — computational evaluation: Does not thoroughly compare speed/accuracy tradeoffs or computational cost against other detectors (R-CNN, Fast/Faster R-CNN).
- Weakness — generality: Evaluation confined to ImageNet detection; results on other datasets would strengthen generality claims.
- Recommendation: Accept — novel, well-motivated, empirically strong contribution with clear impact despite noted limitations.
