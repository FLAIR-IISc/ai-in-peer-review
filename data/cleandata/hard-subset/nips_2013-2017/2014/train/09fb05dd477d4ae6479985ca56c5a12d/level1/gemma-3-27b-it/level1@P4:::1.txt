The paper "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation" proposes a method to leverage image-level classification data for object detection, particularly in scenarios where bounding box annotations are scarce. The core idea is to learn a transformation from classifiers to detectors using categories with both classification and detection labels, and then apply this transformation to adapt classifiers for categories without detection labels into detectors. This builds upon the work of Girshick et al. (R-CNN) [1], but aims to reduce the reliance on bounding box annotations by transferring knowledge from labeled to unlabeled categories. Related work includes Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approaches [6, 7] and domain adaptation techniques [12-22], although the authors argue their approach uniquely handles adapting to held-out categories with no detection data.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a relevant problem:** Scaling object detection to a large number of categories is a significant challenge, and the scarcity of bounding box annotations is a major bottleneck. Leveraging readily available image-level labels is a promising direction.
* **Clear and well-defined approach:** The LSDA algorithm is presented in a logical and understandable manner. The three key insights—recognizing background, transferring category-invariant information, and handling category-specific differences—provide a clear framework.
* **Strong experimental results:** The results on the ImageNet detection task demonstrate a significant improvement (+50% relative mAP) over a baseline using raw classifier weights. The ablation study convincingly shows the contribution of each component of the algorithm.
* **Large-scale demonstration:** The creation of a 7.6K detector showcases the scalability of the approach, and the availability of models and software is a valuable contribution to the community.
* **Error Analysis:** Provides a good qualitative analysis of errors, highlighting improved localization with the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Reliance on ImageNet pre-training:** The algorithm heavily relies on the pre-trained ImageNet model [3]. While this is common practice, it's unclear how well the approach would generalize to other datasets or domains without such pre-training. The discussion doesn't address this limitation.
* **Nearest Neighbor Selection:** The method of adapting categories without bounding box data based on nearest neighbors in the feature space (fc8 weights) seems somewhat arbitrary.  The choice of *k* nearest neighbors (k=10 in the experiments) is not sufficiently justified. A more robust or adaptive method for determining this relationship might be beneficial.
* **Limited comparison to MIL methods:** While the authors mention MIL-based methods [6, 7], a direct quantitative comparison on the ImageNet detection challenge is lacking. This makes it difficult to assess the relative performance of LSDA.
* **Computational cost:** While a faster version is mentioned, the initial implementation requires significant computational resources, which might limit its accessibility for some researchers. This is not explored in the discussion.
* **The nature of the classification data:** The paper highlights that the classification data used is "weakly annotated" (single image-level label, potentially containing multiple objects). It's unclear how this impacts performance and whether stronger classification data would yield further improvements.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a critical challenge in scaling object detection.
* The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated.
* The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate a significant improvement over existing approaches.
* The availability of code and models promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on ImageNet pre-training limits the generalizability of the approach.
* The nearest neighbor selection method feels somewhat ad-hoc.
* The lack of a direct comparison to recent MIL-based methods hinders a comprehensive evaluation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to scaling object detection. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and supported by compelling experimental results. While there are some limitations, the contributions are significant and likely to be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
