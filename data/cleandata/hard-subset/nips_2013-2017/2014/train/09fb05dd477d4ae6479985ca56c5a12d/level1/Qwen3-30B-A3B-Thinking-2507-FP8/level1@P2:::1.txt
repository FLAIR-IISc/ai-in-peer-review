

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

This paper proposes Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a novel method for adapting image classifiers to object detectors using only a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations. The authors address the significant challenge of scaling object detection to thousands of categories, where collecting bounding box annotations for all categories is prohibitively expensive. Their key insight is that the transformation from classification to detection can be learned as a domain adaptation problem, with image classification data as the source domain and detection data as the target domain. They present a three-stage approach: (1) using classification data to pre-train a CNN, (2) adapting the network using a subset of detection-labeled data to learn category-invariant features, and (3) using nearest neighbors in the feature space to adapt category-specific parameters for categories without detection data.

The paper builds on recent work in domain adaptation (e.g., [12-16]) and object detection (e.g., R-CNN [1]), but makes a novel contribution by framing the classification-to-detection transfer as a domain adaptation problem where the adaptation must be generalized to categories without any detection data. This is distinct from prior work on domain adaptation for detection, which assumes labeled detection data is available for all categories.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a clear methodology and thorough experimental validation. The ablation study (Figure 3, Table 1) effectively demonstrates the importance of each component of LSDA, showing a 50% relative mAP improvement over the classification-only baseline. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the gap to oracle performance (which requires bounding box annotations for all categories), and suggest future work on MIL techniques. The experiments on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection dataset are well-designed and provide convincing evidence for the approach's effectiveness. However, the paper could better address the computational cost of adaptation (e.g., time to adapt a classifier to a detector) and more thoroughly compare against state-of-the-art domain adaptation techniques for detection.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The figures (especially Figure 1, 2, and 3) effectively illustrate the key concepts of the LSDA framework. The methodology section (Section 3) provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, though the nearest neighbor approach for category-specific adaptation could be more precisely justified. The writing is accessible to computer vision researchers, with appropriate technical depth.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a meaningful problem (scaling detection to large numbers of categories) with a novel approach. While domain adaptation has been applied to detection before, LSDA is the first method to adapt to held-out categories that have no detection data. The authors correctly position their work relative to related literature, noting that MIL-based methods [6,7] are complementary but haven't been evaluated on large-scale ImageNet detection. The nearest neighbor approach for category-specific adaptation is a novel aspect of their method that distinguishes it from prior work.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly significant for computer vision, as it potentially enables detection for tens of thousands of categories that currently lack bounding box annotations. The demonstration of a 7,604 category detector using ImageNet's classification data is impressive and showcases the practical potential of the approach. The paper demonstrates clear progress toward bridging the gap between large-scale classification and detection. The fast version running at 2fps shows potential for real-world applications, though the paper could better quantify the trade-offs between speed and accuracy.

**Strengths:**
- Addresses a meaningful problem with significant practical implications
- Novel framing of classification-to-detection transfer as domain adaptation
- Thorough experimental validation with ablation study
- Demonstrates scalability to 7,600 categories
- Provides code and models for reproducibility

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited comparison to state-of-the-art domain adaptation methods for detection
- Insufficient justification for using Euclidean distance on fc8 parameters for nearest neighbor search
- Minimal discussion of computational cost of adaptation
- Classification data used is weak (image-level labels), which could impact performance

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Solves a meaningful problem with clear practical applications
- Novel approach that significantly advances the state of the art in scaling object detection
- Well-executed experiments with convincing results
- Provides valuable resources (code, models) for the community

*Con:*
- Limited comparison to related domain adaptation techniques
- Some methodological details could be better justified
- The paper doesn't fully address how their method compares to the best MIL approaches [6,7] on large-scale detection

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field by enabling detection for thousands of categories with minimal bounding box annotations. The LSDA framework is technically sound, well-validated, and has clear potential for practical impact. While there are some limitations in the comparison to related work and methodological justification, these do not undermine the core contribution. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria well and represents a strong contribution to the field of computer vision and machine learning. I recommend acceptance.
