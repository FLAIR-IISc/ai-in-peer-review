- Proposes LSDA: learn a transformation from classifiers to detectors on annotated categories and apply it to categories without bounding boxes.  
- Addresses bounding-box annotation scarcity, enabling large-scale detection (demonstrated 7.6K categories).  
- Builds on R-CNN and positions itself relative to MIL and domain adaptation literature; novelty is adapting held-out categories with no detection data.  
- Leverages deep feature learning and a supervised adaptation transformation; ablation studies show component contributions.  
- Empirical results on ImageNet show substantial improvement over using raw classifier weights; includes error analysis and localization examples.  
- Public release of code and models improves reproducibility.  
- Writing is clear and methodology/experiments are easy to follow.  
- Novelty: transferring classification knowledge to detection for categories without bounding boxes is a significant new idea.  
- Significance: potential to scale detection to tens of thousands of categories with only image-level labels.  
- Technical soundness: transformation approach is well-motivated and supported by experiments.  
- Weakness: lacks direct comparison to state-of-the-art MIL methods on ImageNet.  
- Weakness: category-specific adaptation uses an ad-hoc nearest-neighbor heuristic that needs stronger justification or alternatives.  
- Weakness: marginal gain from category-specific adaptation (k=10) is small, suggesting potential performance saturation.  
- Weakness: computational cost for training/running the full 7.6K detector is not fully discussed and may be a barrier.  
- Weakness: error analysis could be deeper, e.g., identifying categories where LSDA fails.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a critical scaling bottleneck, is novel, demonstrates strong empirical gains, and provides code.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing MIL comparisons, relies on heuristic adaptation, and shows limited further improvement.  
- Overall assessment: strong contribution with minor weaknesses; recommends acceptance with minor revisions addressing MIL comparison, heuristic justification, and computational costs.
