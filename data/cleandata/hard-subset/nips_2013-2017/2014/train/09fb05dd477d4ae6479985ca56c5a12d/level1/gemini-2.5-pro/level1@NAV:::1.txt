This paper addresses the critical challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, for which bounding box annotations are prohibitively expensive to collect. The authors propose Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a method that leverages abundant image-level classification data. The core idea is to learn a general transformation from an image classifier to an object detector. This transformation is learned on a subset of categories for which both classification and detection data are available. It is then applied to adapt classifiers for new categories that lack any bounding box annotations. This work builds upon the fine-tuning paradigm of R-CNN [1] but cleverly extends it to a domain adaptation setting where the target categories for detection are held out during the adaptation training phase.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a highly significant and practical problem. The bottleneck of bounding box annotation is a major impediment to creating truly large-scale detection systems. This work offers a principled and effective approach to mitigate this issue, as demonstrated by the creation of a 7,604-category detector. The idea is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers.
*   **Originality and Quality:** The concept of learning a transferable, category-invariant adaptation from classification to detection is novel and well-executed. The experiments on the ILSVRC2013 dataset are thorough, with a clear ablation study that isolates the contribution of each component. The 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories is substantial. The error analysis compellingly shows that the method successfully learns to reduce localization errors and background confusion, which are key differentiators between classification and detection tasks.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the method is explained logically, and the figures effectively illustrate the proposed architecture and results. The authors provide enough detail to understand the experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Performance Gap:** While the relative improvement is impressive, the absolute mAP on held-out categories (16.15%) remains significantly lower than the performance achievable with full supervision. This suggests that while the learned generic transformation is effective, it cannot fully replace category-specific detection data.
*   **Heuristic for Specific Adaptation:** The category-specific adaptation, which averages the learned weight-deltas from k-nearest neighbors, feels somewhat heuristic. While empirically shown to help, a more theoretically grounded justification for this transfer mechanism would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** This paper presents a novel and significant contribution to a difficult, real-world problem. The proposed method is elegant, the experimental validation is strong, and the results are compelling.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** The performance on held-out classes, while improved, is still modest in absolute terms, highlighting the limitations of the approach.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that clearly advances the state-of-the-art in large-scale object detection. The strengths, particularly the significance and originality of the core idea, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
