This paper addresses the scarcity of bounding-box annotations for large-scale object detection by proposing LSDA, a method that adapts image classifiers into object detectors. The core idea is to learn a category-invariant transformation via fine-tuning a CNN on a subset of categories with detection labels (including a background class), and then to approximate category-specific adaptation for held-out classes by transferring the average parameter “delta” from nearest neighbors in fc8 space. On ILSVRC13, LSDA improves mAP on held-out categories from 10.3% to 16.2% (+50% relative) and scales to a 7.6K-class detector, with a faster variant using SPP and faster proposals. The work builds on R-CNN (Girshick et al.) and AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al.), relates to weakly supervised detection (MIL) and domain adaptation (e.g., Kulis & Saenko; Aytar & Zisserman), and contrasts with adaptation methods that require detection labels for all categories.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound and well-motivated. The ablation cleanly decomposes gains into background modeling, feature-level adaptation, and fc8 deltas, with a significance test. However, the category-specific transfer is heuristic (nearest-neighbor averaging in fc8), and comparisons to strong weakly supervised baselines (MIL on VOC/ILSVRC) or alternative adaptation schemes (e.g., regression from semantic/visual embeddings, hierarchy-aware transfer) are missing. The choice to forego SVMs and bbox regression simplifies training but leaves some accuracy on the table; a quantified trade-off would help.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and organized; training protocol, data splits, and implementation details (Caffe) are described. Some hyperparameters (e.g., k sensitivity, distance normalization) could be detailed further.

Originality: The framing of classification-to-detection as task/domain adaptation with category-invariant and category-specific components is novel relative to R-CNN and prior DA work that assumes full supervision. The simple fc8-delta transfer is an interesting, pragmatic contribution.

Significance: Scaling detection to thousands of classes is important for the community; releasing a 7.6K detector and code increases impact. The absolute performance remains far from full supervision, but the demonstrated gains are substantial and likely to inspire follow-up work.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, practical adaptation framework with significant gains on held-out categories
- Strong empirical analysis and error diagnostics; code/models released
- Demonstrated scalability (7.6K classes) and runtime improvements

Cons for acceptance:
- Heuristic category-specific transfer; limited exploration of alternative transfer signals
- Missing head-to-head with strong weakly supervised baselines on common benchmarks
- Lacks bbox regression/SVM comparisons in the partial-supervision regime to quantify trade-offs

Overall: A solid, impactful contribution; I recommend acceptance.
