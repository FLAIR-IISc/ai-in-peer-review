- Proposes LSDA to enable object detection for categories without bounding-box annotations by adapting classifiers trained on image-level data.  
- Frames the task as domain adaptation from classification (source) to detection (target), learning a generic transformation on a subset with detection data and applying it to held-out categories.  
- Method has three components: category-invariant adaptation (fine-tune shared network layers with detection data), category-specific adaptation (approximate per-category transform via nearest neighbors), and background-aware scoring at inference.  
- Targets the key bottleneck of annotation cost to scale detection to tens of thousands of categories.  
- Strong empirical gains on ImageNet LSVRC-2013: about 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories.  
- Demonstrates scalability with a 7.6K-category detector as a compelling proof-of-concept.  
- Ablation studies and statistical significance testing support the contribution and show the importance of each component.  
- Strengths: solid technical execution, clear methodology, thorough evaluation, and practical real-world relevance.  
- Weaknesses: limited analysis of failure modes for the nearest-neighbor approximation, evaluation confined to ImageNet, and no direct comparison to MIL-based methods.  
- Clarity: well-structured and well-written, though some details of the nearest-neighbor implementation need clarification.  
- Originality: novel and distinctive framing of large-scale detection as domain adaptation, extending prior adaptation work.  
- Recommendation: significant contribution with clear impact; recommend acceptance.
