Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses the scarcity of bounding-box annotations for large-scale object detection by proposing LSDA, a method that adapts image classifiers into object detectors. The core idea is to learn the generic transformation between classification and detection using a subset of categories that have detection labels (set B), and transfer this to categories without bounding boxes (set A). The adaptation has three components: (1) learn a background class and fine-tune the shared feature extractor (layers 1–7) on detection data for B (category-invariant adaptation), (2) adapt category-specific output weights for B via fine-tuning, and (3) for A, approximate category-specific adaptation by adding to each classifier’s weights the average of the fine-tuning deltas from its nearest neighbors in B, measured in the normalized classification weight space.

The method is implemented within a CNN similar to Krizhevsky et al. (AlexNet) and uses an R-CNN-style pipeline with region proposals. On ILSVRC-2013 detection, the authors simulate the partially labeled setting: all 200 classes have image-level labels; only 100 have bounding boxes. LSDA improves mAP on held-out categories from 10.31% (classification-only baseline) to 16.15% (+50% relative), with most gains attributed to the feature-level adaptation and background modeling; category-specific delta transfer yields a modest additional gain. They also demonstrate a 7,604-category detector trained largely without bounding boxes and provide a faster variant using SPP and fast region proposals.

This work builds on R-CNN (Girshick et al.), OverFeat (Sermanet et al.), and SPP-net (He et al.), and relates to weakly supervised detection via MIL (e.g., Song et al.) and to domain adaptation (e.g., Aytar & Zisserman; Hoffman et al.). Unlike supervised detector adaptation approaches, LSDA adapts to categories with no detection labels.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important practical problem: scaling detection to thousands of categories with limited box annotations.
- Simple, well-motivated approach that leverages category-invariant adaptation and background modeling; strong ablations isolate the contribution of each component.
- Demonstrates nontrivial mAP gains on held-out categories without any bounding boxes; error analysis shows reduced localization/background confusions after adaptation.
- Releases models and code; shows scalability to 7.6K classes and a faster variant for practical deployment.

Weaknesses:
- Absolute performance on held-out categories remains low (10.31 → 16.15 mAP), and the incremental benefit of category-specific delta transfer is small; the nearest-neighbor heuristic in fc8 space is ad hoc and insufficiently justified compared to alternatives (semantic/visual similarity).
- Evaluation is limited to a single split (100/100) within the same 200-class universe; no study of performance vs. number of detection-annotated categories m or of which classes constitute B (e.g., diverse vs. semantically targeted).
- Missing comparisons to strong weakly supervised detection baselines trained on the same data (e.g., MIL-based methods) and to OverFeat-style detection/classification transfer; R-CNN upper-bound is shown but not matched due to omitting SVMs/bbox regression.
- The 7.6K detector is only qualitatively evaluated; even a small sampled quantitative audit would strengthen the large-scale claim.
- Some training specifics are underspecified: sampling of background boxes and class/region balancing, learning rates/schedules for classification vs. detection fine-tuning, calibration of “score_i − score_bg” vs. softmax or log-odds.

Quality:
Technically sound overall. The empirical methodology (train/val split; ablations; significance test on per-class AP) is reasonable, though the breadth of baselines is limited. The core claim—that feature-level adaptation plus background modeling transfers to unseen categories—is supported. The category-specific transfer is plausible but contributes little; more rigorous alternatives or analysis would help.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Section 3 could be strengthened by formalizing the objective/loss functions used during fine-tuning and precisely describing the background sampling strategy and class balancing. The definition and choice of k for nearest neighbors, the distance metric, and sensitivity analyses deserve clearer presentation.

Originality:
Moderate. Adapting a classification CNN to detection via fine-tuning and background class is known (R-CNN), but LSDA’s framing as category-invariant transfer to unseen categories plus the simple fc8-delta sharing is novel and timely for 2014. The focus on large-scale deployment is a differentiator.

Significance:
High practical significance. Even with modest absolute mAP, enabling detectors for thousands of categories with zero box labels per class is impactful and likely to spur follow-on work (e.g., better transfer mechanisms, combining with weakly supervised learning). The code/model release increases community value.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Compare against: (a) a tuned OverFeat/cls-to-det baseline; (b) a MIL-based weakly supervised detector trained on set A; and (c) category-invariant bbox regression learned on B and applied to A.
- Analyze performance as a function of m (number of categories with boxes) and the similarity/diversity of B; report results for several random/semi-structured splits.
- Explore alternative similarity for category-specific transfer: WordNet/attributes, visual confusion matrices, features from earlier layers; report sensitivity to k and the distance metric.
- Provide limited quantitative evaluation for a subset of the 7.6K classes (e.g., crowd-sourced bounding boxes for 50–100 categories).
- Discuss calibration: is score_i − score_bg equivalent to log-odds under softmax? Would temperature scaling or Platt scaling improve ranking across categories?

Pros for acceptance:
- Tackles an important, underexplored problem in 2014: zero-box transfer from classification to detection at scale.
- Clear, simple method with demonstrated gains and solid analysis.
- Code/models available; practical impact with 7.6K-class detector and faster variant.
- Insightful error analysis highlighting the role of background and localization.

Cons for acceptance:
- Absolute mAP is low; modest gains from the category-specific transfer.
- Missing comparisons to strong alternative paradigms (MIL) and baselines.
- Limited evaluation scope (single split; no m-sensitivity; qualitative-only large-scale results).
- Some methodological choices appear heuristic without strong justification.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The problem is important, the approach is sound and practically useful, and the results are convincing enough to merit publication, despite missing comparisons and limited absolute performance.

Reviewer confidence: High.
