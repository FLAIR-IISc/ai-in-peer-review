Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses the scarcity of bounding-box annotations for large-scale object detection. It proposes LSDA, a two-stage adaptation approach that converts image classifiers into object detectors by learning the transformation between tasks on a subset of categories with detection data and transferring it to categories without boxes. The method first performs category-invariant adaptation by fine-tuning a CNN (layers 1–7) with detection data on a subset B, introducing a background class to handle the classification-to-detection task shift. It then performs category-specific adaptation for held-out categories A by approximating the class-specific weight changes learned on B: for each category in A, it averages the “delta” of its nearest neighbors in B (nearest in the fc8 classification weight space) and adds this to the classification weights. Detection scores are computed as class score minus background score.

Experiments on ILSVRC-2013 detection (200 classes) simulate having detection annotations for 100 categories and only classification labels for all 200. Ablations show: (i) using a classification network directly on region proposals is a strong baseline; (ii) fine-tuning for detection on B with a background class and updating shared layers substantially helps held-out classes (10.31% to 15.85% mAP); (iii) the category-specific fc-layer adaptation adds modest gains (to 16.15% mAP, p=0.017). Error analysis indicates reduced localization and background false positives after adaptation. The authors also build a 7.6K-category detector by combining detection training for 200 classes with classification-only training for 7.4K categories and report a faster variant using SPP and fast proposals.

This work builds directly on CNN-based classification [4], R-CNN-style detection and fine-tuning [1], and ideas from domain adaptation, while differing from conventional supervised detector adaptation methods [17–22] that require target labels for all categories. It is complementary to weakly supervised/multiple instance learning approaches [6,7], which are not evaluated at this scale.

Quality:
- Methodology: The approach is clearly specified, computationally feasible, and leverages standard CNN training/fine-tuning. The category-invariant transformation via shared-layer fine-tuning with a background class is well-supported empirically and is the dominant contributor to the gains. The category-specific adaptation via nearest neighbors in fc8 space is plausible; while heuristic, it yields statistically significant but small improvements.
- Experiments: The evaluation is careful, with a clear hold-out protocol, ablations isolating background vs. shared-layer vs. fc-layer effects, and error diagnosis (localization/background vs. misclassification). The upper bound with full supervision is reported. Runtime considerations and a faster variant are discussed.
- Limitations: The reliance on 100 fully supervised categories is not varied; sensitivity to the number of supervised categories m is not explored. There is no comparison to contemporary weakly supervised detection (MIL) methods [6,7] on the same benchmark. The nearest-neighbor metric (L2 on normalized fc8 weights) is not contrasted to alternatives (e.g., semantic/WordNet distance, feature-space similarity from earlier layers). No bounding-box regression is used, which is known to improve detection, and removing SVMs simplifies training but conflates differences to R-CNN. Potential label leakage/background contamination when training background on images that may contain unlabeled held-out objects is not quantified.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The definition of sets A (no boxes) and B (with boxes) is consistent, though the index notation for A = {m,…,K} could be clarified. Training details (learning rates, iterations, hardware, k selection) are mostly described; adding a brief sensitivity analysis for k and nearest-neighbor selection would help. The error analysis is illustrative and informative. Providing per-class performance deltas for representative categories would further clarify where adaptation helps most.

Originality:
Adapting from classification to detection across categories with no bounding-box annotations is a novel setting for CNN-based detection in 2014. The category-invariant adaptation via shared-layer fine-tuning with an explicit background and transfer to held-out classes is a useful and original framing. The category-specific nearest-neighbor delta transfer is simple and new in this context, though conceptually related to model transfer and domain adaptation literature.

Significance:
The paper targets a practically important problem—scaling detection to thousands of categories without expensive bounding-box labeling. Demonstrated gains on held-out categories and the release of a 7.6K detector underscore potential impact. While absolute mAP remains far below full supervision, the relative improvement over a strong baseline and the ability to instantiate detectors for many classes are significant contributions likely to influence subsequent large-scale detection work.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Addresses a relevant large-scale problem with a pragmatic, effective approach.
- Clear empirical evidence that category-invariant adaptation (plus background) significantly improves held-out class detection.
- Simple, general method that can be applied to any CNN classifier; implementation details and models released.
- Thorough ablations and error analysis; statistically validated improvement for category-specific adaptation.
- Demonstrates scalability (7.6K categories) and considers runtime.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Limited comparative baselines: no head-to-head with weakly supervised/MIL detectors on the same benchmark.
- Heuristic category-specific transfer; no analysis of alternative similarity metrics or sensitivity to k; modest gains from this step.
- No study of performance as a function of the number of supervised categories m or distributional mismatch between A and B.
- Excludes bounding-box regression and SVM calibration; unclear how much of the gap to the upper bound is due to adaptation vs. missing components.
- Potential training bias if unlabeled A-class objects are treated as background; not quantified.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include comparisons to strong weakly supervised detection baselines [6,7] on ILSVRC (even if on a subset) to contextualize gains.
- Analyze sensitivity to k and alternative neighbor metrics (e.g., WordNet/semantic similarity, cosine in feature space from fc7).
- Vary m (number and diversity of supervised categories) to show how adaptation quality scales and how to choose B.
- Add bounding-box regression (even if trained only on B) and report its effect on held-out categories.
- Quantify the impact of possible background contamination by estimating the fraction of held-out objects in background regions and its effect.

Overall assessment:
Technically sound, clearly presented, and novel in framing classification-to-detection adaptation for held-out categories, with meaningful improvements and strong practical significance. Despite some missing comparisons and limited analysis of the category-specific transfer, the contribution is substantial for the 2014 landscape.
