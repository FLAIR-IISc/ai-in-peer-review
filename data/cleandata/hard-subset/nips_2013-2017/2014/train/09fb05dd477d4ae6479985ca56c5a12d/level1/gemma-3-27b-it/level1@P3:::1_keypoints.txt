- Core idea: adapt classifiers trained on image-level labels into detectors using a domain-adaptation formulation to scale detection to many categories.  
- Method: builds on R-CNN-style architectures and frames classifier→detector transfer as domain adaptation.  
- Results: shows significant improvement over a naive baseline and demonstrates a 7.6K-category detector.  
- Practicality: includes a faster detector variant and publicly released code/models for reproducibility.  
- Technical quality: algorithm is clearly presented, logically structured, and technically sound.  
- Ablation: informative ablation studies isolate contributions of background recognition, category-invariant, and category-specific adaptation.  
- Reproducibility: uses Caffe and releases code/models, aiding replication.  
- Weakness: nearest-neighbor averaging for category-specific adaptation lacks strong theoretical justification and feels ad-hoc.  
- Performance gap: absolute mAP, especially on held-out categories, remains relatively low and could be improved.  
- Clarity strengths: well-written, organized, with helpful figures and clear notation.  
- Clarity weaknesses: experimental setup (exact training image counts, augmentation) and error analysis (Loc/BG/Oth) need more detail and examples.  
- Originality: novel and significant application of domain adaptation to transfer classification knowledge to detection for categories without bounding boxes.  
- Significance: approach enables scalable detectors for thousands of categories and reduces annotation costs, with strong practical impact.  
- Acceptance pros: addresses a critical problem, novel adaptation approach, strong empirical gains, large-scale 7.6K demo, fast variant, and public resources.  
- Acceptance cons: weak theoretical justification for some design choices, low absolute mAP on held-out classes, incomplete experimental details, and unclear error analysis.  
- Recommendation: strongly recommended for acceptance despite minor issues due to originality, impact, and technical merit.
