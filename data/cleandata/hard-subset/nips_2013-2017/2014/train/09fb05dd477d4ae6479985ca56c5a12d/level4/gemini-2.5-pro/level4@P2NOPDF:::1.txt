This paper addresses the challenge of object detection in the context of limited annotation data, specifically the scarcity of bounding box annotations compared to the abundance of category labels. The authors circumvent this issue by proposing a method to adapt object classifiers for the detection task. The algorithm is commendably simple and straightforward. In their experimental evaluation, the model is trained on 100 categories with both category labels and bounding boxes, and then tested on 100 novel categories for which only category labels are available. The results are promising, closing approximately 35% of the performance gap between a baseline method and an oracle on these held-out categories.

**Quality and Clarity**
This is a high-quality, well-written paper. The proposed method and experimental setup are presented with clarity, making the work easy to follow and understand.

**Originality**
The paper's originality lies in its synthesis of two existing concepts: adapting classifiers for detection and adapting models across different sets of object categories. While neither idea is new in isolation, their combination to address the problem of limited bounding box data is, to my knowledge, novel and interesting. The contribution is a valuable incremental step rather than a groundbreaking discovery.

**Significance**
The primary contribution of this work is its novel formulation of the problem and the proposed adaptation approach. From a performance perspective, the long-term significance is difficult to assess. While the results are encouraging for an initial study, it is likely that more sophisticated methods will eventually surpass them. A central issue is that the method inherently trades detection performance (mAP) for a reduction in annotation cost. The paper does not offer an objective framework to evaluate whether this trade-off is worthwhile, making it hard to judge the practical impact.

Furthermore, it is worth considering whether the scarcity of bounding boxes will remain a critical long-term challenge. While they are currently more expensive to acquire than category labels, it is conceivable that this problem could be largely solved by scaling up data collection efforts, much as was done for large-scale image classification datasets in the past.

**Detailed Issues that Should Be Addressed**
*   In Figure 3a, the comparison between methods is unclear regarding their use of auxiliary information. To ensure a fair, apples-to-apples comparison, the authors should explicitly state which of the competing methods (besides their own and R-CNN) also leverage category-level labels during detection.

*   The paper's central claims about performance could be strengthened by focusing on a more insightful metric. I believe the most compelling result is the performance on the held-out categories, as shown by the blue bars in Figure 3b, where the proposed DNN method closes about one-third of the gap between the baseline and the oracle. I would encourage the authors to highlight this result as a main finding. The currently emphasized "78% of oracle performance" (line 341) is less informative for two reasons: (1) performance should be judged primarily on the novel, held-out categories where the adaptation is truly tested, and (2) it is more meaningful to measure improvement relative to the baseline, not just as a fraction of the oracle's score. The same logic applies to the "50% improvement" claim (line 425), which would be more persuasive if contextualized by the baseline.

*   R-CNN appears to be the most closely related work, but the discussion of its differences from the proposed method is currently fragmented throughout the paper. The paper's clarity would be significantly improved by consolidating this comparison into a dedicated subsection or a comparative table. This would help readers quickly grasp the unique contributions of the proposed method and could potentially replace Figure 6, which provides only anecdotal qualitative examples.

**Overall Recommendation**
In summary, this is a solid paper that proposes a novel and sensible approach to a relevant problem. The work is of sufficient quality for acceptance and deserves to be seen by the NIPS community. While the method is simple and its results will likely be surpassed in the future, it serves as an important first step in this direction of research. The long-term impact of the approach, however, remains to be seen. I recommend acceptance.
