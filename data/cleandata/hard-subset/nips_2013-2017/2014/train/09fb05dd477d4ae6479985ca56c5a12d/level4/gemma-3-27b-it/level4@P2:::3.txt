This paper introduces a method for adapting a convolutional neural network (CNN), pre-trained for classification without bounding box annotations, to perform object detection, leveraging a small number of classes with available bounding box labels. The approach involves three key steps: initial CNN training for classification across all categories, CNN training on image regions for classes with bounding box annotations (including a background class), and finally, integrating the background class and adapting network weights for the complete model. At test time, the CNN processes multiple image regions to generate predictions.

**Quality:** This is a technically sound and interesting paper that demonstrates promising results. While the core idea builds upon the successes of models like Krizhevsky et al.'s network, the adaptation strategy is valuable and relevant to the computer vision and machine learning communities. The approach is logically straightforward, and the experimental results warrant further consideration. However, the evaluation would be significantly strengthened by including comparisons to several crucial baselines. Specifically:

1.  Training and fine-tuning the entire CNN using full images as positive examples and image regions from other classes as negative examples.
2.  Employing a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, where, for each image in the set lacking bounding box labels, the highest-scoring region is iteratively selected as the positive example.
3.  Training and fine-tuning the CNN solely for classification. At test time, applying the classifier and predicting a single bounding box centered within the image (e.g., utilizing the mean bounding box coordinates from ImageNet).

It’s unclear whether the proposed method outperforms these baselines. The described approach doesn't necessarily present a clear advantage over the first baseline, and it’s plausible that the second baseline would yield comparable or superior results. Including the third baseline would provide essential context regarding the inherent difficulty of the problem and dataset.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand. However, the clarity of the experiments section could be improved, particularly concerning the explanation of the methods presented in Table 1.  More detail is needed to clarify what each method entails (e.g., does it involve applying Equation 1 or 2 to a specific subset of CNN layers?). A more precise description would enhance the reproducibility and understanding of the results.

**Originality:** The paper presents a sufficiently novel contribution to warrant acceptance, although the underlying approach is relatively straightforward and potentially tailored to specific CNN architectures. The adaptation strategy represents a valuable extension to existing methods.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is relevant, and the results achieved are significant. Obtaining reasonable detection performance on a large-scale dataset without relying on bounding box annotations is a compelling outcome. However, the absence of key baseline comparisons raises concerns about whether the observed improvements are genuinely attributable to the proposed adaptation technique or simply a consequence of the improved representational power of CNN features. 

**Overall Assessment:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical challenge in scaling object detection: the scarcity of bounding box annotations.
*   Demonstrates promising performance on the ImageNet detection task.
*   The LSDA algorithm offers a practical approach for leveraging readily available image-level labels.
*   The released models and software facilitate further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lacks comparison to crucial baseline methods, hindering a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach.
*   The contribution's significance is partially diminished by the lack of baseline comparisons.
*   The potential for capitalizing on the inherent improvements of CNN features rather than a truly novel adaptation strategy needs to be addressed.



I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns raised regarding the inclusion of the suggested baseline comparisons and clarifying the details of the experiments. A more thorough evaluation will strengthen the paper's claims and solidify its contribution to the field.
