This paper addresses the challenge of object detection, particularly the high cost of obtaining bounding box annotations at the same scale as category labels for image classification. The authors circumvent this issue by proposing a method to adapt object classifiers for the detection task. The resulting algorithm is simple and straightforward. In the experiments, the model is trained on 100 categories with both category labels and bounding boxes, and then tested on 100 held-out categories for which only category labels are available during training. The results are reasonably good, closing approximately 35% of the performance gap between a baseline and an oracle method on the held-out categories.

**Quality and Clarity**
This is a high-quality, clearly-written paper. The method is well-described and the experiments are easy to understand.

**Originality**
The paper's main contribution is the synthesis of two existing concepts: adapting object classifiers for detection and adapting models from a known set of categories to a new set. To my knowledge, this combination is novel and presents an interesting direction for research, although it is not a groundbreaking advance.

**Significance**
The primary contribution of this work is its novel approach. The long-term significance of the results is difficult to judge, as they will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods in the future. A central issue is that the method inherently sacrifices performance (mAP) to reduce the reliance on bounding box annotations, and the paper does not offer an objective way to evaluate if this trade-off is worthwhile.

On a related note, the paper's premise that the cost of bounding boxes is a fundamental long-term challenge is debatable. While bounding boxes are more "expensive" than category labels today, it is plausible that this annotation bottleneck could be overcome with sufficient resources in the future, much like the creation of massive image classification datasets (e.g., ImageNet), which once seemed infeasible.

**Detailed Issues to be Addressed**
*   In Figure 3a, for an apples-to-apples comparison, the authors should explicitly state which of the competing methods (other than their own and R-CNN) also use category-level labels to aid detection.
*   This reviewer's assessment of the method's effectiveness is primarily based on the blue bars in Figure 3b, which show the DNN method closes roughly one-third of the gap between the baseline and the oracle on the held-out categories. If the authors agree that this is a main result, it should be highlighted. The current claim of achieving "78% of oracle performance" (line 341) is less informative for two reasons: 1) performance should be judged relative to the baseline, not just the oracle, and 2) the evaluation should focus on the held-out categories, which is the core scenario addressed by the paper. The same critique applies to the "50% improvement" claim (line 425).
*   R-CNN appears to be the closest competing alternative, but the distinctions between the proposed method and R-CNN are scattered throughout the text. The authors should create a dedicated table or sub-section to clearly consolidate and articulate these differences. This would add significant clarity and could replace Figure 6, which currently offers only anecdotal examples.

**Minor Issues (no response needed)**
*   The name of the proposed method is inconsistent. Is it "DNN" or "DDA"?
*   There is a typo on line 431 ("classi?ers").

**Overall Assessment**
This is a solid paper proposing a reasonably novel approach to an important problem. While the results will almost certainly be surpassed and the long-term significance of the approach remains to be seen, the method's simplicity is acceptable for a first paper in this direction. I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance and will be of interest to the NIPS community.
