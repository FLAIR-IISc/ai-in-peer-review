- Paper addresses object detection by adapting object classifiers to detection to avoid large-scale bounding-box annotation.
- Method is simple and direct; experiments train on 100 categories with boxes+labels and evaluate on 100 disjoint categories with labels only.
- Results close roughly 35% of the gap between a baseline and an oracle on the held-out 100 categories, which is reasonably strong.
- Paper is well written and of good quality.
- Originality: combines two established ideas (classifier→detector adaptation and cross-category adaptation); novel combination but not revolutionary.
- Significance: a useful new approach but not clearly transformative; long-term impact is uncertain and may be overtaken by more sophisticated methods.
- Key concern: relying on fewer bounding boxes inherently reduces mAP and the paper lacks an objective criterion to judge whether that trade-off is worthwhile.
- Question whether the bounding-box scarcity problem is a major long-term obstacle given possible increased annotation investment.
- Clarify Figure 3a: explicitly state which competing methods use category labels to aid detection for apples-to-apples comparison.
- Emphasize the blue bars in Figure 3b as a central result (DNN sits ~1/3 from baseline to oracle) and reframe claims (avoid using the 78% figure and “50% improvement” without proper context).
- Provide a clear table or subsection enumerating differences from R-CNN, as R-CNN is the closest competitor; consider removing Figure 6 to save space.
- Minor issues: inconsistent naming of the method (DNN vs DDA) and a typo on line 431 (“classi?ers”).
- Overall recommendation: solid paper, recommend acceptance for the NIPS audience despite limited long-term impact.
