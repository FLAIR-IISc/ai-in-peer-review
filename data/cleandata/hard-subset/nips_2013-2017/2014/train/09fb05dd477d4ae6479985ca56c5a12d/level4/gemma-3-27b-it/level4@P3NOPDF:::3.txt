This paper presents a method for adapting a Krizhevsky CNN, pre-trained for image classification (without bounding box annotations), to the task of object detection given a limited number of classes with bounding box labels. The approach involves a three-stage training process: first, a CNN is trained for classification on images from all classes; second, a CNN is trained on image regions cropped from classes *with* bounding box annotations, augmented with a negative background region class; and third, the background class is integrated into the full CNN model with adaptation of some CNN weights. During testing, the CNN processes multiple image regions within a test image to identify potential object locations.

**Quality:** This is a sound and interesting paper with compelling results. While the work largely builds upon the demonstrated success of Krizhevsky et al.’s model rather than presenting a radically new architectural contribution, the method’s utility to researchers in computer vision and machine learning is significant. The core approach is conceptually simple and logically motivated. The experimental results are promising, but the absence of several key baselines weakens the evaluation. Specifically, I recommend the authors include comparisons to the following:

1) Fine-tune all layers of the CNN for classification using full images as positive examples and images/regions from other classes as negative examples.
2) Implement a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, iteratively selecting the highest-scoring positive region for each image from the dataset and using this for training, instead of using the full image.
3) Train and fine-tune the CNN solely for classification. At test time, apply the classifier to the entire image and predict a single bounding box, potentially centered around a prior like the mean bounding box location from ImageNet. 

It is unclear whether the proposed method would consistently outperform these baselines. Baseline 1, in particular, represents a straightforward and competitive alternative, and its inclusion is crucial to establish the value of the proposed approach. Baseline 2 is a strong MIL alternative and may be more effective. Baseline 3 would help contextualize the difficulty of the task and the dataset itself, clarifying whether the observed improvements stem from the specific training procedure or simply from the improved quality of the CNN features.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clear and well-written. However, the clarity of the experiments section could be enhanced. Specifically, the description of the methods presented in Table 1 requires further elaboration. It is not immediately obvious what applying Eq. 1 or 2 to a subset of CNN layers entails. A more detailed explanation of these variations is needed.

**Originality:** The work presents a sufficient level of novelty to warrant acceptance, although the approach is relatively simple and likely tailored to CNN architectures of a specific type (i.e., those pre-trained for classification).

**Significance:** The problem addressed is significant, and the reported results are valuable. Successfully achieving reasonable object detection performance on a large-scale dataset utilizing only image-level labels is a compelling achievement. However, the lack of thorough baseline comparisons raises the possibility that the observed performance is primarily attributable to the benefits of using pre-trained CNN features rather than a truly improved training methodology for object detection with weak supervision. 

In conclusion, this paper presents a promising approach to object detection with limited supervision. Addressing the concerns regarding baseline comparisons would significantly strengthen the paper and more convincingly demonstrate the value of the proposed method.
