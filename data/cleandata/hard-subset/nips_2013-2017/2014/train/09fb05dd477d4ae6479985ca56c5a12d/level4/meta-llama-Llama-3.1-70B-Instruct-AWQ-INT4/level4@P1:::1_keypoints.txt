- Tackles object detection without large-scale bounding-box annotations by adapting object classifiers into detectors.  
- Proposes a straightforward algorithm combining adaptation of classifiers to detection and category-to-category adaptation.  
- Paper is well-written and of good quality.  
- Experimental protocol is clear: 100 categories trained with boxes, tested on 100 left-out categories with only labels.  
- Main quantitative result: method fills about 35% of the gap between an oracle and a baseline on the 100 left-out categories.  
- Reported 78% number is misleading; evaluation should focus on left-out categories and not simply percent of oracle.  
- Novelty is modest but interesting—combines two existing adaptation ideas rather than being groundbreaking.  
- Trade-off: using fewer bounding boxes reduces mAP, and there is no objective metric to judge if the trade-off is worthwhile.  
- Potential long-term concern: bounding-box annotation scarcity may become less relevant with rapid progress in computer vision.  
- Requested clarification: in Figure 3a, indicate which methods use category labels for detection to ensure fair comparisons.  
- Requested addition: include a table/subsection clearly detailing differences from R-CNN, the closest competing method.  
- Minor fixes: correct typo ("classi?ers") and consistently name the proposed method (DNN vs DDA).  
- Overall recommendation: paper is solid and worth accepting, though long-term significance is uncertain.
