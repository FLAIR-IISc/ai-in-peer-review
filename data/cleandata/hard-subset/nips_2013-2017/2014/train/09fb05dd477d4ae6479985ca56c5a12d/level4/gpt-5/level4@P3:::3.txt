Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LSDA, a method to convert image classifiers into object detectors when only a subset of categories have bounding-box annotations. The approach treats the classification-to-detection shift as a domain adaptation problem. It learns a category-invariant transformation by fine-tuning layers 1–7 of an ImageNet-pretrained CNN (AlexNet) on detection data for m categories, adding a background class, and then transfers this transformation to all K categories. Additionally, it learns a category-specific shift: for categories without detection labels, it approximates the parameter change observed on their nearest labeled neighbors (in fc8 space) and adds the averaged “delta” to the classification weights. At test time, LSDA scores region proposals with a K+1-way softmax and uses score_i − score_background as the final class score.

Relative to R-CNN (Girshick et al.), which fine-tunes on bounding boxes for all categories, LSDA leverages detection labels for only a subset of categories, learns the background class, and transfers both feature-level and output-layer adaptations to held-out classes. The work also connects to weakly supervised detection via MIL, but differs in not relying on latent box selection for held-out classes, and to domain adaptation methods that typically assume category overlap across domains. The authors evaluate on ILSVRC2013 detection, simulating 100/200 categories with detection labels and reporting a 50% relative mAP gain over a classification-only baseline on the 100 held-out classes. They further demonstrate scalability by releasing a 7.6K-category detector and a faster SPP-based variant.

Quality
- Soundness: The methodology is coherent and well-motivated. The category-invariant adaptation (fine-tuning with a background class) is the main driver of gains; category-specific adaptation via nearest-neighbor deltas provides smaller but statistically significant improvements. The ablations and error analysis (localization vs. background vs. other errors) are informative and consistent with the task shift from classification to detection.
- Empirical rigor: The experimental setup on ILSVRC2013 is reasonable (100/200 split, region proposals, val1/val2 protocol). However, several baselines are missing, making it hard to isolate whether the observed gains are due to the proposed adaptation or simply due to stronger CNN features and the addition of a background class. Recommended baselines:
  1) Weakly supervised fine-tuning without adaptation: treat full images with image-level labels as positive, and images/regions from other classes as negatives; fine-tune all layers and add a background class; test on region proposals.
  2) MIL-based weak supervision for set A: iteratively select the highest-scoring positive region per image (for classes without boxes) while using detection labels for set B; compare against LSDA on held-out classes.
  3) Simple localization heuristic: run the classifier and predict a canonical/mean bounding box (or center box) to gauge dataset difficulty and the benefit of proposal-driven detection.
These comparisons would clarify how much of LSDA’s benefit comes from background modeling and proposal evaluation versus the specific adaptation mechanisms.

- Additional analysis suggestions:
  - Report sensitivity to the number of nearest neighbors k and to the distance metric used in fc8 space; consider cosine similarity or learned metrics.
  - Provide per-class gains vs. class frequency and object size to understand where adaptation helps most.
  - Include results with/without bbox regression and SVMs to quantify the performance-cost trade-off more completely (even if the final method favors the fast end-to-end softmax).
  - Clarify whether any hard negative mining was used for background and how many proposals per image were sampled during fine-tuning.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The method description is easy to follow, and the figures aid understanding. The experiments section would benefit from:
- More explicit labeling of the entries in Table 1 (exactly which layers are adapted; whether the background class is included; the value of k for category-specific adaptation).
- A brief, self-contained description of the error taxonomy used in the analysis (Loc, BG, Oth) and the thresholds employed.
- Clearer distinction between the “upper bound” (all 200 classes with boxes) and the LSDA setting, including any differences in training time and components (e.g., absence of SVMs/bbox regression).

Originality
The idea of transferring a classification network to detection via fine-tuning is not new (R-CNN), but LSDA’s framing—learning a category-invariant transformation with a background class and extrapolating category-specific deltas to held-out classes—is novel and practical. To my knowledge, adapting to categories with no detection data in this way was not previously demonstrated at this scale.

Significance
The work addresses an important bottleneck in detection: scarcity of bounding boxes at scale. Demonstrating consistent gains on held-out categories and releasing a 7.6K-category detector are impactful. Even if the core mechanisms are simple, the practical implications for large-vocabulary detection are strong, and the code/models are likely to be used by the community.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Addresses a timely and important problem: scaling detection without exhaustive boxes.
- Conceptually simple, computationally efficient, and practical; training time reduced relative to full R-CNN pipelines.
- Solid empirical gains on a challenging benchmark; informative ablations and error analysis.
- Open-source release and a large-scale 7.6K detector; faster variant broadens applicability.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Missing key baselines (weakly supervised fine-tuning, MIL for held-out classes, simple localization heuristic), which leaves uncertainty about how much the proposed adaptation contributes beyond background modeling and proposal evaluation.
- Category-specific adaptation provides modest gains; the choice of nearest-neighbor averaging in fc8 space is heuristic and could be better justified or compared with alternatives.
- Results rely on AlexNet-era architectures; limited discussion of generalization to other models at the time (though an SPP variant is included).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add the three baselines above to strengthen causal claims about the adaptation’s contribution.
- Expand Table 1 descriptions and provide sensitivity analyses for k and distance metrics.
- Include optional comparisons with bbox regression/SVMs to bound the potential performance gap and clarify the accuracy-speed trade-off.
- Provide more per-class diagnostics (e.g., by object size, similarity clusters) to illustrate where LSDA helps and where it struggles.

Overall recommendation
A solid and timely contribution with clear practical value. While additional baselines would make the case stronger, the paper’s problem framing, method simplicity, empirical gains, and large-scale release argue in favor of acceptance.
