Summary
This paper tackles object detection, specifically the challenge of obtaining bounding boxes at a scale comparable to the availability of category labels for object categorization. The authors sidestep the need for large-scale bounding-box annotations by adapting object classifiers for detection. The algorithm is fairly simple and straightforward, which is not a drawback in itself. The experimental protocol uses 100 categories for training (with both category labels and bounding boxes) and evaluates on 100 held-out categories that have only category labels. Results are reasonably strong: on the 100 held-out categories, the method closes about 35% of the gap between a baseline and an oracle.

Quality and clarity
This is a good-quality, clearly written paper.

Originality
The paper combines two existing adaptation ideas: adapting object classifiers for detection, and adapting from one set of categories to another. To the best of this reviewer’s knowledge, this combination is novel and interesting, though not ground-breaking.

Significance
The main contribution is the novel (but not ground-breaking) approach. From a results standpoint, it is hard to judge long-term significance, and the approach will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods (as is to be expected). The primary concern with the results is that they necessarily sacrifice mAP by relying on fewer bounding boxes, and there is no objective way presented to assess whether this trade-off is worthwhile.

As a side note, it is not clear whether the problem being addressed is an important long-term challenge. While bounding boxes are more “expensive” to obtain than category labels, it is conceivable that increased resources could alleviate this constraint. Not too many years ago, having millions of images labeled with 20,000 object categories seemed almost impossible.

Detailed issues that should be addressed
- Figure 3a: Please clarify which methods do or do not use category labels to assist detection, beyond your method and R-CNN. This is necessary for an apples-to-apples comparison.
- Figure 3b: My assessment of the proposed approach is based on the blue bars. The DNN result is about one-third of the way from the baseline to the oracle. Do the authors agree that this is one of the main results? If so, please highlight this rather than the 78% claim (line 341), which is not quite the right headline for two reasons: (1) performance should be judged only on the left-out categories, and (2) the metric should reflect improvement over baseline, not merely a percentage of oracle performance. An analogous concern applies to the claim of a 50% improvement (line 425).
- Please include a dedicated table or subsection clearly articulating the differences from R-CNN, which appears to be the closest competing alternative. At present, relevant statements are scattered and less prominent than they could be. I suggest “sacrificing” Figure 6, which consists of anecdotal examples, to make room for this comparison.

Minor issues (no need to respond)
- Is the method referred to as “DNN” or “DDA”?
- Typo on line 431 (“classi?ers”).

Overall assessment
This is a reasonably good paper proposing a reasonably novel approach. I believe it deserves to be accepted and will be of interest to the NIPS community. While the results will almost certainly be surpassed soon—given the simplicity of the method (which is acceptable, especially as the first paper to take this approach)—the long-term significance of the work and the approach remains to be seen.
