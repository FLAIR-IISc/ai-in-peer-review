The authors propose a framework to adapt deep neural networks trained from image classification tasks to object detection tasks. Evaluation on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 dataset demonstrates that the proposed method achieves a 50% relative mAP boost over a baseline of just using raw classifier weights on object proposal regions. This paper is clearly presented and generally easy to follow, and the proposed CNN structure is interesting and simple to implement. Overall, the quality of the paper is good.

The main contribution of this work is the adaptation method proposed in this paper. The authors cast the task as a domain adaptation problem, considering the data used to train classifiers (images with category labels) as the source domain, and the data used to train detectors (images with bounding boxes and category labels) as the target domain. They then seek to find a general transformation from the source domain to the target domain, that can be applied to any image classifier to adapt it into an object detector.

The strengths of this paper include the ability to adapt classifiers to detectors without requiring bounding box annotated data for all categories. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the ILSVRC2013 detection dataset, achieving a significant improvement in performance. The proposed method is also able to produce a detection network for all categories of interest, whether or not bounding boxes are available at training time.

However, there are some concerns that the authors could improve. Firstly, the adaptation method proposed in this paper needs better justification. The details of underlying motivations are not presented in a principled fashion. For example, in Section 3.2, it is not clear why the weights for the adapted detection task are a summation of Wj^c and the W{avg}. The physical meaning of this 'offset parameter' is not explained. As a consequence, it is also not clear why the kNN variant is better.

Secondly, in comparing with other methods, the authors used part of the validation set, but not the test set, making the comparison not fair. The authors mentioned in footnote that R-CNN has similar performance on the val2 set, but due to the small size of this set, the performance gain is not statistically justified.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis or experimental results. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and adequately informs the reader. However, the originality of the paper is somewhat limited, as the authors build upon existing work on domain adaptation and deep neural networks.

In terms of significance, the results are important, and other people (practitioners or researchers) are likely to use these ideas or build on them. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research, and it advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel adaptation method for transforming classifiers to detectors.
* The proposed method achieves a significant improvement in performance on the ILSVRC2013 detection dataset.
* The paper is clearly presented and generally easy to follow.
* The proposed CNN structure is interesting and simple to implement.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The adaptation method proposed in this paper needs better justification.
* The comparison with other methods is not fair, as the authors used part of the validation set, but not the test set.
* The originality of the paper is somewhat limited, as the authors build upon existing work on domain adaptation and deep neural networks.
