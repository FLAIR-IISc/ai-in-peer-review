This paper introduces an approach for adapting a Krizhevsky-style CNN trained for image classification (without bounding boxes) into an object detector, using bounding box annotations for only a subset of categories. During training, the method: 1) trains a CNN classifier on images from all classes; 2) fine-tunes the CNN on region proposals for the classes with bounding boxes while introducing a background (negative) class; and 3) incorporates the learned background class into the full model and adjusts selected CNN weights. At test time, the network is applied to multiple region proposals within each test image.

Quality: The paper is interesting and provides reasonably convincing evidence that the method works. While it largely builds on the success of the Krizhevsky et al. architecture rather than introducing a fundamentally new paradigm, the results should be useful to the CV/ML community. The overall strategy is straightforward and sensible. The experiments are encouraging, but several key baselines are missing:
1) Treat entire images as positives, and use images and regions from other classes as negatives; then train and fine-tune all CNN layers.
2) As above, but use a MIL strategy that iteratively selects the highest-scoring positive region per image for set A (instead of using the whole image).
3) Train/fine-tune the CNN for classification only, and at test time run the classifier and output a single bounding box at the image center (e.g., the mean ImageNet bounding box).
It is not clear that the proposed method would outperform these baselines. In particular, it is not obvious that the approach is preferable to baseline 1, and it likely compares unfavorably to baseline 2. Including baseline 3 would also help calibrate task and dataset difficulty.

Clarity: The manuscript is clearly written and well organized. The experimental section could be clearer, especially in explaining exactly what each variant in Table 1 entails (e.g., whether this corresponds to applying Eq. 1 or 2 to a subset of CNN layers, and which layers are adapted).

Originality: The contribution is sufficiently novel for acceptance, though the core idea is relatively simple and tailored to specific CNN architectures.

Significance: The problem is important and the results are valuable. Demonstrating credible detection on a large-scale dataset without bounding box labels is persuasive. That said, the absence of obvious baselines makes it plausible that the gains stem primarily from stronger CNN features rather than from a superior weakly supervised detection training procedure.
