Summary:
This paper proposes a framework for adapting deep neural networks trained for image classification to object detection. The approach adds an adaptation mechanism on top of a classification CNN and achieves competitive performance on the ILSVRC 2013 detection benchmark, ranking 4th. The paper is clearly written, the model is conceptually simple and practical, and the overall quality is good.

Strengths:
- Clear presentation and easy-to-follow methodology.
- A simple, implementable CNN adaptation that performs competitively.
- Strong empirical results on a challenging benchmark, suggesting practical value.

Main concerns:
1) Insufficient motivation and justification for the adaptation rule:
- The core idea in Section 3.2 (Page 5, Line 231) sets the detection weights for class c as a sum of Wj^c and Wavg, described as an “offset.” The rationale for this additive decomposition is not presented in a principled way. What statistical or optimization perspective motivates this form? For example, is Wavg intended as a class-agnostic detection prior and Wj^c as a class-specific deviation? If so, a formalization (e.g., a MAP estimate with a Gaussian prior centered at Wavg, or a multitask/parameter-sharing viewpoint) would clarify why this structure is well founded.
- The “offset parameter” lacks an interpretation tied to detection-specific factors (e.g., background separation, localization sensitivity, bounding-box invariances). Without such grounding, it is difficult to assess when and why this transfer will succeed.
- Relatedly, the kNN variant is reported to perform better, but without an explanation of what neighbors represent, how similarity is defined, and why neighbor-averaging should improve generalization. Please analyze sensitivity to k, the distance metric, and the neighbor pool. Ablations comparing: (i) Wj^c only, (ii) Wavg only, (iii) Wj^c + Wavg (fixed sum), (iv) learned convex combination, and (v) other priors (e.g., class-frequency-weighted averages) would strengthen the case. A comparison to alternative parameter-sharing schemes (e.g., low-rank factorization, L2 regularization toward classification weights, or a multitask loss) would also help.

2) Evaluation protocol and fairness of comparisons:
- Comparisons rely on a subset of the validation set rather than the test set, making cross-method comparisons potentially unfair. The footnote (Line 322) noting that R-CNN has similar performance on val2 is not sufficient; given the small size of val2, differences may not be statistically meaningful.
- To ensure fair comparison, please either report official test-server results for all methods under the same settings, or evaluate all compared methods on the same validation split with matched pipelines (region proposals, NMS, any bounding-box regression, and training data). Reporting confidence intervals (e.g., bootstrap on images) or multiple training seeds would indicate whether observed gains are robust.
- Clarify what data each method used for training and tuning. If hyperparameters were selected on val2, discuss overfitting risk and steps taken to mitigate it.

Additional comments and suggestions:
- Training details: Specify which layers are fine-tuned versus frozen, the learning schedule, regularization, data augmentation, and whether bounding-box regression is used. Runtime and memory costs at training and inference would be useful for practitioners.
- Analysis: Provide per-class APs, qualitative detections, and error analyses (e.g., localization vs. classification errors) to illustrate where adaptation helps or fails. A study across object sizes would also be informative.
- Generalization: If feasible, include results on another dataset (e.g., PASCAL VOC) or a transfer setting to demonstrate that the adaptation generalizes beyond ILSVRC.
- Clarify how Wavg and kNN are computed: Are neighbors chosen in parameter space, feature space, or semantic space (e.g., WordNet)? Do rare classes benefit disproportionately? What happens if visually dissimilar but semantically related categories are included?
- Positioning: More explicitly relate the approach to prior transfer learning and multitask learning literature, and to contemporary detection pipelines (e.g., R-CNN), highlighting what is novel in the adaptation mechanism versus architectural or training differences.

Overall assessment:
The paper presents a practical and competitive method for adapting classification CNNs to detection, and is clearly written. However, the central adaptation rule needs a stronger, principled justification and deeper empirical analysis (ablations and interpretations), and the evaluation should be put on a fair, statistically sound footing. With these issues addressed, the contribution would be significantly strengthened.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the evaluation protocol and providing a more principled motivation and empirical analysis of the adaptation mechanism (including the kNN variant).
