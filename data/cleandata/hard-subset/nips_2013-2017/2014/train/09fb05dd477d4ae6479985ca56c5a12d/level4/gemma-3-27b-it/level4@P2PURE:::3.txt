This paper introduces a method for adapting a classification CNN, pre-trained in the style of Krizhevsky et al., to the task of object detection using a limited number of bounding box annotations. The approach involves three training stages: first, a CNN is trained for classification on all available classes; second, a CNN is trained on image regions from classes with bounding box labels, incorporating a negative background region class; and third, the background class is integrated into the full CNN model with some weight adaptation. During testing, the CNN is applied to multiple image regions within a test image.

Quality: This is a compelling and interesting paper that demonstrates reasonable results. While these results largely benefit from the foundational success of the Krizhevsky et al. model, rather than representing a radical advance, they will be valuable to the computer vision and machine learning communities. The overall approach is logically sound and relatively straightforward. The experiments are promising, but lack comparison to several important baselines: 

1) Training and fine-tuning all CNN layers using full images as positive examples and image regions from other classes as negative examples.
2) Implementing the same training as above, but employing a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, iteratively selecting the highest-scoring positive region for each image.
3) Training and fine-tuning the CNN for classification, then at test time predicting a single bounding box (e.g., the mean bounding box for ImageNet) at the center of the image. 

It is unclear whether the proposed method would outperform these baselines. The proposed approach does not demonstrably improve upon baseline 1, and likely performs worse than baseline 2. Including the third baseline is crucial to establish the inherent difficulty of the problem and the dataset.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The clarity of the experiments section could be enhanced by providing more detailed explanations of the methods presented in Table 1—specifically, clarifying whether these methods refer to applying Equation 1 or 2 to a subset of CNN layers. 

Originality: The paper presents a sufficient degree of novelty to warrant acceptance, though the underlying approach is simple and specific to certain CNN architectures.

Significance: The problem addressed and the results obtained are significant and valuable. The paper tackles a relevant problem, and achieving reasonable detection results on a large-scale dataset without extensive bounding box labels is noteworthy. However, the absence of the aforementioned baselines is a concern, and it is possible that the observed results are primarily attributable to the improved quality of CNN features, rather than to a superior training methodology for object detectors with weak supervision.
