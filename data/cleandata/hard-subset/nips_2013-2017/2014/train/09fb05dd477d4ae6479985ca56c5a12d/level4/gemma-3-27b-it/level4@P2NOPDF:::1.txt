This paper addresses the problem of object detection, specifically tackling the disparity between the availability of bounding box annotations and category labels. The authors propose a novel approach that adapts object classifiers to perform detection, circumventing the need for extensive bounding box annotations. The algorithm’s simplicity is a strength. The experimental setup involves training on 100 categories with both bounding boxes and labels, and evaluating on 100 held-out categories for which only category labels are available. The results demonstrate a promising performance, bridging approximately 35% of the gap between an ideal “oracle” detector and a baseline system on the held-out categories.

**Quality and Clarity:** The paper is well-written and presents the work in a clear and accessible manner.

**Originality:** The work combines established concepts – adaptation of object classifiers for detection and transfer learning across category sets – in a novel way. While not a fundamentally groundbreaking contribution, it represents an interesting and potentially valuable direction for research.

**Significance:** The primary contribution of this work lies in its unique approach to object detection with limited bounding box annotations. Assessing the long-term significance is challenging, as more sophisticated methods are likely to emerge. However, this work provides a strong baseline for future research in this area. A key consideration is the trade-off between performance (mAP) and the reduced need for bounding box annotations; it’s currently unclear whether this trade-off is advantageous. Furthermore, the fundamental importance of the problem itself is debatable, as advancements in data annotation techniques may alleviate the scarcity of bounding box data.

**Detailed Issues to Address:**

*   **Figure 3a Clarification:** The authors should explicitly state which methods utilize category labels for detection, beyond their proposed approach and R-CNN. This will facilitate a clearer, more direct comparison between methods.
*   **Result Emphasis:** The reviewer believes the most significant result is the performance of the proposed method, represented by the blue bars in Figure 3b – achieving approximately one-third of the performance gap between the baseline and oracle. The authors should highlight this result more prominently than the 78% figure (line 341), which is somewhat misleading for two reasons: first, it isn't solely based on the held-out categories, and second, it represents a percentage of oracle performance without accounting for baseline performance. A similar concern arises with the 50% improvement claim (line 425).
*   **R-CNN Comparison:** The authors should dedicate a table or a focused subsection to a thorough comparison with R-CNN, the closest competing method. Currently, these comparisons are dispersed throughout the paper and lack prominence. Figure 6, presenting anecdotal examples, could be removed to make space for this more substantial comparison.

**Minor Issues (No Response Required):**

*   The terminology is inconsistent: is the method referred to as “DNN” or “DDA”?
*   There’s a typo on line 431 (“classi?ers”).

Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a reasonably novel and promising approach to object detection. I recommend acceptance for presentation at NIPS. While the presented results are likely to be improved upon in the future, given the simplicity of the method (understandable, as a first attempt at this approach), the long-term impact of this work and its underlying concepts remains an open and worthwhile question.
