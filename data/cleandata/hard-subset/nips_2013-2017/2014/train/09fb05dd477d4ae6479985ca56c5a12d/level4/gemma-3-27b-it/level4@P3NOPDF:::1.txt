This paper addresses the challenging problem of object detection, specifically the disparity between the availability of bounding box annotations and the abundance of category labels. The authors propose a novel approach that adapts object classifiers to perform detection, effectively circumventing the need for extensive bounding box annotations. The algorithm is commendably straightforward, a virtue in itself. The experimental setup involves training on 100 categories with both labels and bounding boxes, and evaluating on 100 held-out categories for which only category labels are available. The results are encouraging, achieving approximately 35% of the performance gap between an oracle and a baseline on these unseen categories.

**Quality and Clarity:** This is a well-written and clearly presented paper. The ideas are articulated effectively, and the experimental details are generally easy to follow.

**Originality:** The paper uniquely combines existing concepts of adaptation – adapting classifiers for detection and transferring knowledge across category sets. To the best of my knowledge, this specific combination represents a novel and interesting contribution, although not a radical departure from existing work.

**Significance:** The primary contribution lies in this novel approach. While assessing long-term significance is difficult given the rapid pace of progress in this field (and the likely emergence of more sophisticated methods), this work provides a valuable baseline and direction for future research. The core issue the paper tackles – the cost of bounding box annotation – is a practical one. However, the paper rightly acknowledges the possibility that increasing resources dedicated to annotation could alleviate this problem. It remains to be seen whether the proposed trade-off between performance (mAP) and annotation effort will prove consistently beneficial. The inherent reduction in bounding box proposals leads to performance sacrifice, and a clear justification for this trade-off is currently lacking.

**Detailed Issues to Address:**

*   **Figure 3a Clarification:** Please clarify in Figure 3a which methods utilize category labels to aid detection, beyond your approach and R-CNN. Explicitly stating this will ensure a fair “apples-to-apples” comparison.
*   **Result Highlighting:** My evaluation of the method’s effectiveness centers on the blue bars in Figure 3b, which demonstrate performance approximately one-third of the way between the baseline and oracle. The authors should acknowledge this as a key result and highlight it more prominently than the 78% figure reported on line 341. This figure is problematic for two reasons: first, it doesn’t isolate the performance on the left-out categories, and second, it presents performance as a percentage of the oracle, without considering the baseline performance. A similar issue arises with the claimed 50% improvement on line 425; this should also be contextualized against the baseline.
*   **Comparison to R-CNN:** The paper would benefit significantly from a dedicated table or subsection that explicitly outlines the differences between the proposed method and R-CNN, which appears to be the most closely related work. Currently, these distinctions are scattered throughout the text and lack sufficient emphasis. I suggest removing Figure 6, which presents anecdotal examples, to make space for this more rigorous comparison.

**Overall:**

This is a solid paper presenting a reasonably novel approach to object detection. I recommend acceptance for presentation at NIPS. While the results are likely to be surpassed in the future, given the simplicity of the method (and the fact that this appears to be the first work to explore this specific approach), the long-term impact of the paper and the proposed technique remains a worthwhile research direction. The paper’s contribution lies in opening up this new avenue for investigation and providing a baseline for comparison.
