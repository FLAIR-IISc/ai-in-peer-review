This paper presents a framework for adapting deep neural networks trained for image classification to object detection. On the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection benchmark, the method ranks 4th. The paper is clearly written, the proposed CNN architecture is straightforward to implement, and the overall quality is good. However, I have two main concerns.

First, the central adaptation mechanism needs a more principled justification. The motivation and derivation are not clearly articulated. For example, in Section 3.2 (Page 5, Line 231), it is unclear why the detection weights are modeled as the sum of W_j^c and W_avg, and what the “offset” parameter represents intuitively or theoretically. Without a clear rationale, it is also hard to understand why the kNN variant should perform better. Providing a principled derivation or interpretation (e.g., from transfer learning, regularization, or metric-learning perspectives), ablations isolating the effect of the offset, and analyses of how k and neighbor selection impact performance would substantially strengthen the contribution.

Second, the experimental comparisons are not strictly fair. The paper compares against other methods using a subset of the validation set rather than the official test set. Although the footnote (Line 322) notes that R-CNN achieves similar results on val2, the small size of this split limits statistical power, so the reported gains may not be significant. For a fair comparison, results should be reported on the official test set or on exactly the same splits as baselines, accompanied by confidence intervals or statistical tests to support claims of improvement.

In summary, the paper proposes a practical and effective approach to adapt classification CNNs for detection, achieving performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods. The clarity of exposition and ease of implementation are strengths. The main areas for improvement are: (1) a stronger, more principled explanation of the adaptation rule (including the role and interpretation of the offset W_avg and why the kNN variant helps), and (2) fair and statistically sound comparisons on standardized evaluation splits. Addressing these points would significantly enhance the paper’s impact and credibility.
