This paper tackles the significant challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, for which obtaining bounding box annotations is prohibitively expensive. The authors propose an algorithm, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), which frames this problem as a domain adaptation task. The core idea is to learn a generic transformation from an image classifier to an object detector using a set of "source" categories that have both image-level and bounding box labels. This learned transformation is then applied to adapt classifiers for "target" categories that only have image-level labels, effectively turning them into detectors without requiring any bounding box data. This work builds upon recent successes of CNNs in both classification (Krizhevsky et al., 2012) and detection (R-CNN, Girshick et al., 2014). However, unlike R-CNN which requires fine-tuning with bounding boxes for every category, LSDA's main contribution is its ability to generalize the adaptation process to novel classes.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a highly practical and important problem. As classification datasets grow to tens of thousands of categories, the bottleneck for detection is annotation. The proposed method offers a pragmatic and novel solution to bridge this gap. The formulation of "learning to adapt" a classifier to a detector is an original and valuable contribution.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated. The experiments, particularly the ablation study on held-out categories, are thorough and convincingly demonstrate the contribution of each component of the LSDA framework. The quantitative results show a significant relative mAP improvement (+50%) over a strong baseline, validating the approach. The error analysis in Section 4.3 provides excellent insight, showing that the adaptation primarily helps by reducing localization errors and confusion with background—key challenges that differentiate detection from classification.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The methodology is explained logically, and the figures (especially Figure 2) effectively illustrate the pipeline. The authors also plan to release code and pre-trained models, which is commendable and will benefit the community.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:**

*   **Justification for Category-Specific Adaptation:** While the category-invariant adaptation is intuitive and empirically well-supported, the motivation for the category-specific adaptation in Section 3.2 could be strengthened. The method assumes that the transformation from a classification weight vector to a detection weight vector (the δ parameter) is similar for semantically or featurally similar classes. This is plausible, but the paper does not provide a deep justification for why a simple average of the nearest neighbors' delta vectors is the optimal way to model this transfer. A more principled explanation of what this "offset" vector represents and why this specific transfer mechanism is chosen would make the contribution more solid.
*   **Experimental Comparison and Scope:** The evaluation is performed on a split of the ILSVRC2013 validation set. While this is a standard practice for ablation studies, it makes direct comparison to state-of-the-art methods that report on the official test set difficult. The paper establishes a performance upper bound (training with full supervision on all 200 classes) but notes this is still lower than the full R-CNN pipeline, as it omits SVM fine-tuning and bounding box regression for speed and simplicity. While this is a reasonable trade-off, a more detailed discussion of where LSDA stands in relation to other weakly-supervised or transfer-learning approaches for detection (e.g., MIL-based methods, which are mentioned in related work) would help to better position the work's contribution.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and effective solution to a significant problem in computer vision. The strengths in originality, experimental validation, and clarity are substantial. The primary weaknesses relate to the theoretical justification for one of the model components and the scope of the experimental comparison, but these are addressable and do not detract from the overall contribution. The work is a solid step towards enabling large-scale object detection.
