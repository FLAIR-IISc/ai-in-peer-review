Summary
This paper proposes LSDA, a method to adapt CNNs trained for image classification into object detectors when bounding box annotations are available for only a subset of categories. The approach treats classification-to-detection as a domain adaptation problem, learning (1) a background class and a category-invariant transformation via fine-tuning on the categories with detection labels, and (2) a category-specific adjustment by adding an offset to the classification weights for categories without detection labels, estimated from nearest labeled neighbors. Experiments on ILSVRC-2013 show substantial gains over using classification weights directly on region proposals; the paper reports competitive results (including a 4th-place ranking) and demonstrates a 7.6K-category detector with a faster variant. The paper is clearly written and the architecture is simple to implement.

Strengths
- Practical and scalable approach to reduce dependence on bounding box annotations, with potential impact for large vocabularies.
- Clear description of training pipeline and ablation indicating the importance of category-invariant adaptation and background modeling.
- Empirical improvements on held-out categories and release of models/code increase reproducibility and utility.
- The method integrates cleanly with existing CNN detection pipelines (e.g., R-CNN style region proposals).

Weaknesses and concerns
1) Motivation and justification of the adaptation rule. The key category-specific step proposes W^d = W^c + δ, where δ for unlabeled categories is approximated by averaging “deltas” from nearest neighbors (Section 3.2). The rationale for this additive model is not presented in a principled way. It is unclear what the “offset” represents (e.g., a first-order approximation to fine-tuning, a linearization of loss geometry, or an empirical bias correction), why addition (as opposed to other mappings) is appropriate, or why nearest neighbors in the classification weight space should best predict detection-space shifts. The kNN variant improves results, but the underlying reason and sensitivity to k are not well analyzed.

2) Fairness and rigor of comparisons. Most comparisons are made on a split of the validation set (val2) rather than the official test set. This makes cross-paper comparisons difficult. Although a footnote notes that R-CNN has similar performance on val2, the small size of the split weakens statistical conclusions; the reported gains are not clearly justified as statistically significant against strong baselines on the official test server.

Quality
- The method is technically sound as an empirical adaptation strategy and the ablations support the main claims about background modeling and feature-level fine-tuning. However, the category-specific additive rule lacks theoretical grounding or deeper analysis (e.g., alternative mappings, learned regressors from W^c to W^d, or meta-learning approaches).
- Experimental setup is described, but the reliance on validation splits for comparisons limits the strength of the claims.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and easy to follow. Section 3.2 would benefit from clearer notation and intuition for the offset δ, an explanation of the choice of distance metric for nearest neighbors, and a discussion of what the additive offset captures in feature space.

Originality
- Adapting from classification to detection with limited bounding box data and transferring to held-out categories is novel relative to prior work requiring per-class detection labels (e.g., R-CNN). The combination of background learning, category-invariant fine-tuning, and kNN-based category-specific adjustment is a useful contribution.

Significance
- High practical significance: the approach reduces annotation cost and enables scaling to thousands of categories. The demonstrated 7.6K detector and speed-up variant underline applicability. The lack of test-set comparisons somewhat tempers the strength of the empirical claims.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a principled justification or empirical probe for the additive model: e.g., view fine-tuning as a local linear transformation around W^c; compare addition vs. learned linear/nonlinear mappings from W^c to W^d; visualize δ distributions; test cosine vs. Euclidean neighbor definitions; report sensitivity to k and to the choice of neighbor pool.
- Strengthen evaluation on the official test set, or provide multiple random validation splits with confidence intervals and significance tests against strong baselines (e.g., R-CNN fine-tuned per class).
- Include category-level analyses highlighting where kNN transfer helps or fails (e.g., confusion among visually similar classes), and quantify error-type reductions with standard diagnostics.
- Discuss trade-offs with omitted components (SVMs, bbox regression) and whether lightweight regression can be integrated without losing adaptability.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, scalable solution with clear empirical gains on held-out categories.
- Simple, reproducible method with released code and large-scale demonstration.
- Addresses an important problem of reducing bounding box annotation needs.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficiently justified category-specific adaptation rule; limited analysis of why it works.
- Comparisons rely on validation splits rather than the official test set, weakening claims of state-of-the-art performance.
- Some design choices (distance metric, k, additive assumption) appear ad hoc without deeper exploration.
