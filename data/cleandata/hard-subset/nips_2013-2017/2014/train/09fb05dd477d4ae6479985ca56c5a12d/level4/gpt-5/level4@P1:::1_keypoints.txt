- Tackles scaling object detection when bounding-box annotations are scarce by adapting classifiers into detectors (LSDA) using a subset of boxed categories and transferring the transformation to categories with only image labels.  
- Frames the problem as domain adaptation: classification data = source, detection data = target; learns background class and fine-tunes shared layers for category-invariant shift.  
- Approximates category-specific adaptation for unlabeled categories by averaging fc8 shifts from nearest neighbors among boxed categories.  
- Evaluated on ILSVRC-2013: train detection on 100 categories, test on 100 held-out categories; shows sizable gains over using raw classifier weights and scales to a 7.6K-category detector with a faster SPP-based variant.  
- Builds on R-CNN fine-tuning but differs by requiring detection labels only for a subset and transferring to the remainder; contrasts with weakly supervised MIL and standard domain adaptation approaches.  
- Paper is technically sound and clearly written; ablation study effectively shows contributions of background modeling, shared-layer fine-tuning, and category-specific output adaptation.  
- Experimental protocol (100 with boxes vs. 100 held-out) is reasonable and error analysis (localization, background confusion, other) is helpful.  
- Novelty lies in combining classifier→detector adaptation with cross-category transfer; a practical and meaningful extension rather than a radical departure.  
- Method is simple and scalable, reducing reliance on bounding boxes; empirically fills ~35% of the baseline→oracle gap on held-out categories.  
- Practical significance depends on future annotation practices; unclear if reduced-box regimes will remain central as tools/resources improve.  
- Pros: clear problem statement, simple effective method, strong ablation indicating category-invariant adaptations drive most gains, 7.6K demonstration, code/models released.  
- Cons: inevitable mAP loss vs. full supervision without framework to judge acceptability; some percentage-claim framing is misleading; differences from R-CNN not summarized concisely.  
- Detailed requests: clarify which methods use category labels in Figure 3a; emphasize the held-out-category baseline→oracle gap filling (~1/3) rather than “78%” or other misleading percentages; add a table explicitly contrasting LSDA and R-CNN (training/data/runtime/bbox regression/SVM usage).  
- Minor issues: inconsistent naming in figures/text (“DNN” or “DDA” vs. LSDA) and a typo at line 431.  
- Recommendation: solid, timely, and interesting paper — recommend acceptance.
