Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles the challenge of scaling object detection to thousands of categories without requiring bounding boxes for all classes. The authors propose LSDA, a method that learns the transformation from image classification to object detection using a subset of categories with bounding box annotations and transfers this transformation to categories with only image-level labels. Concretely, they:

- Fine-tune a CNN originally trained for classification to perform detection on a subset of categories, thereby learning background modeling and a category-invariant feature transformation (layers 1–7).
- Learn category-specific deltas for classes with detection data and transfer these to held-out classes via nearest-neighbor averaging in the classification output-layer parameter space (fc8).
- Use the adapted network to score region proposals with K class scores plus a background score; they omit per-class SVMs and bounding-box regression for simplicity and training speed.

They evaluate on ILSVRC-2013 detection by training on 100 categories with boxes and testing on the 100 held-out classes with only image-level labels. LSDA provides a sizable improvement over using raw classification models on region proposals and closes a significant fraction of the gap to an oracle detector trained with boxes for all categories. The authors also build a 7.6K-category detector by adapting ImageNet leaf-node classifiers and provide a faster variant using SPP and fast proposals.

The approach builds on R-CNN (which requires detection labels for all categories), weakly supervised detection via MIL, and classic domain adaptation methods, but differs by adapting across tasks (classification→detection) and across categories without detection labels. To my knowledge, this transfer to completely held-out categories within a deep architecture is novel for detection at this scale.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The method is straightforward and well-motivated. The category-invariant adaptation via fine-tuning with a background class matches known benefits in R-CNN, and the category-specific adaptation via nearest neighbors in fc8 space is a reasonable heuristic.
- Empirical validation: The experimental protocol is clear and reasonably fair. The ablations are informative: background modeling and feature adaptation contribute most, while category-specific deltas add a smaller but statistically significant gain. The analysis of error types (localization, background, other) is helpful and consistent with the intended adaptation.
- Gaps and clarifications:
  - Comparisons: Please clarify in the figure comparing methods which baselines do or do not use image-level labels and background modeling, to ensure apples-to-apples comparisons with LSDA and R-CNN.
  - Main result framing: The improvement on held-out categories should be reported relative to both the baseline and the oracle trained with full boxes, ideally quantifying how much of the baseline–oracle gap is closed on the held-out classes. Presenting only percentages of the oracle can be misleading.
  - Nearest-neighbor transfer: Provide sensitivity to the choice of k and to the similarity metric (e.g., fc8 parameter distance vs alternatives such as semantic/WordNet or confusion-based neighbors). A brief analysis would strengthen the category-specific transfer claim.
  - Design choices: Since the pipeline skips per-class SVMs and bounding-box regression, it would be helpful to report the performance and compute trade-offs if these steps were added back in for the classes with boxes, and discuss whether they can be integrated without breaking adaptation for held-out categories.
  - Large-scale results: The 7.6K model is impressive, but only qualitative examples are shown. Any quantitative proxy (e.g., on a subset with boxes, or cross-dataset tests) would help establish practical impact at scale.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The adaptation insights and training procedure are explained concisely. A few suggestions to improve clarity:
- Consolidate and highlight the differences from R-CNN in a dedicated subsection or table (data requirements, training time, background modeling, use of SVMs/bbox regression, performance trade-offs).
- Make the naming consistent (use “LSDA” throughout; avoid DNN/DDA inconsistencies).
- Minor typos (e.g., “classi?ers”) should be corrected.
- Consider replacing or moving the qualitative montage (e.g., Figure 6) to supplementary and using the space for the R-CNN comparison table or the sensitivity analyses above.

Originality
The combination of (i) learning a generic classifier-to-detector transformation within a deep network and (ii) transferring it to categories with no bounding boxes is, to my knowledge, new at the time and distinct from both R-CNN and prior domain adaptation/MIL approaches. The approach is simple, but the insight that much of the classification→detection gap can be captured by a category-invariant transformation plus lightweight category-specific adjustment is interesting.

Significance
This work addresses a central bottleneck in scaling detection: bounding box annotation cost. The reported gains on held-out categories, the demonstration on 7.6K categories, and the released models/software suggest practical utility. The long-term impact will depend on how much of the performance gap to fully supervised detectors can be closed with adaptation. It would further strengthen the paper to contextualize the trade-off (mAP vs annotation effort) quantitatively, even approximately.

Pros
- Clear, simple method with a compelling motivation.
- Strong ablation showing where the gains come from; statistically significant improvements on held-out categories.
- First large-scale demonstration of adapting classifiers to detectors for unseen categories.
- Useful analysis of error types; code and models released; faster variant provided.

Cons
- Absolute mAP on held-out classes remains well below the fully supervised oracle.
- Category-specific transfer is heuristic; limited analysis of alternative similarity measures and sensitivity.
- Large-scale (7.6K) results are qualitative; more quantitative evidence would help.
- Differences from R-CNN could be presented more systematically; some comparisons need clearer baselining.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please clarify which baselines in the comparison figure(s) use image-level labels, background modeling, and the same proposal method, to ensure fair comparisons.
- Reframe the headline results on held-out categories as “fraction of the baseline–oracle gap closed,” and report relative gains over the baseline (on held-out classes only).
- Add a concise table contrasting LSDA and R-CNN (data requirements, training time, components, and performance).
- Report sensitivity of the category-specific transfer to k and to the neighbor definition; consider semantic neighbors (e.g., WordNet) as a comparison.
- If possible, provide a small-scale quantitative check for the 7.6K model (e.g., on a subset with boxes) and/or report calibration/calibration drift across many classes.
- Unify the method name as “LSDA” and fix minor typos.

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a timely and well-executed approach to adapting classifiers into detectors with limited bounding box supervision, demonstrates clear improvements on held-out categories, and provides a credible path toward large-scale detection. While not ground-breaking in technique, the idea is novel in this context, the empirical study is solid, and the potential impact is high.
