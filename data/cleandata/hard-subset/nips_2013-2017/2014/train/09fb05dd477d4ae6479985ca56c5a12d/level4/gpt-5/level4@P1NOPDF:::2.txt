This paper proposes a framework to adapt deep neural networks trained for image classification to object detection. On the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 dataset, the method ranks fourth in the detection challenge. The paper is clearly written and generally easy to follow, and the proposed CNN architecture is interesting and straightforward to implement. Overall, the quality is good. However, I have two main concerns.

First, the central contribution—the adaptation method—needs stronger justification. The underlying motivation is not presented in a principled way. For example, in Section 3.2 (page 5, line 231), it is unclear why the weights for the adapted detection task are defined as the sum of W_j^c and W_avg. The intended meaning of this “offset parameter” is not explained, nor is there an intuition for why this decomposition should be effective. Consequently, it is also unclear why the kNN variant would perform better. A clearer rationale and more thorough analysis of this design choice are needed.

Second, the comparisons with other methods rely on a subset of the validation set rather than the test set, which undermines the fairness of the comparison. The authors note in a footnote (line 322) that R-CNN achieves similar performance on the val2 set, but given the small size of this subset, the reported gains are not statistically convincing. For a fair and robust comparison, results should be reported on the standard test set or accompanied by appropriate statistical analysis to substantiate the claims.

In summary, the paper presents a practical framework for adapting classification networks to detection and achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods. However, the comparisons are made under different evaluation settings, and the core adaptation mechanism requires a more principled explanation and justification.
