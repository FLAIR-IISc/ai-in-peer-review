This paper presents a methodology for adapting a convolutional neural network (CNN), pre-trained for image classification, to the task of object detection. The proposed technique is designed for a scenario where only a small subset of object classes has bounding box annotations. The training process consists of three main stages: 1) A CNN is trained for classification on the full dataset of images. 2) A separate CNN is trained on cropped image regions from the classes with bounding box labels, incorporating an additional class for negative background regions. 3) The background class and its corresponding weights are integrated into the full CNN, and select layers are fine-tuned. During inference, the adapted network is applied to multiple regions of a test image to perform detection.

**Quality**
This is an interesting and valuable paper that achieves compelling results. The success of the method is largely attributable to the powerful features of the underlying Krizhevsky et al. network, rather than a radically new detection paradigm. Nonetheless, the straightforward and logical approach will be of interest to researchers in computer vision and machine learning.

The experiments are promising, but the evaluation is weakened by the absence of several important baseline comparisons. The inclusion of the following baselines would significantly strengthen the paper's claims:

1.  A direct fine-tuning approach: Use full images of the target classes as positive examples and images from other classes as negative examples. Fine-tune all layers of the CNN for this classification task and evaluate its localization performance.
2.  A Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach: Similar to the first baseline, but framing the problem under MIL. During training, iteratively identify and train on the highest-scoring region within each positive image, which is a more natural fit for weakly supervised detection.
3.  A simple localization baseline: After fine-tuning the CNN for classification, simply predict a single, fixed bounding box at the center of the test image (e.g., the mean bounding box size and location from the training data).

Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed method. It is not clear that the proposed multi-stage approach is superior to the more direct fine-tuning strategy of Baseline 1, and it is plausible that the MIL-based approach of Baseline 2 would be conceptually sounder and potentially more effective. Furthermore, Baseline 3 is crucial for contextualizing the results and demonstrating the inherent difficulty of the task and dataset.

**Clarity**
The paper is generally clear and well-written. However, the experimental section could be improved. Specifically, the descriptions for the methods evaluated in Table 1 are ambiguous. It would be beneficial to elaborate on exactly which experimental configuration each entry corresponds to (e.g., which equations were applied, which subset of CNN layers were fine-tuned).

**Originality**
The work possesses sufficient novelty for acceptance. While the core approach is straightforward and tailored to a specific class of CNN architectures, its application to weakly supervised detection is a valuable contribution.

**Significance**
The paper addresses the significant and relevant problem of object detection with limited supervision. The ability to achieve reasonable detection performance on a large-scale dataset without exhaustive bounding box annotations is compelling. However, the significance of the methodological contribution is tempered by the missing baselines. It is difficult to determine whether the strong results are due to a genuinely effective training strategy for weak supervision or if they are primarily capitalizing on the exceptional quality of pre-trained CNN features. A more thorough experimental comparison is needed to disentangle these factors and solidify the paper's contribution.
