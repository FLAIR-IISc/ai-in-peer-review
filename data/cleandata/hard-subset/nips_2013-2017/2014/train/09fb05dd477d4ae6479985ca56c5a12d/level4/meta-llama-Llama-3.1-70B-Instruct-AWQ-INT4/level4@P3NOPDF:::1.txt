This paper addresses the challenge of object detection, particularly in obtaining bounding boxes on a scale similar to the availability of category labels for object categorization. The authors propose a novel approach by adapting object classifiers for the detection task, effectively sidestepping this challenge. Their algorithm is relatively simple and straightforward, which is not inherently a drawback.

The experimental protocol employs 100 categories for training, utilizing both category labels and bounding boxes, and tests on 100 left-out categories with only category labels. The results obtained are reasonably good, bridging approximately 35% of the gap between an oracle and a baseline on the 100 left-out categories.

**Quality and Clarity:** This is a well-written paper of good quality. The authors present their ideas clearly, making it easy to follow their argument.

**Originality:** The paper combines two existing ideas related to adaptation: adapting object classifiers for detection and adapting from one set of categories to another. To the best of this reviewer's knowledge, this combination is novel and interesting, although not groundbreaking.

**Significance:** The main contribution of this paper is its novel approach. However, judging the long-term significance based on the results is challenging, as they will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods in the future. The primary issue with the results is the inevitable sacrifice of performance (mAP) due to the use of fewer bounding boxes, and there is no objective way to evaluate whether this trade-off is worthwhile.

It is also worth noting that the importance of the challenge being addressed is debatable. While bounding boxes are indeed more expensive to obtain than category labels, it is unclear whether this issue cannot be resolved by allocating more resources to the problem.

**Detailed Issues to be Addressed:**

1. In Figure 3a, the authors should clearly state which methods, aside from their approach and R-CNN, utilize category labels to aid in detection. This clarification is necessary for a fair comparison.
2. The proposed approach's effectiveness is primarily demonstrated by the blue bars in Figure 3b. The DNN method is approximately one-third of the way between the baseline and oracle. The authors should acknowledge this as one of the main results and highlight it, rather than the 78% mentioned on line 341. The latter is not the correct number for two reasons: it should only be based on the left-out categories, and it should not simply be a percentage of oracle performance (it should take the baseline into account). A similar issue exists with the claim of 50% improvement on line 425.
3. The authors should devote a table or subsection to clearly outlining the differences between their approach and R-CNN, which appears to be the closest competing alternative. Currently, these statements are scattered or not as prominent as they could be. Consider replacing Figure 6, which presents anecdotal examples, with this information.

**Minor Issues (No Response Required):**

* The proposed method is referred to as both "DNN" and "DDA."
* There is a typo on line 431 ("classi?ers").

Overall, this is a reasonably good paper proposing a novel approach. While the results will likely be surpassed soon, the simplicity of the method (being the first to take this approach) and the long-term significance of the paper and its approach remain to be seen. I believe the paper deserves to be accepted and presented to the NIPS community.
