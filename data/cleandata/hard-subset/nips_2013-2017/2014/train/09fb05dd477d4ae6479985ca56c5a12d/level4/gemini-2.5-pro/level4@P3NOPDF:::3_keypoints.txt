- Proposes a three-stage method to adapt a CNN trained for classification to object detection when most classes have only image-level labels and a few have bounding boxes.  
- Tackles an important, practical weakly-supervised detection problem where exhaustive box annotations are expensive.  
- Demonstrates compelling detection results on a large-scale dataset using limited supervision.  
- Uses a simple, logical approach that leverages a large pre-trained classification model for localization.  
- Main weakness: lacks several crucial baseline comparisons, making it unclear whether improvements come from the training scheme or strong CNN features.  
- Baseline needed 1: simpler weakly-supervised approach—fine-tune all CNN layers on full images as positives and other images/regions as negatives.  
- Baseline needed 2: standard Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) that iteratively picks the highest-scoring region per positive image.  
- Baseline needed 3: trivial localization baseline—predict a fixed central bounding box (mean size/location) to provide a lower bound.  
- Reviewer doubts the proposed method clearly outperforms the simpler and MIL baselines without these comparisons.  
- Clarity issue: experimental descriptions (e.g., what “applying Eq. 1 or 2 to a subset of CNN layers” means) are ambiguous and hurt reproducibility.  
- Originality/significance: sufficiently novel for acceptance but largely a straightforward adaptation of existing CNN ideas; the problem and results are valuable.  
- Conclusion: promising and logical method, but its impact is limited until the suggested baselines clarify whether gains are due to the method or the underlying CNN features.
