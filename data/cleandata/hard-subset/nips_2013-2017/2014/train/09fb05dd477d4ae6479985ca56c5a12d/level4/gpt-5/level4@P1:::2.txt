Summary
This paper proposes LSDA, a method to adapt a CNN trained for image classification into an object detector, even when bounding-box annotations are available for only a subset of categories. The key idea is to learn a generic transformation from classification to detection using categories with detection data (including a learned background class and feature-level adaptation), and then transfer this transformation to categories without detection labels via a category-specific output-layer adjustment. The approach achieves large-scale detection (7.6K categories) by combining classification-only categories with a detection-tuned network. The work builds on R-CNN, which fine-tunes a classification network for detection but requires bounding boxes for all categories, and relates to weakly supervised detection via MIL, though LSDA addresses a different setting.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: bounding-box labels are scarce compared to image-level labels.
- Method is conceptually simple and practical: fine-tune shared layers for detection, learn a background class, and adapt category-specific outputs.
- Strong empirical signal on held-out categories: a reported 50% relative mAP gain over using classification weights directly on region proposals, with an ablation study and error analysis showing improved localization and reduced background confusion.
- Scales to thousands of categories; the authors report competitive performance (4th) in the ILSVRC-2013 detection challenge and demonstrate a fast variant.
- Implementation is straightforward and reproducible (Caffe models provided).

Weaknesses and points for improvement
1) Justification of the adaptation model:
- The category-specific adaptation in Section 3.2 sets the detection weights as W^d = W^c + δ, where δ for categories without detection data is approximated by averaging deltas from k nearest neighbors in the classification weight space. The paper does not provide a principled motivation for the additive form, the choice of similarity metric (l2 on normalized fc8 weights), or why nearest-neighbor averaging is expected to approximate the true δ. The “offset” lacks an interpretation that connects to the task shift (e.g., background rejection, localization sensitivity, part-vs-whole focus).
- The empirical advantage of kNN over a global average is reported, but the rationale for why local neighborhoods in classification-parameter space should correspond to similar detection deltas is not established or analyzed (e.g., sensitivity to k, metric choice, and nearest-neighbor stability).
- Suggested improvements: provide a more principled derivation or empirical analysis of the additive model (e.g., visualize δ distributions; test alternative transforms such as a learned linear mapping from W^c to W^d; analyze alignment between classification similarity and detection-delta similarity; ablate distance metrics and k; evaluate whether neighbors within the same semantic subtree perform better).

2) Fairness and statistical validity of comparisons:
- Comparisons are reported on a split of the validation set (val2), not the test set, while many prior works report on the official test server. Although the paper notes in a footnote that R-CNN performance on val2 is similar, the small size of val2 makes the performance gap difficult to assess statistically across categories.
- Suggested improvements: report official test-set results or provide matched comparisons on identical splits and include per-category confidence intervals or significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests across categories). Clarify exactly which methods are compared on which splits and ensure apples-to-apples evaluation.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: The empirical evaluation on held-out categories is convincing in showing that feature-level adaptation and background modeling are crucial, and the ablation is informative. However, the category-specific adaptation lacks a rigorous justification, and fairness of comparisons is limited by reliance on val2 rather than the test set.
- Clarity: The paper is well-written and easy to follow overall. The main clarity gap is the missing explanation for the additive offset model, the choice of similarity measure, and why kNN averaging should work. Expanding Section 3.2 with these motivations would strengthen the paper.
- Originality: Adapting from classification to detection with limited detection labels and extending to categories without any bounding boxes is novel relative to R-CNN and prior domain adaptation work (which typically assumes detection labels for all categories). The use of a learned background class as part of the adaptation is also a useful insight.
- Significance: The potential impact is high: enabling detection for thousands of categories using abundant classification data is important. The large-scale demonstration and reported challenge ranking support significance, though stronger, standardized test-set comparisons would solidify the claim.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem: scaling detection beyond the limited set of categories with bounding boxes.
- Simple, effective method with clear empirical gains on held-out categories.
- Scales to 7.6K categories and provides publicly available models/code.
- Insightful error analysis showing improvements in localization and background rejection.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient theoretical or empirical justification for the category-specific additive offset and kNN transfer.
- Comparisons rely on validation splits rather than the test set; statistical support for superiority over baselines/competitors is limited.
- Some design choices (metric for nearest neighbors, choice of k) lack principled motivation and sensitivity analysis.

Suggestions to authors
- Provide a principled explanation and analysis of W^d = W^c + δ, including why an additive transform is appropriate and how δ relates to task shift (e.g., background modeling, whole-object vs. part focus).
- Justify the nearest-neighbor approach: analyze metric choices, the effect of k, semantic proximity, and robustness. Consider learning a mapping from W^c to δ using categories with detection data.
- Strengthen empirical comparisons with results on the official test set or fully matched validation splits, and report statistical significance across categories.
- Discuss potential failure cases of the adaptation (e.g., when classification neighbors are semantically similar but visually distinct for detection due to context/scale).

Overall assessment
The paper presents a clear and practically valuable approach with promising results and good scalability. However, the core adaptation mechanism needs a more principled justification, and the comparative evaluation should be strengthened on standardized splits. With these improvements, the contribution would be a strong addition to NIPS.
