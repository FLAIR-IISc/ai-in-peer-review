

# Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

This paper proposes LSDA, a method to adapt image classifiers to object detectors using domain adaptation techniques. The authors address a significant challenge in scaling object detection: the scarcity of bounding box annotations for large numbers of categories. While ImageNet provides rich classification data for 20,000+ categories, bounding box annotations are limited to only 200 categories in the ImageNet detection challenge. The key insight is that the transformation from classification to detection can be learned from a subset of categories with both types of annotations and then transferred to categories with only classification data.

The paper builds on recent work in domain adaptation and R-CNN [1] by treating the classifier-to-detector transformation as a domain adaptation problem. The authors propose a three-component adaptation method: (1) category-invariant adaptation through fine-tuning layers 1-7 using detection data for a subset of categories, (2) category-specific adaptation using k-nearest neighbors to approximate detection weights for categories without bounding box data, and (3) a detection pipeline that directly uses the final score vector without additional SVM training or bounding box regression.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper demonstrates strong technical execution with clear experimental validation. The ablation study (Figure 3, Table 1) shows a 50% relative mAP improvement over the classification-only baseline, with statistically significant gains when using output layer adaptation (k=10). The approach is well-motivated by the need to scale detection to thousands of categories, and the evaluation on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 dataset is appropriate for the problem.

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The three-step adaptation process is logically presented, and the figures (particularly Figure 2 and 3) effectively illustrate the method. The implementation details are sufficiently specific for reproducibility, and the authors have released models and code.

*Originality:* The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of scaling detection to large numbers of categories. While related to MIL methods [6,7] and domain adaptation [12-16], LSDA is the first method to adapt to held-out categories with no detection data, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The connection to the ImageNet taxonomy (using leaf nodes as categories) is particularly novel.

*Significance:* The ability to produce a 7,604 category detector using only classification data represents a major step toward human-level category detection. The paper demonstrates both the technical feasibility and practical impact of the approach, with the potential to significantly expand the scope of object detection applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality:* While the method is technically sound, the justification for the category-specific adaptation technique (Equation 1) lacks theoretical grounding. The paper states that "the category specific output layer (fc8) is comprised of fcA, fcB, δB, and fc-BG" but doesn't explain why the detection weights should be a simple summation of classification weights and an offset parameter. The physical meaning of this offset parameter is unclear, and the choice of k-nearest neighbors is not theoretically justified but rather empirically determined.

*Clarity:* The evaluation protocol is confusing. The authors use half of the validation set for training (val1) and half for evaluation (val2), but footnote 1 claims that "RCNN performance on val2 is similar to the official test set." Given the small size of the validation set (20K images), this comparison is problematic. The paper should use the official test set for comparison with other methods, as is standard practice in object detection competitions.

*Significance:* The paper claims the method enables detection for "tens of thousands of categories," but the experimental validation is limited to a 200-category setting where 100 categories have detection data. The true scalability of the approach to thousands of categories without any detection data remains unproven. The performance gap between LSDA and the oracle (full detection data) is substantial (16.15% vs 22.5% mAP in Table 1), suggesting significant room for improvement.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a technically sound and practically significant approach to scaling object detection to large numbers of categories. The method is well-motivated, clearly described, and demonstrates meaningful improvements over baselines. The main weaknesses concern the theoretical justification of the adaptation technique and the evaluation protocol.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Addresses a significant problem in computer vision with practical implications
- Demonstrates strong empirical results on a standard benchmark
- Provides clear implementation details and code
- Makes a novel contribution to domain adaptation for detection

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The category-specific adaptation technique lacks theoretical justification
- Evaluation protocol does not use the official test set for comparison
- The scalability claim to "tens of thousands of categories" is not sufficiently validated

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of object detection, particularly for scaling detection to large numbers of categories. With minor revisions addressing the theoretical justification and evaluation protocol, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with the following revisions: (1) provide a clearer explanation of why the detection weights are a summation of classification weights and the offset parameter, (2) justify the choice of k-nearest neighbors, and (3) use the official test set for comparison with other methods.

The paper has high potential impact, as it could significantly expand the applicability of object detection systems to domains where bounding box annotations are prohibitively expensive to collect. The approach is elegant in its simplicity and could be widely adopted by the computer vision community.
