This paper presents a method for adapting a Krizhevsky CNN network, trained for classification without bounding box annotations, to perform object detection, leveraging a small number of classes with available bounding box labels. The approach involves three key steps: 1) training a CNN for classification on images of all categories, 2) training a CNN on image regions for categories with bounding box labels, incorporating a negative background region class, and 3) integrating the background class into the full CNN model while adapting network weights. At test time, the CNN is applied to multiple image regions within a test image.

Quality: This is a compelling and interesting paper that demonstrates promising results. While the work builds significantly upon the success of Krizhevsky et al.’s model, rather than introducing a radically new approach, it offers valuable contributions to the fields of computer vision and machine learning. The proposed methodology is logically sound and relatively straightforward. However, the experimental evaluation would be strengthened by comparisons to several baselines, specifically: 1) training and fine-tuning all CNN layers using full images as positive examples and image regions from other classes as negative examples; 2) employing a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, iteratively selecting the highest-scoring positive region for each image from the unlabeled set (set A) and training; and 3) training and fine-tuning the CNN for classification and, at test time, predicting a single bounding box (e.g., the mean bounding box from ImageNet) centered in the image. Determining whether the proposed approach outperforms these alternatives is crucial. It's unclear if the proposed method offers substantial advantages over the first baseline, and potentially less benefit than the MIL baseline. Including these baselines would better contextualize the difficulty of the problem and the effectiveness of the proposed solution.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and clear. The clarity of the experiments section could be enhanced by providing more detailed explanations of the methods presented in Table 1 (specifically, clarifying whether applying Eq. 1 or 2 refers to a subset of CNN layers).

Originality: The paper presents a sufficiently novel contribution to warrant acceptance, although the fundamental approach is relatively simple and tailored to specific CNN architectures.

Significance: The problem addressed and the results obtained are both significant and valuable. The ability to achieve reasonable detection results on a large-scale dataset without relying on extensive bounding box annotations is a compelling achievement. However, the lack of comparison to obvious baselines raises questions about whether the improvements stem from the proposed adaptation technique or primarily from the enhanced performance of CNN features.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper addresses an important problem in scaling object detection to a large number of categories. The proposed method demonstrates a clear improvement over using raw classifier weights for detection. The authors provide code and models for reproducibility.

Arguments against acceptance: The paper lacks comparison to several crucial baselines, potentially overstating the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach. The performance gain may be primarily attributable to the inherent advantages of CNN features rather than the specific adaptation technique. The analysis of error types, while present, could be more in-depth.
