This paper presents a method for adapting a Krizhevsky CNN, pre-trained for classification without bounding box annotations, to the task of object detection with a limited number of classes possessing bounding box labels. The approach involves three training stages: 1) training a CNN for classification on images of all classes, 2) training a CNN on image regions from classes with bounding box annotations, augmented with a negative background region class, and 3) incorporating the background class into the full CNN model while adapting its weights. During testing, the CNN is applied to multiple image regions within a test image.

Quality: This is an interesting and reasonably compelling paper, achieving promising results. However, these results largely build upon the success of Krizhevsky et al.'s model rather than presenting a radically novel contribution; nonetheless, they will be valuable to researchers in computer vision and machine learning. The overall approach is conceptually simple and logical. The experiments are promising, but crucial comparisons to relevant baselines are missing. Specifically, I recommend including the following:

1) Train and fine-tune all layers of the CNN using full images as positive examples and images/regions from other classes as negative examples.
2) Implement a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, iteratively selecting the highest-scoring positive region for each image from the set of proposed regions (instead of using the full image). Train and fine-tune with this approach.
3) Train and fine-tune the CNN for classification. At test time, run the classifier and predict a single bounding box centered within the image (e.g., using the mean bounding box from ImageNet).

I am uncertain whether the proposed approach would outperform these baselines. It is not clear to me that it offers a substantial advantage over baseline 1, and it likely performs less effectively than baseline 2. Including baseline 3 would be important to establish the inherent difficulty of the problem and dataset. 

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-written. However, the clarity of the experiments section could be improved, particularly regarding the explanation of methods presented in Table 1. It is unclear what applying Eq. 1 or 2 to a subset of CNN layers entails.

Originality: The paper demonstrates sufficient novelty to warrant acceptance, although the underlying approach is relatively simple and specific to certain CNN architectures.

Significance: The problem addressed and the results obtained are significant and valuable. The paper tackles a relevant problem, and achieving reasonable detection performance on a large-scale dataset without relying on bounding box labels is a compelling outcome. However, the absence of key baselines raises concerns that the reported results may stem from the improved representational power of CNN features rather than a genuinely better method for training object detectors from weak supervision.
