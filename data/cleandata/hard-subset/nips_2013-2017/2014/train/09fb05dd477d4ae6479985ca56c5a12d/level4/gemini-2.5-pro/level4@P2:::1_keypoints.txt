- Problem: Scaling object detection to many categories where bounding-box annotations are expensive compared to image-level labels.  
- Method: Proposes LSDA—learn a generic classifier-to-detector transformation via domain adaptation on categories with boxes, then apply it to held-out categories.  
- Experimental setup: Evaluated on ILSVRC-2013 using 100 categories with detection annotations to adapt and 100 held-out categories trained only with image labels.  
- Writing and clarity: Paper is well-written, clearly structured, and technically sound with sufficient methodological detail.  
- Analysis: Ablation study and error analysis (Figures 3 and 5) effectively dissect components and show reduced localization and background-confusion errors.  
- Originality: Novel formulation of transferring classifiers to detectors across tasks for held-out categories without bounding-box data.  
- Significance: Practical approach addressing annotation bottleneck; can produce detectors for many categories without per-category box supervision.  
- Empirical strength: Demonstrates substantial improvement over baseline, closing roughly one-third of the gap to a fully supervised oracle on held-out categories.  
- Simplicity: Method is clear, simple, and likely to be adopted and extended by others.  
- Thoroughness: Provides solid ablation and error analyses that explain why adaptation helps.  
- Weakness — R-CNN comparison: Needs a clearer, consolidated comparison to R-CNN (e.g., table or subsection detailing data/training/performance differences).  
- Weakness — metrics framing: The “50% relative mAP boost” claim should be reframed to emphasize gap-closure between baseline and oracle for clearer context.  
- Weakness — long-term perspective: Should include discussion on longevity of the problem as large-scale annotation or alternative weak-supervision methods evolve.  
- Overall verdict: High-quality, well-motivated, and practical contribution; recommended for acceptance.
