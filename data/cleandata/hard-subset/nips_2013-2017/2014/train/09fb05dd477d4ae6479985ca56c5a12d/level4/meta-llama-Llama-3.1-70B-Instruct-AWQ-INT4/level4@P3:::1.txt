This paper proposes a novel approach to object detection, addressing the challenge of obtaining bounding boxes on a scale similar to which category labels exist for object categorization. The authors' algorithm, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), adapts object classifiers for the detection task, leveraging the availability of image-level labels for large numbers of categories. The experimental protocol uses 100 categories for training (with both category labels and bounding boxes) and tests on 100 left-out categories with only category labels.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. The quality of the paper is good, and the clarity of the writing makes it easy to follow. The originality of the paper lies in combining two existing ideas related to adaptation: adapting object classifiers for detection and adapting from one set of categories to another. While this is a novel and interesting idea, it is not groundbreaking.

The significance of the paper is mainly due to its novel approach. However, the results-wise significance is hard to judge, as they will likely be surpassed later by more sophisticated methods. The main issue with the results is that they inevitably sacrifice performance (mAP) by using fewer bounding boxes, and there is no objective way to judge if this trade-off is worth it.

One of the main results of the paper is the improvement in mean Average Precision (mAP) on the held-out categories, which is around 35% of the gap between an oracle and a baseline. This result is more accurately represented by the blue bars in Figure 3b, which show the performance of the LSDA algorithm on the left-out categories.

The authors should clearly state the differences between their approach and the closest competing alternative, R-CNN. Currently, these statements are scattered or not as prominent as they could be. A table or a sub-section devoted to clearly stating these differences would be helpful.

Minor issues include a typo on line 431 ("classi?ers") and the inconsistent naming of the proposed method as "DNN" or "DDA" in some places.

Overall, this is a reasonably good paper proposing a reasonably novel approach. The paper deserves to be accepted and seen by the NIPS community, as it provides a new perspective on object detection. While the results will almost certainly be surpassed soon, the long-term significance of the paper and the approach remains to be seen.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to object detection, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and experimental protocol.
* The results show a significant improvement in mAP on the held-out categories, which is a promising direction for future research.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The results may not be as significant as they seem, as they sacrifice performance (mAP) by using fewer bounding boxes.
* The approach may not be as novel as it seems, as it combines two existing ideas related to adaptation.
* The paper could benefit from a clearer comparison to the closest competing alternative, R-CNN.

Recommendation:

I recommend accepting this paper, as it provides a new perspective on object detection and proposes a novel approach that shows promising results. However, the authors should address the minor issues and provide a clearer comparison to R-CNN to strengthen their paper.
