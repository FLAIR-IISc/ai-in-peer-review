

This paper proposes adapting a Krizhevsky CNN trained for classification (without bounding box annotations) to object detection using a small number of classes with bounding box annotations. The method trains in three phases: 1) a classification CNN on full images across all classes, 2) a region-based CNN on annotated classes plus a negative background class, and 3) integration of the background class into the full model with selective weight adaptation. At test time, the CNN processes multiple image regions within a test image.  

Quality: The work is interesting and demonstrates reasonable detection results. While the approach builds substantially on Krizhevsky et al.'s foundational model rather than introducing radical novelty, it offers valuable insights for computer vision and machine learning researchers. The methodology is straightforward and logical, and the experiments are promising. However, the paper omits critical baseline comparisons:  
1) Training a full-image classifier using positive full images and negative examples (other classes' images/regions), fine-tuning all layers.  
2) Implementing a MIL approach (iteratively selecting top-scoring positive regions per image instead of full images) with full-layer fine-tuning.  
3) Training a classification CNN and predicting a single center-bounded box at test time (e.g., mean ImageNet bounding box).  
The proposed method’s advantage over Baseline 1 is unclear, and it likely underperforms Baseline 2. Including Baseline 3 is essential to contextualize the dataset and problem difficulty.  

Clarity: The paper is well-written and clear overall. The experiments section would benefit from greater detail explaining what each method in Table 1 entails (e.g., whether Equation 1 or 2 is applied to specific CNN layers).  

Originality: The approach is sufficiently novel for acceptance, though its simplicity and specificity to certain CNN architectures warrant note.  

Significance: The problem addressed is relevant, and the ability to achieve decent detection performance on a large-scale dataset without bounding box labels is compelling. However, the results may primarily reflect improved CNN feature representations rather than a superior weakly supervised training paradigm, as the absence of key baselines obscures this distinction.
