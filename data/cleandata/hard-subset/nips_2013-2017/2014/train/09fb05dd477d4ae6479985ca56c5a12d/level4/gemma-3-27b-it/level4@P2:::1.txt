This paper addresses the challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, a task hindered by the difficulty and cost of obtaining bounding box annotations. The authors propose Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), an algorithm that learns to transfer knowledge from image classifiers – trained on readily available image-level labels – to object detectors. The core idea is to learn a transformation between the classification and detection tasks and apply this transformation to classifiers for categories lacking bounding box annotations. The method is evaluated on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge, training on 100 categories with both labels and bounding boxes, and testing on 100 held-out categories with only image-level labels. The results demonstrate a significant improvement over a baseline relying solely on classifier weights, achieving roughly a third of the gap between baseline performance and an “oracle” detector trained with complete annotations.

**Quality and Clarity:** This is a well-written and technically sound paper. The methodology is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is well-defined. The paper demonstrates a careful consideration of both the strengths and limitations of the proposed approach.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel combination of established ideas: adapting classifiers for detection and leveraging labeled data from related categories. While the individual concepts of transfer learning and domain adaptation are not new, their application in this specific context – adapting classifiers to detectors for categories with limited annotation – represents a valuable contribution. Although not groundbreaking, it provides a fresh perspective on tackling the large-scale detection problem. Related work, including Multiple Instance Learning approaches [6, 7] and domain adaptation techniques [12-16], are appropriately discussed.

**Significance:** The primary contribution of this work lies in the proposed approach for leveraging readily available classification data to improve object detection performance in a low-annotation regime. The practical significance of this approach will depend on the trade-off between performance (mAP) and annotation cost, and future work may surpass these results. However, the presented results are promising and demonstrate the potential for scaling detection to a larger number of categories than previously feasible. The authors acknowledge the potential limitations and future directions, which strengthens the significance of the contribution. The question of whether reducing annotation cost is a fundamentally important long-term challenge remains open, but the paper offers a valuable step toward addressing it.

**Detailed Feedback:**

*   **Figure 3a Clarification:** The authors should explicitly state which methods, beyond their proposed approach and R-CNN, utilize category labels during detection. A clearer distinction between methods using only image-level labels versus those leveraging bounding box annotations would enhance the comparison.
*   **Performance Metrics:** The authors should emphasize the performance improvement relative to the baseline, rather than solely focusing on percentage of the gap to the "oracle." Reporting the absolute mAP values for the baseline, DNN, and oracle would provide a more intuitive understanding of the results. The claim of a "50% improvement" (line 425) should be clarified by specifying the baseline used for comparison and focusing on the performance on the held-out categories.
*   **R-CNN Comparison:** A dedicated section or table explicitly outlining the differences between LSDA and R-CNN would be beneficial. Currently, these distinctions are scattered throughout the text. Highlighting the advantages and disadvantages of each approach would provide valuable context for readers. For example, the ability of LSDA to generalize to categories without bounding box annotations is a key differentiator. Consider removing Figure 6 to accommodate this comparison.
*   **Terminology:** The paper sometimes uses “DNN” and “LSDA” interchangeably. Clarifying the preferred terminology would improve consistency.
*   **Typo:** Correct the typo on line 431 ("classi?ers").

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in scaling object detection.
*   The proposed approach is novel and well-motivated.
*   The method is clearly explained and well-implemented.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a significant improvement over a reasonable baseline.
*   The authors acknowledge limitations and suggest directions for future work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The improvement, while statistically significant, is not a dramatic leap forward in performance.
*   The long-term significance of the approach remains to be seen, as more sophisticated methods may emerge.
*   The results are sensitive to the trade-off between performance and annotation cost, and this trade-off is not fully explored.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a solid paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of object detection. While the results may not be revolutionary, the approach is novel, well-executed, and has the potential to enable detection for a significantly larger number of categories. I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2017.
