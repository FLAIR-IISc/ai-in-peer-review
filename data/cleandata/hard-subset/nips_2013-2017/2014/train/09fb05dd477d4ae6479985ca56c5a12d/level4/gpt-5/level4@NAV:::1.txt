This paper tackles object detection, specifically the difficulty of acquiring bounding boxes at a scale comparable to category labels used for classification. The authors avoid this bottleneck by adapting object classifiers to perform detection. The method itself is relatively simple and direct, which is fine. The experimental setup trains on 100 categories with both labels and boxes, and evaluates on 100 disjoint categories that only have category labels. The reported results are reasonably strong, closing roughly 35% of the gap between a baseline and an oracle on the 100 held-out categories.

Quality and clarity: The paper is of good quality and is clearly written.

Originality: The work blends two established adaptation ideas—adapting classifiers for detection and adapting across category sets. To the best of this reviewer’s knowledge, this combination is new and interesting, though not revolutionary.

Significance: The chief contribution is the new (albeit not groundbreaking) approach. It is difficult to assess long-term impact based on current results, which will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods over time (as expected). A central concern is that performance (mAP) is inherently reduced by relying on fewer bounding boxes, and there is no objective criterion provided to evaluate whether this trade-off is worthwhile.
(As an aside, it is unclear whether this problem is a major long-term obstacle. While boxes are more costly than labels, increased investment might alleviate the issue. Not long ago, assembling millions of images labeled with 20,000 categories seemed impractical.)

Detailed issues that should be addressed:
- In Figure 3a, which competing methods do or do not leverage category labels to aid detection, aside from the proposed method and R-CNN? Please spell this out to ensure an apples-to-apples comparison.
- My assessment of the approach hinges on the blue bars in Figure 3b. The DNN result sits about one-third of the way from the baseline to the oracle. Do the authors agree this is a key outcome? If so, please emphasize this rather than the 78% figure (line 341), which is not quite appropriate for two reasons: 1) evaluation should focus only on the held-out categories, and 2) performance should be framed relative to both the baseline and the oracle, not solely as a percentage of the oracle. The same issue applies to the “50% improvement” claim (line 425).
- Please add a table or dedicated subsection clearly enumerating the differences from R-CNN, which appears to be the closest competitor. Currently, these points are scattered and not sufficiently prominent. Consider removing Figure 6, which consists of anecdotal examples, to make space.

Minor issues (no need to respond)
- Is the method referred to as “DNN” or “DDA”?
- Typo on line 431 (“classi?ers”).

Overall, this is a solid paper proposing a reasonably novel approach. I recommend acceptance for the NIPS audience. While the results will likely be overtaken soon—and the method is intentionally simple, which is fine for a first paper in this direction—the long-term impact of the approach remains to be determined.
