This paper addresses the problem of object detection, specifically the difficulty of acquiring bounding box annotations on the same scale as category labels for object categorization. The authors propose a clever solution: adapting existing object classifiers to perform the detection task. Their algorithm is straightforward, a positive attribute in itself. The experimental protocol involves training on 100 categories with both bounding box and category label annotations, and testing on 100 held-out categories for which only category labels are available. The results are reasonably good, achieving approximately 35% of the performance gap between an oracle and a baseline on these held-out categories.

**Quality and clarity:** This is a well-written and clearly presented paper.

**Originality:** The paper combines two established concepts – adapting object classifiers for detection, and transferring knowledge across different category sets. To the best of my knowledge, this combination represents a novel and interesting idea, though not a groundbreaking one.

**Significance:** The primary contribution of this work lies in its novel (but not revolutionary) approach. While it’s difficult to assess the long-term significance given the results, it is likely that more sophisticated methods will eventually surpass its performance – a natural progression. A key limitation of the results is that performance (mAP) is inevitably reduced by the use of fewer bounding boxes. Critically, there’s no objective means to evaluate whether this trade-off is beneficial. 

(It’s worth questioning whether the problem addressed is a truly critical long-term challenge. While bounding box annotations are indeed more expensive to obtain than category labels, it’s plausible that increased resource allocation could mitigate this issue. Recent progress in large-scale image labeling, such as datasets with millions of images labeled with tens of thousands of object categories, demonstrates that previously insurmountable challenges can be overcome.)

**Detailed issues that should be addressed:**

- In Figure 3a, please clarify which methods utilize category labels to aid detection, beyond your proposed method and R-CNN. A clear statement is necessary to ensure an apples-to-apples comparison.
- My evaluation of the proposed approach is centered on the blue bars in Figure 3b, which indicate the method achieves roughly one-third of the way between the baseline and oracle performance. Do the authors agree that this is a key finding? If so, it should be highlighted, rather than the 78% figure presented on line 341, which is misleading for two reasons: 1) it should be assessed based solely on the left-out categories, and 2) it should be evaluated relative to the baseline as well as the oracle, not simply as a percentage of oracle performance. A similar issue arises with the claim of 50% improvement on line 425.
- The authors should dedicate a table or a subsection to explicitly outlining the differences between their method and R-CNN, which appears to be the closest competing approach. Currently, these distinctions are scattered and not sufficiently prominent. I suggest removing Figure 6, which presents anecdotal examples, to make space for this comparison.

Overall, this is a solid paper that proposes a reasonably novel approach to object detection. I recommend acceptance and believe it would be a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. While the results will almost certainly be improved upon in future work – a consequence of the method’s simplicity, as this appears to be the first paper to explore this direction – the long-term significance of the approach remains an open question.
