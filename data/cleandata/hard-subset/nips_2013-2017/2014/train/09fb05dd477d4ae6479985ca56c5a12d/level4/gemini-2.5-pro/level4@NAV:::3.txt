This paper proposes a technique for adapting a convolutional neural network (CNN) of the Krizhevsky et al. style, originally trained for classification with image-level labels, to perform object detection. The method leverages a small set of classes that have bounding box annotations. The training procedure consists of three stages: 1) a CNN is trained for classification using images from all available classes; 2) a separate CNN is trained on image regions derived from the classes with bounding box labels, augmented with a negative background class; and 3) the background class is integrated into the full classification CNN, and a subset of its weights are fine-tuned. For inference, the adapted CNN is applied to multiple regions of a test image.

**Quality:** This is a compelling and interesting contribution that demonstrates reasonable performance. These results are largely attributable to the strength of the underlying Krizhevsky et al. model rather than a fundamentally new concept; nevertheless, the findings will be of value to the computer vision and machine learning communities. The overall methodology is straightforward yet sound. The experiments show promise, but a key weakness is the absence of comparisons against several important baselines:
1)  A full fine-tuning of all CNN layers using whole images as positive samples and images/regions from other classes as negative samples.
2)  A similar approach employing a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) framework, where the highest-scoring region within each positive image is iteratively selected for training, instead of using the entire image.
3)  A naive baseline where a classification CNN is trained, and at test time, a canonical bounding box (e.g., centered in the image, with a mean size derived from the ImageNet dataset) is predicted.
It remains unclear whether the proposed method would outperform these alternatives. The justification for the proposed method over the first baseline is not entirely convincing, and the second baseline may represent a more principled approach. Including the third baseline is crucial for contextualizing the difficulty of the task and the dataset for the reader.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is generally clear and well-articulated. However, the experimental section could be enhanced with greater detail. Specifically, the descriptions of the methods presented in Table 1 are ambiguous; it is not clear which CNN layers are being modified according to Equations 1 and 2 for each reported result.

**Originality:** The work possesses sufficient novelty for acceptance, though it should be noted that the core idea is straightforward and its applicability may be limited to specific CNN architectures.

**Significance:** The paper tackles an important problem, and its findings are both significant and valuable. The achievement of respectable detection performance on a large-scale dataset, primarily using weak supervision, is a compelling result. At the same time, the omission of key baselines is a considerable drawback. It leaves open the question of whether the performance gains stem from the proposed training strategy or are simply a consequence of the powerful feature representations learned by the underlying CNN. The contribution may be more in demonstrating the effectiveness of these features for weakly supervised detection rather than in introducing a superior training paradigm.
