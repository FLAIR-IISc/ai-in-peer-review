This paper presents a method for adapting a convolutional neural network (CNN), pre-trained for image classification, to the task of object detection. The proposed scenario involves a large dataset where most classes only have image-level labels, while a small subset of classes are annotated with bounding boxes. The approach consists of a three-stage training process: 1) A CNN is trained for classification on the full dataset of images. 2) A second CNN is trained specifically on image regions from the classes with bounding box annotations, which are treated as positive examples, along with a newly introduced negative "background" class. 3) The background class is then integrated into the first, fully-trained classification model, and a subset of the network weights are fine-tuned to accommodate it. At test time, this adapted network is applied in a sliding-window fashion across multiple regions of a test image to perform detection.

**Strengths**

*   The paper addresses the important and practical problem of object detection in a weakly supervised setting, where obtaining exhaustive bounding box annotations is prohibitively expensive.
*   The results are compelling, demonstrating that reasonable detection performance can be achieved on a large-scale dataset without full supervision. This finding is valuable for researchers in both computer vision and machine learning.
*   The general approach is simple and logical, effectively leveraging the power of a model pre-trained on a large classification task and adapting it for a more localized one.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

The primary weakness of the paper is the absence of several crucial baseline comparisons. Without these, it is difficult to ascertain whether the performance gains stem from the novelty of the proposed training scheme or simply from the impressive power of the underlying CNN features. The experiments would be significantly more convincing with the inclusion of the following baselines:

1.  **A simpler weakly-supervised baseline:** Train and fine-tune all layers of the CNN using full images of the target classes as positive examples and images/regions from other classes as negative examples. It is not immediately obvious that the proposed multi-stage approach is superior to this more direct method.

2.  **A Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) baseline:** Employ a standard MIL framework where, for each positive image, the model iteratively selects the highest-scoring region as the positive instance to update the detector. This is a common and powerful technique for weakly-supervised object detection, and it would be important to see how the proposed method compares.

3.  **A simple localization baseline:** After fine-tuning the CNN for classification, simply predict a single, fixed bounding box at the center of the image at test time (e.g., a box corresponding to the mean size and location from the ImageNet training set). Including this would help contextualize the difficulty of the task and provide a clear lower bound on performance, informing the reader about the challenges of the dataset.

As it stands, I am unconvinced that the proposed method would outperform the first two baselines suggested above.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally clear and well-written. However, the clarity of the experiments section could be improved. In particular, the description of the methods evaluated in Table 1 is ambiguous. It is unclear what "applying Eq. 1 or 2 to a subset of CNN layers" precisely entails for each configuration. A more detailed explanation is needed for the reader to fully understand the experimental setup and for the results to be reproducible.

**Originality and Significance**

The paper is sufficiently novel for acceptance, although the core idea is a straightforward adaptation of existing CNN architectures rather than a radical departure. The problem it addresses is significant, and the results are valuable to the community. The fact that decent detection results were obtained with such limited supervision is a compelling contribution.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents an interesting and logical method for adapting classification networks for detection with weak supervision. The results are promising and highlight the potential of leveraging large, weakly labeled datasets. However, the paper's impact is currently limited by the lack of comparisons to strong and obvious baselines. It is very possible that the success shown is primarily capitalizing on the strength of modern CNN features, rather than the introduction of a superior training methodology for weakly-supervised detection. Addressing the suggested baseline comparisons would substantially strengthen the paper's claims and clarify its contributions to the field.
