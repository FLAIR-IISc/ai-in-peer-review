- Addresses object detection with limited bounding-box annotations by adapting object classifiers into detectors.  
- Proposes a simple, straightforward algorithm for classifier-to-detector adaptation.  
- Evaluation uses 100 fully annotated categories for training and 100 held-out categories with only labels, testing transfer to unseen categories.  
- Results close roughly 35% of the gap between a baseline and an oracle on the held-out categories.  
- Paper is high-quality and clearly written.  
- Originality: novel combination of adapting classifiers for detection and transferring across category sets, though not groundbreaking.  
- Significance: introduces a useful new direction but long-term impact is uncertain and likely to be surpassed by more complex methods.  
- Lacks an objective framework to judge whether the detection–annotation trade-off (lower mAP for fewer boxes) is worthwhile.  
- Raises the valid question of whether bounding-box scarcity is a persistent problem given potential future large-scale annotation efforts.  
- Clarify Figure 3a: specify which compared methods use category labels for detection to ensure fair comparisons.  
- Emphasize held-out-category results (blue bars in Fig. 3b) and avoid potentially misleading aggregate claims like "78% of oracle" or "50% improvement"; focus on improvement relative to baseline.  
- Recommend a dedicated table/subsection directly comparing the method to R-CNN rather than scattering differences or relying on anecdotal Figure 6.  
- Minor: ensure consistent method naming (DNN vs. DDA) and fix the typo on line 431.  
- Overall recommendation: solid, novel-enough contribution that merits acceptance and interest from the community.
