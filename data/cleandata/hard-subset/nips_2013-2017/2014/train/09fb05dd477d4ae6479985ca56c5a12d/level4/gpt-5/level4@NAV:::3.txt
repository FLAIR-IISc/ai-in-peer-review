Review- This paper proposes a way to repurpose a Krizhevsky CNN trained for classification (without bounding box labels) for object detection when only a small set of classes has bounding box annotations. During training, the method: 1) trains a CNN for classification on images from all classes, 2) trains a CNN on image regions for the classes with bounding boxes, adding a negative “background” region class, and 3) augments the full CNN with the background class while adapting a subset of the CNN’s weights. At test time, the CNN is applied to multiple regions within a test image.

Quality: The paper is interesting and makes a reasonably convincing case that it achieves solid performance. The gains largely leverage the strength of Krizhevsky et al.’s model rather than introducing a fundamentally new idea; nevertheless, the results will be useful to computer vision and machine learning researchers. The overall strategy is simple but sensible. The experiments are promising, but key baselines are missing:
1) Treat full images as positives and use images and regions from other classes as negatives. Train and fine-tune all CNN layers.
2) As above, but use a MIL procedure that iteratively selects, for each image in set A, the highest-scoring positive region (instead of using the whole image).
3) Train and fine-tune the CNN for classification. At test time, run the classifier and output a single bounding box at the image center (e.g., the mean ImageNet bounding box).
I am not sure the proposed method would outperform these baselines. It is not evident that it is preferable to baseline 1, and it likely makes less sense than baseline 2. Including the third baseline is important to convey to readers how easy or difficult the task and dataset are.

Clarity: The paper is clear and well written. The experimental section could be clarified, particularly by elaborating on what each entry in Table 1 corresponds to (e.g., does it involve applying Eq. 1 or Eq. 2 to a subset of CNN layers?).

Originality: The contribution is sufficiently novel for acceptance, although the core idea is straightforward and tailored to specific CNN architectures.

Significance: The problem and results are significant and valuable.

The paper tackles an important problem, and demonstrating respectable detection performance on a large-scale dataset without bounding box labels is compelling. At the same time, the omission of obvious baselines leaves open the possibility that the gains stem from stronger CNN features rather than from a superior approach to training object detectors under weak supervision.
