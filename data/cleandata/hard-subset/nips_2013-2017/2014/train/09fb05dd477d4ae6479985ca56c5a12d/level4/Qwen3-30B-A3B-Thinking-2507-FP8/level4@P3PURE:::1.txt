

This paper addresses object detection by tackling the challenge of obtaining bounding boxes at a scale comparable to category label availability for object categorization. The authors propose adapting object classifiers for detection, bypassing the need for extensive bounding box annotations. Their approach is simple and straightforward, which is a strength. The experimental protocol trains on 100 categories with both category labels and bounding boxes, then tests on 100 disjoint categories with only category labels. Results show the method closes approximately 35% of the gap between an oracle (full bounding box supervision) and a baseline (no adaptation) on the test categories.

**Quality and Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Originality:** The work combines two established concepts—adapting classifiers for detection and adapting across category sets—resulting in a novel and interesting, though not ground-breaking, approach. To the reviewer's knowledge, this specific combination has not been previously explored.

**Significance:** The primary contribution is this novel adaptation strategy. While the results are promising, their long-term significance is uncertain, as they will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods (which is expected). A key limitation is that the approach inherently sacrifices mAP performance by using fewer bounding boxes during training. Crucially, there is no objective metric to evaluate whether this trade-off is justified.

*(As a side note, it is debatable whether the core challenge addressed—obtaining bounding boxes at scale—is a critical long-term bottleneck. Bounding boxes are indeed more expensive to annotate than category labels, but increased resources could readily resolve this issue, as seen historically with the rapid scaling of category-labeled datasets.)*

**Detailed Issues Requiring Clarification:**
- **Figure 3a:** The figure must explicitly state which methods (beyond the authors' approach and R-CNN) utilize category labels to aid detection. This is essential for a valid apples-to-apples comparison.
- **Key Result Presentation:** The primary result is the 35% gap closure (not the 78% mentioned in line 341). The 78% figure is misleading for two reasons: (1) it should be evaluated solely on the 100 left-out categories, and (2) it incorrectly represents oracle performance without accounting for the baseline. Similarly, the claim of "50% improvement" (line 425) should reference the gap closure metric, not an oracle percentage. The authors should highlight the 35% gap closure as the central quantitative finding, not the 78%.
- **Comparison to R-CNN:** A dedicated table or subsection should clearly articulate the differences between the proposed method and R-CNN (the closest competitor). Current descriptions are scattered; Figure 6 (anecdotal examples) should be replaced with this comparative summary.

**Minor Issues:**
- The method is inconsistently labeled as "DNN" and "DDA" (line 127, 431).
- Typo on line 431: "classi?ers" → "classifiers".

Overall, this is a reasonable paper proposing a reasonable novel approach. While the results will likely be surpassed by future work, the method's simplicity and the first-to-address nature of the adaptation strategy warrant publication. The paper deserves acceptance and should reach the NIPS community. The long-term significance of the specific approach remains to be determined.
