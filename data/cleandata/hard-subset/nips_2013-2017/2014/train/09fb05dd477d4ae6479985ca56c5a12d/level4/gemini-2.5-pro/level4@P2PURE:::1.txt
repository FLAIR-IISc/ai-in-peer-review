This paper addresses the challenge of scaling object detection, where obtaining bounding-box annotations is significantly more costly than obtaining image-level category labels. The authors circumvent this issue by proposing a method to adapt object classifiers for the detection task. The proposed algorithm is simple and straightforward. The experimental protocol involves training on 100 categories with both category labels and bounding boxes, and testing on 100 distinct categories for which only category labels are available. The results are promising, closing approximately 35% of the performance gap between a baseline and an oracle on the 100 novel categories.

**Quality and Clarity:** The paper is of high quality and is clearly written.

**Originality:** The paper's contribution lies in combining two existing adaptation concepts: adapting object classifiers for detection, and adapting models from a known set of categories to a new one. To my knowledge, this combination represents a novel and interesting, though not groundbreaking, idea.

**Significance:** The primary contribution of this work is its novel approach. However, its long-term significance is difficult to assess based on the current results, which will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods in the future. A central issue is that the method's reliance on fewer bounding boxes inevitably leads to a trade-off in performance (mAP), and the paper does not provide an objective way to evaluate whether this trade-off is worthwhile.

As a side note, it is debatable whether the problem of expensive bounding-box annotation is a fundamental long-term challenge. It is plausible that this data acquisition bottleneck could be overcome by investing more resources, much like how large-scale image classification datasets with tens of thousands of categories, which once seemed infeasible, have now become commonplace.

**Detailed Issues to be Addressed:**
- In Figure 3a, it is unclear which of the competing methods, besides R-CNN and the proposed approach, also utilize category labels to aid detection. The authors should clarify this to ensure the comparisons are on equal footing.
- The most effective demonstration of the proposed method's performance appears to be in Figure 3b (blue bars), where it closes roughly one-third of the gap between the baseline and the oracle on the novel categories. The authors should highlight this as a main result. The currently emphasized 78% figure (line 341) is less informative for two reasons: 1) the evaluation should focus on the novel, left-out categories, and 2) performance should be contextualized by the baseline, not presented as a simple percentage of the oracle. A similar issue applies to the 50% improvement claim on line 425.
- Given that R-CNN is the closest competing alternative, the authors should dedicate a table or subsection to explicitly detailing the differences between their method and R-CNN. These details are currently scattered throughout the paper and lack prominence. The authors could consider replacing Figure 6, which presents anecdotal qualitative examples, with this more substantive comparison.

**Minor Issues (no need to respond):**
- The paper seems to use "DNN" and "DDA" interchangeably to refer to the proposed method. This should be made consistent.

**Overall Recommendation:**
In summary, this is a solid paper that proposes a reasonably novel approach to a relevant problem. The work merits acceptance and will be of interest to the NIPS community. While the method's simplicity means its performance will likely be surpassed, it is a valuable first step in this direction. The long-term impact of the proposed approach remains to be seen.
