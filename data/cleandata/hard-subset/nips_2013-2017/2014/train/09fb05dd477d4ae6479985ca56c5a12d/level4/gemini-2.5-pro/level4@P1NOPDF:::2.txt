The authors propose a framework for adapting deep neural networks, originally trained for image classification, to the task of object detection. Evaluated on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 dataset, the proposed method achieved a 4th place ranking in the object detection challenge. The paper is clearly written and generally easy to follow. The proposed CNN architecture is both interesting and straightforward to implement. Overall, this is a good quality paper.

However, there are two main concerns that could be addressed to improve the paper.

First, the paper's main contribution—the adaptation method—requires better justification. The underlying motivations for the specific design choices are not presented in a principled manner. For example, in Section 3.2 (Page 5, Line 231), the authors do not clearly explain why the weights for the adapted detection task are formulated as a summation of Wj^c and W{avg}. The intuition behind this 'offset parameter' is not explained. Consequently, it remains unclear why the kNN variant of the model should be expected to perform better.

Second, the experimental comparison to other methods may not be fair. The authors evaluate their model on a portion of the validation set, whereas competing methods are typically benchmarked on the official test set. While the authors note in a footnote (Line 322) that R-CNN achieves similar performance on the val2 set, the small size of this subset makes it difficult to draw statistically significant conclusions about the performance gains of the proposed method.

In summary, the authors propose an interesting framework for adapting classification networks to object detection, achieving strong empirical results. The paper would be significantly strengthened, however, by providing a more principled explanation for the proposed adaptation technique and by conducting a more direct comparison with competing methods on a standard benchmark.
