This paper tackles object detection at scale, focusing on the scarcity of bounding-box annotations compared to the abundance of image-level category labels used for classification. The authors circumvent this data bottleneck by adapting image classifiers into detectors. The proposed algorithm is deliberately simple and direct, which is not a drawback here. The experimental setup uses 100 categories with both image-level and bounding-box labels for training and evaluates on 100 held-out categories that have only image-level labels. The method achieves reasonably strong performance, closing roughly 35% of the gap between a naïve baseline and an oracle on the 100 held-out categories.

Quality and clarity: The paper is solid and clearly written.

Originality: The work merges two lines of adaptation: converting classifiers into detectors and transferring across categories. To the best of my knowledge, this is a new and interesting combination, though not a major breakthrough.

Significance: The chief contribution is the (modestly novel) approach itself. The ultimate impact is hard to predict—results will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods in short order (as expected). A central issue is the inherent performance (mAP) degradation when using fewer bounding boxes; there is no straightforward metric to determine whether this accuracy–annotation trade-off is justified.
(As an aside, it is not obvious that the specific problem addressed is a long-term bottleneck. While bounding boxes are indeed costlier than image-level labels, increased resources and tooling could mitigate this. Not long ago, assembling millions of labeled images over 20,000 categories seemed out of reach as well.)

Detailed issues that should be addressed:
- In Figure 3a, besides the proposed approach and R-CNN, which methods leverage category labels to aid detection? Please make this explicit to ensure an apples-to-apples comparison.
- My assessment of the approach hinges on the blue bars in Figure 3b: the proposed DNN result sits about one-third of the way from the baseline toward the oracle. Do the authors agree this is a key takeaway? If so, please emphasize this instead of the 78% figure (line 341), which is not the right metric for two reasons: (1) it should be computed only on the held-out categories, and (2) it should account for the baseline rather than just reporting a fraction of the oracle. A similar concern applies to the claimed 50% improvement (line 425).
- Please add a dedicated table or subsection that clearly enumerates differences from R-CNN, which is the closest comparator. At present, these points are scattered and not prominent. Consider removing Figure 6 (which offers anecdotal examples) to make room.

Minor issues (no need to respond)
- Is the method referred to as “DNN” or “DDA”?
- Typo on line 431 (“classi?ers”).

Overall, this is a solid paper proposing a reasonably novel idea. I recommend acceptance so the NIPS community can see it. While the empirical results will likely be overtaken soon (as the method is quite simple—appropriate for the first paper pursuing this direction), the long-term significance of the approach remains to be determined.
