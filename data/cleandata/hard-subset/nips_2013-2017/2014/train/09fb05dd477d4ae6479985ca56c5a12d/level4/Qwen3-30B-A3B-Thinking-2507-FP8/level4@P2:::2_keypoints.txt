- Introduces LSDA: adapting image classifiers to object detectors to overcome scarce bounding-box annotations for large-scale categories.  
- Frames classifier-to-detector conversion as a domain adaptation problem (classification = source, detection = target).  
- Proposes two-stage adaptation: category-invariant adaptation via shared feature layers and category-specific adaptation via k-nearest neighbors transfer.  
- Learns transformation from a small subset of bbox-annotated categories to enable detection for many unannotated categories.  
- Demonstrates practical scalability by building a 7,604-category detector using ImageNet leaf-node classification data.  
- Evaluates on ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection dataset with an ablation study showing ~50% relative mAP improvement over a classification-only baseline.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art performance for the large-scale detector and releases code and models for reproducibility.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient methodological detail and figures.  
- Properly situates contributions within domain adaptation, R-CNN, and weakly supervised detection literature.  
- Technically sound with strong empirical validation, but still falls short of oracle (full bbox supervision) performance.  
- Main theoretical weakness: insufficient justification for the weight-offset formulation in Section 3.2 (Equation 1) and the meaning of the offset parameter.  
- Main empirical weakness: comparisons use a subset of validation data (val2) rather than the official test set, limiting statistical significance of gains.  
- Authors acknowledge dataset/validation limitation but do not provide stronger statistical evidence or full-test comparisons.  
- Overall significance is substantial: addresses a key bottleneck for scaling detection to thousands of categories and has clear practical impact.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to strengthen theoretical justification and provide more robust statistical/benchmark comparisons.
