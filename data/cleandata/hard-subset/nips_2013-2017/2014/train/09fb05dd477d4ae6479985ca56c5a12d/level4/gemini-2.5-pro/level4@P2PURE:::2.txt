This paper proposes a framework for adapting deep neural networks, originally trained for image classification, to the task of object detection. Evaluations on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 dataset demonstrate that the method ranks 4th in the object detection challenge. The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the proposed CNN architecture is both interesting and straightforward to implement. Overall, the quality of the paper is good. However, I have two primary concerns that could be addressed to strengthen the work.

First, the paper's central contribution—the adaptation method—requires a more thorough justification. The underlying motivations for the proposed design choices are not presented in a principled manner. For instance, it is unclear why the weights for the adapted detection task are formulated as a summation of Wj^c and W{avg} (Section 3.2, Page 5, Line 231). The paper does not explain the intuition or theoretical basis for this 'offset parameter.' As a consequence, the reason for the superior performance of the kNN variant also remains unclear.

Second, the empirical comparison to other methods may be unfair. The authors evaluate their model on a subset of the validation set, whereas competing methods are typically evaluated on the full test set. While the authors note in a footnote (Line 322) that R-CNN achieves similar performance on their `val2` subset, the small size of this set makes it difficult to conclude that the reported performance gains are statistically significant.

In summary, while the proposed method achieves performance comparable to the state-of-the-art, its central adaptation technique requires a clearer explanation, and the empirical comparisons should be conducted under standard settings to ensure a fair evaluation.
