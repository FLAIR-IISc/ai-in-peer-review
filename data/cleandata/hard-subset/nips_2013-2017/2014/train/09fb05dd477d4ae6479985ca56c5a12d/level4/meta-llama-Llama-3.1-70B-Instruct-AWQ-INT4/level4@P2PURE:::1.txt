This paper addresses the challenge of object detection, specifically the issue of obtaining bounding boxes at a scale comparable to the availability of category labels for object categorization. The authors sidestep this challenge by proposing an adaptation of object classifiers for the detection task. Their algorithm is straightforward and simple, which is not inherently a drawback. The experimental protocol utilizes 100 categories for training, with both category labels and bounding boxes, and tests on 100 left-out categories with only category labels. The results obtained were reasonably good, bridging approximately 35% of the gap between an oracle and a baseline on the 100 left-out categories.

Quality and clarity: This is a well-written paper of good quality.

Originality: This paper combines two existing ideas related to adaptation: adapting object classifiers for detection and adapting from one set of categories to another. To the best of this reviewer's knowledge, this is a novel and interesting idea, although not groundbreaking.

Significance: The main contribution of this paper is the novel approach, which, while not groundbreaking, is still noteworthy. However, judging the long-term significance based on the results is challenging, as they will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods in the future. The primary issue with the results is that they inevitably sacrifice performance (mAP) by using fewer bounding boxes, and there is no objective way to evaluate whether this trade-off is worthwhile.

A related concern is whether the issue being addressed is truly an important long-term challenge. Although bounding boxes are more expensive to obtain than category labels, it is unclear whether throwing resources at the problem would not be a viable solution. Not long ago, having millions of images labeled with 20,000 object categories seemed almost impossible.

Detailed issues that should be addressed:

- In Figure 3a, the authors should clearly state which methods do (or do not) use category labels to aid detection, in addition to their method and R-CNN, to ensure an apples-to-apples comparison.
- This reviewer's assessment of the proposed approach is based on the blue bars in Figure 3b, which show that DNN is approximately one-third between the baseline and oracle. The authors should acknowledge this as one of the primary results and highlight it, rather than the 78% mentioned on line 341, which is not the correct number for two reasons: it should be evaluated based only on left-out categories, and it should not be a simple percentage of oracle performance (but rather take the baseline into account). A similar issue applies to the claim of 50% improvement on line 425.
- The authors should devote a table or subsection to clearly outlining the differences between their approach and R-CNN, which appears to be the closest competing alternative. Currently, these statements are scattered or not as prominent as they could be. Consider replacing Figure 6, which presents anecdotal examples.

Minor issues (no response necessary):

Overall, this is a reasonably good paper proposing a reasonably novel approach. I believe the paper deserves to be accepted and seen by the NIPS community. While the results will likely be surpassed soon, the simplicity of the method (being the first paper to take this approach) makes its long-term significance uncertain.
