

This paper introduces LSDA (Large Scale Detection through Adaptation), a novel framework for adapting image classifiers to object detectors using domain adaptation techniques. The authors address a critical challenge in object detection: the scarcity of bounding box annotations for large-scale categories. While previous work like R-CNN demonstrated how to adapt classifiers to detectors, it required bounding box data for all categories. LSDA overcomes this limitation by learning a transformation from classification to detection using only a small subset of categories with bounding box annotations, enabling detection for tens of thousands of categories without direct bounding box supervision.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it formulates the classifier-to-detector transformation as a domain adaptation problem, with classification data as the source domain and detection data as the target domain. Second, it introduces a two-stage adaptation process: (1) category-invariant adaptation that learns a generic transformation across all categories using the shared feature layers, and (2) category-specific adaptation that transfers detection parameters to categories without bounding box annotations using k-nearest neighbors. Third, it demonstrates practical scalability by producing a 7,604-category detector using ImageNet's leaf nodes as classification data.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the technical contributions accessible. The experimental evaluation on ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection dataset is thorough, with an ablation study showing a 50% relative mAP improvement over a classification-only baseline. The method achieves state-of-the-art performance for the 7,604-category detector, and the authors provide code and models for reproducibility.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with strong empirical validation. The authors properly address both strengths and weaknesses of their approach, particularly noting that their method still lags behind the oracle performance (full bounding box annotations). The experimental design is appropriate for the problem, though the comparison with baseline methods uses a subset of the validation set rather than the official test set, potentially limiting the statistical significance of the results. The authors acknowledge this limitation in footnote 1 but do not fully address the statistical robustness of their gains.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The methodology is logically presented with clear figures and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The paper properly contextualizes its contributions within the relevant literature, including domain adaptation and weakly supervised detection work. The writing is concise and avoids unnecessary jargon.

The originality of the approach is strong. While building on R-CNN and domain adaptation literature, LSDA's specific technique for adapting classifiers to detectors without bounding box data for all categories represents a meaningful advance. The paper effectively distinguishes its contribution from related work, particularly MIL-based approaches that have not been evaluated on large-scale ImageNet detection.

The significance of this work is substantial. It directly addresses a major bottleneck in scaling object detection to thousands of categories by leveraging readily available image-level labels. The 7,604-category detector demonstrates the practical potential of the approach, potentially enabling detection systems that match the scale of modern classification models. This could significantly impact both academic research and industrial applications where annotation costs are prohibitive.

However, the paper could improve in two key areas. First, the theoretical justification for the adaptation method in Section 3.2 (specifically the weight offset approach in Equation 1) requires more explanation. The physical meaning of the "offset parameter" and why it should be a summation of the original weights and the average change is not adequately justified. The authors should clarify the mathematical and conceptual basis for this formulation to strengthen the theoretical foundation of their approach.

Second, while the paper presents strong empirical results, the comparison with baseline methods uses a subset of the validation data (val2) rather than the official test set. As noted by the authors in footnote 1, the small size of val2 makes it difficult to establish statistical significance for the reported performance gains. The authors should either use the full test set for comparison or provide stronger statistical evidence for their claims.

Pro: 
- Addresses a significant problem in scaling object detection to large categories
- Demonstrates strong empirical results with a 50% relative mAP improvement
- Provides a practical solution with a 7,604-category detector
- Well-written and clearly organized
- Properly contextualizes contributions within relevant literature

Con:
- Theoretical justification for the adaptation method (Section 3.2) requires strengthening
- Statistical significance of results is not fully established due to validation set limitations
- The comparison with baseline methods does not use the official test set

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to object detection. The LSDA framework offers a practical solution to a significant problem in the field, with clear potential for real-world impact. With minor improvements to the theoretical justification and statistical validation, this work would be an excellent candidate for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address these two points in the camera-ready version.
