This paper presents a method for adapting a deep convolutional neural network (CNN), initially trained for image-level classification, to the task of object detection. The approach is designed for scenarios where bounding box annotations are available for only a small subset of classes. The training process consists of three stages: 1) A CNN is trained for classification on images of all classes, using only image-level labels. 2) A separate CNN is trained on specific image regions for the subset of classes that have bounding box annotations, which includes a negative 'background' class. 3) The background class is then incorporated into the full classification CNN, and a subset of the network's weights are fine-tuned. At test time, the resulting network is applied to multiple regions within a test image to perform detection.

**Quality:** This is an interesting paper that presents compelling, reasonable results. This success is largely built upon the effectiveness of the Krizhevsky et al. model rather than a radically new approach; however, the findings will be valuable to researchers in computer vision and machine learning. The general method is simple but logical. While the experiments are promising, they lack crucial comparisons to several other baselines:

1.  A method that uses full images as positive examples and images/regions from other classes as negative examples to train and fine-tune all layers of the CNN.
2.  A Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, where the highest-scoring region within each positive image is iteratively selected for training, instead of using the full image.
3.  A simple baseline where the classification CNN is used at test time to predict a single, fixed bounding box at the center of the image (e.g., using the mean bounding box size and location from the training data).

It is unclear whether the proposed approach would outperform these baselines. The justification for the proposed method over baseline 1 is not immediately obvious, and it seems arguably less principled than the MIL approach in baseline 2. Including the third baseline would be particularly valuable for calibrating the difficulty of the task and the dataset for the reader.

**Clarity:** The paper is clear and well-written. However, the clarity of the experimental section could be improved. Specifically, the descriptions of the methods presented in Table 1 are ambiguous. It would be beneficial for the authors to elaborate on what each method entails—for instance, does a given method refer to applying Equation 1 or 2, and to which subset of the CNN layers?

**Originality:** The work is sufficiently novel for publication, even though the core approach is straightforward and tailored to a specific CNN architecture.

**Significance:** The problem and the results are significant and valuable. The paper tackles a relevant and important problem, and the achievement of decent detection results on a large-scale dataset using primarily weak image-level labels is compelling. However, due to the missing baselines, it remains possible that the strong performance is primarily due to the power of the CNN features themselves, rather than the novelty of the proposed training methodology for weakly supervised object detection.
