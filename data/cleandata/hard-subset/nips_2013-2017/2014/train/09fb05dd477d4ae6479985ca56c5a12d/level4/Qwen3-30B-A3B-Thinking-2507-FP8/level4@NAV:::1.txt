

This paper tackles object detection by circumventing the challenge of acquiring bounding boxes at the granularity of category labels used in object categorization. The authors propose adapting object classifiers for detection, yielding a method that is straightforward and well-motivated. Their evaluation trains on 100 categories with both labels and bounding boxes, then tests on 100 held-out categories with only labels. Results demonstrate a 35% gap closure between an oracle and a baseline on these held-out categories.

Quality and clarity: This is a well-written, high-quality contribution.

Originality: The work integrates two established adaptation concepts—classifier adaptation for detection and cross-category adaptation. To the reviewer’s knowledge, this combination is novel and interesting, though not ground-breaking.

Significance: The primary contribution is this novel (but not transformative) approach. While long-term significance is uncertain as results will likely be surpassed by more advanced methods (which is expected), the core limitation lies in the inherent performance trade-off: reduced mAP due to fewer bounding boxes, with no objective metric to evaluate if this trade-off is justified. Additionally, the fundamental problem addressed—bounding box acquisition cost versus label acquisition—may not represent a critical long-term challenge; historical precedent shows that resource-intensive labeling (e.g., millions of images across 20,000 categories) has become feasible.

Detailed issues requiring resolution:
- Figure 3a must explicitly clarify which methods (beyond the proposed approach and R-CNN) utilize category labels for detection to ensure an apples-to-apples comparison.
- The key result (blue bars in Figure 3b) shows DNN achieving ~1/3 of the gap between baseline and oracle on held-out categories. The authors should emphasize this rather than the 78% claim (line 341), which is misleading: (1) it should reference only held-out categories, and (2) it incorrectly uses oracle performance without baseline context. Similarly, the 50% improvement claim (line 425) requires correction.
- A dedicated table or subsection must clearly contrast the proposed method with R-CNN (the closest competitor), moving beyond scattered references. Figure 6 (anecdotal examples) should be replaced with this comparison.

Minor issues (no response needed):
- Inconsistent terminology: Is the method "DNN" or "DDA"?
- Typo on line 431 ("classi?ers").

Overall, this is a solid, novel paper with a simple approach. While results will likely be surpassed, the method’s novelty as the first to pursue this path justifies acceptance for the NIPS community. The long-term significance of the approach remains open for evaluation.
