### **Summary**

This paper addresses the significant challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, for which bounding box annotations are prohibitively expensive to collect. The authors propose Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a method to transform an image classifier (trained on abundant image-level labels) into an object detector. The core idea is to treat this transformation as a domain adaptation problem. Using a subset of categories that have both classification and detection (bounding box) labels, the method learns a general mapping from the classification network to a detection network. This learned transformation is then applied to classifiers for novel categories that lack any bounding box data.

The method consists of three main steps. First, a deep convolutional neural network (CNN) is trained for K-way classification using image-level labels for all categories of interest. Second, using the subset of categories with bounding box data, the shared feature-extraction layers of the network (conv1-fc7) are fine-tuned for the detection task. This step introduces a background class and learns a *category-invariant* adaptation. Finally, a *category-specific* adaptation is performed on the final classifier layer for the held-out categories by transferring the average parameter changes from their nearest-neighbor classes in the annotated set. Experiments on the ILSVRC2013 dataset show that LSDA significantly improves detection performance on held-out categories (a 50% relative mAP gain) compared to using the initial classifier directly.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Ambition:** The paper tackles a crucial and highly relevant problem. The prohibitive cost of bounding box annotation is a primary bottleneck for creating large-scale object detectors. This work proposes a practical and scalable solution, demonstrating its potential by building a detector for over 7,600 categories. The ideas presented could be highly influential for researchers and practitioners aiming to expand the scope of object detection systems.

2.  **Novelty and Elegance of the Approach:** Framing the problem as learning a transferable "classifier-to-detector" transformation is a novel and insightful contribution. While it builds upon the success of CNNs and the R-CNN framework, the core idea of adapting models for categories without any instance-level supervision is original. The distinction between category-invariant and category-specific adaptation is logical and well-motivated.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates a substantial performance gain (+50% relative mAP) on held-out categories over a strong baseline (using the fine-tuned classifier directly on region proposals). The ablation study in Figure 3 and Table 1 effectively demonstrates the contribution of each component of the LSDA algorithm, showing that the category-invariant feature adaptation is the most critical step. The qualitative results and error analysis (Figures 4 and 5) compellingly show that the adaptation specifically helps reduce localization and background confusion errors, which is precisely the goal.

### **Weaknesses**

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its experimental evaluation, which, while showing impressive gains over the authors' chosen baseline, omits comparisons to other simple yet important baselines. This makes it difficult to fully ascertain whether the performance gains are uniquely attributable to the proposed adaptation technique or are largely a result of leveraging powerful pre-trained CNN features in a weakly-supervised setting. The evaluation would be significantly strengthened by including the following comparisons:

1.  **Weakly-Supervised Learning (WSL) Baseline:** A standard WSL approach could be implemented for the held-out categories (Set A). For example, fine-tune the entire network using the full images from Set A as positive examples and random patches from other images as negative examples. This would establish a more direct point of comparison for training detectors from image-level labels.

2.  **Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) Baseline:** As the authors note, MIL is a complementary and highly relevant paradigm. A strong baseline would involve using an MIL-based fine-tuning approach for categories in Set A. In this setup, for each positive image, one could iteratively select the highest-scoring region proposal as the positive training example. Given that the paper mentions MIL in the related work, its absence in the experiments is a notable omission.

3.  **Simple Localization Baseline:** To better contextualize the difficulty of the task and the performance of the initial classification network, a very simple baseline would be valuable. For instance, after running the classifier on the full image, one could simply predict a bounding box of a canonical size/aspect ratio (e.g., the mean from the ImageNet dataset) at the image center. This would help quantify how much of the challenge lies in localization versus recognition.

Without these comparisons, it remains possible that a more direct weakly-supervised fine-tuning approach could perform comparably to LSDA, which would temper the claims about the necessity of the proposed adaptation method.

### **Minor Comments on Clarity**

The paper is generally clear and well-written. However, the presentation of the experimental results could be slightly improved.

*   In Section 4.2 and Table 1, the labels for the different adaptation strategies ("Background", "+FC6,7", "+Conv1-5") are very concise. While understandable from the context, explicitly stating which layers were frozen and which were fine-tuned for each condition in the main text would enhance clarity and reproducibility for the reader.

### **Conclusion**

This paper presents a novel and significant contribution towards scaling object detection to thousands of classes. The core idea of learning an adaptable transformation from a classifier to a detector is compelling, and the results are promising. However, the work in its current form is weakened by the lack of comparisons to several relevant and straightforward baselines from the weakly-supervised and multiple-instance learning literature. Including such comparisons would provide a much stronger validation of the proposed LSDA method. Despite this limitation, the paper addresses an important problem with an elegant solution and is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community. I lean towards acceptance but strongly encourage the authors to address the missing baselines in their final version.
