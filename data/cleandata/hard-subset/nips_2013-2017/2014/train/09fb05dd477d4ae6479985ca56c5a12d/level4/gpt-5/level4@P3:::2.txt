Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses a key bottleneck in scaling object detection: the scarcity of bounding-box annotations relative to abundant image-level labels. The proposed LSDA framework learns to transform an ImageNet-trained classifier into a detector using limited detection annotations. The method casts classification-to-detection as domain adaptation. It performs (1) category-invariant adaptation by fine-tuning shared layers (including learning an explicit background class) on a subset of categories with bounding boxes, and (2) category-specific adaptation by adding a learned residual (δ) to the per-class output weights (fc8) for categories with detection data, then transferring the average δ from nearest labeled neighbors to held-out categories. Detection scores are computed by subtracting the background score from per-class scores. Experiments on ILSVRC2013 evaluate held-out categories and demonstrate a ~50% relative mAP gain over using classification weights directly on region proposals. The authors also demonstrate a 7.6K-category detector using only classification labels for most categories and provide a faster variant with SPP and fast proposals.

The work builds on and extends R-CNN (Girshick et al.) by learning a generic transformation from classification to detection that can be transferred to categories without detection data. It is complementary to weakly supervised/MIL approaches (e.g., Song et al.; Ali and Saenko) and to prior domain adaptation methods that generally assume detection labels for all categories. The idea of learning a background class and adapting shared features is consistent with observations from OverFeat and R-CNN; the new contribution is adapting to held-out categories via a residual transfer.

Strengths
- Importance and scope: Tackles a central problem—scaling detection to thousands of categories with limited bounding-box supervision. The 7.6K detector and released models/code increase impact.
- Simplicity and scalability: The approach is straightforward to implement in standard CNN toolchains and computationally efficient (fine-tuning hours rather than days; faster test-time variant provided).
- Empirical support: Clear ablations demonstrate contributions of background modeling and shared-layer fine-tuning; measured improvements on held-out categories are substantial. The error analysis usefully shows reductions in localization and background confusions.
- Novelty: Adapting to categories with no detection labels via nearest-neighbor transfer of a learned residual appears new relative to prior adaptation/detection work.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
Quality
- Justification of the category-specific adaptation: Section 3.2 introduces an additive update W^d_i = W^c_i + δ_i and, for held-out categories, W^d_j = W^c_j + avg_k(δ_neighbors). The paper does not provide a principled rationale for the choice of an additive residual at the output layer, why the residual should transfer across semantically or visually similar classes, or why L2-nearest neighbors in the fc8 space are appropriate. Suggestions:
  - Provide intuition (e.g., a first-order Taylor approximation of fine-tuning around the classification solution suggests an additive residual; or an empirical observation that δ correlates with class similarity).
  - Analyze δ statistics: sparsity, magnitude, alignment across classes, and correlation with class similarity measures (WordNet, visual confusion).
  - Compare neighbor definitions (fc7 embeddings, confusion matrices, WordNet distances) and report sensitivity to k (including k=1, 5, 10, |B|) and to the choice of similarity metric.
  - Explore alternative transforms (per-class affine transforms, low-rank/shared factors, or meta-learned regressors predicting δ from class embeddings).
- Evaluation protocol and fairness of comparisons: Most comparisons are on val2 rather than the ILSVRC test server, and the split is relatively small. The claim that performance is “similar” to R-CNN on val2 is plausible but does not establish comparability.
  - Please provide test-server results if feasible, or at least cross-validated results across multiple random splits of val for robustness and confidence intervals.
  - Clarify whether R-CNN/SVM and bbox regression were included for the baselines. Since you omit SVMs/bbox regression for LSDA (to keep adaptation simple), a matched comparison to an R-CNN variant without SVMs/regression would be fairest. Alternatively, report both to bracket the performance trade-offs.
- Bounding-box regression for held-out categories: Consider training a category-agnostic regressor on the categories with boxes and applying it to held-out categories. This could reduce localization error further and is consistent with your category-invariant adaptation rationale.
- Sensitivity to the choice of B: How does performance on A vary with which categories are labeled and with m? A study over different B subsets (stratified by visual/semantic diversity) would strengthen the generalization claim.

Clarity
- Section 3.2 could more explicitly define notation and correct minor inconsistencies (e.g., A = {m+1,…,K}). A short schematic contrasting classification vs. detection logits and the role of the background subtraction (score_i − score_bg) would help.
- Briefly justify the score calibration choice (subtracting background) versus using softmax probabilities, and report any calibration effects on mAP.

Originality
- While the category-invariant adaptation via fine-tuning is expected, the held-out category adaptation via neighbor-averaged residuals is novel and practically useful. Related work is adequately cited, though a short comparison to weakly supervised detectors on a shared small-scale benchmark (e.g., PASCAL) would provide additional context.

Significance
- The demonstrated gains on held-out categories and the 7.6K detector suggest practical utility. Releasing models/code increases the likelihood of adoption. Stronger evaluation (test-server or multi-split) would further solidify the contribution.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a simple, effective method.
- Clear empirical gains and insightful error analysis.
- Scales to thousands of categories; resources released to the community.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient justification for the additive residual and neighbor transfer; limited exploration of alternatives.
- Comparisons rely on a small validation split; lack of test-server results and fully matched baselines.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to the choice and size of labeled categories.

Requested revisions
- Provide a principled motivation and empirical analysis for the additive δ and the kNN transfer; report sensitivity to k and alternative similarity metrics.
- Strengthen evaluation with test-server results or multi-split validation and matched baselines (with and without SVMs/bbox regression).
- Consider adding a category-agnostic bbox regressor trained on B and applied to A, and report its effect on localization errors.
- Clarify notation and the rationale for score calibration via background subtraction.

Overall assessment
This is a well-written paper on an important problem, with a simple idea that yields meaningful gains and scales broadly. With stronger justification of the category-specific adaptation and a more robust evaluation, it would be a solid contribution. I am positive about the paper’s impact and lean toward acceptance, contingent on the clarifications and additional experiments above.
