This paper addresses the challenge of object detection, specifically the issue of obtaining bounding boxes on a scale similar to which category labels exist for object categorization. The authors propose to adapt object classifiers for the detection task, sidestepping this challenge. Their algorithm is straightforward, combining two existing ideas related to adaptation: adapting object classifiers for detection, and adapting from one set of categories to another.

Quality and clarity: This is a well-written paper that effectively conveys its ideas. The authors clearly explain their approach, and the experimental protocol is well-described.

Originality: While the paper's approach is novel, it is not groundbreaking. The combination of adapting object classifiers for detection and adapting from one set of categories to another is an interesting idea, but it builds upon existing concepts.

Significance: The main contribution of this paper is its novel approach. However, it is challenging to judge the long-term significance of the results, as they may be surpassed by more sophisticated methods in the future. The main issue with the results is that they inevitably sacrifice performance (mAP) by using fewer bounding boxes, and there is no objective way to judge if this trade-off is worth it.

One of the primary concerns with the results is that they may not address a critical long-term challenge. While bounding boxes are more "expensive" to obtain than category labels, it is unclear whether this issue is a significant obstacle that cannot be overcome by allocating more resources.

Detailed issues that should be addressed:

* In Figure 3a, the authors should clearly state which methods use category labels to help with detection, other than their method and R-CNN, to ensure an apples-to-apples comparison.
* The authors should highlight the blue bars in Figure 3b, which show that their approach is approximately one-third between the baseline and oracle. This is a more accurate representation of their results than the 78% mentioned in line 341.
* The authors should devote a table or subsection to clearly stating the differences between their approach and R-CNN, which appears to be the closest competing alternative.

Minor issues:

* The proposed method is referred to as both "DNN" and "DDA," which may cause confusion.
* There is a typo on line 431 ("classi?ers").

Overall, this is a reasonably good paper proposing a novel approach. While the results may be surpassed soon, the long-term significance of the paper and the approach remains to be seen. The paper deserves to be accepted and seen by the NIPS community.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to object detection that combines two existing ideas related to adaptation.
* The algorithm is straightforward and easy to understand.
* The experimental protocol is well-described, and the results are clearly presented.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper's approach may not be groundbreaking, and the results may be surpassed by more sophisticated methods in the future.
* The main issue with the results is that they inevitably sacrifice performance (mAP) by using fewer bounding boxes, and there is no objective way to judge if this trade-off is worth it.
* The paper may not address a critical long-term challenge, as the issue of obtaining bounding boxes may be overcome by allocating more resources.
