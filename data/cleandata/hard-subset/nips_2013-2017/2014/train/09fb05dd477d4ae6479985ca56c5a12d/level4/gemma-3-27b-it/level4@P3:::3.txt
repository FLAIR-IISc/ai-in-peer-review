This paper presents a method for adapting a convolutional neural network (CNN) pre-trained for classification – leveraging the abundance of image-level labels – to the task of object detection, particularly for categories lacking bounding box annotations. The approach, termed Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), fine-tunes a Krizhevsky et al. [4] network using a combination of categories with and without bounding box labels, learning a transformation to convert classifiers into detectors. The method incorporates category-invariant and category-specific adaptation techniques, and demonstrates performance gains on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge, even enabling the creation of a detector for over 7,600 categories.

This work builds on the recent success of deep CNNs for visual recognition, particularly the adaptation of ImageNet classifiers for detection as demonstrated by Girshick et al. [1]. While not proposing a radically new architectural innovation, the paper offers a valuable contribution by addressing the critical issue of scaling object detection to a large number of categories where labeled bounding box data is scarce. The core idea of learning a transformation between classification and detection tasks is logical and potentially impactful. The experimental results are promising, and the availability of models and software is commendable.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant problem:** The scarcity of bounding box annotations for large-scale object detection is a major bottleneck. This paper directly tackles this challenge.
* **Practical approach:** The LSDA algorithm is relatively straightforward to implement and builds upon existing, well-established CNN architectures and training techniques.
* **Demonstrated effectiveness:** The experiments on ImageNet LSVRC-2013 show a significant improvement (+50% relative mAP) over a baseline using raw classifier weights, demonstrating the efficacy of the proposed adaptation method.
* **Scalability:** The ability to generate a detector for 7,600+ categories is impressive and highlights the potential for scaling up object detection.
* **Publicly available resources:** Providing code and models fosters reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited baseline comparisons:** The paper lacks comparison to several important baselines that would strengthen the evaluation of LSDA. Specifically, the following baselines would be valuable:
    1. Fine-tuning the entire CNN on positive images and using negative samples from other classes.
    2. Implementing a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, selecting the highest-scoring region per image in the absence of bounding boxes.
    3. Directly applying the pre-trained classifier to the image, predicting a single bounding box (e.g., the mean bounding box) at the image center.
* **Clarity of ablation study:** The description of the ablation study in Table 1 could be improved. It is not immediately clear what each method represents in terms of applying the proposed adaptation techniques to specific layers of the CNN. More detailed explanation regarding Equation 1 and 2 would be beneficial.
* **Potential for CNN feature bias:** The observed performance gains could be partially attributed to the inherent power of CNN features rather than the specific adaptation strategy employed by LSDA. The missing baselines would help disentangle these effects.
* **Error analysis limitations:** While the error analysis provides some insights, it primarily focuses on the types of errors made. A more detailed analysis of *why* these errors occur could be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and relevant problem in object detection.
* The proposed method demonstrates a clear performance improvement over a reasonable baseline.
* The approach is practical and builds upon well-established techniques.
* The availability of code and models promotes reproducibility.
* The potential impact of scaling object detection to a large number of categories is considerable.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of comparison to key baselines weakens the evaluation and makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of LSDA.
* The potential for CNN feature bias is not adequately addressed.
* The clarity of the ablation study could be improved.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that addresses an important problem in the field of object detection. While the lack of comprehensive baseline comparisons is a concern, the promising results and the practical nature of the proposed approach warrant consideration for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the baseline comparisons and clarifying the ablation study details. The authors should also discuss the potential influence of CNN feature representations on the observed performance gains.



**Rating:** Accept (with minor revisions)
