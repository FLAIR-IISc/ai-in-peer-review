Summary
The paper tackles the challenge of scaling object detection when bounding box annotations are scarce relative to image-level category labels. The proposed LSDA method adapts classifiers into detectors by learning a transformation from classification to detection using a subset of categories that have bounding boxes, and then transferring that transformation to categories with only image-level labels. The approach is framed as domain adaptation: classification data are the source domain, detection data the target. Concretely, the method (1) learns a background class and fine-tunes shared layers to induce a category-invariant shift from classification to detection, and (2) approximates category-specific adaptation for categories without boxes by averaging the changes observed in their nearest neighbors (in fc8) among categories with boxes. The authors evaluate on the ILSVRC-2013 detection task, training with detection data for 100 categories and testing on 100 held-out categories that have only classification labels. They report sizable gains over using raw classifier weights on region proposals, and also scale the approach to a 7.6K-category detector, with a faster variant using SPP and fast proposals.

Relation to prior work: The approach builds on R-CNN (fine-tuning a classification network for detection), but differs by requiring detection labels only for a subset of categories and transferring to the remainder. It contrasts with weakly supervised MIL methods, which do not use detection labels, and with existing domain adaptation techniques that typically assume labeled target data for all categories or adapt only shallow models/features.

Quality and Clarity
- The paper is technically sound and clearly written. The ablation study is informative, showing the contributions of background modeling, shared-layer fine-tuning, and category-specific output adaptation.
- The experimental protocol (100 train-with-box categories, 100 held-out categories) is reasonable for simulating the intended use case. The error analysis (localization, background confusion, other) is helpful.

Originality
- The combination of adapting from classification to detection and transferring across categories without detection labels is, to my knowledge, novel and interesting. It is not a radical departure from existing fine-tuning and adaptation paradigms, but represents a meaningful and practical extension.

Significance
- The contribution is primarily methodological: a simple, scalable approach that reduces reliance on bounding boxes. Empirically, on the held-out categories, the method fills about 35% of the gap between the baseline (classification weights on proposals) and an oracle with full detection data. This is promising, though likely to be surpassed by more sophisticated methods.
- The practical significance depends on whether the community adopts reduced-box regimes at scale. While bounding boxes are indeed more costly than image-level labels, it is unclear how central this constraint will remain as resources and annotation tools improve.

Pros
- Clear problem statement and simple, effective method.
- Strong ablation demonstrating that category-invariant adaptations (shared layers and background) drive most gains; category-specific output adaptation provides a smaller but measurable boost.
- Demonstration at 7.6K categories and a faster implementation; code/models released.

Cons
- The empirical gains come with an inevitable mAP sacrifice relative to full supervision; the paper does not provide an objective framework to judge whether this trade-off is acceptable for practical applications.
- Some claims (e.g., percentage improvements) would benefit from clearer framing relative to the baseline-oracle gap on held-out categories only.
- Differences from R-CNN, the closest baseline, are not consolidated in one place.

Detailed issues to address
- Figure 3a: Please clarify which methods (beyond LSDA and R-CNN) do or do not use category labels to aid detection, so the comparisons are strictly apples-to-apples.
- Figure 3b: My assessment of your main empirical result is based on the blue bars showing that DNN sits at roughly one-third of the way from baseline to oracle on held-out categories. Do you agree this is a key takeaway? If so, please highlight this, rather than the “78%” figure (line 341), which is less appropriate for two reasons: (1) evaluation should focus on the held-out categories, and (2) improvements should be measured relative to the baseline-oracle gap, not simply as a percentage of oracle. The same concern applies to the “50% improvement” statement (line 425).
- Please add a table or subsection that explicitly contrasts LSDA with R-CNN (training requirements, use of background class, use of SVMs/bbox regression, runtime, and data assumptions). This would be more useful than Figure 6, which shows anecdotal detections and could be omitted.

Minor issues (no response required)
- Naming: Is the proposed method referred to as “DNN” or “DDA” in the figures/text? Please make the nomenclature consistent with “LSDA.”
- Typo at line 431 (“classi?ers”).

Recommendation
Overall, this is a solid paper with a reasonably novel approach and clear empirical support. While the long-term impact remains uncertain and the accuracy trade-offs are context-dependent, the idea is interesting and timely for large-scale detection. I recommend acceptance.
