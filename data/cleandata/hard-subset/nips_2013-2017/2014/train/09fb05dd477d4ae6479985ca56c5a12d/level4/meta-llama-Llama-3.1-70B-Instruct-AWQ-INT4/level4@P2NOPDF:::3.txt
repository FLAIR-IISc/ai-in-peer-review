This paper proposes a method for adapting the Krizhevsky CNN network, trained for classification without bounding box annotations, to perform object detection with a small number of classes having annotated bounding boxes. The approach involves three main steps during training: 1) training a CNN for classification on images of all classes, 2) training a CNN on image regions of classes labeled with bounding boxes, including a negative background region class, and 3) incorporating the background class into the full CNN model while adapting some of the CNN weights. At test time, the CNN is applied to multiple image regions in a test image.

Quality: The paper presents an interesting and fairly compelling method that achieves reasonable results, largely building upon the success of Krizhevsky et al.'s model. Although the approach is not radically new, it will be valuable to researchers in computer vision and machine learning. The general approach is logical, but its simplicity raises questions about its novelty. The experiments show promise, but the absence of comparisons to other baselines, such as using full images as positive examples and images/regions from other classes as negative examples, is notable. Other potential baselines that could be considered include using a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, where the highest-scoring positive region is iteratively selected for each image, and training a classifier to predict a single bounding box at the center of the image. It is unclear whether the proposed approach would outperform these baselines, and including them would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the problem and dataset.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and clear, but the experiments section could be improved with more detailed explanations of the methods presented in Table 1. Specifically, it would be helpful to elaborate on whether the methods refer to applying Eq. 1 or 2 to a subset of CNN layers.

Originality: Although the basic approach is simple and specific to certain types of CNN networks, the paper is sufficiently novel to be considered for acceptance.

Significance: The problem addressed in the paper is relevant, and the results are significant and valuable. The fact that decent detection results were obtained on a large-scale dataset without bounding box labels is compelling. However, the paper's failure to include obvious baselines and the possibility that the results are largely due to improved CNN features rather than a novel training approach for object detectors from weak supervision are notable limitations.
