- Paper proposes adapting a Krizhevsky CNN trained for classification to detection using a small set of bbox-labeled classes via three steps: classification training, region-based training with a background class, and integration/adaptation of weights for the full model; test uses multiple image regions.
- Approach details: train CNN on full-image categories, fine-tune on image regions for bbox classes including negative background regions, and incorporate background into the full CNN before testing on region proposals.
- Quality: promising and compelling results; method is logical and straightforward but builds heavily on Krizhevsky et al. rather than introducing a radical new idea.
- Major experimental weaknesses: lacks comparisons to important baselines that could challenge the claimed advantages.
- Suggested baseline 1: fine-tune all CNN layers using full images as positives and image regions from other classes as negatives.
- Suggested baseline 2: a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach that iteratively selects highest-scoring regions from unlabeled images and trains.
- Suggested baseline 3: train for classification and at test time predict a single bounding box per image (e.g., mean ImageNet box) to test if localization adds value.
- Key concern: unclear whether improvements come from the specific adaptation technique or simply from stronger CNN features; need baselines to determine true gains.
- Clarity: paper is generally clear, but experiments/Table 1 need more detail—specifically which CNN layers Equations 1/2 apply to.
- Originality: novel enough for acceptance but relatively simple and tailored to specific CNN architectures.
- Significance: important problem and meaningful results (detection with limited bbox annotations), but lack of baseline comparisons weakens the impact.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses scalability of detection, shows clear improvement over raw classifier weights, and provides code/models for reproducibility.
- Arguments against acceptance: missing crucial baselines, possible over-attribution of gains to the method rather than CNN features, and error analysis is not sufficiently deep.
