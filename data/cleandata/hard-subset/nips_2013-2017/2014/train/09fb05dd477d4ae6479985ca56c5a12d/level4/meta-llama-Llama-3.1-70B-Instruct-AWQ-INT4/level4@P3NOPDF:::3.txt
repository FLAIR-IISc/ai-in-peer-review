This paper presents a method for adapting a Krizhevsky CNN network, trained for classification without bounding box annotations, to work for object detection given a small number of classes with annotated bounding boxes. The approach involves three key steps at train time: 1) training a CNN for classification on images of all classes, 2) training a CNN on image regions of classes labeled with bounding boxes while adding a negative background region class, and 3) adding the background class to the full CNN model while adapting some of the CNN weights. At test time, the CNN is run on multiple image regions in a test image.

Quality: This paper is interesting and fairly compelling, achieving reasonable results by building upon the success of Krizhevsky et al.'s model. While the approach is not radically new, it will be valuable to researchers in computer vision and machine learning. The general approach is simple yet logical. The experiments are promising, but they lack comparisons to other baselines. To strengthen the paper, the authors should consider including the following baselines: 

1) Using full images as positive examples and images and regions from other classes as negative examples, then training and fine-tuning all layers of the CNN.
2) Adopting a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, where the highest-scoring positive region for each image from set A (instead of the full image) is iteratively selected.
3) Training and fine-tuning the CNN for classification, then running the classifier at test time to predict a single bounding box at the center of the image (e.g., the mean bounding box for ImageNet).

Including these baselines, especially the third one, is crucial to convey the ease or difficulty of the problem and dataset to the reader. It is unclear whether the proposed approach would outperform these baselines, and it is possible that it makes less sense than baseline 2.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and clear, but the clarity of the experiments section can be improved. Specifically, the explanation of each method in Table 1 could be elaborated upon, clarifying whether it refers to applying Eq. 1 or 2 to a subset of CNN layers.

Originality: The paper is sufficiently novel to be accepted, although the basic approach is simple and specific to certain types of CNN networks.

Significance: The problem and results are significant and valuable. The paper addresses a relevant problem, and the fact that decent detection results were obtained on a large-scale dataset without bounding box labels is compelling. However, the paper is missing some obvious baselines, and it is possible that the results are capitalizing on improved CNN features rather than introducing a better way of training object detectors from weak supervision.
