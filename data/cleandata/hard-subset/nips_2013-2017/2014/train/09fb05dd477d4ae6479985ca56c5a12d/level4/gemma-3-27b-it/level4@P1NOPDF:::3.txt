This paper presents a method for adapting a Krizhevsky CNN, pre-trained for classification (without bounding box annotations), to perform object detection with a limited number of classes that *do* have bounding box annotations. The approach involves three training stages: 1) training a CNN for classification on images of all classes; 2) training a CNN on image regions belonging to classes with bounding box labels, incorporating a negative background region class; and 3) integrating the background class into the full CNN model while adapting some of the CNN’s weights. During testing, the CNN is applied to multiple image regions within a test image.

Quality: This is an interesting and reasonably convincing paper, achieving solid results. These results largely build upon the success of the Krizhevsky et al. model, rather than presenting a radically new contribution; however, they will be valuable to researchers in computer vision and machine learning. The general approach is simple and logical. The experiments are promising, but lack comparison to several important baselines:

1) Treat full images as positive examples and images/regions from other classes as negative examples. Train and fine-tune all layers of the CNN.
2) Employ a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, iteratively selecting the highest-scoring positive region for each image from the dataset, instead of using the full image.
3) Train and fine-tune the CNN for classification. At test time, run the classifier and predict a single bounding box centered in the image (e.g., using the mean bounding box from ImageNet).

I am uncertain whether the proposed approach would outperform these baselines. It’s unclear whether it offers a significant advantage over baseline 1, and it likely performs worse than baseline 2. Including the third baseline would be crucial to help readers understand the inherent difficulty of the problem and dataset.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-written. The clarity of the experiments section could be improved, particularly regarding the explanation of what each method in Table 1 entails – specifically, does applying Equation 1 or 2 refer to a subset of the CNN layers?

Originality: The paper is sufficiently novel for acceptance, although the core approach is relatively simple and specific to certain CNN architectures.

Significance: The problem addressed and the results obtained are significant and valuable. 

The paper tackles a relevant problem, and obtaining reasonable detection results on a large-scale dataset without bounding box labels is a compelling achievement. However, the paper omits several obvious baselines, and it’s possible the observed results capitalize on improvements in CNN features rather than stemming from a genuinely better method for training object detectors from weak supervision.
