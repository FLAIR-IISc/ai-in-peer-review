This paper addresses the issue of object detection, particularly the challenge of obtaining bounding boxes on a scale similar to the availability of category labels for object categorization. The authors sidestep this challenge by proposing to adapt object classifiers for the detection task. Their algorithm is fairly simple and straightforward, which is not inherently a drawback. The experimental protocol utilizes 100 categories for training, which include both category labels and bounding boxes, and tests on 100 left-out categories that only have category labels. The results obtained were reasonably good, filling approximately 35% of the gap between an oracle and a baseline on the 100 left-out categories.

Quality and clarity: This is a well-written paper of good quality.

Originality: This paper combines two existing ideas related to adaptation: adapting object classifiers for detection and adapting from one set of categories to another. To the best of this reviewer's knowledge, this is a novel and interesting idea, although not groundbreaking.

Significance: The main contribution of this paper is its novel approach, which, while not groundbreaking, is still noteworthy. However, judging the long-term significance of the results is challenging, and they will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods in the future. The primary issue with the results is that they inevitably sacrifice performance (mAP) by using fewer bounding boxes, and there is no objective way to determine if this trade-off is worthwhile.

It is also worth noting that the importance of the issue being tackled is debatable. While bounding boxes are indeed more expensive to obtain than category labels, it is unclear whether throwing resources at the problem would not be a viable solution. In the past, having millions of images labeled with 20,000 object categories seemed almost impossible, yet it has become a reality.

Detailed issues that should be addressed:

* In Figure 3a, the authors should clearly state which methods do or do not use category labels to aid detection, aside from their method and R-CNN, to facilitate an apples-to-apples comparison.
* This reviewer's assessment of the proposed approach is primarily based on the blue bars in Figure 3b, which indicate that DNN is approximately one-third of the way between the baseline and the oracle. The authors should acknowledge this as one of the main results and highlight it, rather than the 78% mentioned on line 341, which is not the correct number for two reasons: it should be based solely on the left-out categories, and it should not simply be a percentage of the oracle's performance, but rather take the baseline into account. A similar issue applies to the claim of a 50% improvement on line 425.
* The authors should devote a table or a subsection to clearly outlining the differences between their approach and R-CNN, which appears to be the closest competing alternative. Currently, these statements are scattered or not as prominent as they could be. Consider "sacrificing" Figure 6, which presents anecdotal examples.

Overall, this is a reasonably good paper proposing a reasonably novel approach. This reviewer believes the paper deserves to be accepted and presented to the NIPS community. While the results will likely be surpassed soon, the long-term significance of the paper and its approach remains to be seen.
