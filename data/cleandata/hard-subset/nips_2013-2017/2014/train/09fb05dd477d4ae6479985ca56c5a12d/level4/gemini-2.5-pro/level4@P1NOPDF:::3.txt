This paper presents a method for adapting a deep convolutional neural network (CNN), similar to that of Krizhevsky et al., from image classification to object detection. The key idea is to leverage a network trained for classification (without bounding box annotations) and adapt it for detection using only a small set of classes for which bounding box annotations are available.

The proposed training procedure involves three stages: 1) A CNN is trained for classification on the full set of classes using image-level labels. 2) A separate CNN is trained on image regions from the classes that have bounding box annotations, with the addition of a negative "background" class. 3) This background class is then integrated into the full CNN, and a subset of the network's weights are fine-tuned. At test time, the adapted CNN is applied to multiple regions within a test image to perform object detection.

**Quality**

This is an interesting paper that presents reasonably compelling results. The success of the method largely builds upon the powerful features learned by the underlying Krizhevsky et al. model rather than introducing a radically new contribution. Nevertheless, the findings will be valuable to researchers in computer vision and machine learning. The general approach is simple and logical, and the initial experiments are promising.

However, the experimental evaluation is weakened by the absence of several important baselines, making it difficult to properly contextualize the method's performance. I would suggest including comparisons to the following:

1.  A model trained using full images from the target classes as positive examples and images/regions from other classes as negative examples, with all layers of the CNN being fine-tuned.
2.  A similar approach to baseline 1, but incorporating a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) framework. In this setup, the model would iteratively select the highest-scoring region within each positive training image, rather than using the full image.
3.  A simple classification baseline where a fine-tuned CNN is used at test time to predict a single, fixed bounding box at the center of the image (e.g., using the mean bounding box size and location from the ImageNet dataset).

Without these comparisons, it is unclear whether the proposed approach would outperform these alternatives. The justification for the proposed method over baseline 1 is not obvious, and it seems potentially less principled than the MIL approach in baseline 2. Furthermore, including the third baseline would be valuable for conveying to the reader the inherent difficulty of the task and the dataset.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally clear and well-written. However, the clarity of the experiments section could be improved, particularly in its explanation of the methods listed in Table 1. For instance, it is not immediately clear what each method entails (e.g., does it involve applying Equation 1 or 2, and to which subset of CNN layers?). Elaborating on these details would strengthen the paper's presentation.

**Originality**

The work is novel enough for acceptance. While the core approach is straightforward and tailored to a specific type of CNN architecture, it presents a practical solution to a common and important problem.

**Significance**

The paper addresses the significant and relevant problem of adapting classification networks for detection with limited supervision. The ability to achieve decent detection results on a large-scale dataset without exhaustive bounding box labels is compelling.

That said, the main weakness of the paper remains the missing baselines. This omission makes it difficult to ascertain whether the reported success stems from the novelty of the proposed training strategy or is simply a consequence of capitalizing on powerful, pre-trained CNN features. The contribution would be much stronger if the authors could demonstrate a clear advantage over simpler, more direct methods for weakly supervised detector training.
