This paper proposes a method for adapting a Krizhevsky CNN network, originally trained for classification without bounding box annotations, to perform object detection with a limited number of classes having annotated bounding boxes. The approach involves a three-stage training process: 1) training a CNN for classification on images of all classes, 2) training a CNN on image regions of classes with bounding box annotations while introducing a negative background region class, and 3) incorporating the background class into the full CNN model and adapting some of the CNN weights. During testing, the CNN is applied to multiple image regions in a test image.

Quality: The paper presents an interesting and compelling method that achieves reasonable results. Although the success of this approach is largely built upon the foundation laid by Krizhevsky et al.'s model, it still holds value for researchers in computer vision and machine learning. The general approach is straightforward and logical, and the experimental results are promising. However, a comparison to other baselines is lacking, including:
1) Utilizing full images as positive examples and images and regions from other classes as negative examples, followed by training and fine-tuning all CNN layers.
2) Employing a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, where the highest-scoring positive region is iteratively selected for each image from set A.
3) Training and fine-tuning the CNN for classification, and at test time, running the classifier to predict a single bounding box at the center of the image.

It is uncertain whether the proposed approach would outperform these baselines. The proposed method may not be more effective than baseline 1, and it likely makes less sense than baseline 2. Including the third baseline would provide valuable context for the reader to understand the problem and dataset.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and clear. However, the clarity of the experiments section could be improved, particularly in explaining the methods listed in Table 1. It would be beneficial to elaborate on whether each method refers to applying Eq. 1 or 2 to a subset of CNN layers.

Originality: The paper is novel enough to warrant acceptance, despite the basic approach being simple and specific to certain types of CNN networks.

Significance: The problem addressed and the results obtained are significant and valuable. The paper tackles a relevant problem, and the decent detection results achieved on a large-scale dataset without bounding box labels are compelling. Nevertheless, the omission of obvious baselines and the potential capitalization on improved CNN features rather than introducing a better training method for object detectors from weak supervision are notable limitations.
