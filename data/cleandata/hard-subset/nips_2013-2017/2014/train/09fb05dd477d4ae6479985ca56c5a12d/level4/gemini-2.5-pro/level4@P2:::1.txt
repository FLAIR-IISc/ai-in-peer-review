This paper addresses the significant challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, where obtaining bounding box annotations is prohibitively expensive compared to collecting image-level category labels. The authors propose a novel method, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), which frames the problem as one of domain adaptation. The core idea is to learn a generic transformation from an image classifier to an object detector. This transformation is learned on a subset of categories for which both image labels and bounding box annotations are available. It is then applied to adapt classifiers for new categories that lack any bounding box data, effectively turning them into detectors.

The experimental evaluation is conducted on the ILSVRC-2013 dataset. The authors simulate a realistic scenario by using 100 categories with full detection annotations to train the adaptation model and then evaluate its performance on 100 held-out categories that were trained using only image-level labels.

**Quality and Clarity**

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and technically sound. The motivation is strong, and the proposed method is described with sufficient detail for understanding. The experimental setup is logical and effectively demonstrates the core contribution of the work. The ablation study in Figure 3 and the error analysis in Figure 5 are particularly effective in dissecting the components of the method and illustrating its impact on localization and background confusion errors.

**Originality**

The work's primary originality lies in its formulation of the classifier-to-detector problem. While domain adaptation and fine-tuning deep networks for detection (as in R-CNN) are established concepts, this paper uniquely combines them to transfer knowledge *across tasks* for *held-out categories*. To my knowledge, this is a novel approach to circumventing the need for exhaustive bounding box annotation, distinguishing it from prior work that typically requires at least some target-domain detection data for all categories.

**Significance**

The paper's contribution is significant as it proposes a practical solution to a major bottleneck in computer vision. The ability to generate detectors for thousands of categories without direct supervision for each is a compelling prospect. While the performance does not match a fully supervised "oracle," the results are promising. The key question this paper raises is the value of the trade-off between annotation cost and detection accuracy (mAP). While there may not be a single objective answer, the proposed method provides a new and valuable point in this trade-off space. The approach is simple and intuitive, increasing the likelihood that it will be adopted and built upon by other researchers.

**Strengths:**
*   **Novel Problem Formulation:** The idea of learning a general "classifier-to-detector" transformation that can be applied to categories without bounding box annotations is novel and addresses a critical, practical problem.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates a significant performance improvement over a reasonable baseline. Based on the results in Figure 3b, LSDA closes approximately one-third of the performance gap between the baseline classification network and the fully supervised oracle on the held-out categories. This is a substantial gain achieved without any bounding boxes for those categories.
*   **Clear and Simple Method:** The algorithm is straightforward and elegant, which makes the work easy to understand and likely to inspire follow-up research.
*   **Thorough Analysis:** The authors provide a good ablation study and a detailed error analysis that gives insight into *why* their adaptation method works (e.g., by reducing localization and background-confusion errors).

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Comparison with R-CNN:** The relationship with R-CNN [1] is central to the paper's contribution. While the text mentions the key differences, the paper would be strengthened by a more direct and consolidated comparison. I would suggest adding a table or a dedicated subsection that clearly contrasts LSDA and R-CNN in terms of data requirements, training stages, and performance. Such a comparison would be more valuable to the reader than the anecdotal examples in Figure 6, which could be moved to an appendix if space is a concern.
*   **Framing of Quantitative Improvements:** The claim of a "50% relative mAP boost" should be presented with more context. As mentioned above, a more insightful metric is the degree to which the method closes the performance gap between the baseline and a fully supervised oracle. Highlighting that the method bridges a significant portion of this gap would more accurately frame its contribution.
*   **Discussion on the Problem's Longevity:** The paper is motivated by the high cost of annotation. It would be beneficial to add a brief discussion on the long-term perspective of this problem. While it is a major bottleneck now, it is worth considering how this approach fits into a future where large-scale annotation efforts or other forms of weak supervision might become more common.

**Summary**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and practical approach to a significant problem in object detection. The method is simple yet effective, and the results clearly demonstrate its value. While the performance will likely be surpassed by more complex methods in the future, this work introduces a valuable new direction for research. The suggestions above are intended to further strengthen an already solid paper. I recommend acceptance.
