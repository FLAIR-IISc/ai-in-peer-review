This paper proposes a method to adapt an AlexNet-style classification CNN, trained without bounding-box annotations, for object detection when only a small subset of classes has bounding boxes. Training proceeds in three stages: (1) train a classification CNN on all classes; (2) train a region-level CNN on the classes with bounding boxes, adding a background (negative) class; and (3) incorporate the background class into the full CNN and adapt a subset of weights. At test time, the CNN is applied to multiple image regions in each test image.

Quality: The paper is interesting and presents a logical, straightforward approach that yields reasonable results. The gains appear to rely heavily on the strength of the Krizhevsky et al. model rather than on a fundamentally new architecture, but the findings should still be useful to the vision and ML communities. The experiments are promising, yet several natural baselines are missing:

- Baseline 1: Treat whole images of labeled classes as positives, and use images and regions from other classes as negatives. Train and fine-tune all CNN layers end-to-end.
- Baseline 2: As in Baseline 1, but use multiple-instance learning: for each positive image, iteratively select the highest-scoring region as the positive instance instead of using the full image.
- Baseline 3: Train and fine-tune the CNN purely for classification. At test time, run the classifier and predict a single fixed bounding box (e.g., the dataset’s mean box centered in the image).

It is not clear that the proposed method would outperform these alternatives. I am unconvinced it is stronger than Baseline 1 and suspect it may be weaker than the MIL-based Baseline 2. Including Baseline 3 would help calibrate task difficulty and contextualize the reported gains.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well written. The experimental section would benefit from more precise descriptions of what each variant in Table 1 entails—specifically, which CNN layers are adapted, and whether Eq. 1 or Eq. 2 is applied and to which layers. Clarifying these details would make the comparisons easier to interpret.

Originality: The contribution is sufficiently novel for acceptance, though the approach is simple and tailored to specific CNN architectures.

Significance: The problem is important, and demonstrating reasonable detection performance on a large-scale dataset without exhaustive bounding-box labels is compelling. That said, the absence of the obvious baselines above leaves open the possibility that the gains primarily reflect the strength of CNN features rather than a superior method for weakly supervised detector training. Including these comparisons would strengthen the paper’s conclusions.
