This paper proposes a method to adapt a Krizhevsky-style CNN trained for image classification (without bounding box annotations) to object detection, given a small number of classes with bounding boxes. Training proceeds as follows: (1) train a CNN for image-level classification on all classes; (2) train a CNN on image regions for the classes with bounding boxes, introducing a negative background-region class; and (3) add the background class to the full CNN while adapting a subset of the CNN weights. At test time, the CNN is applied to multiple image regions within each test image.

Quality: The paper is interesting and reasonably convincing in demonstrating solid results. These gains largely leverage the strength of Krizhevsky et al.’s model rather than introducing something radically new; nevertheless, the findings are valuable to the computer vision and machine learning communities. The overall approach is simple yet logical. The experiments are promising, but key baseline comparisons are missing:
1) Use full images as positive examples, and images and regions from other classes as negative examples. Train and fine-tune all CNN layers.
2) As above, but use a MIL approach that iteratively selects the highest-scoring positive region for each image from set A (instead of using the full image).
3) Train and fine-tune the CNN for classification. At test time, run the classifier and predict a single bounding box at the image center (e.g., the mean bounding box for ImageNet).

I am unsure whether the proposed approach would outperform these baselines. It is not clear that it makes more sense than baseline 1, and it likely makes less sense than baseline 2. Including baseline 3 is important to calibrate how easy or hard the problem and dataset are.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well written. The experiments section could be improved, particularly by elaborating on what each method in Table 1 refers to (e.g., does it involve applying Eq. 1 or Eq. 2 to a subset of CNN layers?).

Originality: The paper is sufficiently novel for acceptance, though the core idea is straightforward and tailored to certain CNN architectures.

Significance: The problem and results are significant and valuable. It is compelling that decent detection performance is achieved on a large-scale dataset without bounding box labels. At the same time, the omission of obvious baselines leaves open the possibility that the improvements primarily reflect stronger CNN features rather than a superior method for training object detectors from weak supervision.
