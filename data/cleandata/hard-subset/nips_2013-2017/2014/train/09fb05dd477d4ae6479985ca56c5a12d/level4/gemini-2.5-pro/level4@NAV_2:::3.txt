This paper introduces Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a method for adapting a convolutional neural network (CNN) trained for classification into an object detector. The core idea treats this conversion as a domain adaptation task. Using a subset of categories for which bounding box annotations are available, the algorithm learns a generic transformation from a classification network to a detection network. This learned mapping, which involves adapting feature representations and learning a background model, is then transferred to classifiers for a much larger set of categories that possess only image-level labels. At test time, the resulting adapted network is applied to region proposals to perform object detection.

**Quality:** The paper presents an interesting and technically sound approach, and the results demonstrating reasonable detection on a large scale are compelling. The general methodology is logical and builds effectively upon the success of prior work like R-CNN [1] and the Krizhevsky et al. [4] network. The experiments are promising; however, a significant weakness is the absence of comparisons to several key baselines, which makes it difficult to fully assess the method's contribution. The evaluation would be much stronger with comparisons to:
1.  A weakly-supervised approach using full images as positive training examples and images/regions from other classes as negatives, with all CNN layers fine-tuned accordingly.
2.  A Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) variant of the above, where for each positive image, the highest-scoring region proposal is iteratively selected for training.
3.  A simple baseline where a fine-tuned classifier predicts a canonical bounding box (e.g., at the image center) at test time. This would help calibrate the reader on the difficulty of the dataset and task.

Without these comparisons, it is unclear whether the proposed adaptation strategy offers a distinct advantage over more direct weakly-supervised training schemes.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The core idea and the different stages of the algorithm are explained effectively. The clarity could be slightly improved in the experimental section. Specifically, the descriptions for the ablation studies in Table 1 are concise, and a more detailed explanation of what each method entails (e.g., precisely which layers are adapted according to Eq. 1) would benefit the reader's understanding.

**Originality:** The work is sufficiently novel for this conference. While the approach utilizes existing techniques like CNN fine-tuning, its contribution lies in framing the classifier-to-detector conversion as a transferable, learnable transformation. This concept of adapting to held-out categories that have no detection data distinguishes it from prior work in both domain adaptation and weakly-supervised detection. The approach is simple but presents a novel combination of ideas applied to a challenging problem.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a highly significant and practical problem: scaling object detection to thousands of categories where exhaustive bounding box annotation is prohibitive. The ability to generate a detector for over 7,600 categories is an important result that will be valuable to researchers and practitioners. The work demonstrates a viable direction for building large-scale detection systems. However, the methodological significance is tempered by the lack of the aforementioned baseline comparisons. The reported performance gains may be capitalizing on the power of the underlying CNN features rather than offering a fundamentally better way to train detectors from weak supervision, an ambiguity that stronger experimental validation could resolve.
