This paper proposes LSDA, a method for adapting a convolutional neural network (CNN) trained for image classification to the task of object detection. The key idea is to leverage a small set of object categories that have bounding box annotations to learn a generic transformation from a classifier to a detector. This learned transformation is then applied to a large number of other categories for which only image-level classification labels are available. The training process involves three main stages: 1) fine-tuning a pre-trained CNN for classification on all available categories; 2) further fine-tuning the network on region proposals for the subset of categories with bounding box data, which importantly introduces a background class; and 3) applying the learned adaptations—both category-invariant feature transformations and category-specific classifier adjustments—to the remaining classes. At test time, the adapted network scores region proposals to produce detections.

**Quality**
This is an interesting and well-executed paper. The proposed approach is logical, and the experimental results are promising. While the method builds heavily on the success of prior CNN architectures, the contribution is valuable for researchers in computer vision and machine learning who are interested in large-scale detection with limited annotation.

However, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including comparisons to several important baselines:
1.  A simple weakly-supervised baseline where the network is fine-tuned using full images as positive examples and images/regions from other classes as negative examples.
2.  A multiple-instance learning (MIL) baseline, which seems like a natural approach for this problem. This would involve iteratively selecting the highest-scoring region proposal from each positive image as the true positive example during training for the classes that lack bounding box annotations.
3.  A very simple baseline to help contextualize the difficulty of the task: train the classifier, and at test time, simply predict a canonical bounding box (e.g., a centered box of average size) for each positive classification.

Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed adaptation method. It is unclear if the LSDA algorithm would outperform baselines 1 and 2, and including baseline 3 would provide a valuable reference point for the reader regarding the dataset's difficulty.

**Clarity**
The paper is generally clear and well-written. However, the experiments section could be improved. In particular, the descriptions of the different ablation models in Table 1 are too brief. It would be helpful to elaborate on exactly which components of the LSDA framework are included or excluded in each condition (e.g., "All Layers + BG", "k=10", etc.) to make the results easier to interpret.

**Originality**
The work is sufficiently novel for publication. While the core idea is a straightforward application of transfer learning and fine-tuning, its application to transfer knowledge from fully-supervised to weakly-supervised object categories within a single model is a novel contribution.

**Significance**
The paper addresses the significant and practical problem of scaling object detection to thousands of categories where bounding box annotations are scarce. Achieving reasonable detection performance on a large-scale dataset for categories without bounding box labels is a compelling result. However, the paper's main weakness, as mentioned above, is the lack of comparisons to other weakly-supervised methods. Without these, it remains an open question whether the performance gains stem from the proposed adaptation technique itself, or if they are primarily a consequence of the powerful features learned by the underlying CNN. This makes it difficult to ascertain whether this work introduces a fundamentally better way of training detectors from weak supervision.
