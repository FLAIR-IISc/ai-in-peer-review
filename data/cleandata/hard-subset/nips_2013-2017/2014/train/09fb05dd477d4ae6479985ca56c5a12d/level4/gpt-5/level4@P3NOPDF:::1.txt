Summary
This paper tackles object detection under limited bounding-box supervision. Instead of collecting bounding boxes for all categories, the authors propose adapting object classifiers to the detection task and transferring this adaptation across categories. The approach is intentionally simple: train with 100 categories that have both image-level labels and bounding boxes, then adapt and evaluate on 100 held-out categories that have only image-level labels. On the held-out categories, the method closes roughly 35% of the performance gap between a baseline and an oracle with full supervision.

Quality and clarity
The paper is clearly written, well organized, and technically sound. The experimental setup—training on 100 fully supervised categories and testing on 100 weakly supervised categories—is well motivated and carefully executed.

Originality
The work combines two adaptation ideas: (1) adapting object classifiers into detectors and (2) transferring adaptation learned on one set of categories to another. To the best of my knowledge, this specific combination and its instantiation are novel and interesting, though not revolutionary.

Significance
The central contribution is the proposed adaptation framework, which provides an appealing route to reduce dependence on bounding boxes. The empirical results are solid and show clear gains over a baseline, but they also underscore the inherent trade-off: fewer bounding boxes lead to lower mAP than fully supervised detectors. It is difficult to judge the long-term impact from these results alone, and more sophisticated methods will likely surpass the reported performance. That said, the paper opens a useful direction, especially if annotation costs remain a practical bottleneck.

One broader question is whether bounding-box scarcity will remain a dominant constraint long term. While boxes are more expensive than image-level labels, history shows that annotation scale can increase rapidly when the community prioritizes it. This does not diminish the paper’s value, but it tempers claims about the centrality of the problem.

Detailed comments and requests
- Figure 3a: Please clarify which compared methods do and do not use image-level category labels to aid detection, beyond your method and R-CNN. This is essential to ensure apples-to-apples comparisons.
- Main quantitative takeaway: My interpretation of Figure 3b is that the proposed DNN approach sits about one-third of the way from the baseline toward the oracle on the left-out categories. Do the authors agree this is the key result? If so, please highlight this explicitly rather than the “78%” figure (line 341). The 78% number is potentially misleading for two reasons: (1) it should be computed only on the left-out categories, and (2) performance should be framed relative to both baseline and oracle, not just as a percentage of the oracle. The same concern applies to the “50% improvement” claim (line 425).
- Relation to R-CNN: Since R-CNN is the most relevant competing approach, please consolidate the differences in a dedicated subsection or table. As written, the comparisons are scattered and easy to miss. Consider removing or moving Figure 6 (which is mostly anecdotal) to make room for a clearer, more prominent comparison.
- Experimental protocol: It would help to include a short ablation or analysis quantifying how much adaptation benefit comes from the classifier-to-detector step versus the cross-category transfer. This would better illuminate where the gains originate.
- Cost–performance trade-off: Since a major motivation is reducing bounding-box annotation, consider adding an estimate of annotation effort saved versus mAP lost. Even a coarse calculation would make the trade-off more concrete.

Minor issues (no response required)
- Naming consistency: The paper alternates between “DNN” and “DDA.” Please choose one and use it consistently.
- Typo on line 431: “classi?ers.”
- Please ensure that figure captions and text clearly specify whether numbers are over all categories or only the left-out set.

Overall assessment
This is a solid, clearly presented paper that introduces a simple and effective adaptation strategy to reduce reliance on bounding boxes. While the approach is not ground-breaking and the long-term significance is uncertain, the idea is timely, the results are convincing within the stated scope, and the contribution will be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance, with the caveats above regarding claims calibration and clearer comparisons to R-CNN.

Recommendation: Accept.
