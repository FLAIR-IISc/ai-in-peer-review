- Summary: Proposes LSDA to adapt a CNN classifier into a detector using detection data for some categories and transferring adaptations to categories without bounding boxes.  
- Key idea: Learn feature-level transformation and background class from detection categories, then transfer category-specific output-layer adjustments to classification-only categories.  
- Scale: Demonstrates large-scale detection (7.6K categories) and reports competitive ILSVRC-2013 results.  
- Builds on R-CNN: Extends fine-tuning approach to settings with missing bounding-box labels; distinct from MIL-based weakly supervised detection.  

- Strength: Clearly motivated by scarcity of bounding-box labels relative to image-level labels.  
- Strength: Method is simple and practical—fine-tune shared layers, learn background, adapt outputs.  
- Strength: Strong empirical gains on held-out categories (≈50% relative mAP improvement vs. using classification weights directly).  
- Strength: Ablation and error analysis show improved localization and reduced background confusion.  
- Strength: Scales to thousands of categories and provides reproducible implementation (Caffe models).  

- Weakness: Category-specific adaptation W^d = W^c + δ lacks principled justification or interpretation linking δ to task shift.  
- Weakness: Choice of similarity metric (L2 on normalized fc8 weights) and k-NN averaging for δ are not motivated or analyzed (sensitivity to k, metric, stability).  
- Weakness: Empirical advantage of kNN over global average is reported but rationale for neighborhood correspondence is unexplored.  
- Weakness: Comparisons use val2 split rather than official test set, limiting statistical validity and apples-to-apples comparisons.  

- Quality (NIPS): Empirical evaluation shows importance of feature adaptation and background modeling, but category-specific transfer lacks rigorous justification and fair comparisons.  
- Clarity (NIPS): Paper is well-written but Section 3.2 needs expanded motivation for the additive offset and nearest-neighbor choices.  
- Originality (NIPS): Novel extension of classification→detection adaptation to categories without bounding boxes and useful use of a learned background class.  
- Significance (NIPS): High practical impact for scaling detection; results and challenge ranking support significance but require standardized test comparisons.  

- Pros for acceptance: Addresses important scaling problem, simple effective method, strong empirical gains, scales to 7.6K categories, code/models released.  
- Cons for acceptance: Insufficient theoretical/empirical justification for additive offset and kNN transfer; reliance on validation splits limits statistical claims; design choices lack sensitivity analysis.  

- Suggestions: Provide principled explanation/analysis of W^d = W^c + δ and how δ captures task shifts (background, localization, part-vs-whole).  
- Suggestions: Justify nearest-neighbor transfer—analyze metric choices, k effects, semantic proximity, robustness; consider learning a mapping from W^c to δ.  
- Suggestions: Strengthen empirical comparisons with official test-set results or matched validation splits and report statistical significance/per-category confidence intervals.  
- Suggestions: Discuss potential failure cases where classification neighbors are semantically similar but visually unsuitable for detection (context/scale differences).  

- Overall assessment: Practical and promising approach with good scalability and empirical gains, but needs stronger justification for the core adaptation mechanism and more rigorous, standardized comparisons to be a stronger contribution.
