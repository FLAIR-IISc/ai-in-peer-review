- Problem: adapt object classifiers for detection to avoid costly bounding-box annotations.  
- Method: simple, straightforward algorithm trained on 100 categories with boxes and tested on 100 held-out categories with only labels.  
- Main result: method closes roughly 35% of the performance gap between baseline and oracle on held-out categories.  
- Quality/Clarity: high-quality, clearly written; method and experiments are easy to understand.  
- Originality: novel synthesis of two existing ideas (classifier-to-detector adaptation and adapting across category sets), interesting but not groundbreaking.  
- Significance: long-term impact uncertain; method sacrifices mAP to reduce annotation reliance and lacks an objective way to judge that trade-off.  
- Premise concern: the assumption that bounding-box cost is a permanent bottleneck is debatable given historical dataset-building efforts.  
- Figure 3a: authors should state which competing methods (besides theirs and R-CNN) use category-level labels for detection.  
- Figure 3b/result reporting: emphasize the blue-bar result (one-third gap closed) and evaluate relative to the baseline on held-out categories rather than only vs. oracle.  
- Claims critique: the "78% of oracle" and "50% improvement" statements are misleading without baseline-relative and held-out-focused context.  
- R-CNN comparison: distinctions are scattered; provide a dedicated table/subsection to consolidate differences (and consider replacing Figure 6).  
- Minor issues: inconsistent method name ("DNN" vs "DDA") and a typo on line 431.  
- Overall assessment: solid, reasonably novel work worthy of acceptance and of interest to the NIPS community.
