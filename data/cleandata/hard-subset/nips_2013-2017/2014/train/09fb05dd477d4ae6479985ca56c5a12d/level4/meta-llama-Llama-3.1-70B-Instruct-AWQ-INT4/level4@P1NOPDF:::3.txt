This paper proposes a method for adapting a Krizhevsky CNN network, pre-trained for classification without bounding box annotations, to perform object detection with a small number of classes that have annotated bounding boxes. The approach involves three key steps during training: 1) training a CNN for classification on images of all classes, 2) training a CNN on image regions of classes labeled with bounding boxes, including a negative background region class, and 3) incorporating the background class into the full CNN model while adapting some of the CNN weights. At test time, the CNN is applied to multiple image regions in a test image.

Quality: This paper is interesting and presents fairly compelling results, largely building on the success of Krizhevsky et al.'s model. While the approach is not radically new, the results will be valuable to researchers in computer vision and machine learning. The general approach is straightforward and logical. The experiments show promise, but lack comparisons to other baselines, which would strengthen the paper. Three potential baselines that could be considered are: 
1) Using full images as positive examples and images and regions from other classes as negative examples, then training and fine-tuning all layers of the CNN.
2) Following the same approach as above, but employing a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, where the highest-scoring positive region is iteratively selected for each image from set A (instead of using the full image).
3) Training and fine-tuning the CNN for classification, then running the classifier at test time to predict a single bounding box at the center of the image (e.g., the mean bounding box for ImageNet). 
Including these baselines would help assess whether the proposed approach outperforms them, as it is unclear whether it makes more sense than baseline 1, and likely makes less sense than baseline 2. Adding the third baseline would provide context on the ease or difficulty of the problem and dataset.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and clear, but the experiments section could be improved. Specifically, elaborating on the explanation of each method in Table 1 would be beneficial, as it is unclear whether these methods refer to applying Eq. 1 or 2 to a subset of CNN layers.

Originality: Although the basic approach is simple and specific to certain types of CNN networks, the paper is sufficiently novel to be accepted.

Significance: The problem addressed and the results are significant and valuable. The paper tackles a relevant problem, and the fact that decent detection results were obtained on a large-scale dataset without bounding box labels is compelling. However, the paper's omission of obvious baselines raises the possibility that the results may be due to improved CNN features rather than a better training approach for object detectors from weak supervision.
