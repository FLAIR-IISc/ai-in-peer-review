This paper investigates object detection, specifically addressing the disparity between the availability of bounding box annotations and category labels. The authors propose a method that adapts object classifiers to perform detection, offering a straightforward solution to this challenge. Their experimental design involves training on 100 categories with full annotations (both labels and bounding boxes) and evaluating on 100 held-out categories with only category labels available. The results demonstrate a promising performance, bridging approximately 35% of the gap between an ideal oracle and a baseline system on these held-out categories.

**Quality and clarity:** The paper is well-written and presents a clear explanation of the proposed method.

**Originality:** The work uniquely combines adaptation of object classifiers for detection with transfer learning across category sets. While not a radical departure, this combination represents a novel and interesting contribution to the field.

**Significance:** The primary contribution lies in this novel approach. Assessing the long-term impact is difficult, as more complex methods will likely achieve superior results in the future – a natural progression. A key limitation is the inherent trade-off between performance (mAP) and the reduced number of bounding boxes used, and the paper doesn’t provide a clear metric to evaluate the value of this trade-off. 
(It’s worth questioning whether the addressed problem represents a fundamental long-term bottleneck. The cost of obtaining bounding box annotations may decrease with increased resources, similar to the previously daunting task of labeling millions of images with a large number of object categories.)

**Detailed issues that should be addressed:**

- In Figure 3a, please explicitly clarify which methods utilize category labels for detection, beyond your proposed method and R-CNN, to ensure a fair comparison.
- The core result, as I interpret it, is the performance represented by the blue bars in Figure 3b – achieving approximately one-third of the gap between the baseline and oracle. Please emphasize this finding rather than the 78% figure (line 341), which is misleading for two reasons: it includes categories beyond the held-out set, and it’s presented as a percentage of oracle performance without considering the baseline. A similar adjustment is needed for the reported 50% improvement (line 425).
- Dedicate a table or a subsection to a detailed comparison with R-CNN, the closest competing approach. Currently, these distinctions are dispersed throughout the text and lack prominence. Consider removing Figure 6, which provides only anecdotal examples.

**Minor issues (no response needed):**

- The method is referred to as both “DNN” and “DDA” – please ensure consistency.
- Typo on line 431 (“classi?ers”).

Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a reasonably novel approach to object detection. I recommend acceptance for presentation at NIPS. While the results are likely to be improved upon, the simplicity of the method – being a first attempt in this direction – and the potential long-term impact of the approach warrant its dissemination to the research community.
