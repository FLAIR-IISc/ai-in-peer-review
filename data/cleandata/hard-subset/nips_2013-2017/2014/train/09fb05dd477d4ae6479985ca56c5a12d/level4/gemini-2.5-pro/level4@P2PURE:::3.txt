This paper presents a method for adapting a convolutional neural network (CNN), initially trained for classification without bounding box annotations, to perform object detection. The adaptation leverages a small set of classes for which annotated bounding boxes are available.

The proposed training procedure involves three stages:
1.  A CNN is trained for classification on the full set of image-level labels.
2.  A separate CNN is trained on image regions extracted from the classes with bounding box annotations, incorporating an additional class for negative background regions.
3.  The background class is integrated into the original full CNN, and select network weights are fine-tuned.

At test time, the final network is applied to multiple regions within a test image to produce detections.

**Quality:**
The paper is interesting and presents compelling results. While this success builds heavily on the strength of the Krizhevsky et al. model rather than on a radically new technique, the findings are valuable to researchers in computer vision and machine learning. The general approach is simple and logical, and the experiments are promising.

However, the experimental evaluation is incomplete as it lacks comparisons to several important baselines:
1.  **Full-Image Baseline:** Train and fine-tune all layers of the CNN using full images as positive examples and images/regions from other classes as negative examples.
2.  **MIL Baseline:** An extension of the above, but employing a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach that iteratively selects the highest-scoring region within each positive image as the positive training example.
3.  **Fixed-Box Baseline:** Train and fine-tune the CNN for classification, and at test time, simply predict a single, fixed bounding box at the center of the image (e.g., the mean bounding box size and location from the training set).

Without these comparisons, it is unclear whether the proposed method would outperform these alternatives. The justification for the proposed method over baseline (1) is not obvious, and it seems potentially less principled than the MIL approach in baseline (2). Including baseline (3) is crucial for contextualizing the difficulty of the task and establishing a performance floor for the reader.

**Clarity:**
The paper is clear and well-written. The clarity of the experiments section could be improved, particularly by elaborating on the methods presented in Table 1. For instance, it is unclear which equations (e.g., Eq. 1 or 2) or which subset of CNN layers correspond to each reported result.

**Originality:**
The work is sufficiently novel for publication. Although the core approach is straightforward and tailored to a specific CNN architecture, it presents a practical solution to a relevant problem.

**Significance:**
The paper addresses the significant problem of weakly supervised object detection. Achieving decent detection results on a large-scale dataset without exhaustive bounding box labels is a compelling contribution. At the same time, the primary weakness remains the omission of key baselines. This makes it difficult to determine if the method's success is due to a genuinely superior training strategy for weakly supervised detection, or if it is simply capitalizing on the strong discriminative power of pre-trained CNN features.
