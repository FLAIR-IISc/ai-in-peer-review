- Problem: Tackles object detection when bounding-box annotations are scarce compared to category labels.  
- Proposal: Adapt existing object classifiers to perform detection; the algorithm is simple and straightforward.  
- Experimental protocol: Train on 100 categories with boxes and labels, test on 100 held-out categories with only labels.  
- Results: Achieves about one-third of the gap between baseline and oracle (~35% of the gap) on held-out categories.  
- Writing/clarity: Paper is well-written and clearly presented.  
- Originality: Novel combination of classifier-to-detector adaptation and cross-category transfer—interesting but not groundbreaking.  
- Significance: Useful contribution but likely to be surpassed by more sophisticated methods; long-term impact uncertain.  
- Limitation: mAP is reduced due to fewer bounding boxes, and there is no objective way shown to evaluate whether the trade-off is beneficial.  
- Question of problem importance: Unclear if expensive box annotation is a lasting barrier given trends in large-scale labeling and resource allocation.  
- Figure 3a concern: Clarify which methods (besides the authors' method and R-CNN) use category labels for detection to ensure fair comparisons.  
- Figure 3b / metrics concern: The key finding is ~1/3 improvement (blue bars); the 78% figure is misleading and should be reassessed relative to left-out categories and baseline vs. oracle.  
- Percent-improvement claims: Similar reassessment needed for the stated 50% improvement.  
- R-CNN comparison: Provide a dedicated table/subsection explicitly outlining differences between the proposed method and R-CNN; remove Figure 6 to make space.  
- Minor issues: Inconsistent method name (“DNN” vs “DDA”) and a typo on line 431 (“classi?ers”).  
- Recommendation: Solid paper worth accepting for NIPS as a valuable first exploration of this direction.
