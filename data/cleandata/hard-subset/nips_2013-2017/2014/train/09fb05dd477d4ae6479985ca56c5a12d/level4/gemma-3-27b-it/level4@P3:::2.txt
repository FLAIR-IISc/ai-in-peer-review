## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper proposes a novel framework, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), to leverage image-level labels for object detection, addressing the significant challenge of limited bounding box annotations. The core idea is to learn a transformation from classifiers (trained on abundant image-level data) to detectors, and apply this knowledge to categories lacking bounding box annotations. The authors demonstrate promising results on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge, achieving a ranking of 4th place and demonstrating the ability to create a detector for over 7,600 categories.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the proposed approach. The motivation – scaling object detection to a large number of categories given the scarcity of bounding box labels – is well-articulated and relevant to the NIPS community, building upon the success of deep convolutional neural networks for both classification (Krizhevsky et al., 2012) and detection (Girshick et al., 2014). The authors appropriately position their work within the broader context of domain adaptation (Kulis et al., 2011; Hoffman et al., 2016) and weakly-supervised learning approaches like Multiple Instance Learning (Ali & Saenko, 2012; Song et al., 2014). 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of learning a transformation between classification and detection networks to enable detection in data-scarce regimes is a novel contribution.
* **Scalability:**  The potential to generate detectors for a large number of categories (7.6K in the experiment) using readily available image-level labels is significant.
* **Performance:** Achieving 4th place on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection challenge demonstrates the efficacy of the proposed method. The 50% relative mAP improvement over a baseline using raw classifier weights is substantial.
* **Availability:**  Publicly releasing models and software (lsda.berkeleyvision.org) promotes reproducibility and encourages further research.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and generally easy to follow, with helpful figures illustrating the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Justification of Adaptation Method:** The theoretical justification for the proposed adaptation method (Section 3.2) lacks rigor.  The summation of weights `W_c` and `δ_B` is not thoroughly explained, and the physical meaning of this "offset parameter" remains unclear.  The rationale for the k-NN approach for adapting categories without bounding box annotations could be strengthened.  Why is averaging the changes from nearest neighbors an effective approximation of the fine-tuning process?
* **Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison to other methods is slightly weakened by evaluating on a split of the validation set rather than the dedicated test set. While the authors acknowledge this in a footnote and attempt to justify it, a comparison on the standard test set would provide a more robust evaluation. The claim about R-CNN performance being similar on val2, while acknowledged, lacks statistical support given the small set size.
* **Ablation Study Depth:** While an ablation study is presented, it would be beneficial to provide a more granular analysis of the individual components. For example, what is the impact of different architectures for the CNN backbone (beyond Krizhevsky et al., 2012)?
* **Error Analysis Specificity:** The error analysis (Section 4.3) provides some insight into the types of errors made, but could be more specific. Quantifying the frequency of each error type (Loc, BG, Oth) would be useful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in scaling object detection to a large number of categories.
* The proposed approach is novel and demonstrates promising results.
* The availability of code and models fosters reproducibility and further research.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the technical details.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of a strong theoretical justification for the adaptation method.
* The slightly compromised comparison to baselines due to the non-standard evaluation split. 
* The relatively shallow ablation study could leave reviewers questioning the robustness of the method.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The proposed LSDA framework offers a promising approach to tackling the problem of limited bounding box annotations for object detection. While the theoretical justification and evaluation could be strengthened, the novelty, scalability, and demonstrated performance of the method warrant acceptance. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions, specifically requesting the authors to:

1.  Provide a more detailed explanation and motivation for the weight summation in Section 3.2, potentially relating it to a specific optimization objective or theoretical framework.
2.  If feasible, report results on the standard test set for a more rigorous comparison to existing methods.
3.  Expand the ablation study to include a more granular analysis of the different components of the LSDA algorithm.
