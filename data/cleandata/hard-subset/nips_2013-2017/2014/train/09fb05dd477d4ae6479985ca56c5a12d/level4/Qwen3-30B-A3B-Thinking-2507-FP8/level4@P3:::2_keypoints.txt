- Proposes LSDA to adapt image classifiers into object detectors using domain adaptation to scale detection to many categories.

- Key insight: learn the classifier→detector transformation from categories with bounding boxes and transfer it to categories without detection data.

- Method: three-component adaptation — (1) category-invariant fine-tuning of layers 1–7 using detection data, (2) category-specific adaptation via k-nearest neighbors on output weights, (3) detection pipeline using final score vector without SVMs or bbox regression.

- Strong empirical results: ~50% relative mAP improvement over the classification-only baseline; output-layer adaptation with k=10 yields significant gains.

- Demonstrates producing a large-scale detector (7,604 categories) using only classification data, showing practical impact and scalability potential.

- Paper is well-written, well-organized, with clear figures, reproducible implementation details, and released code/models.

- Original contribution: first method to adapt to held-out categories with no detection data, connecting classifier→detector adaptation to ImageNet taxonomy.

- Weakness: category-specific adaptation (Equation 1) lacks theoretical justification and the meaning of the offset parameter is unclear.

- Weakness: choice of k-nearest neighbors for output-layer transfer is empirically motivated without theoretical rationale.

- Weakness: evaluation protocol is confusing—uses val1/val2 split instead of the official test set, making comparisons problematic.

- Weakness: scalability claim to "tens of thousands" of categories is not fully validated; experiments are limited to a 200-category setting with only 100 detection categories.

- Weakness: notable performance gap remains between LSDA and an oracle with full detection data (16.15% vs 22.5% mAP), indicating room for improvement.

- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions addressing theoretical justification and evaluation protocol.

- Requested revisions: explain why detection weights are classification weights plus an offset, justify k-NN choice, and use the official test set for comparisons.

- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, strong empirical results on a standard benchmark, clear implementation and code release, and a novel domain-adaptation approach for detection.

- Arguments against acceptance: lack of theoretical grounding for category-specific adaptation, non-standard evaluation protocol, and insufficient validation of extreme-scale scalability.
