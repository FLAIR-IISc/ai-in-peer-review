### Review of "LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the significant challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, a task hindered by the high cost of acquiring bounding box annotations. The central idea is to learn a general transformation that adapts an image classifier into an object detector. This transformation is learned on a subset of categories for which both image-level labels and bounding box annotations are available. It is then applied to classifiers for novel categories that only have image-level labels, effectively turning them into detectors without requiring any direct bounding box supervision.

The proposed algorithm, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), is presented as a three-stage process. First, a deep convolutional neural network (CNN) is fine-tuned for classification on all categories of interest. Second, the feature representation layers of this network are further fine-tuned for detection using the subset of categories with bounding box data; this step learns a category-invariant adaptation, including a generic background model. Third, a category-specific adaptation is learned for the final classification layer. For categories without bounding box data, this specific adaptation is approximated by averaging the adaptations learned from their nearest neighbors in the classification weight space.

Experiments are conducted on the ILSVRC-2013 detection dataset, simulating a scenario with 100 categories for training the adaptation and 100 held-out categories for evaluation. The results show that LSDA provides a significant performance boost over a baseline using the original classifier for detection, effectively reducing localization and background confusion errors. The authors demonstrate the scalability of their approach by training a detector for over 7,600 categories.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Important and Practical Problem:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and highly relevant problem. The gap between the availability of large-scale classification data (e.g., ImageNet) and the relative scarcity of detection data is a key bottleneck in computer vision. This work proposes a pragmatic and scalable solution to bridge this gap.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Approach:** The core idea of framing the classifier-to-detector problem as a learnable adaptation is novel. While drawing inspiration from domain adaptation and transfer learning (e.g., R-CNN's fine-tuning), the specific formulation to transfer detection knowledge to categories with no bounding box data is an original and valuable contribution. The method itself is straightforward and well-explained.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The ablation study in Figure 3 systematically validates the contribution of each component of LSDA. The analysis of error types in Figure 5 provides convincing evidence that the adaptation successfully learns to address key challenges in detection, namely localization and background rejection, which are less critical for image-level classification.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The motivation is well-established, the method is described logically, and the experiments are designed to convincingly support the main claims.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Framing of Quantitative Results:** The main quantitative results could be presented with more nuance. For example, the claim of a "50% relative mAP boost" (Abstract, Section 5) over the baseline, while technically correct, can be misleading. A more informative perspective is to analyze how much of the performance gap between the baseline (classification-only) and the oracle (fully supervised detection) is closed by the proposed method. Based on Table 1, the baseline achieves 10.31% mAP and the oracle achieves 27.26% mAP on held-out categories. The proposed LSDA model achieves 16.15%. This means it closes (16.15 - 10.31) / (27.26 - 10.31) ≈ 34.5% of the performance gap. This is a solid achievement, but presenting it this way provides a more grounded assessment of the method's impact. The authors are encouraged to highlight this analysis, as it is a key takeaway from their experiments.

2.  **Comparison with R-CNN:** The paper is closely related to R-CNN, which it cites as a key inspiration. However, the differences and trade-offs between the two methods are scattered throughout the text. To improve clarity and better position the work, I would strongly suggest adding a dedicated table or sub-section that explicitly contrasts LSDA with R-CNN. This comparison should cover aspects like training data requirements (per category), the role of pre-training vs. adaptation, the use of SVMs and bounding box regression, and training time. This would make the unique contributions of LSDA much more salient. The anecdotal examples in Figure 6, while illustrative, could potentially be replaced by this more substantive comparison.

3.  **Discussion on the Long-Term Problem:** A minor philosophical point is the paper's premise that obtaining bounding boxes will remain a long-term challenge. While this is true today, one could argue that with sufficient resources (e.g., crowd-sourcing), this annotation gap could eventually be closed. This does not diminish the current value of the work, but a brief discussion on this point could add nuance to the motivation. The strength of this work lies in making large-scale detection practical *now*, a point which is very significant.

4.  **Clarity in Experimental Comparisons:** To ensure all comparisons are perfectly clear, the authors should explicitly state the supervision used for every method shown in comparison plots and tables. For instance, in figures comparing different approaches, a clear legend or caption text clarifying which methods use bounding boxes for all, some, or no categories would be beneficial.

**Minor Issues**

*   There appears to be a typo in the conclusion (line 431 of the original PDF, though line numbers are not in the provided text): "classi?ers" should be "classifiers".

**Conclusion and Recommendation**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, simple, and effective solution to an important problem in computer vision. The idea of learning to adapt classifiers into detectors is compelling, and the paper provides strong empirical evidence to support its claims. While there is room for improvement in how the results are framed and compared to prior work, these are primarily issues of presentation rather than fundamental flaws. The work represents a valuable contribution that is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers.

Therefore, I recommend **acceptance**.
