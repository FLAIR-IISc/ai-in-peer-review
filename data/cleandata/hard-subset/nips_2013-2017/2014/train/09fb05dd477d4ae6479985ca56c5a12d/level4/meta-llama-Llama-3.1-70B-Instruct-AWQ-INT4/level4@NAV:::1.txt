This paper tackles the challenge of object detection by proposing an adaptation of object classifiers for the detection task, effectively sidestepping the issue of obtaining bounding boxes at a scale comparable to category labels. The algorithm is straightforward and uncomplicated, which in itself is not a drawback. The experimental protocol involves training on 100 categories with both category labels and bounding boxes, and testing on 100 categories with only category labels. The results show a reasonable performance, bridging approximately 35% of the gap between an oracle and a baseline on the 100 left-out categories.

Quality and clarity: The paper is well-written and of good quality.

Originality: This work combines two existing adaptation ideas - adapting object classifiers for detection and adapting across categories - in a novel and intriguing way. Although not groundbreaking, it is an interesting concept that, to the best of this reviewer's knowledge, has not been explored before.

Significance: The primary contribution of this paper lies in its novel approach. However, evaluating its long-term significance based on the results is challenging, as they will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods in the future. A major concern with the results is the inevitable trade-off in performance (mAP) due to the use of fewer bounding boxes, and the lack of an objective measure to assess whether this trade-off is worthwhile.

One notable point is that the importance of the problem being addressed is unclear. While bounding boxes are indeed more resource-intensive to obtain than category labels, it is uncertain whether this issue cannot be resolved by allocating more resources to the problem. In the past, labeling millions of images with 20,000 object categories seemed unfeasible, yet it has been achieved.

Detailed issues to be addressed:

- The authors should clearly indicate in Figure 3a which methods, aside from their approach and R-CNN, utilize category labels to aid detection, ensuring an apples-to-apples comparison.
- The proposed approach's effectiveness is primarily evaluated based on the blue bars in Figure 3b, which show that DNN achieves approximately one-third of the gap between the baseline and oracle. The authors should acknowledge this as a key result and highlight it, rather than the 78% mentioned on line 341, which is not an accurate representation for two reasons: it should only consider left-out categories, and it should not simply be a percentage of oracle performance, but rather account for baseline performance. A similar issue applies to the claim of 50% improvement on line 425.
- The authors should dedicate a table or subsection to clearly outlining the differences between their approach and R-CNN, the closest competing alternative. Currently, these statements are scattered or not prominent enough. Consider replacing Figure 6, which presents anecdotal examples, with this information.

Minor issues (no response required):

Overall, this is a reasonably good paper proposing a novel approach that deserves acceptance and presentation to the NIPS community. Although the results will likely be surpassed soon, the long-term significance of the paper and its approach remains to be seen.
