This paper presents a framework, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), designed to adapt deep neural networks from image classification tasks to object detection tasks. The central motivation is to overcome the scarcity of bounding-box annotations required for training detectors, especially when scaling to a very large number of categories. The authors formulate the problem as one of domain adaptation, learning a general transformation from a classification network to a detection network. This transformation, learned on a subset of categories with full annotation, is then applied to classifiers for novel categories that only have image-level labels. The method's efficacy is demonstrated on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection dataset, showing significant improvement for held-out categories compared to a baseline using unadapted classifiers.

The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and addresses a significant and practical problem in computer vision. The overall quality of the work is good, and the proposed method is interesting and appears straightforward to implement. However, there are two primary concerns that could be addressed to strengthen the paper.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles the crucial challenge of scaling object detection to thousands of categories where exhaustive bounding-box annotation is infeasible. The approach of learning a transferable mapping from a classifier to a detector is a novel and promising direction that leverages abundant classification data.
*   **Clarity:** The methodology is explained clearly, and the paper is well-structured and easy to follow.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experiments show a substantial (+50% relative mAP) improvement on held-out categories over the baseline, which strongly supports the paper's core claim that a generic classifier-to-detector transformation can be learned and transferred.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Insufficient Justification for the Adaptation Model:** While empirically successful, the adaptation method proposed in Section 3.2 lacks a principled motivation. The paper models the adapted detector weights (`W_d`) for a given class as the sum of the original classifier weights (`W_c`) and an offset term (`δ`). For new classes without detection data, this offset is approximated by averaging the learned offsets from their k-nearest neighbors. The paper does not provide a clear justification for why this additive update is the appropriate model. For example, what is the geometric or physical meaning of this "offset parameter"? Why should the complex transformation from a classifier to a detector be captured by a simple vector translation in the weight space? Consequently, it is also unclear why averaging offsets from k-nearest neighbors is a sound method for approximating this transformation. A more detailed discussion of the underlying assumptions would significantly bolster the contribution.

2.  **Indirect Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The experimental comparison to other leading methods, such as R-CNN, is not direct. While the authors follow a standard protocol by evaluating on a split of the validation set (`val2`), they do not provide a direct, quantitative comparison of LSDA against a fully-implemented R-CNN on this same set. The performance of R-CNN is only mentioned in a footnote, which notes that R-CNN's performance would be higher due to additional fine-tuning steps like SVM training and bounding box regression. To make a compelling case, the authors should either implement these stages for a fair comparison or, at a minimum, report the exact performance of the original R-CNN on the `val2` set. Furthermore, given that `val2` is a relatively small set, any claims about performance should ideally be supported by statistical significance tests to confirm that the differences are meaningful.

**Summary:**
The paper proposes an interesting and valuable framework for converting pre-trained classifiers into object detectors, enabling detection for categories that lack bounding-box annotations. The method achieves strong performance and addresses a highly relevant problem. However, the work could be improved by providing a more rigorous motivation for the proposed adaptation model and by conducting a more direct and statistically sound comparison to state-of-the-art baselines.
