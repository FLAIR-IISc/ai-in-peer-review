This paper presents a method for adapting a Krizhevsky-style CNN trained for image-level classification (without bounding boxes) to object detection, using a small subset of classes with bounding-box annotations. The training procedure: (1) train a CNN for classification on images from all classes; (2) train a CNN on image regions for the classes with bounding boxes, adding a negative “background” region class; and (3) incorporate the background class into the full CNN and adapt some of the CNN weights. At test time, the CNN is applied to multiple image regions per test image.

Quality: The paper is interesting and makes a reasonably compelling case that it achieves solid results. These gains largely build on the success of the Krizhevsky et al. model rather than introducing something radically new, but the findings should still be valuable to the vision and ML communities. The overall approach is simple and logical. The experiments are promising; however, several important baselines are missing:
1) Use full images as positive examples, and images and regions from other classes as negatives. Train and fine-tune all layers of the CNN.
2) As above, but apply a MIL procedure: iteratively select the highest-scoring positive region for each image from set A, as defined in the paper (instead of using the full image).
3) Train and fine-tune the CNN for classification only. At test time, run the classifier and predict a single bounding box at the image center (e.g., the mean bounding box for ImageNet).

I am unsure whether the proposed approach would outperform these baselines. It is not clear that it makes more sense than baseline 1, and it likely makes less sense than baseline 2. Including the third baseline is important to communicate how easy or hard the problem and dataset are.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well written. The experimental section could be clearer, especially in explaining what each method in Table 1 refers to. For example, does it mean applying Eq. 1 or Eq. 2 to a subset of CNN layers?

Originality: The work is sufficiently novel for acceptance, although the core idea is simple and somewhat specific to particular CNN architectures.

Significance: The problem is important, and the results are valuable. It is compelling that decent detection performance is achieved on a large-scale dataset without bounding-box labels. At the same time, the paper omits obvious baselines, and it is quite possible that the observed gains primarily reflect stronger CNN features rather than a fundamentally better approach to training object detectors from weak supervision.
