Summary
The paper proposes a method to adapt a Krizhevsky-style CNN trained for image-level classification (without bounding box annotations) to perform object detection when only a small subset of classes has annotated bounding boxes. The training procedure is: (1) train a CNN for classification on images from all classes; (2) train a CNN on image regions for the classes with bounding boxes, introducing a negative background region class; and (3) incorporate the background class into the full CNN model while adapting a subset of the CNN weights. At test time, the CNN is applied to multiple image regions within each test image.

Quality
The paper is interesting and makes a fairly compelling case that it achieves reasonable detection results. These results build largely on the strong performance of the Krizhevsky et al. model rather than introducing a radically new idea; nonetheless, the approach is likely to be valuable to the computer vision and machine learning communities. The overall strategy is simple and logical. The experiments are promising, but key baselines are missing:

- Baseline 1: Use entire images as positive examples and use images and regions from other classes as negatives. Train and fine-tune all layers of the CNN.
- Baseline 2: As above, but adopt a MIL scheme in which, for each image in set A (as defined by the paper), you iteratively select the highest-scoring positive region rather than using the full image.
- Baseline 3: Train and fine-tune the CNN purely for classification. At test time, run the classifier and predict a single bounding box at the image center (e.g., using the mean bounding box for ImageNet).

It is unclear whether the proposed approach would outperform these baselines. In particular, it is not obvious that the proposed approach is superior to Baseline 1, and it likely makes less sense than Baseline 2. Including Baseline 3 would also help readers gauge the inherent difficulty of the task and dataset.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well written. The experimental section could be clearer, especially regarding the precise meaning of each method listed in Table 1. For example, does each method correspond to applying Equation 1 or Equation 2 to a specific subset of CNN layers? Explicitly mapping each table entry to the equations and the layers adapted would improve clarity.

Originality
The paper is sufficiently novel to merit acceptance. However, the core idea is relatively simple and tailored to a particular class of CNN architectures.

Significance
The problem is important, and the results are valuable. Demonstrating decent detection performance on a large-scale dataset without bounding box labels is compelling.

Overall assessment
The paper addresses a relevant and timely problem. However, the absence of several obvious baselines makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed method. It is quite possible that the observed gains primarily reflect improvements due to CNN features rather than a fundamentally better approach to training detectors from weak supervision. Including the suggested baselines and clarifying the experimental setup would substantially strengthen the paper.
