This paper addresses the problem of object detection, specifically the challenge of obtaining bounding box annotations on the same scale as category labels for object categorization. The authors propose a clever solution: adapting existing object classifiers to perform the detection task. Their algorithm is relatively simple and straightforward, which is a positive attribute. Their experimental protocol involves training on 100 categories with both bounding box and category label annotations, and testing on 100 held-out categories for which only category labels are available. The results are encouraging, bridging approximately 35% of the gap between an oracle and a baseline on these held-out categories.

**Quality and clarity:** This is a well-written and clear paper.

**Originality:** The paper combines two established ideas – adapting object classifiers for detection and transferring knowledge between category sets. To the best of my knowledge, this combination represents a novel and interesting approach, though not a groundbreaking one.

**Significance:** The primary contribution of this work is its novel (but not groundbreaking) approach. While it is difficult to assess the long-term significance based on the current results – and more sophisticated methods will likely surpass this work in the future (as is often the case) – this is to be expected. A key limitation of the results is that they inherently involve a trade-off: sacrificing mean Average Precision (mAP) by utilizing fewer bounding boxes. There is no objective way to determine whether this trade-off is worthwhile. 

(A related point: it’s unclear whether the problem addressed here represents a truly critical long-term challenge. While bounding box annotations are indeed more expensive to obtain than category labels, it’s reasonable to assume that increased resources dedicated to annotation will mitigate this issue. Not long ago, obtaining millions of images labeled with 20,000 object categories seemed practically impossible…)

**Detailed issues that should be addressed:**

*   In Figure 3a, please clarify which methods utilize category labels to aid detection, beyond your method and R-CNN. A clear statement would ensure a fair, apples-to-apples comparison.
*   My evaluation of the proposed approach is primarily based on the blue bars in Figure 3b. The DNN method performs at approximately one-third of the distance between the baseline and the oracle. Do the authors agree that this is a key result? If so, I recommend highlighting this finding instead of the 78% figure (line 341), which is not entirely accurate for two reasons: 1) it should be evaluated solely on the held-out categories, and 2) the metric should consider the baseline performance, rather than simply representing a percentage of the oracle’s performance. A similar concern applies to the claimed 50% improvement (line 425).
*   The authors should include a table or dedicated subsection that explicitly outlines the differences between their method and R-CNN, which appears to be the closest competing approach. Currently, these distinctions are scattered throughout the text and could be more prominent. I suggest removing Figure 6, which presents only anecdotal examples, to make space for this comparison.

Overall, this is a sound paper presenting a reasonably novel approach. I believe it merits acceptance and should be shared with the NIPS community. While the results will almost certainly be improved upon in future work – given the method’s simplicity (understandable, considering it is the first to take this approach) – the long-term significance of the paper, and the approach itself, remains an open question.
