## Review of LSDA: Large Scale Detection through Adaptation

This paper addresses the challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, motivated by the disparity between the availability of image-level labels and bounding box annotations. The authors propose Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), an algorithm that learns to transform image classifiers into object detectors, leveraging readily available classification data. Their approach focuses on learning a generic transformation applicable to categories lacking bounding box annotations. The experimental protocol involves training on 100 categories with both labels and boxes, and evaluating on 100 held-out categories with labels only. The reported results show a substantial improvement over a baseline using raw classifier weights, achieving approximately 35% of the gap between baseline and oracle performance on the held-out categories.

**Quality and Clarity:** This is a well-written and technically sound paper. The motivation is clearly articulated, the proposed method is explained in a straightforward manner, and the experimental setup is well-defined. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of related work in object detection and domain adaptation. The paper is generally easy to follow, though some minor clarifications, detailed below, would further improve readability.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel combination of existing ideas. While adapting classifiers for detection (as demonstrated by R-CNN [1]) and domain adaptation techniques are established concepts, the specific approach of learning a generalized transformation between classification and detection tasks, and applying this transformation to categories with limited annotation, represents a valuable contribution. The authors clearly position their work within the existing literature, acknowledging related approaches like Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) [6, 7]. Though not a groundbreaking innovation, the method offers a practical approach to address a significant challenge in scaling object detection.

**Significance:** The paper’s significance lies in its potential to enable detection for a vast number of categories with limited bounding box data. The reported results are promising, demonstrating a clear performance gain on the held-out categories. However, as the authors acknowledge, the method inherently involves a trade-off between performance (mAP) and annotation cost. Determining whether this trade-off is worthwhile requires further investigation and comparison with alternative approaches. While the long-term impact remains to be seen, the approach presented offers a valuable step towards scaling object detection to a more realistic and comprehensive level. The publicly available models and software ([lsda.berkeleyvision.org]) further enhance the potential impact of this work.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** A unique and effective method for leveraging readily available classification data for object detection.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written, logically structured, and easy to understand.
* **Promising Results:** Significant performance improvement on held-out categories demonstrates the efficacy of the proposed method.
* **Practicality:** The algorithm is relatively simple and efficient, offering a practical solution for scaling object detection.
* **Publicly Available Resources:** Providing models and software promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Trade-off Analysis:**  A more thorough discussion of the trade-off between performance and annotation cost would be beneficial.  How does the performance compare to training a detector with a smaller number of bounding box annotations?
* **Comparison to R-CNN:** While the authors acknowledge R-CNN [1], a more detailed and centralized comparison of the two approaches, outlining the key differences and advantages/disadvantages of each, would strengthen the paper.
* **Result Presentation:** The interpretation of the performance gains (e.g., the 78% claim on line 341, the 50% improvement on line 425) could be clarified by focusing on the absolute mAP improvement and the relative gain considering the baseline performance, rather than solely relying on the percentage of the gap to the oracle.
* **Terminology Consistency:**  The use of "DNN" and "LSDA" to refer to the proposed method is slightly inconsistent.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a relevant problem in object detection.
* The experimental results are promising and demonstrate a clear improvement over a strong baseline.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and its results.
* The availability of models and software facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The performance gains, while significant, may not be substantial enough to justify the inherent trade-off between performance and annotation cost.
* The comparison to related work, particularly R-CNN, could be more comprehensive and nuanced.



**Specific Suggestions:**

* **Figure 3a:** Clarify whether the listed methods (other than LSDA and R-CNN) utilize category labels during the detection phase. A clear indication of which methods rely solely on bounding box annotations would improve the comparison.
* **Results Section:** Refocus the discussion of performance gains on the absolute mAP improvement and the relative gain considering the baseline performance.
* **R-CNN Comparison:** Dedicate a subsection or table to a detailed comparison of LSDA and R-CNN, highlighting their key differences, advantages, and disadvantages.  Consider sacrificing Figure 6 to accommodate this comparison.
* **Terminology:** Maintain consistency in the terminology used to refer to the proposed method (e.g., consistently use "LSDA").



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **accepting** this paper. While not a revolutionary advancement, it presents a valuable and practical contribution to the field of object detection. The novelty of the approach, the clarity of the presentation, and the promising experimental results make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS conference. Addressing the minor suggestions outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
