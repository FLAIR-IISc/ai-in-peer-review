- Introduces LSDA: transforms image classifiers into object detectors using a subset of categories with bounding boxes, framed as domain adaptation (classification→detection).  
- Leverages abundant image-level classification data to scale detection without expensive bounding-box annotations.  
- Key technical innovations: category-invariant feature adaptation (fine-tuning shared layers 1–7) and category-specific output-layer adaptation (nearest-neighbor for categories without detection data).  
- Demonstrates large-scale capability: produces detectors for 7,604 ImageNet categories using classification data for 7,404 categories.  
- Empirical result: reports a 50% relative mAP improvement over classification-only baselines on ILSVRC-2013 detection.  
- Ablation study: feature adaptation (layers 1–7 fine-tuning) provides the largest performance gain; output-layer adaptation adds modest incremental improvement (k=10: 16.15% vs 15.85%).  
- Missing baseline: no comparison to a standard CNN fine-tuned on full images with other-regions-as-negatives to isolate bounding-box adaptation benefits.  
- Missing baseline: the MIL approach of Ali et al. [6] (a direct competitor using image-level labels) is not evaluated on ImageNet.  
- Missing baseline: the simple “centered bounding box” baseline is omitted, which would clarify task difficulty.  
- Concern: the reported 50% improvement may partly reflect inherent CNN feature strength rather than the adaptation mechanism.  
- Clarity: paper is well-structured and clear; should explicitly define baselines in Table 1 and clarify background-class learning in Figure 3.  
- Originality: novel application of domain adaptation to large-scale detection, extending R-CNN to many categories without per-category bboxes and differing from MIL-based methods.  
- Significance: addresses a practical, important scaling problem; producing a 7.6K detector and 2 fps runtime is a meaningful step toward large-scale detection.  
- Pro: novel framework, strong empirical results on ImageNet, practical software release.  
- Con: missing critical baselines weakens the evaluation; nonetheless, the paper’s novelty and experiments justify acceptance pending stronger comparisons.
