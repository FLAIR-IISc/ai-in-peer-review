This paper presents a method for adapting a Krizhevsky CNN network that is trained for classification (without bounding box annotations) to work for object detection, given a small number of classes that have annotated bounding boxes. At train time, the approach 1) trains a CNN for classification on images of all classes, 2) trains a CNN on image regions on classes labeled with bounding boxes, while adding a negative background region class, and 3) adds the background class to the full CNN model while adapting some of the CNN weights. At test time, the CNN is run on multiple image regions in a test image.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant challenge in scaling object detection to a large number of categories. The authors provide a clear and concise background on the problem, discussing the limitations of existing approaches and the potential benefits of their proposed method.

Quality: This is an interesting paper and is fairly compelling that it gets reasonable results. These results are in large part building off the success of Krizhevsky et al.'s model, rather than something that is radically new; however, they will be valuable to researchers in computer vision and machine learning. The general approach is simple, but logical. Experiments are promising; however, it is missing comparisons to other baselines.

Specifically, the authors should consider including the following baselines:

1. Using full images as positive examples, and images and regions from other classes as negative examples. Train and fine-tune all layers of the CNN.
2. Doing the same as above, but using a MIL approach, where you iteratively select the highest scoring positive region for each image from set A (instead of the full image).
3. Training and fine-tuning the CNN for classification. At test time, run the classifier and predict a single bounding box at the center of the image (e.g., the mean bounding box for ImageNet).

Including these baselines would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach and help to establish its strengths and weaknesses.

Clarity: The paper is clear and well-written. The clarity of the experiments section could be improved, in particular in terms of elaborating on the explanation of what each method in table 1 refers to (does this mean applying Eq. 1 or 2 to a subset of CNN layers?). Additionally, the authors could provide more details on the implementation of their approach, such as the specific hyperparameters used and the computational resources required.

Originality: The paper is sufficiently novel to be accepted, although the basic approach is simple and specific to certain types of CNN networks. The authors provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from existing methods and demonstrate its effectiveness on a large-scale dataset.

Significance: The problem and results are significant and valuable. The paper addresses a relevant problem and the fact that decent detection results were obtained on a large-scale dataset without bounding box labels is compelling. However, the paper is missing some obvious baselines, and it is very possible that results are capitalizing off of improved results of CNN features rather than due to introducing a better way of training object detectors from weak supervision.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper addresses a significant challenge in scaling object detection to a large number of categories.
* The proposed approach is simple and logical, and the experiments are promising.
* The paper provides a clear and concise background on the problem and a thorough explanation of the proposed approach.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper is missing comparisons to other baselines, which makes it difficult to establish the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.
* The clarity of the experiments section could be improved, particularly in terms of elaborating on the explanation of what each method in table 1 refers to.
* The paper is missing some obvious baselines, and it is very possible that results are capitalizing off of improved results of CNN features rather than due to introducing a better way of training object detectors from weak supervision.
