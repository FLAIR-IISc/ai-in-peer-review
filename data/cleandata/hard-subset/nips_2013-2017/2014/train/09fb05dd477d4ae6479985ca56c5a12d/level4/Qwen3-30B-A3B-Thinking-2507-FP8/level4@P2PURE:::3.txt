

This paper proposes a method to adapt a Krizhevsky et al. classification CNN (trained without bounding box annotations) for object detection using a limited set of classes with bounding box annotations. During training, the approach: 1) trains a CNN for classification on all classes; 2) trains a region-based CNN on classes with bounding boxes (including a negative background class); and 3) integrates the background class into the full CNN model while adapting select weights. At test time, the model processes multiple image regions per test image.  

Quality: The paper presents an interesting and compelling approach that achieves reasonable detection results. However, these results largely leverage Krizhevsky et al.'s model success rather than introducing a fundamentally novel method; they remain valuable to computer vision and machine learning researchers. The general approach is simple yet logical, and experiments are promising. Crucially, the paper lacks comparisons to key baselines:  
1) Training on full images as positives and other-class regions/images as negatives, fine-tuning all CNN layers.  
2) Same as (1), but using a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach where the highest-scoring positive region per image is iteratively selected.  
3) Training a classification CNN, then at test time predicting a single bounding box centered on the image (e.g., the mean bounding box for ImageNet classes).  
The proposed method's superiority over these baselines is unclear; it appears less justified than baseline (1) and likely inferior to baseline (2). Including baseline (3) is essential to contextualize the problem's difficulty and dataset characteristics.  

Clarity: The paper is well-written and clear overall. The experiments section could be improved by clarifying what each method in Table 1 entails (e.g., whether Eq. 1 or 2 is applied to a subset of CNN layers).  

Originality: The approach is sufficiently novel for acceptance, though it is simple and specific to certain CNN architectures.  

Significance: The problem addressed is relevant, and obtaining decent detection results on a large-scale dataset without bounding box labels is compelling. However, the results may primarily reflect improved CNN features rather than a superior weakly supervised training strategy for object detectors, as the paper lacks critical baselines.
