This paper proposes a framework for adapting deep neural networks, originally trained for image classification, to the task of object detection. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 dataset, where it ranked 4th in the object detection challenge. The paper is clearly presented and generally easy to follow, and the proposed CNN architecture is both interesting and simple to implement. Overall, the quality of the work is good. However, there are two primary concerns that the authors should address.

First, the core contribution of this work—the adaptation method—requires a more thorough justification. The underlying motivations for the design choices are not presented in a principled fashion. For example, in Section 3.2 (Page 5, Line 231), it is not clear why the weights for the adapted detection task are formulated as a summation of Wj^c and W{avg}. The intuition behind this 'offset parameter' is not explained. Consequently, it is also unclear why the kNN variant of the method performs better.

Second, the comparison with other methods is not conducted on a level playing field. The authors evaluate their model using part of the validation set, while competing methods are typically benchmarked on the test set, which makes a direct comparison unfair. Although the authors mention in a footnote (Line 322) that R-CNN has similar performance on the `val2` set, this subset is small, and therefore the reported performance gains may not be statistically significant.

In summary, the authors have developed a framework that achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art models. The paper would be significantly strengthened, however, by providing a clearer explanation for the proposed adaptation method and by conducting comparisons under standard evaluation settings to ensure a fair assessment against prior work.
