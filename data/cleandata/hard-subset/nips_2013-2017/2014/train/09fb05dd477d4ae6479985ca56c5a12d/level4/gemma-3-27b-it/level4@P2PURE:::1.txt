This paper addresses the problem of object detection, specifically the disparity between the availability of bounding box annotations and the abundance of category labels for object categorization. The authors propose a method that adapts object classifiers to the detection task, offering a relatively simple and straightforward solution. Their experimental design involves training on 100 categories with both bounding boxes and labels, and evaluating on 100 held-out categories for which only category labels are available. The results demonstrate reasonable performance, bridging approximately 35% of the gap between an oracle and a baseline on these held-out categories.

**Quality and clarity:** This is a well-written and clear paper.

**Originality:** The paper uniquely combines adaptation of object classifiers for detection with transfer learning across different category sets. To the best of my knowledge, this is a novel and interesting contribution, though not a groundbreaking one.

**Significance:** The primary contribution of this work lies in its novel approach. Assessing its long-term significance is challenging, as more sophisticated methods are likely to emerge; however, this is a natural progression. A key consideration is the inherent trade-off between performance (mAP) and the use of fewer bounding boxes, and it is unclear whether this trade-off is justifiable. (Furthermore, the importance of the tackled problem is debatable. While bounding boxes are more costly to acquire than category labels, future resource allocation may alleviate this issue. The large-scale labeling of images with numerous object categories, once considered intractable, has become increasingly feasible.)

**Detailed issues that should be addressed:**

- In Figure 3a, please clarify which methods utilize category labels for detection, beyond your method and R-CNN. A clear statement regarding this comparison is needed to ensure an apples-to-apples evaluation.
- My assessment of the proposed approach’s effectiveness centers on the blue bars in Figure 3b, which indicate performance approximately one-third of the way between the baseline and the oracle. Do the authors agree that this constitutes a key result? If so, it should be highlighted, rather than the 78% reported on line 341, which is inaccurate for two reasons: 1) it should be evaluated solely on the held-out categories, and 2) it should consider the baseline performance, not just the oracle. A similar issue arises with the claim of 50% improvement on line 425.
- The authors should dedicate a table or a subsection to clearly delineating the differences between their method and R-CNN, the closest competing approach. Currently, these distinctions are scattered. I recommend removing Figure 6, as it presents only anecdotal examples.

**Minor issues (no need to respond)**

- The paper inconsistently refers to the method as "DNN" or "DDA."
- There is a typo on line 431 (“classi?ers”).

Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a reasonably novel approach. I recommend acceptance and believe it will be a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. While the results are likely to be superseded by more complex methods – a consequence of the method’s simplicity and its pioneering nature – the long-term impact of this work remains to be seen.
