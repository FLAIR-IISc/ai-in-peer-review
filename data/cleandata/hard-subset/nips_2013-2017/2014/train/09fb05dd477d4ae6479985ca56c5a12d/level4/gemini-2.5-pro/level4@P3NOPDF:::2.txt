This paper presents a framework for adapting deep neural networks, pre-trained on image classification, to the task of object detection. The proposed method is evaluated on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 detection dataset, where it achieves a commendable 4th place in the challenge, demonstrating its empirical effectiveness. The paper is clearly written, the methodology is generally easy to follow, and the proposed CNN architecture is interesting and simple to implement. Overall, this is a solid contribution with strong results. However, there are two primary areas where the paper could be significantly improved.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly presented, making the proposed approach accessible to the reader.
*   **Simplicity and Practicality:** The proposed adaptation of the CNN architecture is both interesting and straightforward to implement, which is a valuable quality for reproducibility and future work.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The method's high ranking in the ILSVRC-2013 detection challenge provides compelling evidence of its practical utility and competitiveness.

**Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Justification of the Adaptation Method:** The primary weakness of the paper is the lack of a principled justification for its core technical contribution. While the method is shown to work well empirically, the motivation behind the specific design choices is not sufficiently explained. For instance, in Section 3.2 (Page 5, Line 231), the weights for the adapted detection task are defined as a summation of class-specific weights (Wj^c) and an average weight vector (W{avg}). The paper does not provide a clear intuition or theoretical basis for this formulation. The physical meaning or intended role of this "offset parameter" (W{avg}) remains ambiguous. This lack of a clear explanatory foundation makes it difficult for the reader to understand the underlying mechanics of the model and, as a consequence, to appreciate why the proposed kNN variant yields superior performance. A more thorough explanation of the rationale would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

2.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparison:** A second major concern relates to the experimental evaluation protocol used for comparing against other methods. The authors report results on a subset of the validation data (`val2`) rather than the standard test set, which is typically used to benchmark performance in the literature. This discrepancy makes a direct and fair comparison with prior art, such as the R-CNN model, challenging. The authors mention in a footnote (Line 322) that R-CNN exhibits similar performance on this `val2` set. However, given the small size of this subset, any reported performance gains may not be statistically significant. To make a more convincing case for the proposed method's advantages, a comparison conducted on the standard test set, or at least a more rigorous statistical analysis of the results on the `val2` set, is necessary.

**Conclusion:**

In summary, this paper introduces a simple yet effective framework for adapting image classification networks to object detection, supported by strong performance in a competitive challenge. However, the work is currently held back by an insufficient justification for its core methodology and an experimental comparison that is not on equal footing with competing methods. Addressing these two points by providing deeper theoretical motivation and conducting a more standard comparative evaluation would substantially strengthen the paper and more clearly solidify its contributions to the field.
