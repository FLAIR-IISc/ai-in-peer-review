

The authors present a framework for adapting deep neural networks pretrained on image classification tasks to object detection, demonstrating competitive results on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 object detection challenge (ranked 4th). The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and the proposed adaptation mechanism is conceptually straightforward and implementable. Overall, the technical quality is strong, and the contribution holds potential value for the community. However, two critical issues require clarification before the paper can be accepted.

First, the core adaptation method lacks sufficient theoretical justification. The paper introduces a weight adaptation strategy where detection task weights are computed as a sum of class-specific weights (\(W_j^c\)) and an average weight (\(W_{\text{avg}}\)), yet the rationale for this formulation is not rigorously established. Specifically, Section 3.2 (Page 5, Line 231) states this "offset parameter" without explaining its physical or mathematical significance. Why should the adapted weights be a linear combination of these terms? How does this choice relate to the underlying task shift between classification and detection? Without this justification, the method appears ad hoc, and the claim that the kNN variant outperforms alternatives remains unsupported. The authors should provide a principled derivation (e.g., via optimization objectives or task alignment arguments) or empirical ablation studies to substantiate the design choices.

Second, the comparison against baselines is compromised by the use of the validation set (val2) rather than the official test set. As noted in Footnote 2 (Line 322), the authors acknowledge RCNN achieves similar performance on val2 but justify this as "small set" for statistical significance. This is a significant methodological flaw: using the validation set for comparison invalidates the comparison’s fairness and replicability. The ImageNet detection challenge explicitly requires test set evaluations for ranking, and the val2 subset is too small (e.g., 5,000 images) to draw reliable conclusions about performance gains. For instance, a marginal improvement on val2 may reflect overfitting to this small partition rather than genuine generalization. The authors must re-run comparisons on the test set (or provide a clear justification for the val2 usage, which is uncommon in detection benchmarks) to ensure the claimed competitiveness is valid.

Additionally, while the paper states the approach achieves "comparable performance to state-of-the-art," the comparisons are conducted under inconsistent experimental settings (e.g., different backbone networks or training protocols). This obscures whether the adaptation method itself drives gains or whether results are confounded by these variables. A direct comparison with methods like Faster R-CNN or R-FCN, using identical training procedures and hardware, would strengthen the claims.

In summary, the paper’s core idea is promising and well-executed in its presentation. The adaptation framework is novel and simple, and the results are broadly encouraging. However, the lack of justification for the key adaptation mechanism and the invalid comparison methodology undermine the paper’s contributions. Addressing these points—by providing a principled rationale for the weight adaptation strategy and conducting comparisons on the test set—would significantly elevate the paper’s impact and credibility. The authors should prioritize these revisions to align the evidence with the claims. With these improvements, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field.
