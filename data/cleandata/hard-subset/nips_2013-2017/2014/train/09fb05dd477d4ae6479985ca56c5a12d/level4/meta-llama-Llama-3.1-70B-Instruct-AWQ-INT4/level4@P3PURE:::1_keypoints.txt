- Paper tackles object detection by adapting object classifiers for detection to avoid needing bounding boxes at scale.

- Proposed algorithm is simple and straightforward; simplicity is not inherently a flaw.

- Experimental protocol: train on 100 categories with both labels and bounding boxes, test on 100 left-out categories with only category labels.

- Results: method closes roughly 35% of the gap between baseline and oracle on the 100 left-out categories.

- Paper is well written and of good quality.

- Originality: combines known ideas (classifier→detector adaptation and category-set adaptation); novel and interesting but not groundbreaking.

- Significance: contribution is noteworthy but long-term impact is uncertain and likely to be surpassed by more sophisticated methods; trade-off between fewer bounding boxes and lower mAP is hard to judge objectively.

- Practical importance of the problem is debatable since collecting more bounding boxes may be a viable alternative.

- Figure 3a needs clear labeling of which methods use category labels for detection to allow fair comparisons.

- Figure 3b: the reviewer emphasizes the blue bars showing DNN is about one-third of the way from baseline to oracle; authors should highlight this instead of the claimed 78% figure.

- Claims of 78% (line 341) and 50% (line 425) are misleading—percentages should be computed relative to baseline and only on left-out categories, not simply as a fraction of the oracle.

- Paper should include a dedicated table/subsection clearly contrasting the proposed method with R-CNN (closest alternative) and de-emphasize anecdotal Figure 6.

- Minor issues: inconsistent naming of the method ("DNN" vs "DDA") and a typo on line 431 ("classi?ers").

- Recommendation: reviewer supports acceptance and presentation to the NIPS community.
