This paper addresses the significant challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, where obtaining bounding box annotations is prohibitively expensive. The core idea is to learn a general transformation from an image classifier to an object detector. This transformation is learned on a subset of categories for which both image-level labels and bounding box annotations are available, and is then applied to adapt classifiers for new categories that only have image-level labels. The authors frame this as a domain adaptation problem, transferring knowledge from the classification task (source) to the detection task (target). The proposed method, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), learns both a category-invariant feature transformation and a category-specific model transformation. Experiments on the ILSVRC 2013 detection dataset, simulating a scenario with 100 categories with bounding boxes and 100 held-out categories without, demonstrate that the approach significantly improves upon a baseline classifier.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality:** The paper proposes a novel and interesting approach by combining two ideas: adapting classifiers for detection (as in R-CNN) and adapting models across different sets of categories. To my knowledge, learning a generic "classifier-to-detector" transformation that can be applied to held-out categories is a new and valuable contribution.
*   **Significance:** The problem being addressed is of high practical importance. As classification models scale to tens of thousands of categories, the annotation bottleneck for detection becomes ever more acute. This work presents a pragmatic and direct methodology to help bridge this gap.
*   **Quality and Clarity:** The paper is of high quality, clearly written, and well-organized. The proposed algorithm is straightforward and easy to follow. The experiments are well-designed, with a proper ablation study (Figure 3) that effectively demonstrates the contribution of each component of the LSDA algorithm.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Contextualization of Results:** The reported "50% relative mAP boost" over the baseline is encouraging. However, a more insightful way to frame the main result would be in the context of the gap between the baseline (10.31 mAP) and the performance upper bound (32.8 mAP, Table 1). The full LSDA model (16.15 mAP) closes approximately 26% of this gap on the held-out categories. While this is a solid improvement, I would encourage the authors to highlight this framing to provide a more balanced perspective on the method's effectiveness.
*   **Comparison with R-CNN:** R-CNN is the most closely related work and serves as a major inspiration. The current manuscript mentions the differences in passing, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by a dedicated table or subsection that explicitly contrasts LSDA with R-CNN. This should detail differences in data requirements (bounding boxes for all vs. a subset of categories), training pipeline (e.g., SVMs and bbox regression), and the resulting trade-offs in performance, training time, and scalability. This would help readers more clearly understand the specific contributions of this work. Using the space from Figure 6, which provides anecdotal examples, for this comparison could be a worthwhile trade-off.
*   **Clarity in Experimental Comparisons:** In Figure 3 (and associated tables), it is crucial to clarify which competing methods use which sources of data. For instance, are all methods pre-trained on the same classification data? A clear statement about the data used for each baseline and variant would ensure the comparisons are understood as "apples-to-apples."

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper tackles a significant and practical problem in scaling object detection.
2.  The proposed approach is novel, intuitive, and represents a clever combination of existing concepts.
3.  The experimental results clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the adaptation method over a reasonable baseline.
4.  The paper is well-written, clear, and the ablation study provides valuable insights into the method's components.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The performance improvement, while notable, still leaves a large gap to the fully-supervised oracle. The paper could do a better job of discussing this performance vs. data cost trade-off.

Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a novel and useful technique for a well-motivated problem. The strengths clearly outweigh the weaknesses, and I believe the work is a valuable contribution that deserves to be seen by the NIPS community. The suggestions above are intended to further strengthen the manuscript for its final version.
