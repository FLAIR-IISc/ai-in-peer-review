

This paper proposes a method to adapt Krizhevsky et al.'s CNN (trained for classification without bounding box annotations) for object detection using only a small set of classes with bounding box labels. The approach involves three key training steps: (1) training a classification CNN on all classes; (2) training a region-based CNN on classes with bounding boxes (including a negative background class); and (3) integrating the background class into the full CNN while fine-tuning selected weights. At test time, the model processes multiple image regions.  

**Quality**: The work addresses a relevant problem and demonstrates promising results on a large-scale dataset, leveraging the robustness of Krizhevsky's CNN architecture. While the core methodology is straightforward and builds upon established CNN success rather than introducing radical innovation, it offers practical value for computer vision researchers. The experiments are generally compelling but critically lack comparisons to key baselines:  
1. **Full-image baseline**: Train a classification CNN using full images as positives and regions from other classes (or full images) as negatives, fine-tuning all layers.  
2. **MIL-based baseline**: Apply multiple-instance learning (MIL) to iteratively select top-scoring regions per image (instead of full-image supervision) for training.  
3. **Center-bounding-box baseline**: Train a classification CNN, then at test time predict a single bounding box centered on the image (e.g., the mean bounding box for ImageNet classes).  
The absence of these baselines weakens the evaluation. The proposed method may not outperform Baseline 1 (which uses full-image supervision) and likely underperforms Baseline 2 (MIL). Baseline 3 is essential to establish the difficulty of the task and dataset. Without these comparisons, it remains unclear whether the results stem from the proposed adaptation or merely benefit from superior CNN features.  

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and logically structured. However, the experiments section would benefit from greater precision. Specifically, Table 1’s descriptions of methods (e.g., "Region-based" or "Full-image") require clarification: do these approaches apply Equation 1 or 2 to specific CNN layers, or to the entire network? Explicitly stating this would enhance reproducibility.  

**Originality**: The approach is sufficiently novel for acceptance. While the methodology is conceptually simple and tailored to specific CNN architectures (e.g., Krizhevsky’s), it provides a valuable framework for weakly supervised object detection. The core idea—adapting a pre-trained classifier via region-based training with a background class—is novel in this context.  

**Significance**: The problem addressed (object detection with minimal bounding box annotations) is highly relevant to real-world applications where labeled data is scarce. The demonstration of decent detection performance on a large-scale dataset without bounding box labels is compelling and advances the practical applicability of weakly supervised learning. However, the significance is partially offset by the lack of baseline comparisons, which leaves open whether the gains are attributable to the proposed adaptation or to the inherent quality of the underlying CNN features.  

**Overall Recommendation**: The paper makes a meaningful contribution to weakly supervised object detection. With the addition of the three baseline comparisons (particularly Baseline 3, which establishes a simple but informative heuristic), the evaluation would be substantially strengthened. As currently presented, the results are plausible but not definitively superior to simpler alternatives. The authors should address these baselines in revision to solidify the claim that their method offers a meaningful advancement over existing weakly supervised approaches. Given the problem’s relevance and the paper’s clear presentation, acceptance is appropriate *if* the missing baselines are added. Without them, the work risks being perceived as incremental.
