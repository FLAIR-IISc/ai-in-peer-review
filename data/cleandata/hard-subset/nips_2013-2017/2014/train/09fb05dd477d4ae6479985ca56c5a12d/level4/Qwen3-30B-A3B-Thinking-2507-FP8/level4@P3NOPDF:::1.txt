

This paper tackles a compelling challenge in object detection: leveraging category labels without corresponding bounding boxes. The authors propose adapting object classifiers directly for detection, bypassing the need for box annotations. The method is conceptually simple and well-executed, with clear experimental design. Training on 100 categories (with labels and boxes) and testing on 100 held-out categories (with labels only), the approach closes approximately 35% of the performance gap between an oracle (full box annotation) and a baseline (no box supervision) on the held-out set.

**Quality and Clarity:** The paper is well-written, technically sound, and clearly structured. The methodology and results are presented with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The work combines two established concepts—adaptation of classifiers for detection and domain adaptation across categories—into a novel framework for the specific setting of label-only test categories. While not a breakthrough, this combination represents a meaningful and interesting contribution to the literature. To the best of this reviewer's knowledge, the approach is novel.

**Significance:** The primary contribution is a simple, effective method for detection with minimal box supervision. However, the significance is modest: the results are incremental (closing 35% of the gap) and inherently sacrifice mAP performance by using fewer bounding boxes. Crucially, the paper does not establish whether this trade-off is justified. The long-term impact is uncertain, as the approach is likely to be surpassed by more sophisticated methods. Moreover, the core problem—cost of box annotation—may become less critical as data collection techniques advance (e.g., active learning, weak supervision), making the long-term relevance of this specific solution debatable.

**Detailed Issues:**
1.  **Figure 3a Clarity:** The figure compares methods but does not specify which approaches (beyond the authors' method and R-CNN) utilize category labels for detection. This ambiguity undermines the apples-to-apples comparison. The authors must explicitly state which methods leverage category labels in this figure.
2.  **Key Result Emphasis:** The main finding is the ~35% gap closure (blue bars in Fig 3b), not the 78% figure (line 341) or the 50% improvement claim (line 425). The 78% misrepresents the result: it should reflect performance *relative to the baseline* on the *held-out categories* (not the oracle), and the 50% claim lacks baseline context. The authors should highlight the gap closure metric prominently and correct the other figures.
3.  **Comparison with R-CNN:** The distinction between the proposed method and R-CNN (the closest competitor) is currently scattered. A dedicated table or subsection clearly outlining the differences (e.g., supervision requirements, adaptation strategy, computational cost) would strengthen the contribution. Figure 6 (anecdotal examples) could be removed to prioritize this comparison.

**Minor Issues:**
*   The method is inconsistently referred to as "DNN" and "DDA" (line 431 typo: "classi?ers" → "classifiers").
*   The significance argument regarding the "long-term challenge" of box annotation (side note) is tangential and weakens the review; the core technical contribution is the adaptation approach itself.

**Overall Recommendation:** This is a solid, clear paper presenting a novel (though incremental) adaptation strategy for detection with minimal box supervision. The method is simple, the results are reasonable, and the problem addressed is relevant. While the performance gains are modest and the long-term significance uncertain, the work provides a valuable baseline for future research. The minor issues noted above are easily addressable. The paper deserves acceptance at NIPS as a clear, useful contribution to the field.
