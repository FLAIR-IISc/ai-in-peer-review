The authors propose a framework to adapt deep neural networks trained for image classification to object detection tasks. Evaluation on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 dataset demonstrates that the proposed method achieves competitive performance in the object detection challenge. The paper is clearly presented and generally easy to follow, and the proposed CNN structure is interesting and straightforward to implement. Overall, the paper’s quality is good. However, there are two concerns the authors could address to strengthen the work.

First, while the adaptation method is a key contribution, it lacks a fully principled justification. The underlying motivations are not always clearly presented. For example, in Section 3.2 (Page 5, Line 231), it’s unclear why the weights for the adapted detection task are a summation of  W<sub>c</sub><sup>j</sup> and δ<sub>avg</sub>. The physical meaning of this ‘offset parameter’ is not adequately explained. Consequently, the rationale behind using the k-nearest neighbors (kNN) variant remains unclear.

Second, the comparison with other methods utilizes a portion of the validation set rather than the dedicated test set, potentially leading to an unfair comparison. The authors acknowledge this in a footnote (Line 322), stating that R-CNN exhibits similar performance on the validation set, but argue the performance gain isn’t statistically significant due to the limited set size. 

In summary, the authors propose a valuable framework for leveraging image classification models for object detection. The method achieves competitive results, but further clarification of the adaptation strategy and a more rigorous comparison with existing methods (ideally on a common test set) would significantly enhance the paper's impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem: scaling object detection to a large number of categories with limited bounding box annotations.
*   The proposed method achieves strong performance on the ImageNet detection challenge.
*   The paper is well-written and clear, making it accessible to a broad audience.
*   The code and models are publicly available, promoting reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical justification for the adaptation method, particularly the weighted summation of weights, is weak.
*   The comparison with other methods is not entirely fair due to the use of only a portion of the validation set.
*   The ablation study, while informative, could be expanded to provide more insights into the contribution of each component.
