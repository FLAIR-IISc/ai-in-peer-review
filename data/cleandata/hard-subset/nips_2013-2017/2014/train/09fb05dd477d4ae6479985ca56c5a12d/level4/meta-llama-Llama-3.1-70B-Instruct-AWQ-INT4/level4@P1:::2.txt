The authors proposed a framework to adapt deep neural networks trained from image classification tasks to object detection tasks. The proposed method, Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), is evaluated on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 dataset, where it achieves a significant improvement over a baseline method. This paper is clearly presented and generally easy to follow, and the proposed CNN structure is interesting and simple to implement. Overall, the quality of the paper is good.

The main contribution of this work is the adaptation method, which transforms image classifiers into object detectors using a three-stage process: category invariant adaptation, category specific adaptation, and detection. The authors demonstrate that their method can adapt classifiers for categories without bounding box annotations into detectors, achieving a 50% relative mAP boost over a baseline method. This is a significant improvement, especially considering that the method does not require bounding box annotations for all categories.

However, there are two concerns that the authors could improve. Firstly, the adaptation method proposed in this paper needs better justification. The details of the underlying motivations are not presented in a principled fashion. For example, in Section 3.2, it is not clear why the weights for the adapted detection task are a summation of the original weights and the learned offset. The physical meaning of this 'offset parameter' is not explained. As a consequence, it is also not clear why the kNN variant is better. A more detailed explanation of the adaptation process and its underlying assumptions would strengthen the paper.

Secondly, in comparing with other methods, the authors used part of the validation set, but not the test set, making the comparison not entirely fair. The authors mention in a footnote that R-CNN has similar performance on the val2 set, but due to the small size of this set, the performance gain is not statistically justified. It would be beneficial to include a comparison with state-of-the-art methods on the test set to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.

In terms of related work, the authors provide a thorough discussion of existing methods for object detection and domain adaptation. However, they could further clarify how their method differs from other approaches, such as Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) methods, which also use weak labels for training detectors.

In conclusion, the authors proposed a framework to adapt deep neural networks trained from image classification tasks to object detection tasks. The proposed method has comparable performance to state-of-the-art models, but the comparisons are on different settings. The adaptation method needs better explanation, and a more comprehensive evaluation on the test set would strengthen the paper. Nevertheless, the paper presents an interesting and simple-to-implement CNN structure, and the results demonstrate the potential of the proposed method for large-scale object detection.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The proposed method achieves a significant improvement over a baseline method.
* The method is simple to implement and has a low computational cost.
* The paper presents an interesting CNN structure that can be used for large-scale object detection.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The adaptation method needs better justification and explanation.
* The comparison with other methods is not entirely fair due to the use of part of the validation set.
* The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation on the test set.
