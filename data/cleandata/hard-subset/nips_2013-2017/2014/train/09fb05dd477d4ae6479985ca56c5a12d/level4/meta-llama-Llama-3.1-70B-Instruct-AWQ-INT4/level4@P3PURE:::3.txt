This paper presents a method for adapting a Krizhevsky CNN network, trained for classification without bounding box annotations, to perform object detection with a small number of classes having annotated bounding boxes. The approach consists of three stages: (1) training a CNN for classification on images of all classes, (2) training a CNN on image regions of classes labeled with bounding boxes, with an added negative background region class, and (3) incorporating the background class into the full CNN model while adapting some of the CNN weights. At test time, the CNN is applied to multiple image regions in a test image.

Quality: This paper is interesting and fairly compelling, achieving reasonable results. However, these results largely build upon the success of Krizhevsky et al.'s model rather than introducing a radically new concept. Nonetheless, the findings will be valuable to researchers in computer vision and machine learning. The general approach is simple yet logical. The experiments are promising, but they lack comparisons to other baselines, including: (1) using full images as positive examples and images and regions from other classes as negative examples, training and fine-tuning all layers of the CNN; (2) adopting a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, where the highest-scoring positive region is iteratively selected for each image from set A (instead of the full image); and (3) training and fine-tuning the CNN for classification, then running the classifier at test time to predict a single bounding box at the center of the image (e.g., the mean bounding box for ImageNet). It is unclear whether the proposed approach would outperform these baselines, and it is uncertain whether it makes more sense than baseline 1 or less sense than baseline 2. Including the third baseline would be essential to convey the problem's difficulty and dataset characteristics to the reader.

Clarity: The paper is clear and well-written. However, the clarity of the experiments section could be improved, particularly in elaborating on the explanation of each method in Table 1 (e.g., whether it involves applying Eq. 1 or 2 to a subset of CNN layers).

Originality: The paper is sufficiently novel to be accepted, although the basic approach is simple and specific to certain types of CNN networks.

Significance: The problem and results are significant and valuable. The paper addresses a relevant problem, and the fact that decent detection results were obtained on a large-scale dataset without bounding box labels is compelling. Nevertheless, the paper's omission of obvious baselines raises concerns that the results may be capitalizing on improved CNN features rather than introducing a better way of training object detectors from weak supervision.
