

This paper tackles the object detection challenge of obtaining bounding boxes at a scale matching category label availability, by adapting object classifiers for detection. The approach is conceptually simple and well-executed. The authors train on 100 categories with both labels and bounding boxes, then evaluate on 100 held-out categories (with only labels), achieving results that close approximately 35% of the performance gap between a baseline detector and an oracle (perfect detection with all boxes).  

**Quality and Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly presented.  
**Originality:** The work combines two established concepts—adapting classifiers for detection and adapting across category sets—resulting in a novel and interesting, though not groundbreaking, approach.  
**Significance:** While the method is promising as the first to explore this specific adaptation path, its long-term impact is uncertain. Performance gains are inherently limited by the reduced use of bounding boxes (lower mAP), and the paper lacks a principled evaluation of this trade-off. The core challenge—obtaining bounding boxes at scale—is valid, but the assumption that annotation costs cannot be mitigated by future data collection efforts (e.g., through large-scale labeling projects) warrants further discussion.  

**Key Issues Requiring Clarification:**  
1. **Figure 3a:** The comparison must explicitly state which methods (beyond the authors' approach and R-CNN) use category labels to aid detection. Without this, the "apples-to-apples" evaluation is unclear.  
2. **Performance Metrics:** The primary result is the blue bars in Figure 3b (DNN closing 35% of the gap to oracle relative to baseline), not the 78% claim in line 341 (which misrepresents the gap calculation). The 50% improvement claim (line 425) similarly fails to account for the baseline. The authors should emphasize the gap-closure metric and correct these inaccuracies.  
3. **Comparison to R-CNN:** The differences between the proposed method (DDA) and R-CNN need consolidation into a dedicated table or subsection. Current descriptions are scattered; Figure 6 (anecdotal examples) can be removed to prioritize clarity.  

**Minor Issues:**  
- Clarify the method’s name: It is "DDA" (not "DNN").  
- Correct typo: "classi?ers" (line 431).  

**Recommendation:** This is a well-executed, clearly written paper proposing a novel adaptation strategy for detection. While results are preliminary and likely to be surpassed by more sophisticated methods, the simplicity and conceptual novelty justify its contribution. The method represents a valid first step in leveraging category labels for detection without explicit bounding box annotations. The paper deserves acceptance at NIPS, as it offers a clear, actionable direction for future work in scalable detection. The minor issues noted are easily resolvable in revision.
