This paper tackles the problem of object detection, focusing on the challenge of acquiring bounding box annotations at the same scale that category labels are available. The authors circumvent this difficulty by proposing a method to adapt object classifiers to perform the detection task. The resulting algorithm is elegant in its simplicity, which is a strength. The experimental setup involves training on 100 categories with full supervision (both category labels and bounding boxes) and testing on 100 held-out categories for which only category labels are provided during training. The performance is promising, closing approximately 35% of the gap between a baseline and an oracle on the 100 held-out classes.

**Quality and clarity:** The paper is of high quality and is clearly articulated.

**Originality:** This work synthesizes two established concepts: adapting classifiers for detection and adapting models from a known set of categories to a new one. To my knowledge, this specific combination is novel and presents an interesting direction, though it is not a groundbreaking departure from prior work.

**Significance:** The primary contribution of this paper lies in its novel (though not revolutionary) formulation of the problem. From a results perspective, the enduring impact is difficult to ascertain, as it is probable that future, more complex methods will achieve superior performance. The core issue with the results is that the approach inherently trades detection accuracy (mAP) for a reduction in annotation requirements (fewer bounding boxes), and there is no objective framework to determine if this compromise is beneficial.

(On a related note, a broader question remains regarding the long-term relevance of the problem itself. While bounding boxes are currently more "expensive" to collect than image-level labels, it is conceivable that large-scale annotation efforts could eventually render this issue moot, much as was the case for image classification datasets with tens of thousands of categories.)

**Detailed issues that should be addressed:**

*   For the comparisons in Figure 3a, the authors should explicitly state which of the compared methods—aside from their own and R-CNN—leverage category-level labels during detection. This clarification is crucial for ensuring a fair, "apples-to-apples" evaluation.
*   My evaluation of the proposed method's effectiveness is primarily based on the blue bars in Figure 3b, which indicate that the DNN approach closes roughly one-third of the performance gap between the baseline and the oracle. Do the authors agree that this is one of the main takeaways? If so, this result should be highlighted more prominently than the 78% figure (line 341). That figure is less informative for two reasons: 1) the assessment should focus on the held-out categories, which represent the core challenge, and 2) performance gains should be measured relative to the baseline, not merely as a percentage of the oracle's performance. A similar critique applies to the "50% improvement" claim (line 425).
*   The paper would be strengthened by a more direct and consolidated comparison to R-CNN, which appears to be the most similar competing method. At present, the distinctions are scattered throughout the manuscript. I would suggest the authors dedicate a distinct subsection or a table to clearly enumerate these differences, potentially by sacrificing the anecdotal examples shown in Figure 6.

**Minor issues (no need to respond):**

*   The proposed method's name is inconsistent. Is it "DNN" or "DDA"?
*   There is a typo on line 431 ("classi?ers").

Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a reasonably novel approach. This reviewer believes the paper merits acceptance and will be of interest to the NIPS community. While the quantitative results will almost certainly be surpassed in the future—and the method's simplicity is acceptable for an initial work on this problem—the long-term significance of this line of research remains to be determined.
