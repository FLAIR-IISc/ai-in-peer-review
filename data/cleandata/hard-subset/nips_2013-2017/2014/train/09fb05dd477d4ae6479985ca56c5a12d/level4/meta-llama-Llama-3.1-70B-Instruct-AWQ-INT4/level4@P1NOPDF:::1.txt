This paper addresses the challenge of object detection, particularly the issue of obtaining bounding boxes on a scale comparable to the availability of category labels for object categorization. The authors sidestep this challenge by proposing to adapt object classifiers for the detection task. Their algorithm is relatively simple and straightforward, which is not inherently a bad thing. 

Their experimental protocol uses 100 categories for training (with both category labels and bounding boxes) and tests on 100 left-out categories, which only have category labels. The results obtained were reasonably good, bridging approximately 35% of the gap between an oracle and a baseline on the 100 left-out categories.

Quality and clarity: This is a well-written paper of good quality.

Originality: This paper combines two existing ideas related to adaptation: adapting object classifiers for detection and adapting from one set of categories to another. To the best of this reviewer's knowledge, this is a novel and interesting idea, although not groundbreaking.

Significance: The main contribution of this paper is the novel (albeit not groundbreaking) approach. However, judging the long-term significance based on the results is challenging, as they will likely be surpassed later by more sophisticated methods (which is to be expected). The primary issue with the results is that they inevitably sacrifice performance (mAP) by using fewer bounding boxes, and there is no objective way to assess whether this trade-off is worthwhile.

(As a side note, it is unclear whether the issue being tackled is a genuinely important long-term challenge. Although bounding boxes are more "expensive" to obtain than category labels, it is uncertain whether throwing resources at the problem would not resolve it. Not too many years ago, having millions of images labeled with 20,000 object categories seemed almost impossible...)

Detailed issues that should be addressed:

- In Figure 3a, the authors should clearly state which methods do (or do not) use category labels to aid detection, aside from their method and R-CNN, to ensure an apples-to-apples comparison.

- This reviewer's assessment of the proposed approach is based on the blue bars in Figure 3b, which show that DNN is approximately one-third between the baseline and oracle. The authors should acknowledge that this is one of the main results and highlight it, rather than the 78% (line 341), which is not the correct number for two reasons: 1) the evaluation should only consider the left-out categories, and 2) it should not simply be a percentage of oracle performance (but rather take the baseline into account). A similar issue applies to the claim of a 50% improvement (line 425).

- The authors should devote a table or a subsection to clearly outlining the differences between their method and R-CNN, which appears to be the closest competing alternative. Currently, these statements seem scattered or not as prominent as they could be. This reviewer suggests "sacrificing" Figure 6, which presents anecdotal examples.

Minor issues (no need to respond):

- Is the proposed method referred to as "DNN" or "DDA"?
- Typo on line 431 ("classi?ers")

Overall, this is a reasonably good paper proposing a reasonably novel approach. This reviewer believes the paper deserves to be accepted and seen by the NIPS community. While the results will almost certainly be surpassed soon, given the simplicity of the method (which is acceptable, as this is the first paper to take this approach), the long-term significance of the paper (and the approach in particular) remains to be seen.
