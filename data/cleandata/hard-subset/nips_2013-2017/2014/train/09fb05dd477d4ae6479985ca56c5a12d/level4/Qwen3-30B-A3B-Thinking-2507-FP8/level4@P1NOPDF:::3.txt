

This paper proposes a method to adapt a Krizhevsky-style CNN trained for classification (without bounding box annotations) to object detection, leveraging a small set of classes with bounding box labels. The approach involves three training steps: (1) training a CNN for classification on full images across all classes, (2) training a CNN on image regions for classes with bounding boxes while introducing a negative background class, and (3) integrating the background class into the full CNN model by adapting selected weights. At test time, the model processes multiple image regions within a test image.

**Quality:** The work presents an interesting and reasonably compelling solution. While the results largely build on the success of Krizhevsky et al.'s model rather than introducing a radical novelty, the approach offers tangible value for computer vision and machine learning researchers. The method is straightforward yet logical. However, the paper lacks comparisons to several key baselines that are critical for evaluating the proposed approach’s merit:  
1) A full-image baseline: Train using full images as positives and images/regions from other classes as negatives, fine-tuning all CNN layers.  
2) A MIL-based baseline: Apply multiple-instance learning by iteratively selecting the highest-scoring positive region per image from set A (instead of using full images).  
3) A simple center-bounding-box baseline: Train a classifier for classification, then predict detection by placing a bounding box at the image center (e.g., the mean bounding box from ImageNet).  
It remains unclear whether the proposed method outperforms these baselines, particularly baseline 1. In fact, baseline 1 appears more intuitive, while baseline 2 may be more effective. Including these comparisons is essential to contextualize the difficulty of the problem and dataset. Without them, the results may simply reflect improved CNN features rather than a novel weakly supervised training strategy.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and generally clear. The experiments section would benefit from greater precision, especially in explaining what each method in Table 1 entails—specifically, whether it applies Equation 1 or 2 to a subset of CNN layers.

**Originality:** The approach is sufficiently novel for acceptance, though its simplicity and specificity to certain CNN architectures limit its broad applicability.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is relevant, and the ability to achieve reasonable detection results on a large-scale dataset without bounding box annotations is compelling. The work makes a significant contribution to weakly supervised object detection, though the missing baselines weaken the claimed impact. The results’ reliance on strong CNN features rather than a fundamentally better training paradigm remains a valid concern that the paper does not adequately address.
