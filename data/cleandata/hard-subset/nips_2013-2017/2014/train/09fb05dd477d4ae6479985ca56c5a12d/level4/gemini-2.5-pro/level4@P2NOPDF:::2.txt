This paper presents a framework for adapting deep neural networks, pre-trained for image classification, to the task of object detection. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through its strong performance on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 object detection challenge, where it achieved 4th place. The paper is clearly written, and the proposed architecture is straightforward and simple to implement. Overall, this is a solid contribution. However, I have two main concerns that, if addressed, could significantly strengthen the paper.

First, the core contribution—the adaptation method itself—requires a more thorough justification. The motivation behind the specific design choices is not presented in a principled manner. For instance, in Section 3.2 (Page 5, Line 231), it is not clear why the weights for the adapted detection task are modeled as a sum of a class-specific term (Wj^c) and a class-agnostic average term (W{avg}). The intuition or theoretical reasoning for this "offset parameter" formulation is not explained. Consequently, it is difficult to understand the subsequent finding that the kNN variant of the method yields better performance, as the foundation for the original model is not fully established. Providing a deeper explanation for this modeling choice would make the contribution more compelling.

Second, the experimental comparison to other methods could be made more rigorous. The authors evaluate their method on a portion of the validation set, whereas competing methods are typically benchmarked on the test set. This difference in evaluation protocol prevents a direct and fair comparison. The authors acknowledge this in a footnote (Line 322), stating that R-CNN has similar performance on the `val2` set. However, given the small size of this subset, it is questionable whether the observed performance gains are statistically significant. A more convincing comparison would involve benchmarking against state-of-the-art methods on the standard test set to provide a clearer picture of the method's relative performance.

In summary, the paper introduces an interesting and effective framework for adapting classification networks to object detection. While the empirical results are strong, the paper's impact could be significantly enhanced by providing a more principled motivation for the proposed adaptation technique and by conducting a more direct comparison with leading methods under a standardized evaluation setting.
