This paper addresses the challenge of object detection, specifically the issue of obtaining bounding boxes on a scale similar to which category labels exist for object categorization. The authors propose a novel approach to adapt object classifiers for the detection task, sidestepping the challenge of collecting large amounts of bounding box annotations. Their algorithm is straightforward and combines two existing ideas related to adaptation: adapting object classifiers for detection and adapting from one set of categories to another.

The paper is well-written and of good quality. The experimental protocol is clear, using 100 categories for training (with both category labels and bounding boxes) and testing on 100 left-out categories with only category labels. The results obtained are reasonably good, filling about 35% of the gap between an oracle and a baseline on the 100 left-out categories.

The originality of this paper lies in its novel approach, which combines two existing ideas related to adaptation. While not ground-breaking, this idea is interesting and worth exploring further. The significance of this paper is mainly due to its novel approach, although the results-wise significance is hard to judge in the long term. The main issue with the results is that they inevitably sacrifice performance (mAP) by using fewer bounding boxes, and there is no objective way to judge if this trade-off is worth it.

One potential issue with the problem being tackled is that it may not be an important long-term challenge. With the rapid progress in computer vision, it is possible that the issue of collecting bounding box annotations will become less relevant in the future.

There are a few issues that the authors should address to improve the paper. Firstly, in Figure 3a, it is not clear which methods use category labels to help with detection, other than the proposed method and R-CNN. The authors should clearly state this to ensure an apples-to-apples comparison. Secondly, the authors should highlight the main result of the paper, which is that their approach fills about 35% of the gap between an oracle and a baseline on the 100 left-out categories. This result is more meaningful than the 78% number mentioned in the paper, which is not quite the right number for two reasons: it should be judged based only on the left-out categories, and it should not simply be a percentage of oracle performance. Finally, the authors should devote a table or a sub-section to clearly stating the differences from R-CNN, which seems to be the closest competing alternative.

Minor issues include a typo on line 431 ("classi?ers") and the inconsistent naming of the proposed method ("DNN" or "DDA"??). However, these issues do not detract from the overall quality of the paper.

Overall, this is a reasonably good paper proposing a reasonably novel approach. The paper deserves to be accepted and seen by the NIPS community, although the long-term significance of the paper and the approach remains to be seen.
