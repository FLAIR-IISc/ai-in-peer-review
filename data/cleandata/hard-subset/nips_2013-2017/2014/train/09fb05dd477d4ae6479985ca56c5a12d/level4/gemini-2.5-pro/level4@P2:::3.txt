**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Large Scale Detection through Adaptation (LSDA), a method for converting pre-trained image classifiers into object detectors. The central challenge addressed is the scarcity of bounding-box annotations for a vast number of object categories, in contrast to the relative abundance of image-level labels. The authors frame this problem as one of domain adaptation, where the goal is to learn a general transformation from the "classification domain" (using image-level labels) to the "detection domain" (using bounding-box labels). The method leverages a small set of categories for which both types of labels are available to learn this transformation. The core idea is that this learned adaptation, which includes learning a generic background model and transforming feature representations, can then be applied to the thousands of other classifiers for which no bounding box data exists. The approach is built upon the R-CNN framework and the successful CNN architecture of Krizhevsky et al. Experiments on the ILSVRC 2013 detection dataset show that LSDA significantly improves detection performance on held-out categories (those without bounding box annotations) compared to using the original classifier directly. The authors demonstrate the scalability of their method by producing a detector for over 7,600 categories.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Problem:** The paper tackles a crucial and highly practical problem in computer vision. Scaling object detection to tens of thousands of categories is a major goal, and the primary bottleneck is the prohibitive cost of collecting bounding box annotations. This work proposes a clear and logical strategy to mitigate this issue.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The core idea of learning a transferable, category-invariant transformation from a classifier to a detector is novel and compelling. Framing the task as a domain adaptation problem provides a principled foundation for the approach.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method achieves a 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out categories over a strong baseline (using the fine-tuned classifier directly). The ablation study in Figure 3 effectively demonstrates the contribution of each component of the proposed adaptation. Furthermore, the qualitative results and error analysis convincingly show that the method successfully learns to reduce localization errors and confusion with background—key challenges that distinguish detection from classification.

4.  **Demonstrated Scalability:** The successful creation of a 7,604-category detector is a powerful demonstration of the method's potential impact and usefulness to the research community and practitioners. Releasing the models and code is also a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Missing Key Baselines:** The primary weakness of the paper is the lack of comparison to several important and relevant baselines. While the comparison to the "classification only" network is a good start, it is not sufficient to fully assess the contribution of the proposed adaptation method. Plausible baselines that should be considered include:
    *   **Weakly-Supervised Training:** A standard approach where the network is fine-tuned for detection using full images of the held-out categories as positive examples, and images/regions from other classes as negatives. This would test whether a simpler form of weak supervision is sufficient.
    *   **Multiple Instance Learning (MIL):** A more sophisticated weakly-supervised approach where, during training, the model iteratively identifies and trains on the most likely positive region proposal within each image from the held-out set A. This is a very relevant alternative to the proposed adaptation technique.
    *   **A "Sanity Check" Baseline:** A simple baseline, such as predicting a fixed, mean-sized bounding box at the center of an image for a given class, would help calibrate the difficulty of the task and the dataset for the reader.

Without these comparisons, it is difficult to determine whether the performance gains are truly due to the novel adaptation technique or are largely a consequence of the powerful features from the underlying CNN, which might also elevate the performance of simpler weakly-supervised methods.

**Detailed Comments on Review Criteria**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the presented experiments are well-conducted. The ablation study provides valuable insight into the method's components. However, as noted above, the experimental evaluation is incomplete due to the absence of crucial baseline comparisons. This makes it difficult to fully validate the claim that this specific adaptation approach is superior to other forms of learning from weak supervision.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear and well-written. The motivation is strong and the proposed method is explained logically. The figures, particularly the model diagram (Fig. 2) and the error analysis (Fig. 5), are effective. The experimental section could be slightly improved; for example, the description of the different adaptation methods in Table 1 could be more explicit about exactly which layers are being fine-tuned in each configuration to make the results easier to interpret.

*   **Originality:** The work builds upon the successful R-CNN framework, but the core contribution—formulating the problem as learning a generic transformation from a classifier to a detector that can be applied to new classes without bounding boxes—is novel. It presents a new and interesting perspective on leveraging heterogeneous data sources (image-level vs. box-level annotations) for large-scale detection.

*   **Significance:** The potential impact of this work is very high. If successful and widely adopted, this approach could significantly accelerate progress towards building comprehensive, large-scale object detection systems. The problem is of great interest to both researchers and practitioners, and the strong results suggest this is a promising research direction that others are likely to build upon.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a highly significant and challenging problem in computer vision.
*   Proposes a novel and well-motivated framework for adapting classifiers to detectors.
*   Presents strong quantitative results, including a 50% relative mAP improvement on held-out classes.
*   Includes a thorough error analysis that supports the claims about how the method works.
*   Demonstrates impressive scalability with a >7.6K category detector.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation lacks comparisons to critical baselines, particularly standard weakly-supervised and MIL-based approaches. This makes it hard to contextualize the results and assess the true novelty and advantage of the proposed technique over alternatives.

**Recommendation**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel idea, impressive results, and high potential impact. The main drawback is the lack of a more comprehensive experimental comparison. However, the contributions are significant enough that the paper warrants acceptance. The authors should be strongly encouraged to include a discussion of the missing baselines and, if possible, add experimental results for at least one of them in the final version.
