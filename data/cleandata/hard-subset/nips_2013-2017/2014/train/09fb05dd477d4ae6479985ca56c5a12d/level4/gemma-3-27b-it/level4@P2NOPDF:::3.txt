This paper introduces a method for adapting a CNN pre-trained for image classification (without bounding box annotations) to the task of object detection, requiring only a limited number of classes with bounding box labels. The proposed approach involves three training stages: first, training a CNN for classification on all available classes; second, training a CNN on image regions belonging to classes with bounding box annotations, augmented with a negative background region class; and third, integrating the background class into the full CNN model while selectively adapting the network’s weights. During testing, the CNN processes multiple image regions to generate predictions.

**Quality:** This is a worthwhile contribution that demonstrates promising results. While the work largely leverages the success of Krizhevsky et al.’s CNN architecture, rather than presenting a fundamentally novel approach, it offers practical value to the computer vision and machine learning communities. The method is conceptually straightforward and logically sound. However, the evaluation would be significantly strengthened by comparisons to several important baselines. Specifically, I recommend including:

1) Fine-tuning the entire CNN for classification using full images as positive examples and image regions from other classes as negative examples.
2) Implementing a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, iteratively selecting the highest-scoring positive region per image (from the set of annotated images) instead of using the full image for training.
3) Training and fine-tuning the CNN solely for classification. At test time, applying the classifier to predict a single bounding box centered in the image (e.g., utilizing the mean bounding box location from a large dataset like ImageNet).

It is currently unclear whether the proposed method outperforms these baselines. Baseline 1, in particular, presents a strong alternative, and it’s not immediately obvious why the proposed method would be superior. Baseline 2 seems potentially more effective as well. Including Baseline 3 would provide crucial context, clarifying the inherent difficulty of the problem and the characteristics of the dataset. Determining if the improvements are due to the proposed training scheme or simply leveraging better CNN features is critical. 

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and generally clear. However, the presentation of the experimental results could be improved. Table 1, in particular, lacks sufficient detail. It's unclear what constitutes application of Eq. 1 or Eq. 2 – does this refer to applying the equations to all CNN layers, or only a subset? More specific elaboration on the experimental setup would enhance understanding.

**Originality:** The paper demonstrates sufficient novelty for acceptance, despite the relative simplicity of the core approach and its specific applicability to certain CNN architectures.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is both relevant and significant, and the results are valuable. Obtaining reasonable detection performance on a large-scale dataset without relying on extensive bounding box annotations is a compelling achievement. However, the absence of several intuitive baselines raises concerns about whether the reported improvements stem from the proposed training strategy or simply from the enhanced representational power of the underlying CNN features. Addressing these baselines will increase confidence in the significance of the contribution.
