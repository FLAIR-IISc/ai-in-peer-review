This paper proposes a method for adapting a pre-trained Krizhevsky CNN network for object detection tasks, given a small number of classes with annotated bounding boxes. The approach involves three stages during training: 1) training a CNN for classification on images of all classes, 2) training a CNN on image regions of classes with bounding box annotations, including a negative background region class, and 3) incorporating the background class into the full CNN model while adapting some of the CNN weights. At test time, the CNN is applied to multiple image regions in a test image.

Quality: This paper presents an interesting and fairly compelling approach that achieves reasonable results. While the results build upon the success of Krizhevsky et al.'s model, rather than introducing a radically new concept, they will still be valuable to researchers in computer vision and machine learning. The general approach is straightforward and logical. The experiments show promise, but lack comparisons to other baselines, including: 
1) using full images as positive examples and images and regions from other classes as negative examples, training and fine-tuning all CNN layers, 
2) employing a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, where the highest-scoring positive region is iteratively selected for each image, and 
3) training and fine-tuning the CNN for classification and predicting a single bounding box at the center of the image at test time. 
It is unclear whether the proposed approach would outperform these baselines. In particular, it is uncertain whether the proposed approach is superior to baseline 1, and it likely makes less sense than baseline 2. Including the third baseline would provide valuable context for the reader to understand the difficulty of the problem and dataset.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and clear, but the clarity of the experiments section could be improved. Specifically, the explanation of each method in Table 1 could be elaborated upon, including details on whether Eq. 1 or 2 is applied to a subset of CNN layers.

Originality: The paper is novel enough to warrant acceptance, despite the basic approach being simple and specific to certain types of CNN networks.

Significance: The problem and results are significant and valuable. The paper addresses a relevant problem, and the decent detection results obtained on a large-scale dataset without bounding box labels are compelling. However, the paper's omission of obvious baselines raises the possibility that the results may be attributed to improved CNN features rather than the introduction of a better training method for object detectors from weak supervision.
