

This paper addresses object detection by specifically tackling the challenge of obtaining bounding boxes at the same scale as category labels used in object categorization. The authors propose adapting object classifiers for detection, offering a straightforward algorithm that is not inherently problematic. Their experimental protocol trains on 100 categories with both category labels and bounding boxes, then tests on 100 held-out categories that only provide category labels. Results demonstrate reasonable performance, closing approximately 35% of the gap between an oracle and a baseline on the held-out categories.

Quality and clarity: This is a well-written, high-quality paper.
Originality: The work combines two established adaptation concepts—adapting classifiers for detection and adapting across categories. To the best of this reviewer’s knowledge, this combination is novel and interesting, though not groundbreaking.
Significance: The primary contribution is this novel (but not groundbreaking) approach. Long-term significance is difficult to assess from results alone, as performance gains will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods (which is expected). The main limitation is the inherent trade-off in performance (mAP) due to fewer bounding boxes, with no objective metric to evaluate whether this trade-off is justified.
(Side note: It remains unclear whether the problem addressed is a critical long-term challenge. While bounding boxes are more costly to obtain than category labels, increased resource allocation could potentially resolve this issue—similar to how millions of images with 20,000 categories became feasible relatively recently.)

Detailed issues requiring revision:
- Figure 3a: Clarify which methods (beyond the proposed approach and R-CNN) use category labels for detection to ensure a fair comparison.
- Figure 3b: The key result is the blue bars showing the DNN method achieves roughly one-third of the gap between baseline and oracle on held-out categories. The authors should emphasize this (not the 78% figure in line 341), which is misleading for two reasons: (1) it should be evaluated solely on held-out categories, and (2) it should be relative to the baseline, not a percentage of oracle performance. The same applies to the 50% improvement claim in line 425.
- R-CNN comparison: Consolidate the differences between the proposed method and R-CNN (the closest competitor) into a dedicated table or subsection, as current descriptions are scattered and insufficiently prominent. Consider removing Figure 6, which presents anecdotal examples.

Minor issues (no response needed):
- The method is referred to as both "DNN" and "DDA"—standardize the name.
- Typo on line 431 ("classi?ers").

Overall, this is a strong paper proposing a novel, simple approach. While results will likely be surpassed by future work, the method’s novelty as the first to take this approach warrants acceptance and visibility within the NIPS community. The long-term significance of the approach remains to be determined.
