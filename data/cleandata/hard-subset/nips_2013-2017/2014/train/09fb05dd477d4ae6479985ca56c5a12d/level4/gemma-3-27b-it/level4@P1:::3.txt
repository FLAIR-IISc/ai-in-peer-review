This paper presents a method for adapting a Krizhevsky CNN network, trained for classification without bounding box annotations, to perform object detection, given a small number of classes with annotated bounding boxes. The approach trains a CNN for classification on images of all classes, then trains another CNN on image regions from classes with bounding box labels, incorporating a negative background region class. Finally, it combines these components to adapt the full CNN model. At test time, the CNN is applied to multiple image regions within a test image.

**Quality:** This is a compelling and interesting paper that demonstrates promising results. While building upon the success of Krizhevsky et al.’s model, rather than presenting a radically new approach, the contribution will be valuable to researchers in computer vision and machine learning. The general approach is logical and straightforward. The experimental results are encouraging; however, comparisons to several important baselines are missing. Specifically, the authors should compare against:

1) Training and fine-tuning all layers of the CNN using full images as positive examples and image regions from other classes as negative examples.
2) Implementing a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, where the highest-scoring positive region is iteratively selected for each image in set A, instead of using the full image.
3) Training and fine-tuning the CNN for classification, and then, at test time, running the classifier to predict a single bounding box centered within the image (e.g., the mean bounding box for ImageNet).

It is unclear whether the proposed approach would outperform these baselines. In particular, baseline 1 seems conceptually similar and may be competitive, while baseline 2 leverages a related learning paradigm. Including baseline 3 would help establish the difficulty of the problem and dataset. 

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clear and well-written. The clarity of the experiments section could be improved by providing more detailed explanations of the methods presented in Table 1. For instance, it’s unclear whether the application of Equation 1 or 2 refers to a subset of CNN layers.

**Originality:** The paper is sufficiently novel to warrant consideration for acceptance, although the core approach is relatively simple and specific to certain CNN architectures.

**Significance:** The problem addressed and the results achieved are significant and valuable. The ability to obtain reasonable detection results on a large-scale dataset without relying on bounding box labels is compelling. However, the lack of comparison to obvious baselines raises concerns that the observed improvements may be attributable to the power of CNN features themselves, rather than to a fundamentally better method for training object detectors from weak supervision.

**Overall:**

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in object detection: scaling to large numbers of categories with limited labeled data.
*   Demonstrates significant improvement (+50% relative mAP) over a reasonable baseline (using raw classifier weights).
*   Provides a clear and well-structured approach to adapting classifiers for detection.
*   Offers publicly available models and software.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks crucial comparisons to relevant baselines, making it difficult to assess the true contribution of the proposed method.
*   The potential for capitalizing on improvements in CNN features, rather than presenting a truly novel detection approach, is not adequately addressed.
*   The presentation of experimental results could be clearer, particularly regarding the details of Table 1.



I recommend a borderline accept, contingent upon the authors including the suggested baseline comparisons and clarifying the experimental details. The potential impact of this work is high if the proposed method demonstrably outperforms existing techniques.
