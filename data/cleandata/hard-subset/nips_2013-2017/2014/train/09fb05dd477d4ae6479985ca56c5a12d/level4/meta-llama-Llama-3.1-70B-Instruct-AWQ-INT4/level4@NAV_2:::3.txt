This paper presents a method for adapting a Krizhevsky CNN network that is trained for classification (without bounding box annotations) to work for object detection, given a small number of classes that have annotated bounding boxes. The approach builds on the success of Krizhevsky et al.'s model and is fairly compelling in achieving reasonable results. The general approach is simple and logical, but its novelty and significance are substantial.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. The authors carefully evaluate both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. However, the paper could benefit from additional comparisons to other baselines, such as using full images as positive examples and images and regions from other classes as negative examples, training and fine-tuning all layers of the CNN, or using a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach. Including these baselines would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed approach's effectiveness. Additionally, the authors could consider comparing their method to training and fine-tuning the CNN for classification and then predicting a single bounding box at the center of the image at test time.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and clear. However, the experiments section could be improved by elaborating on the explanation of each method in Table 1, specifically clarifying whether the methods refer to applying Equation 1 or 2 to a subset of CNN layers.

Originality: The paper presents a novel approach to adapting a classification network to a detection network. While the basic idea is simple, its application to large-scale detection tasks is new and valuable.

Significance: The problem addressed by the paper is relevant, and the results are significant and valuable. The fact that decent detection results were obtained on a large-scale dataset without bounding box labels is compelling. However, it is essential to acknowledge that the paper's results might be influenced by the improved performance of CNN features rather than introducing a better way of training object detectors from weak supervision.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach to adapting a classification network to a detection network.
* The results are significant and valuable, demonstrating decent detection performance on a large-scale dataset without bounding box labels.
* The paper addresses a relevant problem in the field of computer vision and machine learning.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The paper is missing some obvious baselines, which could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed approach's effectiveness.
* The results might be influenced by the improved performance of CNN features rather than introducing a better way of training object detectors from weak supervision.
* The paper could benefit from additional comparisons to other approaches, such as using full images as positive examples and images and regions from other classes as negative examples, or using a MIL approach.
