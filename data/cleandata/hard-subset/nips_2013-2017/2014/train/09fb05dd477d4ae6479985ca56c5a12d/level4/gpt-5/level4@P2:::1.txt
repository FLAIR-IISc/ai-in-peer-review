Summary
This paper tackles large-scale object detection when bounding-box annotations are scarce relative to image-level labels. The authors propose LSDA, which learns a category-invariant transformation from classification to detection using detection data for a subset of categories, and then transfers this adaptation to categories with only classification labels. Concretely, they (1) fine-tune a classification CNN into a detection CNN using a background class and detection data for m categories (category-invariant adaptation), and (2) adjust category-specific output weights for the remaining K−m categories by averaging the fine-tuning deltas of their nearest neighbors in the label space (category-specific adaptation). Evaluation on ILSVRC2013 uses 100 categories with boxes for adaptation and 100 held-out categories without boxes. LSDA substantially improves over applying a classification network to region proposals, closing roughly one-third of the gap to a fully supervised oracle on held-out classes. They also scale to a 7.6K-category detector and present a faster variant.

Relation to prior work
The approach is closely related to R-CNN, which fine-tunes a classification network for detection but requires boxes for all categories. LSDA instead learns a transferable classification→detection adaptation on a subset and applies it to unseen categories. It also connects to weakly supervised detection (MIL) and domain adaptation, but differs by adapting across both task (classification→detection) and categories lacking detection labels.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The method is simple and well-motivated. The ablations support the importance of adding a background class and adapting shared layers; category-specific averaging yields smaller gains. Error analysis is helpful, showing reduced localization and background confusions.
- Empirical support: The setup (100 with boxes, 100 held out) is reasonable, and results show meaningful gains over a strong classification baseline. Reporting could be clearer about how improvements are computed (see points below).
- Completeness: The paper would benefit from a more consolidated comparison to R-CNN and clearer description of what supervision competing methods use.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. Figures and ablations communicate the main insights. Some claims and figure captions could be made more explicit to ensure apples-to-apples comparisons (see Detailed issues). Minor naming/typo issues noted below.

Originality
The combination of adapting classification features to detection and transferring the adaptation across categories without boxes is, to my knowledge, novel and interesting, albeit conceptually incremental. It leverages known ideas (fine-tuning, background modeling, nearest-neighbor transfer) in a new configuration for large-scale detection.

Significance
The approach is practical and could enable detectors for many categories lacking boxes, especially given the public release of models/code. While performance necessarily trails fully supervised detectors, the work provides a useful baseline and a path toward scaling. The longer-term impact will depend on whether the supervision–performance trade-off is compelling for applications and how future, more sophisticated methods perform under the same constraints.

Detailed issues to address
- Figure 3a: Please clarify for each method whether detection training uses bounding boxes for the evaluated categories, and whether image-level labels are used during detection training. This will ensure strictly comparable supervision across methods.
- Reporting of improvements: On held-out categories, the blue bars in Figure 3b suggest LSDA closes about one-third of the gap between the classification-only baseline and the fully supervised oracle. If you agree this is a key result, consider highlighting this number explicitly. Also, when reporting “X% improvement,” specify whether this is relative to the baseline mAP on held-out categories and whether the baseline–oracle gap is accounted for. The same applies to the “50% improvement” claim—please define the denominator and restrict to held-out categories for fairness.
- Relation to R-CNN: Consider adding a concise table or subsection that enumerates differences from R-CNN, including supervision requirements (boxes for all vs subset), training time, use of background class, use/non-use of SVMs and bbox regression, and performance trade-offs. Figure 6 (qualitative examples) could be moved to the supplement to make room.

Minor issues (no response required)
- Naming: Ensure consistent naming of the method across text and figures (LSDA vs DNN/DDA).
- Typo: “classi?ers” (line 431).

Pros
- Simple, well-motivated method with clear practical value.
- Strong empirical gains on held-out categories; informative ablations and error analysis.
- Scales to thousands of categories; code/models released.

Cons
- Ultimate accuracy lags fully supervised detectors (by design), and the cost–benefit trade-off is application dependent.
- Some claims and comparisons could be reported more precisely.
- Differences from R-CNN could be summarized more prominently.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a clear, practical contribution to scaling detection with limited bounding boxes, supported by solid experiments and analysis. While not ground-breaking, it is a useful step that the community is likely to build upon.
