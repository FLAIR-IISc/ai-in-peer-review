

This paper tackles object detection by addressing the challenge of obtaining bounding boxes at a scale matching category label availability. The authors sidestep this issue by adapting object classifiers for detection, proposing a straightforward approach whose simplicity is not inherently problematic. Their experiments train on 100 categories (with both labels and bounding boxes) and test on 100 held-out categories (with labels only). The results bridge approximately 35% of the gap between a baseline and an oracle on these held-out categories.

**Quality and Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly presented.  
**Originality:** The work combines two established adaptation concepts—adapting classifiers for detection and cross-category adaptation. To the best of my knowledge, this specific combination is novel and interesting, though not groundbreaking.  
**Significance:** The primary contribution is this novel (but not transformative) approach. While long-term significance is uncertain—results will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods, as is typical—the key limitation is the inherent performance trade-off: the method sacrifices mAP by using fewer bounding boxes, and the paper lacks an objective metric to assess whether this trade-off is justified. (As a side note, the core problem’s long-term importance is debatable: bounding boxes are indeed more costly to obtain than labels, but increased resources may eventually resolve this challenge, as demonstrated by the rapid scaling of labeled datasets in recent years.)  

**Major Concerns:**  
- **Figure 3a:** The figure does not clarify which methods (beyond the authors’ approach and R-CNN) leverage category labels for detection. This is critical for an apples-to-apples comparison; the authors must explicitly state this.  
- **Figure 3b and key results:** The main finding—that DNN bridges ~1/3 of the baseline-oracle gap (blue bars)—is underemphasized. The 78% claim (line 341) is misleading for two reasons: (1) it should reflect performance *only* on held-out categories, and (2) it incorrectly treats oracle performance as the sole reference (ignoring the baseline). Similarly, the "50% improvement" claim (line 425) suffers from the same flaw. The authors should highlight the gap-bridging metric instead.  
- **Comparison to R-CNN:** The distinctions between the proposed method and R-CNN (the closest baseline) are scattered. A dedicated table or subsection would clarify these differences. Figure 6 (anecdotal examples) could be removed to prioritize this comparison.  

**Minor Issues (no response required):**  
- Terminology: The method is referred to as both "DNN" and "DDA"; consistency is needed.  
- Typo: "classi?ers" (line 431).  

Overall, this is a strong paper proposing a novel, simple approach. While the results will likely be superseded soon, the method’s elegance as the first to take this adaptation path merits inclusion in NIPS. The paper deserves acceptance and visibility within the community. The significance of the approach itself remains to be fully validated, but its contribution is meaningful for the field.
