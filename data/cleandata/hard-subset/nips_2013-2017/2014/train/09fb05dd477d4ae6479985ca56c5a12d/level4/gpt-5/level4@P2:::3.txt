Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses the challenge of scaling object detection to many categories without bounding box annotations. It proposes LSDA, a method that adapts a CNN trained for image classification into an object detector using detection labels for only a subset of classes. The approach proceeds in three steps: (1) pre-train a Krizhevsky-style CNN on 1.2M+ classification images and fine-tune it for K target categories using image-level labels; (2) fine-tune layers 1–7 for detection on m categories with bounding boxes (set B), adding an explicit background class and using region proposals; and (3) transfer category-specific “deltas” learned on B to held-out categories (set A) by averaging the changes in the output layer weights over nearest neighbors in parameter space. At test time, the network scores region proposals for K classes plus background, using score_i − score_background to produce final detections. On ILSVRC13 detection, LSDA yields a 50% relative mAP gain over a classification-only baseline for held-out categories and scales to a 7.6K-category detector using ImageNet leaf nodes. A faster variant with SPP and fast proposals is also reported.

The work builds on R-CNN (Girshick et al.), which showed the importance of fine-tuning for detection but requires bounding boxes for all classes. It is also related to domain adaptation methods (feature/model adaptation) and weakly supervised detection via MIL; however, LSDA adapts across both domain (classification vs detection) and task and targets categories with no detection labels.

Strengths:
- Clear and practical problem formulation with a simple, logical algorithm.
- Effective use of background modeling and category-invariant adaptation to bridge classification and detection.
- Large-scale demonstration (7.6K categories) and code/model release increase potential impact.
- Quantitative gains and error analysis indicate reduced localization and background confusions.

Weaknesses and missing baselines:
The paper lacks several informative baselines that would contextualize the gains and test whether the improvements stem from adaptation rather than generic CNN features:
1) Weakly supervised, full-image positives: Treat full images with image-level labels as positives; use images/regions from other classes as negatives; fine-tune all layers. Evaluate on region proposals at test time.
2) MIL baseline: As above, but iteratively select the highest-scoring positive region per weakly labeled image (for set A) and refine. This is a standard weakly supervised detection approach and is arguably the most natural alternative to LSDA for classes without boxes.
3) Default-box baseline: Train/fine-tune for classification only; at test time, predict a single default bounding box (e.g., mean box or centered box) per image. This sanity check helps calibrate task difficulty and the value of region-level processing.

It is not obvious that LSDA will outperform (1) or (2), and including (3) would clarify how much of the gain comes from better localization versus classification.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The method is straightforward and well-motivated. The category-invariant adaptation via fine-tuning with a background class is convincing. The category-specific transfer (nearest-neighbor averaging of output-layer deltas) is reasonable, though somewhat heuristic; sensitivity to the neighbor metric and k would be useful.
- Empirical support: The ablations suggest most gains come from adapting the shared representation and learning a background class, with smaller gains from category-specific deltas. However, the absence of the baselines above leaves open whether LSDA is superior to strong weakly supervised alternatives.
- Completeness/honesty: The paper discusses trade-offs (e.g., skipping SVMs/box regression for training efficiency) and provides an error breakdown.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The experimental section would benefit from:
- Explicit mapping between Table 1 entries and concrete training variants: which layers are adapted, whether the background class is learned, and precisely where Eq. (1) (category-specific delta transfer) is applied.
- Details on nearest neighbor selection (feature space, normalization, k sensitivity) and whether semantic neighbors (e.g., WordNet) were tried.
- A brief quantification of the performance loss from omitting SVMs/box regression specifically on held-out categories, and whether box regression trained only on set B can transfer.

Originality:
Moderate. The key novelty is adapting classifiers to detectors for unseen categories by transferring category-invariant representation changes and approximating category-specific deltas. While conceptually simple and built atop R-CNN-style fine-tuning, the setting (no boxes for many classes) and the specific delta-transfer mechanism are new and useful.

Significance:
High practical significance. Demonstrating substantial gains for categories without boxes and scaling to thousands of classes is valuable for the community. Model/code release increases impact. The contribution would be stronger with the suggested baselines and additional analyses.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, timely problem (large-scale detection with limited box supervision).
- Simple, general method with strong empirical gains over a classification-only baseline.
- Scales to thousands of categories; resources released.
- Clear evidence that learning background and adapting shared features reduce key detection errors.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing comparisons to strong weakly supervised/MIL baselines and simple default-box baseline.
- Category-specific transfer is heuristic; limited analysis of its sensitivity and alternatives.
- Some ambiguity in experimental descriptions (Table 1 variants and layer-level adaptations).

Overall:
A solid and impactful paper with a clear idea and promising results. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on adding the proposed baselines (especially MIL) or providing a compelling rationale for their omission, and clarifying the experimental variants and transfer details.
