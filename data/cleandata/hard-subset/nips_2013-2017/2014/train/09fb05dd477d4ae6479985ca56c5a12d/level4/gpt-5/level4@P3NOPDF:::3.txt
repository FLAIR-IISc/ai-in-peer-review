Summary
The paper proposes a method to adapt a Krizhevsky (AlexNet) CNN trained for image-level classification to object detection with limited bounding-box supervision. The approach proceeds in three stages: (1) train a classification CNN on all categories without box annotations; (2) fine-tune the CNN on region crops for the subset of categories with bounding boxes, introducing an explicit background class; and (3) incorporate the background class into the full CNN and adapt selected layers. At test time, the model scores multiple regions per image to produce detections.

Quality
The approach is simple, logical, and leverages the strong representational power of modern CNNs. The empirical results appear reasonable and suggest that useful detectors can be obtained with weak supervision plus limited box annotations. However, the evaluation lacks several important baselines that would help isolate the contribution of the proposed adaptation versus the underlying CNN features:

- Baseline 1: Train/fine-tune the CNN using full images as positives for labeled classes, and treat images and regions from other classes as negatives (i.e., background). Fine-tune all layers. This tests whether region-level fine-tuning is necessary when limited box supervision is available.

- Baseline 2: As above, but with multiple-instance learning (MIL): for each positive image in the weakly supervised set, iteratively select the highest-scoring region as the positive instance. This is a strong and widely used weakly supervised detection baseline and is conceptually close to the proposed approach.

- Baseline 3: Train/fine-tune the CNN purely for classification. At test time, run the classifier and predict a single canonical bounding box (e.g., the mean box over ImageNet or centered in the image). This sanity check gauges how much of the reported AP stems from strong classification features alone versus true localization.

Without these comparisons, it is difficult to conclude that the proposed method is preferable to simpler alternatives. In particular, it is not clear that the method improves over Baseline 1, and it may underperform a well-implemented MIL baseline (Baseline 2). Including Baseline 3 would calibrate task difficulty and clarify how much gain arises from localization rather than classification confidence.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well written, but the experimental section would benefit from more detail:
- Table 1: Please clearly define each method variant. Which layers are adapted in each case? When you refer to Eq. 1 vs. Eq. 2, specify exactly which layers they are applied to and why.
- Background class: Describe how the background class is constructed (e.g., sampling strategy for negatives, hard-negative mining, ratio of background to foreground samples).
- Region proposals: Specify the proposal method, number of regions per image, and any non-maximum suppression (NMS) or calibration used at test time.
- Training protocol: Report learning rates, schedule, total iterations/epochs, data splits, and whether layers are frozen or fine-tuned in each stage.
- Evaluation: Include per-class AP and variance (if applicable), and ablations isolating the contributions of each training stage.

Originality
The contribution is incremental but meaningful: it adapts a classification CNN for detection with minimal box supervision via a straightforward training pipeline that introduces a background class and region-level fine-tuning. While the overall idea is simple and specific to CNNs of this architecture, the application to large-scale settings without comprehensive box labels is valuable and timely.

Significance
The problem is important, and demonstrating decent detection performance with limited bounding-box annotations is compelling. That said, the reported improvements may largely reflect the strength of CNN features rather than the specific adaptation mechanism. Stronger baselines and ablations are needed to establish the method’s distinct value.

Suggestions for Improvement
- Add the three baselines described above. In particular, the MIL baseline is crucial to compare against a principled weakly supervised alternative.
- Provide ablations for:
  - Which layers are adapted in stage (3) and the impact of adding the background class.
  - Freezing vs. fine-tuning different subsets of layers.
  - The effect of each training stage (remove one stage at a time).
  - Sensitivity to the number of classes with bounding boxes and to the quantity of box annotations per class.
- Compare against training a detector from scratch or with only the classes that have boxes, to show benefits of leveraging the classification-trained network and cross-category transfer.
- Analyze error modes (localization vs. classification errors) and provide qualitative examples of successes and failures.
- Report runtime and memory usage at test time (number of regions processed, throughput), as this is relevant for practical deployment.
- Clarify whether the method can generalize to architectures beyond AlexNet and whether any architecture-specific assumptions are made.

Minor Comments
- Ensure consistent terminology between “adapting” and “fine-tuning,” and explicitly map equations to the layers they modify.
- If class imbalance exists when adding the background class, describe how it is handled (e.g., sampling, loss weighting).

Overall Assessment
This is a relevant and timely paper that presents a simple and practical approach to turn a classification CNN into a detector with limited bounding-box supervision. The results are promising, and the paper is largely clear. However, the empirical evaluation omits several obvious and important baselines, making it difficult to attribute gains to the proposed adaptation rather than to the underlying CNN features. With the addition of these baselines and clearer experimental detail and ablations, the paper would make a stronger and more convincing contribution. I lean toward acceptance if the missing comparisons and clarifications can be addressed.
