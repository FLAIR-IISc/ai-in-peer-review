This paper presents a framework for transferring deep neural networks, initially trained for image classification, to the domain of object detection. When evaluated on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 object detection challenge, the proposed approach achieves a 4th place ranking. The manuscript is well-written and the methodology is straightforward to understand. A key strength is the proposed CNN architecture, which is both novel and practical to implement. Overall, this is a strong contribution. Nevertheless, two primary areas for improvement are noted below.

First, the paper's central contribution—the adaptation method itself—requires a more rigorous justification. The underlying rationale for the proposed technique is not thoroughly explained from a principled standpoint. For instance, in Section 3.2 (Page 5, Line 231), the authors do not provide a clear motivation for why the adapted detection weights are formulated as a sum of Wj^c and W{avg}. The conceptual significance or 'physical meaning' of this offset parameter remains unexplained. This lack of clarity subsequently makes it difficult to understand the reported superiority of the kNN variant.

Second, the experimental comparison to other methods is not conducted on a level playing field. The authors evaluate their model on a subset of the validation data rather than the standard test set, which compromises the fairness of the comparison. While the authors note in a footnote (Line 322) that RCNN shows similar results on the val2 set, any claimed performance improvement on this small subset lacks statistical significance and is therefore not a convincing justification.

In summary, the paper introduces a framework for adapting classification-trained deep networks for object detection. While the method achieves performance comparable to state-of-the-art approaches, the claims are weakened by unfair experimental comparisons and an insufficient explanation of the core adaptation mechanism.
