This paper tackles object detection, specifically the challenge of acquiring bounding boxes at the scale at which category labels are available for classification. The authors sidestep this issue by adapting object classifiers to the detection task. The approach is simple and straightforward—which is not a drawback. The experimental setup trains on 100 categories with both category labels and bounding boxes, and tests on 100 held-out categories that have only category labels. The results are reasonably strong, closing about 35% of the gap between a baseline and an oracle on the 100 held-out categories.

Quality and clarity: The paper is of good quality and clearly written.

Originality: The work combines two adaptation ideas: adapting object classifiers for detection and adapting across category sets. To the best of my knowledge, this combination is novel and interesting, though not ground-breaking.

Significance: The main contribution lies in the novel (though not ground-breaking) approach. The long-term significance is hard to judge from the current results and will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods—which is to be expected. The core trade-off is that reducing the need for bounding boxes sacrifices mAP, and there is no objective criterion provided to determine whether this trade-off is worthwhile.

As a side note, I am not convinced the problem itself is a critical long-term bottleneck. While bounding boxes are more expensive to obtain than category labels, history shows that resource investment can shift feasibility—e.g., large-scale labeling across tens of thousands of categories once seemed out of reach.

Detailed issues to address:
- Figure 3a: Beyond your method and R-CNN, which methods do or do not leverage category labels to improve detection? Please state this explicitly to ensure an apples-to-apples comparison.
- My assessment of the approach is based on the blue bars in Figure 3b: DNN sits about one-third of the way from baseline to oracle. Do the authors agree this is one of the key results? If so, please highlight this rather than the 78% figure (line 341), which is not the right metric for two reasons: (1) the evaluation should be based only on the held-out categories, and (2) performance should be measured relative to the baseline and oracle (i.e., gap closed), not as a raw percentage of the oracle. The same issue applies to the “50% improvement” claim (line 425).
- Please dedicate a table or subsection to clearly articulating the differences from R-CNN, which appears to be the closest competitor. These points are currently scattered and not as prominent as they should be. I recommend “sacrificing” Figure 6, which contains anecdotal examples, to make room for a more systematic comparison.

Minor issues (no response required):

Overall assessment: This is a solid paper proposing a reasonably novel approach. I recommend acceptance and believe it will be of interest to the NIPS community. While the results will almost certainly be surpassed soon, the simplicity is acceptable for a first paper taking this direction. The long-term impact of the approach remains uncertain, but the contribution is worthwhile and timely.
