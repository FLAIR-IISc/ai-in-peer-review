This paper presents a framework for adapting deep neural networks pre-trained for image classification to the task of object detection. The approach achieves strong results, ranking 4th in the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 object detection challenge. The paper is well-written and, for the most part, easy to understand. The proposed convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture is particularly noteworthy for its simplicity and potential for straightforward implementation. Overall, this is a solid contribution. However, I have two primary concerns that, if addressed, would significantly strengthen the work.

My first concern relates to the justification of the proposed adaptation method. While the results are promising, the underlying motivations for key design choices are not clearly articulated. Specifically, Section 3.2 (Page 5, Line 231) describes a summation of weights *W<sub>j</sub><sup>c</sup>* and *W<sub>avg</sub>* for the adapted detection task. The rationale behind this specific weighting scheme—essentially, the role of this ‘offset parameter’—remains unclear. Providing a more principled explanation of *why* this summation is effective would greatly enhance understanding. Consequently, the justification for the improved performance observed with the kNN variant is also lacking; its advantage isn’t readily apparent given the currently presented explanation. A deeper dive into the intuition behind these choices is needed.

Secondly, the comparative analysis with existing methods suffers from a potential fairness issue. The authors appear to have utilized a portion of the validation set for comparison, rather than relying solely on the standardized test set. While a footnote (Line 322) acknowledges similar performance of RCNN on the ‘val2’ set, the authors correctly point out that the small size of this set limits the statistical significance of any observed differences. Reporting results on the standard test set is crucial for a fair and rigorous comparison with the state-of-the-art. 

In summary, the authors have presented a compelling framework for adapting image classification networks to object detection, achieving performance comparable to leading methods. However, the adaptation method itself requires more thorough justification, and the comparative results would benefit from evaluation on a consistent, standardized test set. Addressing these points would elevate the paper’s impact and clarity.
