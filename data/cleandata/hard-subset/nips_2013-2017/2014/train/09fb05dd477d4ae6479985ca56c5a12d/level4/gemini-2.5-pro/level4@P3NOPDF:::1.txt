### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the significant challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories, where obtaining bounding box annotations is far more costly than obtaining image-level category labels. The authors propose a method that circumvents the need for bounding boxes on target categories by adapting object classifiers, trained on source categories with full supervision, to the detection task on novel target categories that only have image-level labels. The proposed algorithm is straightforward and elegant. In their experiments, the model is trained on 100 categories with both bounding boxes and labels and is then evaluated on 100 distinct, held-out categories for which only image-level labels were available during training. The results are promising, with the proposed method closing approximately 35% of the performance gap between a baseline detector and an oracle with full bounding box supervision on the novel categories.

**Strengths**

*   **Quality and Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The problem is well-defined, and the proposed solution is presented in a straightforward manner. The simplicity of the algorithm is a commendable quality, making it easy to understand and likely to be reproducible.
*   **Originality and Novelty:** The core idea of combining two existing concepts—adapting object classifiers for detection and adapting models across different sets of object categories—is novel and interesting. To my knowledge, this specific formulation for reducing annotation dependence in detection is new. While not a groundbreaking paradigm shift, it is a creative and valuable contribution.
*   **Significance of the Problem:** The paper tackles a practical and important long-term challenge in computer vision. Reducing the reliance on expensive bounding box annotations could significantly accelerate progress in large-scale object detection, making the problem addressed highly relevant to the community.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The main weaknesses of the paper lie in the framing of its results and its comparison to related work. The following points should be addressed to strengthen the paper.

1.  **Framing of the Main Quantitative Result:** My assessment of the method's effectiveness is primarily based on the results for the held-out categories shown in Figure 3b, where the "DNN" method's performance appears to be about one-third of the way from the baseline to the oracle. The authors are encouraged to highlight this relative improvement as one of their main results. The claim of achieving "78% of the oracle mAP" (line 341) is less informative for two key reasons:
    *   The evaluation of this approach should focus on the more challenging and relevant scenario of the held-out categories, for which bounding boxes were not used.
    *   Performance gains should be measured relative to the baseline to accurately reflect the contribution of the proposed method. A simple percentage of oracle performance does not account for the problem's inherent difficulty, which is established by the baseline. A similar issue exists with the "50% improvement" claim (line 425). Reframing these results would provide a more nuanced and impactful assessment of the method's contribution.

2.  **Clarity of Experimental Comparisons:** In Figure 3a, it is not immediately clear which of the baseline and competing methods use category-level labels to aid detection. For a fair, apples-to-apples comparison, the authors should explicitly state the level of supervision each method uses. This will help readers better understand the trade-offs and advantages of the proposed approach relative to existing techniques.

3.  **Consolidated Comparison to R-CNN:** R-CNN appears to be the most closely related work, yet the comparisons are scattered throughout the manuscript. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a dedicated subsection or table that clearly and concisely outlines the differences between the proposed method and R-CNN. This comparison should cover methodology, assumptions, and the specific problem setting each method is designed for. To make space for this, I would suggest replacing Figure 6, which presents anecdotal qualitative examples, with this more substantive analysis.

4.  **Discussion of the Performance/Annotation Trade-off:** While the paper's motivation is strong, it sidesteps a direct discussion of the central trade-off: is the achieved performance gain worth the reduction in annotation effort? There is no objective way to answer this, but the authors should acknowledge this trade-off. It would be valuable to include a brief discussion on the long-term implications of this approach. For instance, while bounding boxes are currently expensive, it is debatable whether this will remain a critical bottleneck in the long run, especially as annotation platforms and crowd-sourcing efforts become more efficient.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a solid paper that proposes a novel and sensible approach to a significant problem in object detection. The method is simple yet effective, and the work is presented clearly. While the results will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods in the future, this is to be expected for a paper introducing a new direction. The primary contribution lies in the approach itself. I believe the paper is a valuable contribution that deserves to be seen by the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
