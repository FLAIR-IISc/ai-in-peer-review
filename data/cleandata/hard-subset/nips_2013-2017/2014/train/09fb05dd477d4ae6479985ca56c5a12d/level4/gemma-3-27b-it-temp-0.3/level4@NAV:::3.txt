This paper introduces a method for adapting a classification-trained Krizhevsky CNN to the task of object detection, leveraging a limited number of images with bounding box annotations. The technique involves three training stages: initial classification training on all classes, region-based training on annotated classes with the addition of a background class, and finally, integration of the background class into the full CNN with weight adaptation. During testing, the CNN processes multiple image regions to identify objects.

Quality: This is a worthwhile contribution that demonstrates promising results. The success of the approach is largely attributable to the strong foundation provided by the Krizhevsky et al. model, rather than a fundamentally new concept; nevertheless, it offers value to the computer vision and machine learning communities. The methodology is straightforward and logically sound. While the experiments are encouraging, the evaluation would be strengthened by comparison to several baselines:

1) Training and fine-tuning the entire CNN using full images as positive examples and image regions from other classes as negative examples.
2) Implementing the same training strategy as above, but employing a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, iteratively selecting the highest-scoring positive region per image.
3) Training and fine-tuning the CNN for classification, then applying the classifier at test time to predict a single bounding box (e.g., centered or using the mean bounding box location from a dataset like ImageNet).

The paper does not establish whether the proposed method outperforms these alternatives. The rationale for the proposed approach isn’t demonstrably superior to baseline 1, and appears less compelling than baseline 2. Including baseline 3 would provide crucial context regarding the difficulty of the problem and the dataset characteristics.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and easy to understand. The clarity of the experiments section could be improved by providing more detailed explanations of the methods presented in Table 1 (e.g., clarifying whether the application of Equations 1 or 2 is limited to specific CNN layers).

Originality: The work presents sufficient novelty for acceptance, despite the simplicity of the core approach and its specific applicability to certain CNN architectures.

Significance: The problem addressed and the results achieved are significant and valuable. 

The paper tackles a relevant challenge, and obtaining reasonable detection performance on a large-scale dataset without extensive bounding box annotations is a notable accomplishment. However, the absence of key baselines raises the possibility that the observed results are primarily driven by improvements in CNN feature representation, rather than by a superior training strategy for object detection with weak supervision.
