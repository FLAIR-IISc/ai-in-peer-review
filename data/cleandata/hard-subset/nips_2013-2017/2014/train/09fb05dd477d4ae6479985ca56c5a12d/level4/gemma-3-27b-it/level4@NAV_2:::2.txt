The authors propose a framework for adapting deep neural networks trained for image classification to the object detection task. Evaluation on the ImageNet LSVRC-2013 dataset demonstrates competitive performance, achieving 4th place in the detection challenge. The paper is clearly presented and generally easy to follow, and the proposed CNN architecture is straightforward to implement. Overall, the paper demonstrates good quality research. However, there are areas for improvement.

First, the core adaptation method requires stronger justification. While the approach shows empirical success, the underlying motivations aren’t presented in a fully principled manner. For example, Section 3.2 (Page 5, Line 231) lacks a clear explanation for why the adapted detection weights are computed as a summation of W<sub>c</sub><sup>j</sup> and δ<sub>avg</sub>. The physical interpretation of this 'offset parameter’ remains unclear, and consequently, the rationale for employing the k-NN variant isn’t fully convincing.

Second, the comparison to other methods utilizes a portion of the validation set rather than the dedicated test set, potentially introducing an unfair bias. The authors acknowledge this in a footnote (Line 322), noting similar performance of R-CNN on the validation set, but argue the small set size limits statistical justification. A comparison on the standard test set would strengthen these claims.

In summary, the authors present a valuable contribution by addressing the challenge of scaling object detection to a large number of categories with limited bounding box annotations. The method achieves promising results. Addressing the concerns regarding justification of the adaptation method and ensuring a fair comparison to existing techniques would further enhance the paper’s impact. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The problem addressed is important and relevant to the NIPS community, tackling the data scarcity issue in object detection.
*   The proposed method achieves strong empirical results on a challenging benchmark (ImageNet LSVRC-2013).
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the approach.
*   The code and models are publicly available, fostering reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical justification for the adaptation method is lacking and could benefit from further elaboration.
*   The comparison to other methods is not entirely fair due to the use of a subset of the validation set.
*   The statistical significance of certain results is questioned due to the limited size of the validation set.
