This paper investigates object detection, specifically addressing the disparity in annotation effort between category labels and bounding box annotations. The authors propose a method that adapts object classifiers to perform detection, offering a straightforward solution to this challenge. Their experimental design involves training on 100 categories with complete annotations (both labels and boxes) and evaluating on 100 novel categories with only category labels available. The reported results are encouraging, achieving approximately 35% of the performance gap between an ideal oracle and a baseline system on these unseen categories.

**Quality and clarity:** The paper is well-written and presents a clear and sound methodology.

**Originality:** The work uniquely combines established adaptation techniques – adapting classifiers for detection and transferring knowledge across category sets. While not a fundamentally new direction, this combination appears novel and represents a worthwhile exploration.

**Significance:** The primary contribution lies in this novel approach. While the achieved performance is unlikely to remain state-of-the-art due to the inherent simplicity of the method and the expectation of future advancements, the work offers a valuable baseline. A key limitation is the unavoidable trade-off between detection accuracy (mAP) and the number of predicted bounding boxes, and the paper lacks a definitive justification for this trade-off. Furthermore, the practical importance of addressing the annotation cost disparity is debatable, given the potential for increased resource allocation to resolve annotation bottlenecks.

**Detailed issues that should be addressed:**

- Figure 3a requires clarification regarding which methods utilize category labels for detection, beyond the proposed method and R-CNN. A clear delineation of label usage is necessary for an accurate comparison.
- The central result, as interpreted by this reviewer, is the performance of the proposed method (DNN) represented by the blue bars in Figure 3b – achieving approximately one-third of the gap between baseline and oracle performance. The authors should emphasize this result, rather than the 78% figure presented on line 341, which is misleading due to its basis on all categories rather than just the left-out set, and its presentation as a percentage of oracle performance without considering the baseline. A similar correction is needed for the 50% improvement claim on line 425.
- A dedicated table or subsection detailing the differences between the proposed approach and R-CNN, its closest competitor, would significantly enhance the paper. Currently, these distinctions are dispersed throughout the text and lack prominence. Consider removing Figure 6, which provides only anecdotal evidence, to create space for this comparison.

**Minor issues (no need to respond):**

- The paper inconsistently refers to the method as “DNN” or “DDA.”
- A typographical error exists on line 431 (“classi?ers”). 

Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a reasonably novel approach to object detection. I recommend acceptance for presentation at NIPS. While the results are likely to be superseded, the simplicity of the method, as a first attempt in this direction, is acceptable and the potential long-term impact of the approach warrants consideration by the NIPS community.
