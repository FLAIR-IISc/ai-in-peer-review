This paper addresses a key challenge in object detection: the difficulty of obtaining bounding box annotations on the same large scale that image-level category labels are available. The authors propose a method to circumvent this problem by adapting pre-trained object classifiers for the detection task. Their algorithm, LSDA, is straightforward and consists of learning a generic transformation from a classifier to a detector using a subset of categories that have bounding box data, and then applying this transformation to categories that only have image-level labels. In their experiments, 100 categories with full supervision are used to learn the adaptation, which is then applied to 100 held-out categories. The results are promising, with the proposed method closing approximately 36% of the performance gap between a classification-only baseline and an oracle model trained with full bounding box supervision for all categories.

**Quality and Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clear, and the technical quality is sound. The experimental setup is well-designed to test the central hypothesis.

**Originality:** The core idea is a novel combination of existing concepts: adapting classifiers for detection and transferring knowledge across different sets of categories. While the approach is interesting and, to my knowledge, new in this context, it is more of an incremental step than a groundbreaking one.

**Significance:** The primary contribution is the novel approach itself. In terms of results, it is difficult to assess the long-term significance. While the method will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated techniques in the future, it provides a valuable proof of concept. The central trade-off presented—sacrificing some mAP to avoid the need for exhaustive bounding box annotation—is interesting, but the paper does not provide a clear framework for judging when this trade-off is worthwhile.

A broader question regarding significance is whether the problem itself is a fundamental long-term challenge. While bounding boxes are currently more expensive to annotate than image labels, it is conceivable that this gap could be closed by brute-force data collection efforts, similar to how large-scale classification datasets were created.

**Detailed Issues to be Addressed:**

1.  **Comparison Clarity:** In Figure 3a, it is unclear which of the compared methods use image-level category labels. To ensure an apples-to-apples comparison, the authors should explicitly state the supervision used by each method.

2.  **Framing of Results:** The framing of the main results could be improved. The authors emphasize a “50% relative mAP boost” over the baseline. A more insightful evaluation, in my view, is to measure how much of the performance gap between the 'Classification only' baseline and the 'All 200 cat w/ det data' oracle is closed. Based on Table 1, the proposed method closes this gap by about 36% for the held-out categories ((16.15-10.31)/(26.54-10.31)). I believe this is a more representative summary of the paper's contribution and should be highlighted. Reporting performance as a relative gain over a low baseline can be misleading.

3.  **Comparison to R-CNN:** The paper's relationship to R-CNN, which appears to be the most closely related work, could be clarified. While differences are mentioned, they are scattered throughout the text. A dedicated sub-section or table explicitly comparing LSDA and R-CNN (e.g., in terms of data requirements, training stages, and performance trade-offs) would significantly strengthen the paper. The anecdotal examples in Figure 6 could potentially be moved to an appendix to make space for this more crucial comparison.

**Minor Issues:**

*   Please ensure the proposed method is named consistently throughout the paper. The title and abstract refer to it as LSDA, but I believe I noted other acronyms being used.
*   There appears to be a typo in the submission PDF: "classiﬁers" on line 431.

**Conclusion:**

In summary, this is a solid paper that proposes a novel and intuitive approach to a relevant problem. The method is simple and well-executed, and the results provide a valuable proof of concept. While the performance will likely be surpassed by future work, the paper's value lies in being one of the first to explore this specific direction of adaptation for large-scale detection. For these reasons, I believe the paper is a good contribution to the NIPS community and recommend acceptance. Its long-term impact will depend on how this line of research evolves.
