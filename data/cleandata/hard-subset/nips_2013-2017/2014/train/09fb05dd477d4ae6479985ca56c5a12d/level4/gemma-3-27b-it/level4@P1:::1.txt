This paper addresses the challenge of object detection, specifically the difficulty of obtaining bounding box annotations on the same scale as category labels. The authors propose to adapt object classifiers for the detection task, offering a straightforward algorithm. Their experimental protocol uses 100 categories for training (with both category labels and bounding boxes) and tests on 100 held-out categories, which only have category labels. The results are promising, bridging approximately 35% of the gap between an oracle and a baseline on the held-out categories.

**Quality and Clarity:** This is a well-written and technically sound paper. The presentation is clear and easy to follow.

**Originality:** The paper combines existing ideas – adapting object classifiers for detection and transferring knowledge between categories – in a novel and interesting way. While not a groundbreaking contribution, it represents a valuable step forward.

**Significance:** The primary contribution lies in the novel approach itself. The long-term significance is harder to gauge, as more sophisticated methods will likely surpass these results. The trade-off between performance (mAP) and reduced reliance on bounding box annotations is a valid research direction, though its ultimate value remains to be seen. The practical relevance of fully circumventing the need for bounding box annotation deserves consideration; recent advancements suggest the cost of obtaining these annotations may decrease over time.

**Detailed issues that should be addressed:**

*   In Figure 3a, clarify which methods utilize category labels for detection, beyond your method and R-CNN. A clearer statement about apples-to-apples comparisons would be beneficial.
*   The assessment of the approach’s effectiveness is primarily based on the blue bars in Figure 3b. The authors characterize this as providing a significant improvement.  Please explicitly state this finding and highlight it, rather than the 78% figure (line 341), which is potentially misleading for two reasons: (1) evaluation should focus solely on the held-out categories and (2) performance should be assessed relative to the baseline, not merely as a percentage of oracle performance. A similar concern applies to the claim of a 50% improvement (line 425).
*   A dedicated table or subsection comparing and contrasting the proposed method with R-CNN (the closest competitor) would strengthen the paper. Currently, these distinctions are scattered throughout the text and lack prominence. Consider streamlining Figure 6, which presents anecdotal examples, to accommodate this more rigorous comparison.

**Minor issues (no response needed):**

*   The method is referred to as both "DNN" and “LSDA”; consistency in terminology is recommended.
*   Typo on line 431 ("classi?ers").

Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a reasonable novel approach to a relevant problem. I recommend acceptance for presentation at NIPS. While the results are likely to be improved upon, the simplicity and initial success of the method warrant its dissemination to the NIPS community. The long-term impact of this approach, and its ability to truly lessen the dependence on costly bounding box annotations, remains an open question.
