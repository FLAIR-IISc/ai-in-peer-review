This paper addresses the challenge of object detection, specifically the difficulty of obtaining bounding box annotations at the scale achieved for object categorization. The authors propose to adapt object classifiers for the detection task, offering a solution that leverages readily available image-level labels. Their algorithm is straightforward, utilizing 100 categories for training (with both labels and boxes) and evaluating on 100 held-out categories with only category labels. Results demonstrate a reasonable improvement, closing approximately 35% of the gap between a baseline and an oracle performance on the held-out categories.

Quality and clarity: This is a well-written and technically sound paper. The methodology is clearly explained and the experiments are well-defined.

Originality: The paper presents a novel combination of existing concepts – adapting object classifiers for detection and transferring knowledge between categories. While building on established techniques, the specific approach of adapting classifiers trained on image-level labels to perform detection on categories lacking bounding box annotations represents a valuable contribution. This is a worthwhile exploration, though not a groundbreaking paradigm shift.

Significance: The primary contribution of this work lies in its novel approach. Assessing the long-term significance is challenging; while the results are promising, they are likely to be surpassed by more sophisticated methods in the future (as is typical in this field). A key consideration is the trade-off between performance (mAP) and the reduced reliance on expensive bounding box annotations. Determining the practical value of this trade-off remains an open question. The potential for scaling detection to a vast number of categories is compelling, but the fundamental limitations of relying on weaker image-level supervision should be acknowledged.

Detailed issues that should be addressed:
- In Figure 3a, clarify which methods utilize category labels during the detection phase, beyond your proposed method and R-CNN. Providing a clear comparison regarding label usage will enhance understanding.
- The performance gains highlighted in Figure 3b (DNN achieving roughly 1/3 of the gap between baseline and oracle) seem central to the paper’s contribution. Emphasize this finding and avoid focusing on the 78% value (line 341) which is less informative due to its reliance on oracle performance and exclusion of the baseline. A similar clarification is needed for the reported 50% improvement (line 425).
- A dedicated table or section comparing and contrasting the proposed method with R-CNN – the closest related work – would be beneficial. Currently, these distinctions are presented somewhat scattered throughout the text. Consider streamlining this comparison to highlight the key differences and advantages of your approach. The anecdotal examples presented in Figure 6 could potentially be sacrificed to accommodate this more detailed comparison.

I recommend acceptance for presentation at NIPS, as the work is likely to be of interest to the community.
