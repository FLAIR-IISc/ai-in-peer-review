This paper addresses the challenge of object detection, specifically the issue of obtaining bounding boxes at a scale comparable to category labels for object categorization. The authors propose a novel approach by adapting object classifiers for the detection task, effectively sidestepping this challenge. Their algorithm is straightforward and simple, which is not inherently a drawback.

The experimental protocol involves training on 100 categories with both category labels and bounding boxes, and testing on 100 left-out categories with only category labels. The results obtained are reasonably good, bridging approximately 35% of the gap between an oracle and a baseline on the 100 left-out categories.

Quality and clarity: This is a well-written paper of good quality.

Originality: The paper combines two existing ideas related to adaptation: adapting object classifiers for detection and adapting from one set of categories to another. While not groundbreaking, this combination is novel and interesting to the best of this reviewer's knowledge.

Significance: The main contribution of this paper is its novel approach. Although the results are promising, it is challenging to assess their long-term significance, as they will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods in the future. The primary concern with the results is that they inevitably sacrifice performance (mAP) by using fewer bounding boxes, and there is no objective way to evaluate whether this trade-off is justified.

It is also worth noting that the importance of the challenge being addressed is debatable. While bounding boxes are more expensive to obtain than category labels, it is unclear whether throwing resources at the problem would not be a viable solution. In the past, labeling millions of images with 20,000 object categories seemed almost impossible, yet it has been achieved.

Detailed issues to be addressed:

* In Figure 3a, the authors should clearly state which methods do or do not use category labels to aid detection, aside from their method and R-CNN, to facilitate an apples-to-apples comparison.
* The proposed approach's effectiveness is primarily demonstrated by the blue bars in Figure 3b. The authors should acknowledge that the DNN is approximately one-third of the way between the baseline and oracle. If so, they should highlight this result instead of the 78% mentioned in line 341, which is not an accurate representation for two reasons: it should only be based on left-out categories, and it should not be a simple percentage of oracle performance (it should take the baseline into account). A similar issue applies to the claim of 50% improvement in line 425.
* The authors should devote a table or subsection to clearly outlining the differences between their approach and R-CNN, which appears to be the closest competing alternative. Currently, these statements are scattered or not as prominent as they could be. Consider replacing Figure 6, which only provides anecdotal examples.

Minor issues (no response necessary):

* The proposed method is referred to as both "DNN" and "DDA."
* Typo on line 431 ("classi?ers")

Overall, this is a reasonably good paper proposing a novel approach. It deserves to be accepted and presented to the NIPS community. While the results will likely be surpassed soon, the long-term significance of the paper and its approach remains to be seen.
