This paper addresses the challenge of object detection, particularly the difficulty of obtaining bounding box annotations on the same large scale at which category labels exist. The authors circumvent this challenge by proposing a method to adapt object classifiers for the detection task. Their algorithm is commendably simple and straightforward. The experimental protocol uses 100 categories with full annotations (category labels and bounding boxes) for training and evaluates on 100 held-out categories for which only category labels are available during training. The results are promising, closing approximately 35% of the performance gap between a baseline and an oracle on the 100 held-out categories.

**Quality and Clarity:** This is a high-quality, clearly-written paper.

**Originality:** This work synthesizes two existing ideas: adapting object classifiers for detection and adapting models from one set of categories to another. To the best of my knowledge, this combination is a novel and interesting contribution, though perhaps not a groundbreaking one.

**Significance:** The main contribution of this paper is its novel approach. In terms of results, it is difficult to judge the long-term significance, as the performance will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods in the future (which is to be expected for a new direction of research). The central trade-off of this work is sacrificing some detection performance (mAP) to reduce the reliance on bounding box annotations. However, the paper does not provide an objective framework for evaluating whether this trade-off is worthwhile.

As a side note, it is worth considering whether the problem being addressed—the scarcity of bounding boxes—is a persistent long-term challenge. While bounding boxes are currently more "expensive" to obtain than category labels, it is conceivable that large-scale data collection efforts could mitigate this issue in the future, similar to how millions of images were eventually labeled for 20,000 object categories, a task that once seemed intractable.

**Detailed Issues to be Addressed:**

*   In Figure 3a, it is unclear which of the compared methods, besides the authors' own and R-CNN, use category labels to aid in detection. The authors should clarify this to ensure all comparisons are on an "apples-to-apples" basis.
*   My assessment of the method's effectiveness is primarily based on the results for the held-out categories, as depicted by the blue bars in Figure 3b. Here, the proposed DNN method appears to close approximately one-third of the performance gap between the baseline and the oracle. I believe this is one of the paper's main takeaways. If the authors agree, I would strongly suggest they highlight this result rather than the "78% of oracle performance" figure (line 341). The 78% figure is potentially misleading for two reasons: 1) The evaluation of an adaptation method should focus on the target domain (the held-out categories), and 2) Performance should be measured relative to the baseline, not just as a raw percentage of the oracle's score. The same critique applies to the claim of a "50% improvement" (line 425).
*   The proposed method is closely related to R-CNN, yet the specific differences between the two are scattered throughout the paper. To improve clarity, the authors should dedicate a table or subsection to a direct comparison with R-CNN. This would be a more valuable use of space than Figure 6, which currently shows anecdotal examples.

**Minor Issues (no response needed):**

**Overall Recommendation:**
This is a solid paper that proposes a simple yet reasonably novel approach to a relevant problem. I believe the paper merits acceptance and will be of interest to the NIPS community. While the absolute performance will likely be surpassed by future work, the paper's value lies in introducing this straightforward adaptation strategy. The long-term significance of the approach remains to be seen, but it is a worthy contribution to the field.
