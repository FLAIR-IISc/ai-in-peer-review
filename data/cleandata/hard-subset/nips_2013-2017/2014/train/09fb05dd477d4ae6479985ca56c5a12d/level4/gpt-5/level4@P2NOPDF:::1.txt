This paper tackles object detection, specifically the scarcity of bounding boxes at the scale of category labels used for classification. The authors circumvent this by adapting object classifiers to detection. The approach is deliberately simple, which is a strength in terms of clarity and reproducibility. The experimental setup trains on 100 categories with both labels and bounding boxes and evaluates on 100 held-out categories that have only category labels. The method closes about 35% of the performance gap between a baseline and an oracle on the held-out categories, which is a reasonable outcome.

Quality and clarity: The paper is clearly written and of solid technical quality.

Originality: The work combines two adaptation ideas: adapting classifiers for detection and transferring across category sets. To the best of my knowledge, this combination is novel and interesting, though not transformative.

Significance: The primary contribution is the approach itself—novel but not ground-breaking. The empirical significance is harder to assess long-term; the results will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods, which is expected. The central trade-off—reduced annotation cost (fewer bounding boxes) versus decreased mAP—is real, but the paper does not provide a definitive way to evaluate whether the trade-off is worthwhile in practice.

Side note: It is not entirely clear that the problem setting represents a major enduring challenge. While bounding boxes are more expensive than category labels, increased data collection resources may diminish this gap over time. Not long ago, assembling millions of images labeled across tens of thousands of categories seemed implausible, yet it has since become feasible.

Detailed issues to address:
- Figure 3a: Please state explicitly which competing methods do and do not use category labels to aid detection (beyond your method and R-CNN) to ensure a fair, apples-to-apples comparison.
- Figure 3b: My assessment hinges on the blue bars, where your method appears to bridge roughly one-third of the gap between baseline and oracle. Do you agree this is a key result? If so, highlight this rather than citing the 78% figure (line 341), which is misleading for two reasons: (1) evaluation should focus on the held-out categories only, and (2) performance should be measured relative to the baseline as well as the oracle, not merely as a percentage of the oracle. The same concern applies to the claim of a 50% improvement (line 425).
- Comparison to R-CNN: Since R-CNN is the closest competitor, please consolidate and emphasize the differences in a dedicated subsection or table. Consider removing Figure 6, which provides anecdotal examples that add limited value relative to a clearer comparative analysis.

Minor issues (no response required):
- Please clarify the method’s name: “DNN” or “DDA”?
- Typo on line 431 (“classi?ers”).

Overall assessment: This is a solid and clearly presented paper proposing a reasonably novel approach to reducing annotation requirements for detection by adapting classifiers. I recommend acceptance. While stronger methods will likely emerge soon, this work presents a clean, first-step formulation of a worthwhile idea. The long-term impact—particularly the practical value of the annotation-performance trade-off—remains to be demonstrated.
