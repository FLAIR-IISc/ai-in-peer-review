This paper tackles object detection, focusing on the challenge of obtaining bounding boxes at a scale comparable to the availability of category labels for classification. The authors avoid this bottleneck by adapting object classifiers to the detection task. The approach is simple and straightforward, which is not a drawback in itself. The experimental setup trains on 100 categories with both category labels and bounding boxes, and evaluates on 100 held-out categories that have only category labels. The results are reasonably strong: on the 100 held-out categories, the method closes about 35% of the gap between a baseline and an oracle.

Quality and clarity: This is a solid, clearly written paper.

Originality: The work combines two established adaptation ideas: adapting object classifiers for detection and adapting across category sets. To the best of my knowledge, this combination is novel and interesting, though not ground-breaking.

Significance: The primary contribution is the new (though not ground-breaking) approach. From a results perspective, it is difficult to assess long-term impact; the performance will likely be surpassed by more sophisticated methods in the future (which is to be expected). The key limitation is that using fewer bounding boxes inevitably reduces mAP, and there is no objective way to determine whether this trade-off is worthwhile.
(As a side note, it is unclear whether the problem itself is a long-term priority. While bounding boxes are indeed more “expensive” than category labels, it is possible that increased resources could mitigate this. Not long ago, having millions of images labeled with 20,000 object categories seemed nearly impossible.)

Detailed issues that should be addressed:
- In Figure 3a, which methods, besides the proposed method and R-CNN, use category labels to aid detection? Please specify this clearly to ensure an apples-to-apples comparison.
- My assessment of the approach hinges on the blue bars in Figure 3b: the DNN result is about one-third of the way from the baseline to the oracle. Do the authors agree this is one of the main findings? If so, please emphasize this rather than the 78% figure (line 341), which is not the appropriate number for two reasons: (1) the evaluation should be based only on the held-out categories, and (2) performance should be measured relative to the baseline as well, not merely as a percentage of the oracle. An analogous issue applies to the claimed 50% improvement (line 425).
- Please dedicate a table or subsection to clearly delineating the differences from R-CNN, which appears to be the closest competing alternative. These points are currently scattered and not as prominent as they could be. I recommend “sacrificing” Figure 6, which consists of anecdotal examples, to make space.

Minor issues (no need to respond):
- Is the proposed method referred to as “DNN” or “DDA”?
- Typo on line 431 (“classi?ers”).

Overall, this is a reasonably good paper proposing a reasonably novel approach. I believe it merits acceptance and will be of interest to the NIPS community. While the results will almost certainly be surpassed soon—given the simplicity of the method (appropriate for the first paper to take this approach)—the long-term significance of the paper, and the approach in particular, remains to be seen.
