This paper introduces a method for adapting a pre-trained Krizhevsky CNN – initially trained for image classification without bounding box annotations – to the task of object detection, leveraging a limited set of classes with available bounding box labels. The proposed training procedure involves three key steps: first, training a CNN for classification across all classes; second, training a CNN on image regions corresponding to the labeled bounding box classes, augmented with a negative background region class; and third, integrating the background class into the full CNN model with corresponding weight adaptation. During testing, the CNN is applied to multiple image regions within a test image.

The work presents an interesting and reasonably convincing approach that achieves promising results. While the success largely builds upon the established performance of the Krizhevsky et al. model, rather than presenting a fundamentally novel concept, the contributions will be valuable to the computer vision and machine learning research communities. The methodology is straightforward and logically sound. However, the experimental evaluation would benefit from comparisons against several key baselines:

1) Training and fine-tuning the complete CNN using full images as positive examples and image regions from other classes as negative examples.
2) Employing a Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) approach, iteratively selecting the highest-scoring positive region per image as the representative sample.
3) Training and fine-tuning the CNN solely for classification, then predicting a single bounding box at the image center during testing (e.g., utilizing the mean bounding box from ImageNet). 

The potential performance of the proposed method relative to these baselines remains unclear. Specifically, the advantage over baseline 1 is not immediately apparent, and it appears less motivated than baseline 2. Including baseline 3 would be crucial to establish the difficulty of the problem and the characteristics of the dataset.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. However, the clarity of the experiments section could be enhanced with more detailed explanations of the methods presented in Table 1 – specifically, clarifying whether the application of equations 1 or 2 is limited to specific CNN layers.

The work demonstrates sufficient novelty for acceptance, despite the simplicity of the core approach and its specific applicability to certain CNN architectures. 

The problem addressed and the reported results are both significant and valuable. The ability to achieve reasonable detection performance on a large-scale dataset without extensive bounding box annotations is a compelling outcome. However, the absence of obvious baseline comparisons raises the possibility that the observed results may be attributable to improved CNN features rather than to a superior training method for object detection under weak supervision.
