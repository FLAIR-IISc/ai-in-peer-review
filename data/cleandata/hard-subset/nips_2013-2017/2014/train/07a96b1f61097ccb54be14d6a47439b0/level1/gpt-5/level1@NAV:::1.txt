This paper studies low-rank matrix recovery in the presence of coherent (clustered) data, arguing that standard RPCA/PCP (Candès et al., JACM 2011) can fail as the number of clusters grows because the coherence parameters, especially µ2, increase. The authors analyze Low-Rank Representation (LRR; Liu et al., TPAMI 2013) with a general dictionary A and prove that if A is itself low-rank and its column space contains that of the ground-truth L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), then LRR can recover L0 without dependence on µ2 (and largely on µ3). They provide noiseless and noisy recovery theorems that depend only on µ1(A), with bounds rank(L0) ≤ O(n2/log n1) and |Ω| up to roughly 50% (noiseless) or 35% (noisy). Practically, they propose an unsupervised two-stage algorithm: run RPCA to obtain an estimate of L0, build a low-rank, column-normalized dictionary from it, and then solve LRR. Experiments on synthetic data and corrupted motion sequences (Hopkins155 extensions) show clear gains over RPCA and downstream improvements for SIM, LRRx, and SSC (Elhamifar & Vidal, CVPR 2009). The work relates to NIPS literature on robust subspace methods and matrix factorization (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, NIPS 2010; Srebro & Jaakkola, NIPS 2004).

Quality: The theoretical contributions are technically sound and carefully stated, clarifying how µ2 arises from union-of-subspaces sampling. The proofs (in the full paper) appear to be standard but nontrivial adaptations for general dictionaries. The algorithm is simple and consistent with the theory’s intuition, though guarantees for the learned dictionary are not provided.

Clarity: The paper is well organized, with clear definitions of coherence and a helpful “µ2-phenomenon” narrative. Some assumptions (U0 ⊂ UA) could be further contextualized, and sensitivity to rank estimation and normalization could be discussed.

Originality: Analyzing LRR with low-rank dictionaries to avoid µ2 is novel and provides new insight into coherence. The link to clustering structure within low-rank data is fresh and well motivated.

Significance: Addressing coherent data is important in practice (motion segmentation, vision, recommendation). The theory and empirical gains suggest impact for robust subspace learning.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear theoretical advance: µ2-agnostic recovery via low-rank dictionaries.
- Simple, practical algorithm with notable empirical gains.
- Illuminates the role of coherence in clustered low-rank data.

Cons against acceptance:
- Key assumption (U0 ⊂ UA) is strong; no guarantee the RPCA-initialized dictionary satisfies it.
- Limited baselines (e.g., Outlier Pursuit or robust factorization with side information).
- No ablation on parameter sensitivity or failure modes when µ1 is large (row clustering).
