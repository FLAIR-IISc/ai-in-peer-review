Summary
The paper addresses robust low-rank matrix recovery when the low-rank component exhibits clustering structure, leading to high coherence and degraded performance of Robust PCA (RPCA). It revisits the standard RPCA formulation min ||L||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = L + S, and analyzes why its success conditions depend on coherence parameters µ1, µ2, µ3. The authors identify a “µ2-phenomenon”: for data formed by samples from a union of subspaces (multiple clusters) whose span has rank r0, the second coherence parameter µ2 (and hence µ3) increases with the number of clusters, tightening RPCA’s rank bounds and hurting recovery.

To address this, the paper studies Low-Rank Representation (LRR): min ||Z||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = AZ + S, with a pre-specified dictionary A. The main theoretical claim is that, if A is low-rank and its column space contains the true column space of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), then exact (noiseless) or near-exact (noisy) recovery can be achieved with bounds that do not involve µ2 (only µ1(A) appears). This suggests a way to “avoid” the harmful dependence on µ2 arising from clustering. Building on this, the authors propose a practical unsupervised algorithm: use RPCA to obtain an initial estimate of L0, truncate by estimated rank, column-normalize to form a low-rank dictionary A, and then solve LRR with that A. Empirical results on synthetic union-of-subspace data and corrupted motion sequences (Hopkins-style trajectories) show better recovery and downstream subspace clustering performance compared to RPCA.

Relation to prior work: RPCA [Candès et al.] provides guarantees under incoherence; LRR [Liu et al.] has mostly been studied with A = X. Outlier Pursuit and related variants focus on different corruption models (e.g., column outliers). This paper’s novelty is a theoretical analysis for LRR with general low-rank dictionaries that mitigates coherence issues linked to clustering, together with a simple unsupervised procedure to learn such a dictionary from data.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The theorems are clearly stated for noiseless and noisy settings, with conditions on rank(A), column normalization, support size |Ω|, and the containment U0 ⊂ UA. The claimed “immunity to µ2” is precise in the sense that µ2(L0) does not appear in the bounds; instead, recovery depends on µ1(A) and rank(A). The containment condition is necessary for exact recovery (AZ = L0 implies U0 ⊂ UA). The analysis appears consistent with RPCA literature and typical proof techniques (dual certificates), though the proofs are deferred to the full paper. The algorithmic steps (rank estimation, truncation, normalization) are reasonable heuristics for producing a well-conditioned low-rank A; however, no formal guarantee is provided that the learned A satisfies U0 ⊂ UA when RPCA is imperfect. The experimental methodology is coherent and the outcomes match the theory qualitatively.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized. It provides a clear exposition of coherence parameters and their behavior under clustering. Notation is defined carefully. References to figures and the full paper are frequent; including key proof sketches in the main text would strengthen self-containment. The algorithm is precisely specified. Experimental settings are described, though some details (e.g., exact synthetic subspace generation, threshold choices) could be elaborated slightly.
- Originality: The link between clustering-induced coherence and RPCA failure modes is known qualitatively, but the formal separation and targeting of µ2 with an LRR scheme using low-rank dictionaries appears novel. Prior LRR work emphasized A = X; here, low-rank dictionaries with theoretical guarantees and a practical learning strategy are new contributions.
- Significance: The problem is important, as many real datasets have clustered subspace structure. The theoretical insight that a low-rank dictionary can remove µ2 from recovery conditions is useful and may influence future robust subspace modeling. The practical algorithm is simple and shows improvements over RPCA in both synthetic and real data with meaningful downstream impact (reduced clustering errors). The breadth of experiments is moderate; stronger empirical validation would further support significance.

Strengths
- Clear identification and formalization of the µ2-phenomenon tied to clustering.
- Theoretical guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that avoid dependence on µ2, with both noiseless and noisy results.
- Practical, simple unsupervised algorithm that leverages RPCA to construct a suitable dictionary and empirically improves recovery.
- Demonstrated improvements on synthetic union-of-subspaces data and on motion segmentation benchmarks, with meaningful error reductions.

Weaknesses
- The key assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong; the learning algorithm has no guarantee to achieve it when RPCA is substantially inaccurate.
- Empirical evaluation, while positive, is limited in scope: a single synthetic setup and 11 motion sequences; no ablation on number of clusters vs. µ2, sensitivity to rank estimation threshold, or robustness to heavier dense noise.
- Comparison baselines could be expanded (e.g., LRR with A = X quantified in recovery, reweighted/iterative PCP variants, alternative dictionary learning strategies).
- Proofs are deferred; the main text would benefit from at least outlines to aid verification by readers.
- The treatment of missing entries as Bernoulli ±1 corruptions is a simplification; explicit handling of missing data models would increase relevance.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel theoretical result showing how to circumvent coherence-induced failures of RPCA via low-rank dictionaries in LRR.
- Conceptual clarity on the role of coherence parameters, particularly µ2, in clustered data.
- Practical method with tangible improvements and low additional computational overhead.
- Relevance to robust subspace modeling and downstream tasks like motion segmentation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Strong assumptions for theory and lack of guarantees for the proposed unsupervised dictionary construction when RPCA fails.
- Limited experimental breadth and baselines; unclear how broadly the gains hold across datasets and noise/corruption regimes.
- Dependence on RPCA as a first step may limit benefits exactly where RPCA struggles most.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide proof sketches or key lemmas in the main paper to enhance verifiability.
- Add experiments varying the number of clusters and measuring µ2 to empirically validate the µ2-phenomenon and the robustness of LRR with learned A.
- Include ablations on the rank estimation threshold, dictionary conditioning, and iterative refinement beyond a single LRR step.
- Compare against LRR with A = X and other robust PCA variants more comprehensively in recovery metrics (not only downstream clustering).
- Explore explicit missing-data formulations rather than replacing missing entries with random ±1 values, and report sensitivity to dense noise levels and support sizes.
