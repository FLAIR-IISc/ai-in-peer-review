

This paper addresses a significant limitation of RPCA (Robust Principal Component Analysis) when applied to data with clustering structures that induce high coherence. The authors demonstrate that as the number of clusters increases, RPCA's performance degrades due to heightened coherence parameters (particularly μ2), and propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a properly configured dictionary to overcome this issue. Their key theoretical contribution is proving that when the dictionary itself is low-rank and contains the column space of the true low-rank matrix, LRR can avoid coherence parameter dependencies. They further develop a practical unsupervised algorithm that leverages RPCA's output to construct such a dictionary, validated on synthetic matrices and motion sequences.

The paper excels in quality, with sound theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) that extend RPCA's framework to handle coherent data while providing clear empirical validation. The connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters is well-explained, building naturally on prior RPCA work [4] and LRR literature [13]. The clarity is excellent, with well-organized exposition that effectively connects the problem statement, theoretical analysis, and practical algorithm. The originality is strong, as the authors identify a specific limitation of RPCA (coherence issues from clustering) not fully addressed in previous work, and propose a novel solution using LRR with low-rank dictionaries. The significance is high, as the problem is important in real-world applications where data often exhibits clustering structures, and the proposed method demonstrates clear improvements over RPCA in both synthetic and real motion data experiments.

Weaknesses include limited sensitivity analysis of the proposed algorithm to parameter choices (particularly rank estimation in Step 2), and the paper could better quantify the improvement over RPCA in Figure 4 (e.g., exact success rate differences). While the unsupervised dictionary construction is practical, the authors could more thoroughly discuss potential failure cases when RPCA's initial estimate is poor.

Overall, this is a technically sound, clearly presented paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. It addresses an important limitation of existing methods with novel theoretical insights and practical validation, advancing the state of the art in low-rank matrix recovery. The work is well-motivated, properly contextualized within the literature, and provides a valuable solution to a problem affecting real-world applications. I recommend acceptance.
