This paper addresses the problem of low-rank matrix recovery in the presence of corruptions, focusing on the limitations of Robust PCA (RPCA) when the underlying low-rank matrix exhibits clustering structure, leading to high coherence. The authors argue that coherence hinders RPCA performance and propose utilizing Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully chosen, low-rank dictionary to mitigate this issue. They prove a theorem demonstrating that a low-rank dictionary renders LRR immune to the coherence parameter associated with clustering, and propose an algorithm to learn such a dictionary from the data itself. The paper is well-motivated, tackles a relevant problem, and presents a theoretically grounded approach with supporting experimental results.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly identifies a weakness of RPCA – its sensitivity to data coherence arising from clustering – and frames it as a significant challenge in low-rank matrix recovery.
* **Theoretical Contribution:**  The core theoretical result – that a low-rank dictionary in LRR effectively addresses the coherence issue – is a valuable contribution.  The proof (though referred to as being in the full arXiv version) appears promising based on the description. The connection between dictionary rank and coherence avoidance is insightful.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed algorithm, leveraging RPCA to initialize the dictionary for LRR, is a sensible and practical approach. It builds upon existing methods and offers a potential improvement in performance.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real-world (motion sequences) datasets provide evidence supporting the claims. The performance gain over RPCA is demonstrated convincingly. The use of standard subspace clustering evaluation metrics enhances credibility.
* **Good Related Work:**  The paper cites a substantial body of relevant work in RPCA, low-rank matrix completion, and subspace learning, demonstrating awareness of the existing literature.  The referencing is thorough and appropriate.
* **Well-written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and follows a logical structure, making it relatively easy to follow the arguments.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Reliance on arXiv for Proof:** The review is hampered by the fact that the full proof of Theorem 1 is only available on arXiv. While understandable given conference page limits, a more detailed sketch of the key ideas behind the proof within the paper itself would significantly strengthen it. Assessing the novelty and rigor of the proof is difficult without it.
* **Dictionary Learning Algorithm – Heuristic Nature:** The algorithm for dictionary learning is described as “heuristic.” While practical, it lacks a strong theoretical justification beyond the core theorem. Exploring alternative dictionary learning strategies or providing a more detailed analysis of the algorithm's properties would be beneficial.  How robust is the initial RPCA step?  If RPCA fails, does the subsequent LRR step still perform well?
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of λ (lambda) is fixed at 1/sqrt(n1). While mentioned as following RPCA, a discussion of the sensitivity of the performance to this parameter, and perhaps some parameter tuning experiments, would be helpful.
* **Limited Comparison:** The experiments primarily compare against RPCA. While this is a direct comparison to the method the paper aims to improve, including comparisons to other state-of-the-art robust matrix completion algorithms (beyond just subspace clustering on the recovered data) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.  For instance, a comparison to methods utilizing iterative soft-thresholding or other dictionary learning approaches would be insightful.
* **µ2-Phenomenon Discussion:** The explanation of why µ2 increases with the number of clusters, while alluded to, is not fully fleshed out within the paper. Referring to Figure 2 is helpful, but a more detailed intuitive explanation would improve understanding.
* **Computational Complexity:** While mentioning the computational advantage of LRR with a low-rank dictionary over RPCA, a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, particularly the dictionary learning step, would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant limitation of a widely used algorithm (RPCA).
* Presents a novel theoretical insight connecting dictionary rank and coherence.
* Offers a practical algorithm with demonstrable improvements over existing methods.
* Well-written, well-motivated, and thoroughly referenced.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of full proof within the paper limits assessment of theoretical rigor.
* Dictionary learning algorithm is somewhat heuristic and lacks deeper analysis.
* Limited comparison to alternative robust matrix completion methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery. The theoretical result is insightful, and the proposed algorithm shows promising experimental results. While the reliance on the arXiv for the full proof and the heuristic nature of the dictionary learning algorithm are weaknesses, they are not substantial enough to warrant rejection. The strengths of the paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the following:

1.  Include a more detailed sketch of the key ideas behind the proof of Theorem 1 within the paper itself.
2.  Discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm to the choice of λ and potentially include some parameter tuning experiments.
3.  Expand the experimental comparison to include other state-of-the-art robust matrix completion methods.
4.  Provide a more intuitive explanation of the µ2-phenomenon.
