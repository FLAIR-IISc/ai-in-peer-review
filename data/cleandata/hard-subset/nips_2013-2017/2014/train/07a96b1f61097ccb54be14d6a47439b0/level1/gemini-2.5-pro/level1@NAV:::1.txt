This paper addresses a key limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA), a foundational method for separating a data matrix into low-rank and sparse components. The authors convincingly argue that RPCA’s performance degrades on “coherent” data, a scenario that frequently arises when the underlying data has a multi-subspace or clustering structure. They demonstrate that as the number of clusters grows, a specific coherence parameter increases, violating the conditions for RPCA's guaranteed recovery. The central contribution is to show, both theoretically and empirically, that this challenge can be overcome by using the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework with a properly chosen dictionary. The authors prove that if the LRR dictionary is itself low-rank and its column space contains that of the true data, the recovery becomes immune to the problematic coherence parameter. This insight leads to a practical two-stage unsupervised algorithm: first, an initial estimate is obtained via RPCA, which is then used to construct a dictionary for a second, refining LRR step.

This is a high-quality paper with several notable strengths. Its primary contribution is the novel and significant insight it provides into a practical failure mode of RPCA, linking the abstract concept of coherence to the tangible property of data clustering. This is an important connection that deepens the community's understanding of robust subspace recovery, building upon seminal works like Candès et al. (2011). The theoretical analysis is sound, providing a clear justification for why a low-rank dictionary in LRR is beneficial. Furthermore, the claims are well-supported by extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world motion capture data, where the proposed algorithm demonstrates a clear and substantial improvement over standard RPCA, widening its region of successful recovery and improving performance on downstream tasks.

The paper’s main weakness is that the proposed practical algorithm is a heuristic whose performance is contingent on the initial RPCA step providing a reasonable estimate. The paper could be strengthened by discussing the algorithm’s behavior when the initial RPCA output is very poor. Additionally, the dictionary construction method is straightforward, and exploring alternatives could be a valuable extension.

Despite these minor limitations, the paper’s strengths are compelling. The novel insights, strong theoretical grounding, and convincing empirical results make this a solid and significant contribution to the field. It advances the state of the art in robust low-rank recovery and is likely to be built upon by future work.
