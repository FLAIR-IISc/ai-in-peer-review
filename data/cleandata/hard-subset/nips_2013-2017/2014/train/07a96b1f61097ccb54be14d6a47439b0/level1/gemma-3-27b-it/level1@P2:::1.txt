This paper addresses the problem of low-rank matrix recovery in the presence of corruptions, specifically focusing on the impact of data coherence on the performance of Robust PCA (RPCA). The authors argue that RPCA's performance degrades with increasing data coherence, which arises naturally in datasets with clustering structures. They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary to mitigate this issue, proving that a low-rank dictionary renders LRR immune to the coherence parameter. They further present an algorithm to learn the dictionary in an unsupervised manner, demonstrating improvements over RPCA on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and builds upon established work in RPCA and LRR. The mathematical proofs, while not fully detailed within the submission (referencing arXiv), appear rigorous based on the presented arguments. The connection between data coherence, clustering, and RPCA performance is clearly articulated and motivated. The theoretical results regarding the impact of a low-rank dictionary in LRR are a significant contribution. However, the claim of being "immune" to the coherence parameter feels strong; it’s more accurate to say that the dependence on coherence is shifted to the dictionary itself. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm but could benefit from more detailed statistical analysis (e.g., confidence intervals, p-values) to solidify the claims of improvement. The algorithm proposed is reasonable and practical. The authors are careful to mention limitations (e.g. reliance on a correct rank estimate), and the discussion of noise tolerance is a valuable addition.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly outlines the problem and motivation. The notation is consistent and well-defined, though potentially dense for readers unfamiliar with the field. The explanation of the "µ2-phenomenon" is clear and supported by the provided figure. The transition between theoretical analysis and the proposed algorithm is smooth. However, the paper relies heavily on referencing the arXiv version for full proofs, which hinders reproducibility without access. Expanding on key proof steps within the paper itself would improve clarity.

**Originality:** The problem of coherence in low-rank matrix recovery isn't entirely novel, but the specific insight connecting coherence to clustering structures and the proposed solution using a low-rank dictionary within the LRR framework are original. The theoretical analysis linking dictionary rank to coherence independence is a noteworthy contribution. While LRR itself is not new, the application and theoretical grounding for addressing coherent data are innovative.  The unsupervised dictionary learning algorithm, building on RPCA, represents a practical extension of existing techniques. The paper appropriately cites related work and positions itself within the existing literature. Checking recent NIPS proceedings confirms a lack of directly comparable work tackling coherence through this specific dictionary learning approach.

**Significance:** This work addresses a practical limitation of RPCA, making it more applicable to real-world datasets with inherent clustering. The theoretical results provide a fundamental understanding of the interplay between coherence, low-rank structure, and dictionary design. The proposed algorithm offers a potentially improved method for robust matrix recovery. The experimental validation on motion sequences demonstrates the practical benefits. This work has the potential to be impactful for researchers and practitioners working in areas such as computer vision, signal processing, and machine learning where robust low-rank recovery is crucial.  The insights into the role of coherence are valuable beyond the specific context of RPCA and LRR.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to addressing coherence in low-rank matrix recovery.
*   Clear connection established between coherence, clustering, and RPCA performance.
*   Practical algorithm with demonstrated improvements over RPCA on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   Significant contribution to understanding the role of dictionaries in LRR.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Reliance on the arXiv version for full proofs hinders reproducibility.
*   Experimental results could benefit from more rigorous statistical analysis.
*   The claim of being "immune" to the coherence parameter is slightly overstated.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**. The originality, technical quality, and potential impact of the work outweigh the minor weaknesses. Addressing the concerns regarding reproducibility by including key proof steps and strengthening the statistical analysis of the experimental results would further enhance the paper. This work represents a valuable contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery.
