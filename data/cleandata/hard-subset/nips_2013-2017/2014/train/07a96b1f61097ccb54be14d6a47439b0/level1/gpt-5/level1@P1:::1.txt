Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses low-rank matrix recovery under entrywise gross corruptions when the low-rank component exhibits clustering structure. While Robust PCA (RPCA/PCP; Candès et al., JACM’11) recovers a low-rank L0 and sparse S0 by nuclear- plus l1-norm minimization, its guarantees rely on incoherence of L0. The authors empirically and conceptually link clustering (union-of-subspaces structure) to increases in the second coherence parameter µ2 (and thus µ3), degrading RPCA’s performance as the number of clusters grows (“µ2-phenomenon”). They propose to use Low-Rank Representation (LRR; Liu et al., PAMI’13) with a properly chosen dictionary A, and prove that if A is itself low-rank and its column space contains that of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), then exact (noiseless) and near-exact (noisy) recovery are possible without conditions on µ2. The rank bound they obtain is rank(L0) ≤ O(n2/log n1) (with standard log factors and µ1(A)), which is less restrictive than the classic RPCA bound O(n2/(log n1)^2). They give a simple unsupervised algorithm: run RPCA once, rank-truncate the recovered L̂0, normalize its columns to form A, then run LRR with that dictionary. On synthetic data with clustered subspaces and on corrupted motion sequences (Hopkins-style), the method improves recovery and downstream subspace clustering error versus RPCA preprocessing.

This work builds directly on PCP/RPCA and LRR, as well as the coherence concepts in matrix completion (Candès & Recht; Candès & Plan) and robust subspace methods (Outlier Pursuit: Xu et al.; SSC: Elhamifar & Vidal). The novelty lies in formalizing the effect of clustering on coherence and in theoretical guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that avoid µ2.

Quality:
- Theory: The paper states clear recovery theorems for noiseless and noisy cases under standard random-sparsity assumptions on S0 (implicit in the probability statements) and the key structural condition U0 ⊂ UA with column-normalized A. The “immunity” to µ2 is theoretically meaningful: success conditions do not involve µ2 or µ3, only µ1(A). The bounds are plausible and consistent with PCP analyses; constants and log factors are reasonable, though the near-recovery bound ‖AZ*−L0‖F ≤ 8√(mn)ε is loose.
- Algorithmic contribution: The unsupervised two-stage procedure is simple and practical. However, there is no formal guarantee that the RPCA-based A will satisfy U0 ⊂ UA when RPCA underperforms on highly coherent data; the argument is heuristic (“RPCA is not brittle”). An iterative refinement is suggested but not studied.
- Experiments: Synthetic experiments systematically vary rank and sparsity and show a notably larger exact-recovery region (≈+47%). The motion segmentation study shows meaningful improvements in clustering error after recovery (e.g., SSC from 22.8%→9.5% with RPCA→5.7% with learned-dictionary LRR). More ablations (e.g., sensitivity to dictionary rank/conditioning, effect of iterative updates, robustness to mis-specified A) would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. The µ2-phenomenon is clearly explained and illustrated. Some minor issues: a few typos (e.g., “PRCA”), misplaced footnote markers, and brief statements of randomness assumptions (support/sign model) could be made explicit. The algorithm should address normalization when columns are near-zero. A short discussion of computational complexity with the specific solvers used (e.g., ALM/ADMM) would aid reproducibility.

Originality:
Analyzing the relationship between clustering and coherence in the context of PCP and showing that LRR with low-rank dictionaries can provably avoid µ2 is original and insightful. While LRR itself is known, general-dictionary recovery guarantees tailored to coherent data appear new. The unsupervised dictionary-construction heuristic is simple but natural.

Significance:
The work addresses a real weakness of PCP in clustered data, common in vision and other domains. The theoretical insight gives a principled way to choose dictionaries for LRR, and the practical algorithm shows tangible gains for recovery and subspace clustering. The reliance on a decent RPCA initialization may limit robustness in the most challenging regimes, but the contribution is still likely to influence practice and subsequent theory on robust subspace modeling.

Pros:
- Clear identification and analysis of the “µ2-phenomenon” linking clustering to coherence.
- Recovery theorems for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that eliminate dependence on µ2.
- Simple, practical unsupervised algorithm with consistent improvements over RPCA.
- Demonstrated downstream gains in motion segmentation.

Cons:
- The crucial assumption U0 ⊂ UA is enforced only heuristically; no guarantee that the RPCA-based A satisfies it when RPCA fails significantly.
- Experimental scope is modest; limited ablations and sensitivity analyses.
- Noise model and missing-data handling in the real-data experiment (replacing missing entries with ±1) is somewhat artificial; a formulation that treats missingness explicitly would be preferable.
- Some constants/bounds are loose; computational details are sparse.

Suggestions for improvement:
- State explicitly the randomness assumptions on S0 (support distribution and sign model) in the theorems.
- Provide an ablation varying the number of clusters and measuring µ2 and recovery performance for RPCA vs LRR, to empirically validate the “immunity” claim more directly.
- Explore iterative refinement of A and quantify gains/stability.
- Evaluate sensitivity to λ, ε, and dictionary conditioning; compare to other robust matrix factorization methods (e.g., GoDec) and Outlier Pursuit where appropriate.
- In the motion experiment, handle missing entries via projection operators rather than substitution, or justify the chosen surrogate.

Overall recommendation:
A solid and timely contribution that combines a novel theoretical perspective with a practical algorithm yielding clear improvements over RPCA in clustered settings. With some additional empirical depth and clarity on assumptions, this would make a strong NeurIPS paper.
