- Addresses low-rank matrix recovery with corruption, focusing on coherence from clustering structures.  
- Argues RPCA degrades on coherent data and proposes LRR with a carefully designed dictionary as a solution.  
- Theoretical result: a low-rank dictionary makes LRR immune to coherence.  
- Proposes an algorithm to learn such a low-rank dictionary from data.  
- Experiments on synthetic and real datasets show the method outperforms RPCA.  

- Strength: Well-motivated with a clear connection between coherence, clustering, and RPCA performance.  
- Strength: Significant theoretical contribution providing foundation for the method.  
- Strength: Good placement in related work (RPCA and LRR) and clear novelty.  
- Strength: Empirical results convincingly support claims.  
- Strength: Problem is significant for applications with clustering structure.  

- Weakness: Key proofs are mostly in the arXiv version, limiting on-paper rigor assessment.  
- Weakness: Dense and occasionally confusing notation (e.g., reused symbols like U).  
- Weakness: Dictionary-learning algorithm lacks detailed parameter-selection guidance (beyond λ = 1/√n1).  
- Weakness: Computational complexity discussion is imprecise; runtime comparisons with RPCA on large data are missing.  
- Weakness: Practical implications of the µ2-phenomenon could be better elaborated.  
- Weakness: Explanation that RPCA’s performance is degraded (not brittle) with coherent data is vague.  

- For acceptance: Novel insight into RPCA limitation, solid theoretical proof, empirical validation, and practical significance.  
- Against acceptance: Reliance on external proofs, clarity issues, and insufficient algorithmic/runtime detail.  

- Overall: Despite clarity and detail issues, strengths outweigh weaknesses; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity, parameter details, and include key proofs or better supplements.
