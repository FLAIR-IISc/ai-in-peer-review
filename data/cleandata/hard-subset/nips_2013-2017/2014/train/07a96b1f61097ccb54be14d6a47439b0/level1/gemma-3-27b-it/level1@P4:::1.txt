This paper addresses the problem of low-rank matrix recovery in the presence of corruption, focusing on the challenges posed by data coherence arising from clustering structures. The authors argue that standard Robust PCA (RPCA) struggles with such coherent data and propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully designed dictionary to mitigate this issue. They prove that a low-rank dictionary renders LRR immune to coherence, and present an algorithm to learn such a dictionary from the data. Experiments on synthetic and real data demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.

The paper is well-motivated and tackles an important problem. The connection between data coherence, clustering structures, and the performance degradation of RPCA is clearly explained. The theoretical result demonstrating the benefits of using a low-rank dictionary in LRR is significant and provides a solid foundation for the proposed method. The authors appropriately relate their work to existing literature, particularly RPCA and LRR, and highlight the novelty of their contribution. The experiments on both synthetic and real data provide convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness, showing improvements over RPCA. 

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses regarding clarity and depth of technical explanation. The proofs, while alluded to, are largely relegated to the arXiv version. This makes it difficult to fully assess the rigor of the theoretical contributions. The notation is dense and occasionally confusing, especially with multiple uses of the same symbols for different concepts (e.g., U for singular vectors and the subspace spanned by them). The algorithm proposed for dictionary learning, while reasonable, lacks detailed explanation of parameter selection (beyond stating λ = 1/sqrt(n1)). The discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm could be more precise, and a comparison of runtime performance with RPCA on larger datasets would be beneficial. While the µ2-phenomenon is described well, its connection to practical implications beyond the theoretical framework could be more elaborated. Finally, the explanation of why RPCA is not "brittle" with respect to coherent data but rather has its performance degraded is somewhat vague.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper identifies a limitation of RPCA regarding coherent data and proposes a novel solution based on LRR with a low-rank dictionary.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The proof demonstrating the coherence-immunity of LRR with a low-rank dictionary is a significant contribution.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Experiments demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms RPCA on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   **Significance:**  The problem of handling coherent data is relevant to many applications where clustering structures are prevalent.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Detail in Proofs:** The reliance on the arXiv version for full proofs hinders a thorough evaluation of the theoretical claims.
*   **Clarity Issues:** Dense notation and occasional vagueness in explanations make the paper challenging to follow.
*   **Limited Algorithm Detail:** Insufficient detail regarding parameter selection and runtime analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the clarity and detail concerns, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery. The theoretical results are significant, and the experimental validation supports the proposed method. The identified limitation of RPCA and the proposed solution are important insights. I believe the strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions focusing on improving clarity, providing more details about the algorithm’s parameter settings, and ideally, including key aspects of the proofs within the main paper or in a more accessible supplementary material.
