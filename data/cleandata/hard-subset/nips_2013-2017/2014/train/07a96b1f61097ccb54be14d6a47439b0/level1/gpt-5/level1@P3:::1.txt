Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the degradation of Robust PCA (RPCA) [4] when the low-rank component L0 exhibits additional structure—specifically, clustering (union-of-subspaces) that induces high coherence. Building on the LRR model [13], the authors prove that Low-Rank Representation with an appropriately chosen low-rank dictionary A avoids dependence on the second coherence parameter µ2 (and thus mitigates the “µ2-phenomenon” that worsens with the number of clusters). They provide noiseless and noisy guarantees: if U0 (the column space of L0) is contained in UA (the column space of A) and A itself is low-rank and column-wise normalized, then solving minZ,S ||Z||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = AZ + S exactly (or approximately) recovers L0, S0, without µ2 appearing in the condition. This shifts the critical assumptions from L0’s coherence to the dictionary’s properties.

To operationalize this in unsupervised settings, they propose a simple two-stage algorithm: run RPCA, estimate rank and a low-rank approximation L̃0, normalize columns of L̃0 to form A, then run LRR with A. They show synthetic experiments where this method increases the “success area” versus RPCA and demonstrate improved motion segmentation accuracy on corrupted sequences, compared with running RPCA preprocessing alone.

This work relates to and extends: RPCA [4], LRR [13,14], robust subspace clustering (SSC) [6], SIM [5]. It complements Outlier Pursuit and related robust PCA variants [23,24] that target different corruption models (column-sparse vs elementwise). The analysis also touches classic coherence notions used in matrix completion [2–4,10,16,19].

Strengths
- Clear identification and explanation of the µ2-phenomenon: clustering increases µ2 (and µ3), explaining RPCA’s degradation as the number of clusters grows. The separation of µ1/µ2/µ3 clarifies why data structure matters.
- Novel theoretical result: LRR with a low-rank dictionary whose span contains U0 can avoid µ2; the recovery conditions depend only on rank(A), µ1(A), and standard sparsity assumptions. The rank bound O(n2/log n1) is slightly looser than RPCA’s (removing a log), which is interesting.
- Practical, simple algorithm that leverages RPCA to learn a dictionary, with empirical evidence that it improves over RPCA on both synthetic and real data.
- Good conceptual insight: coherence is not just a proof artifact but reflects intrinsic structure (e.g., union-of-subspaces), suggesting that modeling such structure via a low-rank dictionary is beneficial.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Strength of assumptions: The key condition PUA(U0) = U0 (U0 ⊂ UA) is strong. In the proposed unsupervised pipeline, A is learned from RPCA’s estimate. If RPCA fails substantially due to high µ2 (the regime the method targets), it is unclear when A will satisfy U0 ⊂ UA. The empirical claim that RPCA is “not brittle” is plausible, but the two-stage method would benefit from analysis showing that approximate recovery suffices (e.g., bounds on the principal angle between Û0 and U0 leading to success probability improvements).
- Shifting coherence: While µ2(L0) is avoided, the conditions now require a low µ1(A). When A is formed by normalizing columns of L̃0, under what conditions is µ1(A) controlled? Some guidance or theory linking cluster models to µ1(A) would strengthen the story and address whether coherence has been fundamentally mitigated or merely moved.
- Experimental scope: Synthetic experiments are informative, but more ablations would help:
  - Compare against LRR with A = X (LRRx) for recovery (not just downstream clustering), and against simple subspace-projection baselines (e.g., project X onto span(Û0) from RPCA and then do sparse correction).
  - Sensitivity to λ, ε, and the rank threshold (10−3σ1). How robust are results to these choices?
  - Explore failure modes when RPCA’s initial estimate is poor (e.g., higher sparsity or more clusters).
  - Use the full Hopkins155 (or a larger subset) and avoid replacing missing entries with ±1 synthetic noise; instead, use standard inpainting/occlusion models or leave them masked and use formulations that handle missing data.
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Some statements defer to the “full paper,” e.g., the derivation of µ2 growth with number of clusters. The NIPS submission should be as self-contained as possible; including at least a sketch or intuition in the main text would help.
  - Minor notational issues (e.g., “column-wisely,” “with probability at least 1 − n−101”) should be cleaned up.
  - Complexity discussion: clarify empirical runtimes and solver details. If A has n columns, solving LRR with general A may still be costly; a brief note on algorithms used (e.g., ADMM, PROPACK for SVDs) and convergence criteria would help reproducibility.

Quality
The theoretical contributions appear sound and relevant, contingent on the strong dictionary subspace assumption. The noisy result provides meaningful stability bounds. The empirical evaluation demonstrates improvements but would benefit from broader comparisons, sensitivity analyses, and more realistic corruption models for the motion data.

Clarity
Overall well written and organized, with careful definitions of coherence and norms. Some arguments central to the µ2-phenomenon and the algorithm’s reliability are deferred; adding these would improve self-containment.

Originality
The main novelty is the proof that LRR with a low-rank dictionary avoids µ2 and the explicit connection to clustering-induced coherence. While LRR is known, the dictionary-design perspective and recovery guarantees under general A (beyond A = X) are new and valuable.

Significance
The work addresses a real limitation of RPCA and provides a practical fix with theoretical backing. The idea of shifting to a learned low-rank dictionary could influence robust recovery and subspace clustering pipelines. With stronger empirical validation and some theoretical relaxation (e.g., approximate subspace inclusion), the impact could be substantial.

Questions for the authors
- Can you bound recovery when Û0 is an approximate subspace (e.g., in terms of principal angles), ensuring that Algorithm 1 improves over RPCA with high probability?
- How does µ1(A) behave under your clustering generative model? Is it typically small when µ2(L0) is large?
- Could you compare to a simple baseline: form Û0 via RPCA, then solve minL,S ||L||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = L + S with the additional constraint L ∈ span(Û0)? This would isolate the benefit of LRR’s AZ parameterization.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear theoretical insight addressing a known weakness of RPCA.
- Practical algorithm with improvements on synthetic and real data.
- Connects coherence to data structure and offers a remedy via dictionary design.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong assumptions for the dictionary; limited analysis of the unsupervised pipeline’s guarantees.
- Experimental evaluation could be broader and more realistic.
- Some dependence seems shifted to µ1(A) without full characterization.

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening empirical comparisons and clarifying the robustness of the unsupervised dictionary-learning step.
