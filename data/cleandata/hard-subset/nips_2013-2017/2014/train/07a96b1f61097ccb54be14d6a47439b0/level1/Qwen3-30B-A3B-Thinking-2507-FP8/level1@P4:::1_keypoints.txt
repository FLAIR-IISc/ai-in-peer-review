- Problem: RPCA fails on coherent, clustered data because coherence parameters (µ2, µ3) grow with cluster number and degrade recovery.
- Proposal: Use LRR with a properly chosen low-rank dictionary to avoid the problematic coherence parameters and recover coherent data.
- Theoretical contribution: Proves (Theorem 1) that if the dictionary is low-rank and satisfies U0 ⊂ UA, LRR sidesteps coherence issues that hurt RPCA.
- Link to clustering: Connects clustering structure in real data to increased coherence parameters, explaining RPCA’s degradation as clusters increase.
- Algorithm: Presents an unsupervised Algorithm 1 that constructs a dictionary from RPCA output to enable LRR in practice.
- Experiments (synthetic): Shows a 47% wider success region for the proposed method vs. RPCA on synthetic data.
- Experiments (real): Demonstrates ≈40% relative improvement in clustering error on motion sequences compared to RPCA.
- Practicality: Algorithm 1 is simple and computationally efficient (~1.2× RPCA runtime) while improving performance.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on RPCA and LRR literature and clearly situates the new theoretical and empirical contributions.
- Weakness — scalability: Paper lacks detailed complexity analysis and discussion of dictionary construction scalability for very large datasets.
- Weakness — assumptions: Theoretical guarantees rely on the dictionary being low-rank and satisfying U0 ⊂ UA, with limited discussion on ensuring this in practice.
- Weakness — baselines: No comparison to other coherence-aware or robust subspace clustering methods, weakening empirical claims of superiority.
- Weakness — sensitivity: Limited analysis of sensitivity to the specific dictionary construction steps (Steps 2–3), which affects reproducibility.
- Pros for acceptance: Identifies a real RPCA limitation, provides strong theory and proofs, shows significant empirical gains, and offers a usable unsupervised algorithm.
- Cons for acceptance: Relies on strong dictionary assumptions, omits comparisons to alternative coherent-data methods, and gives limited large-scale complexity analysis.
- Overall verdict: Significant and well-supported contribution that deepens understanding of coherence in matrix recovery and should be accepted despite some practical and comparative limitations.
