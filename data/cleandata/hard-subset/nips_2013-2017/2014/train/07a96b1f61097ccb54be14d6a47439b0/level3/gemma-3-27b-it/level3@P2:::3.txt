This paper addresses the challenging problem of recovering low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse noise, building upon the well-established framework of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA). The authors identify a critical limitation of RPCA: its sensitivity to data coherence, which often arises naturally from clustering structures inherent in many real-world datasets. They demonstrate that this coherence isn't simply an unavoidable constraint, but rather a manifestation of underlying data structure. The core contribution lies in showing that, by employing Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully constructed, low-rank dictionary, the dependence on coherence parameters can be mitigated, leading to improved recovery guarantees. This work is a significant contribution to the field of compressed sensing and has strong connections to related areas like subspace clustering and matrix factorization.

The paper begins with a clear introduction to the low-rank matrix recovery problem and a comprehensive review of existing approaches, particularly RPCA. It then elegantly articulates the issue of coherence and its connection to clustering structures, a key insight that motivates the proposed solution. The theoretical analysis, culminating in Theorem 1 and Theorem 2, provides rigorous justification for the effectiveness of LRR with a low-rank dictionary. The unsupervised algorithm proposed (Algorithm 1), leveraging RPCA to initialize the dictionary, is practical and offers a compelling alternative to standard RPCA.  Experimental results on both synthetic and real-world motion sequences demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method, particularly in the context of subspace clustering.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The work offers a fresh perspective on the challenges of RPCA, directly addressing the often-overlooked issue of data coherence and its link to clustering. The application of LRR with a learned dictionary to overcome this limitation is a novel and insightful approach.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, with clear theorems and proofs that support the proposed method and delineate its limitations. The extension to the noisy case (Theorem 2) further strengthens the theoretical foundation.
* **Significance:** The results are highly significant. The proposed method demonstrably improves upon RPCA in scenarios where data coherence is high, opening up possibilities for more robust and accurate low-rank recovery in various applications. The connection to subspace clustering, as highlighted in Section 4.2, is particularly compelling, effectively proposing a new method with notable improvements on the Hopkins155 dataset.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, providing a clear explanation of the problem, the proposed solution, and the experimental results. The inclusion of a detailed summary of notations is helpful.
* **Completeness:**  The paper feels like a complete piece of work, providing both theoretical justification and empirical validation.  The algorithm is well-defined, and the experiments are well-designed and presented.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity (Minor):**  There are a few instances where the phrasing is slightly ambiguous (e.g., lines 80-82). While understandable, tightening the language in these areas would improve readability.  An incomplete phrase exists on line 88.
* **Related Work Specificity:** While the paper cites a relevant body of work, there's room for more explicit discussion of connections to specific approaches within the subspace clustering literature. For instance, directly contrasting the proposed method with a pipeline of noisy sparse subspace clustering (ℓ1 regularization) followed by per-subspace PCA would provide a more thorough benchmark.
* **Dictionary Initialization:** While Algorithm 1 provides a practical way to initialize the dictionary, further exploration of different dictionary learning strategies, potentially incorporating more sophisticated optimization techniques, could be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant and novel contribution to the field of robust low-rank matrix recovery.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.
* The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, particularly in scenarios where data coherence is high.
* The paper effectively bridges the gap between low-rank matrix recovery and subspace clustering, offering a promising new direction for research.
* The identified link between coherence and clustering structure is a key insight with broader implications for data analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The clarity issues, though minor, could be addressed with careful editing.
* A more comprehensive comparison with existing subspace clustering methods would strengthen the evaluation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a substantial contribution to the field of compressed sensing. The authors have identified a critical limitation of RPCA and proposed a novel and effective solution based on LRR with a learned dictionary. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental results are compelling, and the paper is generally well-written. I recommend acceptance to NIPS. The three key contributions – identifying clustering-induced high coherence, exploiting it via a dictionary in LRR, and partially solving sparse corruption in subspace clustering – collectively represent a significant advance in the state-of-the-art.
