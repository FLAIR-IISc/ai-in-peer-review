This paper studies failure modes of RPCA on coherent low-rank data arising from clustering, and proposes using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with an appropriately chosen low-rank dictionary to recover the low-rank plus sparse components. The authors formalize a “µ2-phenomenon”: as the number of subspaces (clusters) increases, the second (and third) coherence parameters of the low-rank component rise, degrading RPCA’s guarantees. They then show that LRR can avoid dependence on µ2 if the dictionary A is low-rank and its column space contains the column space of the ground-truth low-rank component (U0 ⊂ UA). The noiseless and noisy theorems establish exact and near-exact recovery with high probability under standard sparsity assumptions and with λ = 1/√n1, while relaxing the rank bound from O(n2/log2 n1) (RPCA) to O(n2/log n1). Practically, they propose an unsupervised, two-stage algorithm: run RPCA to estimate L0, form a well-conditioned, low-rank, column-normalized dictionary from that estimate, and then solve LRR. Experiments on synthetic coherent data and corrupted motion sequences demonstrate clear gains over RPCA, and downstream subspace clustering performance improves markedly for SIM, LRRx, and SSC on corrupted Hopkins trajectories.

Relation to prior work: RPCA [Candès et al., JACM 2011] provides elegant guarantees under incoherence; LRR [Liu et al., PAMI 2013] established recovery/segmentation under special choices of A (often A = X). SSC [Elhamifar & Vidal, CVPR 2009] and SIM [Costeira & Kanade, IJCV 1998] address union-of-subspaces clustering but are sensitive to sparse corruptions without preprocessing. Outlier Pursuit and related robust PCA variants address column-sparse outliers. This paper advances the theoretical understanding of LRR with general, low-rank dictionaries and ties the coherence notion to clustering structure, bridging robust low-rank recovery and subspace clustering. The observation that higher inter-cluster structure (i.e., coherence) can benefit clustering while hurting RPCA aligns with intuition and with observations in the subspace clustering literature (e.g., work by Wang & Xu); the paper’s contribution is to leverage this structure via a learned dictionary for robust recovery.

Quality: The theoretical results are technically sound given the assumptions. The main conditions—column-normalized low-rank A with U0 ⊂ UA, sparsity |Ω| ≤ c mn, and standard λ scaling—are clearly stated. The distinction between µ1(A) and avoiding µ2(L0) is important; the analysis appropriately shifts dependence from the data’s row-space coherence to the dictionary’s column-space coherence. The comparison of bounds versus RPCA is informative. The proposed unsupervised procedure is reasonable: RPCA is often “good enough” to estimate U0; turning that estimate into a low-rank dictionary is natural and supported empirically. Experiments are well chosen: synthetic trials systematically vary rank and sparsity; the motion-segmentation study shows practical impact via reduced clustering error. Some aspects could be strengthened (see below), but overall the work is solid.

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized and accessible to readers familiar with RPCA/LRR. The “µ2-phenomenon” is clearly motivated with figures and discussion. A few language issues impede readability: ambiguous phrasing around the discussion of discarding coherence assumptions in the introduction (lines ~80–82) and an incomplete phrase near line ~88 should be fixed; several sentences would benefit from more precise wording and explicit citations when claims are made. Consider adding a concise intuition subsection explaining why a low-rank A that spans U0 neutralizes row-space coherence, and summarizing the proof strategy for Theorems 1–2.

Originality: Novel and clear contributions include (1) identifying and formalizing clustering-induced coherence as a key failure mode for RPCA; (2) new recovery guarantees for LRR with general low-rank dictionaries that remove dependence on µ2; (3) a simple, effective unsupervised scheme to learn such dictionaries from data. While LRR itself is not new, these theoretical insights and the practical pipeline are original and valuable.

Significance: The ability to robustly recover coherent low-rank structure is important in many applications (vision, recommendation, text). The work offers both conceptual clarity—what coherence measures and why it grows with clustering—and actionable guidance: use a low-rank dictionary aligned with the column space. The subspace clustering improvements on corrupted data are substantial and relevant to the NIPS community.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Assumption strength: Exact recovery requires U0 ⊂ UA. The unsupervised procedure hinges on RPCA producing a sufficiently accurate U0 estimate; when RPCA is very poor (e.g., very high coherence and/or high corruption), the guarantee may not apply. Please discuss robustness when the learned A is imperfect and quantify required subspace estimation accuracy.
- Empirics: Add ablations comparing (i) oracle A (true U0) vs. learned A; (ii) using A = X (standard LRRx) vs. low-rank A; (iii) sensitivity to the rank threshold in Step 2 (σi > 10−3σ1) and to column normalization. Report runtimes and solver details (e.g., ADMM parameters, stopping criteria).
- Baselines: Include a pipeline baseline of noise-robust SSC (ℓ1 with noise) on X followed by per-cluster PCA; also compare RPCA+SSC (already partly included), and, if applicable, Outlier Pursuit variants, to better position the gains.
- Theory–practice gap: Theorems depend on µ1(A); provide empirical measures of µ1(A) for learned dictionaries and analyze recovery as a function of dictionary rank/conditioning.
- Notation: Although six norms are defined, only some are used centrally; streamlining could help. Explicitly state dependence on number of clusters k where possible.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear and novel theoretical insight linking coherence, clustering, and recovery
- New guarantees for LRR with general low-rank dictionaries that avoid µ2
- Simple, practical algorithm with strong empirical gains, including downstream clustering
- Well-situated within and advancing an active line of work of interest at NIPS

Cons for acceptance:
- Reliance on an assumption (U0 ⊂ UA) that may be hard to meet when RPCA fails badly
- Limited ablation and baseline comparisons; some clarity/proofreading issues
- Quantitative characterization of the µ2-phenomenon versus cluster number could be strengthened

Recommendation: Accept. The paper offers substantial theoretical and practical contributions, clarifies an important failure mode of RPCA, and provides a principled and effective remedy via low-rank dictionary pursuit in LRR. With minor revisions to clarity and additional empirical comparisons, it will be a strong NIPS paper.
