This paper addresses a key limitation of the widely used Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) method for recovering a low-rank matrix from grossly corrupted observations. The authors observe that RPCA's performance guarantee degrades as the "coherence" of the low-rank component increases. The central and novel insight of this work is that high coherence is not merely a pathological property but can arise naturally from intrinsic data structures, such as the union-of-subspaces or clustering structure common in real-world data.

The paper makes a compelling case that as the number of clusters in a low-rank dataset grows, the coherence parameter (specifically µ2) increases, thereby degrading RPCA's performance. To overcome this, the authors propose leveraging the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework. They prove theoretically that if the dictionary `A` in the LRR model is itself low-rank and its column space contains the column space of the true low-rank data `L0`, then the recovery guarantee for LRR is independent of the problematic coherence parameters µ2 and µ3. This provides a principled way to handle coherent data. Based on this theory, the paper presents a practical two-stage algorithm: first, run RPCA to obtain an initial estimate of `L0`; second, use this estimate to construct a low-rank dictionary for LRR to refine the recovery. This approach is related to prior work on RPCA, LRR, and subspace clustering, but offers a novel synthesis by using the LRR framework to explicitly address the coherence issue in RPCA.

**Strengths and Weaknesses (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance)**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are significant and appear sound, providing strong justification for the proposed method. Theorems 1 and 2 establish recovery guarantees for the LRR-based approach in both noiseless and noisy settings, importantly removing the dependence on the µ2 parameter that plagues RPCA in the presence of clustering structures. The experimental validation is thorough, with well-designed simulations on synthetic data that clearly illustrate the performance gap between RPCA and the proposed method. The experiments on corrupted motion sequences from the Hopkins155 dataset further demonstrate the practical utility of the work, showing substantial improvements in a downstream subspace clustering task.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the connection between clustering, coherence, and RPCA's limitations is well-explained. However, the clarity could be further improved with careful proofreading. For instance, the phrasing around lines 80-82 describing the effect of clustering behavior could be more precise. There also appears to be a minor grammatical issue or incomplete phrase around line 88 ("As will be seen later..."). A final pass to polish the language and ensure all claims are stated as explicitly as possible would benefit the manuscript.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds on the established frameworks of RPCA and LRR, its core contribution—identifying the link between clustering structure and high coherence and then proposing a dictionary-based LRR solution—is novel. It reframes a perceived limitation of RPCA as an opportunity to exploit additional data structure. This perspective is fresh and distinguishes the work from prior contributions. The proposed algorithm, while conceptually simple, is a direct and effective consequence of this new insight.

**Significance:** The results are important and likely to be influential. The paper provides a deeper understanding of the coherence parameter, which is a cornerstone of the analysis of many low-rank recovery methods. The proposed algorithm offers a practical and demonstrable improvement over RPCA for a class of problems (data with union-of-subspaces structure) that is very common in machine learning and computer vision. The application shown in Section 4.2 effectively constitutes a new and powerful method for subspace clustering in the presence of sparse corruptions, a significant problem in its own right. The ideas presented here are likely to be used and built upon by other researchers.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

1.  The connection between coherence and clustering is fascinating. The authors might find it interesting to discuss the work of Wang & Xu (e.g., "Provable Subspace Clustering: When is LRR More Powerful than L1?"), who show that higher coherence can sometimes be *beneficial* for subspace clustering. The present paper provides a complementary perspective, focusing on the challenge high coherence poses for low-rank recovery and how the underlying subspace structure can be leveraged to overcome it.

2.  To further strengthen the experimental section, it would be valuable to compare against a different, plausible baseline for the task of robust subspace clustering. For example, one could first apply a sparse subspace clustering method (like SSC) directly to the corrupted data to get cluster assignments (robustly, though perhaps imperfectly) and then apply PCA within each estimated cluster to clean the data. Comparing the proposed method to such a two-stage clustering-then-denoising pipeline would provide a more complete picture of its performance.

3.  The algorithm proposed in Section 4.2 for motion segmentation is a key contribution. By preprocessing the corrupted data with the proposed recovery method, the authors achieve state-of-the-art results on a challenging task. This should be highlighted as a significant practical outcome of the theoretical development.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper makes a novel and significant conceptual contribution by linking RPCA's coherence limitation to the exploitable clustering structure of data.
*   **Pro:** It provides strong theoretical guarantees that demonstrate how an LRR-based approach with a learned dictionary can provably overcome this limitation.
*   **Pro:** The proposed algorithm is simple, practical, and shows substantial empirical improvements over standard RPCA on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   **Pro:** The work effectively advances the state of the art in robust subspace clustering, a problem of broad interest.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The manuscript would benefit from minor improvements in clarity and language precision.
*   **Con:** The experimental comparison could be strengthened by including an alternative pipeline for robust subspace clustering as a baseline.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a substantial contribution to the theory and practice of robust low-rank matrix recovery. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed in a final version. I strongly recommend acceptance.
