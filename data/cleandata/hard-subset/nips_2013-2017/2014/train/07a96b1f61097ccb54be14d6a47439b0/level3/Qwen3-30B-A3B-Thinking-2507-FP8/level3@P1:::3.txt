

This paper addresses an important limitation of Robust PCA (RPCA) when dealing with coherent data, which is ubiquitous in real-world applications. The authors demonstrate that RPCA's performance degrades as data exhibits clustering structures (e.g., union of subspaces), because the coherence parameters increase with the number of clusters. The paper's key insight is that these coherence parameters are not merely theoretical assumptions but reflect intrinsic data structures, and that Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a properly configured dictionary can overcome this limitation.

The paper makes three significant contributions. First, it establishes the connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters, showing that the µ2-phenomenon (where µ2 increases with cluster number) directly explains RPCA's performance drop. Second, it proves that LRR with a low-rank dictionary A (satisfying U0 ⊂ UA) can avoid dependence on µ2, providing stronger theoretical guarantees than RPCA for exact and noisy recovery. Third, it proposes a practical unsupervised algorithm for dictionary learning that outperforms RPCA in both synthetic and real-world experiments.

The theoretical contribution is strong and well-supported. The authors prove Theorem 1 (noiseless case) and Theorem 2 (noisy case), showing that LRR with a low-rank dictionary achieves recovery under looser rank constraints than RPCA (O(n2/log n1) vs. O(n2/(log n1)2)). The proof techniques extend the RPCA framework while incorporating the new dictionary learning perspective. The experimental validation is thorough: Figure 4 demonstrates 47% wider success area for the proposed method compared to RPCA on synthetic matrices, and Table 1 shows a 40% relative improvement in motion segmentation error rates on the Hopkins155 dataset.

The paper's originality is significant. While LRR with A = X has been studied extensively, the authors provide the first comprehensive analysis of LRR with general dictionaries for coherent data recovery. The connection to subspace clustering is particularly valuable, as Section 4.2 demonstrates how the proposed method substantially improves motion segmentation performance. The paper effectively bridges theoretical insights (coherence as reflecting clustering structure) with practical applications, making it relevant to both theoretical and applied machine learning communities.

The significance of this work is high. The problem of handling coherent data is fundamental to real-world applications where data naturally clusters (e.g., motion sequences, image collections, text documents). The authors' approach provides a principled solution that can be integrated into existing pipelines without major modifications. The Hopkins155 results are particularly compelling, showing that the method improves upon RPCA in a real-world computer vision application.

For clarity, the paper has some minor issues that could be addressed. The phrasing around lines 80-82 is ambiguous, and there's an incomplete phrase at line 88. The connection to Wang & Xu's work on coherence aiding subspace clustering would benefit from more explicit discussion. A brief empirical comparison to a pipeline of sparse subspace clustering followed by per-subspace PCA would strengthen the experimental claims.

The paper's theoretical foundation is solid, with careful attention to the conditions required for exact recovery. The authors are appropriately cautious about the limitations of their approach, noting that the method works best when the dictionary approximately contains the true subspace information. The unsupervised dictionary learning algorithm (Algorithm 1) is practical and computationally efficient, with only a 20% increase in runtime over RPCA.

The work builds meaningfully on prior NIPS contributions. It extends the LRR framework (Liu et al., NIPS 2010), connects to RPCA (Candès et al., JACM 2011), and relates to subspace clustering (Elhamifar & Vidal, CVPR 2013). The paper's key insight that coherence parameters reflect data structure rather than being an unavoidable theoretical bottleneck represents a significant conceptual advance beyond previous work.

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to robust matrix recovery. The theoretical insights are novel and well-justified, the experimental validation is thorough and relevant, and the practical implications for applications like motion segmentation are compelling. The minor clarity issues do not detract from the paper's strong technical contribution. Given its significance, originality, and quality, I recommend acceptance to NIPS. The work advances the state of the art in low-rank matrix recovery and provides valuable insights into how to handle coherent data structures that are common in real-world applications.
