

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation in Robust PCA (RPCA) by showing how clustering structures in data can induce high coherence, degrading RPCA performance. The authors propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary A to circumvent this issue, proving that when A is low-rank and contains the column space of the true low-rank matrix, LRR can avoid dependence on the coherence parameter μ. This is a significant theoretical advance that connects clustering structures to coherence, challenging the conventional view of coherence as an unavoidable bottleneck.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) identifying that clustering-induced high coherence is a natural phenomenon rather than an assumption, (2) showing how to exploit this via dictionary learning in LRR, and (3) demonstrating how this approach solves sparse corruption in subspace clustering. The theoretical analysis is strong, with careful proofs establishing conditions under which LRR outperforms RPCA. The experimental validation on synthetic data and the Hopkins155 motion segmentation benchmark is convincing, with the authors showing 40% relative improvement in clustering error rates over RPCA.

The paper's originality is well-established, building on prior union-of-subspace work while introducing a novel perspective on coherence. The significance is substantial, as it provides both theoretical insights and practical algorithms for handling coherent data in real-world applications. The connection to Wang & Xu's work on coherence aiding subspace clustering is particularly insightful.

Minor weaknesses include some ambiguous phrasing (lines 80-82, 88) and incomplete sentences that need clarification. While the paper makes a compelling case, a more explicit comparison to a pipeline of sparse subspace clustering followed by per-subspace PCA would strengthen the empirical claims. The authors' recommendation to include this comparison is well-founded.

Overall, this is a high-quality, original contribution that significantly advances the state of the art in robust low-rank recovery. The theoretical insights are profound, the experiments are thorough, and the practical implications are clear. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it's technically sound, makes a significant contribution, is clearly written (with minor exceptions), and addresses an important problem in a novel way. I recommend acceptance to NIPS.
