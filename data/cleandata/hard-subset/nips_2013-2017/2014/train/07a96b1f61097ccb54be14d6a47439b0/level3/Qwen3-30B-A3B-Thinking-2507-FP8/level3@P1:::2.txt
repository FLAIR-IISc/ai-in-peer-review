

This paper investigates a limitation of Robust PCA (RPCA) when dealing with coherent data (data with high coherence parameters), which often arises from clustering structures in real-world applications. While RPCA excels at recovering low-rank matrices from grossly corrupted observations, its performance degrades as data coherence increases with more clusters. The authors propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a properly configured dictionary to overcome this limitation, proving that if the dictionary itself is low-rank, LRR becomes immune to coherence parameters that increase with cluster number.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it establishes a theoretical connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters, demonstrating that the second coherence parameter µ2 (and µ3) increases with cluster number, directly impacting RPCA's recovery performance. Second, the authors prove that LRR with a low-rank dictionary avoids these coherence issues, providing a theoretical guarantee for exact recovery without relying on coherence parameters. Third, they propose a practical unsupervised algorithm that leverages RPCA to construct the dictionary, showing that this approach outperforms RPCA in both synthetic and real-world motion sequence experiments.

The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with well-justified proofs for both noiseless and noisy recovery scenarios. The authors establish clear success conditions for LRR that are looser than RPCA's requirements (O(n2/log n1) versus O(n2/(log n1)2)), which is a meaningful theoretical advance. The empirical evaluation on randomly generated matrices and motion sequences demonstrates consistent improvements over RPCA, with the proposed algorithm achieving 47% wider success area in synthetic experiments and 40% relative improvement in clustering error rates on real motion data.

The paper builds on previous NIPS work, particularly Candès et al.'s RPCA (NIPS 2011) and Liu et al.'s LRR (NIPS 2013), but makes a significant contribution by identifying the coherence issue and providing a principled solution. The connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters is novel and helps clarify the physical meaning of coherence, which has been widely used but poorly understood in the literature.

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses. The main contribution is buried in technical details, making it difficult for non-specialists to grasp the core insight. The concept of coherence is not well-explained in the main text, requiring readers to dig into the appendix for proper interpretation. The exposition is dense and lacks clear motivation for why the proposed solution is necessary beyond the technical details. The significance of the work for broader machine learning and data analysis communities is not adequately articulated, making it hard to appreciate the paper's impact.

The writing requires substantial improvement to make the contribution accessible to a wider audience. The authors should clearly state their main insight early in the paper and provide intuitive explanations of the coherence concept. The connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters could be better illustrated with simple examples. The paper would benefit from a more structured presentation that emphasizes the practical implications of the theoretical results.

While the technical content is strong and the empirical results are convincing, the paper's accessibility issues significantly hinder its potential impact. The authors should address these concerns in a revision by improving the exposition, clarifying the coherence concept, and better articulating the significance of their work for the broader ML community.

Quality: The theoretical analysis is sound and well-justified, with appropriate experiments supporting the claims. The paper provides a complete piece of work with both theoretical and empirical validation.

Clarity: The paper is poorly written for its intended audience. Key concepts are buried in technical details, and the main contribution is not clearly articulated. Significant improvements in exposition are needed.

Originality: The paper makes a novel contribution by identifying the coherence issue in RPCA and providing a theoretically grounded solution using LRR with low-rank dictionaries. It builds on previous work but advances the state of the art in understanding and handling coherent data.

Significance: The results provide a clear improvement over RPCA for coherent data, which is common in real applications like motion segmentation. However, the significance is not fully realized due to poor exposition, limiting the paper's potential impact.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation, clear empirical improvements over RPCA, addresses an important limitation of RPCA.

Con: Poorly explained core contribution, confusing exposition, lacks clarity for broader audience.

Overall, this paper makes a meaningful theoretical contribution to the field of robust low-rank matrix recovery. While the technical content is strong and the empirical results are convincing, the paper needs substantial rewriting to make its contribution accessible to the broader NIPS community. With significant improvements in exposition and clarity, this would be a valuable contribution to the literature. As it stands, it would benefit from a major revision before acceptance.
