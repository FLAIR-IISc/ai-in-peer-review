Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies robust recovery of a low-rank matrix L0 from grossly corrupted observations X = L0 + S0 by addressing a key failure mode of RPCA/PCP: high coherence induced by clustering (union-of-subspaces) structure. The authors formalize how the second coherence parameter µ2 (and consequently µ3) increases with the number of underlying clusters (“µ2-phenomenon”), which tightens RPCA’s success conditions and degrades performance. They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a suitably chosen dictionary A to mitigate this effect. The central theoretical result shows that if A is low-rank and its column space contains the column space of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), then exact recovery via LRR no longer depends on µ2; the rank bound improves from O(n2/(log n1)2) (RPCA) to O(n2/log n1) under standard random support assumptions on S0. A noisy version provides near-recovery bounds. Building on this, they propose a practical unsupervised pipeline: run RPCA to obtain an estimate of L0, estimate its rank and truncate, normalize columns to form a low-rank dictionary A, and then solve LRR with A. Experiments on synthetic data (union-of-subspaces with varying sparsity and rank) and motion segmentation (Hopkins155 variants) show consistent improvements over RPCA and over standard LRR with A = X.

The work is closely related to RPCA/PCP [Candès et al.], LRR [Liu et al.], Outlier Pursuit and robust subspace methods, as well as subspace clustering methods such as SSC and SIM. The novelty is to connect coherence growth caused by clustering to RPCA’s limitations and to prove that LRR with an appropriately low-rank dictionary can avoid dependence on µ2, with a practical, unsupervised construction of such a dictionary.

Strengths
- Clear identification and empirical/theoretical characterization of the µ2-phenomenon: clustering increases coherence and hurts RPCA, a point of practical importance that is often underappreciated.
- Solid theory: noiseless and noisy guarantees for LRR with a low-rank dictionary that contains U0, avoiding µ2 (and thus µ3) in the conditions and slightly improving the rank bound.
- Practical algorithm: a simple RPCA-initialized procedure to learn A that is effective in unsupervised settings and only modestly increases computation (~1.2×).
- Strong empirical validation: synthetic experiments over a broad grid show a 47% larger success region than RPCA; on corrupted motion data, the method substantially improves downstream subspace clustering error (e.g., SSC from 9.50% to 5.74%).
- Conceptual insight: interprets coherence parameters as reflecting intrinsic data structure (union-of-subspaces) rather than mere technical assumptions.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong; while necessary for exactness, the unsupervised construction lacks a formal guarantee that the learned A meets it (even approximately). It would strengthen the paper to analyze when the RPCA-initialized A achieves sufficient alignment with U0 and how deviations affect guarantees.
- Heuristic choices (rank threshold at 10−3σ1, column normalization, single pass vs. iterative refinement) are not ablated. Please include sensitivity analyses and an ablation of Steps 2–4.
- Baselines: consider comparisons to Outlier Pursuit, alternative PCP variants (e.g., weighted PCP), and a pipeline of noisy sparse subspace clustering (ℓ1) followed by per-subspace PCA. Also compare to LRR with A formed directly from RPCA’s L̂0 but without truncation/normalization to isolate the gain from low-rank A.
- Experimental realism: in motion data, replacing missing entries by ±1 is adversarial but somewhat artificial; reporting results with more realistic missing-at-random imputation or leaving entries as missing (and using algorithms that handle missingness) would be informative.
- Clarity and proofreading: there are a few ambiguous or incomplete phrases early in the introduction; please tighten language, make claims precise (e.g., “immune to µ2” vs. “does not depend on µ2 under stated conditions”), and ensure all related work is properly cited and contrasted. Explicitly discuss how your guarantees relate to those of LRR with A = X.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: The theoretical contributions are technically sound and well-motivated; proofs (as sketched here) align with the literature. The experimental design is sensible, though adding the suggested baselines/ablations would improve completeness.
- Clarity: Generally well-organized with a helpful discussion of µ1/µ2/µ3 and their roles. Some phrasing issues and a couple of incomplete sentences should be fixed. Algorithm 1 is clear and reproducible (parameters specified).
- Originality: The identification of clustering-induced coherence as the culprit for RPCA’s degradation and the demonstration that LRR with a low-rank dictionary avoids µ2 are original and nontrivial extensions of prior LRR/RPCA work.
- Significance: High. Robust low-rank recovery under realistic clustered data is central to many applications (vision, recommendation, text). The approach advances both theory and practice and will likely influence follow-up work in robust subspace methods and subspace clustering.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a real and common failure mode of RPCA with new theory and an effective practical fix.
- Simple algorithm with strong empirical gains on both synthetic and real tasks.
- Conceptual contribution clarifying the role of coherence and its link to union-of-subspaces structure.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong assumption U0 ⊂ UA with no guarantee for the learned dictionary in the unsupervised setting.
- Missing comparisons and ablations; experimental setup for missing data could be broadened.
- Some writing issues and overstrong wording (“immune”) that should be tempered.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper makes a substantial and timely contribution by connecting coherence to clustering, providing improved recovery guarantees via LRR with a low-rank dictionary, and delivering a practical algorithm that improves over RPCA in realistic settings. With minor revisions to clarity and expanded experiments, this will be a strong NIPS paper.
