Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses low-rank plus sparse matrix recovery when the low-rank component exhibits clustering structure, which leads to high coherence and degrades the guarantees and empirical performance of RPCA (Candès et al., JACM 2011). The authors formalize how clustering increases the second coherence parameter µ2 (“µ2-phenomenon”), thereby tightening RPCA’s rank bounds. They propose to mitigate this by using Low-Rank Representation (LRR; Liu et al., ICML/JMLR/PAMI) with a carefully chosen dictionary A. The key insight is that if A is low-rank and its column space contains the ground-truth column space (U0 ⊂ UA), then recovery becomes insensitive to µ2: theorems establish exact or near-exact recovery without invoking µ2 (or µ3), with bounds depending on µ1(A) instead. This generalizes prior LRR analyses that typically take A = X and clarifies when a learned dictionary can “shield” recovery from coherence. Practically, the paper proposes a simple unsupervised heuristic: run RPCA to get an estimate of L0, truncate by rank, normalize columns to form A, and then run LRR. On synthetic data and corrupted motion sequences (Hopkins155 variants), the method improves over RPCA and benefits downstream subspace clustering (SIM, LRRx, SSC), echoing the historical role of RPCA/LRR in vision and signal processing.

Quality
The technical contributions are sound on inspection. The noiseless and noisy theorems state clear conditions (column-normalized A, U0 ⊂ UA, rank bounds involving µ1(A), sparsity up to a constant fraction) and give unique/near-unique solutions with high probability using λ = 1/√n1. The assumptions are well-motivated: exact recovery necessarily requires U0 ⊂ UA; the move from µ(L0) to µ1(A) captures the immunity to µ2. The algorithmic proposal is coherent: RPCA provides a reasonable proxy for U0 even under coherence, and column normalization plus rank truncation temper dictionary conditioning. Experiments support the claims: a substantially larger success region on synthetic trials and a notable (≈40%) relative error reduction for SSC after recovery on real sequences. Some caveats: (i) the theory still depends on µ1(A), so “immunity to coherence” is partial; (ii) U0 ⊂ UA is strong and verifying it in practice is indirect; (iii) the heuristic reuses X to build A, so a discussion of leakage of sparse corruption into A and its impact would strengthen the case. Complexity claims (LRR O(n2 rA) vs RPCA O(n3)) are plausible; wall-clock comparisons and solver details would be helpful.

Clarity
While the core idea is valuable, the exposition is dense and will be accessible mainly to specialists. The roles of µ1, µ2, µ3 and the µ2-phenomenon are scattered and partly deferred; intuition for why clustering inflates µ2 and why a low-rank A suppresses its effect should be presented earlier, with simple geometric diagrams. The transition from RPCA to LRR with general A would benefit from a concise summary of known LRR cases (A = X, A = I) and what changes here. The algorithm description is clear, but practical guidance on thresholding, rank selection, and normalization should be consolidated. Language could be tightened throughout; several statements of contribution repeat similar points.

Originality
The paper builds on well-established lines (RPCA, LRR, subspace clustering, matrix factorization) but makes a novel and useful connection: identifying µ2 as the culprit under clustering and proving that a low-rank dictionary with U0 ⊂ UA removes µ2 from the guarantees. The theorems for general A and the simple RPCA-then-LRR heuristic constitute a new angle on robust low-rank recovery with structured data.

Significance
The contribution is meaningful for robust subspace modeling in scenarios where cluster structure is ubiquitous (vision trajectories, recommendation, biomedical signals). Theoretical insights clarify the role of coherence beyond assumptions; the empirical gains are nontrivial and likely to influence practice where RPCA is a baseline. The approach could inform dictionary design in LRR and related factorization methods.

Pros
- Clear identification of a failure mode of RPCA under clustering via µ2-phenomenon.
- New recovery guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that avoid dependence on µ2/µ3.
- Simple, practical algorithm with consistent empirical gains on synthetic and real data.
- Useful perspective connecting coherence, clustering, and dictionary design.

Cons
- Clarity and accessibility issues: heavy notation, delayed intuition, and scattered explanations of coherence.
- Practical assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong; robustness to violations needs deeper empirical/theoretical treatment.
- Limited ablations: sensitivity to dictionary rank/conditioning, rank-threshold choice, and iterative refinement are not explored.
- Comparisons could be broader (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, other robust factorization/dictionary-learning baselines).

Suggestions for improvement
- Front-load intuition: a short section with geometric sketches explaining µ2 growth under clustering and how a low-rank A projects away µ2.
- Provide ablations: vary dictionary rank, conditioning, and construction (RPCA vs alternative initializers), and report sensitivity to the rank threshold and normalization.
- Evaluate robustness when UA only partially overlaps U0; quantify performance vs principal angle between U0 and UA.
- Report wall-clock times, solver details, and scaling studies.
- Compare against stronger baselines (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, robust NMF/Matrix Factorization, LRR with A = X) across more datasets.
- Clarify the scope of “immunity”: explicitly note dependence on µ1(A) and discuss how to keep µ1(A) small in practice.
- Editing for language and structure; move some notation details to appendix; tighten the statement of contributions.

Recommendation
The paper offers a solid theoretical and practical advance for coherent data recovery and is likely to interest the NIPS community working on robust subspace methods. However, the clarity issues are significant. I lean toward acceptance contingent on substantial rewriting and additional experiments/ablations; absent that, it is borderline. Post-rebuttal: the paper is publishable and interesting, but I would have preferred a major revision focused on accessibility and empirical depth.
