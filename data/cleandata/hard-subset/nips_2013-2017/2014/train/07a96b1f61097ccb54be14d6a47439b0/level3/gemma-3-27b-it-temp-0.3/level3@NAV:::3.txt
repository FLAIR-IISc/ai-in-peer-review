## Review of “Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit”

This paper addresses a critical limitation of Robust PCA (RPCA): its performance degradation with increasing data coherence, a phenomenon often linked to clustering structures. The authors convincingly argue that coherence isn’t simply an unavoidable bottleneck, but rather a reflection of underlying data structure. Their core contribution lies in demonstrating that employing a learned, low-rank dictionary within a Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework can mitigate the impact of coherence, offering stronger theoretical guarantees for low-rank and sparse recovery. 

The work is highly relevant to the NIPS audience, building upon established concepts like RPCA and LRR while offering a novel and significant extension. It connects to prior work on union-of-subspaces and subspace clustering, but presents a less restrictive clustering assumption, potentially broadening its applicability. The theoretical analysis is a strong point, providing conditions under which the dictionary-based LRR approach circumvents the limitations imposed by coherence. The experimental validation, using both synthetic and real-world motion sequence data, supports the theoretical claims and demonstrates practical improvements over standard RPCA, particularly in the context of subspace clustering (Section 4.2). The authors correctly identify potential applications beyond the immediate scope of the paper, such as text analysis.

However, the paper suffers from clarity issues. The phrasing around lines 80-82 is ambiguous, and the incomplete phrase at line 88 detracts from readability. A more rigorous proofreading pass is necessary to improve precision and ensure all claims are explicitly supported by references. While the connection to Wang & Xu’s work on coherence aiding subspace clustering is acknowledged, a more detailed comparison would be beneficial. 

A valuable addition would be an empirical comparison to a pipeline combining noisy sparse subspace clustering (e.g., using ℓ1 regularization) followed by per-subspace PCA. This would provide a stronger baseline for evaluating the proposed method's performance.  The authors highlight Section 4.2 as a novel approach to subspace clustering with sparse corruption, and the significant improvements on Hopkins155 are compelling.

**In summary, the paper’s three key contributions are:** 1) identifying the link between clustering and high coherence, 2) exploiting this through a dictionary in LRR, and 3) partially resolving sparse corruption in subspace clustering.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Novelty, theoretical soundness, significant performance improvements, relevance to NIPS, potential impact on compressed sensing and related fields.
**Arguments against Acceptance:** Clarity issues, need for more comprehensive empirical comparisons.

**Recommendation:** Despite the minor clarity concerns, I strongly recommend acceptance to NIPS. The paper presents a substantial contribution to the field of compressed sensing and robust matrix recovery. Addressing the suggested improvements would further strengthen an already compelling work.
