Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies recovery of low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse gross errors when the clean data exhibit structure such as clustering (union-of-subspaces). Building on robust PCA (RPCA; Candès et al., 2011), the authors diagnose a failure mode: as the number of clusters increases, the row-space coherence parameter µ2 rises (“µ2-phenomenon”), tightening RPCA’s recovery conditions. They then analyze Low-Rank Representation (LRR; Liu et al.) with a general dictionary A and prove that if A is itself low-rank and its column space contains the true column space (U0 ⊂ UA), then LRR’s recovery guarantees no longer depend on µ2 (nor µ3), yielding strictly stronger conditions than RPCA in clustered settings. Noiseless and noisy theorems are provided, with explicit rank and sparsity bounds and the usual λ = 1/√n1 normalization.

Because U0 is unknown in practice, the paper proposes a simple unsupervised heuristic: (1) run RPCA to get an initial estimate L̂0, (2) rank-truncate and column-normalize it to form a low-rank dictionary Â, (3) run LRR with Â. This “low-rank dictionary pursuit” combines the robustness of RPCA with the structure-preserving projection of LRR.

On synthetic data with varying ranks and corruption levels, the method expands the exact-recovery region by ~47% over RPCA. On corrupted motion sequences (Hopkins155 variants), using the learned dictionary before subspace clustering (SIM, LRR with A=X, SSC) consistently lowers error, e.g., SSC error drops from 9.50% (after RPCA) to 5.74% (after the proposed approach).

Strengths
- Clear conceptual insight: clustered (union-of-subspaces) structure inflates µ2, explaining RPCA’s degradation; using a low-rank dictionary aligned with the data avoids dependence on µ2.
- Theorems for noiseless and noisy regimes precisely state conditions (rank(L0) ≤ rank(A) ≤ O(n2/(µ1(A) log n1)), column-wise normalization, support bounds |Ω| ≤ c mn), and show LRR reduces to RPCA when A=I and matches RPCA with orthogonal A.
- Practical, simple heuristic for dictionary learning that outperforms RPCA empirically, while costing only ~1.2× RPCA in runtime.
- The proofs appear technically sound on spot-checking and align with known RPCA/LRR techniques.
- Empirical results support the theory: synthetic recovery phase plots and downstream motion-clustering improvements.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and exposition: the main contributions, intuition, and practical impact are hard to follow. Much of the meaning of “coherence,” especially µ2’s interpretation and the µ2-phenomenon, is buried in appendices; the narrative is accessible mainly to specialists.
- The practical meaning and verifiability of the key assumption U0 ⊂ UA are underexplained; readers need guidance on when the heuristic dictionary approximates this.
- The heuristic’s design and sensitivities (rank estimation threshold, column normalization, non-iterative choice) are not ablated; no comparison to other dictionary selection strategies or iterative refinement.
- Experimental scope is modest: one synthetic suite and a motion segmentation setting; broader datasets and baselines (e.g., Outlier Pursuit; other robust subspace recovery methods) would strengthen the case.
- Language and organization need substantial editing.

Quality
Technically solid overall. The main theorems are carefully stated; the probabilistic statements and scaling match the RPCA/LRR literature. Assumptions (random sparse support, column normalization, λ choice) are standard. The empirical section convincingly shows gains despite the heuristic nature of dictionary learning, and the theory justifies why: low-rank A with UA capturing U0 removes µ2 from the conditions.

Clarity
Below NeurIPS standards. The paper should:
- Upfront state the problem and contributions in a concise bullet list, and highlight how the results differ from RPCA and prior LRR analyses.
- Introduce µ1/µ2/µ3 with intuition and visual examples in the main text; move key intuition for µ2-phenomenon from appendix to the main body.
- Provide a practitioner’s checklist for dictionary construction and diagnostics (e.g., how to detect whether UA likely contains U0; sensitivity to r̂0, normalization).
- Streamline notation and emphasize the implications of theorems before proofs.

Originality
Builds on well-known RPCA/LRR frameworks but contributes new, nontrivial recovery theorems for LRR with general low-rank dictionaries that explicitly avoid µ2, plus an effective, simple dictionary-learning heuristic and empirical validation. The articulation of coherence as a manifestation of clustering structure is useful.

Significance
High practical and theoretical relevance. The results give a principled path to handle coherent, clustered data where RPCA struggles, and demonstrate measurable downstream improvements in subspace clustering. The approach should influence future robust subspace recovery and dictionary learning work.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the role and intuition of coherence (with figures) early; define µ2-phenomenon succinctly with a simple example.
- Reorganize the introduction to make the contributions and implications explicit, including the “immunity to µ2” under low-rank dictionary assumptions.
- Provide ablations: effect of rank thresholding, column normalization, and an iterative variant (RPCA → LRR → update A → LRR).
- Add comparisons to alternative robust subspace methods and to LRR with other dictionary choices (random, PCA basis).
- Offer practical guidance: how to choose λ, ε; computational complexity details; code availability.
- Language and editing pass to improve readability.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, well-motivated theory with clear improvement over RPCA in coherent settings.
- Simple, effective method with significant empirical gains.
- Addresses an important, common failure mode (clustered data) and advances understanding of coherence.

Arguments against acceptance
- Exposition and clarity issues hinder accessibility and reproducibility.
- Limited experimental breadth and analysis of the heuristic’s design choices.
- Assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong; more discussion on how closely the heuristic achieves it is needed.

Post-rebuttal recommendation
Publishable and interesting, but the paper requires substantial rewriting for broader accessibility and added experimental analysis. I would have preferred a major revision; in the current process, I lean toward weak accept contingent on improving clarity and adding the suggested analyses.
