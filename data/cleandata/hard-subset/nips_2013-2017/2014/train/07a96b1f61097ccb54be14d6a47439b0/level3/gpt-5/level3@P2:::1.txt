Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies robust recovery of a low-rank component from grossly corrupted data when the clean data exhibit coherence due to clustering. The authors argue that while RPCA/PCP (Candès et al., JACM 2011) achieves exact recovery under incoherence assumptions, its performance deteriorates as data become more coherent, notably when the number of clusters increases. They formalize this “µ2-phenomenon,” showing that the row-space coherence parameter µ2 tends to grow with the number of underlying subspaces (clusters), tightening RPCA’s recovery conditions and degrading performance.

To address this, the paper analyzes Low-Rank Representation (LRR) (Liu et al., PAMI 2013) with a suitably constructed dictionary A. The main theoretical result shows that if A is itself low-rank and its column space contains the true column space (U0 ⊂ UA), then LRR avoids dependence on µ2 in its exact recovery guarantees. The authors provide both noiseless and noisy recovery theorems with bounds that depend on µ1(A) but not on µ2(L0), thereby “immunizing” recovery against the coherence that grows with the number of clusters. Building on this, they propose a practical two-stage algorithm: (1) run RPCA to obtain an initial estimate of the low-rank component; (2) form a low-rank, column-normalized dictionary from this estimate and solve LRR. This bootstrapped dictionary satisfies the low-rank and span conditions approximately in practice. Experiments on synthetic data and corrupted motion sequences (Hopkins-like trajectories) demonstrate improved recovery and downstream clustering performance relative to RPCA and LRR with A = X.

The work is well connected to prior literature on RPCA/PCP, LRR, Outlier Pursuit, and subspace clustering (e.g., SSC, SIM), and clarifies how coherence assumptions used in RPCA analysis relate to underlying clustering structure.

Strengths
- Clear identification and analysis of a real limitation of RPCA: the µ2-phenomenon induced by clustered data. This connection between coherence and clustering is insightful and practically relevant.
- Solid theory: new recovery guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that avoid µ2. The assumptions (low-rank A, U0 ⊂ UA, column normalization) are explicit and interpretable.
- Practical algorithm: a simple, unsupervised, two-stage procedure that leverages RPCA as a bootstrap to learn a suitable dictionary for LRR.
- Empirically convincing: on synthetic data, a substantial expansion of the “success region” over RPCA; on motion segmentation, meaningful reductions in clustering error after recovery, with further gains over RPCA-based preprocessing.
- Useful perspective: clarifies the role and meaning of coherence and offers a principled criterion for dictionary construction in LRR beyond the common A = X setting.

Weaknesses and questions
- Dependence on U0 ⊂ UA: Theoretical guarantees critically rely on the dictionary spanning the true column space. In the unsupervised setting, A is constructed from an RPCA estimate, but there is no formal analysis of when this bootstrap yields UA that adequately contains U0 (especially when RPCA is inaccurate). A stability analysis quantifying how deviations from U0 ⊂ UA affect recovery would strengthen the contribution.
- Practical sensitivity: The choice λ = 1/√n1 assumes column-normalized A. How robust is performance to imperfect normalization or to other λ choices? An empirical sensitivity study would help.
- Scope of experiments: Real-data evaluation is limited (11 sequences). It would be helpful to include more diverse settings (e.g., background subtraction, facial images with shadows, recommendation/matrix completion with structure) and report standard metrics beyond clustering error (e.g., recovery error versus corruption level).
- Coherence beyond rows: The paper notes that µ1 can also be large (e.g., when rows have clustering), but does not address this case. Discussion of whether analogous constructions can mitigate µ1-related issues would be valuable.
- Algorithmic details: Please specify solvers and stopping criteria for both RPCA and LRR, and report runtime comparisons more broadly. The claim that total time is ~1.2× RPCA is promising; more detailed complexity versus rank(A) and problem size would aid reproducibility.
- Relation to subspace clustering: The presented pipeline uses recovery as preprocessing for SIM/LRRx/SSC. Could the learned dictionary be used directly to improve subspace segmentation within LRR (e.g., affinity construction from Z with learned A)? A comparison to LRR with A = X under the same corruption but with the new dictionary design would clarify the added value for clustering per se, beyond denoising.

Quality
- The theory appears technically sound and addresses a clear gap: avoiding µ2 in recovery conditions by designing A. The empirical results are consistent with the claims. The two-stage algorithm is simple and well justified, though a theoretical analysis of the learned-dictionary setting would further bolster quality.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written. The exposition of the µ2-phenomenon is helpful. Suggestions: provide more intuition for why low-rank, span-containing dictionaries neutralize µ2; clarify the role of column normalization and λ; and detail solvers and parameters. A brief ablation on the post-processing steps (rank truncation, normalization) would improve clarity.

Originality
- While LRR is known, the main novelty lies in: (i) identifying and formalizing how clustering inflates µ2 and harms RPCA; (ii) proving µ2-free guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries; and (iii) proposing a practical unsupervised dictionary-learning strategy. These are new and nontrivial contributions.

Significance
- The results matter in practice because coherent, clustered data are ubiquitous. The approach suggests a principled path to improve robust recovery and, downstream, tasks like motion segmentation. The theoretical insights about coherence and dictionary design should influence subsequent work on robust subspace methods.

Requests for authors
- Please comment on applicability to subspace clustering/segmentation beyond preprocessing: Can your learned dictionary be used within an LRR-based clustering pipeline to directly build better affinities? How does it compare to standard LRRx or SSC when integrated this way?
- Provide an empirical sensitivity analysis for λ and for the rank-threshold used in step 2 (σi > 10−3σ1).
- Discuss how far the theory can be extended to the case where A only approximately spans U0, and to scenarios with large µ1.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, practical failure mode of RPCA with new theory and a usable algorithm.
- Clear conceptual advance (decoupling recovery from µ2 by dictionary design).
- Demonstrated empirical gains in both synthetic and real settings.
- Well aligned with NIPS topics in machine learning theory and robust modeling.

Cons for acceptance
- Guarantees rely on conditions that are only approximately met by the unsupervised procedure; limited theoretical support for the two-stage learned dictionary.
- Real-data experiments are relatively narrow; more breadth and baselines would strengthen the case.
- Limited discussion of µ1-driven coherence and broader applicability.

Recommendation: Accept
Confidence: 4/5
