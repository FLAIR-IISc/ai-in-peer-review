Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies low-rank matrix recovery in the presence of gross corruptions when the clean low-rank matrix exhibits coherent structure (e.g., clustered data drawn from a union of subspaces). It argues that RPCA’s guarantees and empirical performance deteriorate as coherence increases (the “µ2-phenomenon”) and proposes using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a suitably chosen dictionary A to mitigate this effect. The core theoretical result shows that if A is itself low-rank and its column space contains the true column space U0 of the low-rank component (U0 ⊂ UA), then LRR recovers the components without dependence on the second coherence parameter µ2; analogous bounds are given for the noisy case. An unsupervised heuristic is proposed: run RPCA to get an initial estimate of L0, build a column-normalized low-rank dictionary from it, and then solve LRR to refine the recovery. Experiments on synthetic data and corrupted motion sequences (Hopkins) show improvements over RPCA in recovery success rates and downstream clustering error.

This work builds on a substantial literature on robust low-rank recovery, including RPCA (Candes et al.), Outlier Pursuit (Xu et al., NIPS 2010), and LRR (Liu et al., ICML 2010; T-PAMI 2013). The novel contribution here is to analyze LRR with general, low-rank dictionaries for coherent data, make explicit how coherence inflates with the number of clusters, and provide a simple, practical dictionary-construction heuristic.

Strengths
- Clear identification of an important failure mode of RPCA for clustered (coherent) data, tied to µ2.
- New recovery guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that explicitly avoid dependence on µ2 under U0 ⊂ UA; noisy counterpart included.
- A simple, unsupervised pipeline (RPCA → build dictionary → LRR) that improves recovery empirically; computationally reasonable.
- Demonstrated gains on both synthetic data and motion segmentation (subspace clustering) with meaningful relative improvements.
- Helps interpret the role of coherence and offers a principled criterion for dictionary design in LRR.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and accessibility: the exposition of coherence, its intuition, and the µ2-phenomenon is difficult to follow and scattered; key intuition is buried. The main contributions could be highlighted more clearly, and the paper needs editing for language and organization.
- Assumptions for exact recovery (U0 ⊂ UA, A low-rank, column-normalized) are strong; the unsupervised algorithm can only approximately satisfy them and the gap between theory and practice is not quantified.
- Limited empirical analysis of dictionary choices and ablations. It would be helpful to vary the number of clusters to directly illustrate the µ2-phenomenon and to compare with LRR using A = X and other baselines in the recovery task (not only downstream clustering).
- Parameter sensitivity (λ, ε), effect of column normalization, and robustness when the initial RPCA estimate is poor are not thoroughly explored.
- Minor inconsistencies/typos (e.g., “PRCA”) and notational overload impede readability.

Quality
- The theoretical results appear technically sound on a careful read, with reasonable constants and clear statements (noiseless and noisy). The requirement U0 ⊂ UA is necessary for exactness in the stated form.
- The empirical evaluation supports the claims: synthetic phase diagrams show a substantial expansion of the success region; on Hopkins sequences with injected corruption, the proposed method reduces clustering error further than RPCA preprocessing.
- However, more thorough experiments would strengthen the case: explicit dependence on the number of clusters, noise-level sweeps, and ablations for Steps 2–3 (conditioning the dictionary), along with comparisons to LRR with A = X as a recovery method and to other robust factorization methods.

Clarity
- Currently below NIPS standards for a broad audience. Suggestions:
  - Move a concise, intuitive explanation of coherence (µ1, µ2, µ3) and the µ2-phenomenon into the main text with a simple figure; explain why clustering inflates µ2 but not necessarily µ1.
  - Clearly state in the introduction what is new: (i) µ2-avoidance via low-rank dictionary LRR under U0 ⊂ UA, (ii) noisy theorem, (iii) practical unsupervised algorithm, and (iv) empirical evidence.
  - Provide a high-level proof sketch highlighting where µ2 drops out.
  - Streamline notation and fix typos; ensure consistent use of symbols and norms; justify the choice λ = 1/√n1 when columns are normalized.
  - Clarify the computational complexity discussion and when LRR is faster than RPCA in practice.

Originality
- Moderate-to-high. LRR is known, but the combination of (a) identifying µ2 as the culprit for clustered data in RPCA, (b) proving µ2-free guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries, and (c) a practical unsupervised construction, is new and relevant. The theoretical lens on dictionary design for coherent data is a novel contribution.

Significance
- The problem is important in robust subspace recovery and downstream tasks (e.g., motion segmentation). The results provide actionable guidance (learn low-rank dictionaries) and demonstrate tangible improvements. Likely to be used or extended by practitioners and researchers working on robust representation learning and subspace clustering.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the accuracy of U0 ⊂ UA? Can you provide empirical curves as the subspace angle between U0 and UA increases?
- Can you directly vary the number of clusters while keeping rank fixed to visualize the µ2-phenomenon and corresponding recovery success?
- How does the method perform under higher dense noise and different ε settings? Any guidance beyond ε = 1e−6‖X‖F?
- Provide ablations: omit Steps 2–3, try alternative dictionary constructions (e.g., top singular vectors, random projections), and compare to LRR with A = X for recovery.
- Complexity: report wall-clock times and ranks rA vs r0 across datasets.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses a real and common failure mode of RPCA with a principled fix.
- Solid theoretical results (noiseless and noisy) with practical implications.
- Simple algorithm with clear empirical gains on synthetic and real data.
- Advances understanding of coherence and dictionary design in LRR.

Cons:
- Exposition needs substantial improvement; currently hard for non-specialists.
- Strong assumptions for exact recovery; limited quantification of the gap to the heuristic.
- Empirical evaluation could be broader with key ablations and sensitivity analyses.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a meaningful theoretical and practical advance on an important problem, with convincing initial evidence. With improved clarity and expanded experiments (which the rebuttal could outline), it would make a valuable NIPS contribution.

Reviewer confidence: High on technical assessment; medium on breadth of empirical comparisons.
