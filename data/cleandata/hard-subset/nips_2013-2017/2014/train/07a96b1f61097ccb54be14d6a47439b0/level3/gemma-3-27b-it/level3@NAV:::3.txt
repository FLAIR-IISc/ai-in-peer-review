## Review of “Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit”

This paper addresses a critical limitation of Robust PCA (RPCA): its performance degradation with increasing data coherence, a common phenomenon arising from clustering structures. The authors convincingly argue that coherence isn't simply an unavoidable bottleneck, but a manifestation of underlying data structure. They propose leveraging Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned, low-rank dictionary to mitigate the effects of coherence, providing theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. This is a significant contribution to the field of compressed sensing and robust matrix recovery.

The core idea—that a carefully chosen dictionary can decouple LRR from the detrimental effects of coherence—is novel and well-motivated. The theoretical results, specifically Theorem 1 and 2, are strong, demonstrating that a low-rank dictionary, under specific conditions, can lead to exact or near-exact recovery even with coherent data. The connection to previous work on union-of-subspaces and subspace clustering is clearly articulated, particularly the insightful observation regarding Wang & Xu’s work on how higher coherence can *aid* subspace clustering, which this paper cleverly combines with low-rank structure.  

The experimental validation is thorough, demonstrating improvements over RPCA on both synthetic and real-world motion sequence data (Hopkins155). Section 4.2, in particular, is compelling, effectively proposing a new method for subspace clustering with sparse corruption and showing significant gains.  The identified three key contributions – recognizing clustering-induced coherence, exploiting it with a dictionary, and addressing sparse corruption – accurately encapsulate the paper’s impact. 

However, the paper suffers from clarity issues. As noted by the authors, the phrasing around lines 80-82 is ambiguous, and the incomplete phrase at line 88 is distracting. More generally, while the theoretical contributions are solid, the language could be more precise. Claims should be explicitly stated and fully supported with citations. An empirical comparison to a pipeline of noisy sparse subspace clustering (e.g., using ℓ1 minimization) followed by per-subspace PCA, as suggested, would strengthen the results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty & Significance:** Addresses a key limitation of RPCA with a theoretically grounded and empirically validated solution.
*   **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** Provides conditions for robust recovery even with coherent data.
*   **Practical Algorithm:** Offers a readily implementable method with demonstrated performance gains.
*   **Clear Connection to Prior Work:** Positions the work within the broader context of subspace clustering and robust PCA.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Clarity of Writing:** Requires careful editing for improved precision and readability.
*   **Missing Empirical Comparison:**  Would benefit from comparison to a relevant baseline.



**Overall:** Despite the clarity concerns, the substantial contribution to compressed sensing warrants acceptance to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the identified language and experimental shortcomings.
