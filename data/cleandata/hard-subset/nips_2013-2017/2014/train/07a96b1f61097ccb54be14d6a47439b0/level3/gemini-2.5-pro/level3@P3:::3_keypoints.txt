- Problem: Recovery of a low-rank matrix corrupted by gross sparse errors (RPCA) suffers when data are "coherent" (large μ).
- Key insight: High coherence often arises from underlying clustering / union-of-subspaces structure, not just a pathological case.
- Solution: Use Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary A to handle coherent data.
- Theory: If A is low-rank and its column space contains that of the true low-rank component L0, recovery guarantees become independent of the coherence parameter μ2.
- Advantage: This removes the μ2 bottleneck, allowing recovery in regimes where standard RPCA fails and relaxing rank conditions.
- Algorithm: Practical two-stage pipeline — run RPCA for an initial L0 estimate, construct dictionary A from it, then run LRR.
- Strength — originality: Recasts the coherence parameter as a meaningful geometric signal linked to clustering.
- Strength — technical quality: Strong theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) formally support the main claims.
- Strength — empirical validation: Substantial improvements on Hopkins155 (≈40% relative reduction in clustering error) and synthetic experiments showing larger recovery region.
- Strength — unification: Effectively connects low-rank recovery and subspace clustering literatures.
- Weakness — clarity: Some parts of the writing need tightening and more direct phrasing.
- Weakness — proofreading: Minor errors and at least one incomplete phrase should be fixed.
- Weakness — experiments: Lacks comparison to natural baselines such as robust subspace clustering followed by PCA (e.g., SSC+PCA).
- Recommendation: Strong accept — significant, well-supported, and practically validated contribution.
