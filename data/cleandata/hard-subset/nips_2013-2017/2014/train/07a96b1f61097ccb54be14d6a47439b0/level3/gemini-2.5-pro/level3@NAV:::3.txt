This paper addresses a key limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA), whose performance degrades on "coherent" data matrices. The authors provide the crucial insight that high coherence is not just a theoretical bottleneck but often an indicator of an underlying clustering or union-of-subspaces structure. The core contribution is a method that leverages this structure by using a Low-Rank Representation (LRR) model with a learned, low-rank dictionary `A`. Theoretically, the paper proves that under appropriate conditions on the dictionary, this approach can partially remove the dependency on the coherence parameter µ, yielding stronger recovery guarantees than standard RPCA in both noiseless and noisy settings. This work builds upon and synthesizes ideas from RPCA, LRR, and the subspace clustering literature.

**Strengths:**
The paper’s contributions are novel, significant, and technically sound.
1.  **Originality and Insight:** The primary strength is its re-framing of the coherence parameter. Identifying that clustering-induced high coherence can be an exploitable feature, rather than an unavoidable weakness, is a valuable conceptual shift.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis provides strong justification for the proposed method. The proofs show how a low-rank dictionary can make the recovery guarantees in LRR independent of the cluster-related coherence parameter, providing a solid foundation for the approach.
3.  **Significance and Impact:** The proposed algorithm offers a new and effective method for robust subspace clustering in the presence of sparse corruption. The empirical results are compelling, with Section 4.2 demonstrating a significant 40% relative improvement over RPCA-preprocessed methods on the challenging Hopkins155 dataset. This represents a demonstrable advance for an important problem.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper is strong but could be improved with minor revisions.
1.  **Clarity:** The manuscript would benefit from careful proofreading to improve language precision. There are minor issues, such as ambiguous phrasing around lines 80–82 and an incomplete phrase at line 88, which should be corrected.
2.  **Experimental Comparison:** While the experiments validate the theory, the paper would be stronger with an empirical comparison to a direct pipeline baseline: first applying a noisy sparse subspace clustering algorithm (e.g., based on ℓ1 minimization) to identify clusters, followed by applying PCA to the data within each recovered subspace.

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to compressed sensing and robust subspace learning. The connection between data coherence and clustering structure is insightful, and the proposed LRR-based solution is elegant and effective. I strongly recommend acceptance.
