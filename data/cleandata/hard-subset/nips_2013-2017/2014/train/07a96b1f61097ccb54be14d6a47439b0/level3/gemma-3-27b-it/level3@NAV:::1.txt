## Review of “Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit”

This paper addresses the important problem of robust low-rank matrix recovery (RPCA) when data exhibits coherence, a known bottleneck for standard RPCA methods. The authors identify that increasing cluster structure in the underlying low-rank matrix leads to higher coherence, degrading RPCA performance. They cleverly propose leveraging Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary to circumvent this coherence issue, providing theoretical justification and empirical validation. This is a significant contribution, as coherence is a commonly encountered challenge in many real-world applications where cluster structure is prevalent.

The paper builds upon established work in RPCA [Candès et al., 2009] and LRR [Liu et al., 2013], clearly situating itself within the existing literature. The analysis connecting cluster structure to coherence is insightful and well-motivated. The theoretical results demonstrating that a low-rank dictionary can mitigate the coherence problem are a strong point. The experimental results on both synthetic and real motion sequence data convincingly support the proposed method, showing a clear improvement over standard RPCA, particularly when combined with subspace clustering techniques. The connection to Generalized PCA [Vidal et al., 2005] is also a valuable observation.

A strength of the paper lies in its clarity of presentation and the thoroughness of the analysis. The notation is well-defined, and the mathematical arguments are generally easy to follow. The algorithm proposed is relatively straightforward to implement. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, especially regarding dictionary learning. 

One potential direction for future work, and a question for the authors, is whether this approach could be directly applied to subspace clustering or segmentation problems. Instead of using the recovered low-rank matrix as a pre-processing step for subspace clustering, could the LRR framework itself be adapted to directly perform clustering/segmentation? Specifically, could the dictionary learning step be modified to encourage cluster-specific structures?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and practical problem in RPCA.
*   Provides a novel and theoretically sound solution that bypasses a known limitation of existing methods.
*   Empirically demonstrates strong performance on both synthetic and real data.
*   Clearly written and well-motivated.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   None significant. While the computational analysis could be more detailed, the existing results are sufficient to justify acceptance.

**Overall:** This is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery. It tackles an important problem, proposes a clever solution, and provides strong theoretical and experimental evidence to support its claims. I recommend acceptance.
