This paper tackles robust subspace recovery when observations are grossly corrupted and inherently coherent due to cluster structure. Building on RPCA [4] and LRR [13], the authors identify the well-known coherence bottleneck—especially the µ2-phenomenon tied to clustered row spaces—that degrades RPCA as the number of clusters grows. Their main contribution is a principled way to circumvent this bottleneck: show that LRR with a suitably low-rank dictionary A can avoid dependence on µ2, provided U0 ⊂ UA, and give noiseless and noisy recovery guarantees with near-optimal rank bounds. They further propose a practical unsupervised algorithm that uses RPCA to estimate a low-rank dictionary for LRR. Experiments on synthetic data and motion segmentation (with SIM [5], LRRx [14], SSC [6]) demonstrate clear gains over RPCA, highlighting practical relevance since clustered data are ubiquitous.

Quality: The theory is careful and technically sound, with explicit conditions, probability bounds, and a clean insight (remove µ2 via low-rank A). The empirical section is convincing and measures both recovery and downstream clustering error. Some aspects could be elaborated: sensitivity to the RPCA initialization quality; robustness to rank estimation and the 10−3 threshold; what happens when U0 ⊄ UA; and ablations of Steps 2–4 in Algorithm 1.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and well organized. Providing more intuition early for why a low-rank dictionary neutralizes µ2, and a brief schematic of the proof, would aid readers. A short complexity discussion and notation cleanup in Sec. 3 would also help.

Originality: The identification of the coherence–clustering link and the theoretical route to bypass µ2 via dictionary design are novel relative to RPCA and prior LRR work focused on A = X.

Significance: The contribution is important. Many real datasets exhibit unions of subspaces; improving robustness under coherence will likely be adopted by practitioners. The results advance the state of the art both theoretically and empirically.

Questions/suggestions: Could the learned dictionary be used directly for subspace clustering/segmentation (beyond preprocessing), e.g., to form better affinities or as a joint recovery–segmentation model? Please comment on this connection and on adaptive selection of rank(A).

Pros:
- Clear, principled advance on a central RPCA limitation
- Strong theory and practical algorithm
- Solid experiments with meaningful downstream impact

Cons:
- Limited sensitivity and ablation analyses
- Assumption U0 ⊂ UA deserves empirical stress tests

Recommendation: Accept.
