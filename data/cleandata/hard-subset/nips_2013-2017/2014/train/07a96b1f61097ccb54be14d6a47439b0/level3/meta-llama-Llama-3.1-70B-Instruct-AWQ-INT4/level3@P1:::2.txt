This paper presents a novel approach to recover low-rank matrices from grossly corrupted observations, addressing the limitations of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA). The authors propose a framework that combines RPCA with dictionary-based structure-preserving projection, leveraging the idea that using a dictionary to represent structured data can yield stronger recovery guarantees.

The paper's main contributions are threefold. Firstly, it studies the problem of recovering low-rank and coherent matrices from their corrupted versions, providing new insights into the physical regime that raises coherent data. Secondly, it proves some basic theories for resolving the problem and establishes a practical algorithm that outperforms RPCA in experimental studies. Lastly, it provides insights into the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) model, showing that LRR equipped with proper dictionaries can handle coherent data.

The authors' key insight is that using a dictionary to represent structured data yields stronger recovery guarantees than RPCA alone. They provide theorems that state conditions under which reconstruction is possible given a dictionary, addressing the problem of dictionary learning with a heuristic algorithm that computes RPCA and then represents the result with a dictionary.

The empirical results justify the heuristic, and the proofs and theory are technically sound. However, the paper suffers from major clarity issues, with main contributions, claims, and impact not well explained and accessible mainly to specialists. The concept of coherence is hard to understand, and its interpretation is indirect and buried in the appendix. The language and exposition require editing, and the authors should comment on how they will address these issues.

In terms of originality, the paper builds on recent techniques but contributes new theorems and empirical validation. Its significance lies in advancing the state of the art with rigorous results and clear empirical improvements, despite a heuristic dictionary method.

Overall, the paper is publishable and interesting but needs substantial rewriting for broader accessibility. The reviewer would have voted for a major revision.
