This paper, "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit", presents a novel approach to low-rank matrix recovery by combining robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection. The authors address the limitations of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) in handling coherent data, where the low-rank matrix exhibits clustering structures. They propose a new method, Low-Rank Representation (LRR), which uses a dictionary matrix to represent the low-rank component of the data.

The main contribution of this paper is the proof that LRR can avoid the coherence parameters that increase with the underlying cluster number, as long as the dictionary matrix is low-rank. The authors provide two theorems, one for the noiseless case and one for the noisy case, which establish the conditions under which LRR can exactly or nearly recover the low-rank and sparse components of the data. They also propose a heuristic algorithm for learning the dictionary matrix in an unsupervised environment, which is shown to outperform RPCA in experiments.

The paper is technically sound, and the proofs are rigorous and well-presented. The experiments are also well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The authors provide a clear and concise summary of the main notations and concepts, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with the basics of linear algebra and convex optimization.

However, the paper suffers from some clarity issues. The main contributions, claims, and impact of the paper are not well-explained and are mainly accessible to specialists. The concept of coherence is hard to understand, and its interpretation is indirect and buried in the appendix. The language and exposition require editing, and the authors should comment on how they will address these issues.

In terms of originality, the paper builds on recent techniques but contributes new theorems and empirical validation. The significance of the paper is that it advances the state of the art with rigorous results and clear empirical improvements, despite using a heuristic dictionary method.

Overall, this paper is publishable and interesting, but it needs substantial rewriting for broader accessibility. The reviewer would have voted for a major revision.
