

# Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper addresses a significant limitation of Robust PCA (RPCA), which has been a cornerstone of low-rank matrix recovery since its introduction in NIPS 2009 [4]. While RPCA provides elegant theoretical guarantees for recovering low-rank matrices from gross corruptions, the authors demonstrate that its performance degrades substantially when data exhibits coherent structures (e.g., clustering), which are ubiquitous in real-world applications. The authors' key insight is that incorporating a dictionary to represent structured data yields stronger recovery guarantees than RPCA alone, particularly when the dictionary itself is low-rank.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it establishes theoretical conditions under which low-rank dictionary pursuit (via LRR with a low-rank dictionary) can recover coherent data without being affected by coherence parameters that degrade RPCA performance. The authors prove Theorem 1 (noiseless case) and Theorem 2 (noisy case), showing that LRR with a low-rank dictionary A satisfying U0 ⊂ UA can avoid coherence parameters that increase with cluster number. Second, they propose a practical heuristic algorithm (Algorithm 1) that uses RPCA to construct a dictionary for LRR, which empirically outperforms RPCA. Third, the paper provides insights into the physical meaning of coherence, demonstrating it's not merely a theoretical assumption but relates to intrinsic data structures like clustering.

The theoretical contributions are significant, extending RPCA's framework to handle coherent data with rigorous proofs. The authors carefully analyze how coherence parameters (µ1, µ2, µ3) behave with clustering structures, identifying the "µ2-phenomenon" where µ2 increases with cluster number, degrading RPCA performance. Their main result (Theorem 1) shows LRR with low-rank dictionary avoids this issue, with a looser rank condition (O(n2/log n1) vs. RPCA's O(n2/(log n1)2)).

Empirically, the paper demonstrates clear improvements over RPCA on both synthetic matrices (47% wider success area) and real motion sequences (40% relative improvement in clustering error rates). These results validate the theoretical claims in practical settings.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The main contributions, claims, and impact are not well-explained for non-specialists. The concept of coherence is particularly challenging to understand, with its interpretation buried in the appendix rather than clearly presented. The paper would benefit from a more accessible explanation of why coherence matters and how the dictionary-based approach addresses it.

The language and exposition require substantial editing. For instance, the distinction between "coherent data" and "less incoherent data" is confusing, and the relationship between clustering structures and coherence parameters needs clearer explanation. The authors should provide a more intuitive explanation of coherence and its connection to data structures.

The paper builds on recent work (RPCA [4], LRR [13]) but makes a novel contribution by establishing the theoretical connection between dictionary structure and coherence. The significance is clear: it advances the state of the art with rigorous results and empirical validation, despite the heuristic dictionary learning approach. The authors successfully demonstrate that structured data representations can overcome limitations of standard RPCA.

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims, but the exposition needs significant improvement to reach a broader audience. The authors should address the clarity issues by providing a more intuitive explanation of coherence, better connecting theory to practice, and improving the overall flow of the paper.

In summary, this paper makes an important theoretical contribution that addresses a real limitation of RPCA, with compelling empirical validation. While the core ideas are strong and the theory is sound, the presentation needs substantial improvement to make the contributions accessible to the broader NIPS community. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite focusing on clarity and accessibility, particularly regarding the coherence concept.

The paper is publishable with major revision. The authors should address the clarity issues and provide a more accessible explanation of their key contributions and the significance of coherence. The technical content is strong, but the current exposition limits its impact. With improved clarity, this would be a valuable contribution to the field.
