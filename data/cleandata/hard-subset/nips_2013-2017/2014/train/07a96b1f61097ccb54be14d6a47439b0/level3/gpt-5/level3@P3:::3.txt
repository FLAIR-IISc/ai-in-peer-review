Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies recovery of low-rank plus sparse matrices when the low-rank component exhibits clustering (union-of-subspaces) structure that induces high coherence. It shows that the standard incoherence parameter μ, central to RPCA guarantees, is not an unavoidable bottleneck but a surrogate for clustering structure, particularly via μ2 (row-space coherence). The authors propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a suitably chosen (learned) low-rank dictionary A to “absorb” the clustered structure. They prove that if the dictionary’s column space contains the ground-truth column space (U0 ⊂ UA) and A is low rank with column-normalized atoms, then LRR can recover exactly (noiseless case) or stably (noisy case) without dependence on μ2 (and hence μ3), depending only on μ1(A). This partially removes μ-dependence compared to RPCA and yields stronger bounds on the allowable rank/sparsity. They also provide a practical two-stage algorithm: run RPCA, form a low-rank, column-normalized dictionary from the RPCA estimate, and run LRR; this improves recovery in simulations and motion segmentation on Hopkins155.

The work is well-positioned relative to RPCA (Candès et al.), LRR (Liu et al.), SSC (Elhamifar & Vidal), and union-of-subspaces/subspace clustering (Vidal). It clarifies that high coherence is often induced by clustered sampling, which degrades RPCA but can be helpful for clustering—echoing insights in the subspace clustering literature (e.g., Wang & Xu on coherence aiding connectivity). The paper effectively combines low-rank plus sparse recovery with union-of-subspaces modeling via a learned dictionary.

Quality
- Theory: Clear noiseless and noisy guarantees under natural assumptions on A, with λ = 1/√n1 and column-normalized atoms. Importantly, the rank bound depends on μ1(A) and log n1 (not μ2), and the sample complexity improves over RPCA’s log^2 factor. The required condition U0 ⊂ UA is necessary for exactness; the results carefully articulate that A need only be low rank and sufficiently aligned, not identical to U0.
- Algorithm: The RPCA→LRR pipeline is simple and justified: RPCA often gives a decent U0 estimate even under coherence; using that to construct A can reduce effective μ2 sensitivity. Complexity is reasonable; with low-rank A, solving LRR is cheaper than RPCA.
- Experiments: Random synthetic grids show a substantial expansion of the exact recovery region (+47% area). On corrupted Hopkins155 sequences, the pipeline notably reduces clustering error (e.g., SSC error drops from 22.8% raw to 9.5% with RPCA, and to 5.7% with learned-dictionary LRR). These results align with the theory.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with a good exposition of μ1/μ2/μ3 and the “μ2-phenomenon.” However:
- There are ambiguous phrases around lines 80–82; please rephrase to explicitly distinguish which coherence parameter increases with the number of clusters and why (row-space concentration raises μ2; μ1 remains unaffected unless rows cluster).
- Line 88 contains an incomplete phrase; please complete or remove it.
- Throughout, be more explicit about which μ is being controlled where: emphasize that the main theorems remove dependence on μ2 (hence μ3), but still depend on μ1(A). Also restate assumptions (column normalization, U0 ⊂ UA) at theorem statements.
- Improve language precision (e.g., avoid “immune to coherence” without specifying “immune to μ2/μ3”), and ensure all claims are linked to precise theorems or experiments with proper citations.

Originality
- Novel theoretical observation: coherence is not merely a nuisance assumption but reflects clustering; μ2 increases as the number of subspaces grows (μ2-phenomenon).
- Novel use of LRR with a learned, low-rank dictionary to neutralize μ2, coupled with guarantees for exact/noisy recovery that improve on RPCA’s dependence.
- A practical two-stage algorithm that leverages RPCA to learn A is simple and effective.
- Section 4.2 implicitly proposes a new robust subspace clustering pipeline (matrix recovery via learned-dictionary LRR, then clustering), demonstrating significant gains.

Significance
- Advances understanding of when RPCA fails and how to fix it using structure-aware dictionaries; this is important for robust low-rank + sparse recovery and compressed sensing.
- Provides stronger guarantees likely to influence future theory and practice in robust matrix recovery under union-of-subspaces models.
- The empirical improvements on Hopkins155 are substantial; the approach should be of interest across vision, imaging, and text (where row clustering may raise μ1).

Strengths
- Clear identification of clustering-induced high coherence and its specific impact (μ2).
- Theoretically grounded method that partially removes μ dependence via A.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with solid empirical validation.
- Bridges low-rank recovery and subspace clustering in a principled way; results are relevant and timely.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong; while necessary for exactness, discuss robustness when RPCA provides a biased U0 estimate. Provide sensitivity experiments varying the subspace angle between U0 and UA.
- Baselines: Please compare against a pipeline of noisy SSC (ℓ1 affinity) followed by per-subspace PCA (denoising within clusters), and also against LRR with A = X plus post-denoising. This would clarify the added value of learning A beyond standard robust clustering pipelines.
- Parameter sensitivity: Report performance versus λ and dictionary normalization; discuss when column normalization is violated.
- Runtime: Provide wall-clock comparisons and scaling with n, rA to substantiate the O(n^2 rA) vs O(n^3) claim.
- Broaden datasets (e.g., text or recommender data) to explore cases where μ1 is large due to row clustering and discuss how the framework extends.
- Minor proofreading and citation precision as noted above; ensure claims are explicitly tied to μ1/μ2/μ3 and reference related work (SSC, GPCA, LRR variants) consistently.

Connections to prior observations
- The paper complements findings that higher coherence can aid subspace clustering (e.g., Wang & Xu), by showing coherence hurts RPCA but can be exploited via a learned dictionary inside LRR—effectively combining low-rank and union-of-subspace structure.

Pro/con arguments for acceptance
Pros:
- Strong and novel theory with practical impact; clarifies the role of coherence.
- Simple algorithm with significant empirical gains on standard benchmarks.
- Advances robust recovery under realistic clustered sampling; likely to be influential.

Cons:
- Assumption on A and reliance on RPCA for learning A could be fragile in worst cases.
- Some missing baselines and limited dataset diversity.
- Minor clarity/proofreading issues.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a substantial contribution to robust low-rank recovery and compressed sensing, offering new insight into coherence, improved guarantees via LRR with learned dictionaries, and a practical method that advances state of the art, especially for subspace clustering with sparse corruption.
