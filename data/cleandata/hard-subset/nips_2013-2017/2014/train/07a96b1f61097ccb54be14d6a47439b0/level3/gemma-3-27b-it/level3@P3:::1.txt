This paper addresses the important problem of robust low-rank matrix recovery, specifically focusing on the degradation of Robust PCA (RPCA) performance when data exhibits high coherence, a phenomenon often linked to clustering structures. The authors identify a key bottleneck in existing RPCA methods related to data coherence and propose a clever solution leveraging Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully constructed dictionary. The problem is highly relevant, as real-world datasets frequently contain clustering and, therefore, inherent coherence. The theoretical grounding and empirical validation presented make a strong case for the proposed approach.

The paper builds upon the established work of Candès et al. [4] on RPCA, which introduced a convex program for recovering low-rank matrices from corrupted observations. However, the authors correctly point out that RPCA’s assumptions of incoherence are often violated in practice. They link this limitation to clustering structures and the resultant increase in coherence parameters, drawing on concepts explored in earlier work on coherence in matrix recovery [2, 3, 4, 25]. The work also connects to the LRR framework introduced by Liu et al. [13], but importantly extends it by analyzing the impact of the dictionary choice on coherence robustness, a topic less explored in previous LRR literature. The connection to matrix factorization techniques [20, 23] is also aptly noted, providing broader context.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant problem:** The issue of coherence degrading RPCA performance is a well-known limitation, and this paper provides a novel and effective solution.
* **Theoretical soundness:** The authors provide a mathematical proof demonstrating that using a low-rank dictionary in LRR can circumvent the coherence-related bottleneck. Theorem 1 and 2 are strong contributions.
* **Practical algorithm:** Algorithm 1 offers a practical method for constructing a suitable dictionary in an unsupervised setting, building upon the existing RPCA framework.
* **Empirical validation:** The experiments on both randomly generated matrices and motion sequences convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over RPCA. The results on the Hopkins155 dataset are particularly compelling.
* **Clear explanation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the problem, the proposed solution, and the experimental results. The discussion of the µ2-phenomenon and its connection to clustering is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Dictionary construction limitations:** While Algorithm 1 is effective, the reliance on an initial RPCA step introduces a dependency on RPCA’s performance. If RPCA fails significantly, the resulting dictionary may be suboptimal. A discussion of this potential sensitivity would be beneficial.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors mention the computational cost being only marginally higher than RPCA, a more detailed complexity analysis comparing the two algorithms (and potentially others) would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited exploration of dictionary initialization:** The paper focuses on using RPCA output for dictionary initialization. Exploring other dictionary learning strategies might reveal further improvements.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* This paper tackles an important and challenging problem in robust matrix recovery.
* The proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically validated.
* The paper provides a novel insight into the relationship between data coherence, clustering structures, and the performance of RPCA.
* The writing is clear and concise, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.
* The authors adequately address related work and clearly position their contribution within the existing literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on an initial RPCA step could be seen as a limitation, particularly in scenarios where RPCA performs poorly.
* The computational complexity analysis could be more detailed.

**Response to Reviewer Question:**

The reviewer asks whether the method can be applied to subspace clustering/segmentation. The authors correctly note the link to these problems, as demonstrated by their experiments on motion sequences. The method *is* directly applicable to subspace clustering and segmentation. The LRR framework, with its carefully chosen dictionary, provides a robust representation of the data, which can then be used as input to standard subspace clustering algorithms (as demonstrated in their experiments). The improvement in recovery accuracy directly translates to improved clustering performance. Further research could investigate integrating the dictionary learning step directly into the subspace clustering optimization process.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery. The problem is important, the proposed solution is well-motivated and effective, and the paper is clearly written. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance to NIPS.
