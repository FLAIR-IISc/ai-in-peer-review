Summary
This paper addresses the problem of robustly recovering a low-dimensional subspace from observations contaminated by sparse gross errors when the clean data are inherently coherent due to cluster (multi-subspace) structure. The authors first diagnose a standard theoretical bottleneck of RPCA/PCP: its success guarantees depend on incoherence, and the “µ2-phenomenon” shows that µ2 (and µ3) grows with the number of underlying clusters even when the rank is fixed, causing RPCA performance to degrade. To circumvent this, the paper studies Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a deliberately low-rank dictionary A. The main theoretical result shows that, under the condition U0 ⊂ UA and a low-rankness bound on A, LRR’s exact recovery guarantee becomes independent of µ2 (and thus immune to the growth in cluster number), both in noiseless and noisy settings. Building on this, the paper proposes a practical unsupervised algorithm: run RPCA once to get a coarse estimate of L0, denoise/truncate it to make a well-conditioned low-rank approximation, normalize columns to build a dictionary A, and then run LRR to obtain the final recovery. Experiments on synthetic data and corrupted motion sequences confirm the theory and show clear gains over RPCA.

Relation to prior work
The work is grounded in and extends the theoretical foundations of RPCA/PCP and related low-rank+sparse modeling (Candès et al., Wright et al.), Outlier Pursuit (Xu et al.), and LRR (Liu et al.). Prior LRR work often used A = X for subspace segmentation; here, the key novelty is the identification of coherence as a manifestation of clustering and the demonstration that a low-rank dictionary design avoids the µ2 bottleneck. The paper connects this insight to classical matrix factorization ideas and robust GPCA, and provides conditions under which LRR generalizes RPCA without its sensitivity to µ2.

Strengths
- Important problem: coherent (clustered) data are ubiquitous; addressing RPCA’s degradation with increasing coherence is practically meaningful.
- Clear theoretical contribution: exact and stable recovery guarantees for LRR with a low-rank dictionary that avoid µ2; explicit conditions (U0 ⊂ UA, rank bounds, sparsity levels).
- Simple, clever algorithm: uses RPCA to bootstrap a low-rank dictionary for LRR, with modest extra computation; principled and easy to implement.
- Empirical validation: synthetic studies show a substantially larger “success region” (≈47% increase) over RPCA; motion segmentation experiments demonstrate meaningful downstream benefits (e.g., SSC error reduced from 9.50% after RPCA to 5.74% after the proposed method).
- Conceptual insight: clarifies the role of coherence parameters as reflections of extra structure (clustering), not arbitrary proof artifacts.

Weaknesses and questions
- Dependence on U0 ⊂ UA: In unsupervised settings this is achieved via an initial RPCA pass. How sensitive is the method when RPCA’s initial estimate is poor (e.g., very high coherence or higher corruption levels)? A robustness study to initial dictionary quality would help.
- Rank estimation and conditioning: The heuristic threshold (10^−3 σ1) for determining r̂0 and the conditioning steps are reasonable but ad hoc. Sensitivity analyses and guidance would strengthen reproducibility.
- Comparisons: Beyond RPCA, comparisons against alternative robust recovery baselines that may mitigate coherence (e.g., reweighted PCP, truncated/weighted nuclear norm variants, column-sparse/outlier pursuit variants) would provide a fuller empirical picture.
- Noise model: Theory assumes dense Gaussian noise; experiments could explore heavy-tailed noise or structured missingness beyond random Bernoulli to test robustness.
- Scalability: Complexity discussion is helpful; reporting wall-clock times and scaling with n and rA on larger datasets would be informative.

Quality
Technically sound with clear statements of assumptions and probabilistic guarantees for both noiseless and noisy cases. Proof details are deferred but appear consistent with prior analyses in RPCA/LRR. Experiments are well-aligned with claims and include both synthetic and real data.

Clarity
Generally well written and well organized. The discussion of the µ2-phenomenon is especially helpful. Minor suggestions: provide more intuition in the main text for why a low-rank A removes µ2 from the bounds; clarify constants (e.g., ca), and explicitly discuss the effect of dictionary column normalization on λ selection.

Originality
The identification of coherence growth due to clustering and the demonstration that a low-rank dictionary in LRR circumvents the µ2 bottleneck is novel and insightful. The unsupervised two-stage procedure is simple but nontrivial and practically useful.

Significance
High. The work addresses a core limitation of a widely used technique (RPCA) and offers both theoretical and practical advances. Given the prevalence of clustered data, the approach is likely to influence future robust subspace learning and downstream tasks.

On applicability to subspace clustering/segmentation
- The paper already shows that improved recovery improves clustering accuracy when used as preprocessing. Can the learned low-rank dictionary also be used directly within LRR-based clustering (e.g., affinity construction from Z with A learned as proposed), and would the same µ2 immunity carry over to subspace segmentation guarantees?
- Comments on how to integrate the dictionary-learning step with spectral clustering pipelines (SIM, LRRx, SSC) would be valuable, as would a theoretical or empirical investigation of segmentation performance when A is low-rank vs A = X.

Arguments for acceptance
- Tackles an important, well-motivated limitation of RPCA.
- Sound theory that removes a known bottleneck (coherence) under interpretable conditions.
- Practical, efficient algorithm with strong empirical results.
- Clear potential impact on robust subspace learning and segmentation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited breadth of baselines and sensitivity analyses.
- Dependence on a good initial RPCA estimate could be further quantified.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a high-quality paper that advances robust low-rank recovery both theoretically and practically, with clear significance for applications where clustering (and thus coherence) is the norm. I encourage the authors to add discussion/experiments on direct application to subspace clustering/segmentation, robustness to initial dictionary quality, and sensitivity to rank estimation.
