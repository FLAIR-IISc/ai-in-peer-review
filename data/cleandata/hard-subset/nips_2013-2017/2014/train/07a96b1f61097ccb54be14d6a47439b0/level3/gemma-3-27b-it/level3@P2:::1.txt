This paper addresses a critical challenge in robust low-rank matrix recovery: the degradation of performance with increasing data coherence, a common phenomenon when underlying data exhibit clustering structures. While Robust PCA (RPCA) provides a powerful framework, its theoretical guarantees weaken as coherence increases. This work elegantly circumvents this limitation by demonstrating that employing a low-rank dictionary within a Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework can mitigate the effects of coherence. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating the conditions under which LRR avoids the coherence parameter dependence and propose a practical algorithm to learn a suitable dictionary in an unsupervised setting. Extensive experiments on both synthetic and real motion sequence data validate their claims, showing improvements over RPCA.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles a significant problem within the field of robust matrix recovery. It builds upon established techniques like RPCA and LRR, identifying a key limitation of RPCA and offering a principled solution. The connection between data coherence and clustering structure is clearly articulated, highlighting the practical relevance of this work. The theoretical results are rigorously presented, and the proposed algorithm is straightforward and effective. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach, particularly in the context of motion sequence analysis. 

The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical grounding and practical impact. The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of the underlying principles of RPCA and LRR, and their ability to identify and address a key bottleneck in the field. The insights into the relationship between coherence, clustering, and recovery performance are valuable. The empirical validation with motion sequences, linking the method to a real-world application, is also a strong positive aspect. The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow the line of reasoning. Related work is appropriately cited, including foundational papers on RPCA [4], LRR [13], and relevant follow-up research.

However, there are a few minor points that could be addressed. While the authors mention the computational complexity of their algorithm is comparable to RPCA, a more detailed analysis or empirical comparison of runtime would be beneficial. Additionally, the discussion of the iterative refinement of the dictionary (mentioned toward the end of Section 3.3) could be expanded; even a brief negative result explaining why it didn’t yield further improvement would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Significant problem:** Addresses a fundamental limitation of RPCA, a widely used technique.
*   **Novel approach:** Provides a clever and theoretically sound solution using low-rank dictionaries within LRR.
*   **Strong theoretical results:** Offers rigorous analysis of the conditions for avoiding coherence-related issues.
*   **Compelling experimental validation:** Demonstrates improved performance on both synthetic and real data.
*   **Clear and well-written:** The paper is easy to follow and understand.
*   **Practical relevance:** The solution is applicable to real-world problems like motion sequence analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The computational analysis could be more detailed.
*   The discussion on iterative dictionary refinement could be expanded. 

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery. The proposed approach is novel, theoretically well-grounded, and empirically validated. The identified limitation of RPCA and the proposed solution are highly relevant to a broad range of applications. While there are minor points that could be addressed, they do not detract from the overall strength of the paper. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Confidence:** 9/10.
