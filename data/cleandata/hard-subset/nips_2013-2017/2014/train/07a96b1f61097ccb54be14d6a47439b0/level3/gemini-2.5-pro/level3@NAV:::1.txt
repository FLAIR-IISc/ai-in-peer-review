This paper addresses the problem of robustly estimating a low-dimensional subspace from observations contaminated with gross, sparse errors. It builds upon the well-established framework of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA), notably the work of Candès et al. (JACM 2011). The authors identify a key limitation of standard RPCA: its performance guarantees degrade as the coherence of the low-rank data matrix increases. This is a significant practical bottleneck, as high coherence is common in real-world data that exhibits clustering structures. The core contribution is a novel method, based on Low-Rank Representation (LRR), that circumvents this coherence-related issue by using a learned low-rank dictionary. The authors provide a practical two-stage algorithm and support their claims with both theoretical guarantees and strong empirical results.

**Strengths:**
-   **Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and practically relevant limitation of RPCA. By identifying the connection between data coherence and cluster structures, it addresses a scenario that is ubiquitous in modern datasets, thereby advancing the state-of-the-art in a meaningful way.
-   **Quality:** The work is technically sound. The authors provide theoretical analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) that formally proves their method is immune to the coherence parameters that hinder standard RPCA. These theoretical claims are convincingly validated through experiments on synthetic data—where the proposed algorithm demonstrates a 47% wider success area—and on real-world motion segmentation tasks.
-   **Originality:** The idea of using a learned low-rank dictionary within the LRR framework to specifically counteract the effects of high coherence is novel and clever. It provides new insight into the LRR model and the nature of the coherence parameters themselves.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper is strong overall, with no major weaknesses. A minor point for discussion is the dependency of the proposed algorithm on the initial RPCA step. While the method shows clear improvement, a brief discussion on its performance in cases where the initial RPCA estimate is particularly poor could strengthen the paper.

**Comments to Authors:**
The experiments demonstrate a significant improvement in a downstream motion segmentation task, which relies on subspace clustering. This is a compelling result. Could you comment on whether your framework could be more directly applied to the problem of subspace clustering or segmentation itself, perhaps by integrating the dictionary learning and clustering steps, rather than using it as a pre-processing stage?

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents an original, well-motivated, and thoroughly validated solution to an important problem in robust subspace recovery. I strongly recommend acceptance.
