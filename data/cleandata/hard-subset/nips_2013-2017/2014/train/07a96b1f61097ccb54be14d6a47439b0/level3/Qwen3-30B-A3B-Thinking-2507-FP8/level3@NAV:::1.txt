

This paper addresses a critical limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA), which is widely used for low-rank matrix recovery from corrupted data. The authors identify that RPCA performance degrades as data coherence increases—a common scenario when data exhibits clustering structures, which are ubiquitous in real applications. The key insight is that when the dictionary in Low-Rank Representation (LRR) is low-rank and contains the underlying subspace information, LRR becomes immune to coherence parameters that plague RPCA. The authors prove this theoretically and propose a practical algorithm that uses RPCA to construct an appropriate dictionary for LRR, which outperforms RPCA in both synthetic and real motion data experiments.

The paper makes significant contributions to understanding coherence in low-rank recovery problems. It clarifies that coherence parameters aren't merely theoretical assumptions but reflect real data structures (like clustering), which is a valuable insight for the field. The theoretical results are well-supported, showing that LRR with low-rank dictionaries achieves better recovery conditions than RPCA (O(n²/log n₁) vs. O(n²/(log n₁)²)). The empirical results on motion sequences demonstrate clear practical benefits, with a 40% relative improvement in clustering error rates compared to RPCA.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a strong case for its contribution. The main weakness is that while the method improves data recovery, the authors don't explicitly discuss how this translates to subspace clustering/segmentation performance—a natural application of the recovered low-rank data. I'd appreciate the authors' comments on whether their method can be directly applied to subspace clustering tasks, and if so, how it compares to existing approaches.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that addresses an important gap in the RPCA literature with both theoretical rigor and practical relevance. It advances the state of the art in low-rank matrix recovery and should be accepted.
