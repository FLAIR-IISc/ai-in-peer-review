Paper addresses low-rank matrix recovery with noise/outliers and shows RPCA fails when underlying data is clustered/coherent.  
Key insight: a carefully chosen low-rank dictionary in LRR removes dependence on the coherence parameter that grows with number of clusters.  
Theoretical contribution: rigorous proofs that a low-rank dictionary makes LRR immune to the problematic coherence.  
Practical contribution: an unsupervised dictionary-learning algorithm initialized from an RPCA solution.  
Empirical validation: synthetic and real motion-sequence experiments show clear improvement over RPCA and other methods.  
Technically sound paper that builds logically on RPCA and LRR literature and cites relevant prior work.  
Mathematical notation is consistent and proofs are available on arXiv.  
Major clarity issues: presentation is convoluted and difficult to follow even for field experts.  
Coherence concept is not explained intuitively and is largely relegated to the appendix.  
Dictionary-learning algorithm is heuristic and lacks deeper analysis of its properties.  
Paper is highly technical and not accessible to a broader audience; language needs substantial editing.  
Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important RPCA limitation, offers rigorous theory, empirical gains, and a practical algorithm.  
Arguments for rejection: poor clarity reduces impact, heuristic nature raises robustness/generalizability concerns, and limited accessibility.  
Overall recommendation: accept with major revisions contingent on substantial rewriting for clarity, better coherence explanation, and improved exposition.
