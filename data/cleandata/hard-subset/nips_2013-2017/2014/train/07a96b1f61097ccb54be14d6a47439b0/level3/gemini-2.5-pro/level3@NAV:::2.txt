This paper addresses the problem of recovering a low-rank matrix from grossly corrupted observations. Building upon the well-known Robust PCA (RPCA) framework, the authors identify a key weakness: RPCA's performance degrades when the underlying low-rank data is "coherent," a common scenario for clustered data. The central contribution is a method that combines RPCA with a dictionary-based structure-preserving projection, specifically Low-Rank Representation (LRR). The key insight is that by using a low-rank dictionary that properly represents the data's structure, the recovery process can be made immune to the coherence parameter that limits RPCA.

The paper’s primary strengths lie in its technical quality and significance. The theoretical analysis appears sound, providing novel theorems that formalize the conditions under which the dictionary-based approach succeeds. This rigorously advances the state of the art in matrix recovery. While the dictionary learning approach is a heuristic—first estimating the low-rank component with RPCA and then using it to build a dictionary—it is well-justified by strong empirical results on both synthetic and real-world motion data, demonstrating a clear performance improvement over standard RPCA. The work is original in its novel combination of existing techniques and its analysis of a specific failure mode of RPCA.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues that severely hinder its potential impact. The main contributions, claims, and broader significance are not well explained, making the paper difficult to follow for readers who are not already specialists in this specific subfield. A central concept, "coherence," is not explained intuitively in the main text; its interpretation is indirect and buried in the appendix, which is a major drawback for understanding the paper's core motivation. The overall exposition and language require substantial editing for readability.

In summary, this is a valuable contribution with sound theory and promising results. The paper is publishable and interesting, but the manuscript is not ready in its present form. I would recommend acceptance on the condition of a major revision. The authors must substantially rewrite the paper to improve clarity and accessibility, ensuring that the core concepts and contributions are clearly communicated to the broader NIPS audience.
