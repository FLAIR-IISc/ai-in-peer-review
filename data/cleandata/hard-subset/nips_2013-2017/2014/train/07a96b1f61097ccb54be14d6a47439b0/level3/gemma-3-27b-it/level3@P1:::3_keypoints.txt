- Problem: Recovering low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse noise; RPCA performance degrades with increasing data coherence often due to clustering.
- Insight: Coherence reflects underlying clustering structure and can be exploited rather than treated only as a hindrance.
- Proposal: Use Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully designed dictionary matrix A to mitigate coherence effects.
- Theoretical contribution: Show that a low-rank dictionary A makes LRR less sensitive to coherence, providing recovery guarantees in noiseless and noisy settings.
- Empirical validation: Synthetic and real motion-sequence experiments demonstrate LRR with a learned dictionary outperforms RPCA.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on Candès et al. (RPCA) and Liu et al. (LRR), and relates to union-of-subspaces and subspace-clustering literature.
- Strength: Rigorous technical analysis and well-constructed mathematical proofs supporting the approach.
- Strength: Clearly identifies the coherence problem and offers a theoretically grounded solution.
- Strength: Strong experiments, including notable improvements on the Hopkins155 subspace-clustering benchmark.
- Weakness: Practical conditions to guarantee the dictionary A satisfies required properties (e.g., P_UA(U0)=U0) are not fully explored.
- Weakness: Algorithm 1 depends on an RPCA initialization that may be vulnerable to high coherence; robustness and parameter tuning need more analysis.
- Weakness: Some writing is ambiguous in places and would benefit from clearer phrasing and precision.
- Weakness: Empirical comparisons could be broader, e.g., include noisy sparse subspace-clustering pipelines and other alternatives.
- Suggestion: More explicitly connect prior observations (e.g., Wang & Xu) about coherence aiding subspace clustering to the paper’s low-rank/union-of-subspaces framing.
- Recommendation: Despite minor weaknesses, the reviewer recommends acceptance due to novelty, theoretical soundness, empirical validation, and significance.
