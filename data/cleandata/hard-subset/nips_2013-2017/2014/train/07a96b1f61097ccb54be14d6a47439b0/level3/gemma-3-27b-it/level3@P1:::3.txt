This paper addresses the problem of recovering low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse noise, building upon the foundation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA). The authors identify a key limitation of RPCA: its performance degrades with increasing data coherence, a phenomenon often linked to underlying clustering structures within the data. They argue that coherence isn’t merely an unavoidable constraint, but a characteristic reflecting the data’s inherent structure. To mitigate this, they propose leveraging Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully designed dictionary matrix *A*.  The core theoretical contribution lies in proving that if this dictionary *A* is itself low-rank, LRR becomes less susceptible to the coherence parameter, leading to improved recovery guarantees, both in noiseless and noisy settings.  Empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world motion sequence data confirms these theoretical findings, demonstrating superior performance compared to RPCA.

This work connects to a rich body of literature in matrix completion, robust PCA, and subspace clustering. The paper builds directly on the work of Candès et al. [4] on RPCA and leverages the LRR framework introduced by Liu et al. [13]. It also relates to methods for dealing with corrupted data in applications like image processing, computer vision, and radar imaging, as cited throughout the introduction.  The consideration of clustering structures aligns with the broader field of union-of-subspaces modeling, though the authors suggest their approach provides a potentially more general framework as it does not explicitly require a pre-defined number of clusters. 

The strengths of this paper are substantial.  The technical analysis is sound, providing rigorous justification for the proposed approach. The mathematical proofs are presented (with details available in the arXiv version) and appear well-constructed. The paper identifies a non-trivial issue with existing methods – the impact of coherence – and offers a theoretically grounded solution.  The experiments are well-designed, comparing the proposed method to RPCA on both synthetic and real datasets. The results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the LRR approach with a learned dictionary.  Notably, Section 4.2 effectively presents a novel method for subspace clustering in the presence of sparse corruption, achieving significant improvements on the Hopkins155 dataset, which is a well-established benchmark. The three key contributions – identifying clustering-induced high coherence, exploiting it via a dictionary in LRR, and achieving improved sparse corruption handling in subspace clustering – are clearly articulated.  

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. While the theoretical results are promising, the practical conditions for ensuring the dictionary *A* satisfies the required properties (particularly *P<sub>UA</sub>(U<sub>0</sub>) = U<sub>0</sub>*) are not fully explored.  The algorithm proposed for learning the dictionary (Algorithm 1) relies on an initial RPCA step, which the authors acknowledge can be susceptible to high coherence.  While the algorithm demonstrably outperforms RPCA, a more detailed analysis of its robustness to initialization and parameter tuning would be beneficial.  The writing, while generally clear, occasionally suffers from ambiguity.  For example, the phrasing around lines 80-82 is somewhat unclear, and the sentence at line 88 feels incomplete. Increased precision in language and clearer articulation of claims would improve readability. A more thorough comparison to existing subspace clustering methods, perhaps including a pipeline of noisy sparse subspace clustering (e.g., using ℓ1-based methods) followed by per-subspace PCA, could further strengthen the empirical evaluation.  Furthermore, while related work is cited, explicitly connecting the observations about coherence aiding subspace clustering (as noted by Wang & Xu) to the current work’s combination of low-rank and union-of-subspace structure would add context.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper proposes a novel approach to addressing a limitation of RPCA by leveraging LRR with a learned dictionary.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis provides rigorous justification for the proposed method.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the approach on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental issue in low-rank matrix recovery and has potential applications in a variety of domains.
*   **Clear Contributions:** The key contributions are clearly articulated.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** Some sections of the paper could benefit from improved clarity and precision.
*   **Practical Considerations:**  The practical aspects of learning the dictionary *A* and ensuring it satisfies the required conditions could be further explored.
*   **Comparison to alternatives:** The empirical evaluation could be strengthened by a more comprehensive comparison to related methods.




**Overall:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of robust low-rank matrix recovery. The authors have identified a critical limitation of RPCA, provided a theoretically sound solution, and demonstrated its effectiveness through compelling experiments. I recommend acceptance to NIPS.
