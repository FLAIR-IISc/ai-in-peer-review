This paper addresses the problem of recovering a low-rank matrix from observations that are corrupted by sparse, gross errors. This problem is famously addressed by Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA), which decomposes an observed matrix `X` into a low-rank component `L` and a sparse component `S`. The authors identify a key limitation of RPCA: its performance degrades when the low-rank component `L0` is "coherent," a situation that arises frequently when the data points (columns of `L0`) exhibit a clustering structure (i.e., are drawn from a union of subspaces).

The core contribution of this work is to show that this limitation can be overcome by using the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) model. Instead of representing the low-rank component as `L`, LRR models it as `AZ`, where `A` is a dictionary. The key theoretical insight is that if the dictionary `A` is itself low-rank and its column space contains the column space of the true data `L0`, then the recovery guarantees for LRR are immune to the coherence parameters (specifically µ2 and µ3) that grow with the number of clusters and degrade RPCA's performance. Since finding an appropriate dictionary in an unsupervised setting is challenging, the authors propose a practical two-stage algorithm: first, run RPCA to get an initial estimate of `L0`; second, use this estimate to construct a dictionary `A`, and then run LRR with this learned dictionary to obtain a refined, more accurate recovery.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Technical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis to back its central claim. Theorems 1 and 2 establish formal recovery guarantees for LRR under the condition of a suitable low-rank dictionary. These theorems demonstrate, under specific assumptions, that the LRR approach can succeed in regimes where RPCA is known to struggle due to high coherence. The analysis appears technically sound and builds upon the established framework for analyzing convex relaxations for low-rank and sparse recovery.

2.  **Significance and Novelty:** The work addresses an important and practical limitation of a very popular method (RPCA). The insight connecting data clustering, coherence parameters, and recovery failure is valuable and clarifies the conditions under which RPCA may be suboptimal. Proposing LRR as a principled way to mitigate this issue is a novel contribution. This advances the state of the art in robust low-rank matrix recovery by providing both a theoretical understanding and a practical algorithm for handling more structured, coherent data.

3.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively support the paper's claims. The phase transition diagrams on synthetic data show a clear and substantial improvement of the proposed two-stage algorithm over standard RPCA, widening the region of successful recovery. The results on corrupted motion sequences further demonstrate the practical utility of the method, showing that the improved matrix recovery leads to significantly lower error rates in the downstream task of subspace clustering. This justifies the use of the proposed heuristic for dictionary learning.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity and Exposition:** The paper's primary weakness is its clarity. The presentation is very dense and targeted at specialists already deeply familiar with the theoretical literature on RPCA and LRR. The main contributions and their impact are not explained in a way that is easily accessible to a broader NIPS audience. Key concepts that motivate the entire work are not introduced with sufficient intuition.

2.  **Explanation of Coherence:** The concept of "coherence" is central to the paper's motivation, yet it is not well explained. The paper provides the mathematical definitions for µ1, µ2, and µ3 but offers little intuition for what these parameters physically represent. The crucial link between the number of clusters and the increase in µ2 (the "µ2-phenomenon") is asserted, but the reasoning is not presented clearly in the main text, making it difficult for the reader to grasp why RPCA fails and why the proposed method is the right solution. A more intuitive, high-level explanation of coherence would significantly strengthen the paper's narrative and impact.

3.  **Writing and Organization:** The overall language and exposition could be significantly improved. The paper would benefit from a clearer narrative structure that guides the reader from the problem, through the theoretical insight, to the final algorithm. A discussion on how the authors plan to address these clarity issues would be beneficial.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper presents a valuable and technically solid contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery. It identifies a key weakness in RPCA when dealing with structured, coherent data and provides a novel theoretical and algorithmic solution based on LRR. The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate a clear improvement over the state of the art.

However, the paper is hindered by significant clarity issues. The dense technical presentation and lack of intuitive explanations for core concepts make it difficult to appreciate for non-experts.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant and practical limitation of the widely-used RPCA method.
*   Provides novel and seemingly sound theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) that formalize the advantage of using a low-rank dictionary.
*   The proposed algorithm demonstrates strong empirical performance, with clear improvements on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The paper is poorly written and not easily accessible to a general NIPS audience. The main claims and impact are obscured by dense, specialist language.
*   The core concept of coherence, which motivates the entire paper, is not explained intuitively, weakening the paper's narrative.

In summary, the paper contains an interesting and publishable contribution. However, it requires substantial rewriting to improve its clarity and accessibility. Had a "major revision" option been available, I would have selected it. As it stands, the technical novelty and strong empirical results marginally outweigh the severe presentation issues.
