This paper, "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit," addresses a crucial issue in Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA), namely, the robust estimation of a low-dimensional subspace from contaminated observations that are inherently coherent. The authors identify that previous RPCA methods degrade as data coherence increases, which is a standard theoretical bottleneck. To circumvent this coherence-related bottleneck, they propose a clever method that leverages the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework with a carefully designed dictionary matrix.

The paper begins by reviewing the RPCA method, which is a popular approach for recovering low-rank matrices from grossly corrupted observations. However, the authors point out that RPCA is not an ultimate solution to the low-rank matrix recovery problem, as its performance may not be perfect even when the data are strictly low-rank. This is because RPCA ignores clustering structures of the data, which are ubiquitous in applications. The authors argue that the coherence parameters, which increase with the number of clusters, can enlarge the coherence parameters and degrade the performance of RPCA.

To address this issue, the authors propose using the LRR framework, which is a more general approach that can handle coherent data. They show that when the dictionary matrix is low-rank and contains information about the ground truth matrix, LRR can be immune to the coherence parameters that increase with the underlying cluster number. The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the LRR framework and establish a practical algorithm that outperforms RPCA in their experiments.

The paper's contributions are significant, as it is the first to study the problem of recovering low-rank and coherent matrices from their corrupted versions. The authors provide insights into the physical meaning of coherence and show that the coherence parameters are not just assumptions for a proof but rather excellent quantities that relate to the extra structures intrinsically existing in the data. The paper also provides a new perspective on the LRR model and shows that it can be used to handle coherent data.

The experimental results are impressive, with the proposed algorithm outperforming RPCA on both randomly generated matrices and real motion sequences. The authors also demonstrate the effectiveness of their dictionary learning strategy in realistic environments.
