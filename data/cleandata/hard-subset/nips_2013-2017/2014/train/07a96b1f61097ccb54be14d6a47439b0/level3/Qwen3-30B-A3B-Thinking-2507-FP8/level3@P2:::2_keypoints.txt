- Addresses RPCA's limitation in recovering low-rank matrices when data is highly coherent.  
- Observes RPCA performance degrades with high coherence caused by clustering structure.  
- Proposes using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a properly configured low-rank dictionary to mitigate coherence issues.  
- Proves theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1 & 2) that LRR with a low-rank dictionary can be immune to coherence parameters.  
- Identifies the "µ2-phenomenon": more clusters increase coherence parameters (µ2, µ3), harming RPCA.  
- Presents a practical unsupervised dictionary learning algorithm that leverages RPCA output.  
- Demonstrates empirical improvements: up to 47% wider success area on synthetic data and ~40% relative improvement on motion data.  
- Theoretical analysis is technically sound with well-constructed proofs extending existing results.  
- Experimental design is comprehensive, using both controlled synthetic data and Hopkins155 motion sequences.  
- Main clarity issues: the coherence concept is central but poorly explained and buried in technical details/appendix.  
- Writing is dense and targeted to specialists, limiting accessibility for a broader NIPS audience.  
- Originality is moderate: builds on RPCA and LRR but adds novel theoretical insights and a practical dictionary strategy.  
- Significance is good: addresses a practical RPCA limitation and provides substantial theoretical and empirical gains.  
- Arguments for acceptance: meaningful problem, sound theory, clear empirical gains, and a simple effective algorithm.  
- Arguments against acceptance: poor exposition/clarity, insufficient explanation of coherence significance, heuristic dictionary learning limitations, and dense technical writing.  
- Overall recommendation: publishable with major revision—needs clearer explanation of coherence, reorganized presentation, and improved exposition.
