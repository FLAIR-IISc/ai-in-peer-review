### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of recovering a low-rank matrix from grossly corrupted observations, a task for which Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) is a state-of-the-art method. The authors identify a key weakness of RPCA: its performance degrades when the underlying low-rank data is "coherent," a property that arises naturally when data is structured into multiple clusters or subspaces. The core contribution is to show, both theoretically and empirically, that this limitation can be overcome by combining RPCA with a dictionary-based structure-preserving method, namely Low-Rank Representation (LRR). The key insight is that if a suitable low-rank dictionary is used to represent the data, the recovery guarantees of LRR are immune to the coherence parameters that hamper RPCA. The paper provides theorems formalizing the conditions under which this recovery is possible, contingent on the dictionary spanning the column space of the true low-rank data. To address the practical problem of finding such a dictionary in an unsupervised setting, the authors propose a simple but effective heuristic algorithm: first, obtain an initial estimate of the low-rank component using RPCA, and then use this estimate to construct a dictionary for a second recovery stage using LRR. Experimental results on both synthetic and real-world motion segmentation data demonstrate a significant performance improvement over standard RPCA.

### Strengths

*   **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis that advances the understanding of robust low-rank recovery. The main theorems establish novel recovery guarantees for LRR with a low-rank dictionary, explicitly demonstrating how this approach avoids the dependence on the µ2 and µ3 coherence parameters that limit RPCA. This provides a formal justification for why dictionary-based methods can outperform RPCA on structured (e.g., clustered) data. The theoretical work appears technically sound.
*   **Novelty and Originality:** While building on the established frameworks of RPCA and LRR, the paper’s contribution is original. It is the first to formally connect the degradation of RPCA performance to data coherence arising from clustering structures (the "µ2-phenomenon"). The subsequent theoretical resolution via a low-rank dictionary pursuit is a novel and important insight.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide clear and compelling evidence for the paper's claims. The phase transition plots on synthetic data show that the proposed two-stage algorithm has a substantially larger success region than RPCA. Furthermore, the application to corrupted motion sequences demonstrates practical value, showing a ~40% relative improvement in subspace clustering error after recovery compared to using RPCA alone. This confirms that the theoretical insights translate into real-world performance gains.
*   **Importance of the Problem:** The problem of robustly recovering structured data from corrupted observations is of high importance in machine learning and computer vision. By addressing a known but poorly understood limitation of a cornerstone method like RPCA, this work makes a significant contribution to the field.

### Weaknesses

*   **Major Clarity and Exposition Issues:** The paper's primary weakness is its lack of clarity. The writing is dense, and the main contributions are not communicated in a way that is accessible to a general NIPS audience. Key concepts that are central to the paper's motivation, such as the different coherence parameters (µ1, µ2, µ3) and their physical interpretation, are difficult to grasp without deep prior expertise. The critical insight—that clustering increases µ2 and that LRR with a proper dictionary avoids this dependency—is buried in technical details and not explained intuitively. The abstract and introduction could do a much better job of motivating the problem and summarizing the key results in simpler terms.
*   **Heuristic Nature of the Practical Algorithm:** The theoretical results rely on having a dictionary `A` that satisfies certain properties (e.g., `U0 ⊂ UA`). The proposed practical algorithm is a heuristic two-step process that uses the output of RPCA to construct this dictionary. While this approach is justified empirically, there is a disconnect between the conditional nature of the theory and the heuristic algorithm. The paper would be stronger if it discussed the limitations of this approach, for example, under what conditions the initial RPCA estimate might be too poor to yield a useful dictionary.
*   **Accessibility:** Due to the expositional issues, the paper is likely to be understood only by specialists in the subfield of low-rank matrix recovery. This severely limits its potential impact and its ability to disseminate its valuable ideas to the broader machine learning community.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Rewrite for Clarity:** The paper requires substantial rewriting to improve its accessibility. I strongly urge the authors to revise the introduction to clearly and simply state: (1) The problem: RPCA fails on clustered data. (2) Why it fails: Clustered data has high "coherence." (3) The proposed solution: Use a dictionary in an LRR framework. (4) The key results: Theorems proving this works and an algorithm that implements it.
2.  **Explain Coherence Intuitively:** The concept of coherence is critical but poorly explained. An intuitive explanation, perhaps with a simple 2D or 3D geometric example in Section 3.1, would be immensely helpful for readers to understand *why* the µ2 parameter increases with the number of clusters. The current explanation is insufficient for non-experts.
3.  **Address Reviewer Concerns:** The authors should explicitly state in their author response how they will address these clarity and exposition issues in the final version. A commitment to a significant rewrite would greatly strengthen the case for acceptance.
4.  **Discuss Algorithm Limitations:** A brief discussion about the failure modes of Algorithm 1 would be beneficial. For example, if the corruption is so severe that the initial RPCA step produces a very poor estimate `L_hat`, will the subsequent LRR step still provide an improvement, or could it potentially worsen the result?

### Conclusion

This paper presents an important and technically sound contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery. It identifies a key limitation of RPCA on coherent, clustered data and provides a novel theoretical and practical solution by integrating a dictionary-based approach. The empirical results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method. However, the paper is severely hampered by poor clarity and exposition, making its significant contributions inaccessible to a broad audience. While the work is interesting and publishable on its technical merits, it needs a major revision to improve its presentation. If a "major revision" option were available, that would be my recommendation. Given the high quality of the core ideas, I lean towards acceptance, but with the strong condition that the authors undertake a thorough rewrite to address the clarity issues for the camera-ready version.
