This paper studies robust low-rank recovery when the low-rank component is coherent, a regime where standard RPCA degrades. The authors identify a concrete source of coherence—clustering of columns on a union of subspaces—and formalize how the second coherence parameter µ2 (and thus µ3) increases with the number of clusters (“µ2-phenomenon”). They propose to use Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a suitably constructed low-rank dictionary A to avoid dependence on µ2. The main theoretical result shows that if A is low-rank, column-normalized, and spans the true column space (U0 ⊂ UA), then exact recovery via LRR holds with high probability under sparse corruption, with a rank bound scaling as O(n2/log n1), thereby removing the µ2 dependence that limits RPCA. A noisy variant establishes stability. Building on this, they propose a simple unsupervised algorithm: run RPCA once, rank-truncate the estimate to form a well-conditioned low-rank dictionary A, then solve LRR to obtain the final estimate. Experiments on synthetic coherent data and corrupted motion trajectories demonstrate clear gains over RPCA and over LRR with A = X (LRRx), and lead to lower downstream subspace clustering error.

Relation to prior work: RPCA (Candès et al., JACM) provides exact recovery guarantees under incoherence (captured by µ1, µ2, µ3). LRR (Liu et al., PAMI; ICML) is widely used for subspace segmentation, often with A = X, but its behavior with general dictionaries has been less understood. Outlier Pursuit and other robust subspace methods (Xu et al., NIPS; IT) address column-sparse corruptions but not general coherence. The present work is novel in tying the degradation of RPCA to clustering-induced µ2, proving that a low-rank dictionary can circumvent this dependence, and delivering a practical unsupervised procedure to construct such a dictionary.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The conditions are clearly stated (column-wise normalization, U0 ⊂ UA, low rank of A, random sparse support, λ = 1/√n1). The noiseless and noisy theorems mirror RPCA-style guarantees but replace µ(L0) with µ1(A), effectively trading incoherence of data for incoherence/conditioning of the dictionary. The improvement from O(n2/(log n1)2) to O(n2/log n1) in rank scaling is plausible given the avoided µ2 factor. The constructive algorithm aligns with the assumptions: an initial RPCA estimate provides an approximate U0 to embed into UA.
- Empirical support: Synthetic tests sweep rank and sparsity and show a substantial expansion (≈47%) of the success region relative to RPCA. On motion segmentation, the recovery step reduces clustering errors across SIM, SSC, and LRRx, with additional gains from the proposed dictionary over RPCA preprocessing (e.g., 9.50% to 5.74% for SSC).
- Limitations: The key assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong; when RPCA’s initial estimate is poor (high corruption, very high coherence), the learned A may not satisfy this and LRR may not help. The corruption model is entrywise sparse; column-sparse/outlier models are not addressed. The choice λ = 1/√n1 is justified by normalization but sensitivity analyses are limited. Rank estimation and the 10−3σ1 threshold could be brittle; guidance or robustness checks would help.

Clarity:
- The exposition is generally clear and well organized. The definitions and roles of µ1, µ2, µ3 and the “µ2-phenomenon” are helpful.
- Suggestions: Provide more intuition on why low-rank A eliminates µ2 from the analysis; add an ablation showing failure cases when A is not low-rank or U0 ⊄ UA; report runtime and conditioning of A; discuss sensitivity to the rank threshold and to λ, ε; clarify handling of missing data versus arbitrary sparse corruptions (replacing missing with ±1 may not reflect typical noise).

Originality:
- New conceptual link between clustering-induced coherence and RPCA failure, and a new theoretical regime for LRR with general low-rank dictionaries that avoids µ2.
- The unsupervised “RPCA-then-LRR” pipeline with guarantees (conditional on properties of A) is a practical and novel contribution beyond prior LRRx.

Significance:
- Coherent data are ubiquitous in vision, recommendation, and text. The proposed principle—use a low-rank dictionary that covers the underlying subspace—has broad implications and can inform future designs of robust subspace methods.
- The gains in both synthetic recovery and real downstream clustering are meaningful; the theory advances understanding of coherence in robust recovery.

Questions and requests to the authors:
1. Subspace clustering/segmentation: Beyond preprocessing, can your theory or method be integrated into clustering directly (e.g., using AZ as an affinity or as a denoised self-representation)? How does your approach compare to LRRx and SSC when used end-to-end on coherent data?
2. Robustness of dictionary learning: How sensitive is performance to RPCA errors when coherence and corruption are high? Can you characterize conditions under which the initial RPCA step is “good enough” for U0 ⊂ UA to hold approximately?
3. Rank selection and conditioning: How does the 10−3σ1 threshold affect results? Would information criteria or cross-validation help? Do you regularize A to avoid near-duplicates/ill-conditioning?
4. Corruption models: Do your results extend to column-sparse outliers (Outlier Pursuit setting) or structured/masked corruption (true missing at random)? How would the theory and algorithm adapt?
5. Parameter choices: Is λ = 1/√n1 essential beyond normalization, and how sensitive are results to ε in (3.9)?
6. Row coherence: You note µ1 can be large when rows have clustering (e.g., text). Can the low-rank dictionary idea be adapted to that regime (e.g., via transposed formulation)?

Pros:
- Clear identification and analysis of a practically important failure mode of RPCA.
- Solid theory showing LRR with low-rank dictionary avoids µ2; noisy stability included.
- Simple, practical unsupervised algorithm with strong empirical gains.
- Broad relevance to robust GPCA and subspace methods.

Cons:
- Reliance on a good initial RPCA estimate; limited analysis of failure modes and sensitivity.
- Assumption U0 ⊂ UA and random sparse corruption may limit generality.
- Experimental scope could be expanded (more datasets, parameter sweeps, runtime).

Recommendation: Accept. The paper addresses an important gap in robust low-rank recovery, combines novel theory with a practical algorithm, and demonstrates clear benefits on coherent data.
