

# Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper investigates a critical limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) - its performance degradation with coherent data arising from clustering structures. The authors propose a novel solution using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary to address this issue, making a significant contribution to the field of robust low-rank matrix recovery.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it establishes a fundamental connection between clustering structures in data and the coherence parameters (μ) that limit RPCA's performance. The authors demonstrate that μ₂ (and μ₃) increase with the number of clusters, which directly degrades RPCA's performance. This insight reinterprets coherence parameters as reflecting intrinsic clustering structures rather than being mere theoretical constraints. Second, the paper proves that LRR with a low-rank dictionary can avoid dependence on μ₂, providing stronger theoretical guarantees for exact and noisy low-rank + sparse recovery. Third, the authors develop a practical unsupervised algorithm that learns an appropriate dictionary from RPCA outputs, outperforming RPCA in both synthetic and real-world experiments.

The paper connects well to prior work, particularly the LRR framework proposed by Liu et al. [13] and the subspace clustering literature. It builds on the observation from Wang & Xu that higher coherence can aid subspace clustering, but extends this by combining low-rank and union-of-subspace structures in a unified framework. The theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) are significant as they provide conditions under which LRR can overcome the coherence limitations of RPCA, with the key insight being that the dictionary itself should be low-rank and contain the column space of the true low-rank matrix.

The experimental validation is thorough, with synthetic data experiments demonstrating the theoretical claims and real-world motion segmentation experiments on the Hopkins155 database showing significant improvements (40% relative error reduction over RPCA). Section 4.2 effectively demonstrates the application to subspace clustering with sparse corruption, showing substantial improvements on Hopkins155.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and comprehensive experimental validation. The proofs appear correct, and the experiments properly test the theoretical claims. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the requirement for the dictionary to contain the true column space, and discuss how this can be approximately achieved in unsupervised settings.

Regarding clarity, the paper is generally well-written but contains several issues that need addressing. There are ambiguous phrases around lines 80-82 (regarding the relationship between the dictionary and the column space) and an incomplete phrase at line 88. The paper would benefit from more precise language, particularly in stating the exact conditions under which the theoretical guarantees hold. The connection to Wang & Xu's work could be made more explicit earlier in the paper.

The originality is strong. While LRR has been previously studied with A = X, the paper's insight about using low-rank dictionaries to handle coherent data represents a novel approach. The connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters is a fresh perspective that distinguishes this work from previous RPCA and subspace clustering research.

The significance of this work is substantial. It addresses a fundamental limitation of RPCA that has been largely overlooked in the literature. By showing that coherence parameters reflect clustering structures and can be mitigated via dictionary learning, the paper provides a principled approach to handling a common data structure in real-world applications. The practical algorithm developed in Section 3.3 has immediate applicability to numerous domains, including computer vision (as demonstrated on Hopkins155), and the authors rightly note potential extensions to text data.

The paper's main weakness is the lack of empirical comparison to a pipeline of noisy sparse subspace clustering (ℓ₁) followed by per-subspace PCA, as suggested in the key points. This would strengthen the claim that their method provides a more effective solution than existing approaches.

Overall, this is a high-quality, significant contribution that advances the state of the art in robust low-rank matrix recovery. The theoretical insights are novel and well-supported, the experimental validation is comprehensive, and the practical implications are clear. The minor clarity issues can be addressed in revision, but they do not undermine the paper's substantial contribution.

I recommend acceptance to NIPS. This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field by resolving a fundamental limitation of RPCA and providing a principled approach to handling coherent data with clustering structures. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and has clear practical implications across multiple domains.
