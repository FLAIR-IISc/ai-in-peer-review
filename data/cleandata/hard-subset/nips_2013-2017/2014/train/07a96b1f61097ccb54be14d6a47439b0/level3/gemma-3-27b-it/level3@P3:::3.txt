This paper proposes a novel approach to robust PCA (RPCA) by leveraging Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary, addressing the challenges posed by coherent data – a common scenario in applications exhibiting clustering structures. The key insight is that the incoherence parameter, often considered a limitation in RPCA, is actually reflective of these underlying data structures. The authors prove that a low-rank dictionary in LRR can mitigate the dependence on this coherence parameter, leading to stronger recovery guarantees. This work is significant, well-motivated, and builds upon the existing literature on RPCA, subspace clustering, and low-rank matrix completion.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The paper begins by outlining the limitations of RPCA when dealing with data containing inherent clustering, which increases data coherence and degrades RPCA performance. It proposes using LRR with a carefully chosen dictionary to overcome this issue. The core theoretical result demonstrates that if the dictionary itself is low-rank, LRR becomes less susceptible to the detrimental effects of high coherence. The authors also present a practical algorithm for learning the dictionary in an unsupervised manner, utilizing an initial RPCA estimate. Experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world motion sequence data supports the theoretical findings, demonstrating improvements over standard RPCA.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The work offers a fresh perspective on the coherence parameter in RPCA, reframing it as a characteristic of the data rather than an unavoidable bottleneck. The proposed approach of utilizing a learned low-rank dictionary within LRR is a significant contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, proving that a low-rank dictionary can partially decouple LRR from the coherence parameter, yielding stronger recovery guarantees. This is a valuable addition to the theoretical understanding of LRR and its applicability.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed algorithm for dictionary learning is practical and relatively simple, building upon existing RPCA techniques. This makes the approach readily implementable and potentially useful for a wide range of applications.
* **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real data convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in the context of motion sequence analysis. The improvements over RPCA are substantial.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively situates its work within the broader context of RPCA, subspace clustering, and low-rank matrix factorization, clearly highlighting its relationship to prior art. The connection to Wang & Xu’s work on coherence aiding subspace clustering is a particularly insightful observation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Precision:** While generally well-written, the paper suffers from occasional instances of ambiguity. The phrasing around lines 80-82 requires clarification, and the incomplete phrase at line 88 should be addressed. A more rigorous pass for language precision is recommended throughout.
* **Insufficient Comparison:** The paper would benefit from an empirical comparison to a pipeline of noisy sparse subspace clustering (e.g., using ℓ1-minimization) followed by per-subspace PCA. This would provide a stronger baseline for evaluating the performance of the proposed method.
* **Limited Discussion of Dictionary Learning:** While the algorithm for dictionary learning is presented, a more detailed discussion of the impact of different dictionary learning strategies (beyond the RPCA-based approach) would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a substantial contribution to the field of compressed sensing and robust matrix recovery, addressing a critical limitation of RPCA.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the interplay between data coherence and recovery performance.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The work is well-motivated, clearly written (despite minor clarity issues), and appropriately positioned within the existing literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The minor clarity issues and occasional ambiguity could detract from the overall impact of the paper.
* The lack of comparison to a standard subspace clustering pipeline (ℓ1-SSC + PCA) leaves a slight gap in the empirical evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The novel approach to addressing coherence in RPCA, coupled with the theoretical guarantees and compelling experimental results, make it a valuable contribution to the field. Despite the minor weaknesses regarding clarity and completeness of comparison, the strengths of the paper outweigh its shortcomings. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance to NIPS. Addressing the minor clarity issues and adding a comparison to a standard subspace clustering pipeline would further strengthen the paper. Section 4.2 is particularly noteworthy, effectively proposing a new method for subspace clustering with sparse corruption and demonstrating significant improvements on the Hopkins155 dataset. The three key contributions highlighted by the authors – identifying clustering-induced high coherence, exploiting it via a dictionary in LRR, and partially solving sparse corruption in subspace clustering – are all substantial and well-supported.
