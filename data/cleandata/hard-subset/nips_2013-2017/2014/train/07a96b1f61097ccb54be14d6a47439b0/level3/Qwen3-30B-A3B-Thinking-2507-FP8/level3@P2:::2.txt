

# Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper addresses a significant limitation of Robust PCA (RPCA), which is widely used for recovering low-rank matrices from grossly corrupted observations. The authors observe that RPCA's performance degrades when data exhibits high coherence, which often occurs when data has clustering structures (as demonstrated in Figure 1). They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a properly configured dictionary to address this issue, demonstrating theoretically and empirically that LRR can be made immune to coherence parameters when the dictionary itself is low-rank. This builds upon the RPCA framework [4] and the LRR model [13], extending them with new theoretical insights and practical algorithms.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it identifies the "µ2-phenomenon" where increasing cluster number leads to higher coherence parameters (specifically µ2 and µ3), which degrades RPCA performance. Second, it proves that LRR with a low-rank dictionary can avoid this coherence issue, providing theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1 and 2) that the recovery is exact or near-exact under certain conditions. Third, it proposes a practical unsupervised algorithm for dictionary learning that builds on RPCA's output, which shows clear empirical improvements over RPCA on both synthetic and real motion data.

The quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with well-constructed proofs that extend existing results. The experimental design is comprehensive, including both synthetic data with controlled parameters and real-world motion sequences from the Hopkins155 database. The authors carefully consider both the strengths (e.g., the theoretical guarantees) and weaknesses (e.g., the limitations of the heuristic dictionary learning approach) of their method.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The main contributions, claims, and impact are not well-explained for a broader audience. The concept of coherence is central to the paper but is hard to understand and its interpretation is buried in technical details and the appendix. The writing is dense and accessible mainly to specialists in matrix recovery, making it difficult for general NIPS readers to grasp the significance of the work. The authors should significantly reorganize and clarify the presentation to make their contributions more accessible.

The originality of the paper is moderate. While it builds on established techniques (RPCA and LRR), it provides novel theoretical insights about coherence and its relationship to data structures, as well as a practical dictionary learning strategy. The paper is sufficiently distinct from previous NIPS papers on matrix recovery, particularly in its focus on coherent data and the specific conditions under which LRR outperforms RPCA.

The significance of the work is good. The paper addresses a practical limitation of a widely used method (RPCA) and provides both theoretical and empirical evidence that their approach improves upon it. The improvements are substantial (up to 47% wider success area on synthetic data and 40% relative improvement on motion data), demonstrating clear value for real-world applications. The insights about coherence and its relationship to data structures contribute to a deeper understanding of matrix recovery problems.

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper addresses a meaningful limitation of a widely used method (RPCA)
- The theoretical contributions are sound and extend the field's understanding of coherence
- The empirical results clearly demonstrate practical improvements over RPCA
- The proposed algorithm is simple yet effective for real-world applications

Arguments against acceptance:
- The paper's clarity and exposition need substantial improvement to be accessible to a broader audience
- The significance of the coherence concept is not well-explained, making it difficult for readers to appreciate the contribution
- The dictionary learning approach is heuristic, and the paper does not fully address limitations of this approach
- The writing is dense and technical, limiting the paper's impact

Overall, this is an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of matrix recovery. The theoretical insights and empirical results are strong, but the paper's current presentation significantly limits its accessibility and impact. The authors should substantially revise the paper to clarify their main contributions, explain the coherence concept more clearly, and improve the overall exposition. With these revisions, this paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS.

The paper is publishable with major revision. The core ideas and results are sound, but the presentation needs significant improvement to make the contribution accessible to the broader NIPS audience. The authors should address the clarity issues highlighted above, particularly by providing a clearer explanation of coherence and its relationship to data structures, and by reorganizing the paper to better highlight their main contributions.
