This paper addresses the problem of recovering a low-rank matrix from observations contaminated with gross, sparse errors. This problem, often tackled by Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA), is central to many applications in machine learning and computer vision. The authors build upon the well-established RPCA framework [4] and identify a key limitation: its performance degrades as the "coherence" of the low-rank matrix increases. The paper provides the important insight that such high coherence is not an esoteric corner case, but rather a natural consequence of structures commonly found in real-world data, such as the presence of multiple clusters or subspaces. This is termed the "µ2-phenomenon."

The main contribution is a method to circumvent this coherence-related bottleneck. The authors propose using the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) model [13], where the low-rank matrix L is represented as a product of a dictionary A and a low-rank coefficient matrix Z (i.e., L = AZ). Their key theoretical result (Theorem 1) shows that if the dictionary A is itself low-rank and its column space spans that of the true low-rank data L0, then the LRR objective can recover L0 exactly without its success depending on the problematic coherence parameters (µ2, µ3). To make this approach practical in unsupervised settings, the paper presents a simple and effective two-stage algorithm: first, run standard RPCA to get an initial estimate of the low-rank component, and second, use this estimate to construct a low-rank dictionary for a subsequent LRR-based recovery step. The effectiveness of this "Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit" is demonstrated empirically on both synthetic data and a challenging motion segmentation task, showing significant improvements over standard RPCA.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-known and important bottleneck in the theory and practice of RPCA—the incoherence assumption. The authors' connection of high coherence to the ubiquitous clustering structure in data makes the problem highly relevant. The proposed solution is novel and clever; rather than modifying the RPCA objective directly, it leverages the LRR framework in a new way, providing a new perspective on why dictionary-based and matrix factorization methods can be powerful.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. It provides a clear theoretical analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) that formally proves the central claim: a low-rank dictionary makes the LRR recovery immune to the µ2 coherence parameter. The proposed algorithm is a simple, intuitive, and practical realization of the theoretical idea. The experiments are well-designed and convincingly support the paper's claims, showing a 47% larger success region on synthetic data and a 40% relative improvement in clustering error on real motion data.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It does an excellent job of motivating the problem by first explaining the limitations of RPCA and the practical origins of coherent data (the "µ2-phenomenon"). The theoretical results and the proposed algorithm are presented clearly. The figures and tables effectively illustrate the main points.

**Weaknesses / Questions for Authors:**

The paper is of high quality and has no major weaknesses. The following is a point for further discussion that could strengthen the paper's impact:

The experiments in Section 4.2 convincingly show that the proposed method serves as an excellent pre-processing step for subspace clustering algorithms on corrupted data. The conclusion also rightly connects the overall problem to robust Generalized PCA (GPCA). Could the authors comment further on the direct applicability or integration of their framework into subspace clustering/segmentation itself? While using it for pre-processing is clearly effective, it would be interesting to know if the dictionary learning and representation steps could be more deeply integrated into a unified model for robustly learning both the subspaces and the segmentations simultaneously.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the important field of low-rank matrix recovery. It identifies a key practical limitation of the widely-used RPCA method and proposes an elegant, theoretically-grounded, and empirically successful solution. The work is novel, technically solid, and clearly presented. It advances the state of the art and the ideas presented are likely to be built upon by other researchers.

In summary:
*   **Quality:** High. The claims are well-supported by both theory and experiments.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Originality:** High. The paper offers a novel perspective and solution to a known limitation in a popular model.
*   **Significance:** High. The problem is important, and the proposed method offers a demonstrable improvement over the state of the art, with relevance to applications like motion segmentation.

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper.
