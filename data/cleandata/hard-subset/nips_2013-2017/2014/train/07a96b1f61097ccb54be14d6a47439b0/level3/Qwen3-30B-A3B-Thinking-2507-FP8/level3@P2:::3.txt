

# Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper addresses a significant limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) when dealing with coherent data, where the low-rank component exhibits clustering structures. The authors demonstrate that the coherence parameters, often considered as unavoidable theoretical bottlenecks in RPCA, actually reflect underlying clustering structures in the data. They show that by using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with an appropriately constructed low-rank dictionary, one can mitigate the negative effects of high coherence, which increases with the number of clusters. This work builds upon the RPCA framework established in Candès et al. (2011) and extends the LRR framework introduced in Liu et al. (2010), while providing novel theoretical insights and a practical algorithm for unsupervised dictionary learning.

**Quality** - The paper presents technically sound theoretical contributions, establishing theorems (1 and 2) that prove LRR with a low-rank dictionary can avoid dependence on the coherence parameter μ₂. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported by experiments. The authors carefully address both strengths and weaknesses, acknowledging that their method requires a low-rank dictionary that contains information about the ground truth matrix, which is not trivial to obtain in unsupervised settings. The experimental validation on both synthetic data and the Hopkins155 motion sequences is comprehensive and convincingly demonstrates the method's superiority over RPCA, particularly in coherent data regimes. The paper also properly references related work, including the connection to Wang & Xu's findings about higher coherence aiding subspace clustering.

**Clarity** - The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results to experimental validation. However, there are some clarity issues that could be improved. The phrasing around lines 80-82 is somewhat ambiguous regarding the relationship between clustering and coherence, and there's an incomplete phrase at line 88. The authors could more explicitly state their assumptions about the clustering structure and its relationship to the dictionary construction. Additionally, the connection between the theoretical results and the practical algorithm could be made more explicit.

**Originality** - This work makes several significant original contributions. First, it identifies and characterizes the relationship between clustering structures and coherence parameters, revealing that coherence is not merely a theoretical assumption but reflects real data structures. Second, it establishes a novel theoretical framework showing how LRR with a low-rank dictionary can overcome coherence limitations. Third, it provides a practical unsupervised algorithm for dictionary learning that significantly outperforms RPCA. While LRR has been previously studied, the authors' specific focus on coherence and the dictionary construction strategy represents a meaningful advancement beyond prior work.

**Significance** - The significance of this work is substantial. The authors address a fundamental limitation in RPCA that affects its performance on real-world data where clustering structures are ubiquitous (e.g., motion sequences, image data, document collections). The paper demonstrates clear improvements on the Hopkins155 benchmark, reducing clustering error rates by 40% relative to RPCA. This work has potential applications in various domains beyond motion segmentation, including text analysis and other structured data. The paper effectively shows how to combine low-rank matrix recovery with subspace clustering structures, advancing the state of the art in robust matrix recovery.

**Strengths**:
- Novel theoretical insight connecting coherence parameters to clustering structures
- Rigorous proofs demonstrating LRR's ability to avoid coherence dependence
- Practical unsupervised algorithm with clear experimental validation
- Significant improvements over RPCA on real-world motion data
- Clear connection to related subspace clustering work
- Addressing a meaningful limitation of existing RPCA methods

**Weaknesses**:
- Some ambiguous phrasing that could be clarified (lines 80-82)
- Incomplete sentence at line 88
- More explicit discussion of the relationship between clustering structure and dictionary construction
- Suggestion for additional experimental comparison (e.g., pipeline of sparse subspace clustering followed by PCA)

**Recommendation**: I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work represents a substantial contribution to the field, addressing a meaningful limitation of RPCA with both theoretical and practical advances. The authors provide a novel perspective on coherence parameters, establish rigorous theoretical guarantees, and demonstrate significant empirical improvements. While there are minor clarity issues that could be addressed in the final version, these do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance. This work advances the state of the art in robust low-rank matrix recovery and will likely be of interest to the broader NIPS community working on structured data analysis, subspace learning, and matrix recovery.
