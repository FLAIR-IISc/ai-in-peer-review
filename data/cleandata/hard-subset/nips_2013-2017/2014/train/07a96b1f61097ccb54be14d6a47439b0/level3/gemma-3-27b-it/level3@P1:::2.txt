This paper addresses the problem of recovering low-rank matrices corrupted by noise and outliers, building on the foundations of Robust PCA (RPCA). The authors identify a limitation of RPCA – its performance degrades when the underlying low-rank data exhibits clustering structures, leading to increased coherence. Their central insight is that employing a well-configured dictionary within a Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework can mitigate this issue, particularly when the dictionary itself is low-rank. The paper provides theoretical results demonstrating conditions for successful recovery and proposes a practical algorithm that leverages RPCA to learn a suitable dictionary for LRR. Experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world motion sequence data supports their claims.

The work builds upon a significant body of research in low-rank matrix recovery, including RPCA [Candès et al., 2009], matrix completion [Candès & Recht, 2009], and low-rank representation [Liu et al., 2013].  It appropriately cites relevant work and positions itself within this existing literature. The theoretical contributions, specifically Theorems 1 and 2, are noteworthy and appear technically sound upon cursory inspection. The proofs, while not detailed within the paper, are referenced to a preprint, allowing for thorough examination. The experimental setup and results are well-presented and demonstrate a clear improvement over RPCA in certain scenarios. 

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a mathematically rigorous analysis of the impact of data coherence on RPCA and proposes a theoretically grounded solution. The theorems provide concrete conditions for recovery.
* **Originality:** While building upon established techniques, the paper offers a novel combination of RPCA and LRR, coupled with a specific focus on dictionary learning to address the coherence problem.  The analysis of coherence in relation to clustering structures is a valuable contribution.
* **Significance:** The results demonstrate a tangible improvement in recovery performance, especially in scenarios with clustered data. This has potential implications for applications in areas like motion segmentation, image processing, and dimensionality reduction.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real data provide strong evidence supporting the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity:** This is the most significant weakness. The paper is dense and difficult to follow, particularly for readers not already deeply familiar with the field. The core ideas, especially the concept of coherence and its relation to clustering, are not explained in an intuitive and accessible manner. The connection between the theoretical results and the practical algorithm is also not entirely clear.
* **Exposition and Language:**  The writing is often convoluted and requires careful reading. The paper could benefit from more illustrative examples and a more streamlined presentation. The numerous mathematical notations, while standard, are not consistently explained or referenced. 
* **Accessibility of Key Concepts:** The meaning of "coherence" and how it relates to the effectiveness of different recovery methods is not clearly defined. The discussion is buried in the appendix and does not easily translate into an intuitive understanding. 
* **Heuristic Dictionary Learning:**  The proposed algorithm for dictionary learning relies on an RPCA initialization, making it a heuristic approach. While effective empirically, the theoretical properties of this dictionary learning strategy are not fully explored.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in low-rank matrix recovery with a novel approach.
* The theoretical results are promising and provide a solid foundation for the proposed method.
* The empirical results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
* The work has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in areas where low-rank matrix recovery is a crucial component.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of clarity and accessibility significantly hinders the impact of the paper.
* The heuristic nature of the dictionary learning algorithm raises concerns about its generality and robustness.
* The paper may be too specialized for a broad NIPS audience without substantial rewriting to improve clarity.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and potentially significant contribution to the field of low-rank matrix recovery. However, its impact is currently limited by its lack of clarity and accessibility.  I believe the paper is publishable, but *only* with a major revision that addresses the identified weaknesses. Specifically, the authors should focus on: 

1. **Simplifying the exposition:** Explain the core concepts (especially coherence) in a more intuitive manner, providing illustrative examples and avoiding overly technical jargon.
2. **Restructuring the paper:**  Clearly articulate the problem, the proposed solution, and the key contributions at the beginning of the paper. 
3. **Improving the writing quality:** Revise the language for clarity and conciseness.
4. **Discussing limitations:** Acknowledge the limitations of the heuristic dictionary learning approach and suggest potential directions for future work.
5. **Providing more context:** Briefly outline the broader applications of this work to make it more accessible to a wider audience.

Without these revisions, the paper will likely only be of interest to a small group of specialists. With significant rewriting, it has the potential to make a broader impact on the NIPS community.
