This paper studies recovery of low-rank matrices from grossly corrupted observations when the clean data exhibit clustered structure, which increases coherence and degrades RPCA. Building on RPCA (Candès et al.) and LRR (Liu et al.), the authors show that using a low-rank dictionary A in the LRR formulation can eliminate the dependence on the problematic µ2 coherence parameter (tied to clustered row-space structure). They prove noiseless and noisy recovery theorems under conditions such as U0 ⊂ UA and rank(A) sufficiently small relative to n2/µ1(A), yielding guarantees that are stronger than RPCA (a looser log factor and no µ2). For the unsupervised setting, they propose a simple heuristic: run RPCA, truncate the SVD to form a low-rank estimate, column-normalize it to obtain A, then solve LRR. Experiments on synthetic data (phase transitions) and corrupted motion trajectories show clear improvements over RPCA and deliver better downstream subspace clustering accuracy.

Quality: The theoretical statements are technically sound on reasonable checking; the conditions are natural for dictionary-based models and the proofs appear careful. Empirical evidence aligns with the theory and justifies the RPCA→LRR heuristic, though stronger baselines and ablations would help (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, Stable PCP, LRR with A=X, GoDec/IRLS, sensitivity to λ, ε, dictionary rank/misspecification, larger scales).

Clarity: This is the main weakness. The paper does not clearly foreground its contributions, intuition, or impact; the notion and role of coherence (especially µ2) are difficult to parse and largely relegated to the appendix. Language and organization need editing. I encourage the authors to move the intuition for coherence and the “µ2-phenomenon” into the main text with figures, explicitly state the key theorem assumptions/implications, provide pseudocode with complexity and failure modes, and discuss when the dictionary assumption may not hold.

Originality: While grounded in recent literature, the use of a low-rank dictionary to neutralize coherence and the accompanying theorems constitute a meaningful advance, paired with an effective heuristic.

Significance: The work addresses a real limitation of RPCA, offers rigorous guarantees, and demonstrates practical gains on synthetic and real data. It is likely to influence subsequent robust subspace recovery and clustering methods.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong and relevant theoretical contribution (µ2-independence) with practical algorithm
- Clear empirical improvements on both synthetic and real tasks
- Builds a principled bridge between RPCA and dictionary-based LRR

Arguments against acceptance:
- Substantial clarity and exposition issues limit accessibility
- Limited ablations and baseline coverage; scalability not thoroughly evaluated
- Heuristic dictionary learning lacks deeper analysis of failure cases

Post-rebuttal: Publishable and interesting; I would have preferred a major revision for clarity, but I recommend acceptance contingent on substantial rewriting.
