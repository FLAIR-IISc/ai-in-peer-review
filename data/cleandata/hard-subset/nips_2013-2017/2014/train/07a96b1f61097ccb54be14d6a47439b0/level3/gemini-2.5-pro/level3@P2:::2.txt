This paper addresses the problem of recovering a low-rank matrix from grossly corrupted observations, a task for which Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) has become a standard tool. The authors identify a key limitation of RPCA: its performance degrades when the low-rank data is "coherent," a property that arises when the data exhibits clustering structures. The central idea of this work is to leverage the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework, which seeks a representation of the data `X` as `AZ+S`, to overcome this challenge. The authors provide a novel theoretical analysis showing that if the dictionary `A` is itself low-rank and its column space contains that of the true low-rank data `L0`, the LRR approach is immune to the coherence parameter that hampers RPCA. Based on this insight, they propose a practical two-stage algorithm: first, an initial estimate of `L0` is obtained via RPCA, and second, this estimate is used to construct a low-rank dictionary for a final recovery step using LRR.

### Strengths

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The main theoretical contributions, Theorems 1 and 2, provide formal guarantees for the recovery of coherent data using LRR with a suitable dictionary. These results are rigorous and directly support the paper's central claim that this approach can avoid the coherence parameters that limit RPCA.
*   **Originality:** While the paper builds upon well-established techniques like RPCA and LRR, the combination and the underlying motivation are novel. The key original contribution is the theoretical insight that connects the coherence of a low-rank matrix to its clustering structure and demonstrates how a low-rank dictionary in the LRR framework can mitigate this issue. This provides a new perspective on the LRR model and its relationship to RPCA.
*   **Significance:** The results are important and likely to be impactful. RPCA is a widely used technique, and this work addresses one of its fundamental limitations. By showing that data with ubiquitous clustering structures can be challenging for RPCA and then providing a demonstrably better solution, the paper advances the state of the art. The empirical results are compelling, showing a 47% larger success region on synthetic data and a 40% relative improvement in clustering error on a real-world motion segmentation task. This suggests the proposed method could be highly useful for practitioners.

### Weaknesses

The primary weakness of this paper is its lack of clarity and accessibility.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very difficult to read for anyone who is not already an expert in this specific sub-area of matrix recovery. The main contributions and their broader impact are not explained clearly, making the work less accessible to the general NIPS audience. The writing style is dense, and the exposition could be significantly improved.
*   The core concept of "coherence" is central to the paper's motivation, yet it is not explained intuitively. The paper presents the mathematical definitions (3.4-3.6) but fails to provide a clear, high-level interpretation of what these parameters mean physically. The connection between coherence and data clustering (the "µ2-phenomenon") is asserted rather than explained, with the reader referred to the full paper for details. This makes it difficult to fully appreciate the problem the authors are trying to solve.
*   The proposed algorithm for learning the dictionary is heuristic. It relies on the initial RPCA step to provide a reasonable estimate of the low-rank component. While the experiments validate this practical approach, the theoretical guarantees only apply to the second LRR step, assuming a good dictionary is already available.

### Suggestions for Improvement

To improve the paper, I strongly recommend that the authors focus on rewriting for clarity.
1.  The introduction should be revised to state the problem, the limitations of prior work, and the paper's key contributions in a way that is accessible to a broad machine learning audience.
2.  A more intuitive explanation of the coherence parameters is crucial. Perhaps a simple illustrative example or figure in Section 3.1 could help readers build intuition for why clustering increases `µ2` and why this is problematic for RPCA.
3.  The overall language and exposition require careful editing to improve flow and readability.

### Summary of Review

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of robust low-rank matrix recovery. It provides a sound theoretical analysis identifying a key weakness of RPCA when dealing with clustered ("coherent") data and proposes an effective solution using LRR with a learned low-rank dictionary. The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear advance over the state of the art.

However, the paper is severely hampered by major clarity issues that make its important contributions inaccessible to a general audience. The core concepts are not explained intuitively, and the writing is dense. While the technical content is strong, the poor presentation is a significant flaw. The work is interesting and the results are significant enough for publication, but it would require a substantial revision to realize its full potential impact.
