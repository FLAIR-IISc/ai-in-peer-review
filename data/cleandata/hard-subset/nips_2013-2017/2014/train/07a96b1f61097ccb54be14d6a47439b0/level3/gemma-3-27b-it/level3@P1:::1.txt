This paper addresses the important problem of robustly recovering low-rank structure from data corrupted by outliers, specifically focusing on the challenges posed by *coherent* data – a situation where the underlying low-rank subspace exhibits clustering, leading to performance degradation in standard Robust PCA (RPCA) methods. The authors identify that increasing coherence, driven by cluster structure, is a key bottleneck for RPCA’s effectiveness, a well-known theoretical limitation.  Their contribution lies in proposing a Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework with a carefully constructed dictionary that mitigates the impact of coherence, offering a significant improvement over RPCA, both theoretically and empirically. The significance of this work stems from the prevalence of clustered data in real-world applications, making this a practically relevant problem.

The paper is well-structured, building logically from the limitations of RPCA to the motivation for LRR and the crucial role of the dictionary. The introduction clearly establishes the problem context and the paper’s contribution. The authors accurately position their work within the existing literature on robust PCA and low-rank matrix recovery, referencing key papers like [4, 13]. They explicitly state how their approach differs and improves upon prior work, specifically highlighting the issue of coherence and its connection to clustering structures.  The theoretical analysis, culminating in Theorem 1, provides a strong foundation for their proposed method, showing that a low-rank dictionary can indeed circumvent the coherence problem. The algorithm proposed (Algorithm 1) is straightforward and practical, leveraging RPCA as a starting point for dictionary learning. 

The experiments presented offer compelling evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The synthetic data results convincingly demonstrate the improvement over RPCA, particularly in scenarios with higher coherence.  More importantly, the application to motion sequence data—a realistic and challenging task—shows that the proposed method, combined with subspace clustering techniques, outperforms RPCA-based approaches. The reported 40% relative improvement in clustering error rates on the Hopkins155 dataset is substantial. The authors also acknowledge the limitations of their approach and provide avenues for future research.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and supports the claims made about the benefits of using a low-rank dictionary in LRR. The theorems are clearly stated and provide a solid mathematical foundation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The notation is clearly defined, and the explanations are concise yet comprehensive.
*   **Originality:** While building upon existing work in RPCA and LRR, the paper identifies a crucial limitation of RPCA (sensitivity to coherence) and proposes a novel solution to address it. The connection between coherence and cluster structure is insightful.
*   **Significance:** The problem addressed is important, and the proposed solution has the potential to improve performance in various applications involving noisy, high-dimensional data with underlying low-rank structure, such as image processing, computer vision, and motion analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The algorithm relies on an initial RPCA step.  While this is practical, the performance of the overall method is therefore still tied to the limitations of RPCA, at least initially.  A fully end-to-end optimization of the dictionary within the LRR framework could be a future direction.
*   The authors mention the potential for an iterative dictionary learning process but do not explore it in detail. Investigating the convergence properties and performance gains of such an iterative approach would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important and well-defined problem in robust matrix recovery.
*   The proposed solution is theoretically sound and empirically validated.
*   The paper demonstrates a clear improvement over existing state-of-the-art methods (RPCA).
*   The writing is clear, and the paper is well-organized.
*   The connection between data coherence and cluster structure provides valuable insight.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on an initial RPCA step could be seen as a limitation.
*   Further investigation of iterative dictionary learning might be beneficial. (However, this is not a fatal flaw.)

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery. The authors have identified a key limitation of RPCA, proposed a novel and effective solution, and provided strong theoretical and empirical evidence to support their claims. The paper is well-written, clear, and impactful. I strongly recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2014.
