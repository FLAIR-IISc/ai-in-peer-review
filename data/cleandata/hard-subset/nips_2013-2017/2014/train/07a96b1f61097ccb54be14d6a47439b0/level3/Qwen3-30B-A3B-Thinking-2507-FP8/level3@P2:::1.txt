

**Review**

This paper addresses a significant limitation of RPCA (Robust Principal Component Analysis), a standard method for recovering low-rank matrices from grossly corrupted observations. The authors identify that RPCA performance degrades as data coherence increases—a common situation in real-world data with clustering structures. They propose a novel approach using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary to circumvent this coherence bottleneck.

The paper builds upon RPCA [4] and LRR [13], making a key theoretical contribution by proving that LRR with a low-rank dictionary is immune to coherence parameters (especially µ2) that increase with cluster number. This provides an elementary principle for handling coherent data. The authors demonstrate that when the dictionary itself is low-rank and contains information about the ground truth matrix, LRR can effectively recover low-rank matrices without being affected by coherence parameters. They also propose a practical unsupervised algorithm that learns the dictionary using RPCA results, which outperforms RPCA in both synthetic and real-world experiments.

The theoretical contributions (Theorems 1 and 2) are sound and well-motivated, providing clear insights into the relationship between coherence, clustering structures, and recovery performance. The authors effectively demonstrate that coherence parameters are not merely theoretical assumptions but relate to intrinsic data structures—specifically, clustering structures that increase µ2. This is a significant conceptual advance beyond previous work.

The experimental results are compelling. On synthetic data, the proposed algorithm achieves a 47% wider success area compared to RPCA. On real motion sequences from the Hopkins155 database, their approach reduces clustering error rates by 40% relative improvement over RPCA. These results demonstrate clear practical significance, as clustering structures are ubiquitous in applications like motion segmentation.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and thorough experimental validation. The authors carefully address both strengths and limitations of their approach. The theoretical bounds require the condition PUA(U0) = U0, which is strong in unsupervised settings, but the authors provide a practical algorithm to approximately satisfy this condition.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, with consistent notation and clear explanations. Figures effectively illustrate key concepts (e.g., the µ2-phenomenon in Figure 2), and the manuscript provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by identifying the coherence problem in RPCA due to clustering structures and proposing a solution using LRR with a low-rank dictionary. The connection between coherence parameters and clustering structures is particularly novel, providing new insights into the physical meaning of coherence beyond its use as a theoretical assumption.

**Significance:** This work addresses an important practical limitation of RPCA that has been overlooked in the literature. Coherent data with clustering structures is common in real-world applications, and the proposed method significantly outperforms RPCA in both synthetic and real-world experiments. The insights about coherence parameters could inform future work in robust matrix recovery.

**Strengths:**
- Identifies a meaningful limitation of RPCA that has been overlooked
- Theoretical analysis provides new insights into coherence and clustering
- Practical algorithm that significantly outperforms RPCA
- Strong experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world data

**Weaknesses:**
- The connection to subspace clustering/segmentation could be more explicitly developed (the authors mention it in experiments but don't elaborate on the theoretical relationship)
- The strong assumption PUA(U0) = U0 in the theory might limit applicability in some unsupervised scenarios

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery by addressing a practical limitation of RPCA, providing a theoretically sound solution, and demonstrating clear empirical advantages. The insights about coherence parameters and clustering structures are particularly valuable. While the connection to subspace clustering could be strengthened, the paper is of high quality and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
