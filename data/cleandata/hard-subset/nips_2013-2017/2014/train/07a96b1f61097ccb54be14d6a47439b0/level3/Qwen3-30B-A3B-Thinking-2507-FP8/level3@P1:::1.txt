

This paper addresses a critical limitation in Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) for recovering low-rank matrices from grossly corrupted observations. While RPCA (Candès et al., NIPS 2009) is theoretically elegant and empirically powerful, it degrades when data exhibit high coherence—a common scenario in real-world applications where data clusters (e.g., motion sequences in video) induce structured sparsity. The authors identify that RPCA’s success condition (rank ≤ O(n²/(log n)²)) depends on coherence parameters (µ₁, µ₂, µ₃), which increase with cluster count, causing performance drops. This "µ₂-phenomenon" (Figure 2) reveals coherence as a natural consequence of data structure, not merely a theoretical artifact.  

The core contribution is a principled solution via Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a *low-rank dictionary*. The authors prove (Theorem 1) that if the dictionary A is low-rank and satisfies U₀ ⊂ Uₐ (where U₀ spans the true low-rank data), LRR avoids µ₂ entirely. Crucially, they establish a practical unsupervised algorithm (Algorithm 1) that constructs A from RPCA’s output, requiring only 20% more computation than RPCA. Experiments validate this: on synthetic data, the success region widens by 47% (Figure 4); on Hopkins155 motion sequences, clustering error drops 40% (Table 1) versus RPCA.  

**Strengths**  
*Originality & Theoretical Rigor*: The paper is the first to link coherence to clustering structures and prove that low-rank dictionaries circumvent this bottleneck. It advances NIPS 2010’s LRR work (Liu et al.) by resolving the "dictionary design" problem for coherent data, moving beyond the common A = X case. The µ₂-phenomenon analysis (Section 3.1.2) fundamentally clarifies coherence’s role—showing it reflects real data structure, not just a technicality.  
*Significance*: Clustering is ubiquitous (e.g., motion segmentation, document analysis). The method’s 40% error reduction on real motion data (Table 1) demonstrates tangible impact. It directly addresses robust GPCA (Vidal, NIPS 2005), a long-standing open problem.  
*Quality & Clarity*: The proofs (Theorems 1–2) are meticulous, with tight bounds (rank ≤ O(n²/log n)), and experiments comprehensively cover synthetic and real data. The writing is clear, with intuitive figures (e.g., Figure 1’s clustering example) and concise notation.  

**Weaknesses**  
*Algorithmic Scope*: The unsupervised algorithm (Algorithm 1) assumes RPCA’s recovery is "good enough" to build A. While empirically effective, the paper could better quantify how errors in RPCA’s L̂₀ propagate to A. An iterative refinement (e.g., updating A using ÂZ∗) might further improve robustness, as hinted in Section 3.3.  
*Theoretical Bound*: The rank bound (O(n²/log n)) is looser than RPCA’s O(n²/(log n)²), though the trade-off for coherence resilience is justified. A discussion of when this gap matters (e.g., for extremely high-rank data) would strengthen the analysis.  

**Comparison to NIPS Work**  
This paper significantly extends NIPS 2009 RPCA (Candès et al.) and NIPS 2010 LRR (Liu et al.). While RPCA assumes incoherence, this work *explains why coherence arises* (from clustering) and *solves it*. It also advances beyond Liu et al.’s A = X focus, providing a general dictionary design principle. The problem—coherent data recovery—was implicitly acknowledged in RPCA’s limitations (Candès et al., Section 5.1) but never resolved, making this a clear novelty.  

**Reviewer Assessment**  
*Pro Acceptance*:  
- Solves a well-motivated, practical bottleneck in RPCA (high coherence from clustering).  
- Theoretical contribution is substantial and rigorously proven.  
- Empirical results are compelling and cover synthetic and real-world scenarios.  
- Directly enables better subspace clustering (e.g., 40% error reduction on Hopkins155).  

*Con Acceptance*:  
- Minor gap in error propagation analysis for Algorithm 1.  
- Theoretical rank bound is slightly weaker than RPCA’s (but justified).  

**Recommendation**  
This paper is a high-quality contribution to robust low-rank recovery. It identifies a critical gap in RPCA’s applicability (coherence due to clustering), resolves it with a novel, principled method, and demonstrates strong empirical gains. The work is original, technically sound, and significant for practitioners in computer vision and signal processing. The minor weaknesses do not undermine its core contributions. **I recommend acceptance**.  

*Additional Note for Authors*: The method’s applicability to subspace clustering (e.g., as a preprocessing step for SSC/SIM) is validated in Table 1. However, explicitly discussing how the dictionary A *directly* improves clustering (beyond just matrix recovery) would further highlight the significance. For instance, does A’s low-rank structure enhance cluster separation? This could strengthen the conclusion.
