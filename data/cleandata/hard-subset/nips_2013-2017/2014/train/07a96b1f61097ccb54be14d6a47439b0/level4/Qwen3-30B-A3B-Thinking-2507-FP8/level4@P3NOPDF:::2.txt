

This paper proposes a novel approach to restoring corrupted observation matrices by combining robust PCA with dictionary-based structure-preserving projection. The core insight addresses a limitation in standard robust PCA: for structured data (e.g., clustered), its theoretical guarantees are insufficient. The authors argue that representing such structure via a dictionary enables stronger recovery bounds, supported by two theorems establishing reconstruction conditions given a dictionary. For dictionary learning, they propose a heuristic algorithm—first applying robust PCA, then representing the result via a dictionary—justified solely by empirical performance.

**Quality:** The technical content is sound, with theorems rigorously establishing theoretical conditions for recovery. However, the dictionary learning component lacks theoretical justification, relying entirely on empirical validation. While the algorithm demonstrates practical efficacy, the absence of a principled learning method weakens the theoretical completeness.

**Clarity:** This is the paper’s most significant weakness. Despite a logical structure, the contributions and broader impact remain opaque to non-specialists. Key concepts like "coherence" are introduced without intuitive explanation or practical interpretation, forcing readers to navigate complex appendix material to grasp their significance. The paper fails to articulate *why* the dictionary-based approach resolves robust PCA’s limitations for structured data or how this advances practical applications. For instance, it vaguely suggests the method "sidesteps earlier problems" but provides no concrete examples or comparative analysis of prior limitations. Language errors (e.g., "observartion," "by by") further hinder readability. The authors must clarify the conceptual framework, define terms accessibly, and explicitly link theoretical results to tangible benefits.

**Originality:** The work synthesizes two recent techniques (robust PCA and dictionary learning) in a novel context, yielding new theorems and empirical validation. While the combination is not entirely unprecedented, the specific theoretical guarantees for structured data represent a meaningful extension.

**Significance:** The paper advances the state-of-the-art with rigorous theoretical results and a practical algorithm that outperforms alternatives empirically. The heuristic dictionary-learning step, though theoretically unsound, demonstrates clear utility in practice. This suggests the core insight—leveraging structure via dictionaries to strengthen recovery—holds promise for real-world applications where data exhibit inherent organization (e.g., image clusters, sensor networks).

**Post-Rebuttal Assessment:** After author discussion, the paper’s publishable content and technical merit are clear. However, the clarity deficit remains a critical barrier to impact. The authors acknowledge the need for substantial rewriting to articulate contributions and contextualize results for a broader audience. The current presentation risks limiting the work’s reach to a narrow specialist circle, undermining its significance. While the theoretical and empirical results are robust, the paper’s accessibility must improve significantly to justify publication. Given the authors’ commitment to addressing these issues, **a major revision is strongly recommended**. The required changes—clarifying key concepts (e.g., coherence), explicitly stating practical implications, and restructuring the narrative for wider accessibility—are substantial but achievable. Without these revisions, the paper’s value will remain unrealized for most readers. If the authors can deliver a substantially revised manuscript that meets these goals, the work will be a valuable contribution to the field.
