This paper addresses the problem of recovering a low-rank matrix from grossly corrupted observations, focusing on the challenging case where the underlying data is "coherent." The authors first provide the valuable insight that high coherence, a known theoretical bottleneck for the standard Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) method, is often a natural consequence of clustering structures within the data—a common scenario in many applications. They then demonstrate, both theoretically and empirically, that the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework can circumvent this limitation. The core of their contribution is to show that if the dictionary used in LRR is itself low-rank and spans the column space of the true data, the recovery process becomes immune to the coherence parameters that degrade RPCA's performance. Based on this principle, they propose a practical two-stage algorithm that first uses RPCA to obtain an initial estimate of the low-rank component and then uses this estimate to construct a suitable dictionary for a final LRR step, leading to a more robust recovery.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a significant and practical limitation of the widely-used RPCA framework. While the performance degradation of RPCA with high coherence is known, this work is the first to clearly connect this theoretical issue to the concrete and common phenomenon of data clustering. This provides a much-needed physical intuition for the coherence parameter. The proposed solution of using LRR with a learned low-rank dictionary is an elegant and novel way to address this problem, effectively improving upon a cornerstone method for a broad and important class of data.
*   **Quality:** The claims are very well-supported. The theoretical analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) provides rigorous guarantees for the proposed LRR-based approach in both noiseless and noisy settings, formally establishing its insensitivity to the problematic coherence parameters (µ2 and µ3). The empirical evaluation is also convincing. The experiments on synthetic data clearly illustrate the expanded region of successful recovery compared to standard RPCA, and the results on corrupted motion sequences demonstrate a substantial real-world benefit for downstream tasks like subspace clustering.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established, the theoretical results are stated precisely, and the proposed algorithm is straightforward to understand and implement.

**Questions for the Authors:**

The experiments in Section 4.2 compellingly demonstrate that the improved data recovery from your method leads to significantly better performance on a downstream subspace clustering task. This naturally raises a question about the broader applicability of the framework:

*   Could the proposed method be more directly adapted for the subspace clustering problem itself? The original LRR papers [13, 14] use the learned representation matrix Z to construct an affinity graph for clustering. Could the coefficient matrix Z* obtained from your algorithm also be used for this purpose, and would it offer advantages over standard LRR-based clustering approaches (e.g., using A=X)? A brief discussion on this potential application would be a welcome addition to the paper.

**Overall Recommendation:**

This is an excellent paper that identifies and provides a clever solution to an important problem in robust low-rank matrix recovery. The connection drawn between data coherence and clustering structure is insightful, and the proposed method is theoretically sound, empirically effective, and of high practical relevance. The work represents a solid and meaningful contribution to the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
