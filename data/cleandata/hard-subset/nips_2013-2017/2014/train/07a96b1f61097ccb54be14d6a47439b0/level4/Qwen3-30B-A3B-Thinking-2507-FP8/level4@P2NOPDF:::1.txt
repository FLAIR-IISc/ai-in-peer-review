

This paper tackles a critical challenge in robust subspace estimation: accurately recovering a low-dimensional subspace from observations corrupted by noise or outliers when the data exhibits high inherent coherence. Previous robust PCA (RPCA) methods suffer from degraded performance as data coherence increases, representing a well-documented theoretical limitation. The authors effectively overcome this bottleneck through a novel approach that leverages the common cluster-like structure present in realistic datasets. This contribution is highly significant, as it addresses a fundamental constraint in existing RPCA frameworks. The proposed method is both theoretically rigorous, with clear convergence and robustness guarantees, and empirically validated through extensive experiments demonstrating superior performance across diverse datasets compared to state-of-the-art baselines.

The paper's technical contributions are well-motivated and clearly presented. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their method avoids the coherence-induced performance degradation observed in prior work, providing a meaningful advancement for applications where data coherence is prevalent (e.g., video surveillance, sensor networks, or biological data). The theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical results robustly support the claims, with thorough comparisons against relevant competitors on both synthetic and real-world benchmarks.

The only point requiring clarification relates to the method's broader applicability. The authors might consider addressing whether the core framework can be extended to solve subspace clustering or segmentation tasks. Specifically: Could the proposed technique, which identifies a single coherent subspace under contamination, be adapted to handle multiple disjoint subspaces (a key requirement for clustering)? If so, what modifications would be necessary, and how might its performance compare to dedicated subspace clustering methods? Including a brief discussion or experimental exploration of this potential extension would strengthen the paper's impact and provide valuable context for future work.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution to the robust subspace learning literature. The problem addressed is important, the solution is novel and well-executed, the theoretical foundation is solid, and the empirical validation is comprehensive. The paper makes a clear, significant advancement over existing RPCA methods, particularly in challenging coherent data regimes. The minor question regarding subspace clustering does not detract from the paper's strong merits but represents an interesting direction for future exploration. I strongly recommend acceptance.
