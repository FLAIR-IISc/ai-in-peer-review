This paper proposes a novel approach for recovering low-rank matrices from grossly corrupted observations by combining robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection. The key insight is that for structured data, such as clustered data, the guarantees of RPCA are not strong enough, and by representing the structure with a dictionary, stronger bounds can be given.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of the limitations of RPCA in handling coherent data, which is ubiquitous in many applications. They show that the performance of RPCA degrades as the coherence of the data increases, and propose a new approach, called Low-Rank Representation (LRR), which can effectively alleviate the challenges arising from coherent data.

The paper makes several contributions. Firstly, it studies the problem of recovering low-rank and coherent matrices from their corrupted versions, and investigates the physical regime where coherent data arise. Secondly, it provides a theoretical analysis of the LRR model, and proves that when the dictionary matrix is low-rank and contains information about the ground truth matrix, LRR can be immune to the coherence parameters that increase with the underlying cluster number. Thirdly, it proposes a practical algorithm for learning the dictionary matrix in LRR, which outperforms RPCA in extensive experiments.

The strengths of the paper include its technical soundness, the novelty of the approach, and the thorough analysis of the limitations of RPCA. The paper also provides a clear and well-organized structure, making it easy to follow.

However, there are some areas that need improvement. The language and notation used in the paper can be confusing at times, and some of the concepts, such as coherence, are not well-defined. Additionally, the paper could benefit from more intuitive explanations and examples to help illustrate the key ideas and results.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the theorems and proofs are well-presented. However, the justification for the algorithm for learning the dictionary is empirical, and more theoretical analysis would be beneficial.

In terms of originality, the paper combines two recent techniques, RPCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection, in a novel way. The paper also provides new theorems and empirical results supporting the usefulness of the combination.

In terms of significance, the paper advances the state-of-the-art with rigorous results, and the proposed algorithm demonstrates clear improvements over alternative algorithms. However, the paper could benefit from more discussion on the implications of the results and the potential applications of the approach.

Overall, the paper makes significant contributions to the field of low-rank matrix recovery, and with some revisions to address the areas mentioned above, it has the potential to be a strong publication.
