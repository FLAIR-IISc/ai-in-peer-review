This paper combines two recent techniques, robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection, to address the restoration of corrupted observation matrices. The core idea is that for structured data (e.g., clustered data), the theoretical guarantees of robust PCA may be insufficient, and representing the data’s structure with a dictionary can lead to stronger recovery bounds. The paper presents theorems supporting these claims and proposes an algorithm—first computing robust PCA, then using its result to construct a dictionary—justified by empirical results.

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound, with theorems providing conditions for successful reconstruction given a dictionary. The dictionary learning algorithm, while empirically validated, lacks a strong theoretical justification. The mathematical arguments appear reasonable, pending detailed verification.

**Clarity:**
Clarity is the primary concern. While the paper’s structure is logical, its main contributions and overall impact are not clearly articulated for a broad audience within the field. Key concepts, such as coherence, are difficult to grasp and require indirect interpretation. The paper would benefit from more precise definitions and a clearer explanation of the results’ implications. For instance, the potential of dictionaries to circumvent limitations of earlier methods is not fully elaborated. The language requires careful review and editing. Seeking author feedback on how to improve accessibility would be valuable.

**Originality:**
The work builds upon recent advances in robust PCA and dictionary learning, but offers a novel combination of these techniques, supported by new theorems and empirical results. This combination demonstrates a tangible benefit in addressing data with inherent structure.

**Significance:**
The paper advances the state of the art with rigorous theoretical results. Even though the proposed dictionary learning algorithm is heuristic, the demonstrable improvements over existing methods highlight the value of the insights presented. The work addresses a significant challenge in matrix recovery, particularly when dealing with structured data, and offers a promising avenue for future research.
