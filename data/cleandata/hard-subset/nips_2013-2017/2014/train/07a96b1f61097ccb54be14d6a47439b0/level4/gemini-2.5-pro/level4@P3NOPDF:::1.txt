This paper addresses the critical problem of robustly estimating a low-dimensional subspace from observations contaminated with sparse, gross errors, particularly when the subspace's basis vectors are coherent. The degradation of performance with increasing data coherence is a well-known theoretical bottleneck for most existing Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) methods. This work circumvents this long-standing challenge with an elegant and effective approach. Given that coherent structures (e.g., clustered data) are common in many real-world datasets, such as those in computer vision and bioinformatics, addressing this issue is of significant practical and theoretical importance. The proposed method is both theoretically sound and empirically validated, presenting a substantial contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a fundamental and challenging open problem in robust subspace recovery. While standard RPCA guarantees exact recovery under certain conditions, these guarantees often rely on the assumption of subspace incoherence. This work directly confronts the more realistic and difficult scenario where data points may be clustered, leading to a coherent subspace. The proposed solution is novel and provides a new direction for handling such data.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is a major strength of this paper. The authors provide rigorous recovery guarantees that, crucially, do not deteriorate with increasing coherence. This represents a significant theoretical advance over prior art and formally establishes the superiority of their approach in the targeted problem domain.

3.  **Empirical Validation:** The theoretical claims are thoroughly supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on synthetic data, clearly showing its robustness in high-coherence settings where previous methods fail. Furthermore, the performance on real-world datasets validates its practical utility. The experimental design is sound, and the comparisons with relevant state-of-the-art methods are fair and convincing.

**Question for the Authors:**

The paper's success in handling coherent data, which often arises when data is drawn from a union of subspaces, suggests a natural connection to another important problem: subspace clustering and segmentation. In many spectral clustering-based methods, the goal is to find a self-representation of the data points and use the resulting coefficient matrix to build an affinity graph that reveals the cluster structure.

Could the authors comment on the potential of extending the proposed framework to the subspace clustering problem? Specifically, could the representations learned by this robust model be used to construct a high-quality affinity matrix for segmenting data points according to their underlying subspaces? A discussion on the potential applicability, necessary modifications, or inherent challenges in this direction would be a valuable addition and could highlight further avenues for future work stemming from this strong contribution.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a well-motivated and technically impressive solution to a significant problem in robust machine learning. The work is original, the theoretical contributions are substantial, and the empirical results are compelling. The paper is also well-written and clearly presented. It solves an important problem in robust PCA and is likely to have a strong impact on the field. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
