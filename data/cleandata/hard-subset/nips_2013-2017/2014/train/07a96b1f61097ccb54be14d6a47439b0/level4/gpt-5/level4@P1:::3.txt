Summary
This paper studies recovery of low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse errors when the data exhibit clustering (union-of-subspaces) structure, which induces high coherence and degrades RPCA. The authors show that the second coherence parameter µ2 increases with the number of clusters even when the global rank remains fixed, explaining RPCA’s deterioration on “coherent” data. They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a suitably chosen dictionary A. If A is itself low-rank and its column space contains the true column space U0, LRR’s guarantees can avoid dependence on µ2 (and µ3). The paper provides recovery theorems for both noiseless and noisy settings under these conditions. Practically, the authors propose an unsupervised pipeline: run RPCA to get an initial estimate of L0, construct a well-conditioned dictionary by normalizing a rank-truncated version of that estimate, then run LRR. Experiments on synthetic data and corrupted motion sequences show improved recovery and downstream subspace clustering accuracy compared with RPCA.

Relation to prior work
This work is closely related to subspace clustering under union-of-subspaces models, including Wang and Xu (Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering; Provable Subspace Clustering: When LRR Meets SSC), Soltanolkotabi and Candès (geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers), and Soltanolkotabi et al. Unlike those, this paper targets global low-rank plus sparse recovery while leveraging the union-of-subspaces structure through the dictionary in LRR. It also complements results in RPCA and matrix completion showing incoherence is necessary without extra structure: here, additional structure (a low-rank dictionary spanning U0) lets one bypass µ2. The observation that higher coherence can help subspace clustering (Wang and Xu) is consistent: their setting requires each cluster to be low-rank, not the entire dataset, whereas this paper addresses global low-rank recovery but benefits from clustered structure when A is chosen appropriately.

Strengths
- Clear identification of the “µ2-phenomenon”: coherence increases with the number of clusters, explaining RPCA’s failure mode on coherent data.
- Theoretical advances: noiseless and noisy recovery guarantees for LRR with low-rank A that contains U0, with bounds that avoid µ2 and are slightly looser in rank than RPCA’s (log factors).
- Practical and simple algorithm to construct A in an unsupervised way using RPCA, with modest extra computation.
- Empirical validation on synthetic data (substantial expansion of the success region over RPCA) and on corrupted motion sequences, where the pipeline improves subspace clustering error rates significantly.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions on A: Exact recovery requires U0 ⊂ UA and a low-rank dictionary. While the proposed heuristic often works, the paper should better discuss sensitivity when RPCA’s initial estimate is imperfect—the regime where coherence is high is also where RPCA may fail the most.
- Baselines: It would be informative to compare against a pipeline that performs noisy subspace clustering with ℓ1 regularization to estimate clusters, followed by PCA within each cluster, then recombines. Although no guarantee exists for this baseline, it is a natural competitor in practice.
- Scope of experiments: Beyond motion segmentation, additional real applications (e.g., text data where rows may also be clustered) would strengthen the case.
- Clarity and writing: Several passages are unclear or imprecise (see detailed comments). Better exposition of assumptions (column normalization of A, λ choice, ε setting) would help reproducibility.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The technical claims are plausible and supported by theorems and proofs (per the arXiv version). The conditions and bounds are clearly stated. More discussion of robustness to dictionary misspecification would improve completeness.
- Clarity: The paper needs careful editing for language precision and organization. Some statements are vague or awkwardly phrased (examples below).
- Originality: The idea of mitigating coherence via a low-rank dictionary in LRR and showing µ2 can be avoided under additional structure is novel.
- Significance: High. The results are likely to be used by practitioners facing clustered data, and they advance understanding of when and how to sidestep incoherence constraints.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Lines 80–82: Rewrite for clarity. Avoid “our data” and be precise about the problem setting and limitations. Even with correct modeling of extra structures, perfect recovery requires conditions on rank, sparsity, and dictionary span; please articulate these explicitly.
- Line 88: “is no longer a method of perfect” → “is no longer sufficient for perfect recovery” or “does not guarantee perfect recovery.”
- Please proofread carefully. Be precise about what each claim pertains to (RPCA vs. LRR, global vs. per-cluster low-rankness).
- Discuss related work noting that higher coherence can benefit subspace clustering (Wang and Xu). Clarify that your setting differs because the global matrix is low-rank, and your approach combines global low-rankness with union-of-subspaces structure via A.
- Compare against a baseline that performs noisy subspace clustering with ℓ1 penalty, then PCA per cluster. Even without theory, a practical comparison would be valuable.
- Section 4.2: This pipeline effectively yields a robust subspace clustering method under sparse corruptions with improved results over RPCA+SSC on Hopkins155 variants; highlight this as an additional contribution.

Minor issues
- State clearly the normalization of A’s columns and the rationale for λ = 1/√n1.
- Specify how ε is chosen in the noisy LRR and its sensitivity.
- Clarify computational complexity and report actual runtimes.
- Consider mentioning potential iterative refinement (RPCA → LRR → update A), even if convergence is typically two iterations.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a well-known limitation of RPCA with a principled and practical solution.
- Solid theory (noiseless and noisy) avoiding µ2 under clear additional structure.
- Simple unsupervised algorithm with strong empirical gains.
- Relevant to robust GPCA and subspace clustering with sparse corruptions.

Cons for acceptance
- Dependence on a good initial estimate from RPCA may limit gains in the most coherent regimes.
- Writing and clarity need improvement.
- Missing comparisons to natural subspace-clustering-first baselines.

Recommendation
Accept. This paper makes a substantial contribution to low-rank recovery and subspace-structured data analysis, with theoretical insights, a practical algorithm, and convincing experiments.
