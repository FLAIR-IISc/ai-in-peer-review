

This paper addresses a critical limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) when applied to structured data with clustering patterns. The authors demonstrate that RPCA's performance degrades as data coherence increases with more clusters, a common scenario in real-world applications. They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully designed low-rank dictionary to overcome this issue, providing theoretical guarantees and a practical algorithm.

The main contribution is establishing that LRR with a low-rank dictionary can avoid coherence parameters that plague RPCA. The authors prove that when the dictionary itself is low-rank and satisfies specific subspace conditions, LRR can exactly recover low-rank matrices from corrupted observations without being affected by increasing coherence. This provides a theoretical foundation for handling coherent data, which was previously considered a limitation for RPCA. The paper also introduces an unsupervised algorithm that constructs a suitable dictionary by first applying RPCA to the data and then normalizing the resulting low-rank approximation.

Quality: The paper presents technically sound theoretical results with rigorous proofs for both noiseless and noisy scenarios. The theorems establish clear conditions under which LRR with a low-rank dictionary outperforms RPCA, particularly regarding the relationship between coherence parameters and cluster structure. The experimental validation on both synthetic data and motion sequences is comprehensive and demonstrates consistent improvements over RPCA. The main limitation is that the dictionary learning algorithm, while empirically effective, lacks theoretical guarantees. The authors acknowledge this by presenting it as a heuristic approach, but a more principled method for dictionary learning would strengthen the paper.

Clarity: The paper suffers from significant clarity issues that hinder accessibility beyond a narrow specialist audience. Key concepts like coherence and its relationship to clustering structures are not sufficiently explained. The paper states that coherence is "related in nature to extra structures" but doesn't provide intuitive explanations or examples that would help readers understand why coherence matters in practical applications. The theoretical framework is dense and would benefit from more intuitive explanations. The connection between the mathematical results and their practical implications is not sufficiently developed. For instance, the paper mentions that "the coherence parameters are not 'assumptions' for a proof, but rather some excellent quantities that relate in nature to the extra structures," but doesn't provide concrete examples of how this insight can be applied beyond the specific problem at hand. The paper would be significantly improved with clearer explanations of these concepts, perhaps through additional examples or visualizations.

Originality: The paper makes a valuable contribution by connecting coherence parameters to clustering structures and demonstrating how LRR with a low-rank dictionary can address this limitation. While both RPCA and LRR have been previously studied, the specific theoretical insights about coherence and the conditions under which LRR can avoid coherence parameters represent novel contributions. The paper effectively distinguishes its approach from previous work by showing that standard LRR with arbitrary dictionaries doesn't solve this problem, but a low-rank dictionary specifically designed to match the data's structure does. This represents a meaningful advancement beyond the current state of the art.

Significance: The work addresses an important practical limitation of RPCA that affects many real-world applications where data naturally exhibits clustering structures. The proposed algorithm consistently outperforms RPCA in both synthetic and real-world experiments, with a 47% wider success area on random matrices and a 40% relative improvement in motion segmentation error rates. These results demonstrate tangible improvements that would be valuable to practitioners. The theoretical insights also advance our understanding of the relationship between data structure and recovery guarantees, which could inform future work on robust matrix recovery. While the dictionary learning algorithm is heuristic, the empirical results convincingly demonstrate that the theoretical insights translate into practical benefits.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1) Providing clearer explanations of coherence and its practical significance
2) Adding more intuitive examples to illustrate the theoretical results
3) Expanding the discussion of how the dictionary learning approach could be generalized or improved
4) Clarifying the connection between the theoretical results and practical applications

Overall, this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery by addressing a previously overlooked limitation of RPCA. The theoretical insights are sound, and the empirical results demonstrate clear practical benefits. While the paper's current clarity issues prevent it from reaching its full potential, the core ideas and results are strong and would be valuable to the NIPS community. With appropriate revisions to improve clarity and accessibility, this work would make an excellent contribution to the conference.
