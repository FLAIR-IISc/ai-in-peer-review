This paper presents a novel approach to recover low-rank matrices from grossly corrupted observations by leveraging the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) model with a learned dictionary. The authors demonstrate that the incoherence parameter, often considered a bottleneck in Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA), can be related to additional clustering structures in the data. By exploiting this structure using LRR, the authors show that it is possible to partially remove the dependence on the incoherence parameter and obtain a theoretically stronger guarantee for both exact low-rank and sparse decomposition and its noisy extension.

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of compressed sensing. The proposed method is closely related to previous subspace clustering papers, but it explicitly addresses the assumption on "clustering," which is reasonable and more general. The simulation and real data experiments verify the theoretical analysis, and it is likely that the new structure can be found in many other real applications.

However, there are some areas that require improvement. The language in the paper needs to be more precise and specific, particularly when describing the problem and its structures. The authors should proof-read the paper more carefully to avoid unclear descriptions and ambiguous claims. For example, the description of "our data" in lines 80-82 needs to be rewritten for clarity. Additionally, the paper should explicitly state what is meant by "perfect recovery" in line 88.

The authors may also want to consider comparing their proposed algorithm against other methods, such as first solving noisy subspace clustering with an ℓ1 penalty and then performing PCA for each subspace. While there is no provable guarantee for this approach, it would be interesting to see how it performs in comparison to the proposed method.

Furthermore, the paper presents a new method for solving subspace clustering problems with sparse corruption in the data, which leads to significant improvements over the standard RPCA+SSC on the Hopkins155 dataset. This contribution should be highlighted as one of the paper's key achievements.

In conclusion, the paper contains at least three important contributions to the field: (1) a description of a clustering structure that leads to high coherence, (2) an example of how to implicitly exploit such structure using a dictionary in LRR, and (3) a partial solution to the sparse corruptions problem in subspace clustering. These contributions make the paper a valuable addition to the field, and it should be accepted for publication.
