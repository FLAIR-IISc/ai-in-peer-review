- Integrates RPCA and dictionary-based LRR to recover low-rank components from heavily corrupted matrices.
- Central observation: RPCA’s guarantees degrade for structured (clustered) data because coherence increases with number of clusters.
- Key claim: encoding structure via a suitable dictionary strengthens recovery bounds.
- Main theoretical result: if the dictionary is low-rank and contains the ground-truth subspace (U0 ⊂ UA), LRR can recover exactly (or nearly so with noise) without dependence on RPCA’s problematic coherence parameter.
- Practical challenge: how to obtain such a dictionary in real applications.
- Proposed heuristic: run RPCA to estimate the low-rank part, form a column-normalized dictionary from that estimate, then solve LRR; justified empirically but not theoretically.
- Quality: technically sound; theorems precisely state conditions for exact and near-exact recovery.
- Limitation: the dictionary-learning approach is heuristic and lacks theoretical justification.
- Clarity: primary weakness—contributions, implications, and coherence notion are not presented accessibly and rely on supplemental material for intuition.
- Writing needs language/style edits and clearer, more explicit claim statements and suggested revisions.
- Originality: builds on RPCA and LRR but provides new theoretical results and supporting experiments.
- Significance: advances the state of the art with formal guarantees and demonstrated practical gains despite heuristic dictionary construction.
- Post-rebuttal conclusion: paper is publishable and meaningful but requires substantial rewriting; would merit a major revision.
