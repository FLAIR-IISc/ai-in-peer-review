The paper proposes to use Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary *A* to improve the effectiveness of RPCA. In particular, the authors demonstrate that the incoherence parameter μ, often considered a bottleneck in the recovery guarantee for RPCA, can actually reflect underlying clustering structure in the data. Under certain conditions on the dictionary *A*, the authors show that one can exploit this structure using LRR, partially removing the dependence on μ and achieving a theoretically stronger guarantee for both exact low-rank and sparse decomposition and its noisy extension.

This method is novel and significant to the field. It is closely related to the union-of-subspace structure assumed in previous subspace clustering papers, such as those by Wang and Xu ("Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering & Provable subspace clustering: When LRR meets SSC") and Soltanolkotabi and Candès ("A geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers") and Soltanolkotabi et al ("Robust subspace clustering"), but is less explicit about the assumption of “clustering,” which is reasonable and potentially more general. The simulations and real-data experiments verify the theoretical analysis, and I believe the identified structure could be found in many other real-world applications, such as text data, as the authors remark.

In summary, the paper presents a substantial contribution to the field of compressed sensing and should be accepted by NIPS.

However, I have a number of comments and stylistic suggestions, detailed below.

1. Lines 80-82: This section needs rewriting for clarity. What is meant by “our data”? Furthermore, even if the problem captures all the structures present, there’s still an additional condition required for “perfect recovery.”

2. Line 88: “…is no longer a method of perfection.” (Please rephrase – “perfect” is unclear in this context.)

3. Please proofread the paper more carefully for similar issues. Be specific and precise in claims and references within the text. In general, focus on improving the clarity of the language.

4. Wang and Xu also demonstrated in their paper "Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering" that a higher coherence parameter can actually be beneficial for subspace clustering (unlike in RPCA and matrix completion). The key difference is that the data need not be globally low-rank; only each cluster needs to be low-rank. The structure exploited in this paper can be viewed as a combination of a global low-rank structure and a union-of-subspace cluster structure.

5. It would be interesting to compare the proposed algorithm against a two-step approach: first, solving noisy subspace clustering with an ℓ₁ penalty, and then performing PCA on each subspace. However, no provable guarantee currently exists for the latter version.

6. Section 4.2 essentially proposes a new method for solving the subspace clustering problem with sparse corruption in the data. The method leads to significant improvements over the standard RPCA+SSC combination on the Hopkins155 dataset, which I believe is a hidden contribution of this paper. The authors should highlight this. This paper should be accepted because it contains at least three important contributions to the field: 1) a description of a clustering structure that leads to high coherence, 2) an example showing that one can implicitly exploit this structure using a dictionary in LRR, and 3) a partial solution to the sparse corruption problem in subspace clustering.
