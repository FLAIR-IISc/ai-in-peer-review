This paper integrates robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection to restore corrupted observation matrices. A key insight is that for structured data (e.g., clustered data), the guarantees provided by robust PCA are insufficient, and representing the structure using a dictionary can yield stronger bounds. The authors provide theorems to support these claims. However, learning the dictionary remains a challenge, and the proposed algorithm, justified by empirical results, involves first computing robust PCA and then representing the result using a dictionary.

Quality:
The paper is technically sound, as verified up to a reasonable level of scrutiny. The two theorems elaborate on the conditions under which reconstruction is possible, given a dictionary. Although the justification for dictionary learning is empirical, the results appear to be well-supported.

Clarity:
The primary issue with the paper is its clarity. While the overall structure is clear, the main contributions and impact of the paper are not adequately explained, limiting accessibility to a narrow specialist audience. To improve clarity, the claims should be more well-defined, and the impact of the results should be explicitly stated. For instance, the concept of coherence is difficult to understand, and interpreting it requires indirect means (presented in the appendix). One potential impact of the paper is that using dictionaries may allow sidestepping of earlier problems. Additionally, the language requires further review. It would be beneficial to obtain a comment from the authors regarding potential solutions to these issues.

Originality:
The paper builds upon a combination of recent techniques, incorporating new theorems and empirical results that demonstrate the usefulness of this combination.

Significance:
The paper advances the state-of-the-art with rigorous results. Although the proposed algorithm for finding the dictionary is heuristic, clear improvements over alternative algorithms demonstrate the usefulness of the insights presented in this paper.

There is clearly publishable content and interesting contributions in this paper. My only remaining concern is that rewriting the paper to make it accessible to a broader specialist audience will be a challenging task. If a "major revision" option were available, I would recommend it. The paper effectively combines two recent ideas, providing both theoretical results and a heuristic yet empirically well-performing algorithm.
