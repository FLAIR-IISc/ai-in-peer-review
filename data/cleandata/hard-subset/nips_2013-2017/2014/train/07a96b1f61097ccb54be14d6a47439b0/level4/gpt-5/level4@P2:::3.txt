Summary
This paper studies recovery of low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse errors when the data are coherent due to clustering. The authors argue that RPCA’s success conditions hinge on incoherence, which is violated in many real settings where data lie in a union of subspaces. They show that the second coherence parameter (mu2) grows with the number of clusters, degrading RPCA. They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a suitably chosen low-rank dictionary A whose column space contains the true column space. Under this condition, they prove exact (noiseless) and near-exact (noisy) recovery guarantees that avoid dependence on mu2. They also provide a practical, unsupervised pipeline: first run RPCA to get an estimate of the low-rank component, build a column-normalized low-rank dictionary from it, then solve LRR. Experiments on synthetic data and corrupted motion trajectories show substantial gains over RPCA, and the approach improves downstream subspace clustering (SIM, LRR with A=X, SSC). The work connects RPCA, LRR, and union-of-subspaces models, and offers a new interpretation of coherence as reflecting clustering structure.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on RPCA (Candès et al.) and LRR (Liu et al.), and relates to subspace clustering methods such as SSC (Elhamifar and Vidal) and SIM. Recent theory on subspace clustering under noise and outliers (e.g., Soltanolkotabi and Candès; Wang and Xu) highlights settings where higher coherence can be beneficial for clustering, contrasting with RPCA’s needs. This paper contributes by showing how to mitigate coherence harms for low-rank plus sparse recovery via a dictionary in LRR, yielding stronger guarantees when A is low-rank and informative.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical insight: links increased coherence to clustering and proves that LRR with a low-rank, column-space-containing dictionary can avoid mu2 dependences.
- Both noiseless and noisy guarantees with explicit conditions and probability bounds; requires milder rank scaling than RPCA.
- Simple, practical algorithm to learn a useful dictionary in an unsupervised setting; modest computation overhead relative to RPCA.
- Convincing empirical results on synthetic and motion data, with meaningful downstream benefits for subspace clustering.
- Conceptual contribution: clarifies the role and “physical meaning” of coherence parameters.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Exposition needs polishing. Several sentences are unclear or imprecise (see detailed comments).
- The assumption U0 ⊂ UA is necessary for exact recovery but nontrivial to ensure without supervision; the paper uses a heuristic (RPCA then LRR) without a formal guarantee for the learned A.
- Comparisons could be broader. In particular, pipelines that first perform noisy subspace clustering and then per-cluster PCA are not evaluated.
- The discussion of how coherence relates differently to recovery (harmful) versus clustering (sometimes helpful) could be expanded and better situated within prior work.
- Sensitivity analyses (e.g., to lambda, thresholding in rank estimation, dictionary conditioning) are limited.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and significant theoretical results that strengthen recovery guarantees by removing mu2 dependence under plausible conditions.
- Practical algorithm with clear improvements over RPCA in realistic scenarios.
- Bridges communities (RPCA/LRR and subspace clustering) and offers a useful perspective on coherence and clustering.

Cons for acceptance
- Some clarity issues and occasional imprecise phrasing.
- The unsupervised dictionary learning strategy, while effective empirically, lacks a formal performance guarantee.
- Missing comparisons to certain alternative pipelines.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Clarify early motivation: explicitly define “our data” and specify which properties (low-rankness, clustering/union-of-subspaces) are considered. Acknowledge that perfect recovery always requires additional conditions (e.g., sparsity level, support randomness).
- Line 80–82: Reword to avoid vague claims; be precise about when RPCA fails and why (mu2 increases with cluster count, violating incoherence).
- Line 88: Replace “no longer a method of perfect” with “no longer achieves perfect recovery” or “its performance deteriorates.”
- Throughout: tighten language, avoid ambiguous pronouns, and ensure claims are specific and referenced. A careful proofread will improve readability.
- Prior work: Contrast with results showing that higher coherence can aid subspace clustering (e.g., Wang and Xu; Soltanolkotabi and Candès). Emphasize the difference in objectives: global low-rank recovery vs. clustering with per-cluster low-rankness.
- Baselines: Consider an alternative pipeline that performs noisy subspace clustering with an l1 penalty, followed by PCA within each cluster. While formal guarantees are limited, empirical comparison would be informative.
- Hidden contribution: Section 4.2 effectively proposes a robust subspace clustering workflow under sparse corruptions. Highlight this more prominently as a contribution.
- Practical guidance: Briefly discuss sensitivity to lambda and the threshold used in rank estimation; comment on dictionary conditioning and its effect on LRR.
- Computational aspects: Provide more details on runtime and scalability, including how dictionary rank impacts solver complexity.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a strong theoretical advance, a simple and effective algorithm, and solid empirical evidence. With improved clarity and expanded discussion of related work and baselines, it will be a valuable contribution.
