This paper integrates two recent approaches—robust PCA (RPCA) and a dictionary-based low-rank representation (LRR)—to recover low-rank components from heavily corrupted observation matrices. The central observation is that for structured data (e.g., with clustering), RPCA’s theoretical guarantees degrade because coherence increases with the number of clusters; by encoding structure through an appropriately chosen dictionary, the recovery bounds can be strengthened. The paper provides theorems establishing that when the dictionary is low-rank and contains the ground-truth subspace (U0 ⊂ UA), LRR can recover (or nearly recover, in the noisy case) without dependence on the second coherence parameter that hampers RPCA. The remaining issue is how to obtain such a dictionary in practice. The authors propose an empirically validated heuristic: first run RPCA to estimate the low-rank component, then form a column-normalized dictionary from this estimate and solve LRR.

Quality:
The work appears technically sound under reasonable scrutiny. The two main theorems precisely state conditions under which exact and near-exact recovery are achievable given a suitable dictionary. The approach to learning the dictionary is heuristic, and its justification relies on empirical evidence rather than theory.

Clarity:
Clarity is the primary weakness. While the paper’s structure is serviceable, the key contributions and their broader impact are not presented in a way that is accessible beyond a narrow expert audience. Claims should be delineated more explicitly, and the implications of the results articulated more directly. The notion of coherence is difficult to grasp from the current exposition, and the intuition provided is indirect and largely deferred to supplemental material. One potential takeaway—that suitable dictionaries can mitigate limitations of prior formulations—could be emphasized more clearly. The writing also needs language and style edits. It would be helpful for the authors to explain what concrete revisions they can make to address these issues.

Originality:
Although the method builds on established ideas (RPCA and LRR), the paper contributes new theoretical results and experiments that substantiate the benefits of combining them in the proposed way.

Significance:
The paper advances the state of the art with formal guarantees and demonstrates practical gains. Even though the dictionary-construction procedure is heuristic, empirical improvements over baseline methods indicate the utility of the underlying insights.

Comment after the author rebuttal and reviewer discussion:
The paper contains publishable material and meaningful contributions. My remaining concern is that substantial rewriting will be required to make the presentation accessible beyond specialists. If a “major revision” option were available, I would recommend it. The work combines two recent ideas and supplies both theoretical guarantees and a heuristic, empirically strong algorithm.
