This paper combines two recent techniques—robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection—to restore corrupted observation matrices. The key insight is that for structured data (e.g., clustered data), the guarantees of robust PCA alone are insufficient; by explicitly representing structure with a dictionary, stronger recovery bounds can be established. The paper provides theorems to support these claims. The remaining challenge is how to learn the dictionary. To address this, the authors propose an empirically justified algorithm: first run robust PCA, then represent the result using a dictionary.

Quality:
The paper appears technically sound to a reasonable level of scrutiny. The two theorems specify conditions under which reconstruction is possible, given a dictionary. However, the justification for learning the dictionary is primarily empirical.

Clarity:
Clarity is the main weakness. While the overall structure is clear, the paper does not explain its primary contributions and impact in a way accessible beyond a very narrow specialist audience. The claims need to be more precisely defined, and the significance of the results should be made explicit. For example, the concept of coherence is difficult to understand, and its interpretation is indirect and relegated to the appendix. One potential impact is that, by using dictionaries, many earlier problems might be sidestepped. The language also needs careful editing. It would be important for the authors to comment on how they can address these issues.

Originality:
Although the work builds on a combination of recent techniques, it introduces new theorems and empirical results that support the value of the combination.

Significance:
The paper advances the state of the art with rigorous results. Even though the proposed dictionary-learning algorithm is heuristic, it shows clear improvements over alternative algorithms, demonstrating that the paper’s insights are useful.

Comment after the author rebuttal and reviewer discussion:
There is clearly publishable content and interesting contributions. My remaining concern is that substantial effort will be required to rewrite the paper to make it accessible beyond a narrow specialist audience. If a “major revision” option were available, I would choose it. The paper combines two recent ideas and offers both theoretical results and a heuristic but empirically strong algorithm.
