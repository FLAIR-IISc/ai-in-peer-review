

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) when dealing with coherent data—data exhibiting clustering structures that are ubiquitous in real-world applications. The authors identify a critical theoretical bottleneck in RPCA: as the number of clusters in the data increases, the coherence parameter µ2 (and consequently µ3) increases, degrading RPCA's recovery performance even when the underlying matrix is strictly low-rank. This is a significant issue since real-world data often exhibits clustering structures, making RPCA less effective in practical scenarios.

The authors make several important contributions. First, they rigorously analyze the relationship between clustering structures and coherence parameters, demonstrating the "µ2-phenomenon" where coherence increases with cluster number (Figure 2). Second, they prove that Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a properly constructed low-rank dictionary can avoid this coherence problem. Specifically, they show that when the dictionary matrix A is low-rank and contains the column space of the true low-rank matrix L0 (i.e., U0 ⊂ UA), LRR can exactly recover L0 and S0 without being affected by the coherence parameters that plague RPCA. Third, they propose a practical unsupervised algorithm for learning such a dictionary by leveraging RPCA's output, which significantly outperforms RPCA in both synthetic and real-world motion sequence experiments.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS literature. It builds upon and extends the RPCA work of Candès et al. (2011) and the LRR framework of Liu et al. (2013), while addressing a gap that has been noted in the literature but not formally analyzed. The authors demonstrate their approach on the Hopkins155 motion database, showing a 40% relative improvement in clustering error rates over RPCA. This connects to recent NIPS work on subspace clustering (e.g., SSC by Elhamifar & Vidal, 2013) and robust matrix completion (e.g., Candes & Plan, 2010).

The paper excels in quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with well-structured proofs of Theorems 1 and 2 that establish the conditions under which LRR can avoid coherence parameters. The experimental design is thorough, including both synthetic data (Figure 4) and real-world motion sequences (Table 1), with appropriate baselines and metrics. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach (e.g., the need for a low-rank dictionary in unsupervised settings) and provide a clear algorithm for practical implementation.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The authors provide precise definitions of coherence parameters (µ1, µ2, µ3) and carefully explain their relationship to clustering structures. The notation is consistent throughout, and the proofs are well-motivated. The algorithm description is clear and the experimental results are presented in a way that directly supports the theoretical claims.

The originality of this work is significant. While RPCA and LRR have been studied extensively, the authors are the first to formally analyze the relationship between clustering structures and coherence parameters, and to propose a principled solution using LRR with a low-rank dictionary. This represents a novel perspective on why factorization techniques are useful in robust PCA, as noted in the paper's final contribution point. The authors have also properly contextualized their work against recent NIPS proceedings, avoiding overlap with prior work.

The significance of this work is substantial. Many real-world applications involve data with clustering structures (e.g., motion segmentation, image processing), where RPCA's performance may be suboptimal. By providing a theoretically grounded method that handles coherent data, this paper advances the state of the art in robust low-rank matrix recovery. The proposed algorithm is practical, with computational complexity that is only marginally higher than RPCA (1.2x runtime), and it demonstrates clear improvements in real-world applications.

One minor weakness is the lack of discussion about the specific conditions under which the unsupervised dictionary learning algorithm (Algorithm 1) might fail. While the paper demonstrates strong empirical performance, a more detailed analysis of failure cases would strengthen the work. Additionally, while the authors briefly mention the potential application to subspace clustering (in their response to my comment), they could more thoroughly elaborate on how their dictionary learning approach would specifically improve existing subspace clustering methods like SSC or LRRx.

Strengths: 
- Rigorous theoretical analysis of coherence parameters and their relationship to clustering structures
- Novel solution using LRR with low-rank dictionary that avoids coherence bottleneck
- Practical unsupervised algorithm with clear implementation details
- Comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world data
- Clear connection to real-world applications (motion segmentation)

Weaknesses:
- Limited discussion of failure cases for the unsupervised dictionary learning
- Brief mention of subspace clustering application (needs more detail)

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- Addresses a fundamental limitation of RPCA that has been recognized but not formally resolved
- Provides a theoretically sound solution with practical implementation
- Demonstrates significant performance improvements over RPCA in both synthetic and real-world settings
- Connects to important NIPS topics (robust PCA, subspace clustering)
- Well-written with clear presentation of contributions

Con-acceptance arguments:
- Minor theoretical gap regarding failure cases of unsupervised dictionary learning
- Subspace clustering application could be more thoroughly developed

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of robust low-rank matrix recovery. The theoretical insights are valuable, the experimental results are compelling, and the practical algorithm is well-designed. The paper addresses a gap in the literature that has important implications for real-world applications. I strongly recommend acceptance.
