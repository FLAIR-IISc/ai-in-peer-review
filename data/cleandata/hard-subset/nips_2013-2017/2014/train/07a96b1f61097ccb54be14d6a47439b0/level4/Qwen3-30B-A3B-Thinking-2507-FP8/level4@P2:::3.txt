

This paper addresses a significant limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) when applied to data with clustering structures. The authors demonstrate that RPCA's performance degrades as data coherence increases, which often occurs when data exhibits clustering patterns (as shown in Figure 1). The key insight is that this coherence issue can be mitigated by using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with an appropriately designed dictionary. The authors prove that when the dictionary itself is low-rank and contains the column space of the true low-rank component (U₀ ⊂ U_A), LRR can avoid dependence on the coherence parameter μ₂, which typically limits RPCA's performance. This provides a theoretical foundation for handling coherent data while offering a practical algorithm for dictionary construction that outperforms RPCA in both synthetic and real-world experiments.

The paper makes several substantial contributions to the field. First, it identifies and formalizes the relationship between clustering structures in data and the coherence parameters that limit RPCA's performance—addressing a gap in the literature where coherence has often been treated as a technical assumption rather than a meaningful data property. Second, it provides a concrete method for leveraging this relationship through dictionary learning within the LRR framework. Third, it demonstrates the practical utility of this approach through experiments on both synthetic matrices and real motion sequences from the Hopkins155 dataset, showing significant improvements over RPCA in subspace clustering applications.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS literature. It builds upon and extends recent work on robust subspace clustering (e.g., Liu et al. "Robust Recovery of Subspace Structures by Low-Rank Representation" and Soltanolkotabi et al. "Robust Subspace Clustering"), while making a more explicit connection between coherence parameters and data structures. The authors correctly note that while Wang and Xu have previously observed that higher coherence can benefit subspace clustering (when each cluster is low-rank), their approach is distinct in that it explicitly addresses the coherence issue in the context of RPCA and provides a method to exploit this structure.

Theoretical contributions are strong and well-supported. The paper provides rigorous proofs for both noiseless and noisy recovery scenarios, establishing conditions under which LRR can outperform RPCA. The theoretical guarantees are meaningful, with the rank constraint in Theorem 1 (rank(L₀) ≤ O(n₂/log n₁)) being looser than RPCA's constraint (rank(L₀) ≤ O(n₂/(log n₁)²)). The practical algorithm (Algorithm 1) is well-motivated and computationally efficient, with experiments demonstrating a 47% wider success area in synthetic data and a 40% relative improvement in clustering error rates on real motion sequences.

The paper excels in significance. It addresses a fundamental limitation of a widely used method (RPCA) and provides both theoretical insights and practical solutions that can be applied across multiple domains. The connection between data structures (clustering) and coherence parameters is particularly valuable, as it helps explain why certain data patterns challenge standard methods. The authors' observation that such structures may be present in text data as well suggests broad applicability beyond the motion analysis examples provided.

Regarding clarity, the paper is generally well-written but contains some imprecise statements that could confuse readers. For instance, the phrase "RPCA is no longer a method of perfection" (line 88) should be clarified to specify what aspect of the method is imperfect. The authors should also more precisely explain how their approach differs from previous work on subspace clustering with union-of-subspaces structure. The paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison with methods that first solve subspace clustering with ℓ₁ penalties and then apply PCA to each cluster, as suggested in the comments.

Quality is strong: the theoretical results are well-supported by both rigorous proofs and comprehensive experiments. The authors carefully consider both strengths and limitations of their approach, particularly noting that the dictionary learning strategy is designed for unsupervised environments where supervision information about U₀ is unavailable.

Originality is high: while LRR has been previously studied, this paper provides the first systematic analysis of how dictionary design can address coherence issues in low-rank recovery. The connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters is novel and well-justified.

Significance is clear: the paper advances both the theoretical understanding of low-rank recovery and provides practical improvements for real-world applications where data exhibit clustering structures. The authors successfully demonstrate that these improvements can be achieved without additional supervision, making the approach broadly applicable.

The paper should be accepted. It makes a substantial contribution to the field by addressing a fundamental limitation of RPCA through a theoretically sound and practically effective method. The work is well-positioned within the NIPS literature, builds on previous work while making clear advances, and has been thoroughly validated through both synthetic and real-world experiments. The minor clarity issues noted can be addressed in the camera-ready version.

The authors should consider explicitly highlighting the subspace clustering contribution as a secondary but significant outcome of their method, as it represents an important practical application that would be valuable to the broader community. This would strengthen the paper's significance and make the connection to real-world applications even clearer.
