Summary
This paper proposes using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary A to improve the recovery guarantees of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA). The key insight is that the incoherence parameter μ—often regarded as a bottleneck in RPCA theory—can correspond to an underlying clustering (union-of-subspaces) structure in the data. By exploiting this structure through an appropriately chosen dictionary A, the authors partially remove the dependence on μ and obtain stronger theoretical guarantees for both exact low-rank plus sparse decomposition and its noisy extension. Theoretical results are supported by simulations and real data experiments.

Significance and novelty
- The idea of linking high coherence to latent clustering structure and then exploiting it via a dictionary in LRR is novel and important. It reframes μ not purely as an adversarial obstacle but as a signal of useful structure.
- The work connects RPCA with subspace clustering literature, including Wang and Xu (Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering; Provable Subspace Clustering: When LRR Meets SSC) and Soltanolkotabi and Candès (and coauthors) on robust subspace clustering. Compared to prior work, the present approach is less explicit about the clustering assumptions yet appears more general and practically reasonable.
- The experimental results, including on Hopkins155, substantiate the theory and demonstrate practical benefits. The identified structure is likely present in other domains (e.g., text), as the paper notes.

Technical contributions
- Formalizes conditions under which the dictionary A enables LRR to reduce the harmful effect of high μ in RPCA-style recovery.
- Provides guarantees for both the noiseless and noisy cases.
- In Section 4.2, essentially proposes a robust subspace clustering method handling sparse corruptions, yielding large improvements over the RPCA+SSC pipeline.

Overall assessment
This paper makes a substantial contribution to robust low-rank modeling and subspace clustering and should be accepted to NIPS.

Major comments and suggestions
1) Clarity and precision. Some passages need rewriting for clarity and rigor.
- Lines 80–82: Clarify what “our data” refers to (the observed matrix? underlying clean data?). Also, even if the model captures all relevant structure, perfect recovery still requires appropriate conditions (e.g., on sparsity patterns, rank, dictionary suitability). Please make the logical prerequisites explicit.
- Line 88: The sentence “... is no longer a method of perfect ...” is incomplete. Specify what “perfect” refers to (perfect recovery? separation? identifiability?) and under what conditions it fails or holds.

2) Assumptions on the dictionary A. The theory hinges on properties of A. Please:
- Clearly specify how A is constructed or learned in practice (from data, from clean samples, iteratively?), whether it uses the same data being decomposed, and whether any side information is required.
- State the precise assumptions on A (e.g., span containment, block structure, rank conditions, mutual incoherence with sparse component) and how realistic they are in typical applications.
- Discuss sensitivity to misspecification of A, and provide an ablation comparing learned A vs. generic choices (e.g., SVD-based, random, or identity) to illustrate the gain stems from exploiting clustering structure.

3) Relation to subspace clustering literature. The paper’s perspective nicely complements known results where higher coherence can help subspace clustering (e.g., Wang and Xu). The difference is that SSC does not require the whole dataset to be globally low-rank; only each cluster is low-rank. Your approach can be seen as combining global low-rank structure with a union-of-subspaces structure via A. It would help to more explicitly position your guarantees relative to:
- SSC/LSR-type methods with sparse/noisy corruptions,
- Prior LRR variants and their assumptions,
- Conditions under which your guarantees strictly improve over standard RPCA.

4) Baseline comparisons. It would be informative to include:
- A pipeline that first performs noisy subspace clustering with an ℓ1 penalty, followed by PCA within each cluster, then compares decomposition quality. While no provable guarantee exists for this pipeline, it is a practical and competitive baseline.
- Additional ablations against RPCA+SSC and alternative robust subspace clustering methods, especially on Hopkins155 and at least one non-motion dataset (e.g., text or face data), to support generality claims.

5) Highlight the “hidden” contribution in Section 4.2. This section effectively proposes a new method for subspace clustering under sparse corruptions, with strong empirical gains over RPCA+SSC on Hopkins155. Please make this an explicit contribution in the introduction and abstract, and clarify whether any theoretical statements (even informal) can be made about this setting.

6) Scope of guarantees and practical guidance.
- Explicitly separate conditions for exact recovery vs. stable recovery (with noise), and summarize the dependence on rank, sparsity level, sample size, and μ, indicating where your dependence improves over RPCA.
- Provide guidance on hyperparameter choices (e.g., regularization weights) and report sensitivity analyses if possible.
- Discuss computational complexity and scalability relative to RPCA and SSC baselines.

Minor comments
- Please proofread carefully for grammatical issues and incomplete sentences (e.g., Lines 80–82 and 88). Be precise in claims and references.
- Define all notation upon first use and avoid ambiguous pronouns such as “our data.”
- Ensure that figures and tables are self-contained with clear captions; report standard deviations and parameter settings for reproducibility.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a compelling conceptual and technical advance by turning the incoherence bottleneck into an opportunity via learned dictionaries in LRR, provides stronger theoretical guarantees, and demonstrates practical improvements, including a noteworthy contribution to subspace clustering with sparse corruptions. Clarifying the assumptions on A, tightening the writing, and adding the suggested comparisons would further strengthen the paper.
