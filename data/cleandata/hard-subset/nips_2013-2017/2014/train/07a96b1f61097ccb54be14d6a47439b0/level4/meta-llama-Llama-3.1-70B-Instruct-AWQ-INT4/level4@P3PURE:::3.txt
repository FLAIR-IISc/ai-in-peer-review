The paper proposes a novel approach to improve the effectiveness of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) by leveraging Low Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary A. The authors demonstrate that the incoherence parameter \mu, often considered a bottleneck in RPCA's recovery guarantee, can actually correspond to additional clustering structure in the data. Under certain conditions on the dictionary A, the authors show that LRR can exploit this structure, partially removing the dependence on \mu and yielding a theoretically stronger guarantee for both exact low-rank and sparse decomposition and its noisy extension.

The proposed method is novel and significant to the field, and its connection to the union-of-subspace structure assumed in previous subspace clustering papers is noteworthy. Although related to works such as "Wang and Xu: Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering & Provable subspace clustering: When LRR meets SSC" and "Soltanolkotabi and Candes: A geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers" "Soltanolkotabi et al: Robust subspace clustering", this paper is less explicit about the assumption on "clustering", which is reasonable and likely more general. The simulation and real data experiments verify the theoretical analysis, and it is likely that the new structure can be found in many other real applications, such as text data, as remarked in the paper.

In summary, the paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of compressed sensing, and its acceptance by NIPS is warranted.

However, there are several discussions and stylistic suggestions that can improve the paper's clarity and precision:

1. The description in lines 80-82 requires rephrasing for clarity. The term "our data" is ambiguous, and even if the problem captures all the structures in the data, there is still an additional condition necessary for "perfect recovery".

2. The sentence in line 88 is unclear and should be revised to specify what is no longer a method of perfect recovery.

3. A thorough proofread of the paper is necessary to address issues like the above two. The authors should strive to be specific and precise in their claims and references.

4. The authors may want to note that Wang and Xu's paper "Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering" highlights how a higher coherence parameter can be beneficial for subspace clustering, unlike in RPCA and matrix completion. The key difference lies in the fact that the data need not be low-rank overall, but rather each cluster must be low-rank. The structure exploited in this paper can be seen as a combination of low-rank structure and union-of-subspace cluster structure.

5. It would be interesting to compare the proposed algorithm against an approach that first solves noisy subspace clustering with an \ell_1 penalty and then performs PCA for each subspace. Although no provable guarantee exists for the latter method, such a comparison could provide valuable insights.

6. Section 4.2 proposes a new method for solving the subspace clustering problem with sparse corruption in the data. This method yields significant improvements over the standard RPCA+SSC on the Hopkins155 dataset, which is a notable contribution that the authors should highlight. This paper deserves acceptance due to its at least three important contributions to the field: 1) the description of a clustering structure that leads to high coherence, 2) the example of implicitly exploiting such structure using a dictionary in LRR, and 3) the partial solution to the sparse corruptions problem in subspace clustering.
