This paper proposes using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary A to strengthen Robust PCA (RPCA). The key insight is that the incoherence parameter mu—typically viewed as a bottleneck in RPCA recovery guarantees—can reflect an underlying clustering structure in the data. Under suitable conditions on the dictionary A, the method leverages this structure via LRR to partially remove the dependence on mu, yielding stronger theoretical guarantees for both exact low-rank plus sparse decomposition and its noisy extension.

The approach is novel and significant. It is closely related to the union-of-subspaces structure assumed in prior subspace clustering work, including Wang and Xu (“Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering” and “Provable Subspace Clustering: When LRR Meets SSC”), Soltanolkotabi and Candès (“A Geometric Analysis of Subspace Clustering with Outliers”), and Soltanolkotabi et al. (“Robust Subspace Clustering”). Compared to these, the present paper is less explicit about a strict “clustering” assumption, which seems reasonable and likely more general. The simulation and real-data experiments support the theory, and the proposed structural perspective appears applicable to other domains (e.g., text), as the paper notes.

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to compressed sensing and related areas. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.

Detailed comments and suggestions:
1. Lines 80–82: Please rewrite for clarity. The phrase “our data” is ambiguous—specify precisely what data model or dataset you mean. Also, even if the proposed formulation captures all relevant structure, additional conditions may still be required to guarantee “perfect recovery.” Please state these conditions explicitly or clarify what notion of recovery is claimed.

2. Line 88: The sentence “… is no longer a method of perfect (WHAT?)” is incomplete or unclear. Please specify the missing noun (e.g., “recovery,” “decomposition,” “clustering”) and restate the claim precisely.

3. More generally, please proofread carefully. Be specific and precise about claims and references within the text. Several statements (including the two above) would benefit from clearer wording and unambiguous terminology.

4. Relation to prior work: Wang and Xu (in “Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering”) note that higher coherence can actually help subspace clustering, in contrast to RPCA and matrix completion. A key distinction is that their setting does not require the entire dataset to be globally low-rank; only each cluster (subspace) must be low-rank. The structure exploited in this paper can be viewed as a combination of global low-rank structure and a union-of-subspaces clustering structure. Please make this connection explicit and contrast the assumptions and guarantees.

5. Empirical comparison: It would be informative to compare the proposed method against a two-stage pipeline: first perform noisy subspace clustering with an ℓ1 penalty, then run PCA within each subspace. While there is no provable guarantee (to my knowledge) for the latter stage, such a baseline would help isolate the benefits of the learned dictionary within LRR relative to a strong, practically relevant alternative.

6. Section 4.2 effectively proposes a new approach for subspace clustering with sparse corruptions. The observed improvements over the RPCA+SSC baseline on the Hopkins155 dataset are substantial. This is, in my view, a hidden but important contribution of the paper and should be highlighted more prominently. In particular, the paper contributes:
   - A clear description of a clustering structure that leads to high coherence, reframing mu as signal rather than solely a hindrance.
   - An example showing that one can implicitly exploit this structure by incorporating a dictionary into LRR, thereby improving robustness and reducing dependence on mu.
   - A practical method that partially resolves the sparse-corruption challenge in subspace clustering, with strong empirical performance.

Minor suggestions:
- Clarify the assumptions on the dictionary A required for the theoretical guarantees (e.g., properties relative to the subspace clusters and the sparse component). A concise, self-contained statement of the conditions would aid readability.
- When discussing “noisy” settings, specify the noise model and bounds (e.g., adversarial vs. stochastic, norms used) to align theorems and experiments.

In sum, the paper is well-motivated, technically sound, and impactful. With improved clarity and a few additional comparisons, it will be even stronger. I recommend acceptance.
