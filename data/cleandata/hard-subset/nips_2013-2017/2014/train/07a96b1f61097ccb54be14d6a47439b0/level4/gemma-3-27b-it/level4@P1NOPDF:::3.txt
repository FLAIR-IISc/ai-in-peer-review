The paper proposes leveraging Low Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary *A* to enhance the performance of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA). Specifically, the authors demonstrate that the incoherence parameter μ, often considered a bottleneck in RPCA recovery guarantees, can in fact reflect inherent clustering structure within the data. Under certain conditions on the dictionary *A*, they show that this structure can be exploited using LRR, partially reducing the dependence on μ and leading to stronger theoretical guarantees for both exact low-rank and sparse decomposition, and its noisy extension.

This method is novel and represents a significant contribution to the field. It is closely related to the union-of-subspace structure assumed in prior subspace clustering work, such as "Wang and Xu: Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering & Provable subspace clustering: When LRR meets SSC", and "Soltanolkotabi and Candes: A geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers," and “Soltanolkotabi et al: Robust subspace clustering,” but it is less restrictive in its assumptions regarding “clustering,” which is a reasonable and potentially more general approach. The simulation and real data experiments validate the theoretical analysis, and I believe the identified structure could be applicable to many other real-world scenarios – such as text data, as the authors note.

In summary, this paper presents a substantial contribution to the field of compressed sensing and I recommend its acceptance to NIPS. 

However, I have several points for discussion and stylistic improvement, detailed below.

1. Lines 80-82: This section requires rewriting for clarity. What is meant by "our data"? Even if the problem formulation captures all relevant structures, an additional condition is still necessary for “perfect recovery.”
2. Line 88: “…is no longer a method of perfect [what?]”. Please complete the sentence.
3. Please proofread the paper more carefully to address issues like the ones above. Be specific and precise in your claims and references within the text. In general, please prioritize clarity of language throughout the paper.
4. Wang and Xu’s paper, “Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering,” demonstrates that a higher coherence parameter can actually *benefit* subspace clustering (contrary to its effect in RPCA and matrix completion). The key distinction is that the data need not be globally low-rank; only each individual cluster needs to exhibit low-rank properties. The structure exploited in this paper can be viewed as a combination of global low-rank structure and a union-of-subspace cluster structure.
5. It would be interesting to compare the proposed algorithm against a two-step approach: first solving noisy subspace clustering with an ℓ<sub>1</sub> penalty, then performing PCA on each resulting subspace. While a provable guarantee for the latter step currently does not exist, it would provide a useful benchmark.
6. Section 4.2 essentially proposes a novel method for solving the subspace clustering problem with sparse corruption in the data. This method achieves significant improvements over standard RPCA+SSC on the Hopkins155 dataset, which I believe is an important, yet currently understated, contribution of this paper. This paper deserves acceptance based on at least three key contributions: 1) the identification of a clustering structure that can lead to high coherence; 2) a demonstration of how to implicitly exploit this structure using a dictionary within an LRR framework; and 3) a partial solution to the sparse corruption problem in subspace clustering.
