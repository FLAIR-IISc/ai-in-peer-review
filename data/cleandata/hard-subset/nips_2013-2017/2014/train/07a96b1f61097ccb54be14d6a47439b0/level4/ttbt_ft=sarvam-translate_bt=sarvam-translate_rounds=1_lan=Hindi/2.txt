This paper combines two recent techniques, robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection, in the task of reconstructing corrupted observation matrices. The main point is that robust PCA is not guaranteed to be strong enough for structured (for example, clustered) data, and strong limits can be given by representing the structure with a dictionary. Theorets are given to support these claims. The remaining problem is how to learn the dictionary. An algorithm supported by empirical results is given for this: first calculate robust PCA, then represent the result with a dictionary.

**Quality:**

This paper is technically sound to a reasonable level of scrutiny. Two theorems extend the conditions under which reconstruction is possible given a dictionary. For dictionary learning, the justification is empirical.

**Clarity:**

Clarity is the main problem of this paper. While the structure is clear, the main contribution and impact of the paper are not explained clearly enough to be accessible beyond a very narrow expert audience. The claims need to be better defined, and the impact of the results needs to be clarified. For example, the concept of coherence is difficult to understand, and the means of explaining it are very indirect (and given in the appendix). One of the potential impacts of the paper may be that many old problems can be avoided by using dictionaries. The language also needs to be examined. It would be important to get comments from the authors on what can be done about these issues.

**Originality:**

This paper is based on a combination of recent techniques, but it includes new theorems and empirical results that support the usefulness of the combination.

**Importance:**

This paper advances the state of the art with rigorous results. Even though the algorithm proposed for dictionary search is heuristic, the clear improvements over alternative algorithms show that the insights given by this paper are useful.

Commentary after the author's rebuttal and reviewer's discussion:

There are clearly publishable materials and interesting contributions. My only concern is that the paper will be difficult to rewrite to make it accessible to a very narrow expert audience. I would vote for it if there was an option for "major revision."

This paper combines two recent ideas, and gives both theoretical results and a heuristic but empirically well-performing algorithm.
