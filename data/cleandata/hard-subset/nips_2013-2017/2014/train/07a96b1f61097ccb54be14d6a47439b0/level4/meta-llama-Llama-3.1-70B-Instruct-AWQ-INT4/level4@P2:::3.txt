The paper proposes to use Low Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary A to improve the effectiveness of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA). The authors show that the incoherence parameter μ, often considered a bottleneck in the recovery guarantee in RPCA, can correspond to additional clustering structure in the data. Under certain conditions on the dictionary A, the authors demonstrate that one can exploit such structure using LRR and partially remove the dependence on μ, obtaining a theoretically stronger guarantee in both exact low-rank and sparse decomposition and its noisy extension.

The method is novel and significant to the field. It is closely related to the union-of-subspace structure assumed in previous subspace clustering papers, but is more explicit about the assumption on "clustering", which is reasonable and more probably more general. The simulation and real data experiments verify the theoretical analysis, and the new structure can be found in many other real applications.

The paper's contributions include the description of a clustering structure that leads to high coherence, an example of how one can implicitly exploit such structure by using a dictionary in LRR, and a partial solution to the sparse corruptions problem in subspace clustering. These contributions make the paper a substantial addition to the field of compressed sensing.

However, some revisions are necessary to improve the paper's clarity and precision. For instance, the description of "our data" in lines 80-82 needs rephrasing for better understanding. Additionally, the paper should be proof-read carefully to address problems with specificity and precision in claims and references.

A comparison of the proposed algorithm against first solving noisy subspace clustering with ℓ1 penalty and then performing PCA for each subspace would be interesting, as it could provide further insights into the method's effectiveness. Furthermore, the authors should highlight the significant improvements achieved by their method over the standard RPCA+SSC on the Hopkins155 dataset, as this is a notable contribution to the field.

Overall, the paper contains at least three important contributions to the field, making it a strong candidate for acceptance.
