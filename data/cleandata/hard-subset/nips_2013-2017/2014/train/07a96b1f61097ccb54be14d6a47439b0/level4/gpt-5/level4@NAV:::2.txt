This paper integrates two recent methods, robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection, to address the restoration of corrupted observation matrices. The main insight is that for structured data (e.g., clustered), the guarantees of robust PCA alone are insufficient; by encoding the structure via a dictionary, stronger bounds can be established. Theorems are provided to substantiate these claims. The remaining challenge is learning the dictionary. For this, an empirically justified algorithm is proposed: first perform robust PCA, then represent the result with a dictionary.

Quality:
The paper appears technically sound under reasonable scrutiny. The two theorems specify conditions under which reconstruction is achievable, given a dictionary. The support for the dictionary-learning component is empirical.

Clarity:
Clarity is the primary weakness. While the overall structure is clear, the key contributions and their impact are not articulated clearly enough for readers outside a narrow specialist audience. The claims should be more precisely defined, and the implications of the results made explicit. For example, the notion of coherence is difficult to grasp, and the means of interpreting it are indirect (and relegated to the appendix). One potential implication is that using dictionaries may sidestep many prior difficulties. The writing also needs language polishing. It would be important for the authors to comment on how they will address these issues.

Originality:
The work builds on a combination of recent techniques but introduces new theorems and empirical evidence that support the usefulness of the combination.

Significance:
The paper advances the state of the art with rigorous results. Although the proposed method for learning the dictionary is heuristic, clear improvements over competing algorithms indicate that the insights presented here are valuable.

Comment after the author rebuttal and reviewer discussion:
There is clearly publishable material and interesting contributions. My only remaining concern is that revising the paper to make it accessible beyond very specialized readers will require substantial effort. If a “major revision” option existed, I would choose it. The paper combines two recent ideas and provides both theoretical results and a heuristic yet empirically strong algorithm.
