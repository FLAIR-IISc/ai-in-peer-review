This paper combines robust PCA with a dictionary-based, structure-preserving projection to restore corrupted observation matrices. The core insight is that for structured data (e.g., clustered samples), standard robust PCA guarantees can be insufficient; by encoding structure via a dictionary, stronger recovery bounds are obtained. The paper presents theorems supporting these claims and proposes a practical algorithm: apply robust PCA, then represent the result using a learned dictionary. Empirical results are used to justify the dictionary-learning stage.

Quality:
The paper is technically sound to a reasonable level of scrutiny. The two main theorems articulate conditions under which accurate reconstruction is possible given an appropriate dictionary, and they clarify how structural information can improve upon vanilla robust PCA guarantees. Assumptions appear plausible but would benefit from more intuitive exposition (see Clarity). The learning procedure for the dictionary is heuristic—robust PCA followed by dictionary fitting—and is justified empirically rather than theoretically. The empirical evidence suggests the heuristic works well in practice, though the lack of theoretical guidance on dictionary learning leaves a gap between the recovery guarantees (which assume a good dictionary) and the proposed pipeline (which must learn one).

Clarity:
Clarity is the principal weakness. While the paper’s overall structure is coherent, the main contributions and their potential impact are not articulated clearly enough for a broad audience.

- The notion of coherence is difficult to parse, and its interpretation is indirect and largely relegated to the appendix. A concise, intuitive explanation in the main text—ideally with a small illustrative example—would greatly help.
- Clearly enumerate the contributions early (e.g., improved recovery guarantees under structured data via dictionaries; conditions under which these guarantees hold; a practical algorithm and empirical validation).
- State assumptions explicitly and in plain language: which structural properties must the data exhibit? What properties must the dictionary satisfy (e.g., incoherence, sparsity patterns, overcompleteness)? How do these relate to the theorems?
- Clarify the relationship to standard robust PCA guarantees: where exactly do they fail on structured data, and how do the new conditions remedy that?
- Move key interpretive content from the appendix to the main text, especially intuition for the bounds, the role of coherence, and practical regimes of interest.
- Improve language and proofreading; several typographical errors and awkward phrasings hinder readability.
- Provide more guidance for practitioners: how to select dictionary size and regularization; sensitivity to hyperparameters; computational complexity and runtime.

Originality:
The work builds on two recent lines—robust PCA and dictionary-based projections—but the combination is nontrivial and is supported by new theorems and empirical results. The framing that structural priors encoded via dictionaries can tighten recovery bounds over robust PCA alone is a meaningful conceptual contribution. To strengthen the originality claim, better positioning against closely related approaches (e.g., structured sparsity in low-rank plus sparse models, subspace clustering with corruption, union-of-subspaces robust recovery) would be helpful.

Significance:
The paper advances the state of the art by providing rigorous results under structured data models and by demonstrating a practical algorithm with improved performance over alternatives. Even though the dictionary-learning step is heuristic, the reported empirical gains indicate that the proposed perspective is useful in practice and may allow one to sidestep limitations of standard robust PCA. The main caveat is the reliance on a good dictionary: without stronger theoretical or practical guidance on learning it, the applicability of the guarantees remains partly aspirational. Nonetheless, the combination of theory and empirics suggests real impact for applications where structure is prominent (e.g., clustered or union-of-subspaces data).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide an intuitive, self-contained explanation of the coherence measures and their operational meaning; include a toy example linking coherence to recovery.
- Summarize theorems with clear, interpretable conditions and implications, and contrast them directly with classic robust PCA assumptions.
- Offer practical guidance for dictionary learning within this framework: initialization, regularization, stopping criteria, and diagnostics for when a learned dictionary is “good enough.”
- Include ablations isolating the contributions of robust PCA and the dictionary step; analyze sensitivity to dictionary size and sparsity.
- Report computational complexity and runtime comparisons; discuss scalability to larger matrices.
- Strengthen related work to delineate differences from prior structured robust recovery methods.

Comment after the author rebuttal and reviewer discussion:
The paper contains publishable content and interesting contributions, both in theory and in a practical, empirically strong algorithm. My remaining concern is accessibility: substantial rewriting is needed to make the ideas clear beyond a narrow specialist audience. If a “major revision” option were available, I would recommend it. In its current form, the work is promising and technically solid but would benefit significantly from improved exposition to maximize its impact.
