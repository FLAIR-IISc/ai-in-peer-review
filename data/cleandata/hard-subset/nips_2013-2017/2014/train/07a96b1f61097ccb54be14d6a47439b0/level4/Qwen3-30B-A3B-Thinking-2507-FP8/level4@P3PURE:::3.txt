

The paper introduces a novel approach leveraging Low Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary $ \mathbf{A} $ to enhance Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA). The key insight demonstrates that the incoherence parameter $ \mu $, typically viewed as a bottleneck in RPCA recovery guarantees, can correspond to an inherent clustering structure in the data. Under specific conditions on $ \mathbf{A} $, the authors show that this structure can be exploited via LRR to reduce dependence on $ \mu $, yielding theoretically stronger guarantees for both exact low-rank and sparse decomposition and their noisy extensions.  

The method is significant and well-motivated. It builds on prior subspace clustering work (e.g., Wang and Xu, "Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering"; Soltanolkotabi and Candes, "A geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers"; Soltanolkotabi et al., "Robust subspace clustering"), but explicitly characterizes the clustering structure underlying high coherence—a refinement that is both reasonable and likely more general. Experimental results on synthetic and real data (including text data, as noted in the paper) corroborate the theoretical analysis, suggesting broad applicability.  

The paper makes a substantial contribution to subspace clustering and should be accepted. However, the following points require attention:  

1.  **Lines 80–82**: Clarify the reference to "our data" and explicitly state that even with the proposed formulation, an additional condition is required for perfect recovery.  
2.  **Line 88**: Correct the fragment "is no longer a method of perfect (WHAT?)" to "is no longer a method for perfect recovery."  
3.  **General clarity**: Proofread for precision. Ensure all claims (e.g., conditions for recovery, references to prior work) are explicitly and unambiguously stated.  
4.  **Comparison to Wang & Xu**: Note that Wang and Xu observe higher coherence can benefit subspace clustering (unlike RPCA), as their setting requires only *cluster-wise* low-rank structure, not global low-rankness. The current paper’s approach combines low-rank and union-of-subspace clustering structures.  
5.  **Algorithmic comparison**: It would be valuable to compare against a two-step method (e.g., first solving noisy subspace clustering with $ \ell_1 $-penalty, then applying PCA per subspace), though no provable guarantees exist for the latter.  
6.  **Section 4.2**: The proposed subspace clustering method with sparse corruption (validated on Hopkins155) represents a significant hidden contribution. The authors should explicitly highlight this as a key advancement beyond standard RPCA+SSC.  

In summary, the paper delivers at least three important contributions: (1) a characterization of clustering structure that induces high coherence; (2) a demonstration that LRR with a dictionary implicitly exploits this structure; and (3) a practical solution to sparse corruption in subspace clustering. These contributions substantially advance the field, and the paper merits acceptance.
