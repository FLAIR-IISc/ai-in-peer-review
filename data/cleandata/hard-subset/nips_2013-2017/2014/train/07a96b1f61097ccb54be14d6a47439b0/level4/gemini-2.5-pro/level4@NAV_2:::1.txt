This paper addresses the problem of recovering a low-rank matrix from grossly corrupted observations, focusing on the challenging scenario where the underlying data is "coherent." The authors correctly identify that the performance of state-of-the-art methods like Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) degrades as data coherence increases, a phenomenon they link to the presence of clustering structures, which are common in real-world data. The central contribution is to show, both theoretically and empirically, that this limitation can be overcome by leveraging the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework. Specifically, the paper proves that if a low-rank dictionary is used whose column space contains that of the true low-rank data, the LRR recovery guarantee becomes immune to the problematic coherence parameters. Based on this insight, the authors propose a practical two-stage algorithm: first, an initial estimate of the low-rank component is obtained via RPCA, which is then used to construct a suitable dictionary for a final LRR recovery step.

**Strengths (Pro-Acceptance):**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a well-known and important limitation of RPCA. The problem of handling coherent data arising from multiple underlying subspaces is highly relevant for numerous applications, such as motion segmentation, making the contribution significant to both theoreticians and practitioners.
*   **Originality and Quality:** The connection established between data coherence, clustering structures, and the properties of the LRR dictionary is novel. The theoretical analysis is sound, providing a clear justification for why the proposed approach works by making the recovery bound independent of the `µ2` parameter. The proposed Algorithm 1 is an elegant and intuitive practical realization of this theoretical insight.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, particularly the explanation of the "µ2-phenomenon," which helps the reader understand the core challenge being addressed.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The claims are convincingly supported by a thorough set of experiments. The phase transition plots on synthetic data provide a clear quantitative comparison against RPCA, demonstrating a substantially larger region of successful recovery. The results on corrupted motion sequences further validate the method's effectiveness in a realistic setting, showing a significant reduction in clustering error for downstream tasks.

**Weaknesses (Minor Points for Improvement):**

*   The paper effectively demonstrates the proposed method as a data pre-processing step to improve the performance of existing subspace clustering algorithms. Given that the LRR framework (typically with A=X) is itself a prominent method for subspace clustering, it would be interesting if the authors could comment on whether the learned coefficient matrix Z* from their final LRR step could be used directly for clustering. This might offer a more integrated approach to robust subspace clustering.
*   The success of the proposed two-stage algorithm seems to depend on the initial RPCA step providing a reasonably good estimate. A brief discussion on the potential failure modes would be beneficial. For instance, in regimes of extreme coherence or corruption where the initial RPCA result is very far from the ground truth, how does the overall algorithm perform?

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a theoretically sound and practically effective solution to an important problem in robust low-rank matrix recovery. The contributions are significant, original, and well-validated. The identified weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall strength of the work. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
