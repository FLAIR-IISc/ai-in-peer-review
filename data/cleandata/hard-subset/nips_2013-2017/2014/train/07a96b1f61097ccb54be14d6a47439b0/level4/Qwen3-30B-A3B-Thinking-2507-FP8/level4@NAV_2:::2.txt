

This paper addresses the limitations of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) for recovering low-rank matrices from corrupted observations when data exhibit clustering structures. The authors demonstrate that RPCA's performance degrades as data coherence increases with the number of underlying clusters, and propose a solution using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully constructed dictionary. The paper establishes theoretical guarantees showing that LRR with a low-rank dictionary can circumvent coherence-related limitations, and provides a practical unsupervised algorithm for dictionary learning that outperforms RPCA in experiments. The work connects to prior NIPS contributions on RPCA [4] and LRR [13], while extending them to address coherence challenges in structured data.

Quality: The paper presents technically sound theoretical results with two theorems establishing recovery conditions under specific dictionary constraints. The theoretical framework is rigorous and well-motivated, though the dictionary learning algorithm relies primarily on empirical validation rather than theoretical guarantees. The experimental results on synthetic data and motion sequences provide convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness.

Clarity: The paper's main challenge is accessibility. While the technical structure is logical, the core contributions and their broader significance are not clearly articulated for a general machine learning audience. Key concepts like coherence lack intuitive explanations and are not adequately contextualized within the main text (with most interpretation relegated to the appendix). The writing contains grammatical issues that impede readability. The paper would benefit significantly from more explicit discussion of how the coherence parameter relates to real-world data structures and why the proposed approach represents a meaningful advancement over existing methods.

Originality: The work combines established techniques (RPCA and LRR) in a novel way to address a specific limitation in structured data recovery. The theoretical contributions (Theorems 1 and 2) provide new insights into how dictionary structure affects recovery performance, and the proposed dictionary learning algorithm represents a meaningful extension of prior LRR work [13, 14].

Significance: The paper makes a valuable contribution to the robust matrix recovery literature by addressing a previously overlooked limitation of RPCA. The theoretical results provide a principled approach to handling coherent data, and the empirical improvements over RPCA in both synthetic and real-world motion segmentation tasks demonstrate practical significance. The insights about coherence parameters and their relationship to data structure offer important theoretical contributions to the field.

Comment after the author rebuttal and reviewer discussion: The paper contains publishable content with interesting theoretical insights and practical improvements. The main remaining concern is the need for substantial revisions to improve clarity and accessibility for a broader audience. If the authors can significantly enhance the exposition of key concepts (particularly coherence) and better articulate the paper's contributions, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance. Given the current state, I recommend major revision.
