

The paper introduces a novel approach using Low Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary \( \mathbf{A} \) to enhance Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA). The authors demonstrate that the incoherence parameter \( \mu \), traditionally viewed as a bottleneck in RPCA recovery guarantees, can correspond to an inherent clustering structure within the data. Under specific conditions on the dictionary \( \mathbf{A} \), they show that LRR can exploit this structure to partially alleviate dependence on \( \mu \), yielding theoretically stronger guarantees for both exact low-rank and sparse decomposition and its noisy extension.  

The method is significant and novel, building upon union-of-subspace structures in prior subspace clustering work (e.g., Wang and Xu: "Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering," "Provable subspace clustering: When LRR meets SSC"; Soltanolkotabi and Candes: "A geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers"; Soltanolkotabi et al.: "Robust subspace clustering"). While previous works implicitly assume clustering structure, this paper explicitly characterizes its role in enabling high coherence, which is both reasonable and likely more general. Simulation and real-data experiments validate the theoretical analysis, and the authors note that this structure may be prevalent in other applications (e.g., text data).  

In summary, the paper makes a substantial contribution to compressed sensing and should be accepted. However, the following points require attention:  
1. Lines 80–82: Clarify the phrase "our data" and explicitly state that even with captured structures, an additional condition is required for "perfect recovery."  
2. Line 88: Correct the phrase "is no longer a method of perfect recovery."  
3. Proofread for precision: Ensure all claims are specific and unambiguous (e.g., clarify references to "clustering" and "recovery").  
4. Wang and Xu’s work ("Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering") establishes that higher coherence benefits subspace clustering (unlike RPCA/matrix completion), as data need not be globally low-rank—only per-cluster low-rank. The paper’s structure combines low-rank and union-of-subspace clustering, a key distinction.  
5. Comparing the proposed method against solving noisy subspace clustering via \( \ell_1 \)-penalized SSC followed by per-subspace PCA would be insightful, though no provable guarantees exist for the latter.  
6. Section 4.2 proposes a new method for subspace clustering with sparse corruption, achieving significant improvements over RPCA+SSC on the Hopkins155 dataset. This constitutes a hidden contribution: the paper should explicitly highlight it.  

The paper merits acceptance for at least three key contributions: (1) characterizing clustering structures that induce high coherence; (2) demonstrating how LRR with a dictionary implicitly exploits this structure; and (3) advancing the solution to sparse corruption in subspace clustering.
