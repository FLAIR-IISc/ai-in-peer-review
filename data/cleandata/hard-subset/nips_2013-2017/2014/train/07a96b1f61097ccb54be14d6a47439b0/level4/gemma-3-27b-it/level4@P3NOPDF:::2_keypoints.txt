- Proposes a novel method that combines robust PCA with dictionary-based structure-preserving projection to restore corrupted observation matrices.
- Argues that leveraging an explicit dictionary strengthens recovery guarantees where standard robust PCA bounds fail.
- Provides theoretical analysis with theorems that give conditions for successful reconstruction using a learned dictionary.
- Proposes a practical two-step algorithm: run robust PCA, then learn a dictionary representation of the recovered low-rank component.
- Quality: technically sound and well-motivated theorems, but the dictionary-learning step lacks rigorous theoretical justification and relies on empirical validation.
- Empirical results are encouraging and indicate practical effectiveness, but stronger theory for the learning phase would improve the contribution.
- Clarity: presentation is weak—paper is targeted to specialists, central claims and impacts are not communicated clearly, and key concepts like “coherence” lack intuitive explanation.
- Writing needs careful editing and relocation of essential explanations from the appendix to the main text to improve accessibility.
- Originality: non-trivial and meaningful integration of robust PCA and dictionary methods, yielding synergistic theoretical and empirical advances.
- Significance: advances matrix recovery theory and shows practical performance gains, but substantial rewriting is needed to make the work accessible to a broader ML audience.
- Post-rebuttal: contributions are publishable and interesting, but recommend a major revision to improve clarity and accessibility.
