The paper proposes using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary A to strengthen RPCA. In particular, the authors argue that the incoherence parameter μ—often viewed as the bottleneck in RPCA recovery guarantees—can actually reflect additional clustering structure in the data. Under appropriate conditions on the dictionary A, they show that LRR can exploit this structure to partially remove the dependence on μ, yielding stronger theoretical guarantees for both exact low-rank-plus-sparse decomposition and its noisy extension.

The method is novel and significant. It is closely related to the union-of-subspaces structure assumed in prior subspace clustering work, such as Wang and Xu (“Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering” and “Provable Subspace Clustering: When LRR Meets SSC”), Soltanolkotabi and Candès (“A Geometric Analysis of Subspace Clustering with Outliers”), and Soltanolkotabi et al. (“Robust Subspace Clustering”), but this paper is less explicit about a “clustering” assumption—which seems reasonable and likely more general. The simulations and real-data experiments support the theory, and the proposed structure likely appears in many other real applications (e.g., text data, as noted in the paper).

In summary, the paper makes a substantial contribution to compressed sensing, and I recommend acceptance to NIPS.

That said, I have several comments and stylistic suggestions:

1. Lines 80–82: Please revise for clarity. What exactly does “our data” refer to? Also, even if the model captures all relevant structure, an additional condition is still required to ensure “perfect recovery.”

2. Line 88: “… is no longer a method of perfect [WHAT?].” The sentence is incomplete; please complete or rephrase.

3. Please proofread the paper carefully for issues like the above. Be specific and precise about claims and references within the text. Overall, the exposition would benefit from clearer language.

4. Wang and Xu note in “Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering” that higher coherence can actually help subspace clustering (unlike in RPCA and matrix completion). The key difference here is that the data need not be globally low-rank; only each cluster must be low-rank. The structure exploited in this paper can be viewed as combining global low-rank structure with a union-of-subspaces clustering structure.

5. It would be informative to compare the proposed method against a pipeline that first performs noisy subspace clustering with an ℓ1 penalty, followed by PCA within each subspace. While no provable guarantees are known for the latter, the empirical comparison would be valuable.

6. Section 4.2 effectively proposes a new approach to subspace clustering with sparse corruption, yielding significant improvements over the standard RPCA+SSC pipeline on the Hopkins155 dataset. This seems like a hidden but important contribution and should be highlighted. The paper merits acceptance for at least three contributions: (i) articulating a clustering structure that induces high coherence, (ii) showing that such structure can be implicitly exploited via a dictionary in LRR, and (iii) partially addressing sparse corruptions in subspace clustering.
