Summary
This paper studies recovery of low-rank matrices from grossly corrupted observations when the data are coherent, e.g., clustered. The authors argue that RPCA’s guarantees weaken as the number of clusters increases because the second (and third) coherence parameters grow. They propose addressing this with Low-Rank Representation (LRR), showing that if the dictionary A is itself low-rank and its column space contains the true column space, then LRR can recover the low-rank component without depending on the problematic coherence parameter. The paper provides recovery theorems for both noiseless and noisy settings. Since an appropriate dictionary may be unknown, the authors introduce a simple unsupervised procedure: first apply RPCA to get an estimate of the low-rank component, use this estimate to build a normalized low-rank dictionary, and then solve LRR. Experiments on synthetic data and corrupted motion trajectories demonstrate improved recovery and downstream clustering performance relative to RPCA.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on RPCA/PCP and LRR, and builds on the coherence framework standard in matrix recovery. It connects the growth of μ2 with clustered data, clarifying when RPCA struggles, and analyzes LRR with general dictionaries (beyond A = X), which is less explored.

Strengths
- Clear identification of a practically important failure mode for RPCA (cluster-induced coherence).
- Theoretical guarantees showing LRR with a low-rank dictionary can avoid the μ2 dependence, with results for both noiseless and noisy cases.
- A practical, unsupervised algorithm that leverages RPCA’s partial success to construct a suitable dictionary for LRR.
- Empirical improvements over RPCA on both synthetic and real motion data, with meaningful downstream benefits for subspace clustering.

Weaknesses
- Exposition is difficult to follow for non-specialists. The role of coherence and its connection to clustering require clearer intuition and earlier, more accessible explanations.
- The key assumption U0 ⊂ UA (and low rank of A) is necessary for the theorems but its practical attainability and sensitivity are only informally addressed.
- The dictionary-learning procedure is heuristic; theoretical analysis of its success probability or conditions would strengthen the contribution.
- Experimental scope could be broader (e.g., sensitivity to cluster number, dictionary rank, noise levels; runtime comparisons; additional baselines such as Outlier Pursuit or variants of LRR).

Quality
The technical claims are plausible and appear sound at a reasonable level of scrutiny. Theorems clearly state conditions under which LRR achieves exact or near recovery without depending on μ2, given a suitable dictionary. The unsupervised dictionary-learning step is justified empirically rather than theoretically; this is acceptable but leaves a gap between the theoretical insight and the practical algorithm. The experimental results are consistent with the theory and demonstrate meaningful gains.

Clarity
Clarity is the main issue. The paper’s structure is reasonable, but the narrative is dense, and the main messages and implications are not made sufficiently accessible. Suggestions:
- Provide an early, intuitive explanation of coherence parameters, especially μ2, with concrete toy examples showing how clustering inflates μ2.
- Clearly separate what the theorems guarantee (given A) from what the algorithm aims to achieve (constructing A), and discuss failure modes when U0 ⊄ UA.
- Summarize assumptions and their practical plausibility in a dedicated subsection.
- Tighten language and reduce reliance on appendices for core intuition; improve notation recap and figure captions.

Originality
The combination of insights is novel: analyzing LRR with low-rank dictionaries to circumvent μ2 in coherent data, and linking coherence growth to clustering. Theoretical conditions for dictionary-based recovery beyond A = X add new understanding to LRR. The proposed unsupervised pipeline is simple but effective.

Significance
The contribution is significant for robust matrix recovery and subspace learning in clustered data regimes. The theoretical results clarify when RPCA degrades and how to mitigate it. The practical algorithm shows improved recovery and downstream clustering performance, suggesting that dictionary-based formulations can broaden the applicability of robust low-rank recovery.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and common setting (clustered data) where RPCA weakens.
- New theory for LRR with low-rank dictionaries avoiding μ2 dependence.
- Practical algorithm with clear empirical gains on synthetic and real data.
- Provides insight into the meaning of coherence in practice.

Cons for acceptance
- Exposition needs substantial revision to be broadly accessible.
- Dictionary-learning step lacks theoretical guarantees; dependence on U0 ⊂ UA is strong.
- Experimental evaluation could be expanded with sensitivity analyses and additional baselines.

Requested clarifications/improvements
- Add intuitive examples and diagrams illustrating coherence growth with clustering.
- Analyze sensitivity to dictionary rank, mismatch in U0 ⊂ UA, and parameter λ.
- Report runtime comparisons and convergence behavior; include ablations (e.g., RPCA-only vs. RPCA+LRR with/without normalization).
- Compare against more baselines (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, alternative LRR dictionaries such as A = X).

Overall recommendation
The paper contains solid theoretical contributions and a practical method that improves over RPCA on coherent data. With a significant rewrite emphasizing intuition, assumptions, and practical takeaways, it would be a strong contribution.

Comment after the author rebuttal and reviewer discussion
The paper has clear, publishable contributions. My remaining concern is that making the presentation accessible beyond specialists will require substantial revision. If a “major revision” option existed, I would recommend it. In its current form, the paper combines two timely ideas and offers both theoretical results and a heuristic yet empirically strong algorithm.
