- Summary: Paper studies recovery of low-rank matrices with sparse corruptions under clustering-induced coherence and shows LRR can avoid dependence on μ2 with a low-rank dictionary A whose column space contains the true subspace U0.  
- Summary (algorithm): Proposes practical pipeline: run RPCA, form low-rank column-normalized dictionary A from its output, then solve LRR; shows improved recovery on synthetic and motion data.  

- Theory: Provides noiseless and noisy recovery guarantees for LRR that do not depend on μ2 when U0 ⊂ UA, with explicit rank(A) and sparsity bounds and λ = 1/√n1.  
- Theory (note): Rank bound is looser (O(n2/log n1)) than typical RPCA bounds, which is noteworthy.  

- Algorithm: Dictionary-learning heuristic (RPCA → low-rank A → LRR) is reasonable and empirically effective; runtime claims plausible given low-rank A.  
- Empirics: Synthetic and motion segmentation experiments show substantially larger exact-recovery region and reduced clustering errors versus RPCA, LRR(A=X), SIM, and SSC.  

- Clarity (main weakness): Manuscript is hard to follow for non-specialists; coherence parameters μ1, μ2, μ3 and the μ2-phenomenon need more accessible explanations and intuition.  
- Clarity (assumptions): Must state when U0 ⊂ UA can be approximately satisfied, provide robustness bounds or sensitivity to subspace mismatch and dictionary conditioning.  
- Clarity (claims): Clarify main contributions up front and distinguish novel results from prior work (RPCA, original LRR).  
- Clarity (presentation): Language, figures, references, and parameter guidance (λ, ε) need polishing; clarify role of column normalization.  

- Originality: Novel theoretical guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries avoiding μ2, connection between clustering and coherence, and a practical RPCA-based dictionary pipeline—more than a straightforward combination.  

- Significance: Addresses an important practical problem (coherent data); theoretical and empirical results offer a principled and useful approach for robust subspace learning and motion segmentation.  

- Pros for acceptance: Strong theory avoiding μ2, clear analysis of RPCA failure due to clustering, simple practical algorithm with empirical gains, and potential practical impact.  
- Cons for acceptance: Major clarity/exposition issues, heuristic dictionary learning lacking failure-mode analysis, and limited experimental scope (parameter sensitivity, datasets, ablations).  

- Suggestions: Add intuitive visualizations for coherence, quantify robustness to U0 ⊂ UA violations, include parameter-sensitivity and ablation studies, and improve writing/organization and relation to prior LRR variants.  

- Final remark: Work is publishable and contributes meaningful theory and algorithms, but requires substantial rewriting to be accessible beyond specialists (recommend major revision).
