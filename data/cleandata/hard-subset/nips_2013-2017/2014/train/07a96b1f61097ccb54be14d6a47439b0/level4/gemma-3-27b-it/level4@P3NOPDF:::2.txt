This paper presents a novel approach to restoring corrupted observation matrices by combining robust PCA with dictionary-based structure-preserving projection. The core idea is that leveraging inherent data structure, through the use of a dictionary, can strengthen the recovery guarantees offered by robust PCA, particularly in scenarios where standard robust PCA bounds are insufficient. The authors support this claim with theoretical analysis, presenting theorems that delineate conditions for successful reconstruction given a learned dictionary. A practical algorithm for dictionary learning is also proposed, consisting of initial robust PCA followed by dictionary representation of the resulting low-rank component.

**Quality:**

The paper demonstrates technical soundness, with the two presented theorems providing formal conditions for reconstruction. The theoretical contributions appear well-motivated and reasonably justified. However, the dictionary learning procedure relies primarily on empirical validation, which is a notable limitation. While the empirical results are encouraging, a more rigorous theoretical justification for this learning step would strengthen the overall contribution.

**Clarity:**

Clarity is the primary concern. While the overall structure of the paper is logical, the core contributions and potential impact are not communicated effectively to a broader audience. The paper is currently geared towards a very specialized readership already deeply familiar with both robust PCA and dictionary learning. Several areas require significant improvement: the central claims need to be more precisely defined, and the implications of the results should be clearly explicated. Specifically, the concept of “coherence” is introduced without sufficient intuitive explanation, and its interpretation relies on information relegated to the appendix. A clearer explanation of how leveraging dictionaries overcomes limitations in existing approaches would significantly enhance understanding. It is possible that this work sidesteps significant challenges in the field, and this potential impact deserves greater emphasis. Furthermore, the language throughout the paper requires careful editing for clarity and precision. A response from the authors addressing these clarity concerns would be valuable.

**Originality:**

The work presents a meaningful combination of existing techniques – robust PCA and dictionary-based methods – and extends them with new theoretical results and empirical validation. This integration is not merely a trivial combination; the authors demonstrate a synergistic effect, achieving improvements through this combined approach.

**Significance:**

This paper advances the state-of-the-art in matrix completion and robust recovery with rigorous theoretical insights. The proposed algorithm, while heuristic in its dictionary learning phase, demonstrates clear performance improvements over alternative approaches, indicating the practical utility of the insights presented. The empirical results strongly suggest that the theoretical framework translates to tangible benefits in real-world applications.

My remaining concern centers on the substantial effort required to revise the paper to ensure accessibility for specialists in the field. If a “major revision” option were available, I would recommend it, as substantial rewriting will be necessary to effectively communicate the contributions to a wider audience within the machine learning community.
