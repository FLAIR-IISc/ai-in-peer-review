The paper advocates using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary A to enhance RPCA. Specifically, the authors argue that the incoherence parameter μ, commonly viewed as a limiting factor for RPCA recovery guarantees, can actually reflect an underlying clustering structure in the data. Under appropriate conditions on the dictionary A, they demonstrate that LRR can leverage this structure to reduce the dependence on μ, yielding stronger theoretical guarantees for both exact low-rank plus sparse decomposition and its noisy counterpart.

The approach is novel and impactful. It is closely connected to the union-of-subspaces model used in prior subspace clustering work such as “Wang and Xu: Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering & Provable subspace clustering: When LRR meets SSC,” “Soltanolkotabi and Candès: A geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers,” and “Soltanolkotabi et al.: Robust subspace clustering,” but it is less explicit about the precise “clustering” assumption, which seems reasonable and potentially more general. The simulations and real-data experiments support the theory, and the proposed structure likely appears in many practical settings (e.g., text data, as noted in the paper).

In sum, this paper makes a substantial contribution to compressed sensing and should be accepted to NIPS.

That said, I have several discussion points and stylistic suggestions, summarized below.
1. Lines 80–82: This passage should be clarified. What exactly does “our data” mean? Moreover, even if the formulation captures all relevant structure, an additional condition is still required to certify “perfect recovery.”
2. Line 88: “… is no longer a method of perfect (WHAT?).” Please complete or clarify this statement.
3. Please proofread carefully for issues like the above. Be precise about claims and references in the text. Overall, the clarity of the writing should be improved.
4. Wang and Xu also note in “Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering” that larger coherence can benefit subspace clustering (in contrast to RPCA and matrix completion). The key difference is that the global data matrix need not be low-rank; only each cluster is low-rank. The structure leveraged here can be viewed as a blend of global low-rank structure and a union-of-subspaces clustering structure.
5. It would be interesting to compare against a baseline that first performs noisy subspace clustering with an ℓ1 penalty and then applies PCA within each subspace. There is, to date, no formal guarantee for the latter stage, however.
6. Section 4.2 effectively introduces a new approach for subspace clustering with sparse corruptions. It significantly outperforms the standard RPCA+SSC pipeline on the Hopkins155 dataset, which I view as an important, somewhat hidden contribution that should be emphasized. This paper merits acceptance because it contains at least three key contributions: (1) articulating a clustering structure that induces high coherence, (2) showing that such structure can be implicitly exploited via a dictionary in LRR, and (3) partially addressing sparse corruptions in subspace clustering.
