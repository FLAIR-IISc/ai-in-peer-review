This paper combines two recent techniques, robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projections, to recover corrupted observation matrices. The main insight is that for structured (for example, clustered) data, the robustness of robust PCA is not strong enough, and a stronger bound can be given by representing the structure through a dictionary. Theorets are provided to support these claims. The remaining problem is how the dictionary should be learned. An algorithm is provided for this, which is supported by empirical results: first compute robust PCA, then represent the results by a dictionary.

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound, up to a reasonable level of verification. The two theorems detail the conditions under which reconstruction is possible given a dictionary. For dictionary learning, the rationality is empirical.

**Clarity:**

Clarity is the main problem of this paper. Although the structure is clear, the paper's main contribution and impact are not clearly explained, making it incomprehensible even to very narrow expert audiences. The claims need to be better defined and the impact of the results needs to be explained. For example, the concept of coherence is difficult to understand, and the ways to explain it are very indirect (and are given in the appendix). One of the potential impacts of the paper could be that many earlier problems can be avoided by using a dictionary. Also, some aspects of the language need to be verified. It is important to get a comment from the authors on these problems.

**Originality:**

The paper is based on a combination of recent techniques, but new theorems and empirical results support the usefulness of this combination.

**Importance:**

The paper advances sophistication through rigorous results. Although the algorithm proposed for finding a dictionary is heuristic, the clear improvements over alternative algorithms prove that the insights given in this paper are effective.
