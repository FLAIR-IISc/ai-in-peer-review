Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies robust low-rank matrix recovery when the low-rank component exhibits clustering structure, leading to high coherence. The authors argue—and empirically illustrate—that RPCA’s guarantees and performance degrade as the number of clusters increases because the second (and third) coherence parameters grow (the “µ2-phenomenon”). They propose addressing this by replacing the equality constraint X = L + S in RPCA with X = AZ + S (Low-Rank Representation, LRR), and by choosing a low-rank dictionary A whose column space contains the true column space of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA). Under these conditions, they prove exact (noiseless) and near-exact (noisy) recovery guarantees for LRR that avoid dependence on µ2 (and µ3), relying only on µ1(A). They show that the rank bound improves from O(n2/(log n1)2) for RPCA to O(n2/log n1) for their approach.

Beyond the theoretical results, the paper proposes a practical unsupervised procedure: first estimate L0 with RPCA, construct a well-conditioned low-rank dictionary by normalizing the columns of a truncated SVD approximation of the RPCA estimate, and then solve LRR with this learned dictionary. Experiments on synthetic data and motion trajectories (Hopkins sequences with added corruption) show consistent improvements over RPCA, including downstream gains for subspace clustering methods such as SSC, SIM, and LRR with A = X.

Quality:
The paper is technically solid. The analysis clarifies when and why RPCA fails for coherent data and provides recovery guarantees for LRR with general, low-rank dictionaries. The dependence on µ1(A) rather than µ2(L0) directly targets the identified failure mode. The noiseless and noisy theorems are clearly stated, and the conditions (column-wise unit norm for A, U0 ⊂ UA, bounds on rank(A), and sparsity |Ω|) are interpretable. The unsupervised algorithm is sensible: if RPCA is “good enough,” it provides supervision to build a dictionary satisfying U0 ⊂ UA approximately, which in turn improves recovery.

A few aspects merit further discussion or validation:
- The condition U0 ⊂ UA is necessary for exact recovery, but in practice the learned dictionary only approximates this. It would help to quantify how sensitive recovery is to violations of U0 ⊂ UA (e.g., bounds in terms of subspace angles).
- The method’s first step depends on RPCA; when RPCA is poor (very high coherence, high corruption), how robust is the pipeline? An ablation showing performance versus the quality/conditioning of A would strengthen the empirical case.
- Experimental breadth could be increased. The real-data study uses 11 motion sequences with synthetic ±1 replacements for missing entries; more realistic noise/missingness and additional domains (e.g., recommendation, surveillance videos) would reinforce significance. Comparisons to other robust subspace recovery methods (e.g., Outlier Pursuit) would also be informative.
- Implementation details such as dictionary normalization when columns are (near-)zero, and guidance on selecting ε in the noisy case, could be clarified. The “47% wider” success region on synthetic data is compelling; stating the exact success criterion would improve reproducibility.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-organized and readable. The exposition of the coherence parameters and the “µ2-phenomenon” is helpful and ties physical structure (clustering) to the mathematical quantities. Some notation is heavy (e.g., overloading of U, V, Ω), and a compact summary of assumptions before each theorem would aid readability. A brief intuitive explanation of why a low-rank A mitigates µ2’s effect would also help non-experts.

Originality:
The key ideas are original and valuable: (i) linking clustering to increases in µ2 and RPCA’s degradation, (ii) showing that LRR with a properly chosen low-rank dictionary avoids dependence on µ2, and (iii) proposing an unsupervised pipeline to learn such a dictionary from data. While LRR and RPCA are established, analysis of LRR with general low-rank dictionaries (beyond A = I or A = X) and its immunity to µ2 in this setting represents a meaningful contribution.

Significance:
The results matter. Coherent data are ubiquitous; alleviating RPCA’s sensitivity to coherence addresses a real limitation. Theoretical guarantees and an implementable algorithm together increase the likelihood of adoption. The downstream improvements in subspace clustering highlight practical impact.

Pros:
- Clear diagnosis of RPCA’s failure mode on clustered data via the µ2-phenomenon.
- Theoretical recovery guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that avoid µ2, in both noiseless and noisy settings.
- Practical, unsupervised algorithm that leverages RPCA to learn A and consistently improves recovery and downstream clustering.
- Empirical validation on synthetic and motion data; improvement over standard baselines.

Cons:
- The U0 ⊂ UA assumption is strong; empirical sensitivity to approximate satisfaction is not quantified.
- Reliance on RPCA to build A raises questions about robustness when RPCA underperforms.
- Real-data experiments are limited in scope; noise model and dataset diversity could be expanded.
- Some constants, parameter choices, and implementation details (e.g., normalization, ε) could be better justified.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Can Z* (or an affinity derived from Z*) obtained with the learned low-rank dictionary be used directly for subspace clustering, rather than only as a preprocessing step? How does it compare to LRR with A = X and to SSC in terms of clustering accuracy and computational cost?
- Please include sensitivity analyses to the dictionary’s rank and conditioning, and to deviations from U0 ⊂ UA (e.g., principal angle perturbations).
- Consider broader and more realistic experiments (e.g., other vision datasets, video background modeling, recommendation data) and comparisons to additional robust subspace recovery baselines.

Recommendation:
This paper addresses an important limitation of RPCA with clear theoretical insight, a practical algorithm, and promising empirical results. I recommend acceptance.
