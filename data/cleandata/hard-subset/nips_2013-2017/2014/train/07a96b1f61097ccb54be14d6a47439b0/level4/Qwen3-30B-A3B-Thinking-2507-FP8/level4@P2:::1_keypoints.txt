- Paper identifies a fundamental RPCA limitation: clustering increases coherence (µ2, µ3), degrading recovery even for strictly low-rank data.
- Authors rigorously characterize the "µ2-phenomenon" showing coherence grows with cluster number.
- Main theoretical contribution: LRR with a properly constructed low-rank dictionary avoids the coherence bottleneck.
- They prove that if dictionary A is low-rank and contains L0's column space (U0 ⊂ UA), LRR exactly recovers L0 and S0 independent of RPCA coherence parameters.
- Practical contribution: an unsupervised algorithm that learns such a dictionary using RPCA outputs.
- Empirical results: significant gains over RPCA on synthetic data and a 40% relative improvement in clustering error on Hopkins155.
- Strong positioning in literature: extends Candès et al. (2011) RPCA and Liu et al. (2013) LRR, connects to recent subspace clustering and robust matrix completion work.
- Theoretical analysis and proofs (Theorems 1 and 2) are rigorous and well-structured.
- Experimental design is thorough with appropriate baselines, metrics, and both synthetic and real-world tests.
- Presentation is clear: precise coherence definitions, consistent notation, and well-motivated proofs and algorithm description.
- Originality: first formal analysis linking clustering to coherence parameters and proposing LRR with low-rank dictionary as a principled solution.
- Significance: addresses a practical RPCA shortcoming with a theoretically grounded, computationally practical method (≈1.2x RPCA runtime).
- Weakness: limited discussion/analysis of when the unsupervised dictionary learning algorithm might fail.
- Weakness: brief treatment of how the approach would specifically improve existing subspace clustering methods like SSC or LRRx.
- Pro-accept: resolves a recognized RPCA limitation, offers theoretical and practical advances, and shows clear empirical improvements.
- Con-accept: minor theoretical gaps on failure cases and incomplete development of subspace clustering applications.
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — significant, well-executed contribution to robust low-rank recovery.
