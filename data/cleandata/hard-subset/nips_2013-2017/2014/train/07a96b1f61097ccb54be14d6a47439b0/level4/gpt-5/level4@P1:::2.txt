Summary
The paper studies recovery of low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse gross errors when the data exhibit clustering structure (i.e., are coherent). While RPCA (Candès et al., 2011) gives exact recovery guarantees under incoherence, the authors show that clustering increases the second (and third) coherence parameters (μ2, μ3), degrading RPCA. They analyze this “μ2-phenomenon” and prove that Low-Rank Representation (LRR; Liu et al., 2013) can avoid dependence on μ2 if the dictionary A is appropriately chosen: specifically, if A is low-rank and its column space contains the true column space U0 of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA). They provide noiseless and noisy recovery theorems under column normalization, bounds on rank(A), and sparsity of S0, with λ = 1/√n1. They also propose a practical unsupervised procedure: run RPCA to obtain an initial estimate of L0, form a low-rank approximation, column-normalize to construct A, and then solve LRR with this learned dictionary. Experiments on synthetic data and corrupted motion trajectory matrices (derived from Hopkins155-like sequences) show improved exact recovery regions and lower downstream clustering error versus RPCA, LRR with A = X, SIM, and SSC.

Quality
- Theory: The paper provides clear conditions under which LRR achieves exact (noiseless) or near-exact (noisy) recovery without invoking μ2. The requirement U0 ⊂ UA is necessary for exact recovery (since AZ* = L0), and the rank(A) and sparsity bounds are explicit. The claimed rank bound O(n2/log n1) is looser than RPCA’s typical O(n2/(log n1)2), which is noteworthy.
- Algorithm: The dictionary-learning procedure is heuristic but reasonable: RPCA is relatively robust even on coherent data, and using its output to form a low-rank, better-conditioned A is empirically effective. Runtime claims (≈1.2× RPCA) are plausible given LRR’s O(n2 rA) complexity with low-rank A.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments show a substantially larger success region (≈47% by area) over RPCA, and motion segmentation results show sizable relative error reductions (e.g., SSC 22.81% → 9.50% with RPCA preprocessing → 5.74% with learned-dictionary LRR).

Clarity
Clarity is the main weakness. While the structure is logical, the manuscript is difficult to follow for readers outside a narrow subcommunity.
- Coherence: The definition and intuition behind μ1, μ2, μ3 should be explained more accessibly, with direct geometric interpretations and simple examples. The μ2-phenomenon deserves a more intuitive explanation (why clustering along row space inflates μ2 and hurts RPCA) in the main text rather than deferring to appendices.
- Assumptions: Clearly state when and how U0 ⊂ UA can be approximately satisfied in practice, and what happens under mismatch. Provide robustness bounds or empirical sensitivity to violations of subspace inclusion and to the rank/conditioning of A.
- Claims: Make the main contributions precise at the outset, distinguish what is new (theoretical avoidance of μ2 under low-rank A; practical unsupervised construction of A) from what is prior (RPCA, LRR with A = X).
- Presentation: The language needs careful editing for grammar and flow. Some figures and references need polishing. Please add guidance on parameter choices (e.g., λ, ε), and clarify the dependence on column normalization.

Originality
The work combines two lines of recent research—RPCA and LRR—and contributes:
- New theoretical recovery guarantees for LRR with general low-rank dictionaries that avoid μ2 under natural subspace-inclusion conditions.
- A connection between clustering structure and coherence, clarifying when RPCA struggles.
- A practical unsupervised pipeline that leverages RPCA to construct a suitable dictionary for LRR.
This goes beyond a straightforward combination and offers new insights.

Significance
Addressing coherent data is important in practice (e.g., subspace clustering, motion segmentation). The theoretical results provide a principled way to handle clustered data, and the empirical gains over strong baselines suggest practitioners may benefit. The work also offers conceptual clarity on the role of coherence and why factorization-style models (via AZ) can help.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid and interesting theory showing LRR with low-rank dictionaries can avoid μ2, with both noiseless and noisy guarantees.
- Clear identification and analysis of when RPCA fails (clustering-induced coherence).
- Practical and simple algorithm with measurable improvements on synthetic and real data.
- Potential impact on robust subspace learning and motion segmentation pipelines.

Cons for acceptance
- Clarity and exposition need substantial improvement; key concepts (coherence, μ2-phenomenon, subspace inclusion) are not sufficiently intuitive in the main text.
- The dictionary-learning procedure is heuristic, with limited analysis of failure modes and sensitivity to assumptions.
- Experimental scope could be expanded (e.g., parameter sensitivity; more diverse real datasets; ablations comparing oracle dictionaries, varying numbers of clusters, noise levels).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide intuitive explanations and visualizations for coherence parameters and their relation to clustering.
- Discuss the practicality of U0 ⊂ UA and quantify robustness when this is only approximately true.
- Include parameter-sensitivity studies (λ, ε, rank thresholds), and ablations on the conditioning and rank of A.
- Improve language and organization; ensure references and figures are complete and polished; relate more explicitly to prior LRR variants (including A = I and A = X).

Comment after author rebuttal and reviewer discussion
There is clearly publishable content and interesting contributions. My remaining concern is that substantial rewriting is needed to make the paper accessible beyond specialists. If a “major revision” option existed, I would recommend it. The paper combines two recent ideas and provides both theoretical results and a heuristic yet empirically strong algorithm.
