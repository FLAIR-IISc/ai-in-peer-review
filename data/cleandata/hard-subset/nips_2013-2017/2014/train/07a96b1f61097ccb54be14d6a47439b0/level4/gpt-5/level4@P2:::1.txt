Summary
This paper tackles robust recovery of low-rank matrices when the data are coherent, a regime where standard RPCA (Candes et al., 2011) is known to degrade. The authors identify that clustering structures—common in practice—inflate the µ2 and µ3 coherence parameters as the number of clusters grows, harming RPCA. They propose addressing coherence by using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a suitably chosen dictionary. The central theoretical result shows that if the dictionary A is itself low-rank and its column space contains the true column space U0, then LRR achieves exact (or near-exact, in the noisy case) recovery without dependence on µ2. Building on this insight, they present a practical unsupervised algorithm: use RPCA to obtain an initial low-rank estimate, construct a column-normalized low-rank dictionary from it, and then solve LRR. Experiments on synthetic data and corrupted motion sequences demonstrate clear improvements over RPCA, especially as coherence increases.

Relation to prior work
- RPCA provides guarantees under incoherence assumptions that can be violated by clustered data. This paper explains and empirically illustrates the resulting µ2-phenomenon.
- LRR has been studied largely for the A = X case; this work analyzes LRR with general dictionaries and proves that a low-rank dictionary that covers U0 can eliminate µ2 from the guarantees.
- The paper also connects to GPCA by highlighting robustness issues in clustered subspace settings.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: Clear theorems (noiseless/noisy) establishing recovery with LRR using low-rank dictionaries and avoiding µ2 dependence, with looser rank bounds than RPCA.
- Conceptual insight: Coherence parameters are linked to intrinsic structures (e.g., clustering), not merely proof artifacts; the paper elucidates why coherence grows with cluster count.
- Practical algorithm: Simple, effective procedure that leverages RPCA as an initializer to build a good dictionary, with modest additional computational cost.
- Empirical validation: Phase diagrams on synthetic data and improvements in motion segmentation (Hopkins sequences) substantiate the claims.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumption on A: The main guarantees require U0 ⊆ UA and a low-rank A. In unsupervised settings this is approximated via RPCA. When RPCA is already strained by large µ2, how robust is the initialization? Empirical ablations (e.g., varying coherence/cluster count) would clarify.
- Sensitivity: The algorithm depends on rank thresholding and column normalization. How sensitive are results to the singular-value cutoff and to λ = 1/√n1, especially under varying noise levels or non-Gaussian corruption?
- Comparisons: Beyond RPCA, comparisons to other robust PCA variants (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, stable PCP variants, factorization-based robust methods) would strengthen significance claims.
- Scope of experiments: The motion experiments are promising but limited in size. Additional real datasets and controlled studies varying the number of clusters would better illustrate the µ2-phenomenon and the proposed remedy.
- When µ1 is large: The analysis notes µ1 may also become large (e.g., clustered rows as in text data). It would help to discuss whether the proposed approach mitigates such cases or if performance deteriorates similarly.
- Theoretical clarity: The necessity and practical verification of PUA(U0) = U0 could be discussed more explicitly, including how far violations can be tolerated and any diagnostics for checking adequacy of A.

Quality
- The paper is technically sound, with well-stated assumptions and proofs (details deferred to the full version). The algorithmic design is coherent with the theory.
- Some implementation details (e.g., choice of ε in the noisy case, stopping criteria, and computational complexity measurements) could be elaborated.

Clarity
- Overall clearly written and well organized. Minor suggestions: briefly restate the intuition behind µ2 growth with clustering in the main text; clearly separate assumptions from empirical practices (e.g., how A is constructed); ensure figures and tables are fully self-contained.

Originality
- The idea of immunizing recovery guarantees against µ2 by using a low-rank dictionary is novel and well motivated. The link between coherence and clustering structures is articulated convincingly.

Significance
- Addressing coherent data is practically important. The theoretical results and the simple algorithm are likely to influence future work on robust subspace recovery and dictionary-based methods, and have immediate practical utility.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong theoretical advance that addresses a known limitation of RPCA.
- Simple, effective algorithm with empirical gains.
- Clear insights into the role of coherence and the utility of LRR with learned dictionaries.

Cons against acceptance
- Dependence on RPCA initialization in the most challenging regimes could limit robustness.
- Limited breadth of experimental comparisons and datasets.
- Some practical sensitivities and theoretical assumptions (U0 ⊆ UA) remain to be stress-tested.

Question to the authors
- Can the proposed method be directly leveraged for subspace clustering/segmentation? For example, does Z* or an affinity derived from AZ* offer improved clustering over LRR with A = X (LRRx) or SSC, particularly under high coherence? Clarifying this connection and adding a small experiment would broaden impact.

Recommendation
This paper addresses an important limitation of RPCA with solid theory and a practical algorithm that performs well. I recommend acceptance.
