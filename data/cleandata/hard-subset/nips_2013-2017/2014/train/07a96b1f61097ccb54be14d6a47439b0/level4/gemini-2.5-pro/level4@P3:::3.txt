### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses a key limitation of the widely used Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) method for recovering a low-rank matrix `L_0` from grossly corrupted observations `X = L_0 + S_0`. The authors begin by observing that RPCA's performance guarantee degrades as the "coherence" of `L_0` increases. The central insight of this work is to provide a physical interpretation for this high coherence: it naturally arises when the data in `L_0`, while globally low-rank, possesses an underlying clustering structure (i.e., lies on a union of subspaces).

To overcome this limitation, the authors propose using the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework with a learned dictionary `A`, such that `L_0` is approximated by `AZ`. They provide a theoretical analysis showing that if the dictionary `A` is itself low-rank and its column space contains the column space of the true low-rank matrix `L_0`, the recovery guarantee for LRR becomes immune to the coherence parameters (`μ2` and `μ3`) that grow with the number of clusters. This results in a stronger theoretical guarantee for exact recovery than standard RPCA in this "coherent data" regime. Based on this theory, they propose a practical two-stage algorithm: first, an initial estimate of `L_0` is obtained via RPCA; second, this estimate is used to construct a dictionary for a subsequent LRR step to refine the recovery. The effectiveness of this approach is demonstrated through experiments on both synthetic data and real-world motion segmentation data.

### **Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance**

This is an excellent paper that makes several significant and novel contributions to the field. I strongly recommend its acceptance.

**1. Originality and Significance:** The paper's contributions are substantial and address a well-known challenge in low-rank matrix recovery.
*   **Novel Interpretation of Coherence:** The connection established between the abstract coherence parameter and the concrete, ubiquitous phenomenon of data clustering is a major conceptual contribution. It provides valuable intuition about when and why RPCA might struggle and moves beyond treating coherence as a mere technical assumption.
*   **Theoretical Advancement:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis showing how LRR with a suitable dictionary can provably overcome the coherence bottleneck of RPCA. The resulting recovery guarantee (Theorem 1), which avoids dependence on `μ2` and `μ3`, is a significant theoretical improvement for this class of structured data.
*   **Practical Algorithm and State-of-the-Art Results:** The proposed two-stage algorithm is simple, intuitive, and effective. The experimental results are convincing, showing a 47% increase in the success region on synthetic data compared to RPCA. Furthermore, the application to robust subspace clustering on corrupted motion sequences is particularly impressive, demonstrating a 40% relative improvement over the standard RPCA+SSC pipeline. This result is a significant contribution to the motion segmentation and subspace clustering literature in its own right.

**2. Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs (referenced in the full version), and the experimental design is solid, corroborating the theory with both synthetic and real-world data. The work is a complete and polished piece of research.

**3. Connection to Broader Field:** The paper insightfully connects the fields of robust matrix recovery (RPCA) and subspace clustering (LRR, SSC). By demonstrating how a technique from one area (LRR) can solve a problem in the other, it bridges these domains in a meaningful way.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is very strong, and my suggestions are primarily aimed at further improving its clarity and impact.

**1. Clarity and Presentation:** While the core ideas are clear, the manuscript would benefit from a careful proofread to enhance precision and readability.
*   On lines 80-82, the phrasing "RPCA captures only the low-rankness property ... but essentially ignores the extra structures ... which specify in more detail how the data points locate on the subspace" could be sharpened. The key point is that RPCA's model is correct (the data *is* low-rank), but its performance guarantee is not tight enough to account for this finer-grained structure, which the LRR model can exploit.
*   On line 88, the sentence "Whenever the data are exhibiting some clustering structures, RPCA is no longer a method of perfection" is incomplete. It should likely be "a method of perfect recovery" or similar.
*   A general pass on the writing to ensure every claim is stated precisely would elevate the manuscript's overall quality.

**2. Discussion of Related Work and Alternative Approaches:**
*   The connection to subspace clustering literature is noted, but it could be deepened. For instance, the work of Wang and Xu ("Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering") is relevant. They show that in the context of subspace clustering, higher coherence can actually be *beneficial* for identifying clusters. This presents an interesting contrast to the RPCA setting, where it is detrimental. Discussing this nuance would further highlight the contributions of the current paper, which operates in a setting where the data is assumed to be *globally* low-rank, a key difference from many subspace clustering settings.
*   For the motion segmentation experiment, the proposed method is effectively a new pipeline for robust subspace clustering. It would be valuable to discuss how this compares to other potential pipelines. For example, one could first apply a robust subspace clustering algorithm directly to the corrupted data and then clean the data within each identified cluster. While such an alternative may lack theoretical guarantees, acknowledging it would provide a more complete picture of the problem landscape.

**3. Highlighting the Contribution to Subspace Clustering:**
*   The results in Section 4.2 represent a significant advance for the problem of subspace clustering with sparsely corrupted data. This contribution feels somewhat understated in the current draft. I would encourage the authors to explicitly frame this as one of the key contributions of the paper in the introduction and conclusion, as it is a result of high practical importance to the computer vision and machine learning communities.

### **Final Recommendation**

This paper presents a novel, theoretically sound, and empirically verified solution to an important problem in robust low-rank matrix recovery. It provides a key insight into the physical meaning of matrix coherence and leverages this to design a superior algorithm. The contributions are significant for researchers in compressed sensing, robust statistics, and subspace clustering. The weaknesses are minor and addressable through revisions. I strongly recommend acceptance.
