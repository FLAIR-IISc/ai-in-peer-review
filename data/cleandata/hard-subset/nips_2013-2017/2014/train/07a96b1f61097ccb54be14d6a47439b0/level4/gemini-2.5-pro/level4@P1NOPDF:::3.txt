This paper proposes a method to improve the performance of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) by incorporating a learned dictionary `A` within the Low Rank Representation (LRR) framework. The central insight is that the incoherence parameter, `μ`, often considered a bottleneck for recovery guarantees in RPCA, can in fact be linked to an underlying clustering structure within the data. The authors demonstrate that by imposing certain conditions on the dictionary `A`, this clustering structure can be exploited via LRR. This approach leads to theoretically stronger recovery guarantees for both exact and noisy low-rank and sparse decomposition, partially mitigating the restrictive dependence on `μ`.

The proposed method is both novel and significant. It builds upon a rich body of work on subspace clustering that assumes a union-of-subspaces structure (e.g., "Wang and Xu: Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering & Provable subspace clustering: When LRR meets SSC," and "Soltanolkotabi and Candes: A geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers," "Soltanolkotabi et al: Robust subspace clustering"). However, this paper's approach is arguably more general, as its assumptions about the clustering structure are less explicit. The theoretical analysis is well-supported by simulations and experiments on real-world data. I agree with the authors' remark that this newly identified structure is likely present in many other applications, such as text analysis.

In summary, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the fields of low-rank matrix recovery and subspace clustering. I strongly recommend its acceptance at NIPS. I offer several comments below for the authors' consideration, which include suggestions for improving clarity and further discussion points.

**Detailed Comments:**

1.  **Lines 80-82:** The description here could be clarified. For instance, the phrase "our data" is ambiguous. Additionally, the text seems to imply that satisfying one condition is sufficient for "perfect recovery," whereas additional conditions are typically required. Please refine this statement for greater precision.

2.  **Line 88:** There appears to be a missing word after "perfect" (e.g., "perfect recovery"?).

3.  **General Clarity:** The paper would benefit from a thorough proofread to catch minor errors, such as the two noted above. More broadly, I encourage the authors to revise for clarity and precision throughout the manuscript. Being specific about the claims being made and the concepts being referenced would significantly improve readability.

4.  It is worth noting the connection to the work of Wang and Xu ("Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering"), who observed that a high coherence parameter can be beneficial for subspace clustering—a counterintuitive result compared to its role in RPCA and matrix completion. The key distinction, as I see it, is that their work assumes each cluster is low-rank, without requiring the entire dataset to be low-rank. The structure exploited in the present paper can be viewed as a novel combination of a global low-rank structure with a local union-of-subspaces structure.

5.  A potentially interesting baseline for comparison would be a two-stage approach: first, perform noisy subspace clustering (e.g., with an ℓ1 penalty) to identify clusters, and then apply PCA to each resulting subspace. While I acknowledge that this heuristic approach currently lacks theoretical guarantees, a comparison could provide valuable empirical context for the proposed method's performance.

6.  I believe the authors understate a significant contribution in Section 4.2. The proposed method effectively provides a new, high-performing algorithm for subspace clustering in the presence of sparse corruptions. The substantial improvements over the standard RPCA+SSC baseline on the Hopkins155 dataset are a testament to this. I would encourage the authors to explicitly highlight this in the paper. To me, this paper offers at least three major contributions: (1) it identifies and formalizes a specific clustering structure that explains high coherence in data; (2) it demonstrates how this structure can be implicitly exploited using a dictionary-based LRR approach to strengthen recovery guarantees; and (3) it delivers a state-of-the-art method for robust subspace clustering.
