

This paper investigates the limitations of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) when applied to coherent data—data exhibiting clustering structures—which are ubiquitous in real-world applications. The authors demonstrate that RPCA's performance degrades as the coherence of the low-rank component increases with more clusters, and propose a novel solution using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a properly constructed dictionary. The main contribution is proving that LRR can avoid coherence parameters when the dictionary itself is low-rank, and providing an unsupervised algorithm to construct such dictionaries using RPCA as a preprocessing step.

The paper builds upon the seminal RPCA work by Candès et al. (JACM 2011) and extends the LRR framework proposed by Liu et al. (TPAMI 2013). While RPCA has been widely adopted for low-rank matrix recovery, the authors correctly identify a gap in its applicability to structured data with clustering patterns. Previous work has primarily focused on RPCA's performance on incoherent data, and this paper makes a valuable contribution by analyzing the problem through the lens of coherence parameters and their relationship to clustering structures.

**Quality:** The theoretical contributions are technically sound, with two well-structured theorems establishing recovery conditions for LRR when the dictionary is low-rank. The proofs follow the established RPCA analysis framework, and the authors correctly identify the relationship between coherence parameters (particularly µ2) and clustering structures. However, the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion about how the theoretical conditions translate to practical performance, especially regarding the requirements for the dictionary matrix. The empirical validation on synthetic data and motion sequences is thorough and demonstrates clear improvements over RPCA, though the comparison with other recent methods (e.g., robust PCA variants) could be more comprehensive.

**Clarity:** The paper suffers from significant clarity issues that hinder its accessibility. The concept of "coherence" is introduced without sufficient intuitive explanation, and its connection to clustering structures is explained indirectly through technical derivations rather than clear examples. The paper would be greatly improved by providing concrete visualizations of how clustering affects coherence parameters, and by explicitly stating the practical implications of the theoretical results. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is clear, but the rationale for the specific steps (particularly the rank estimation and normalization) needs more justification. The paper's writing is also somewhat technical and dense, making it difficult for non-specialists to grasp the main contributions.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel perspective on the relationship between coherence parameters and clustering structures in low-rank data, which has not been previously explored in depth. While the combination of RPCA and LRR has been considered before, the authors provide new theoretical insights about the role of dictionary structure in overcoming coherence limitations. The specific focus on the µ2-phenomenon (where coherence increases with cluster number) is a valuable contribution that distinguishes this work from previous studies.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the application of robust low-rank recovery methods to real-world data, where clustering structures are common. The proposed approach demonstrates clear empirical improvements over RPCA, with a 47% wider success area on synthetic data and a 40% relative improvement in motion segmentation error rates. This represents a tangible advancement in the state of the art for practical applications. The insights about coherence parameters also provide a deeper understanding of RPCA's limitations that could inform future work.

**Strengths:**
- Identifies a meaningful limitation of RPCA that affects practical applications
- Provides theoretically grounded insights about coherence and clustering
- Proposes a practical algorithm with clear empirical validation
- Demonstrates significant performance improvements over RPCA in real-world applications

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient explanation of coherence parameters and their practical significance
- Theoretical results lack clear connection to practical implementation details
- Paper is overly technical without sufficient intuitive explanations
- Comparison with recent robust low-rank recovery methods is limited
- Clarity issues make the contributions difficult to grasp for non-specialists

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by addressing a meaningful limitation of RPCA and providing both theoretical insights and a practical solution. The main issue is the clarity of the presentation, which would benefit from more intuitive explanations of coherence parameters, better connection between theory and practice, and clearer articulation of the main contributions. With these revisions, the paper would be significantly more accessible to the broader NIPS audience.

The paper is technically sound, presents novel insights, and demonstrates significant empirical improvements over existing methods. While the clarity issues are substantial, they can be addressed through revision without requiring additional experiments or theoretical development. The authors' approach is a meaningful advance that should be shared with the community, and the paper would be significantly strengthened by improving its accessibility to a wider audience.
