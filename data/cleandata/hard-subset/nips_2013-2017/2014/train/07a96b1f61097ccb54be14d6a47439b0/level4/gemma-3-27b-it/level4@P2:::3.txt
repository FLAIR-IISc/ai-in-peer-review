This paper addresses the challenge of recovering low-rank matrices when the data exhibits coherence, a phenomenon often linked to underlying clustering structures. The authors demonstrate that standard Robust PCA (RPCA) can struggle in such scenarios due to its sensitivity to coherence. They propose leveraging Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully chosen dictionary to mitigate this issue. Their key theoretical contribution is proving that if the dictionary itself is low-rank, LRR becomes less susceptible to the coherence parameter, offering improved recovery guarantees—both in noiseless and noisy settings. 

The work builds upon the established framework of RPCA [Candès et al., 2011] and LRR [Liu et al., 2013], connecting them to the broader problem of subspace clustering and robust dimensionality reduction. It relates to prior work on subspace clustering with outliers [Elhamifar & Vidal, 2009; Soltanolkotabi & Candès, 2012; Soltanolkotabi et al., 2013] and the interplay between coherence and clustering [Wang & Xu, 2014], offering a nuanced perspective by explicitly linking data coherence to underlying cluster structure. While existing work often treats clustering as a separate assumption, this paper provides a rationale for *why* coherence arises in clustered data, offering a more general and insightful approach. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world motion sequence data further strengthens the claims.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The connection between coherence, clustering structure, and the effectiveness of LRR with a low-rank dictionary is a novel and important contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing conditions for successful recovery under both noiseless and noisy conditions.
*   **Significance:** The results have the potential to improve performance in a range of applications where data coherence is a concern, particularly in areas like motion analysis, image processing, and potentially text data. The paper addresses a significant limitation of RPCA, broadening its applicability.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide compelling evidence supporting the theoretical findings, demonstrating improvements over RPCA in various settings.
*   **Hidden Contribution:** The application to sparse corruptions in subspace clustering, achieving significant improvements on the Hopkins155 dataset, represents a valuable contribution that should be explicitly highlighted.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Precision:** The paper sometimes suffers from a lack of clarity and precision in its language. Certain statements require more careful wording and explanation (e.g., lines 80-82, 88). A thorough proofreading pass is recommended.
*   **Relationship to Existing Work:** While the paper acknowledges related work, a more in-depth discussion of how this approach differs from or complements the work of Wang and Xu [2014]—specifically, the scenario where higher coherence is beneficial for subspace clustering—would be valuable.
*   **Algorithm 1 Iteration:** The authors mention the potential for an iterative refinement of Algorithm 1 but dismiss it without extensive investigation. Exploring this further could potentially yield additional performance gains.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding the impact of data coherence on low-rank matrix recovery.
*   The proposed approach offers a practical solution for mitigating the limitations of RPCA in clustered datasets.
*   The experimental results validate the theoretical findings and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The hidden contribution of improved sparse corruptions in subspace clustering further strengthens the impact.
*   The paper addresses a core challenge in the field of compressed sensing and dimensionality reduction.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper’s clarity could be improved through more precise language and careful editing.
*   A more thorough discussion of the relationship to related work, particularly Wang and Xu [2014], would be beneficial.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a strong contribution to the field. Despite some minor issues with clarity, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and empirical validation make it deserving of acceptance to NIPS. The paper contains at least three important contributions: 1) a description of a clustering structure that leads to high coherence, 2) a demonstration that one can exploit such structure using a dictionary in LRR, and 3) a partial solution to the sparse corruption problem in subspace clustering. The authors have identified a crucial limitation of existing methods and proposed a compelling solution.
