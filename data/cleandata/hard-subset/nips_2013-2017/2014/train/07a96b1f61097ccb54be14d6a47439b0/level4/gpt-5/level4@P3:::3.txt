Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies low-rank matrix recovery in the presence of structured, “coherent” data. The authors argue that standard RPCA (Candes et al., 2011) can fail even when the underlying matrix is strictly low-rank, because its guarantees depend on coherence parameters that grow with clustering in the data. They formalize this via the µ2-phenomenon: when data arise from a union of subspaces (multiple clusters within a low-dimensional subspace), the second (and consequently the third) coherence parameter increases with the number of clusters, degrading RPCA’s performance.

To address this, the paper proposes using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully chosen dictionary A. The main theoretical result shows that if A is low-rank and its column space contains the true column space (U0 ⊂ UA), then LRR’s exact recovery guarantee does not depend on µ2; analogous stability results are given for the noisy setting. This yields a practical principle: use a low-rank dictionary aligned with the data subspace. In unsupervised settings, the authors propose a simple two-stage algorithm: run RPCA to estimate L0, build a normalized low-rank dictionary from that estimate, and then run LRR. Synthetic experiments and motion segmentation experiments (Hopkins sequences with added corruptions) demonstrate wider success regions than RPCA and improved clustering accuracy when recovery is used as a preprocessing step.

The work relates to and complements the union-of-subspaces literature for subspace clustering, including SSC (Elhamifar and Vidal), LRR with A = X (Liu et al.), and recent analyses such as Wang & Xu (“Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering,” “Provable subspace clustering: When LRR meets SSC”) and Soltanolkotabi & Candès (“A geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers”): those works exploit clustering to segment subspaces, whereas RPCA/MC typically suffer from high coherence. This paper bridges the two by showing that coherence reflects clustering structure and can be neutralized for recovery by selecting an appropriate low-rank dictionary.

Quality:
- Theory: The noiseless and noisy theorems are clearly stated and technically plausible. The key assumptions—low-rank A with U0 ⊂ UA, column normalization, and sparsity level—are transparent. The rank condition rank(L0) ≤ O(n2/log n1) is slightly looser than the standard RPCA bound, and the notable point is the removal of µ2 from the conditions under the stated dictionary assumptions. The necessity of U0 ⊂ UA for exact recovery is well explained.
- Algorithm: The two-stage RPCA→LRR procedure is simple and well motivated. The discussion on computational cost and the empirical observation that the second stage does not double runtime is helpful.
- Experiments: Synthetic results show a substantially larger success region than RPCA; motion segmentation experiments demonstrate meaningful reductions in clustering error after recovery, with additional gains when using the learned dictionary for LRR rather than RPCA alone.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable but would benefit from careful proofreading and sharper language in the introduction and motivation. Some sentences are vague or awkward and should be revised for precision (see detailed suggestions below). Figures and definitions are helpful; the introduction to coherence and the µ2-phenomenon is a useful pedagogical contribution. It would also help to state assumptions and implications (e.g., U0 ⊂ UA) earlier and to clearly separate what is necessary for exact recovery from what is used in proofs for convenience (e.g., column normalization for setting λ).

Originality:
The connection between coherence growth and clustering structure, and the demonstration that LRR with a low-rank, subspace-aligned dictionary removes µ2 from the recovery conditions, is novel and insightful. While LRR is known, the dictionary-design principle and theoretical guarantees in this context appear new. The proposed unsupervised construction of A via RPCA is simple but effective.

Significance:
The work advances robust low-rank recovery by addressing a key limitation of RPCA in clustered data regimes common in practice (vision, motion trajectories, potentially text). The principle of low-rank, subspace-aligned dictionaries for recovery is likely to influence both theory and practical pipelines. The improvements in motion segmentation after recovery are practically meaningful and suggest broader impact.

Strengths:
- Clear identification of a failure mode of RPCA via the µ2-phenomenon and its link to clustering.
- Theoretical guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that do not depend on µ2.
- Practical, unsupervised algorithm with strong empirical gains on synthetic and real data.
- Bridges robust recovery and subspace clustering perspectives.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Assumptions and practicality: Exact recovery requires U0 ⊂ UA; the unsupervised algorithm relies on RPCA’s estimate of U0, which may be weak in highly coherent regimes. Please discuss robustness to imperfect A, and provide ablations showing sensitivity to dictionary rank, column normalization, and the rank truncation step.
- Baselines: It would strengthen the empirical section to compare against a “direct” robust subspace clustering pipeline: noisy SSC (with ℓ1) followed by per-cluster PCA, as well as Outlier Pursuit-type methods and LRR with A = X. Some of these are included (e.g., SSC/LRRx), but the alternative pipeline comparison would contextualize the proposed approach.
- Iterative refinement: Since you note that iterating RPCA→LRR tends to converge quickly, showing a small study (1 vs 2 iterations) would be informative.
- Relation to subspace clustering theory: Explicitly discuss how higher coherence can help subspace clustering (e.g., Wang & Xu) while harming RPCA, and clarify how your approach leverages clustering implicitly for recovery.
- Clarity and style: Improve several sentences for precision. For example:
  - Early in the introduction, avoid “our data are...,” and state the setting concretely. Clarify that even when data are strictly low-rank, additional structure (clustering) increases coherence and can violate RPCA’s success conditions.
  - Replace “no longer a method of perfection” with “is not guaranteed to recover perfectly.”
  - Throughout, tighten claims and avoid vague phrasing; define terms before use and ensure consistent notation.
- Scope and future work: You note that row-wise clustering (e.g., text data) can inflate µ1; a brief discussion or experiment would broaden the impact. Consider releasing code and implementation details for reproducibility (parameter choices, stopping criteria).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important and widespread limitation of RPCA with a principled, theoretically grounded solution.
- Offers a simple, practical algorithm that yields substantial empirical gains.
- Provides new insight into coherence as a manifestation of clustering structure and a clear prescription for dictionary design in LRR.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Dependence on a good initial RPCA estimate may limit robustness in the most challenging regimes.
- Empirical comparisons could be broader; some baselines and ablations are missing.
- Writing issues in parts of the paper reduce clarity.

Overall recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a substantive theoretical and practical contribution by explaining and overcoming the effect of coherence induced by clustering, and it demonstrates clear improvements on real tasks.
