- Paper studies low-rank matrix recovery and identifies that RPCA fails with increasing data coherence (e.g., due to clustering).  
- Key insight: a carefully chosen low-rank dictionary within LRR can mitigate coherence-related failures.  
- The authors prove that a low-rank dictionary makes LRR immune to coherence parameters tied to the number of clusters.  
- A dictionary-learning algorithm is proposed, initialized from RPCA, and tested on synthetic and real motion-sequence data.  
- Theoretical results are mathematically rigorous but primarily address noiseless scenarios.  
- The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the algorithm’s behavior in the presence of noise beyond the stated theorem.  
- Experimental validation supports the claims but would benefit from broader comparisons to other dictionary-learning or weighted RPCA methods.  
- Clarity is a major weakness: dense writing, heavy notation, and insufficient intuitive explanation of “coherence” and its link to clustering.  
- Presentation would improve with more intuitive exposition, illustrative examples, and moving technical definitions to an appendix.  
- Originality lies in combining RPCA and LRR and theoretically linking low-rank dictionary structure to coherence mitigation.  
- The work is potentially significant for applications with inherent clustering (motion segmentation, image processing, sensor networks).  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important RPCA limitation, provides novel theory, shows empirical improvements, and has application potential.  
- Arguments against acceptance: poor clarity/accessibility, theoretical focus on noiseless case, and limited experimental comparisons.  
- Overall recommendation: reject but encourage major revision and resubmission addressing clarity, noise analysis, and more comprehensive experiments.
