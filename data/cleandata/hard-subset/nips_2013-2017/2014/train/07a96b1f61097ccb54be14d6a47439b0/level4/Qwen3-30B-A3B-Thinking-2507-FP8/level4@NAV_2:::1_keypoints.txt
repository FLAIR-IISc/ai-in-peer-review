- Addresses RPCA's failure on coherent data from clustering, identifying the "µ2-phenomenon" where RPCA degrades as cluster count (coherence) increases.  
- Shows theoretically that LRR with a low-rank dictionary avoids dependence on µ2, proving exact recovery without µ2 constraints when the dictionary spans the true subspace (Theorem 1).  
- Extends prior LRR work [13] by providing a principled low-rank dictionary construction and theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1–2).  
- Introduces an unsupervised dictionary learning algorithm (Algorithm 1) usable without supervised dictionary information.  
- Empirically validates claims on synthetic data and Hopkins155 motion sequences.  
- Reports a 47% larger success area over RPCA in experiments.  
- Reduces subspace clustering error rates by 40% compared to RPCA.  
- Strength: Rigorous theoretical contributions with clear proofs and comprehensive experiments.  
- Strength: Original analysis linking coherence to clustering and moving beyond RPCA incoherence assumptions.  
- Strength: Significant practical impact for real-world tasks like motion segmentation and general low-rank recovery with clustering.  
- Strength: Clear presentation and illustrative figures (e.g., Fig. 2); minor notation consistency issues noted.  
- Weakness: Focus is limited to clustering-induced coherence and may not generalize to other coherence sources (e.g., non-uniform text sampling).  
- Weakness: Claimed computational efficiency (1.2× RPCA) depends on maintaining a low dictionary rank and may not hold if the estimated rank is high.  
- Pro/con summary: Strong theoretical and empirical case for acceptance; main con is limited applicability beyond clustering coherence.  
- Constructive question: Request clarification on how the dictionary-based LRR directly improves subspace clustering beyond RPCA pre-processing (e.g., better cluster separation in low-rank space).  
- Recommendation: Accept — substantial contribution with strong technical rigor and practical relevance.
