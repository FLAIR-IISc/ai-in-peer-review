This paper tackles the problem of robustly estimating a low-dimensional subspace from contaminated observations when the data are inherently coherent (e.g., exhibit clustered structure). Classical RPCA methods typically degrade as data coherence increases; the authors propose a method that explicitly circumvents this bottleneck. Given that clustered structure is common in real data, addressing coherence directly is an important and timely contribution.

Strengths:
- Significance: The coherence issue is a well-known limitation in RPCA. Providing an approach that remains effective in coherent regimes substantially broadens the applicability of robust subspace recovery.
- Technical soundness: The theoretical analysis appears rigorous and directly targets the coherence bottleneck. The conditions under which the method succeeds are well motivated and align with practical scenarios.
- Empirical validation: Experiments convincingly demonstrate strong performance in both synthetic and real settings, particularly as coherence increases. The results indicate consistent gains over standard RPCA baselines in the regimes of interest.
- Practical relevance: Since many high-dimensional datasets naturally form clusters within a low-dimensional subspace, the proposed approach addresses a setting of clear practical importance.

Questions and suggestions:
- Applicability to subspace clustering/segmentation: Can the proposed method be adapted to the union-of-subspaces setting, where data lie near multiple low-dimensional subspaces? For example, could it be used as a building block for subspace clustering by iteratively identifying one subspace at a time or by informing an affinity matrix for spectral clustering? Clarifying this connection, even at a high level, would be valuable for readers working on segmentation problems.
- Scope and assumptions: A brief discussion of the method’s limitations would help position the contribution. In particular, how sensitive is performance to deviations from the assumed clustering/coherence model, and what failure modes should practitioners anticipate?
- Practical guidance: If applicable, providing guidance on hyperparameter selection and a summary of computational complexity relative to standard RPCA methods would further aid adoption.

Overall assessment:
This paper addresses an important and challenging problem in robust PCA by directly handling data coherence, a key failure mode for many existing methods. The contributions are well motivated, theoretically supported, and empirically validated. I recommend acceptance.
