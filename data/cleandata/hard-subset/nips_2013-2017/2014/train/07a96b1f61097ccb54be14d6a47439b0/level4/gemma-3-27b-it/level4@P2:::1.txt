This paper tackles the problem of robust low-rank matrix recovery in the presence of corruption, specifically addressing the performance degradation of Robust PCA (RPCA) when the underlying data exhibits coherence – a common characteristic arising from clustering structures. This is a significant issue, as increasing data coherence is a known theoretical limitation for RPCA. The authors circumvent this limitation through a novel approach leveraging Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully designed dictionary. Given the prevalence of clustered data in real-world applications, addressing this problem is both meaningful and impactful. The proposed method is supported by strong theoretical analysis and demonstrated to perform well empirically.

The paper builds upon the established framework of RPCA [4], noting its limitations when data isn’t strictly incoherent. It identifies that clustering structures, ubiquitous in many applications, contribute to data coherence and subsequently degrade RPCA’s performance. The key insight is that by employing LRR [13] with a low-rank dictionary, the impact of coherence can be mitigated. A crucial theoretical contribution is the proof demonstrating that a low-rank dictionary renders LRR immune to the coherence parameter that grows with the number of clusters. This establishes a fundamental principle for handling coherent data and leads to a practical algorithm for dictionary learning in unsupervised settings.  Experiments on both synthetic and real-world motion sequence data validate the claims.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** The paper directly addresses a significant limitation of RPCA, namely its sensitivity to data coherence.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The core contribution – the immunity of LRR to coherence with a low-rank dictionary – is rigorously proven.
*   **Practical Algorithm:** The paper provides a concrete algorithm for unsupervised dictionary learning, making the theoretical results actionable.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, outperforming RPCA.
*   **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making the technical details accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discussion of Computational Complexity:** While the authors mention the computational efficiency relative to RPCA, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of their algorithm would be beneficial.
*   **Dictionary Learning Heuristic:** The dictionary learning algorithm relies on an initial RPCA estimate. The sensitivity of the overall performance to the quality of this initial estimate isn’t thoroughly explored.
*   **Scope of Applicability:** The paper primarily focuses on scenarios where clustering structures are the primary source of coherence.  The performance in other scenarios where coherence arises from different factors could be investigated further.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a crucial limitation of a widely used technique (RPCA) and proposes a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution.
*   The results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods in handling coherent data.
*   The paper’s contribution is significant and likely to be of interest to the NIPS community working on robust PCA, dimensionality reduction, and related fields.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   While the results are promising, the practical impact might be limited if the dictionary learning algorithm is sensitive to noise or initial conditions. This concern, however, is minor.
*   The limited discussion of computational complexity may hinder wider adoption of the proposed method.

**Specific Question/Suggestion for the Authors:**

The authors demonstrate strong performance on low-rank recovery tasks. Given the connection between LRR and subspace clustering/segmentation, could they comment on the applicability of their method (or modifications thereof) to these related problems? Specifically, would the proposed dictionary learning strategy offer advantages in subspace clustering scenarios?



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of robust PCA. The theoretical results are insightful, the proposed algorithm is practical, and the empirical validation is convincing. The strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and I **strongly recommend acceptance** to NIPS 2014.
