This paper addresses the critical problem of robustly estimating a low-dimensional subspace from contaminated observations, particularly when the data exhibits high coherence. A well-known theoretical bottleneck for many existing Robust PCA (RPCA) methods is that their performance degrades significantly as data coherence increases. This paper elegantly circumvents this long-standing issue. Given that coherent or clustered data structures are prevalent in numerous real-world applications, addressing this limitation is of significant practical importance. The proposed method is not only theoretically sound, with clear guarantees provided, but is also convincingly demonstrated to be effective in practice.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and widely recognized limitation in the RPCA literature. The inability of previous methods to handle coherent data has restricted their applicability. By successfully addressing this challenge, this work represents a major step forward for robust subspace recovery.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The proposed approach is novel and well-motivated. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, clearly establishing the conditions under which the method succeeds and formally differentiating it from prior art, especially in the high-coherence regime.

3.  **Empirical Validation:** The practical utility of the method is convincingly demonstrated through a comprehensive set of experiments. The results on both synthetic and real-world data clearly show its superiority over existing state-of-the-art algorithms, particularly in the challenging scenarios it is designed to solve.

**Question for the Authors:**

The paper focuses on recovering a single subspace from coherent data. Given that the method is robust to such structures—which can be interpreted as data points forming distinct clusters—a natural question arises regarding its potential extension to the subspace clustering problem. Could the principles of the proposed approach be adapted to handle data drawn from a union of subspaces, where the goal is to both identify the subspaces and segment the data accordingly? I would appreciate the authors' comments on the potential applicability or necessary modifications for tackling this related, but more complex, problem.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend acceptance. This is a high-quality paper that solves an important and challenging problem in the field of robust subspace recovery. The contribution is significant, the method is technically solid, and the results are compelling. The question raised above is a minor point of curiosity regarding future extensions and does not detract from the excellent quality of the current submission.
