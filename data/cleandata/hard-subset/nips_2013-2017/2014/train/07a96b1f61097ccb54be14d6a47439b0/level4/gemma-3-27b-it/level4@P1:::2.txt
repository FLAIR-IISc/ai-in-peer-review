This paper combines two recent techniques, robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection, in the task of restoring corrupted observation matrices. The key insight is that for structured (for instance clustered) data, the guarantees of robust PCA are not strong enough, and by representing the structure with a dictionary, stronger bounds can be given. Theorems are provided to support these claims. The remaining problem is how to learn the dictionary. For that, an algorithm justified by empirical results is presented: First compute robust PCA, then represent the result with a dictionary. 

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound, to a reasonable level of checking. The two theorems elaborate conditions under which reconstruction is possible, given a dictionary. For the learning of the dictionary, the justification is empirical. 

**Clarity:**

Clarity is the main area for improvement in this paper. While the structure is clear, the main contributions and impact of the paper have not been explained clearly enough to be accessible beyond a very narrow specialist audience. The claims would benefit from being more well-defined, and the impact of the results more explicitly stated. For instance, the concept of coherence is difficult to understand, and its interpretation is indirect (and provided in the appendix). A potential impact of the paper is that using dictionaries may circumvent many earlier problems; this should be highlighted.

The language also needs careful review. It would be helpful to receive a response from the authors regarding how they plan to address these issues.

**Originality:**

The paper is based on a combination of recent techniques, but includes new theorems and empirical results supporting the usefulness of the combination. 

**Significance:**

The paper advances the state of the art with rigorous results. Even though the proposed algorithm for finding the dictionary is heuristic, clear improvements over alternative algorithms demonstrate that the insights given in this paper are valuable. 

---

**Comment after the author rebuttal and reviewer discussion:**

There is clearly publishable content and interesting contributions. The primary remaining concern is that significant effort would be required to rewrite the paper to make it accessible to a broader specialist audience. If a “major revision” option were available, I would recommend it. The paper effectively combines two established ideas and provides both theoretical results and a heuristic but empirically well-performing algorithm.
