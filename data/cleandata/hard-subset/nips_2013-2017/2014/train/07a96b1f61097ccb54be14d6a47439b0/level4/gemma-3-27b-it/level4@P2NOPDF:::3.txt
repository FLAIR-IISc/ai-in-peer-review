This paper presents a novel and significant approach to improving Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) by integrating Low Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary. The authors demonstrate a compelling connection between the often-restrictive incoherence parameter *μ* in RPCA recovery guarantees and an underlying clustering structure within the data. Through carefully defined conditions on the dictionary *A*, they show that leveraging this structure via LRR can reduce the dependence on *μ*, resulting in stronger theoretical guarantees for both exact low-rank and sparse decomposition, and its noisy extension.

The work is closely related to, and builds upon, existing research in subspace clustering, such as the union-of-subspace models explored in papers by Wang and Xu ("Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering & Provable subspace clustering: When LRR meets SSC"), and Soltanolkotabi and Candes ("A geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers") and Soltanolkotabi *et al.* ("Robust subspace clustering"). However, this paper distinguishes itself by making a more nuanced and potentially more general argument regarding the assumption of clustering, remaining less prescriptive about its precise form. The empirical validation through simulations and real-world datasets supports the theoretical analysis, and the authors correctly identify potential applicability to other domains like text data.

In summary, this paper constitutes a substantial contribution to the field of compressed sensing and, in my assessment, is worthy of acceptance to NIPS. 

That said, I have several suggestions for clarification and stylistic improvement, detailed below.

1. Lines 80-82: This section requires rewriting for improved clarity. The reference to “our data” is ambiguous; please specify what data is being discussed. Furthermore, even if the proposed method captures all inherent structures within the problem, a separate condition will still likely be necessary for achieving “perfect recovery.”

2. Line 88: The phrase “… is no longer a method of perfect…” is incomplete and unclear. Please complete the sentence to articulate the intended meaning.

3. I recommend a thorough proofreading of the manuscript to address minor imprecisions and ambiguities. The authors should be specific and precise in their claims and consistently clear in their references to data and concepts. Enhancing the overall clarity of the language will significantly improve the paper’s impact.

4. Wang and Xu’s work on “Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering” highlights a contrasting observation: that a higher coherence parameter can actually *benefit* subspace clustering, unlike the situation in RPCA and matrix completion. The key distinction lies in the assumption that the overall data need not be globally low-rank, only that individual clusters exhibit low-rank properties. The approach presented in this paper can be viewed as cleverly combining the advantages of both global low-rank structure and a union-of-subspace clustering framework.

5. It would be insightful to compare the performance of the proposed algorithm against a two-step approach: first applying noisy subspace clustering with an ℓ₁ penalty, followed by Principal Component Analysis (PCA) within each identified subspace. While a provable guarantee for this latter approach currently remains elusive, it would serve as a useful benchmark.

6. Section 4.2 effectively introduces a novel method for tackling the subspace clustering problem in the presence of sparse corruption. The significant improvements observed on the Hopkins155 dataset, compared to standard RPCA+SSC, represent a noteworthy contribution that the authors should explicitly acknowledge. I believe this paper merits acceptance based on at least three key contributions: 1) the characterization of a clustering structure that explains high coherence, 2) a demonstration of how this structure can be implicitly exploited using a dictionary within an LRR framework, and 3) a partial solution to the challenging problem of sparse corruptions in subspace clustering.
