## Review of Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit

This paper addresses a critical limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA): its performance degradation with increasing data coherence, a common phenomenon when data exhibit clustering structures. The authors convincingly argue that standard RPCA, while powerful, is not a complete solution for low-rank matrix recovery, particularly when facing coherent data. They demonstrate that leveraging Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully constructed, low-rank dictionary can alleviate this issue, providing a theoretically sound and empirically validated approach. The significance of addressing this problem is substantial, given the prevalence of clustered data in numerous applications.

The paper builds logically upon existing work in robust PCA [4, 13, 24] and matrix completion [3]. It correctly identifies the coherence parameter as a key bottleneck in RPCA’s performance and positions the work within the broader context of GPCA [22]. The mathematical analysis presented, culminating in Theorem 1, provides a clear and rigorous justification for the proposed approach. The connection made between coherence and underlying data structure – specifically, clustering – is insightful and well-explained. The proposed algorithm, utilizing RPCA to initialize dictionary learning for LRR, strikes a practical balance between theoretical elegance and computational efficiency. 

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The problem is clearly defined and motivated. The theoretical contributions are significant, offering a novel perspective on handling coherent data. The experimental results, demonstrating improved performance on both synthetic and real-world motion sequence data, strongly support the claims made. The clarity of the presentation is generally good, with definitions and notation clearly established. The inclusion of related work and a detailed discussion of the limitations of RPCA enhances the paper's context and impact.

However, there are areas for potential improvement. While the paper acknowledges the computational complexity benefits of using the learned dictionary in LRR, a more detailed discussion of the practical scalability of the proposed algorithm – especially for very large datasets – would be beneficial. Furthermore, the discussion of the iterative refinement of the dictionary (mentioned at the end of Section 3.3) could be expanded. While the authors state it often converges quickly, exploring its convergence properties or providing empirical evidence on its effectiveness would be valuable.

Regarding the reviewer's comment on subspace clustering/segmentation, this is an excellent and relevant question. Exploring the applicability of this method to subspace clustering problems would significantly broaden the impact of the work. While not essential for the core contribution, addressing this point, even briefly, would be a positive addition.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a novel approach to addressing a known limitation of RPCA, offering a theoretically grounded solution.
*   **Significance:** The problem addressed is important, as data coherence is common in many real-world applications.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and supports the proposed method.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach on both synthetic and real data.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the concepts and methodology.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Scalability discussion could be expanded:** A more thorough analysis of the computational cost and scalability for very large datasets would be helpful.
*   **Potential connection to subspace clustering not fully explored:** Addressing the reviewer's comment could further strengthen the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of robust PCA. The theoretical and empirical results are compelling, and the proposed method offers a practical solution to a challenging problem. Despite the minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
