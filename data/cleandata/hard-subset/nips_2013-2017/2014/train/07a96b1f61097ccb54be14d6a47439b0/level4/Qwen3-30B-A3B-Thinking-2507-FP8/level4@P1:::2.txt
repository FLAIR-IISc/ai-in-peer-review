

This paper addresses a significant limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) when applied to coherent data, which frequently arises in real-world applications due to clustering structures. The authors demonstrate that RPCA's performance degrades as data coherence increases (particularly with more clusters), and propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary to overcome this limitation. The core insight is that when the dictionary matrix A in LRR is itself low-rank and satisfies U₀ ⊂ U_A (where U₀ is the column space of the true low-rank matrix L₀), the method becomes immune to the coherence parameters that degrade RPCA's performance.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it formally establishes the relationship between clustering structures in data and the coherence parameters (particularly μ₂ and μ₃) that limit RPCA's performance. Second, it provides theoretical guarantees showing that LRR with a low-rank dictionary avoids these coherence dependencies. Third, it proposes a practical unsupervised algorithm that learns an effective dictionary from RPCA results, demonstrating superior performance on both synthetic data and motion sequences.

In terms of quality, the paper presents technically sound arguments supported by two main theorems. The noiseless and noisy recovery theorems establish precise conditions under which LRR with a low-rank dictionary can exactly or nearly exactly recover the low-rank component. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-justified, though the authors should clarify how their coherence parameter analysis differs from the unified μ(L₀) used in RPCA. The empirical validation is comprehensive, showing clear improvements over RPCA on both synthetic matrices (47% wider success area) and motion sequences (40% relative error reduction in subspace clustering).

Regarding clarity, the paper's main weakness is its presentation. The concept of coherence is introduced without sufficient intuitive explanation or practical interpretation, making it difficult for non-specialists to grasp why it matters. The relationship between clustering structures and coherence parameters is theoretically sound but not sufficiently explained for broader accessibility. The paper would benefit from a more intuitive discussion of coherence and clearer articulation of how the dictionary learning algorithm addresses the practical challenges of real-world data. The theoretical results are well-presented but could be made more accessible with better organization and more explicit connections to the main contributions.

The paper demonstrates strong originality by combining two recent techniques (RPCA and LRR) in a novel way to address a previously unexplored limitation of RPCA. The authors establish new theoretical connections between clustering structures, coherence parameters, and recovery guarantees that weren't previously recognized. While LRR with dictionary learning has been studied, the specific focus on coherence and the theoretical analysis of how low-rank dictionaries mitigate coherence issues is novel.

In terms of significance, this work makes a meaningful contribution to the field. It provides a theoretically grounded approach to handling coherent data, which is ubiquitous in real applications, and demonstrates practical improvements over the state-of-the-art RPCA method. The proposed dictionary learning algorithm is simple yet effective, with only a modest computational overhead (about 1.2x RPCA). The results on motion sequences are particularly compelling, showing that their approach significantly improves subspace clustering performance when applied as a preprocessing step.

The paper's main limitations are its lack of accessibility to non-specialists and the empirical nature of the dictionary learning algorithm (though the empirical results are strong). The authors should address these concerns by providing clearer explanations of coherence and its practical implications, and by better articulating how the dictionary learning approach relates to the theoretical guarantees.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides theoretically grounded solution to a significant limitation of RPCA
- Demonstrates clear empirical improvements over RPCA on both synthetic and real-world data
- Establishes novel theoretical connections between clustering structures and coherence parameters
- Proposes a practical algorithm with modest computational overhead
- Makes a meaningful contribution to robust low-rank matrix recovery

Arguments against acceptance:
- Current presentation is too technical for broad accessibility
- Dictionary learning approach is heuristic despite strong empirical results
- Some key concepts (like coherence) need better explanation for wider audience

Overall, this is a strong paper with clear theoretical contributions and compelling empirical results. The main concern is that the paper's presentation may limit its accessibility to a broader audience, which is particularly important for NIPS. With careful revision to improve clarity and accessibility (particularly around the coherence concept), this would be an excellent contribution to the field. The authors have demonstrated a significant advance in understanding and handling coherent data, which is a common challenge in real-world applications. Given the quality of the technical content and the empirical validation, I recommend acceptance with major revision to improve the paper's accessibility.
