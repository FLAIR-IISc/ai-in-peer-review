This paper addresses the problem of robustly estimating the low-dimensional subspace of contaminated observations when the observations are inherently coherent. A standard theoretical bottleneck of previous Robust PCA (RPCA) methods is that performance degrades with increasing data coherence. This paper circumvents this problem in a clever manner. Considering that such cluster structure is rather common in realistic data, solving this issue is certainly significantly meaningful. The proposed method is both theoretically sound and well demonstrated to perform well in practice.

The authors begin by observing that RPCA’s performance suffers when the underlying low-rank matrix exhibits clustering structures, leading to increased coherence. They demonstrate that this coherence can be avoided by employing Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully constructed dictionary. Specifically, they prove that if the dictionary itself is low-rank, LRR becomes immune to the coherence parameter that increases with the number of clusters. This provides a fundamental principle for dealing with coherent data and leads to a practical algorithm for dictionary learning in unsupervised settings. The approach is validated through experiments on both randomly generated matrices and real motion sequences. 

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The problem addressed is important, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are convincing. The connection between coherence and underlying data structure (clustering) is insightful and provides a clear motivation for the proposed approach. The proposed algorithm is also practical and offers improvements over existing methods, as demonstrated by the experimental results. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making the technical contributions accessible.

A potential weakness, although not a major one, is that the paper focuses primarily on the noiseless case in the theoretical analysis. While a noisy case is addressed, it’s less detailed than the noiseless case. Additionally, while the experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on motion sequences, exploring a wider range of real-world datasets could further strengthen the claims.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in robust PCA.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides a strong foundation for the proposed method.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach and its superiority to RPCA.
*   The insights regarding the relationship between coherence and data structure are valuable.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis is more focused on the noiseless case.
*   The experiments could be expanded to include a more diverse range of real-world datasets.

I have a question for the authors: Can the proposed method also be applied to solve subspace clustering/segmentation problems? Providing some comments on this point would be valuable.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of robust PCA. The quality of the paper is very good and I recommend acceptance.
