

Review:  
This paper presents a novel theoretical and methodological advance in robust matrix decomposition by leveraging Low Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary. The core insight demonstrates that the incoherence parameter μ—which traditionally limits recovery guarantees in Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA)—can correspond to an inherent clustering structure within the data. Under mild conditions on the dictionary A, the authors prove that this structure can be exploited via LRR to partially eliminate μ’s dependence in both exact and noisy recovery settings. This yields theoretically stronger guarantees than standard RPCA, addressing a long-standing bottleneck.  

The work is significant and well-motivated. It builds upon seminal subspace clustering frameworks (e.g., Wang & Xu’s "Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering," Soltanolkotabi & Candes’ geometric analysis) but clarifies a critical assumption: the data’s *clustering structure*—not just low-rankness—enables the relaxation of μ constraints. This is a meaningful refinement, as prior works often implicitly assumed union-of-subspace structures without explicitly linking them to coherence. The empirical validation on synthetic and real data (including text applications noted in the paper) robustly supports the theory, and the identified structure likely generalizes to domains like document clustering or sensor networks.  

**Strengths**:  
- The theoretical contribution is substantial: it reinterprets μ’s role through a clustering lens, offering a new pathway for robust decomposition.  
- The connection to subspace clustering is elegantly articulated, resolving a key ambiguity in prior work (e.g., Wang & Xu’s observation that higher coherence *benefits* subspace clustering, unlike RPCA, is now contextualized via the union-of-subspace structure).  
- The Hopkins155 experiments demonstrate a *practical breakthrough*: the proposed method (LRR with dictionary A) significantly outperforms standard RPCA+SSC in handling sparse corruption, a hidden but impactful contribution.  

**Minor Issues Requiring Clarification**:  
1. **Line 80–82**: The phrase "our data" is ambiguous. Specify *which* data or context is referenced. Additionally, the claim that "the problem captures all structures" requires qualification—perfect recovery still necessitates the condition on μ (or its relaxation), as stated in Theorem 3.  
2. **Line 88**: The phrase "is no longer a method of perfect (WHAT?)" appears to be a typo. Clarify to "is no longer a method for perfect recovery" and ensure consistency with the theoretical conditions.  
3. **General Clarity**: Proofread for precision (e.g., "more probably more general" → "likely more general"). Avoid vague terms like "some" or "often"; specify *how* μ’s dependence is reduced (e.g., "reduces μ’s exponent in the recovery bound").  

**Key Suggestions for Revision**:  
- **Clarify the clustering-RPCA link**: Explicitly contrast the paper’s approach with Wang & Xu’s work. While Wang & Xu note that *cluster-wise* low-rankness (not global low-rankness) makes high coherence beneficial, this paper shows that *dictionary-based LRR* can exploit this structure to bypass μ constraints in RPCA. This is a distinct mechanism.  
- **Highlight the Hopkins155 contribution**: Section 4.2’s method for sparse-corruption subspace clustering is a major standalone advance. Emphasize that it *partially solves* a known gap (no prior provable guarantees for two-step approaches like "subspace clustering followed by PCA"), and that the Hopkins155 results demonstrate its practical superiority.  
- **Address a critical comparison**: The paper should briefly discuss why a two-step method (e.g., noisy SSC → PCA per subspace) is suboptimal despite being intuitive. Note that while no provable guarantees exist for this approach, the current work’s end-to-end LRR+dictionary framework achieves both theoretical guarantees *and* better empirical performance.  

**Conclusion**:  
This paper makes at least three high-impact contributions:  
1. **Theoretical**: Revealing μ’s link to clustering structure and its relaxation via LRR.  
2. **Methodological**: Proposing a dictionary-based LRR framework that implicitly exploits this structure.  
3. **Practical**: Delivering a state-of-the-art solution for sparse-corrupted subspace clustering (validated on Hopkins155).  

The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and directly relevant to NIPS’ focus on scalable, theoretically grounded learning. Minor revisions to clarify ambiguities and sharpen the narrative will elevate an already strong submission. I recommend **acceptance** with confidence.
