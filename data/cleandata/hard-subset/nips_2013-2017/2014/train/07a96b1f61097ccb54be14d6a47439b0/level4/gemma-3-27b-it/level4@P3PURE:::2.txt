This paper combines two recent techniques – robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection – to address the problem of restoring corrupted observation matrices. The key insight is that, for structured data (e.g., clustered data), the theoretical guarantees of robust PCA may be insufficient, and that representing the underlying structure with a learned dictionary can yield stronger recovery bounds. The authors support these claims with theoretical results, presenting two theorems that elaborate on conditions under which reconstruction is possible given a dictionary. The remaining challenge – dictionary learning – is addressed with an algorithm justified by empirical results: first performing robust PCA, then representing the resulting low-rank component using a dictionary.

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound, pending a thorough verification of all details. The two theorems rigorously define conditions under which reconstruction is feasible, given a dictionary. However, the justification for the dictionary learning algorithm relies primarily on empirical evidence. 

**Clarity:**

Clarity is the paper’s main weakness. While the overall structure is logical, the core contributions and potential impact of the work are not clearly articulated, limiting accessibility to a very specialized audience. Claims would benefit from more precise definition, and the significance of the results needs to be more thoroughly explicated. For instance, the concept of “coherence” is difficult to grasp, and its interpretation is indirect (and relegated to the appendix). A potential impact of this work is that leveraging dictionaries may circumvent limitations in prior approaches, but this is not explicitly stated. The language also requires careful editing. It would be valuable to solicit feedback from the authors regarding potential improvements in clarity and presentation.

**Originality:**

The paper builds upon a combination of recent techniques, but introduces new theorems and empirical results that demonstrate the benefit of this combination.

**Significance:**

This paper advances the state-of-the-art with rigorous theoretical results. Although the proposed algorithm for dictionary learning is heuristic, its clear improvements over alternative algorithms suggest that the insights presented are valuable and practically relevant.
