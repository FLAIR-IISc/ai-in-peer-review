This paper combines two recent techniques – robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection – to address the problem of restoring corrupted observation matrices. The key insight is that, for structured data (e.g., clustered data), the theoretical guarantees of robust PCA may be insufficient. By representing the underlying structure with a dictionary, the authors demonstrate that stronger reconstruction bounds can be achieved. Theorems are presented to support these claims. The remaining challenge lies in dictionary learning, for which the paper proposes an algorithm justified by empirical results: first, perform robust PCA, and then represent the resulting low-rank matrix using a dictionary.

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound, with a level of rigor appropriate for careful review. The two theorems clearly articulate the conditions under which reconstruction is possible given a dictionary. However, the justification for the dictionary learning algorithm is primarily empirical.

**Clarity:**

Clarity is the primary weakness of the paper. While the overall structure is logical, the main contributions and potential impact are not explained clearly enough to be accessible to a broad specialist audience. The claims require more precise definition, and the significance of the results needs to be more thoroughly explicated. For instance, the concept of “coherence” is difficult to grasp, and its interpretation is indirect (and relegated to the appendix). A potential impact of this work is that leveraging dictionaries may allow one to circumvent challenges encountered in previous approaches. Furthermore, the language throughout the paper would benefit from careful editing. 

It would be valuable to solicit feedback from the authors regarding how these clarity issues could be addressed.

**Originality:**

The paper builds upon a combination of recent techniques, but it also includes novel theorems and empirical results that demonstrate the benefits of this combination.

**Significance:**

This paper advances the state-of-the-art with rigorous theoretical results. Although the proposed algorithm for dictionary learning is heuristic, clear improvements over alternative algorithms indicate that the insights presented in this work are valuable.

The paper presents clearly publishable content and contains interesting contributions. My remaining concern is that substantial rewriting will be required to make the paper accessible to specialists in the field. If a “major revision” option were available, I would recommend it. The paper effectively combines two recent ideas and provides both theoretical results and a heuristic, yet empirically well-performing, algorithm.
