

This paper addresses a significant limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) when dealing with coherent data, which is common in real-world applications due to clustering structures. While RPCA has been widely adopted for low-rank matrix recovery from grossly corrupted observations, its performance degrades as data coherence increases—particularly when data exhibits clustering structures that cause the second and third coherence parameters (µ2, µ3) to rise with the number of clusters. The authors demonstrate that this limitation can be overcome by using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a properly configured low-rank dictionary, providing both theoretical guarantees and a practical algorithm.

The paper builds upon the foundational RPCA work of Candès et al. (2011) and extends it by addressing a previously overlooked aspect of data coherence. While RPCA has been successfully applied in various domains like image processing and computer vision, its theoretical guarantees depend on the coherence assumption (µ(L0) = max{µ1(L0), µ2(L0), µ3(L0)}), which is often violated in real-world data with clustering structures. The authors' key insight is that when the dictionary matrix A in LRR is low-rank itself, LRR becomes immune to the coherence parameters that increase with cluster number. They prove this theoretically (Theorems 1 and 2) and provide an unsupervised algorithm (Algorithm 1) that constructs the dictionary using RPCA's output, which consistently outperforms RPCA in experiments.

The paper's main strengths are its theoretical rigor, clear exposition, and practical validation. The authors provide detailed proofs showing that their method avoids the coherence parameters that limit RPCA, with success conditions that are looser than RPCA's (O(n2/log n1) vs. O(n2/(log n1)2)). The experimental results on both synthetic data (Figure 4) and motion sequences (Table 1) are convincing, demonstrating a 47% wider success area for their algorithm compared to RPCA on random matrices and a 40% relative improvement in clustering error rates on real motion data. The paper also makes an important conceptual contribution by revealing the physical meaning of coherence parameters, showing they are not merely theoretical assumptions but reflect intrinsic data structures.

For the strengths, the paper successfully identifies and addresses a meaningful limitation of RPCA that has been overlooked in the literature. The theoretical results are well-supported, and the practical algorithm is straightforward to implement. The connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters is particularly insightful and well-explained. The paper is clearly written with consistent notation, and the experimental evaluation is comprehensive and relevant to real-world applications.

The main weakness is that the paper could better connect its method to subspace clustering applications, which are a natural extension of the matrix recovery problem. The authors note that their method improves subspace clustering results (Table 1), but they do not explicitly discuss how their dictionary learning approach could be integrated into subspace clustering algorithms as a preprocessing step. This would strengthen the significance claim and provide more context for how their method fits into the broader landscape of subspace learning.

Another minor point is that while the paper provides theoretical guarantees for the noiseless case, the practical implications of the noisy case (Theorem 2) could be further explored in the context of real-world noise models. However, this is a minor consideration given the strong empirical results presented.

In terms of the four criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and thorough experimental validation. The authors carefully address both strengths and limitations of their approach.
- Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear mathematical notation and logical flow. The experiments are well-designed and effectively support the theoretical claims.
- Originality: The paper identifies a previously overlooked limitation of RPCA (coherence due to clustering structures) and proposes a novel solution using LRR with a low-rank dictionary. This is a significant contribution that advances the state of the art in robust matrix recovery.
- Significance: The problem addressed is highly significant for real-world applications where data often exhibits clustering structures (e.g., motion segmentation, image processing). The proposed method consistently outperforms RPCA in both synthetic and real data experiments, demonstrating clear practical value.

The paper makes an important contribution to the robust matrix recovery literature by addressing a key limitation of RPCA that has been overlooked in previous work. The theoretical insights about coherence parameters are valuable for understanding the fundamental limitations of current methods, and the proposed algorithm provides a practical solution that works well in real-world scenarios. The connection to clustering structures is particularly compelling, as this is a ubiquitous phenomenon in modern data.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel solution that advances the state of the art. It makes a clear contribution to the field by identifying a limitation of RPCA that has not been previously addressed and providing both theoretical guarantees and practical algorithms to overcome it.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important limitation of RPCA that has been overlooked in the literature
- Provides strong theoretical guarantees with clear proofs
- Demonstrates consistent practical improvements over RPCA in both synthetic and real data
- Offers valuable insights about the physical meaning of coherence parameters
- Well-written, clear, and well-organized

Arguments against acceptance:
- Minor opportunity to strengthen connection to subspace clustering applications
- Theoretical bounds in the noisy case could be more thoroughly discussed in practical contexts

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery. The authors have identified a significant limitation of RPCA, provided a theoretically sound solution, and demonstrated its practical value through comprehensive experiments. The paper is well-written and would be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
