This paper combines two recent techniques—robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection—to restore corrupted observation matrices. The key idea is that for structured data (e.g., clustered data), the guarantees of robust PCA alone are insufficient; by explicitly representing structure with a dictionary, stronger recovery bounds can be established. The paper presents theorems supporting these claims. The remaining challenge is learning the dictionary. For this, the authors propose an empirically justified algorithm: first apply robust PCA, then represent the result using a learned dictionary.

Quality:
The paper appears technically sound to a reasonable degree of scrutiny. The two theorems specify conditions under which accurate reconstruction is possible, provided an appropriate dictionary is available. The approach to learning the dictionary is heuristic and supported primarily by empirical evidence.

Clarity:
Clarity is the main weakness. While the overall structure is clear, the core contributions and their impact are not explained in a way that is accessible beyond a narrow specialist audience. The claims need more precise statements, and the practical significance of the results should be articulated more explicitly. In particular, the notion of coherence is difficult to understand and is interpreted only indirectly (and mainly in the appendix). One potential contribution of the paper is that using dictionaries may allow prior limitations in related work to be avoided, but this point is underdeveloped. The writing would also benefit from careful language editing. It would be valuable for the authors to comment on how they plan to improve clarity, define key concepts more directly, and highlight the broader impact.

Originality:
The work combines recent ideas but contributes new theorems and empirical results that support the value of the combination.

Significance:
The paper advances the state of the art with rigorous results. Although the proposed dictionary-learning method is heuristic, it shows clear improvements over alternative algorithms, indicating that the paper’s insights are practically useful.

Comment after the author rebuttal and reviewer discussion:
The paper contains publishable content and interesting contributions. My remaining concern is that substantial rewriting is likely needed to make it accessible beyond a small group of specialists. If a “major revision” option were available, I would recommend it. The paper effectively combines two recent ideas, providing both theoretical results and a heuristic, empirically strong algorithm.
