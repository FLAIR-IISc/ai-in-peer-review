

The paper investigates the limitations of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) when dealing with coherent data—data exhibiting clustering structures that increase coherence parameters, degrading RPCA's performance. The authors propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a properly designed dictionary matrix A to overcome this limitation. They prove that when A is low-rank and satisfies U₀ ⊂ U_A (where U₀ is the column space of the true low-rank matrix L₀), LRR becomes immune to the coherence parameters that plague RPCA. This provides a theoretical foundation for leveraging clustering structures in data, and the authors develop a practical unsupervised algorithm that constructs A using RPCA's output, followed by simple post-processing steps.

The work builds upon and extends previous NIPS contributions in robust matrix recovery, particularly the RPCA framework from Candès et al. (NIPS 2009) and LRR from Liu et al. (NIPS 2010). While prior work on subspace clustering (e.g., Soltanolkotabi & Candes, NIPS 2011; Wang & Xu, NIPS 2013) has explored union-of-subspace structures, this paper uniquely identifies how such structures manifest as high coherence parameters in RPCA, and provides a principled method to address this issue. The authors also connect their findings to broader matrix factorization techniques, noting that their approach helps explain why factorization methods are effective in practice.

**Strengths:**
1. **Originality and Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters, which had not been explicitly addressed in previous work. The proof that LRR with low-rank dictionaries avoids dependence on coherence parameters is novel and well-executed.
   
2. **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed unsupervised algorithm (Algorithm 1) is straightforward, computationally efficient (only ~1.2× the cost of RPCA), and demonstrates clear empirical improvements over RPCA in both synthetic and real-world motion sequence data.

3. **Significance:** The work addresses a genuine limitation of RPCA that impacts real-world applications where data naturally exhibit clustering structures (e.g., motion segmentation, image processing). The Hopkins155 experiments show a 40% relative improvement in clustering error rates over RPCA, demonstrating tangible practical impact.

4. **Clarity of Presentation:** Despite minor grammatical issues in the draft, the paper is well-organized, with clear mathematical notation and thorough experimental validation.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Clarity Issues:** Some statements require refinement for precision (e.g., "the method might not produce perfect recovery" should specify under what conditions). The draft contains a few grammatical errors and ambiguous phrases that could confuse readers (e.g., "is no longer a method of perfect" on line 88).

2. **Comparison to Related Work:** While the connection to subspace clustering literature is noted, the paper could more explicitly contrast its approach with Wang & Xu's "Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering," which demonstrates that higher coherence can be beneficial for subspace clustering (unlike RPCA), though under different assumptions (local low-rankness vs. global low-rankness).

3. **Theoretical Scope:** The theoretical results are limited to the noiseless and noisy LRR settings. A comparison to alternative approaches for handling coherent data (e.g., first performing subspace clustering with ℓ₁ penalties followed by PCA for each cluster) would strengthen the contribution.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The paper identifies a fundamental limitation of RPCA that has been overlooked in prior work.
- It provides a theoretically grounded solution that improves upon RPCA both in theory and practice.
- The unsupervised algorithm is practical, efficient, and demonstrates significant empirical gains.
- The insights about coherence parameters and their relationship to clustering structures have broad applicability beyond the specific problem addressed.

*Con:*
- Minor grammatical and clarity issues in the draft that need to be addressed.
- The theoretical results are somewhat limited to the LRR framework without broader exploration of alternative methods.
- The connection to the "noisy subspace clustering" literature could be more explicit.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a strong paper that makes a substantial contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery. The theoretical insights are novel, the proposed method is practical, and the experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The connection between coherence parameters and clustering structures is particularly insightful and has potential implications for a wide range of applications. While the draft contains minor clarity issues that should be addressed, the core contribution is significant and well-supported.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria:
- **Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound and well-supported by experiments.
- **Clarity:** The paper is well-written with clear notation and organization (minor improvements needed).
- **Originality:** It identifies a new problem (coherent data in RPCA) and provides a novel solution, distinct from previous NIPS work.
- **Significance:** The results have clear practical impact, as demonstrated by the Hopkins155 experiments.

I recommend acceptance. The paper's contribution to understanding coherence in low-rank matrix recovery is important, and the proposed method offers a practical improvement over the state-of-the-art that will be valuable to practitioners.
