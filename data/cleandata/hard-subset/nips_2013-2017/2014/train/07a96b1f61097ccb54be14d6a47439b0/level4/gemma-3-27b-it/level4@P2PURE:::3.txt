The paper proposes leveraging Low Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary A to enhance the performance of RPCA. Specifically, the authors demonstrate that the incoherence parameter μ, often considered a limitation in RPCA recovery guarantees, can in fact reflect underlying clustering structure within the data. Under certain conditions on the dictionary A, they show that exploiting this structure via LRR can reduce the dependence on μ, leading to theoretically stronger guarantees for both exact low-rank and sparse decomposition, and its noisy extension.

This work is novel and represents a significant contribution to the field. It relates to prior work on subspace clustering assuming a union-of-subspace structure, such as the papers by Wang and Xu ("Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering & Provable subspace clustering: When LRR meets SSC") and Soltanolkotabi and Candes ("A geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers") and Soltanolkotabi et al ("Robust subspace clustering"), but is less restrictive in its assumptions regarding the explicit nature of the “clustering” structure, potentially making it more general. The simulation and real data experiments validate the theoretical analysis, and I believe the identified structure could be relevant to numerous other applications, including text data as noted by the authors.

In summary, the paper presents a substantial contribution to compressed sensing, and I recommend its acceptance to NIPS. 

However, I have several discussion points and stylistic suggestions, detailed below.

1. Lines 80-82 require rewriting for clarity. The meaning of “our data” is unclear. Furthermore, even if the problem formulation captures all relevant data structures, additional conditions are still necessary for “perfect recovery.”
2. Line 88: “…is no longer a method of perfect [what?]” – please complete this thought.
3. The paper would benefit from careful proofreading to address issues like the examples above. Claims should be specific and precise, and the text should clearly define its references. Generally, the clarity of the language should be improved.
4. Wang and Xu (“Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering”) previously demonstrated that a higher coherence parameter can be beneficial for subspace clustering (in contrast to RPCA and matrix completion). The key distinction is that their approach does not require the overall data to be low-rank, only that each individual cluster possesses a low-rank structure. The method presented in this paper can be viewed as a combination of global low-rank structure and a union-of-subspace cluster structure.
5. It would be insightful to compare the proposed algorithm against a two-step approach: first performing noisy subspace clustering with an \ell_1 penalty, and then applying PCA to each resulting subspace. While a provable guarantee for the latter step currently does not exist, a practical comparison would be valuable.
6. Section 4.2 effectively introduces a novel method for solving subspace clustering problems with sparse corruption. The significant performance improvements over standard RPCA+SSC on the Hopkins155 dataset represent a noteworthy, yet currently understated, contribution of this paper. I believe the paper should be accepted based on at least three key contributions: 1) the characterization of a clustering structure that can lead to high coherence, 2) the demonstration of how this structure can be implicitly exploited through a dictionary in LRR, and 3) a partial solution to the sparse corruption problem in subspace clustering.
