This paper combines two recent techniques—robust PCA and dictionary-based, structure-preserving projection—for restoring corrupted observation matrices. The key insight is that for structured data (e.g., clustered data), the standard guarantees of robust PCA are not strong enough; by explicitly representing structure with a dictionary, the authors can provide stronger bounds. Theorems are presented to support these claims. The remaining challenge is learning the dictionary. To address this, the paper proposes an empirically justified algorithm: first compute robust PCA, then represent the result with a dictionary.

Quality:
The paper appears technically sound to a reasonable degree of scrutiny. The two theorems articulate conditions under which reconstruction is possible, given a suitable dictionary. However, the approach to learning the dictionary is justified only empirically.

Clarity:
Clarity is the main limitation. While the overall structure is clear, the core contributions and their impact are not explained clearly enough to be accessible beyond a very narrow specialist audience. The claims need to be more precisely defined, and the implications of the results should be made explicit. For example, the concept of coherence is difficult to understand as presented, and the ways to interpret it are indirect (and relegated to the appendix). One potential impact—namely, that using dictionaries may allow one to sidestep many earlier problems—deserves clearer articulation. The language also needs careful editing. It would be important for the authors to comment on how they plan to address these issues.

Originality:
The paper combines recent techniques and contributes new theorems and empirical results that support the usefulness of the combination.

Significance:
The work advances the state of the art with rigorous results. Although the proposed algorithm for learning the dictionary is heuristic, the clear improvements over alternative algorithms indicate that the paper’s insights are useful in practice.

Comment after the author rebuttal and reviewer discussion:
There is clearly publishable content and interesting contributions. My remaining concern is that substantial effort will be required to rewrite the paper to make it accessible to a broader audience beyond specialists. If there were an option for a “major revision,” I would vote for it. The paper combines two recent ideas and offers both theoretical results and a heuristic, but empirically strong, algorithm.
