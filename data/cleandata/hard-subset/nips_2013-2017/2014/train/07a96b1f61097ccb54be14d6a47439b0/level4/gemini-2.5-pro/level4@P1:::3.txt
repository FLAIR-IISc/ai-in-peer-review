This paper addresses the problem of recovering a low-rank matrix from observations corrupted by gross, sparse errors. The authors extend the popular Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) framework by demonstrating that a key parameter limiting its performance—the incoherence parameter µ—can arise from an underlying clustering structure within the data. They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary to exploit this structure. Under certain conditions on the dictionary, the proposed approach can partially remove the dependence on µ, leading to theoretically stronger recovery guarantees for both noiseless and noisy data. The paper presents a practical two-stage algorithm where RPCA is first used to generate an initial estimate, which then serves to construct a dictionary for a subsequent LRR step.

This is a strong paper that makes several significant contributions to the field of robust low-rank matrix recovery. I recommend it for acceptance.

The main strengths of the work are:
1.  **Novel Connection between Coherence and Data Structure:** The paper provides a valuable insight by formally connecting the high coherence parameter in RPCA, often seen as a restrictive theoretical assumption, to the presence of natural clustering structures in the data. This helps explain why RPCA's performance can degrade on real-world datasets that exhibit such properties.
2.  **Principled Use of LRR:** The authors demonstrate how LRR with a properly chosen low-rank dictionary can effectively mitigate the challenges posed by coherent data. The theoretical analysis, which shows that LRR can be immune to the coherence parameter µ2, is a key contribution and provides a clear principle for dictionary design.
3.  **A New State-of-the-Art Method for Subspace Clustering:** A significant, and perhaps understated, contribution is the practical application to subspace clustering on corrupted data. As shown in Section 4.2, the proposed algorithm serves as a new state-of-the-art preprocessing step, achieving substantial performance gains over the standard RPCA+SSC pipeline on the Hopkins155 dataset.

The theoretical claims are well-supported by thorough experiments on both synthetic and real-world motion data, confirming the practical benefits of the proposed approach. The framework is also sufficiently general, as its assumption of "clustering" is less explicit than the strict union-of-subspaces model seen in some prior work, potentially broadening its applicability.

While the paper is strong, I have a few suggestions for improvement that could further strengthen the manuscript:

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper would benefit from a careful proofreading to enhance clarity and precision. For instance, some phrasing is ambiguous (e.g., "our data," line 81) or incomplete (e.g., "a method of perfection," line 88). Ensuring precise language throughout would improve the paper's readability.

2.  **Discussion of Related Work:** The paper could be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of its relationship to the subspace clustering literature. For example, Wang and Xu ("Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering") have shown that higher coherence can actually be beneficial for subspace clustering, where the goal is to segment data lying in a union of subspaces rather than recover a single global low-rank matrix. The structure exploited in this paper seems to be a hybrid of a global low-rank model and a local union-of-subspaces model. Clarifying these connections and differences would provide valuable context for the reader.

3.  **Additional Baselines:** It would be interesting to compare the proposed algorithm against an alternative two-stage baseline: first, use a robust subspace clustering method to identify data clusters, and then apply PCA to each cluster individually. While this approach may lack the theoretical guarantees of the proposed method, an empirical comparison would be informative.
