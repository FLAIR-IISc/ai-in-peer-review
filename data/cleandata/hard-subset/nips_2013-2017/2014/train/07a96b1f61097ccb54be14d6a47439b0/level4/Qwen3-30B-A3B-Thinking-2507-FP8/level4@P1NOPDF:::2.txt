

This paper combines robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection to restore corrupted observation matrices. The key insight addresses a limitation of robust PCA for structured (e.g., clustered) data: its guarantees are insufficient, but representing structure via a dictionary enables stronger theoretical bounds. Theorems establish conditions for successful reconstruction given a dictionary, though the paper leaves the dictionary-learning problem unresolved. The proposed solution—first applying robust PCA, then representing the result with a dictionary—is empirically justified but lacks theoretical grounding.  

**Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically sound within reasonable verification limits. The two theorems rigorously characterize reconstruction under dictionary-based assumptions. However, the dictionary-learning approach relies solely on empirical validation, which weakens the theoretical contribution.  

**Clarity:** This is the paper’s most significant weakness. While the structure is logical, the core contributions and broader impact remain poorly articulated, limiting accessibility beyond a narrow specialist audience. Key claims require sharper definition, and the implications of the results need explicit elaboration. For instance, the concept of "coherence" is introduced without intuitive explanation and its interpretation is relegated to the appendix, making it difficult to grasp. The paper also hints at a potential impact—using dictionaries to circumvent limitations in prior work—but fails to develop this insight. Language inconsistencies and grammatical errors further hinder readability. The authors must clarify these points to make the work accessible to a wider audience.  

**Originality:** The work integrates recent techniques but introduces novel theorems and empirical validation demonstrating the value of their combination. The theoretical contributions are non-trivial.  

**Significance:** The paper advances the state of the art with rigorous results. Despite the dictionary-learning step being heuristic, the empirical performance demonstrates clear improvements over alternatives, validating the core insights. The combination of theoretical guarantees and a well-performing algorithm represents a meaningful contribution.  

**Post-Rebuttal Note:** The authors have addressed concerns about the algorithm’s empirical validity. The work contains publishable content and interesting insights. However, the primary challenge remains improving accessibility. Substantial revision is required to clarify the contributions, define key concepts (e.g., coherence), and articulate the broader impact. If the authors can significantly enhance the exposition, this would be a strong candidate for publication. Given the current presentation, a major revision is essential to meet NIPS standards. The paper’s core ideas are promising, but the current form risks limiting its influence.
