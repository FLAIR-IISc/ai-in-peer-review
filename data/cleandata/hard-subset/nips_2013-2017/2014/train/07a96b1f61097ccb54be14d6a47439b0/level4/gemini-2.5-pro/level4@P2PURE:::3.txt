This paper proposes a method to improve the effectiveness of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) by using Low Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary `A`. The authors demonstrate that the incoherence parameter `μ`, often considered a bottleneck for recovery guarantees in RPCA, can correspond to an underlying clustering structure in the data. They show that under certain conditions on the dictionary `A`, LRR can exploit this structure to partially remove the dependence on `μ`. This leads to theoretically stronger recovery guarantees for both the exact low-rank and sparse decomposition problem and its noisy extension.

The proposed method is novel and significant. It is closely related to the union-of-subspaces structure assumed in prior work on subspace clustering, such as "Provable subspace clustering: When LRR meets SSC" by Wang and Xu, and "A geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers" by Soltanolkotabi and Candes. However, this paper's assumption on "clustering" is less explicit, which is reasonable and arguably more general. The theoretical analysis is well-supported by simulations and experiments on real data, and the identified structure is likely to be present in many other applications, such as text analysis.

In summary, the paper makes a substantial contribution to the field and merits acceptance at NIPS. I do, however, have several suggestions for improvement, which are detailed below.

**Detailed Comments:**

1.  The description in lines 80-82 requires clarification. For instance, the term "our data" is ambiguous. Furthermore, even if the proposed formulation captures all relevant data structures, perfect recovery is contingent upon additional conditions that should be stated.

2.  Line 88: The phrase "...is no longer a method of perfect" appears to be missing a word.

3.  The paper would benefit from careful proofreading to address minor issues like the ones noted above. The authors should strive for greater precision in their claims and references throughout the text to improve overall clarity.

4.  The observation that a high coherence parameter can be beneficial is related to the findings of Wang and Xu in "Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering." A key distinction in their work is that the data is not assumed to be globally low-rank; rather, only the data within each cluster must be low-rank. Consequently, the structure exploited in this paper can be viewed as a combination of a global low-rank structure and a union-of-subspaces clustering structure.

5.  It would be interesting to compare the proposed algorithm with a two-stage approach: first, performing noisy subspace clustering with an \(\ell_1\) penalty, and second, applying PCA to each identified subspace. It is worth noting, however, that theoretical guarantees for the latter approach are not yet established.

6.  Section 4.2 presents a significant, though understated, contribution: a new method for subspace clustering in the presence of sparse corruptions. This method yields substantial improvements over the standard RPCA+SSC baseline on the Hopkins155 dataset, and the authors should explicitly highlight this as a key result. The paper's acceptance is justified by at least three major contributions: 1) the identification of a clustering structure that can lead to high data coherence; 2) a demonstration that this structure can be implicitly exploited using LRR with a suitable dictionary; and 3) a novel approach that partially solves the problem of sparse corruptions in subspace clustering.
