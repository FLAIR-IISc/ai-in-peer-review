This paper introduces a method that utilizes Low Rank Representation (LRR), in conjunction with a learned dictionary A, to enhance the performance of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA). Specifically, the work demonstrates that the incoherence parameter μ, typically considered a limiting factor for recovery guarantees in RPCA, can actually signify an underlying clustering structure within the data. The authors prove that, under certain conditions on the dictionary A, this structure can be leveraged through LRR to mitigate the dependence on μ, thereby achieving a theoretically superior guarantee for both exact and noisy versions of low-rank and sparse decomposition.

The proposed methodology is both novel and represents a significant contribution to the area. It is conceptually related to the union-of-subspaces assumption prevalent in prior subspace clustering literature, including "Wang and Xu: Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering & Provable subspace clustering: When LRR meets SSC," "Soltanolkotabi and Candes: A geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers," and "Soltanolkotabi et al: Robust subspace clustering." However, the current work makes a less explicit, and arguably more general and reasonable, assumption about this "clustering" property. Both simulations and experiments on real-world datasets corroborate the theoretical findings, and I concur with the authors' remark that this newly identified structure is likely present in various other real-world applications, such as text data.

In conclusion, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the compressed sensing field, and I strongly recommend it for acceptance at NIPS. Nevertheless, I have several points for discussion and suggestions for stylistic improvement, which are detailed below.

1.  Lines 80-82: This section requires revision for clarity. The term "our data" is ambiguous. Furthermore, the claim of "perfect recovery" is contingent upon more than just capturing the problem's structure; additional conditions must be met and stated.
2.  Line 88: The sentence ending "...is no longer a method of perfect" is incomplete.
3.  The manuscript would benefit from a thorough proofreading to address issues like the two mentioned above. I encourage the authors to be more precise in their claims and definitions throughout the text to improve the overall clarity of the presentation.
4.  A relevant connection can be made to the work of Wang and Xu ("Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering"), where they also note that a higher coherence parameter can be beneficial for subspace clustering (in contrast to RPCA). The crucial distinction is that their framework only requires individual clusters to be low-rank, not the entire dataset. The structure leveraged in the present work can be viewed as an amalgamation of a global low-rank structure and a local union-of-subspaces cluster structure.
5.  It would be an interesting extension to compare the proposed algorithm with a two-stage baseline: first, performing noisy subspace clustering using an ℓ1 penalty, followed by applying PCA to each identified subspace. It is worth noting, however, that this alternative approach currently lacks a provable recovery guarantee.
6.  Section 4.2 presents what is effectively a new algorithm for subspace clustering in the presence of sparse data corruption. The resulting performance gains over the standard RPCA+SSC approach on the Hopkins155 dataset are significant and, in my view, constitute a noteworthy contribution that the authors should emphasize more explicitly. Ultimately, this paper merits acceptance based on at least three key contributions: 1) the characterization of a clustering structure that gives rise to high coherence; 2) a demonstration that LRR with a learned dictionary can implicitly exploit this structure; and 3) a partial solution to the challenging problem of sparse corruptions in subspace clustering.
