

This paper integrates two recent techniques—robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection—to restore corrupted observation matrices. The key insight is that robust PCA provides insufficient guarantees for structured (e.g., clustered) data, and representing such structure via a dictionary enables stronger theoretical bounds. Theorems are provided to substantiate this claim. The remaining challenge is dictionary learning, addressed by an algorithm supported solely by empirical results: first apply robust PCA, then represent the result using a dictionary.  

Quality:  
The paper is technically sound and sufficiently verified. The two theorems specify conditions under which reconstruction is possible given a dictionary. However, the dictionary learning approach lacks theoretical justification and relies solely on empirical validation.  

Clarity:  
Clarity is the paper’s primary weakness. While the structure is logical, the main contributions and broader impact are not sufficiently explained to be accessible beyond a narrow specialist audience. Claims require greater precision, and the significance of the results must be explicitly articulated. For instance, the concept of coherence is difficult to interpret and is only indirectly explained (with minimal interpretation provided in the appendix). One potential impact—that dictionaries may circumvent many prior challenges—remains underdeveloped. Additionally, language errors require correction. A response from the authors addressing these issues would be essential.  

Originality:  
The work combines recent techniques with novel theorems and empirical results demonstrating the utility of this combination.  

Significance:  
The paper advances the state-of-the-art with rigorous theoretical results. Although the dictionary-learning algorithm is heuristic, clear empirical improvements over alternatives confirm the practical value of the proposed insights.  

---  
Comment after author rebuttal and reviewer discussion:  
The paper contains publishable content and meaningful contributions. The sole remaining concern is the substantial effort required to revise the paper for accessibility to non-specialists. If a "major revision" option were available, I would endorse it. The work successfully combines two recent ideas, providing both theoretical results and a heuristic algorithm that performs well empirically.
