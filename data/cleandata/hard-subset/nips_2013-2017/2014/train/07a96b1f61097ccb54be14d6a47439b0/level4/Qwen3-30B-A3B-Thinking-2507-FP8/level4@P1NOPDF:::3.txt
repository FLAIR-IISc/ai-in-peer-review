

The paper introduces a novel approach leveraging low-rank representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary **A** to enhance robust principal component analysis (RPCA). Crucially, the authors demonstrate that the coherence parameter **μ**—typically viewed as a bottleneck in RPCA recovery guarantees—can instead correspond to an implicit clustering structure within the data. Under specific conditions on **A**, they show that LRR can exploit this structure to partially eliminate the dependence on **μ**, yielding theoretically stronger guarantees for both exact low-rank and sparse decomposition and their noisy extensions.  

This work is both novel and significant. It builds upon the union-of-subspaces framework central to subspace clustering literature (e.g., Wang & Xu, *Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering*; Soltanolkotabi & Candes, *A Geometric Analysis of Subspace Clustering with Outliers*; Soltanolkotabi et al., *Robust Subspace Clustering*), but offers a more explicit characterization of the "clustering" assumption, which is reasonable and likely more general. The simulation and real-data experiments robustly validate the theoretical findings, and the authors correctly note that this structure may manifest in diverse applications, including text data.  

The paper makes three substantial contributions to the field: (1) identifying a clustering structure that explains high coherence values, (2) demonstrating how LRR with a learned dictionary implicitly exploits this structure, and (3) providing a practical solution to the sparse-corruption problem in subspace clustering. The latter is particularly noteworthy: Section 4.2 proposes a new method for subspace clustering with sparse corruption, achieving significant improvements over standard RPCA+SSC on the Hopkins155 dataset—a contribution the authors should emphasize.  

**Minor concerns for clarification and precision:**  
1. *Lines 80–82*: Clarify the reference to "our data" and explicitly state that even with the proposed formulation, additional conditions are required for perfect recovery.  
2. *Line 88*: The phrase "is no longer a method of perfect (WHAT?)" is unclear; revise to precisely specify the context (e.g., "is no longer a method for perfect recovery").  
3. *Citation consistency*: Wang & Xu’s observation that higher coherence *benefits* subspace clustering (unlike RPCA) merits explicit contrast: their method requires only per-cluster low-rankness, whereas RPCA demands global low-rankness. The paper’s approach effectively combines low-rank structure with union-of-subspaces clustering.  
4. *Section 4.2*: Highlight the subspace clustering contribution more prominently. A comparison with the heuristic of first applying noisy subspace clustering (with ℓ₁ penalty) followed by per-subspace PCA would be valuable, though no provable guarantees currently exist for the latter.  

The writing requires careful proofreading to ensure precision. For instance, avoid ambiguous phrasing like "the problem captures all structures" and instead specify *which* structures are captured under what conditions. All technical claims should be unambiguously tied to the paper’s assumptions and results.  

In summary, the paper advances compressed sensing and subspace clustering by resolving a key theoretical bottleneck (coherence dependence) through an elegant structural insight. The contributions are substantial, the theory is sound, and the empirical validation is compelling. The work merits acceptance at NIPS.
