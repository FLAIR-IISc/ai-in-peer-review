This paper proposes a method for restoring corrupted observation matrices by combining two recent techniques: robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection. The central premise is that for structured data, such as clustered data, the recovery guarantees of standard robust PCA are insufficient. The authors argue that by explicitly representing this structure with a dictionary, stronger theoretical bounds on recovery can be established. This claim is supported by theorems that provide conditions for successful reconstruction, given a suitable dictionary. To address the practical problem of learning this dictionary, the paper proposes a two-step heuristic algorithm: first, apply robust PCA to the corrupted data, and second, learn a dictionary from the resulting low-rank component. The justification for this algorithm is primarily empirical.

**Quality:**
The paper appears technically sound. The two main theorems establish the conditions under which data reconstruction is guaranteed, assuming a known dictionary. The proposed method for learning the dictionary, however, is not theoretically justified and relies instead on empirical validation.

**Clarity:**
Clarity is the paper's primary weakness. Although the paper is well-structured, the exposition of its main contributions and potential impact is not accessible to a general machine learning audience, limiting its reach to a narrow subfield. The central claims and the significance of the theoretical results need to be stated more precisely and explained more thoroughly. For example, the concept of 'coherence' is difficult to grasp, and its interpretation is made more challenging by being indirect and relegated to the appendix. Furthermore, the paper hints at a potentially significant impact—that using dictionaries could sidestep limitations of prior methods—but this point is not sufficiently developed. The manuscript would also benefit from a thorough proofreading to improve language and grammar.

I would encourage the authors to comment in their rebuttal on how they plan to address these clarity issues to make the work more accessible.

**Originality:**
The paper's originality lies in its novel synthesis of two existing techniques. While the approach combines recent methods rather than introducing a fundamentally new one, it contributes original theorems and compelling empirical results that demonstrate the value of this combination.

**Significance:**
The paper advances the state of the art by providing rigorous theoretical results for dictionary-based recovery. Despite the heuristic nature of the proposed dictionary learning algorithm, its strong empirical performance and clear improvements over existing methods demonstrate the practical utility of the paper's core insights.

The paper contains publishable material and makes interesting contributions. My primary remaining concern is the significant effort that will be required to revise the paper for accessibility beyond a specialist audience.
