This paper presents a novel approach to restoring corrupted observation matrices by integrating robust PCA with dictionary-based structure-preserving projection. The core idea is that leveraging a dictionary to represent inherent data structure allows for stronger recovery guarantees than those achievable with robust PCA alone, particularly for structured datasets like those exhibiting clustering. The authors provide theoretical support for this claim in the form of two theorems governing reconstruction conditions given a dictionary. A practical algorithm for dictionary learning, supported by empirical results, is then proposed – it involves initially performing robust PCA and subsequently representing the outcome using a dictionary.

**Quality:** The paper is technically solid, demonstrating a reasonable level of rigor. The theorems clearly delineate the conditions under which reconstruction is possible given a learned dictionary. However, the justification for the dictionary learning process itself is primarily empirical.

**Clarity:** The main weakness of the paper lies in its clarity. While logically structured, the key contributions and broader impact of the work are not readily accessible to those outside a very specialized field. Claims require more precise definition, and the significance of the results needs to be more thoroughly explained. Specifically, the concept of “coherence” is difficult to grasp, and its interpretation is indirect, residing in the appendix. The potential for this work to circumvent limitations of previous approaches is a promising impact that should be highlighted. Furthermore, the manuscript would benefit from careful language editing. Author feedback on addressing these clarity concerns would be valuable. 

**Originality:** This work builds upon a combination of recent advancements, offering new theoretical results and empirical validation that support the benefits of this integration.

**Significance:** The paper represents a genuine advance in the field, substantiated by rigorous theoretical analysis. Despite the heuristic nature of the proposed dictionary learning algorithm, its demonstrable improvements over existing methods highlight the value of the insights presented.

**Comment after author rebuttal and reviewer discussion:** The paper contains publishable content and presents interesting contributions. My remaining concern centers on the substantial effort required to revise the paper for accessibility, even for specialists. If a “major revision” option were available, that would be my recommendation. The work effectively combines established techniques and delivers both theoretical findings and a practical, empirically successful algorithm.
