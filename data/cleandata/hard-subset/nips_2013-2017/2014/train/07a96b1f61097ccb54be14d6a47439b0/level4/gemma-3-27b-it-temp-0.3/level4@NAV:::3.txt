This paper presents a novel and significant advancement in Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) by integrating Low Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary, *A*. The authors demonstrate a compelling connection between the often-limiting incoherence parameter, μ, in RPCA recovery guarantees, and inherent clustering structure within the data. By leveraging this connection – and under specific conditions on the dictionary *A* – they show that LRR can exploit this structure to diminish the dependence on μ, leading to improved theoretical guarantees for both exact low-rank and sparse decomposition, and its noisy counterpart.

The work is closely related to existing research on union-of-subspace structures in subspace clustering, such as the work of Wang and Xu ("Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering & Provable subspace clustering: When LRR meets SSC"), and Soltanolkotabi and Candes ("A geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers") and Soltanolkotabi et al ("Robust subspace clustering"). However, this paper distinguishes itself by being less prescriptive about the explicit assumptions regarding “clustering,” potentially offering a more general framework. The empirical validation through simulations and real data experiments strongly supports the theoretical findings, and the authors rightly suggest the potential applicability of this structure to other domains, like text data.

In summary, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of compressed sensing and is highly recommendable for acceptance to NIPS. 

However, I have several suggestions for discussion and stylistic improvements, detailed below:

1. Lines 80-82: This section requires clarification. The reference to “our data” is unclear. Furthermore, even if the problem formulation captures all relevant data structures, additional conditions are still necessary for achieving “perfect recovery.”
2. Line 88: The sentence “… is no longer a method of perfect…” is incomplete and requires finishing.
3. The manuscript would benefit from careful proofreading to address imprecisions and ambiguities. Claims should be stated specifically and with clear references to the context. Overall clarity of language should be prioritized.
4. Wang and Xu ("Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering") previously demonstrated that a higher coherence parameter can be beneficial for subspace clustering, a contrast to the typical behavior in RPCA and matrix completion. The key distinction lies in the fact that the overall data need not be low-rank; rather, only individual clusters are required to exhibit low-rank properties. The approach presented in this paper can be viewed as a synergistic combination of low-rank structure and a union-of-subspace cluster structure.
5. It would be insightful to compare the proposed algorithm’s performance against a two-step approach: first performing noisy subspace clustering with an ℓ₁ penalty, followed by applying PCA to each identified subspace. While a provable guarantee for this latter approach currently remains elusive, it would provide a valuable benchmark.
6. Section 4.2 effectively introduces a novel method for addressing the subspace clustering problem with sparse data corruption. The significant improvements observed over standard RPCA+SSC on the Hopkins155 dataset represent a noteworthy, yet currently understated, contribution. I believe the paper merits acceptance based on at least three key contributions: 1) the characterization of a clustering structure that leads to high coherence, 2) a demonstration of how this structure can be implicitly exploited through the use of a dictionary within LRR, and 3) a partial solution to the sparse corruption problem in subspace clustering.
