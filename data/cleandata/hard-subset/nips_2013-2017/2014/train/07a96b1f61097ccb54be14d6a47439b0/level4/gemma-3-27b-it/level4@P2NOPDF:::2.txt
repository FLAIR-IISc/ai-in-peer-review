This paper presents a novel approach to restoring corrupted observation matrices by integrating robust PCA with dictionary-based structure-preserving projection. The core idea is that leveraging prior structural knowledge of the data – such as clustering – through a learned dictionary can strengthen the theoretical guarantees of robust PCA, which are often limited when dealing with structured data. The authors support this claim with theoretical results in the form of two theorems that delineate conditions for successful reconstruction given a dictionary. The challenge of dictionary learning is addressed with an algorithm empirically demonstrated to be effective: first performing robust PCA, then representing the result using a dictionary.

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound and appears well-researched, pending standard verification. The theorems clearly define the conditions under which reconstruction is feasible, given a pre-defined dictionary. However, the justification for the proposed dictionary learning algorithm relies primarily on empirical evidence. 

**Clarity:**

Clarity represents the paper’s primary weakness. While the overall structure is logical, the paper fails to clearly articulate the central contributions and their broader impact to readers beyond a highly specialized audience. The claims made require more precise definition, and a more explicit discussion of the results’ implications is needed. In particular, the concept of “coherence” is difficult to grasp, and its interpretation is presented indirectly, relegated to the appendix. A significant potential benefit of this work may be its ability to circumvent limitations in previous approaches by utilizing dictionary learning; this aspect should be highlighted. The language throughout the paper also requires careful editing for clarity and precision. Requesting feedback from the authors regarding potential improvements to address these clarity issues would be beneficial.

**Originality:**

This work builds upon existing techniques, specifically robust PCA and dictionary-based projection. However, the authors contribute novel theorems and empirical results that demonstrate the advantages of this combined approach.

**Significance:**

The paper advances the state-of-the-art through rigorous theoretical results and a practical algorithm. While the dictionary learning algorithm is heuristic in nature, its consistent performance improvements compared to alternative methods highlight the value of the insights presented in this work.

The paper contains publishable content and presents interesting contributions. My remaining concern lies in the substantial effort required to revise the paper sufficiently to make it accessible to specialists in the field. Were a “major revision” option available, that would be my recommendation. This work effectively combines recent advances and delivers both theoretical foundations and a heuristically motivated, yet empirically successful, algorithm.
