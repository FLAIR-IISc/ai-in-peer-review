This paper combines two recent techniques, robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection, in the task of reconstructing corrupted observation matrices. The key point is that robust PCA is not guaranteed to be strong enough for structured (for example, clustered) data, and strong limitations can be given by representing the structure with a dictionary. Theorets are given to support these claims. The remaining problem is how to learn the dictionary. An algorithm supported by empirical results is given for this: first calculate the robust PCA, then present the result with a dictionary.

**Quality:**

This paper is technically good for the appropriate level of investigation. Two theorems extend the conditions under which reconstruction is possible given a dictionary. The justification for dictionary learning is empirical.

**Clarity:**

Clarity is the main problem of this paper. While the structure is clear, the paper's main contribution and impact are not clearly explained enough to be accessible beyond a very narrow expert audience. The claims need to be better defined, and the impact of the results needs to be clarified. For example, the concept of consistency is difficult to understand, and the means of explaining it are very indirect (and given in the appendix). One of the potential effects of the paper may be that many old problems can be avoided by using dictionaries. The language also needs to be investigated. It would be important to get comments from the authors on what can be done about these issues.

**Originality:**

This paper is based on a combination of recent techniques, but it includes new theorems and empirical results that support the usefulness of the combination.

**Importance:**

This paper advances the state of the art with rigorous results. Even though the algorithm proposed for dictionary search is approximate, the clear improvements over alternative algorithms show that the insights given by this paper are useful.

Commentary after the author's rebuttal and reviewer's discussion:

There are clearly publishable materials and interesting contributions. My only concern is that the paper will be difficult to rewrite to make it accessible to a very narrow expert audience. I would vote for it if there was an option for "major revision."

This paper combines two recent ideas, and gives both theoretical results and an approximate but empirically well-performing algorithm.
