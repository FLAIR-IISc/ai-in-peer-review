This paper combines robust PCA with dictionary-based structure-preserving projection to address the problem of restoring corrupted observation matrices. The core idea is that, for data exhibiting structure (e.g., clustering), the performance guarantees of robust PCA alone are insufficient. By representing this structure with a learned dictionary, the authors demonstrate the potential for stronger theoretical bounds. The paper provides theorems to support these claims. The remaining challenge – dictionary learning – is addressed with an algorithm validated by empirical results: first performing robust PCA, then representing the result using a dictionary.

Quality:
The paper is technically sound, pending standard verification. The theorems clearly articulate the conditions under which reconstruction is possible given a dictionary. However, the justification for the dictionary learning approach is primarily empirical.

Clarity:
Clarity is the paper’s primary weakness. While the overall structure is logical, the key contributions and potential impact are not explained accessibly to readers beyond a highly specialized audience. The claims require more precise definition, and the significance of the results needs further explication. Specifically, the concept of “coherence” is difficult to grasp, and its interpretation is indirect and relegated to the appendix. A potential benefit of this work lies in its ability to circumvent limitations in previous approaches by leveraging dictionary learning. The language also requires careful editing. 
It would be valuable to solicit feedback from the authors regarding how these clarity issues can be addressed.

Originality:
The work builds upon a combination of recent techniques, but contributes novel theorems and empirical evidence demonstrating the efficacy of this combination.

Significance:
The paper represents an advancement in the state of the art, supported by rigorous results. Although the proposed dictionary learning algorithm is heuristic, its clear performance improvements over alternative algorithms indicate the insights presented are valuable.

Comment after the author rebuttal and reviewer discussion:
The paper contains publishable content and presents interesting contributions. My remaining concern is the substantial effort required to revise the paper to ensure accessibility, even for specialists. Were a “major revision” option available, that would be my recommendation. The paper effectively combines two existing ideas and provides both theoretical results and a heuristic, yet empirically successful, algorithm.
