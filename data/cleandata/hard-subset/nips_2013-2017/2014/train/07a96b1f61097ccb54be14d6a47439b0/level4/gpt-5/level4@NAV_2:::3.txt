The paper studies improving Robust PCA (RPCA) by using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary A. The key observation is that the coherence parameter µ, often viewed as a limiting factor in RPCA’s guarantees, reflects additional structure in the data—specifically, clustering/union-of-subspaces structure. The authors show that, under suitable conditions on A, LRR can leverage this structure and reduce or eliminate the harmful dependence on µ (in particular, µ2), yielding stronger theoretical guarantees for both exact recovery and its noisy counterpart.

The contribution is novel and significant. It connects closely to union-of-subspaces assumptions common in subspace clustering (e.g., Wang and Xu: Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering; Provable subspace clustering: When LRR meets SSC; Soltanolkotabi and Candès: A geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers; Soltanolkotabi et al.: Robust subspace clustering), while being less explicit (and arguably more general) about the exact clustering assumptions. The simulations and real-data experiments support the theory, and the proposed structure seems likely to arise in other applications (e.g., text), as the paper notes.

Overall, the paper offers a substantial advance relevant to compressed sensing and robust low-rank modeling, and I recommend acceptance to NIPS.

That said, I have several points for discussion and stylistic suggestions:

1. Lines 80–82: Please revise for clarity. What specifically does “our data” refer to? Also, even if a model captures salient structure, additional conditions are typically required to guarantee “perfect recovery”; this should be stated precisely.

2. Line 88: “... is no longer a method of perfect (...)” is confusing. Perhaps “no longer a perfect method” or “no longer achieves perfect recovery”—please clarify the intended meaning.

3. Please proofread carefully for issues like the above and aim for greater precision and specificity in claims and references throughout. Improving overall clarity would notably strengthen the manuscript.

4. Wang and Xu discuss how higher coherence can actually benefit subspace clustering (in contrast to RPCA/matrix completion). The main distinction is that subspace clustering does not require the entire dataset to be globally low-rank; only each cluster is low-rank. The structure exploited here can be seen as combining global low-rank modeling with a union-of-subspaces (clustering) perspective.

5. It would be interesting to compare against a pipeline that first solves noisy subspace clustering with an ℓ1 penalty, followed by PCA within each cluster. While there is no known guarantee for this approach, an empirical comparison would be informative.

6. Section 4.2 effectively proposes a new approach to subspace clustering with sparse corruptions, showing substantial gains over the RPCA+SSC baseline on Hopkins155. This is, in my view, an important (and somewhat implicit) contribution and should be highlighted. The paper merits acceptance for at least three reasons: (i) articulating a clustering-induced mechanism for high coherence; (ii) demonstrating that one can implicitly exploit this via an LRR dictionary; and (iii) partially addressing sparse corruptions in subspace clustering.
