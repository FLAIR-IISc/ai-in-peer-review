- Addresses recovery of low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse gross errors and shows RPCA fails on "coherent" data.  
- Key insight: high coherence often arises from clustering (union-of-subspaces) in real data.  
- Main theoretical contribution: LRR with a low-rank dictionary whose column space contains the true low-rank space yields recovery guarantees independent of coherence.  
- Practical contribution: a two-stage algorithm—use RPCA to get an initial estimate, build a dictionary, then run LRR for improved recovery.  
- Thorough theoretical analysis: recovery guarantees provided for both noiseless and noisy settings.  
- Strong experimental validation: clear phase transition on synthetic data and large improvements on corrupted Hopkins155 motion segmentation.  
- Quality: technically sound and well-supported by theory and experiments.  
- Clarity weakness: manuscript language is often imprecise and requires careful proofreading and revision.  
- Specific wording issues noted (examples of vague/incomplete sentences) that should be fixed for readability.  
- Originality: novel framing connecting coherence to clustering and showing how a low-rank dictionary circumvents coherence issues.  
- The two-stage practical algorithm is simple, novel, and effective.  
- Significance: advances understanding of when dictionary-based low-rank methods outperform RPCA and provides a new state-of-the-art for robust subspace clustering.  
- Suggested improvements: clarify claims/definitions, contrast with related work (e.g., Wang & Xu), and emphasize strong motion segmentation results.  
- Suggested additional baseline: compare to pipeline of robust subspace clustering followed by per-cluster PCA.  
- Overall recommendation: despite clarity issues, the contributions are substantial and the paper is strongly recommended for acceptance.
