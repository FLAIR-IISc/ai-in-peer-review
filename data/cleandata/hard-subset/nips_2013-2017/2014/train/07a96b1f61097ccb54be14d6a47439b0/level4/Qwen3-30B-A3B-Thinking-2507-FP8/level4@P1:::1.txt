

This paper addresses a critical limitation in robust principal component analysis (RPCA), where performance degrades significantly as data coherence increases due to underlying clustering structures. While RPCA [4] has been a cornerstone for low-rank matrix recovery from grossly corrupted observations, it assumes incoherent data, which is often inconsistent with real-world applications where data naturally exhibits clustering patterns. The authors demonstrate that as the number of clusters increases, the coherence parameter (particularly µ2) rises, directly degrading RPCA's recovery performance. 

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it theoretically establishes that low-rank representation (LRR) [13] can overcome this coherence limitation when the dictionary matrix A is itself low-rank and satisfies U₀ ⊂ U_A (where U₀ is the column space of the true low-rank matrix L₀). The authors prove that under these conditions, LRR achieves exact recovery without requiring the coherence parameter µ₂. Second, they propose a practical unsupervised algorithm that constructs an appropriate dictionary using RPCA's output, making their approach applicable in real-world scenarios where labeled data is unavailable. Third, they provide theoretical bounds showing that their method achieves recovery under less restrictive conditions than RPCA (O(n₂/log n₁) versus O(n₂/(log n₁)²)).

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated, addressing a genuine gap in the literature. The authors effectively demonstrate the "µ₂-phenomenon" through both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, showing how clustering structures naturally increase coherence parameters and degrade RPCA performance. Their experimental validation on synthetic data and motion sequences from the Hopkins155 database provides compelling evidence of the method's superiority over RPCA, particularly in scenarios with moderate rank and sparsity.

Regarding quality, the paper excels in theoretical rigor. The proofs are sound, and the theoretical results are clearly presented with appropriate mathematical notation. The experimental design is comprehensive, covering both synthetic matrices with controlled parameters and real-world motion data. The authors appropriately address the limitations of their approach, noting that the method assumes the dictionary must be low-rank and contain information about the ground truth matrix.

In terms of clarity, the paper is exceptionally well-written. The technical concepts are explained with precision, and the notation is consistently applied. The figures effectively illustrate key concepts like the "µ₂-phenomenon" and the performance comparison between RPCA and the proposed method. The algorithm description is clear and implementable.

The originality of the work is significant. While LRR has been previously studied with A = X [13], the authors provide novel theoretical insights into the role of dictionary properties in handling coherent data. They establish a fundamental relationship between coherence parameters and data structures, revealing that coherence is not merely an assumption but reflects intrinsic data characteristics. This perspective offers new insights into both RPCA and LRR, distinguishing this work from previous contributions at NIPS.

The significance of this work is substantial. Many real-world applications (computer vision, motion analysis, etc.) involve data with natural clustering structures, making the authors' approach highly relevant. The demonstrated 40% relative improvement in clustering error rates on motion sequences (Table 1) illustrates practical impact. The method provides a principled way to handle coherent data, advancing the state of the art in robust low-rank recovery.

The paper also offers valuable insights into the physical meaning of coherence parameters, which have been standard in the literature but not well understood. By revealing their connection to intrinsic data structures, the authors deepen our theoretical understanding of robust matrix recovery.

Minor points for consideration: The authors might want to more explicitly discuss how their method relates to subspace clustering/segmentation, as this is a natural application of their approach. While the paper demonstrates improved clustering performance when using their method for data recovery (Table 1), a dedicated discussion of how their dictionary learning approach could be directly applied to subspace clustering would strengthen the connection between their theoretical contribution and this important application area.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery. It addresses a significant theoretical limitation of RPCA with both rigorous theory and practical implementation, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages. The work is well-positioned to advance both the theoretical understanding and practical application of robust low-rank recovery methods.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper solves an important problem with significant theoretical and practical contributions, provides rigorous proofs, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over state-of-the-art methods.

Arguments against acceptance: None of substantive significance. The paper is well-written, well-motivated, and makes a clear contribution to the field.

This paper is of high quality, original, and significant. It should be accepted.
