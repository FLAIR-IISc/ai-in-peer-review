This paper proposes using Low Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary to improve the effectiveness of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA). The authors demonstrate that the incoherence parameter μ, often considered a bottleneck in the recovery guarantees for RPCA, can in fact correspond to an underlying clustering structure within the data. They show that, under certain conditions on the dictionary A, one can exploit this structure using LRR to partially remove the dependence on μ, leading to theoretically stronger guarantees for both exact low-rank and sparse decomposition and its noisy extension.

The proposed method is novel and represents a significant contribution to the field. The work is closely related to the union-of-subspaces structure assumed in prior subspace clustering literature (e.g., Wang and Xu, "Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering"; Soltanolkotabi and Candes, "A geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers"; Soltanolkotabi et al., "Robust subspace clustering"). However, this paper's assumption on the "clustering" structure is less explicit, which is a reasonable and potentially more general formulation. The theoretical analysis is well-supported by simulation and real-data experiments. As the authors remark, it is plausible that this structure exists in many other real-world applications, such as text data.

In summary, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of robust low-rank matrix recovery. I recommend acceptance. I also offer several points for discussion and suggestions for improving the paper's presentation, which are detailed below.

**Detailed Comments:**

1.  Line 80-82: This sentence requires rewriting for clarity. The phrase "our data" is ambiguous. Furthermore, the claim that the formulation captures all relevant structures is insufficient for "perfect recovery"; additional conditions are necessary and should be stated.

2.  Line 88: The sentence ends with "...is no longer a method of perfect," which is incomplete. Please specify what is no longer perfectly recovered.

3.  More broadly, the paper would benefit from careful proofreading to address issues like the two mentioned above. Please be precise in your claims and explicit about your references. Improving the overall clarity of the language would strengthen the manuscript.

4.  It is worth noting the connection to Wang and Xu ("Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering"), who observed that a high coherence parameter can be beneficial for subspace clustering (in contrast to its role in RPCA). A key difference is that their work assumes each cluster is low-rank, but not necessarily the entire dataset. The structure exploited in this paper can be viewed as a combination of a global low-rank structure and a union-of-subspaces cluster structure.

5.  It would be interesting to see a comparison against a two-stage baseline: first, perform noisy subspace clustering (e.g., with an L1 penalty), and then apply PCA to each resulting subspace. While we acknowledge that provable guarantees for such a procedure may not exist, the empirical comparison would be valuable.

6.  Section 4.2 effectively proposes a novel method for subspace clustering in the presence of sparse corruptions. The significant improvements shown over the standard RPCA+SSC baseline on the Hopkins155 dataset represent a major, and perhaps under-emphasized, contribution of this work. The authors should consider highlighting this more explicitly. The paper's value is underscored by at least three key contributions: (1) the identification of a clustering structure that can lead to high coherence; (2) a demonstration that this structure can be implicitly exploited via LRR with a learned dictionary; and (3) a new, effective approach to the problem of subspace clustering with sparse corruptions.
