This paper integrates two recent techniques, robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection, to restore corrupted observation matrices. The key insight is that for structured data (e.g., clustered data), the guarantees of robust PCA are insufficient, and representing the structure with a dictionary can provide stronger bounds. The authors support these claims with theorems. However, the remaining challenge is learning the dictionary, for which they propose an algorithm justified by empirical results: first computing robust PCA, then representing the result with a dictionary.

Quality:
The paper is technically sound, having been checked to a reasonable level. The two theorems elaborate on the conditions under which reconstruction is possible, given a dictionary. However, the justification for learning the dictionary is empirical.

Clarity:
The main issue with the paper is its clarity. Although the structure is clear, the primary contributions and impact of the paper are not explained clearly enough to be accessible to a broad audience beyond specialists. The claims need to be more well-defined, and the impact of the results should be explicated. For instance, the concept of coherence is difficult to understand, and interpreting it requires indirect means (provided in the appendix). One potential impact of the paper is that using dictionaries may allow sidestepping many earlier problems.

Additionally, the language requires checking to ensure accuracy and fluency. It would be essential to obtain a comment from the authors regarding potential solutions to these issues.

Originality:
The paper combines recent techniques but includes new theorems and empirical results supporting the usefulness of this combination.

Significance:
The paper advances the state-of-the-art with rigorous results. Although the proposed algorithm for finding the dictionary is heuristic, clear improvements over alternative algorithms demonstrate that the insights provided in this paper are useful.

Comment after the author rebuttal and reviewer discussion:
There is clearly publishable content and interesting contributions in this paper. My only remaining concern is that making the paper accessible to a broad audience will require significant rewriting. If the option for a "major revision" were available, I would vote for it. The paper integrates two recent ideas and provides both theoretical results and a heuristic but empirically well-performing algorithm.
