This paper integrates robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection techniques to restore corrupted observation matrices. A key observation is that robust PCA's guarantees are insufficient for structured data, and representing this structure using a dictionary enables stronger bounds. Supporting theorems are provided, addressing the reconstruction problem given a dictionary. However, learning the dictionary remains a challenge, and an empirically justified algorithm is proposed: computing robust PCA and then representing the result using a dictionary.

Quality:
The paper is technically sound, having been checked to a reasonable extent. The two theorems outline conditions under which reconstruction is feasible, given a dictionary. While the dictionary learning justification is empirical, it is well-supported.

Clarity:
The primary issue with the paper is its clarity. Although the structure is well-organized, the main contributions and impact are not clearly explained, limiting accessibility to a narrow specialist audience. Claims require clearer definition, and results need more explicit interpretation. For example, the concept of coherence is difficult to grasp, with indirect explanations found in the appendix. One potential impact of the paper is that using dictionaries can sidestep earlier problems. Additionally, language refinement is necessary. Author feedback on addressing these concerns would be valuable.

Originality:
The paper combines recent techniques, introducing new theorems and empirical results that demonstrate the usefulness of this combination.

Significance:
The paper advances the state-of-the-art with rigorous results. Despite the heuristic nature of the proposed dictionary-finding algorithm, clear improvements over alternative algorithms demonstrate the paper's useful insights.

Comment after the author rebuttal and reviewer discussion:
The paper contains publishable content and interesting contributions. My only remaining concern is the significant effort required to re-write the paper for broader specialist accessibility. If a "major revision" option were available, I would recommend it. The paper combines two recent ideas, providing both theoretical results and a heuristic yet empirically well-performing algorithm.
