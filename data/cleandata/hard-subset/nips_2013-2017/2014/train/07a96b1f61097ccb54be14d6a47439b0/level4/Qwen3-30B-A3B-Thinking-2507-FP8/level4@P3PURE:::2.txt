

This paper combines robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection to restore corrupted observation matrices. The key insight is that robust PCA provides insufficient guarantees for structured data (e.g., clustered data), and representing structure via a dictionary yields stronger theoretical bounds. Theorems establish conditions under which reconstruction is possible given a dictionary. The remaining challenge—learning the dictionary—is addressed by an empirically justified algorithm: first apply robust PCA, then represent the result using a dictionary.

**Quality:**  
The paper is technically sound, with reasonable verification. The two theorems rigorously specify reconstruction conditions under a given dictionary. However, the dictionary-learning approach lacks theoretical justification and relies solely on empirical validation.

**Clarity:**  
Clarity is the paper's primary weakness. While the structure is logical, the main contributions and broader impact remain inadequately explained for non-specialist audiences. Key claims require sharper definition, and the significance of results needs explicit elaboration. For instance, the concept of *coherence* is poorly defined and interpreted only indirectly (via an appendix). A notable potential impact—using dictionaries to circumvent limitations of prior approaches—is not clearly articulated. The writing also requires substantial grammatical refinement.

**Originality:**  
The work synthesizes recent techniques with novel theorems and empirical validation demonstrating the value of their combination.

**Significance:**  
The paper advances the state of the art through rigorous theoretical results. Although the dictionary-learning algorithm is heuristic, clear empirical improvements over alternatives confirm the practical utility of the proposed insights.

**Post-Rebuttal Comment:**  
The paper contains publishable content and meaningful contributions. The sole remaining concern is the significant effort required to revise the manuscript for accessibility to a broader audience. If a "major revision" option were available, I would strongly endorse it. The work successfully combines two recent ideas, delivers theoretical results, and presents a heuristic algorithm with strong empirical performance.
