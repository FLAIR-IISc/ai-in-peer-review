This paper combines two recent techniques: powerful PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projections, to recover corrupted observation matrices. The main insight is that for structured (for example, clustered) data, powerful PCA is not strong enough, and a powerful limit can be given by representing the structure through a dictionary. Theorets are provided to support these claims. The remaining problem is how the dictionary should be learned. An algorithm is provided for this, which is supported by empirical results: first calculate the powerful PCA, then represent the results by a dictionary.

**Quality:**

The paper is technically good, up to a reasonable level of verification. Two theorems detail the conditions under which reconstruction is possible given a dictionary. For dictionary learning, empiricism is logical.

**Clarity:**

Clarity is the main problem of this paper. Although the structure is clear, the paper's main contribution and impact are not clearly explained, which is not understandable even to very narrow-minded expert audiences. The claims need to be better defined and the impact of the results needs to be explained. For example, the concept of clustering is difficult to understand, and the ways to explain it are very indirect (and given in the appendix). One of the potential impacts of the paper could be that many earlier problems can be avoided using a dictionary. Also, some aspects of the language need to be verified. It is important to get a comment from the authors on these issues.

**Originality:**

The paper is based on a combination of recent techniques, but new theorems and empirical results support the usefulness of this combination.

**Importance:**

The paper advances refinement through rigorous results. Although the algorithm proposed for finding a dictionary is heuristic, clear improvements over alternative algorithms prove that the insights given in this paper are effective.
