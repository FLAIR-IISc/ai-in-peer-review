The paper introduces a novel approach to enhance the effectiveness of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) by incorporating Low Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary A. Notably, the authors demonstrate that the incoherence parameter \mu, often viewed as a bottleneck in RPCA recovery guarantees, can actually correspond to additional clustering structure in the data. Under specific conditions on dictionary A, the authors show that LRR can exploit this structure, partially removing the dependence on \mu and yielding a stronger theoretical guarantee for both exact low-rank and sparse decomposition, as well as its noisy extension.

The proposed method is both novel and significant to the field, building upon the union-of-subspace structure assumed in previous subspace clustering papers. However, this work is more general and less explicit about the clustering assumption. The simulation and real data experiments validate the theoretical analysis, suggesting that the new structure can be applied to various real-world applications, such as text data.

In summary, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of compressed sensing and is worthy of acceptance by NIPS.

However, I have several discussions and stylistic suggestions, summarized in the detailed comments below:

1. Lines 80-82 require rephrasing to clarify the description. The term "our data" is ambiguous, and even if the problem captures all the structures, an additional condition is necessary to achieve "perfect recovery".

2. Line 88 is unclear and requires revision.

3. The authors should proofread the paper more carefully to address issues like the above two. It is essential to be specific and precise about claims and references in the text. In general, the authors should work on improving the clarity of the language.

4. Wang and Xu's paper "Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering" highlights that a higher coherence parameter can be beneficial for subspace clustering, unlike in RPCA and matrix completion. The key difference lies in the fact that the data need not be low-rank overall, but rather each cluster must be low-rank. The structure exploited in this paper can be seen as a combination of low-rank structure and union-of-subspace cluster structure.

5. It would be interesting to compare the proposed algorithm against first solving noisy subspace clustering with \ell_1 penalty and then performing PCA for each subspace. Although no provable guarantee exists for the latter approach, the comparison could provide valuable insights.

6. Section 4.2 proposes a new method for solving the subspace clustering problem with sparse corruption in the data. This method leads to significant improvements over standard RPCA+SSC on the Hopkins155 dataset, which is a notable contribution that the authors should emphasize. The paper's acceptance is justified by its three key contributions: 1. describing a clustering structure that leads to high coherence, 2. demonstrating how to implicitly exploit this structure using a dictionary in LRR, and 3. partially solving the sparse corruptions problem in subspace clustering.
