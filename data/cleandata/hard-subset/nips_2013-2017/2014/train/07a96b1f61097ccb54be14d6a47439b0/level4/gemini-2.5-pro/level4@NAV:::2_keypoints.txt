- Addresses restoration of corrupted observation matrices by combining robust PCA with dictionary-based structure-preserving projection.
- Central claim: for structured data (e.g., clustered), robust PCA alone is insufficient and an explicit dictionary gives stronger theoretical guarantees.
- Theoretical contribution: formal theorems showing more stringent reconstruction bounds when the dictionary is known.
- Practical method: a two-step, empirically-justified algorithm — perform robust PCA, then construct the dictionary from its result.
- Quality: technically solid; the two theorems clearly delineate feasibility conditions for a known dictionary.
- Limitation: the dictionary-learning procedure is empirical/heuristic and lacks theoretical justification.
- Clarity issues: main contributions, impact, and claims are not articulated clearly for non-specialists.
- Definitions problem: the notion of “coherence” is hard to parse and relegated to the appendix, reducing accessibility.
- Impact underemphasized: the potential of dictionaries to overcome earlier methods’ challenges is not sufficiently highlighted.
- Language and presentation require correction; authors should state how they will address these clarity issues.
- Originality: novel theorems and empirical findings emerge from combining recent techniques.
- Significance: advances the state-of-the-art with rigorous theory and demonstrable empirical gains despite heuristic dictionary learning.
- Post-rebuttal stance: content is publishable but needs substantial rewriting for broader accessibility; reviewer would recommend a major revision.
