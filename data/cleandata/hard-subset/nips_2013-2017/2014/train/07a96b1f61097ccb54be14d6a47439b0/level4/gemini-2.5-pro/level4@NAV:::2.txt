This paper addresses the restoration of corrupted observation matrices by integrating two modern approaches: robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection. The central premise is that for data with inherent structure, such as clustered data, the guarantees offered by robust PCA alone are insufficient. By explicitly representing this structure with a dictionary, the authors show that more stringent theoretical bounds can be established. This claim is substantiated by formal theorems. To address the practical problem of learning the dictionary, the paper proposes a two-step, empirically-justified algorithm: first perform robust PCA, then use its result to construct the dictionary.

**Quality:**
The paper appears technically solid upon a reasonable inspection. The two theorems presented delineate the conditions under which reconstruction is feasible, given a known dictionary. The justification for the dictionary learning algorithm, however, is empirical rather than theoretical.

**Clarity:**
The primary weakness of this submission is its clarity. While the overall structure is logical, the paper's main contributions and impact are not articulated clearly enough to be understood by anyone outside of a very narrow group of specialists. The claims need to be more precisely defined, and the implications of the results should be explicated more thoroughly. For instance, the concept of "coherence" is difficult to parse, and its interpretation is highly indirect and confined to the appendix. One of the paper's potential impacts—that dictionaries can be used to bypass challenges in earlier methods—is not sufficiently highlighted. Additionally, the manuscript requires language correction. It would be valuable to receive the authors' feedback on how they intend to resolve these issues.

**Originality:**
The work is founded on a combination of recent methods, but it introduces novel theorems and empirical findings that support the utility of this synthesis.

**Significance:**
This paper advances the state-of-the-art with rigorous theoretical results. Although the algorithm for dictionary discovery is heuristic, the clear performance improvements over alternative methods effectively demonstrate the value of the insights presented.

---
**Comment after the author rebuttal and reviewer discussion:**
The submission certainly contains publishable content and interesting contributions. My only lingering concern is that a significant amount of work will be needed to rewrite the paper for accessibility beyond a highly specialized audience. If a "major revision" were a possible outcome, I would advocate for it. The paper successfully merges two recent ideas, providing both theoretical results and a heuristic algorithm that demonstrates strong empirical performance.
