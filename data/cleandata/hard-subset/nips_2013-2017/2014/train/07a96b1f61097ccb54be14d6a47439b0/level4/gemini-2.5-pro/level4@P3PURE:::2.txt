This paper proposes a method for restoring corrupted observation matrices by combining two recent techniques: robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection. The key insight is that for structured data (e.g., clustered data), the performance guarantees of standard robust PCA are insufficient. The authors argue that by representing the data's structure with a dictionary, stronger recovery bounds can be established. The paper provides theorems to support these claims. To address the problem of learning the dictionary, the authors propose an algorithm: first, compute the robust PCA of the data, and then, use the result to construct a dictionary. This algorithm is justified through empirical results.

**Quality:**
The paper appears to be technically sound. The two main theorems elaborate the conditions under which reconstruction is possible, given a suitable dictionary. The proposed method for learning the dictionary, however, is justified empirically rather than theoretically.

**Clarity:**
The primary weakness of this paper is its lack of clarity. While the overall structure is logical, the main contributions and potential impact are not explained clearly enough, making the work inaccessible to readers outside of a narrow subfield. The authors' claims need to be more precisely defined, and the implications of their results should be better explicated. For instance, the concept of "coherence" is difficult to understand, and its interpretation is explained only indirectly in the appendix. A potential impact of this work is that the dictionary-based approach may circumvent challenges present in earlier methods, but this is not highlighted sufficiently. The manuscript would also benefit from a thorough proofreading to improve language and grammar. It is critical that the authors address these clarity issues.

**Originality:**
The work is based on a novel combination of existing techniques. This synthesis is supported by new theoretical results and empirical evidence that demonstrates its value.

**Significance:**
The paper advances the state-of-the-art by providing rigorous theoretical results. Although the proposed algorithm for learning the dictionary is heuristic, its empirical performance shows clear improvements over alternative algorithms, demonstrating the utility of the paper's core insights.
