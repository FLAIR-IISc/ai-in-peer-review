This paper addresses the problem of low-rank matrix recovery in the presence of corruption, specifically focusing on the challenges posed by data coherence. The authors demonstrate that data exhibiting clustering structures leads to increased coherence, degrading the performance of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA). They propose utilizing Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully chosen dictionary to mitigate the effects of this coherence, proving that a low-rank dictionary renders LRR immune to the coherence parameter associated with clustering. The paper provides both theoretical guarantees and experimental validation on synthetic and real datasets, showing improved performance over RPCA.

This is a significant and novel contribution to the field of matrix recovery and robust statistics. The work builds upon the established framework of RPCA [4] and LRR [13], offering a principled approach to handling a common data characteristic – clustering – that often hinders the performance of standard techniques. The connection made between data coherence and underlying clustering structure is insightful and well-motivated. The approach is related to, but distinct from, previous work on union-of-subspace clustering [5, 6, 7], offering a complementary perspective that focuses on exploiting the structure within the low-rank component rather than directly clustering. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, and the identification of a new method for sparse corruption removal in subspace clustering (Section 4.2) is a notable additional contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper identifies a key limitation of RPCA – its sensitivity to data coherence caused by clustering – and proposes a novel solution using LRR with a low-rank dictionary.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The authors provide mathematical proofs supporting their claims about the immunity of LRR to coherence under the given conditions.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The proposed method demonstrates performance improvements on both synthetic and real-world motion sequence data.
*   **Multiple Contributions:** The work offers at least three key contributions: characterizing the link between clustering and coherence, leveraging dictionaries in LRR to address this issue, and improving sparse corruption removal in subspace clustering.
*   **Clear Connection to Prior Work:** The paper appropriately positions itself within the existing literature on RPCA, LRR, and subspace clustering, acknowledging related work and highlighting the unique aspects of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Language:** The writing could be improved for clarity and precision. Certain phrases and sentences are ambiguous or require careful re-reading to fully understand (e.g., lines 80-82, 88).
*   **Nuance in Related Work:** While the paper acknowledges related work, a deeper discussion of the differences and synergies between their approach and, for example, Wang & Xu’s findings on the benefits of higher coherence in subspace clustering [4] would be beneficial.
*   **Algorithm Complexity:** The paper briefly mentions the computational efficiency of the proposed algorithm, but a more detailed discussion of the complexity compared to RPCA would strengthen the presentation.
*   **Potential Extension:** While acknowledged as future work, exploring an iterative refinement of the dictionary learning process (Algorithm 1) could potentially further improve performance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a substantial theoretical and practical contribution to the field of matrix recovery.
*   The proposed method addresses a significant limitation of existing techniques (RPCA) and offers a principled solution.
*   The experimental results are compelling and validate the theoretical findings.
*   The paper’s clear writing and thorough exploration of related work demonstrates a deep understanding of the field.
*   The additional contribution of a new subspace clustering technique in Section 4.2 strengthens the paper’s overall impact.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The clarity of the writing in certain sections could be improved.
*   A more nuanced discussion of related work, particularly concerning the role of coherence in subspace clustering, would enhance the paper’s context.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The strengths clearly outweigh the weaknesses. While improvements in clarity and a more in-depth discussion of related work would be beneficial, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and practical relevance of the proposed method warrant acceptance.

**Rating:** Accept.



**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Line 80-82:** Rephrase for clarity. Instead of “our data”, specify what kind of data is being referred to. Also clarify that achieving perfect recovery requires conditions beyond simply capturing data structure.
*   **Line 88:** Replace “perfect” with a more precise descriptor (e.g., optimal, complete).
*   **General:** Carefully proofread the entire paper to ensure precise language and avoid ambiguity.
*   **Related Work (line 184):** Expand on the distinction between the approach in this paper and that of Wang & Xu [4], highlighting that their method doesn’t necessarily assume an overall low-rank structure, only low-rank clusters.
*   **Algorithm Complexity:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of Algorithm 1 compared to RPCA.
*   **Section 4.2:** Explicitly state that the results in Section 4.2 represent a significant improvement in subspace clustering methodology and emphasize this as a key contribution of the paper.
