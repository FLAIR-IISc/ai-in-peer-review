- Problem: RPCA fails on "coherent" low-rank data, a property the authors link to clustering in real-world datasets.  
- Proposal: Use Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with factorization L = A Z to overcome RPCA failures.  
- Theoretical claim: If dictionary A is low-rank and its column space contains the true data subspace, LRR recovery is immune to RPCA’s coherence parameters.  
- Practical challenge: Finding such a dictionary in unsupervised settings is difficult.  
- Algorithm: Two-stage heuristic — run RPCA to estimate the low-rank part, then build a low-rank dictionary from that estimate and run LRR.  
- Empirical results: Substantial improvements over RPCA on synthetic data (47% larger success region) and reduced errors on motion segmentation.  
- Strength — importance: Addresses a well-defined, significant limitation of a widely-used method (RPCA).  
- Strength — theory: Provides an elegant theoretical insight (Theorem 1) linking dictionary structure to robustness against coherence.  
- Strength — empirical: Heuristic algorithm yields strong, practical performance gains.  
- Strength — synthesis: Constructive combination of RPCA and LRR is creative and effective.  
- Weakness — clarity: Presentation is dense and assumes high familiarity; key concepts like "coherence" lack intuitive explanation.  
- Weakness — theory–algorithm gap: Theoretical guarantees assume a suitable dictionary, but the practical two-step dictionary construction is a heuristic without formal guarantees.  
- Quality: Theorems appear correct and proofs are rigorous; experiments support claims.  
- Clarity suggestions: Add geometric intuition/figures for coherence, better explain why AZ avoids coherence issues, and improve writing and flow.  
- Originality: Novel connection between clustering-induced coherence and dictionary-based recovery; original combination of RPCA and LRR.  
- Significance: Results are important and likely influential for robust subspace recovery applications.  
- Recommendation: Paper is publishable and promising, but requires major revision for clarity; reviewer leans toward acceptance.
