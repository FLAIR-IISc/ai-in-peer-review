This paper presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of compressed sensing by demonstrating a connection between the incoherence parameter in Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) and underlying clustering structure within the data. The authors show that a high incoherence, often considered a limitation in RPCA recovery guarantees, can actually indicate the presence of a union-of-subspaces structure. By leveraging this insight with a learned dictionary within a Low Rank Representation (LRR) framework, they achieve theoretically stronger guarantees for both exact low-rank and sparse decomposition, and its noisy extension, potentially mitigating the dependence on a low incoherence parameter.

The work is closely related to existing research on subspace clustering, such as the papers by Wang and Xu (“Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering & Provable subspace clustering: When LRR meets SSC”) and Soltanolkotabi and Candes (“A geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers”), as well as subsequent work by Soltanolkotabi et al (“Robust subspace clustering”). However, the approach taken here is less prescriptive about the explicit assumptions regarding “clustering,” which is a reasonable choice and likely leads to greater generality. The experimental validation on both simulated and real datasets supports the theoretical findings, and the authors rightly suggest potential applications beyond those immediately presented, such as in the analysis of text data.

In summary, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the understanding of low-rank matrix decomposition and its relationship to subspace clustering. I recommend acceptance to NIPS.

That said, there are a few points for discussion and stylistic improvement that would further strengthen the manuscript.

1. Lines 80-82 require clarification. The phrase "our data" is vague; please specify what dataset or context is being referenced. Furthermore, even if the model accurately captures all inherent structure, a separate condition is still necessary for perfect recovery. This should be explicitly stated.

2. Line 88: The sentence “… is no longer a method of perfect…” is incomplete and unclear. Please revise to clearly articulate the point being made.

3. A more thorough proofreading pass is recommended. Several instances of imprecise language and missing details detract from the clarity of the presentation. The authors should strive for greater specificity in their claims and when referencing elements within the text. 

4. The paper by Wang and Xu (“Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering”) highlights that a higher coherence parameter can be *beneficial* for subspace clustering – a contrast to the traditional view in RPCA and matrix completion. The key distinction is that their method doesn’t require the entire dataset to be low-rank; rather, only individual clusters need to exhibit a low-rank structure. The approach presented in this paper can be viewed as a powerful combination of exploiting both overall low-rank properties *and* union-of-subspace cluster structure. Highlighting this connection would be valuable.

5. It would be insightful to compare the proposed algorithm against a two-step approach: first, performing noisy subspace clustering with an \ell_1 penalty, followed by applying PCA to each identified subspace. While a provable guarantee for this latter step currently doesn’t exist, empirical comparison could provide further context for the strengths of the proposed method.

6. Section 4.2 effectively introduces a novel method for addressing the subspace clustering problem in the presence of sparse corruption. The significant improvements observed on the Hopkins155 dataset, compared to standard RPCA+SSC, represent a noteworthy contribution that the authors should explicitly emphasize.  I believe this paper merits acceptance based on at least three key contributions: (1) characterization of a clustering structure that explains high coherence; (2) demonstration of how this structure can be implicitly exploited through a dictionary-based LRR approach; and (3) a partial solution to the challenging problem of sparse corruption in subspace clustering.
