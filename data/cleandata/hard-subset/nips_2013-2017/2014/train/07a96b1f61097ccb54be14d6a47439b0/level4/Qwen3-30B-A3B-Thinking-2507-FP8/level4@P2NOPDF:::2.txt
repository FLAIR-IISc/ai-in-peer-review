

This paper integrates robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection to restore corrupted observation matrices. The core insight addresses a gap in robust PCA's applicability to structured data (e.g., clustered), where its guarantees are insufficient. By representing structure via a dictionary, the authors derive stronger theoretical bounds. Two theorems establish reconstruction conditions given a dictionary, though the paper does not provide a principled method for dictionary learning—instead proposing an empirical algorithm: first apply robust PCA, then represent the result using a dictionary.  

**Quality.** The technical content is sound, with two theorems rigorously detailing reconstruction conditions under dictionary-based structure. The dictionary-learning algorithm is justified empirically but lacks theoretical grounding. The paper demonstrates clear empirical gains over alternatives, validating the approach’s practical utility despite the heuristic nature of dictionary learning.  

**Clarity.** This is the paper’s most significant weakness. While the structure is logical, the main contributions and broader impact remain inadequately explained for a general ML audience. Key claims lack precision: the concept of *coherence* is poorly defined in the main text (only addressed indirectly in the appendix), hindering interpretation. The potential impact—e.g., that dictionaries enable bypassing limitations of prior methods—is asserted but not substantiated with concrete examples or comparisons. Language issues further reduce accessibility (e.g., "observartion," awkward phrasing like "means of interpreting it"). The paper risks being accessible only to a narrow specialist audience without substantial revision.  

**Originality.** The work combines recent techniques (robust PCA and dictionary-based projection) to yield new theoretical results and empirical validation. The novel theorems and algorithmic framework constitute a meaningful contribution beyond a simple fusion of existing ideas.  

**Significance.** The paper advances the state of the art through rigorous theoretical analysis and empirically effective heuristics. While the dictionary-learning step is heuristic, the consistent empirical improvements over baselines demonstrate the practical value of the proposed insights. The approach offers a viable path to address structured data recovery challenges where prior methods fall short.  

**Comment after author rebuttal and reviewer discussion.** The paper contains publishable content with interesting contributions. The primary remaining concern is the significant effort required to revise the paper for broader accessibility. The technical content is strong, and the combination of theoretical results and empirically effective heuristics is compelling. However, the current presentation limits its impact. If a "major revision" option were available, this would be the recommended track. The authors must substantially clarify contributions, define key concepts (e.g., coherence), and explicitly articulate the practical implications of their approach to reach a wider audience. Without these revisions, the paper’s accessibility and influence remain constrained.
