This paper combines two recent techniques—robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection—to restore corrupted observation matrices. The key insight is that for structured (e.g., clustered) data, the guarantees of robust PCA alone are insufficient; by encoding structure via a dictionary, one can obtain stronger recovery bounds. Theorems are provided to support these claims. The remaining challenge is how to learn the dictionary. The authors propose an empirically justified algorithm: first compute robust PCA, then represent the result with a dictionary.

Quality:
The paper appears technically sound to a reasonable level of scrutiny. The two theorems specify conditions under which reconstruction is possible, given a dictionary. However, the approach to learning the dictionary is justified only empirically.

Clarity:
Clarity is the main weakness of the paper. While the overall structure is clear, the main contributions and their impact are not explained clearly enough to be accessible beyond a very narrow specialist audience. The claims need to be more precisely defined, and the implications of the results should be made explicit. For instance, the concept of coherence is hard to understand, and guidance for interpreting it is indirect and relegated to the appendix. One potential impact of the paper is that, by using dictionaries, many earlier problems could be sidestepped. The language also needs careful editing. It would be important to hear from the authors on what can be done to address these issues.

Originality:
The paper builds on a combination of recent techniques, but it contributes new theorems and empirical results that support the usefulness of the combination.

Significance:
The paper advances the state of the art with rigorous results. Although the proposed algorithm for finding the dictionary is heuristic, clear improvements over alternative algorithms demonstrate that the insights in this paper are valuable.

Comment after the author rebuttal and reviewer discussion:
There is clearly publishable content and interesting contributions. My only remaining concern is that it will require substantial effort to rewrite the paper to make it accessible beyond a very specialized audience. If a “major revision” option were available, I would vote for it. The paper combines two recent ideas and provides both theoretical results and a heuristic but empirically strong algorithm.
