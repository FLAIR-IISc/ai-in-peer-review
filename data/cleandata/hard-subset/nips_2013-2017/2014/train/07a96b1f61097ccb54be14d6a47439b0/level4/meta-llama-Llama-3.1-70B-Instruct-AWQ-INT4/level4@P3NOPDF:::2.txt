This paper integrates two recent techniques, robust PCA and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection, to restore corrupted observation matrices. The key insight is that for structured data (e.g., clustered data), the guarantees provided by robust PCA are not strong enough. However, by representing the structure using a dictionary, stronger bounds can be established. The authors provide theorems to support these claims. The remaining challenge is learning the dictionary, for which an algorithm justified by empirical results is proposed: first, compute robust PCA, and then represent the result using a dictionary.

Quality:
The paper is technically sound, having been reviewed up to a reasonable level of scrutiny. The two theorems elaborate on the conditions under which reconstruction is possible, given a dictionary. While the justification for learning the dictionary is empirical, the theoretical foundations of the paper are well-established.

Clarity:
The primary issue with the paper is its clarity. Although the overall structure is clear, the main contributions and impact of the paper have not been adequately explained to be accessible to a broad audience beyond specialists in the field. The claims need to be more clearly defined, and the impact of the results should be explicitly stated. For instance, the concept of coherence is difficult to understand, and the means of interpreting it are indirect and relegated to the appendix. One potential impact of the paper is that using dictionaries can help sidestep many earlier problems.

Furthermore, the language requires careful review to ensure that it is clear and concise. It would be beneficial to obtain a comment from the authors regarding potential revisions to address these concerns.

Originality:
The paper is based on a combination of recent techniques but includes new theorems and empirical results that support the usefulness of this combination.

Significance:
The paper advances the state-of-the-art with rigorous results. Although the proposed algorithm for finding the dictionary is heuristic, clear improvements over alternative algorithms demonstrate that the insights provided in this paper are valuable.

Comment after the author rebuttal and reviewer discussion:
There is undoubtedly publishable content and interesting contributions in this paper. My only remaining concern is that significant effort will be required to revise the paper to make it accessible to a broad audience. If a "major revision" option were available, I would vote for it. The paper successfully integrates two recent ideas, providing both theoretical results and a heuristic but empirically well-performing algorithm.
