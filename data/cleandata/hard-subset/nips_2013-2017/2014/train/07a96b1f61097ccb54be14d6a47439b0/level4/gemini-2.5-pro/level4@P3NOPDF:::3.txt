### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses a key limitation in Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA), namely the performance degradation associated with high coherence in the low-rank component. The central thesis is that the incoherence parameter, often considered a bottleneck for recovery guarantees, can be indicative of an underlying clustering structure within the data. The authors propose using Low Rank Representation (LRR) with a suitable dictionary `A` to exploit this latent structure. They provide theoretical guarantees demonstrating that this approach can partially decouple the recovery performance from the incoherence parameter, leading to stronger guarantees for both exact and noisy data decomposition.

The work is novel and makes several significant contributions to the field. I recommend acceptance, as the paper presents a substantial advance. The key contributions include:

1.  **A Novel Conceptual Framework:** The paper provides an insightful reinterpretation of the coherence parameter in RPCA, linking high coherence to a latent union-of-subspaces structure. This is a valuable contribution that connects two important lines of research and provides a new lens through which to analyze complex datasets.

2.  **A Theoretically-Grounded Method:** The authors propose an elegant solution using LRR with a dictionary to implicitly leverage this clustering structure. The theoretical analysis is solid, showing that under certain conditions on the dictionary, this approach yields stronger recovery guarantees than standard RPCA by mitigating the dependence on `μ`. This work skillfully bridges the gap between traditional RPCA and subspace clustering literature (e.g., Wang & Xu, Soltanolkotabi & Candes), but with a more general and less explicit assumption on the data structure.

3.  **An Effective New Algorithm for Robust Subspace Clustering:** A noteworthy, though understated, contribution is a new method for subspace clustering in the presence of sparse corruptions. The experiments in Section 4.2 on the Hopkins155 dataset demonstrate a significant performance improvement over the standard approach of applying RPCA followed by Subspace Clustering (SSC). This result alone is a valuable contribution to the subspace clustering community and should be highlighted more prominently by the authors.

The experimental results, on both synthetic and real-world data, effectively validate the theoretical claims and demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method. The potential for applying this framework to other domains, such as text analysis, is also promising.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the paper's core contributions are strong, the presentation could be significantly improved. The following suggestions are intended to help the authors strengthen the manuscript for the final version.

**Major Comments:**

*   **Clarity and Precision of Language:** The paper would benefit from a thorough proofreading and revision to enhance clarity. Several key statements are imprecise or ambiguous, which can hinder the reader's understanding. Being more specific about claims and terminology would strengthen the paper's impact.

    *   **Example 1 (Lines 80-82):** The description here needs rewriting. The phrase "our data" is vague. Furthermore, the claim that the proposed problem formulation "captures all the structures" is too strong. While the formulation is a necessary condition for perfect recovery under the assumed model, it is not sufficient on its own without additional qualifications.
    *   **Example 2 (Line 88):** A word appears to be missing in the phrase "is no longer a method of perfect...". This should be corrected. These are just two examples of a broader pattern that should be addressed throughout the manuscript.

**Minor Comments and Discussion Points:**

*   **Relation to Prior Work on Coherence in SSC:** The authors correctly connect their work to the subspace clustering literature. It is worth noting that Wang and Xu ("Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering") have also discussed how high coherence, which is detrimental for RPCA, can actually be beneficial for subspace clustering. The key distinction is that in their model, the entire dataset need not be low-rank, only the data within each cluster. The current paper can be viewed as an elegant synthesis, addressing the case where a global low-rank structure coexists with a local union-of-subspaces structure that induces high coherence. A brief discussion of this nuance could further contextualize the paper's contribution.

*   **Alternative Baselines:** It would be interesting to discuss or experimentally compare the proposed algorithm against a two-stage baseline: first performing noisy subspace clustering (e.g., using an `ℓ₁` penalty) to identify clusters and remove outliers, and then applying PCA to each identified subspace. While theoretical guarantees for such a two-stage method may not exist, the empirical comparison could further highlight the benefits of the proposed integrated approach.

### **Conclusion**

In summary, this paper makes several substantial contributions to the fields of low-rank matrix recovery and subspace clustering. The connection it establishes between data coherence and clustering structure is insightful, and the proposed LRR-based method is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. Despite some minor issues with presentation that can be addressed in a revision, the paper's contributions are significant and impactful. I recommend acceptance.
