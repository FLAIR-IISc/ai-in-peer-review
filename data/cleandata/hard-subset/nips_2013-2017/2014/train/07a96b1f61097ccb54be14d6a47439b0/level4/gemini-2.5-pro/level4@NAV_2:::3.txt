This paper addresses the problem of recovering a low-rank matrix from observations corrupted by gross, sparse errors, a task for which Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) is a standard tool. The authors make the key observation that RPCA's performance degrades on "coherent" data, and they provide the valuable insight that such coherence naturally arises from clustering structures within the data—a common scenario in practice. The core contribution is to show, both theoretically and empirically, that Low-Rank Representation (LRR) can overcome this limitation when supplied with an appropriate dictionary. Specifically, the paper proves that if the dictionary is low-rank and its column space contains the column space of the true low-rank matrix, LRR's recovery guarantee becomes independent of the coherence parameters that are problematic for RPCA. This theoretical insight leads to a practical two-stage algorithm where RPCA is first used to generate an initial estimate, which is then used to construct a dictionary for a second LRR-based recovery step.

This work connects well with the extensive literature on RPCA and low-rank matrix recovery, but its most direct relatives are in the subspace clustering literature, such as "Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering" by Wang and Xu, and "A geometric analysis of subspace clustering with outliers" by Soltanolkotabi and Candes. While those works also deal with union-of-subspace structures, this paper's framing of the problem in terms of coherence is a novel perspective that is arguably more general. The proposed method is shown to yield significant improvements over standard RPCA on both synthetic data and a real-world motion segmentation task.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical claims, providing recovery guarantees for LRR in both noiseless and noisy settings, are well-supported. The experimental validation is thorough, with a clear phase transition plot on synthetic data and strong results on the corrupted Hopkins155 dataset, which convincingly demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed algorithm.

*   **Clarity:** The main weakness of the manuscript is its clarity. The language is often imprecise and would benefit from careful proofreading and revision. For example:
    *   Lines 80-82 ("...RPCA captures only the low-rankness property, which however is not the only property of our data..."): This statement is vague. It would be clearer to explicitly state what additional structures (like clustering) are being referred to.
    *   Line 88 ("...RPCA is no longer a method of perfect..."): This sentence is incomplete.
    *   Overall, a thorough pass to improve grammatical precision and sentence structure would significantly enhance the paper's impact and readability.

*   **Originality:** The work presents several original contributions. The connection established between the coherence parameter and the underlying clustering structure of the data is a novel and important insight. While using LRR with a dictionary is not new, the specific theoretical analysis showing how a low-rank dictionary circumvents the coherence issue is a significant advance. The proposed two-step algorithm is a simple yet novel and effective practical application of this theory.

*   **Significance:** The results are important and likely to be built upon by other researchers. The paper makes at least three significant contributions:
    1.  It provides a clear, physical interpretation for high coherence in low-rank data, moving it from a theoretical "assumption" to an observable consequence of data structure.
    2.  It shows how to implicitly exploit this structure using the LRR framework with a learned dictionary, advancing our understanding of how and why matrix factorization and dictionary-based methods can outperform monolithic approaches like RPCA.
    3.  The algorithm effectively serves as a new state-of-the-art method for subspace clustering on data with sparse corruptions. The dramatic error reduction on the motion segmentation task is a major contribution in its own right, and the authors should consider highlighting this more explicitly.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  The authors should carefully revise the manuscript to improve clarity, as noted above. Being more precise about claims and definitions will strengthen the paper.

2.  The paper notes a connection to Wang and Xu's work on subspace clustering, where high coherence can actually be beneficial. It would be valuable to add a short discussion contrasting that setting with the one in this paper, perhaps by clarifying that this work addresses a combined global low-rank and union-of-subspaces structure, which is different from scenarios where only individual clusters are assumed to be low-rank.

3.  The performance improvement on the motion segmentation task is very strong. This suggests the proposed algorithm is a significant contribution to the problem of robust subspace clustering. The authors should consider framing this as a key practical result of their work in the introduction and conclusion.

4.  A potential point for future discussion or experimentation could be a comparison against an alternative pipeline: first, run a robust subspace clustering algorithm on the corrupted data to identify clusters, and then perform PCA on each cluster individually. While such a method may lack theoretical guarantees, it would serve as an interesting baseline.

**Summary and Recommendation**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and significant theoretical insight into the relationship between data coherence and clustering structure.
*   Proves that LRR with a low-rank dictionary can overcome a key limitation of standard RPCA.
*   Presents a practical algorithm that demonstrates a substantial state-of-the-art performance improvement on a challenging real-world task.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The clarity of the writing needs significant improvement.

Despite the need for revisions to the text, the scientific contributions of this paper are substantial and impactful. I strongly recommend acceptance.
