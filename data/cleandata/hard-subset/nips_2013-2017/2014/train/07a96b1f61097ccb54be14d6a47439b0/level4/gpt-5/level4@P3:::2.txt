Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies recovery of low-rank matrices from grossly corrupted observations when the data exhibit clustering (i.e., are “coherent”). The authors argue that RPCA’s guarantees degrade with increasing coherence, particularly via the second coherence parameter µ2, which grows with the number of underlying clusters. They analyze this “µ2-phenomenon” and propose addressing it using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with an appropriately chosen dictionary A. The main theoretical result shows that if A is low-rank and its column space contains the true column space of the clean matrix (U0 ⊂ UA), then LRR recovers the low-rank and sparse components without depending on µ2 (and µ3), both in noiseless and noisy settings. They further propose a practical unsupervised two-stage algorithm: run RPCA to obtain an initial low-rank estimate, build a low-rank, column-normalized dictionary from it, and then run LRR.

This work builds on RPCA (Candès et al.) and LRR (Liu et al.), and relates to robust subspace clustering (e.g., SSC; Elhamifar & Vidal) and motion segmentation. The contribution lies in connecting coherence growth due to clustering with RPCA’s limitations, and in showing that LRR with low-rank dictionaries can sidestep µ2-driven failures.

Theoretical contributions:
- Theorem 1 (noiseless): With λ = 1/√n1, if rank(L0) ≤ rank(A) ≤ O(n2/(µ1(A) log n1)), U0 ⊂ UA, columns of A are unit-norm, and up to roughly 50% of entries are corrupted, then LRR exactly recovers AZ∗ = L0 and S∗ = S0 with high probability, without depending on µ2 or µ3.
- Theorem 2 (noisy): Under dense noise with ‖X − L0 − S0‖F ≤ ε and up to ~35% gross corruptions, the LRR solution yields bounded errors ‖AZ∗ − L0‖F and ‖S∗ − S0‖F proportional to ε.

Algorithmic contribution:
An unsupervised procedure constructs a low-rank dictionary from an RPCA estimate (rank-truncated and column-normalized), then solves LRR. On synthetic data (200×1000, union of five subspaces), the success region increases by 47% relative to RPCA. On motion sequences with missing entries, preprocessing with the proposed method improves downstream subspace clustering error (e.g., SSC error reduced from 22.81% on corrupted data to 9.50% with RPCA and further to 5.74% with LRR+dictionary).

Quality:
- Theory: The conditions and proofs appear sound and clearly articulate how a low-rank dictionary containing U0 removes dependence on µ2. The rank bounds (O(n2/log n1)) and corruption tolerances are competitive.
- Practicality: The unsupervised dictionary-learning step is heuristic; there is no formal guarantee that the RPCA-derived dictionary satisfies U0 ⊂ UA, though empirically it helps. Computational claims are plausible, but runtimes are not systematically reported.

Clarity:
Clarity is the main weakness. The paper is notation-heavy and assumes familiarity with coherence parameters. The intuition behind µ1, µ2, µ3 and the µ2-phenomenon is not sufficiently motivated in the main text, making the narrative difficult for non-specialists. The roles of assumptions (U0 ⊂ UA, unit-norm columns, rank bounds) and their practical enforceability need clearer, earlier explanation. Language and typography require careful editing.

Originality:
The identification and analysis of the µ2-phenomenon for clustered data, and the demonstration that LRR with a low-rank dictionary can avoid µ2, are novel and insightful. The two-stage RPCA→LRR pipeline is simple but well-motivated, and the theoretical decoupling from µ2 is a meaningful advance over RPCA-only analyses.

Significance:
The work advances robust low-rank recovery in regimes common in practice (clustered data) and provides a pragmatic recipe with empirical gains on synthetic and motion data. The principle “use low-rank dictionaries that cover the signal subspace” has potential impact across subspace recovery and clustering. That said, broader empirical validation and clearer guidance would increase practical uptake.

Specific suggestions for improvement:
- Provide geometric intuition and simple examples for µ1, µ2, µ3 and why µ2 grows with the number of clusters. Bring key intuition from the appendix into the main text.
- Clarify the necessity and practicality of U0 ⊂ UA; discuss how approximate containment affects recovery and whether the theory can tolerate subspace mismatch.
- Analyze sensitivity to hyperparameters (λ, ε), the rank-thresholding heuristic (σi > 10−3σ1), and column normalization. Include ablations.
- Report runtimes and memory usage for RPCA, LRR, and the full pipeline; quantify when the approach is faster/slower than RPCA.
- Expand comparisons: include robust matrix completion/baselines (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, RPCA variants) and discuss handling of missing data without replacing with ±1.
- Clarify statements about equivalence to RPCA when A is orthogonal and when LRR reduces to RPCA (A = I).
- Thoroughly edit for language and typos; tighten exposition to highlight contributions and practical takeaways.

Pros:
- Addresses an important failure mode of RPCA with coherent, clustered data.
- Solid theoretical results that avoid dependence on µ2/µ3 under clear conditions.
- Simple, practical algorithm with notable empirical improvements.
- Insightful connection between coherence, clustering, and dictionary design.

Cons:
- Clarity and accessibility are limited; heavy notation and insufficient intuition.
- Strong assumptions on the dictionary; heuristic learning lacks theoretical backing.
- Limited experimental breadth (datasets, baselines, runtime analysis).
- Some experimental choices (e.g., filling missing values with ±1) are questionable.

Overall assessment:
There is publishable content with meaningful theoretical and empirical contributions. My main reservation is clarity: substantial rewriting is needed to make the paper accessible beyond a narrow audience. If a “major revision” option existed, I would recommend it. In the current format, I lean toward weak accept contingent on significant revisions to improve exposition and to strengthen empirical evaluation and practical guidance.
