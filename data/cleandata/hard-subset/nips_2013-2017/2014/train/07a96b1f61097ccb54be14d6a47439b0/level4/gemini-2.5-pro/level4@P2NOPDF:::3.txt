This paper proposes a novel approach to improve the recovery guarantees of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) by using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a dictionary, A. The central insight is that the incoherence parameter μ, often considered a bottleneck for RPCA guarantees, can arise from an underlying clustering structure in the data. Under certain conditions on the dictionary A, the authors demonstrate that this structure can be exploited via LRR to mitigate the dependence on μ. This leads to theoretically stronger guarantees for both the exact low-rank and sparse decomposition problem and its noisy extension.

The method presented is both novel and significant. It is closely related to the union-of-subspaces assumption prevalent in the subspace clustering literature (e.g., Wang & Xu; Soltanolkotabi & Candès). However, the framework in this paper is arguably more general, as it is less explicit about the clustering assumption. The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world data corroborate the theoretical findings. I concur with the authors' remark that the structure they identify is likely present in other important applications, such as text data analysis.

In summary, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the literature on low-rank matrix recovery. I recommend its acceptance to NIPS. The following points are intended as constructive feedback for further improving the manuscript.

**Detailed Comments**

1.  **Clarity of Description (Lines 80-82):** The description in this section could be rephrased for greater clarity. For instance, the term "our data" is ambiguous. Furthermore, the statement about "perfect recovery" seems to imply that satisfying the problem's structural assumptions is sufficient. It would be more precise to acknowledge that other conditions are also necessary for the theoretical guarantee to hold.

2.  **Incomplete Phrase (Line 88):** The phrase "...is no longer a method of perfect" appears to be incomplete. Please clarify what is meant here.

3.  **General Readability:** The two points above are examples of minor issues that could be resolved with careful proofreading. I encourage the authors to review the manuscript for similar instances to improve overall precision and clarity in the language.

4.  **Connection to Prior Work:** It is worth noting the connection to Wang and Xu's "Noisy Sparse Subspace Clustering," where they also observe that high coherence can be beneficial for subspace clustering (in contrast to its detrimental role in standard RPCA). The key distinction is that their work assumes each cluster is low-rank while the overall data matrix is not necessarily so. The structure exploited in this submission can thus be viewed as a compelling synthesis of a global low-rank structure and a local union-of-subspaces structure. A brief discussion of this relationship could further contextualize the paper's contribution.

5.  **Additional Experimental Comparison:** A valuable addition to the experimental section would be a comparison against a two-stage baseline: first, perform noisy subspace clustering (e.g., using an ℓ1 penalty) to identify clusters, and then apply PCA to each identified subspace. While theoretical guarantees for such a two-stage approach are not yet established, an empirical comparison would provide useful context for the proposed method's performance.

6.  **Highlighting Contributions:** A significant, and perhaps under-emphasized, contribution is the method in Section 4.2 for subspace clustering in the presence of sparse corruptions. The demonstrated improvements over the standard RPCA+SSC pipeline on the Hopkins155 dataset are substantial. The authors should consider highlighting this more explicitly as one of the paper's key contributions. In my view, the paper offers at least three major contributions that merit its acceptance:
    *   It formalizes a clustering structure that explains the emergence of high coherence in data.
    *   It demonstrates how this structure can be implicitly exploited using a dictionary-based LRR framework to strengthen recovery guarantees.
    *   It provides a new and effective algorithm for robust subspace clustering, which is a valuable contribution in its own right.
