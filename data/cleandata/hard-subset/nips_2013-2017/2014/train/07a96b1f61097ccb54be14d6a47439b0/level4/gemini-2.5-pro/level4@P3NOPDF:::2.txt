This paper addresses the problem of restoring corrupted observation matrices by combining two powerful techniques: Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) and dictionary-based structure-preserving projection. The central insight is that for data with inherent structure (e.g., clusters), the recovery guarantees provided by standard RPCA are insufficient. The authors posit that by explicitly representing this structure with a dictionary, one can establish significantly stronger theoretical bounds for recovery.

The paper's contributions are twofold. First, it presents theorems that formalize the conditions under which exact reconstruction is possible, given an appropriate dictionary. Second, it proposes a practical, two-stage algorithm to learn this dictionary: first, apply RPCA to the corrupted data, and then learn a dictionary from the recovered low-rank component. This algorithm is justified through a series of empirical experiments.

**Quality and Technical Soundness**

The technical contributions of the paper appear to be sound. The two main theorems rigorously elaborate the conditions for successful reconstruction, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed framework when a suitable dictionary is known. The empirical validation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed heuristic algorithm for dictionary learning, showing clear improvements over alternative methods. This suggests that the core insights of the paper are valuable and translate to practical performance gains.

**Clarity**

Clarity is the most significant issue with this manuscript. While the overall structure of the paper is logical, the explanation of the core contributions and their impact is not clear enough to be accessible to a general machine learning audience. The paper is currently written for a very narrow specialist audience, which limits its potential impact.

Specific issues include:
*   **Undefined Claims and Impact:** The paper's central claims and the significance of its results need to be more precisely defined and explicated. For instance, a key potential impact—that using dictionaries can sidestep challenges faced by earlier methods—is alluded to but never fully developed. A more direct explanation of *why* these new theoretical bounds are important and what practical advantages they confer would greatly strengthen the paper.
*   **Inaccessible Concepts:** Key concepts are not explained intuitively. The notion of "coherence," which is central to the theoretical guarantees, is particularly difficult to understand from the main text. Relying on an indirect explanation in the appendix is not sufficient for a concept of this importance.
*   **Language and Readability:** The manuscript would benefit from careful proofreading to improve grammar and sentence structure, which would enhance overall readability.

Given that the underlying work is strong, it is crucial that the authors address these presentational issues. I would ask the authors to comment on how they plan to revise the paper to make its important contributions more accessible.

**Originality**

The work is based on a creative combination of two recent and powerful techniques. While the individual components are not new, their synthesis is novel and well-motivated. The paper further contributes new theoretical results (the two theorems) and novel empirical findings that support the utility of this combination, making it a valuable and original contribution to the field.

**Significance**

This paper advances the state-of-the-art in structured data recovery with rigorous results. Even though the proposed algorithm for learning the dictionary is heuristic, its superior empirical performance demonstrates that the insights presented are highly useful. The work provides both a new theoretical lens through which to analyze this problem and a practical algorithm that outperforms existing approaches.

**Post-Rebuttal Assessment**

This paper contains publishable content and makes interesting contributions. The combination of RPCA and dictionary-based methods is insightful, and this is supported by both new theoretical analysis and a heuristic algorithm that performs well empirically. My only remaining concern is the significant effort required to rewrite the paper for clarity and accessibility. The work is currently comprehensible only to a very small subset of experts. If a "major revision" were an option, I would strongly recommend it to ensure the paper's valuable contributions can be appreciated by the wider community.
