

The paper investigates the limitations of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) when handling coherent data, which commonly arises from clustering structures in real-world applications. The authors propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with an appropriately learned dictionary A to overcome these limitations, demonstrating theoretically that when the dictionary itself is low-rank, LRR can effectively mitigate the negative impact of coherence parameters (particularly μ2 and μ3) that typically degrade RPCA's performance as the number of clusters increases. This work extends previous NIPS contributions on RPCA [4] and LRR [13], while connecting to subspace clustering literature such as [5, 6, 14, 21].

The paper makes three substantial contributions: (1) It identifies the relationship between clustering structures in data and increased coherence parameters, (2) It establishes theoretical conditions under which LRR with a low-rank dictionary can overcome coherence limitations, and (3) It proposes a practical unsupervised algorithm for dictionary learning that outperforms RPCA in both synthetic and real-world experiments. The experimental results on randomly generated matrices and motion sequences validate the theoretical claims, showing significant improvements over RPCA, especially when data exhibit clustering structures.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS community, as it builds upon and extends established techniques while addressing a fundamental limitation in low-rank matrix recovery. It connects meaningfully to recent NIPS work on robust subspace clustering [5, 6, 14, 21] and provides new insights into the physical meaning of coherence parameters.

Strengths:
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides meaningful insights into the relationship between data structures and recovery guarantees.
- The proposed algorithm is practical and demonstrates significant improvements over RPCA in both synthetic and real-world settings.
- The work connects to important applications like motion segmentation, where coherent data structures are common.
- The experiments are thorough and well-designed, covering both synthetic data and real-world motion sequences.

Weaknesses:
- The description in lines 80-82 could be clarified to better specify what "our data" refers to and to more precisely state the conditions required for perfect recovery.
- Line 88 contains an incomplete phrase ("is no longer a method of perfect (WHAT?)").
- The paper could better highlight the connection between their approach and the work by Wang and Xu on noisy sparse subspace clustering, which also addresses the role of coherence in clustering settings.
- The paper could more explicitly position its contributions relative to the "union-of-subspace" structure that has been explored in previous subspace clustering literature.

The paper has substantial technical merit and addresses an important limitation in low-rank matrix recovery. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the authors should improve the clarity of their presentation and more thoroughly contextualize their work within the broader literature on subspace clustering.

I recommend acceptance, as the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery with clear theoretical and practical implications.
