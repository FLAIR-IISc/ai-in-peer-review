

The paper addresses a significant limitation of RPCA (Robust Principal Component Analysis) when dealing with coherent data that exhibits clustering structures. The authors demonstrate that RPCA's performance degrades as data coherence increases—a common scenario when data points cluster within subspaces—because RPCA implicitly assumes incoherent low-rank matrices. They propose a solution using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully constructed dictionary A, proving that when A is low-rank and satisfies certain conditions, LRR can avoid coherence parameter dependence and achieve stronger theoretical guarantees.

This work builds on recent advances in subspace clustering, particularly Liu et al.'s "Robust Subspace Segmentation by Low-Rank Representation" and Wang & Xu's "Provable Subspace Clustering: When LRR Meets SSC." While these papers explore union-of-subspace structures for clustering, they don't explicitly connect clustering to coherence issues in RPCA. The current paper bridges this gap by showing that coherence parameters aren't merely theoretical assumptions but reflect intrinsic clustering structures in the data.

The paper makes three key contributions: First, it characterizes how clustering structures increase coherence parameters (particularly µ2), explaining RPCA's failure in such scenarios. Second, it establishes theoretical conditions under which LRR with a low-rank dictionary can overcome this limitation, proving that coherence parameters can be avoided. Third, it provides a practical algorithm for constructing such dictionaries in unsupervised settings, demonstrating significant improvements over RPCA in both synthetic and real-world experiments.

The theoretical contributions are compelling. The authors prove that when the dictionary A is low-rank and contains the column space of the true low-rank matrix L0, LRR achieves exact recovery without depending on coherence parameters. Their noise analysis extends these results to practical scenarios with dense Gaussian noise. The proof techniques are sound and build appropriately on existing RPCA theory, with clear distinctions between the behavior of different coherence parameters (µ1, µ2, µ3).

The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The synthetic experiments show that the proposed algorithm achieves a 47% wider success region than RPCA across various rank and corruption levels. The motion sequence experiments on Hopkins155 demonstrate a 40% relative improvement in clustering error rates over RPCA when using their dictionary learning approach. These results strongly support the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical utility.

I have a few constructive suggestions for refinement:
1. The paper would benefit from more precise distinction between the roles of µ1, µ2, and µ3. The current explanation of how µ2 increases with clustering while µ1 remains constant could be clearer.
2. The connection to subspace clustering is valuable, but the paper should explicitly state how their dictionary learning approach differs from standard subspace clustering methods that use LRR with A=X. The current Section 4.2 describes a novel approach to subspace clustering with sparse corruption, which is a significant contribution that could be emphasized more prominently.
3. The phrase "method of perfection" (line 88) is unclear and should be revised to "method of choice" or "optimal method."
4. The paper references Wang & Xu (2013) on coherence in subspace clustering but doesn't fully explore the contrast between their findings (higher coherence is beneficial for subspace clustering) and the current work's focus on coherence as a problem for RPCA. This contrast would strengthen the paper's position.

Overall, this is a well-motivated, technically sound paper that makes a significant contribution to understanding low-rank matrix recovery. The theoretical insights about coherence parameters and their relation to clustering structures are novel and important. The practical algorithm provides a clear improvement over RPCA, with compelling experimental validation.

The paper satisfies all NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical results are sound, the experiments are thorough, and the authors carefully discuss limitations.
- Clarity: The paper is well-organized and presents complex ideas accessibly.
- Originality: It identifies a new perspective on coherence parameters and provides a novel solution to a recognized problem.
- Significance: The method has immediate practical applications in image processing, computer vision, and other fields where data exhibit clustering structures.

The paper is clearly written and addresses an important limitation of current methods. I recommend acceptance. This work advances both the theory and practice of low-rank matrix recovery, with implications for numerous applications in data analysis and machine learning.
