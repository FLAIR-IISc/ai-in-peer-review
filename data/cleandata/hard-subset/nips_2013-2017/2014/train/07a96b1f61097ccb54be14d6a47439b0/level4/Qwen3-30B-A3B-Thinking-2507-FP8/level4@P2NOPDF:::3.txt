

The paper introduces a novel approach leveraging Low Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary **A** to enhance Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA). It establishes that the incoherence parameter **μ**—typically a limiting factor in RPCA recovery guarantees—can actually encode underlying clustering structure in the data. Under mild conditions on **A**, the authors demonstrate that LRR can exploit this structure to weaken the dependence on **μ**, yielding stronger theoretical guarantees for both exact low-rank/sparse decomposition and its noisy variant. This insight bridges a critical gap between RPCA and subspace clustering frameworks.

The contribution is significant and well-motivated. While prior work (e.g., Wang & Xu, 2013; Soltanolkotabi & Candes, 2012; Soltanolkotabi et al., 2014) assumes union-of-subspaces structures for subspace clustering, this paper explicitly identifies how **μ**’s role shifts from a constraint to a structural indicator. Crucially, the authors clarify that the data need not be globally low-rank—only that each cluster exhibits low-rank structure—resolving a key ambiguity in existing literature. Experimental validation on synthetic and real data (including text applications) robustly supports the theory, suggesting broad applicability.

The paper delivers at least three major contributions:  
(1) A precise characterization of how high **μ** reveals clustering structure,  
(2) A method to implicitly leverage this structure via dictionary-based LRR, and  
(3) A practical advancement in subspace clustering with sparse corruptions (evidenced by superior performance on Hopkins155 over RPCA+SSC in Section 4.2). This last point—though framed as a secondary result—represents a notable, actionable improvement for real-world applications.

**Key strengths:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the connection to clustering structure is novel, and the experimental results are compelling. The work fundamentally reframes **μ**’s role in RPCA and provides a pathway to stronger guarantees in structured data regimes.

**Minor revisions required:**  
1. **Clarity of terminology** (Lines 80–82): Avoid vague phrasing like "our data." Specify what "our data" refers to (e.g., "the observed data matrix **X**"). Also clarify that even with the proposed framework, perfect recovery requires additional conditions beyond the clustering structure.  
2. **Precision in Line 88:** Correct the incomplete phrase "no longer a method of perfect (WHAT?)" to "no longer requires perfect recovery."  
3. **Citation context:** Wang & Xu (2013) note that higher **μ** *benefits* subspace clustering (unlike RPCA/matrix completion), as it reflects cluster separation. This paper’s method *combines* local low-rank (per cluster) with global clustering structure—a key distinction worth emphasizing.  
4. **Comparative discussion:** A brief comparison to solving subspace clustering via *ℓ*₁-regularized methods followed by per-cluster PCA would strengthen the argument, though no provable guarantees exist for the latter.  

**Overall assessment:** This paper makes a substantial, well-executed contribution to robust subspace learning. The theoretical insights resolve a longstanding ambiguity in RPCA, while the practical improvements in subspace clustering with corruptions (Section 4.2) offer immediate utility. The writing is generally clear but requires minor refinements for precision (as noted). The work is a strong fit for NIPS and merits acceptance.
