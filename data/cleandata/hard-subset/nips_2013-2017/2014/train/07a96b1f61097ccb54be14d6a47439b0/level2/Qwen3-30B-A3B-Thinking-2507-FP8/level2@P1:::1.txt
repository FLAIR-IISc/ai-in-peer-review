

This paper addresses an important limitation of RPCA (Robust Principal Component Analysis) when dealing with coherent data, which is common in real-world applications with clustering structures. The authors identify that RPCA's performance degrades as data coherence increases (with more clusters), and propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a properly configured dictionary to overcome this issue. The key theoretical contribution is proving that LRR is immune to coherence parameters when the dictionary itself is low-rank, providing a clear criterion for dictionary design. The paper also introduces a practical unsupervised algorithm that uses RPCA to construct the dictionary, which outperforms RPCA in experiments on synthetic data and motion sequences.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it clarifies the physical meaning of coherence parameters by linking them to clustering structures in data, which is a valuable insight for the community. Second, it provides a principled approach to handling coherent data through LRR with a low-rank dictionary, going beyond the previous work on LRR that primarily focused on the special case where A=X. Third, the proposed algorithm is straightforward to implement and shows consistent improvements over RPCA in both synthetic and real-world experiments.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a good balance between theoretical analysis and experimental validation. The authors provide detailed proofs for their main theorems, and the experimental section thoroughly evaluates their approach on both randomly generated matrices and motion sequences from the Hopkins155 database. The results clearly demonstrate that their method outperforms RPCA, with the clustering error rate reduced by 40% in the motion sequence experiments.

In terms of originality, the paper identifies a specific limitation of RPCA that hasn't been previously addressed in the literature, and provides a theoretically grounded solution that builds upon but extends beyond existing work on LRR. While the connection between coherence and clustering is important, the paper could have provided a more detailed explanation of why RPCA fails with coherent data beyond just stating the coherence parameter bound. The authors should clarify how the coherence parameters specifically relate to the clustering structure in the data.

Regarding significance, the problem addressed is important for real-world applications where data often exhibits clustering structures (e.g., motion segmentation, image processing). The proposed method provides a practical solution that can be readily applied to existing RPCA pipelines, with only a modest computational overhead (1.2x the time of RPCA). The results show meaningful improvements in real-world applications, suggesting this could be valuable to practitioners.

Some minor weaknesses include the conservative theoretical bounds (e.g., requiring rank(L0) ≤ O(n2/log n1) compared to RPCA's O(n2/(log n1)^2)), which might limit the practical applicability. The experimental section could have included more comparisons against alternative methods that handle coherent data, such as the robust subspace clustering methods mentioned in the paper. The paper also doesn't discuss how the dictionary learning algorithm might perform when RPCA's recovery is already poor.

The paper is technically sound, with clear theoretical results and well-designed experiments. The claims are well-supported by both the theoretical analysis and empirical results. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach, particularly noting that their method is most beneficial when RPCA is already performing reasonably well.

Overall, this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by addressing a specific limitation of RPCA that has been overlooked in previous work. The theoretical insights about coherence parameters and clustering structures are valuable, and the proposed algorithm provides a practical solution that improves upon existing methods. The paper is well-written and the experiments are convincing. While the theoretical bounds could be tightened, the practical improvements demonstrated in the experiments are significant.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It addresses a real problem in a meaningful way, provides clear theoretical insights, and demonstrates practical benefits through well-designed experiments. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and makes a valuable contribution to the field of low-rank matrix recovery.
