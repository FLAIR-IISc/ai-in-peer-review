This paper presents a novel approach to recover low-rank matrices from grossly corrupted observations, particularly when the data exhibits clustering structures. The authors argue that the state-of-the-art Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) method may not be effective in such cases due to its preference for incoherent data. To address this issue, they propose using the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework with a low-rank dictionary matrix. The main contribution of the paper is the theoretical proof that LRR can avoid the coherence parameters that increase with the underlying cluster number, provided that the dictionary matrix is low-rank and contains information about the ground truth matrix.

The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the problem statement, the proposed approach, and the theoretical results. The authors also provide extensive experimental results on both synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide insights into the physical meaning of coherence, which is a widely used concept in various literatures. The authors show that the coherence parameters are not just assumptions for a proof but rather quantities that relate to the extra structures (beyond low-rankness) intrinsically existing in the data.

The proposed algorithm, which uses the output of RPCA to construct the dictionary matrix in LRR, is simple and effective. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm, showing that it is much lower than that of RPCA when the obtained dictionary matrix is fairly low-rank.

The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating a significant improvement over RPCA in both synthetic and real-world data. The results on the Hopkins155 database, in particular, show that the proposed approach can effectively recover the corrupted trajectory matrices and improve the performance of subspace clustering methods.

However, there are a few minor issues that could be improved. For example, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and the potential future work directions. Additionally, some of the notations and symbols used in the paper could be clarified or defined more explicitly.

Overall, I believe that this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of low-rank matrix recovery and provides a novel approach to handling coherent data. The paper is well-written, and the results are impressive. I would recommend accepting this paper for publication.
