**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses a key limitation of the popular Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) method for recovering a low-rank matrix `L0` from grossly corrupted observations `X = L0 + S0`. The authors observe that RPCA's performance degrades when the low-rank component `L0` is "coherent". The main contribution is to provide both a physical intuition for and a practical solution to this problem. First, the paper compellingly argues that high coherence (specifically, the µ2 and µ3 parameters) naturally arises when the data points (columns of `L0`) exhibit a clustering structure, i.e., they are drawn from a union of multiple low-dimensional subspaces. As the number of clusters increases, coherence increases, making RPCA less effective.

To overcome this, the authors propose leveraging the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework, where the recovery problem is formulated as `X = AZ + S`. Their key theoretical insight, presented in Theorem 1, is that if the dictionary `A` is itself low-rank and its column space contains the column space of the true data `L0`, then the LRR recovery guarantee is immune to the problematic coherence parameters µ2 and µ3. This insight motivates a practical, two-step unsupervised algorithm: first, run standard RPCA to obtain an initial estimate of `L0`; second, use this estimate to construct a low-rank dictionary `Â`; finally, solve the LRR problem using `Â` to obtain a refined and more accurate recovery. This approach is shown to significantly outperform standard RPCA in experiments on both synthetic data with controlled clustering structures and real-world motion segmentation data.

**Quality and Clarity**

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical claims (Theorems 1 and 2) are well-stated and provide a strong foundation for the proposed method. They formally establish the conditions under which LRR can overcome the coherence limitations of RPCA. The paper is very well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is particularly strong; the "µ2-phenomenon" is explained intuitively and illustrated with a clear figure, making the problem tangible and the proposed solution well-justified. The experimental section is thorough, with a large-scale synthetic experiment that clearly delineates the performance advantage (the phase transition plots are very effective) and a real-world application that demonstrates practical utility.

**Originality and Significance**

The paper's contributions are both original and significant. While RPCA and LRR are established methods, this work is the first to systematically study the failure mode of RPCA on coherent data, link this coherence to a common data structure (clustering), and propose a principled LRR-based solution. This provides a deeper understanding of the widely used coherence "assumptions" in the low-rank recovery literature, reframing them as properties related to intrinsic data structure. The proposed method represents a tangible advance over RPCA for a class of problems that are common in practice (e.g., motion segmentation, where data comes from multiple moving objects). Given the widespread use of RPCA, a work that identifies a key limitation and provides an effective, practical fix is of high significance to the NIPS community. The insights into the general LRR model (with a learned dictionary `A` not equal to `X` or `I`) are also a valuable contribution.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**
1.  **Important Problem:** The paper addresses a practical and important limitation of RPCA, a cornerstone method in the field.
2.  **Clear Motivation and Insight:** The connection established between clustering structure and the µ2 coherence parameter is a novel and valuable insight that clearly motivates the entire paper.
3.  **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The main theorems provide a rigorous justification for why a low-rank dictionary helps LRR avoid the issues that plague RPCA on coherent data.
4.  **Effective Practical Algorithm:** The proposed two-step algorithm is simple, intuitive, and does not require significant changes to existing solvers. It provides a practical way to realize the theoretical benefits in an unsupervised setting.
5.  **Convincing Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate a clear and significant performance improvement over the RPCA baseline on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  **Theoretical-Practical Gap:** The theoretical results assume an ideal dictionary `A` whose column space `UA` contains the true `U0`. The practical algorithm, however, constructs a dictionary `Â` from the output of RPCA, which may be imperfect precisely in the challenging, coherent regime this paper targets. While the algorithm works well empirically, the paper would be strengthened by a discussion on the robustness of the LRR step to an imperfectly learned dictionary where `U0` is only approximately contained in `UÂ`.
2.  **Scope of Coherence:** The paper focuses almost entirely on the µ2-phenomenon. It is briefly mentioned that µ1 could also be large, but this case is left for future work. It would be clearer to state upfront that the proposed method is specifically designed to handle high µ2/µ3 coherence arising from clustered columns, and may not apply if high µ1 coherence (from clustered rows) is the primary issue.
3.  **Computational Cost:** The authors claim the algorithm is only about 1.2 times as expensive as RPCA. While the reasoning is plausible, it would be beneficial to include actual runtime comparisons in the experimental section to formally substantiate this claim.
4.  **Algorithm Details:** In Algorithm 1, the dictionary `Â` is constructed from the n columns of `L̃0`, making it an `m x n` matrix. This is a valid choice, but it might be helpful to explicitly state that the dictionary size `d` is set to `n` in the practical implementation.

Overall, the weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the paper's core contributions. They are primarily suggestions for clarification and potential avenues for future investigation. The paper presents a well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated improvement for a fundamental problem in machine learning.
