

This paper addresses a limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) when applied to coherent data, which arises in many real-world applications due to clustering structures. The authors argue that RPCA's performance degrades as data coherence increases (with more clusters), and propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a properly configured dictionary to overcome this issue. The key theoretical contribution is proving that LRR with a low-rank dictionary becomes immune to the coherence parameter that increases with cluster number, providing a practical algorithm for dictionary construction in unsupervised environments.

The paper makes several strong contributions. The theoretical analysis is sound, with clear proofs establishing conditions under which LRR outperforms RPCA. The connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters is well-motivated and supported by theoretical analysis and experiments. The proposed algorithm for dictionary learning is practical, computationally efficient (only 1.2x the cost of RPCA in practice), and demonstrates significant improvements over RPCA on both synthetic and real motion data. The experiments are comprehensive, covering a range of rank values, corruption levels, and real-world motion sequences from the Hopkins155 database.

The work demonstrates high quality through its rigorous theoretical analysis, well-designed experiments, and clear presentation of results. The paper appropriately references related work, including the foundational RPCA paper and LRR framework. The significance of the findings is well-established through both theoretical guarantees and empirical results showing up to 47% wider success area for the proposed method compared to RPCA on synthetic data, and 40% relative improvement in clustering error rates on motion sequences.

The paper shows strong originality by identifying and addressing a specific limitation of RPCA that has not been previously explored in depth. While LRR with general dictionaries has been studied before, the authors provide novel theoretical insights about the role of dictionary properties in handling coherent data. The paper makes a clear contribution to understanding how to handle coherent data, which is an important practical issue in many applications.

Clarity is generally good, with well-organized presentation and clear explanations of key concepts. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the figures effectively illustrate the main findings. The paper could be improved by providing more intuition about why a low-rank dictionary is effective, as this is a key insight. The connection between the theoretical guarantees and practical implementation could also be strengthened.

The paper could be improved by addressing a few points. First, while the paper establishes that a low-rank dictionary is sufficient, it doesn't fully explore whether it's necessary, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution. Second, the comparison with other methods beyond RPCA is limited; including comparisons with other robust low-rank recovery approaches would better establish the relative significance of the contribution. Third, the paper claims that "the coherence parameters are not 'assumptions' for a proof, but rather some excellent quantities that relate in nature to the extra structures," but this claim could be better supported with more concrete examples.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of low-rank matrix recovery. The theoretical insights about coherence parameters and their relationship to data structures provide important understanding beyond just a new algorithm. The practical algorithm is well-designed and demonstrates clear improvements over the state-of-the-art on both synthetic and real data. The work has potential to impact applications where coherent data structures are prevalent, such as motion segmentation, video analysis, and other computer vision tasks.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it makes a significant contribution to a fundamental problem in machine learning with both theoretical and practical impact. The results are well-supported, the writing is clear, and the contribution is novel and significant. The authors have addressed a gap in the literature that has practical implications, and their approach provides a principled way to handle coherent data that was previously not well-understood.

The main area for improvement would be to provide more detailed comparison with other methods for handling coherent data, and to strengthen the theoretical discussion about the necessity of low-rank dictionaries. However, these are minor points for a paper that otherwise makes a strong contribution to the field. The work is of high quality, original, and significant, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented.
