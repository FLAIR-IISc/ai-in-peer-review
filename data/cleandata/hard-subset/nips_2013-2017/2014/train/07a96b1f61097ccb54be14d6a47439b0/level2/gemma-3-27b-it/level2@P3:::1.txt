This paper addresses the problem of low-rank matrix recovery in the presence of corruptions, specifically focusing on the impact of data coherence on the performance of Robust PCA (RPCA). The core contribution is demonstrating that using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary can mitigate the issues caused by data coherence, which arises naturally with clustering structures. The paper provides a theoretical analysis and proposes an algorithm leveraging RPCA to estimate a dictionary for LRR. Experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world motion sequence data supports the claims.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly identifying a limitation of RPCA and proposing a solution. The connection between data coherence, clustering structures, and the performance degradation of RPCA is clearly explained. The mathematical analysis, particularly Theorem 1, provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed approach. The algorithm proposed is straightforward and builds upon existing methods (RPCA and LRR), making it practical and potentially easy to implement. The experiments demonstrate improved performance compared to RPCA, and the results on motion sequences are particularly compelling. The related work is reasonably comprehensive, citing key papers in the field of robust PCA and low-rank matrix recovery.

However, several aspects could be strengthened. While the paper demonstrates that LRR with a low-rank dictionary *can* avoid the coherence parameter issue, it doesn't fully quantify *how much* improvement is expected in different scenarios. The theoretical analysis focuses heavily on noiseless data; extending the analysis to incorporate noise more realistically would be beneficial. While the paper mentions a connection to Generalized PCA, this connection could be explored in more detail to provide further insight and context. The explanation of why the algorithm improves on RPCA could be made more intuitive; specifically, the advantage conferred by the dictionary learning step isn't fully highlighted beyond stating it addresses coherence. The experiments, while demonstrating improvement, lack a comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art robust PCA methods beyond RPCA itself. Including comparisons to methods specifically designed for handling coherence, if any exist, would strengthen the evaluation. Furthermore, the ablation study on the dictionary learning phase is missing. How much does each step contribute to the final performance of the proposed algorithm?

Arguments for acceptance:
*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to addressing a limitation of RPCA.
*   Clear and well-motivated problem statement.
*   Strong experimental validation on both synthetic and real data.
*   Practical algorithm built upon established methods.

Arguments against acceptance:
*   Theoretical analysis limited to noiseless data.
*   Quantification of improvement over RPCA could be more detailed.
*   Limited comparison to other state-of-the-art robust PCA methods.
*   Missing ablation study.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery. The proposed approach offers a principled way to address the challenges posed by data coherence, and the experimental results are promising. Addressing the weaknesses identified above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. A revision addressing these points is recommended.
