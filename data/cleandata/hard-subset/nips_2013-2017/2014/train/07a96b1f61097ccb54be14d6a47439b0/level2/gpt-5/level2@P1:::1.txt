Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies robust recovery of a low-rank matrix L0 from grossly corrupted observations X = L0 + S0, where S0 is sparse. While RPCA (Candès et al., 2011) provides exact recovery guarantees under incoherence assumptions, the authors argue that these assumptions are violated in common scenarios where data exhibit clustering (union-of-subspaces) structure. They empirically and conceptually link increasing number of clusters to an increase in the “row-space coherence” parameter µ2, which tightens RPCA’s recoverability condition. The main technical contribution shows that Low-Rank Representation (LRR; Liu et al., 2010/2012) can avoid dependence on µ2 if the dictionary A is low-rank and its column space contains that of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA). Under these conditions, the authors prove exact recovery (noiseless) and stability (Gaussian noise) with bounds depending on µ1(A) but not µ2(L0). Motivated by this, they propose a practical two-stage algorithm: (1) run RPCA, truncate the estimate L̂0 to rank r̂0, column-normalize to form a low-rank dictionary Â; (2) run LRR with Â. Experiments on synthetic coherent data and corrupted motion trajectories (Hopkins155 variants) show improved recovery and downstream subspace clustering error compared to RPCA and to clustering without recovery.

Strengths:
- Clear identification and empirical illustration of the “µ2-phenomenon”: clustering increases µ2 and degrades RPCA, consistent with prior theory.
- New recovery guarantees for LRR with a general low-rank dictionary that depends on µ1(A) and not on µ2(L0), clarifying when and why LRR can handle coherent data.
- Practical algorithm requiring only standard convex programs, with sensible design choices (rank truncation, column normalization). The method is simple to adopt.
- Synthetic experiments are extensive and visualize recovery phases; real-data results show meaningful gains in downstream motion segmentation.
- The work connects coherence to data geometry (union-of-subspaces), offering insight beyond technical assumptions.

Weaknesses and questions:
- The key assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong in unsupervised settings. The proposed algorithm uses RPCA to approximate U0; however, the paper does not provide a formal guarantee for Algorithm 1 beyond the trivial “if RPCA is exact then so is the two-stage method.” It would be helpful to quantify robustness when Â only approximately contains U0 (e.g., subspace distance), and how this affects constants in Theorems 1–2.
- Probabilistic assumptions are not fully specified in the statements: theorems provide “with probability” guarantees but do not clearly state the randomness model (e.g., uniformly random support Ω and signs, independence from U0), which are standard in RPCA analyses. Please clarify.
- Sensitivity of the heuristic choices (rank threshold 10−3 σ1, column normalization) is not studied. How do results vary with the truncation threshold, especially when singular spectra decay slowly?
- Constructing Â by normalizing columns of L̃0 may amplify noise in near-zero columns. Are columns with very small norms pruned? A discussion of conditioning of Â and its effect on µ1(Â) would be useful.
- Complexity and runtime: while the paper argues the second stage is cheaper (O(n^2 rA)), two convex programs are solved. Reporting solver details, stopping criteria, and wall-clock comparisons would strengthen the practicality claim.
- Experimental scope: motion is a good testbed, but additional domains where coherence is expected (e.g., illumination subspaces for faces, background subtraction, text/topic data) would better assess generality. Also, direct recovery metrics (‖L̂ − L0‖F/‖L0‖F) on synthetics would complement phase plots and downstream clustering.
- Baselines: It would be informative to compare with (i) LRR with A = X (LRRx) as a recovery method, (ii) structured/variant RPCA methods (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, GoDec/AltProj), and (iii) simple RPCA+PCA without LRR to isolate the benefit of the learned dictionary.

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical results are interesting and technically plausible, but assumptions and randomness models should be stated precisely. The practical algorithm is well-motivated, though its guarantees are not analyzed. Empirical evidence supports claims.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized. The explanation of the µ2-phenomenon is helpful. Please make theorem assumptions explicit and consolidate notation (e.g., define probability spaces).
- Originality: Provides a new angle on coherence in robust recovery and new guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries. The two-stage dictionary-construction strategy is simple yet novel in this context.
- Significance: The work addresses a known limitation of RPCA and offers both theory and a usable algorithm. If broadly validated, the approach could influence robust subspace learning in clustered data.

Specific suggestions for improvement:
- State explicitly the probabilistic model for Ω and S0, and the source of randomness in Theorems 1–2.
- Provide bounds or experiments quantifying performance as a function of subspace angle between U0 and UA.
- Add ablations on the rank threshold and dictionary preprocessing (e.g., column pruning, whitening).
- Report runtimes and solver details; consider releasing code.
- Include additional datasets and direct recovery error metrics.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important and practical limitation of RPCA with a principled and simple method.
- New theoretical insight: LRR with low-rank dictionaries can avoid µ2 dependence.
- Strong synthetic and meaningful real-data improvements.

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong dictionary assumption with limited analysis of approximate cases.
- Missing clarity on randomness assumptions and lack of guarantees for the proposed two-stage algorithm.
- Experimental comparisons could be broader.

Overall assessment:
A thoughtful and promising contribution that combines new theory with a practical algorithm to tackle coherent data in robust low-rank recovery. With clearer assumptions and expanded empirical validation, this paper would be a solid addition to NIPS. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the theory’s probabilistic model and strengthening the empirical section as suggested.
