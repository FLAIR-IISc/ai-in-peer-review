Summary
The paper studies low-rank matrix recovery in the presence of gross corruptions when the low-rank component exhibits clustering structure, leading to high coherence and degraded RPCA performance. The authors identify a “µ2-phenomenon”: as the number of clusters (subspaces) increases while the overall rank stays fixed, the second coherence parameter µ2 grows and RPCA’s guarantees weaken. They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a suitably chosen dictionary A. The main theoretical result shows that if A is itself low-rank and its column space contains that of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), then exact (or near-exact in the noisy case) recovery via LRR can be obtained without any dependence on µ2, with bounds depending on µ1(A) instead. This yields the practical principle: learn a low-rank dictionary aligned with the column space. The authors propose a simple unsupervised algorithm that first runs RPCA to estimate L0, forms a low-rank approximation, normalizes columns to define A, and then runs LRR. Experiments on synthetic data and motion trajectories indicate improvements over RPCA in recovery success regions and downstream subspace clustering error.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on RPCA (Candès et al.) and LRR (Liu et al.). While LRR with A = X is well-studied for subspace clustering, the role of general dictionaries for robust recovery has been less explored. The paper provides new analysis showing how a low-rank dictionary aligned with the underlying subspace mitigates coherence issues that harm RPCA, and empirically demonstrates the benefit.

Quality
- Theory: The noiseless and noisy theorems are well-motivated and, if correct, interesting. They explicitly assume PUA(U0) = U0, column-normalized A, and provide rank and sparsity conditions. The resulting bounds relax the log factor relative to RPCA and remove the µ2 dependence by shifting requirements to µ1(A). This makes precise the intuition that a dictionary aligned with the subspace absorbs row-space coherence caused by clustering.
- Assumptions: The key condition U0 ⊂ UA is necessary for exact recovery and is central. In unsupervised settings this is nontrivial; the algorithm relies on RPCA to provide a good enough estimate. The paper argues RPCA is often “not brittle,” but this is the main circularity risk: when RPCA fails badly, UA may not capture U0 and the theoretical guarantees do not apply. Clarifying the minimal closeness needed (e.g., principal angle bounds) to still benefit would strengthen the contribution.
- Experimental evaluation: Synthetic experiments show a sizable expansion (47%) in the recovery success region using the learned dictionary. The motion segmentation experiment on 11 sequences shows consistent downstream improvements across clustering methods. These are positive, but additional evaluation would increase confidence: (i) varying the number of clusters while keeping total rank fixed to directly demonstrate the µ2-phenomenon’s impact on recovery and its mitigation; (ii) comparisons when corruptions are not i.i.d. Bernoulli ±1 (e.g., heavy-tailed magnitudes, structured corruptions, block occlusions); (iii) robustness to rank misestimation; and (iv) sensitivity to the RPCA stage quality.
- Computational aspects: The claim of ~1.2× RPCA runtime is encouraging, but depends on solvers. A more detailed account of optimization methods and stopping criteria would help reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is generally clearly written, with helpful notation and explanations of coherence. The link between cluster structure and µ2 is well explained at a high level, though the precise conditions for increases in µ2 are deferred. The theorems are stated cleanly, but the submission repeatedly refers to the “full paper” for proofs and some figures; for a conference submission, including proof sketches or supplemental details is important. Algorithm 1 is clearly specified.

Originality
The identification of coherence growth due to clustering as a specific failure mode for RPCA is known qualitatively but is articulated here in terms of µ2 with concrete empirical evidence. The main novelty is the theoretical result that LRR with a low-rank, subspace-containing dictionary avoids dependence on µ2, together with a simple practical approach to learn such a dictionary in an unsupervised setting. This is a meaningful extension of LRR theory and practice.

Significance
The work addresses a relevant and common failure mode for RPCA, offering both theory and a simple algorithm that could be adopted by practitioners using robust low-rank recovery in vision and related domains. The dependence on a good initial estimate is a limitation, but the approach demonstrates improved recovery and downstream clustering. If further validated, the dictionary design principle could influence robust recovery pipelines.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Dependence on RPCA initialization: Provide analysis or empirical studies quantifying how imperfect UA affects performance (e.g., as a function of principal angles between U0 and UA), and whether a second iteration consistently helps.
- Assumptions on S0: Explicitly state the randomness model for Ω and signs/magnitudes in the theorems. If the results require uniform random support, discuss behavior under structured sparsity (e.g., contiguous occlusions common in vision).
- µ1 sensitivity: Since guarantees depend on µ1(A), discuss scenarios with row-wise clustering (large µ1(L0)). How does column normalization and dictionary construction influence µ1(A)? Are there heuristics to control µ1(A)?
- Experimental breadth: 
  - Add experiments varying the number of clusters with fixed rank to directly validate the µ2-phenomenon and the proposed mitigation.
  - Test different corruption types (heavy-tailed, adversarial magnitudes, block sparsity).
  - Evaluate sensitivity to rank thresholding (10−3σ1) and alternative rank estimation methods.
  - Report solver details, convergence criteria, and wall-clock times.
- Related work: Discuss robust subspace recovery alternatives (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, Tyler’s M-estimator-based PCA, nonconvex factorization) in the context of coherent data, and clarify differences in guarantees.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear identification of a practical failure mode for RPCA and an elegant, theoretically justified remedy via LRR with a low-rank dictionary.
- New recovery guarantees that avoid µ2 dependence under interpretable conditions.
- Simple, practical algorithm with promising empirical gains on synthetic and real data.
- Good potential impact for applications where clustering structures are prevalent.

Cons against acceptance
- Key guarantee requires U0 ⊂ UA; the unsupervised method relies on RPCA to satisfy this, with limited analysis of when this holds.
- Experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in breadth and realism of corruptions; reliance on ±1 noise and small number of motion sequences.
- Proofs and some empirical details are deferred to a “full paper,” reducing self-containedness.

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution that advances understanding and practice of robust low-rank recovery for coherent data. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and clarifying assumptions and robustness would further improve the paper.
