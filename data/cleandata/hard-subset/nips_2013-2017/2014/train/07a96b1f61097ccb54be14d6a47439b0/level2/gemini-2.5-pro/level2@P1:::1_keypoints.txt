- Problem: recover a low-rank matrix from observations corrupted by sparse, gross errors; RPCA fails when the low-rank component is "coherent."

- Key insight: links abstract coherence to clustering—when columns lie in multiple subspaces, µ2 grows with cluster count, degrading RPCA.

- Proposed model: use LRR (L = A Z) with a low-rank dictionary A whose column space contains the true column space to avoid dependence on µ2 and µ3.

- Theoretical contribution: Theorems 1–2 show LRR with a suitable low-rank dictionary yields recovery guarantees independent of problematic coherence parameters.

- Practical algorithm: a two-stage, unsupervised procedure—run RPCA for an initial estimate, build a low-rank dictionary from that estimate, then run LRR to refine recovery.

- Empirical validation: synthetic phase-transition plots and motion-segmentation experiments show the method significantly outperforms RPCA, especially on coherent data.

- Strength: clear and important problem formulation connecting theory (coherence) to a concrete data structure (clustering).

- Strength: strong, novel theoretical justification of LRR’s robustness to coherence beyond its subspace-clustering use.

- Strength: simple, intuitive, and easy-to-implement two-stage algorithm motivated by the theory.

- Strength: convincing experiments that quantify improvements and reduce downstream clustering error.

- Weakness: gap between theory and practice—the required UA ⊇ U0 condition may not hold if the initial RPCA estimate is poor; no analysis of a required error threshold for the bootstrap step.

- Weakness: limited scope—focuses on column clustering/µ2 but does not address clustered rows/µ1; should explicitly state this limitation.

- Overall: high-quality paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions, minor limitations, and a strong recommendation for acceptance.
