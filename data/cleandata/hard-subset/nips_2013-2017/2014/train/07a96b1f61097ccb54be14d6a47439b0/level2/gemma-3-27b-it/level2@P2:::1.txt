This paper addresses the problem of low-rank matrix recovery in the presence of corruption, focusing on the impact of data coherence on the performance of Robust PCA (RPCA). The authors argue that RPCA’s performance degrades with increasing coherence, particularly when data exhibits clustering structures. They propose utilizing Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully chosen, low-rank dictionary to mitigate this issue, and provide theoretical justification and an algorithm for dictionary learning in an unsupervised setting.

The paper is well-motivated. The limitations of RPCA regarding data coherence are a valid concern, and exploring how to address them is a valuable contribution. The connection between data clustering, coherence, and RPCA performance is clearly articulated. The theoretical analysis establishing conditions for avoiding coherence parameters in LRR when using a low-rank dictionary is a significant result. The proposed algorithm, leveraging RPCA for initial estimates and then utilizing LRR, seems reasonable and practical. The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world motion sequence data support the claim that the proposed method outperforms RPCA.

However, there are several weaknesses. Firstly, the paper heavily relies on existing work without fully contextualizing it within the broader field of robust matrix completion. While references like [2, 3, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 16, 19, 24, 25] are listed, the specific contributions of each are not clearly explained, and a more comprehensive comparison to related methods (e.g., those based on iterative reweighting, or exploiting different sparsity patterns) is missing. The discussion of the relationship to matrix factorization ([20, 23]) feels superficial.

Secondly, the theoretical analysis, while present, is somewhat dense and difficult to follow without a strong background in matrix theory. The assumptions and conditions for Theorem 1, particularly PUA(U0) = U0, could be more clearly explained and their implications discussed more thoroughly. It is not immediately obvious how practically achievable this condition is, especially in high-dimensional scenarios. While the authors acknowledge the noiseless setting, the noisy case analysis (Theorem 2) could benefit from more justification for the parameter settings and a clearer link to the noise level (ε).

Thirdly, while the experimental results are encouraging, the ablation study is limited. The performance gain is demonstrated, but a more detailed analysis of the impact of different dictionary learning strategies, or the sensitivity to the parameter λ, would strengthen the conclusions. The choice of λ = 1/√n1 seems somewhat arbitrary, and the impact of different values isn't explored.  Furthermore, a comparison against other dictionary learning techniques specifically designed for low-rank representation would be valuable.

Finally, the writing style, while generally clear, suffers from some redundancy and a tendency to restate ideas without adding new information. The introduction is particularly lengthy and could be more concise.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The problem addressed is relevant and important: improving the robustness of low-rank matrix recovery is a fundamental challenge.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a novel insight into the relationship between data coherence, dictionary learning, and LRR performance.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a practical improvement over RPCA.
*   The proposed algorithm is conceptually simple and potentially scalable.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to the broader landscape of robust matrix completion techniques.
*   The theoretical analysis is dense and requires further clarification of assumptions and their practical implications.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more thorough, with a better ablation study and comparison to alternative dictionary learning methods.
*   The writing quality could be improved through greater conciseness and reduction of redundancy.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field, but it requires revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. The core idea is sound, and the theoretical results are interesting. However, a more thorough contextualization within the existing literature, a clearer explanation of the theoretical assumptions, and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation are necessary to fully justify acceptance. I recommend a **weak accept** contingent on significant revisions addressing the identified shortcomings.
