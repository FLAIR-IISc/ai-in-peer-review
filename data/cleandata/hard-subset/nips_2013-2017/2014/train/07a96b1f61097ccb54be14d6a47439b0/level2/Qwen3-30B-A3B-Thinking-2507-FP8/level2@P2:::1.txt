

This paper addresses a limitation of RPCA (Robust Principal Component Analysis) when dealing with "coherent data" - data with high coherence parameters that arise from clustering structures in the data. The authors propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a properly configured low-rank dictionary to handle such coherent data, providing theoretical guarantees and an unsupervised algorithm for dictionary construction.

The paper's main contributions are: (1) identifying the connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters that degrade RPCA's performance, (2) proving that LRR with a low-rank dictionary can avoid these coherence parameters, and (3) proposing an unsupervised algorithm that constructs a dictionary from RPCA's output.

The theoretical contributions (Theorems 1 and 2) are generally well-justified, though the conditions for exact recovery are quite restrictive. The requirement that "U0 is a subspace of UA" (where U0 is the column space of the true low-rank matrix L0 and UA is the column space of the dictionary A) is strong and may be difficult to satisfy in practice. The authors' unsupervised algorithm (Algorithm 1) attempts to address this by using RPCA to construct the dictionary, but it's not clear how this guarantees the subspace condition. The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of how well Algorithm 1 satisfies this condition in practice.

The experimental section provides evidence that their method outperforms RPCA on both synthetic data (Figure 4) and motion sequences (Table 1). However, the paper doesn't compare to other relevant methods that might handle coherent data better, such as LRR with different dictionary constructions. For example, in Table 1, they compare to RPCA but don't include results for LRR with A=X (LRRx), which is mentioned in the paper as a baseline. This comparison would strengthen their claims about the superiority of their dictionary learning approach.

The paper's claim that "the success area of our algorithm is 47% wider than that of RPCA" is somewhat vague. The paper doesn't clearly define what "success area" means or how it was calculated. A more detailed explanation of this metric would improve the paper's clarity.

The paper provides valuable insights about the physical meaning of coherence parameters, showing they are not just "assumptions" for proofs but relate to intrinsic data structures. This is a significant contribution to the understanding of RPCA's limitations.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical notation and good experimental design. The connection to prior work is generally good, though the paper could better contextualize how their approach differs from the LRR work in [13], which already discusses the general dictionary case.

The significance of the paper is moderate. While the problem of coherent data is important, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this limitation is widespread in real-world applications. The motion sequence experiments are promising, but more examples would strengthen the case for practical significance.

Strengths:
- Clear identification of a limitation of RPCA with coherent data
- Theoretical insights about the relationship between coherence parameters and clustering structures
- Practical unsupervised algorithm for dictionary construction
- Good experimental validation on both synthetic and real data

Weaknesses:
- Theoretical conditions for exact recovery are restrictive and not well-justified in practice
- Lack of comparison to relevant methods (e.g., LRR with different dictionaries)
- Vague claim about "success area" widening by 47%
- Unclear how the unsupervised algorithm actually satisfies the key subspace condition

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding the limitations of RPCA and proposes a promising approach to handle coherent data. The theoretical insights are interesting, and the experimental results support the main claims. However, the practical applicability of the theoretical conditions needs further clarification, and the paper would be stronger with more comprehensive comparisons to relevant methods. The authors should address these issues in a revision to strengthen their claims and demonstrate the broader significance of their work.
