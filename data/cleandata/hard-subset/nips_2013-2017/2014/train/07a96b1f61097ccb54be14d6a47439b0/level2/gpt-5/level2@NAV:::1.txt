Summary and relation to prior work: The paper addresses a known limitation of RPCA [4]: its reliance on incoherence, which can be violated when low-rank data exhibit cluster structure (union-of-subspaces), inflating µ2 and degrading recovery. The authors show that Low-Rank Representation (LRR) [13] with a properly chosen dictionary A can avoid dependence on µ2, and provide theorems (noiseless/noisy) establishing exact/near recovery when A is low-rank and its column space contains that of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA). They propose a practical two-stage pipeline—RPCA to estimate L0, then LRR using a column-normalized, low-rank dictionary built from the RPCA estimate—and demonstrate gains on synthetic data and corrupted motion trajectories, improving downstream subspace clustering (SIM, SSC, LRRx). This builds on extensive literature in robust low-rank modeling and subspace segmentation (e.g., [4, 6, 13, 14]) and is relevant to NeurIPS work on low-rank learning and robust PCA (e.g., [19, 23]).

Strengths:
- Clear identification and empirical illustration of the “µ2-phenomenon” linking clustering to coherence growth, clarifying when RPCA underperforms.
- Theoretical contribution: LRR with low-rank A removes µ2 from recovery conditions, depending only on µ1(A), under U0 ⊂ UA. The noisy result is practical.
- Simple, unsupervised algorithm with consistent parameterization (λ = 1/√n1) and tangible empirical benefits, including on real motion sequences.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- The key assumption U0 ⊂ UA is an oracle condition; the unsupervised construction of A via RPCA lacks a corresponding guarantee. Provide analysis showing conditions under which the RPCA-derived A satisfies U0 ⊂ UA with high probability, or quantify degradation when it is approximate (e.g., principal angle bounds).
- Sensitivity and completeness: the rank-thresholding heuristic (10−3σ1) and column normalization may affect performance. Report sensitivity studies; consider adaptive rank selection or cross-validation.
- Empirics: include ablations isolating the effect of dictionary low-rankness (e.g., random vs RPCA-derived vs ground-truth A), and quantify µ1(A), µ2(L0), and recovery error vs number of clusters. Compare against additional robust recovery baselines (e.g., Outlier Pursuit [24]) and factorization-based methods; report runtime and scalability.
- Clarify the scope: discuss failure modes when rows are clustered (large µ1), as noted; consider extensions or experiments for that regime.

Pros for acceptance:
- Timely, well-motivated problem; crisp theoretical insight; practical method with measurable gains.

Cons:
- Reliance on a strong subspace-inclusion condition without guarantees for the learned dictionary; limited baseline breadth and sensitivity analysis.

Overall, this is a constructive advance; with stronger empirical breadth and guarantees for the unsupervised dictionary, it would be very compelling.
