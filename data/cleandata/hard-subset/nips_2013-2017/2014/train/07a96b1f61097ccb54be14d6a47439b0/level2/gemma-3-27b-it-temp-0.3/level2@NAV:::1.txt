## Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper addresses the challenge of low-rank matrix recovery when the underlying low-rank matrix exhibits coherence, a phenomenon that degrades the performance of Robust PCA (RPCA). The authors argue that coherence arises from clustering structures within the data and propose a Low-Rank Representation (LRR) approach with a carefully designed dictionary to mitigate this issue. They theoretically demonstrate that a low-rank dictionary renders LRR immune to coherence and present an algorithm to learn such a dictionary in an unsupervised manner, validated through experiments on synthetic and real motion sequence data.

The core contribution lies in connecting the coherence parameter to underlying data structure (clustering) and providing a theoretical justification for using a low-rank dictionary within the LRR framework. This is a valuable insight, as it moves beyond simply treating coherence as a nuisance parameter. The theoretical analysis, while concise as is typical for conference papers, appears sound and the proof sketch provided suggests a rigorous approach. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the improvement of their proposed algorithm over RPCA, particularly in scenarios with significant clustering. The use of motion sequence data adds practical relevance to the work.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The discussion of the “µ2-phenomenon” feels somewhat hand-wavy without a more detailed explanation of *why* µ2 specifically increases with cluster number. While Figure 2 illustrates this, a more formal justification would strengthen the argument. Furthermore, the unsupervised dictionary learning algorithm is presented as a heuristic, and a more detailed analysis of its convergence properties or sensitivity to initialization would be beneficial. The connection to existing matrix factorization techniques, while mentioned, could be explored more deeply. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel theoretical connection between coherence and data clustering.
*   Clear demonstration of improved performance over RPCA.
*   Addresses a practical limitation of RPCA in real-world applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited theoretical depth in explaining the µ2-phenomenon.
*   Heuristic nature of the unsupervised dictionary learning algorithm.
*   Could benefit from a more thorough comparison to related work in dictionary learning.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery. While some aspects could be further refined, the core idea is novel, well-motivated, and supported by experimental evidence. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more detailed explanation of the µ2-phenomenon and a discussion of the algorithm’s limitations. The authors should also consider expanding the related work section to include more recent advances in dictionary learning.
