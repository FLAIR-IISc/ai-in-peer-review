This paper addresses a notable limitation of the widely-used Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) method for recovering a low-rank matrix from grossly corrupted observations. The authors' central claim is that RPCA's performance degrades on "coherent" data, a situation that arises naturally when data exhibits clustering structures (e.g., points drawn from a union of subspaces). They demonstrate that as the number of clusters increases, a key coherence parameter grows, hindering RPCA's recovery guarantees. To overcome this, the paper leverages the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework. The main theoretical contribution is proving that if LRR is equipped with a low-rank dictionary whose column space contains that of the true data, its recovery guarantee becomes immune to this problematic coherence parameter. This work builds directly upon foundational literature in RPCA and LRR, providing a novel analysis that connects these methods and clarifies the physical meaning of coherence.

The paper's primary strength lies in its clear identification and rigorous treatment of a practical failure mode for RPCA. The concept of the "µ2-phenomenon" is an important insight, and the theoretical result provides a strong justification for the proposed approach. The empirical evaluation is also a significant strength; experiments on both synthetic data and real-world motion sequences show that the proposed algorithm provides a substantial performance improvement over standard RPCA, validating the paper's claims. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and technically sound.

The main weakness lies in the heuristic nature of the proposed unsupervised algorithm. The two-step procedure—using RPCA to generate an initial estimate for constructing the LRR dictionary—is sensible, but its success is fundamentally dependent on the initial RPCA step providing a reasonably good recovery. The paper could be strengthened by discussing the failure modes of this approach. For example, under what conditions of corruption or coherence does the initial RPCA estimate become too poor to yield a useful dictionary? Furthermore, the rank estimation in the algorithm relies on a simple threshold, and a discussion of the method's sensitivity to this hyperparameter would be beneficial.

Despite this, the paper makes a significant contribution by identifying a key challenge for RPCA and proposing a well-justified, effective solution. The strengths—a novel theoretical insight into a significant problem, backed by strong empirical results—clearly outweigh the weaknesses. This work advances our understanding of robust low-rank recovery and is a valuable contribution to the field.
