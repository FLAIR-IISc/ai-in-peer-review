

This paper addresses a significant limitation of RPCA (Robust Principal Component Analysis) when dealing with coherent data, which arises naturally from clustering structures in real-world applications. The authors make the compelling claim that RPCA's performance degrades as data coherence increases (due to more clusters), and that Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with an appropriately configured low-rank dictionary can overcome this limitation. The paper's main contributions include a theoretical proof showing LRR with low-rank dictionaries avoids coherence parameters, a practical unsupervised algorithm for dictionary learning, and experimental validation on synthetic and motion data.

The paper excels in theoretical rigor, providing solid proofs (Theorems 1 and 2) that establish the conditions under which LRR can handle coherent data. The authors carefully relate coherence parameters to clustering structures, offering a new perspective on a fundamental concept in low-rank recovery. The experimental section demonstrates clear improvements over RPCA in both synthetic data (47% wider success area) and real motion sequences (40% relative improvement in clustering error rates), with reasonable computational complexity (only 1.2x RPCA's runtime).

The work shows strong significance as it addresses a genuine limitation of a widely used method (RPCA) that appears in many applications. The authors correctly contextualize their work within the RPCA literature and properly cite relevant prior work, demonstrating good field knowledge. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

A minor limitation is that the unsupervised dictionary learning algorithm relies on RPCA's output, which may not be perfect when data is highly coherent. However, the authors acknowledge this and note their method improves upon RPCA's success probability. The paper could benefit from more discussion about the relationship between dictionary rank and performance, and how the proposed algorithm scales to extremely large datasets.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to low-rank matrix recovery. The theoretical insights are valuable, the experimental validation is convincing, and the proposed algorithm is practical. The work clearly advances the state of the art in handling coherent data, which is a common challenge in real-world applications. I recommend acceptance.
