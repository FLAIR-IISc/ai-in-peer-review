Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles robust recovery of a low-rank matrix L0 from X = L0 + S0 where S0 is sparse with arbitrary magnitude, a setting classically addressed by Robust PCA (RPCA/PCP), which solves min ||L||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = L + S. The authors argue that RPCA’s guarantees rely on incoherence of L0 (captured by coherence parameters μ1, μ2, μ3), and that in many real datasets with clustering/union-of-subspaces structure, the second coherence μ2 increases with the number of clusters (“μ2-phenomenon”), hurting RPCA.

They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR): min ||Z||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = AZ + S, where A is a dictionary chosen a priori. Main theoretical claim: if A is low-rank and its column space contains the column space of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), then exact/near recovery is possible without invoking μ2 (only μ1(A) appears). Theorems are given for noiseless and noisy settings with corruption fractions up to 0.5/0.35 and λ = 1/√n1.

For unsupervised use, they propose a practical two-stage algorithm: first run RPCA to estimate L0, truncate its SVD to estimate rank and produce a well-conditioned low-rank approximation L̃0, construct A by normalizing columns of L̃0, then solve LRR to obtain the final estimate. Experiments on synthetic clustered data and corrupted motion trajectory matrices (Hopkins) show improvements over RPCA as a preprocessing step for subspace clustering (SIM, LRR with A=X, SSC).

This work builds on and extends prior LRR papers (Liu et al.), PCP (Candès et al.), and subspace clustering. The novelty here is the focus on “coherent” data due to clustering, a theoretical condition under which a suitably chosen low-rank dictionary A avoids μ2, and a simple unsupervised recipe to learn A from RPCA’s output.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important failure mode of RPCA (coherent/clustered data) and articulates it clearly via the μ2-phenomenon.
- Provides recovery theorems for LRR with a general dictionary A that depend on μ1(A) but not on μ2(L0), relaxing the rank bound from O(n2/log^2 n1) (PCP) to O(n2/log n1) under the subspace inclusion assumption.
- Simple, practical algorithm leveraging RPCA to build a low-rank dictionary; plausible in many applications.
- Empirical results indicate nontrivial gains over RPCA on both synthetic clustered data and motion segmentation, with clear downstream benefits for clustering.
- Conceptually clarifies the role of coherence and connects LRR to matrix factorization intuitions.

Weaknesses and limitations
- The key theoretical assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong and supervised; the unsupervised algorithm does not have guarantees. If RPCA is poor (e.g., in highly coherent regimes), the learned A may not satisfy the inclusion, undermining benefits.
- No theory for approximate inclusion (principal angle bounds) or for the end-to-end Algorithm 1, despite the centrality of this unsupervised scenario.
- The experimental evaluation is limited: comparisons focus on RPCA; missing comparisons to other robust PCA variants tailored to coherence (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, trimmed/structured RPCA, nonconvex surrogates, weighted PCP), or to dictionary-based/feature-augmented RPCA.
- The motion data protocol replaces missing entries with random ±1 noise, which is not reflective of realistic missingness; recovery quality is not reported directly (e.g., reconstruction error, support recovery), only downstream clustering error.
- Several details are left to a “full paper,” including the derivation of the μ2-phenomenon; for NeurIPS, the submission should be self-contained with proofs in the supplement.
- Some notation/typos and clarity issues (e.g., “PRCA” typo, “column-wisely,” parameter choices), and dependence on figures/tables that are not visible here.

Quality
- The theoretical results are plausible and technically nontrivial; avoiding μ2 by constraining A to be low-rank and to span U0 is insightful. However, the strongest practical claim—improved recovery in unsupervised settings—is not theoretically supported. Bounds in the noisy case scale with √(mn)ε and seem loose; a discussion of tightness would help.
- Experiments are positive but not exhaustive; runtime claims would benefit from wall-clock numbers; ablations (rank threshold, normalization, dictionary rank) and sensitivity to λ are missing.

Clarity
- Generally well written, with a good narrative around coherence and clustering. However, some definitions and claims are deferred. The algorithm is clear, but parameter choices (rank threshold 10^-3σ1, λ = 1/√n1 regardless of A) need justification. Improve language and fix typos.

Originality
- The angle of handling coherence via a learned low-rank dictionary within LRR and the analysis avoiding μ2 is novel relative to PCP and prior LRR work emphasizing A=X. The unsupervised two-stage recipe is simple but practically meaningful.

Significance
- If broadly validated, the approach could impact robust low-rank modeling in computer vision and beyond, where union-of-subspaces is common. The theoretical insights about coherence are valuable. Current empirical scope limits claims, but the direction is promising.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Provide a theorem for approximate subspace inclusion: if principal angles between U0 and UA are small, quantify degradation in recovery. This would connect theory to the unsupervised algorithm.
- Analyze Algorithm 1: under conditions where RPCA yields an approximation with error δ and subspace distance θ, show conditions for LRR to improve or at least not worsen recovery.
- Expand experiments: compare to Outlier Pursuit and other robust PCA variants; include real missing data handling (without random ±1 imputation); report recovery metrics (||L̂−L0||F, support precision/recall) in addition to clustering error; provide runtime comparisons.
- Study sensitivity to λ and to the SVD truncation threshold; consider data-driven selection.
- Clarify the μ2-phenomenon with a lemma relating μ2 to the number of clusters and sample allocation; include in the supplement if space is limited.
- Discuss how to construct A when clean training data are partially available; connections to supervised/semi-supervised settings.
- Improve writing and fix typos; ensure the submission is self-contained with proofs and accessible figures.

Pros
- Clear problem motivation; insightful theoretical angle
- Practical, simple algorithm with encouraging gains
- Bridges coherence intuition with LRR design

Cons
- Strong assumptions in theory; no guarantees for the main unsupervised use case
- Limited comparisons and experimental breadth
- Some clarity and completeness issues

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a useful conceptual and theoretical contribution and a practical method with promising results. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and adding theory for approximate inclusion would make it a clear accept.
