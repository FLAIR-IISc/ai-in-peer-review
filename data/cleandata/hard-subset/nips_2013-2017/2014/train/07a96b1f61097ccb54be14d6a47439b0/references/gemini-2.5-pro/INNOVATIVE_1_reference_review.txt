### Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

**Summary**

This paper addresses a key limitation of the widely-used Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) method for decomposing a data matrix `X` into a low-rank component `L0` and a sparse component `S0`. The authors astutely observe that the theoretical guarantees for RPCA rely on the "incoherence" of the low-rank component `L0`. They argue, and convincingly demonstrate, that common data structures, such as the presence of multiple clusters, naturally lead to "coherent" data. This coherence, specifically through what they term the "µ2-phenomenon," degrades the performance of RPCA.

The central contribution is a novel approach to overcome this limitation. Instead of modifying RPCA, the authors propose leveraging the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework, `X = AZ + S`, where the low-rank component is modeled as `AZ`. Their key theoretical insight is that if the dictionary `A` is itself low-rank and its column space contains the column space of the true `L0`, the recovery conditions for LRR no longer depend on the problematic coherence parameters (`µ2`, `µ3`). This provides a principled way to handle coherent data. To make this operational in an unsupervised setting, they propose a practical and elegant two-stage algorithm: first, run standard RPCA to get an initial estimate of `L0`, and second, use this estimate to construct a dictionary `A` for a subsequent LRR step to refine the recovery. The method is supported by theoretical recovery guarantees under ideal dictionary conditions and validated by strong empirical results on both synthetic and real-world motion segmentation data.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** The paper's primary strength lies in its novel conceptual contribution. It moves beyond simply proposing an incremental improvement to an existing algorithm. Instead, it identifies a fundamental weakness in a cornerstone method (RPCA), provides a clear and intuitive physical explanation for when this weakness will manifest (clustering structures leading to coherence), and proposes a new direction for solving it. The link established between the mathematical concept of coherence and the practical reality of clustered data is a significant insight for the community.

2.  **Bold and Principled Approach:** The decision to switch from the RPCA framework (`L`) to LRR (`AZ`) is bold and well-justified. The theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) provides a clear rationale for this choice, demonstrating that a properly constructed dictionary can provably circumvent the coherence issue. This elevates the work from a simple heuristic to a theoretically grounded method.

3.  **Elegant and Practical Algorithm:** The proposed two-stage algorithm is simple, intuitive, and effective. The idea of using the output of a standard (though potentially imperfect) method to bootstrap a more powerful, tailored one is clever. It provides a practical solution to the otherwise challenging problem of constructing a suitable dictionary in an unsupervised setting.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively support the paper's claims. The phase transition plot (Figure 4) provides a clear and compelling visualization of the proposed algorithm's superior performance over RPCA in precisely the regime it was designed for. The success on the corrupted motion sequence task further demonstrates its practical utility and its ability to improve downstream tasks like subspace clustering.

**Weaknesses**

While this is a strong paper, there are a few points that could be further clarified or strengthened. These are minor and do not detract from the core contribution.

1.  **Dependence on Initial RPCA Performance:** The success of the practical unsupervised algorithm hinges on the initial RPCA step providing a reasonably good estimate of the low-rank subspace. The paper acknowledges that RPCA's performance is depressed for coherent data but still usable. However, a more detailed discussion of the failure modes would be beneficial. For instance, if the data is so coherent that the initial RPCA estimate is very poor, could the second LRR step potentially degrade the solution further? While the empirical results suggest this is not a major issue, the robustness of this bootstrapping approach could be explored more deeply.

2.  **Gap between Theory and Practice:** The theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1 and 2) rely on the crucial assumption that the true low-rank column space is a subspace of the dictionary's column space (`U0 ⊂ UA`). In the proposed algorithm, `A` is constructed from an *estimate* of `L0`, so this condition is only met approximately. The paper would be even stronger if it could provide some theoretical analysis that bridges this gap, perhaps by showing that if the initial estimate is within some error bound, the final estimate is guaranteed to be an improvement. As it stands, the leap from the ideal theory to the heuristic algorithm is justified primarily by the excellent empirical results.

3.  **Dictionary Construction:** The method for constructing the dictionary—by rank-truncating and column-normalizing the initial RPCA estimate—is a sensible heuristic. However, it is one of several possibilities. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the algorithm to this specific construction method or potential alternatives could add more depth.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper presents a novel, significant, and well-executed piece of research. It tackles an important and subtle problem in robust low-rank matrix recovery, offering a fresh perspective and a new direction for research. The core idea is bold and the connection it makes between coherence and data structure is a valuable contribution in itself. While the practical algorithm has a heuristic element, it is well-motivated by a sound theoretical principle and backed by convincing experiments. The weaknesses identified are minor and represent avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws. This paper is a perfect fit for NIPS, prioritizing a powerful new idea over incremental methodological refinement.
