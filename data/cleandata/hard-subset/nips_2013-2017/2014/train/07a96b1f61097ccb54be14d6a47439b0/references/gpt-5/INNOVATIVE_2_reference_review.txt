Summary
The paper tackles robust recovery of low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse gross errors when the low-rank component is coherent (e.g., exhibits clustering/union-of-subspaces structure). It argues and empirically illustrates a “µ2-phenomenon”: as the number of underlying clusters grows (with fixed total rank), the second coherence parameter µ2 increases, degrading RPCA guarantees and practice. The main technical contribution is a recovery theory for Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a suitably chosen dictionary A. If A is itself low-rank and its column space contains the true subspace U0, the authors prove exact (noiseless) and stable (noisy) recovery without dependence on µ2 (only µ1(A) appears). This suggests a way to “avoid coherence” by moving it into a learned dictionary. Building on that, they propose a simple unsupervised procedure: run RPCA once to estimate L0, normalize its columns to form a low-rank dictionary A, and then solve LRR to get a refined recovery. On synthetic coherent data and corrupted motion trajectories, this approach empirically improves over RPCA, and consequently improves downstream subspace clustering error.

Relation to prior work
- RPCA (Candès et al.) establishes guarantees under incoherence involving µ1, µ2, µ3; it can falter on clustered data.
- LRR (Liu et al.) is well known, but most theory and practice consider A = X. This work studies general dictionaries and proves guarantees specifically tailored to coherent L0, with the aim of removing µ2-dependence.
- Outlier Pursuit and other robust PCA variants handle different corruption models (e.g., column-sparse outliers) but do not directly address the coherence-vs-rank tradeoff driven by clustering.
- The paper situates coherence as a proxy for latent structure (e.g., union of subspaces), and connects LRR to matrix factorization viewpoints.

Strengths
- Novel direction: Focuses on coherent data recovery and explicitly analyzes how clustering inflates µ2, which is both practically relevant and under-explored in RPCA theory.
- Clean theoretical message: If A is low-rank and spans U0, exact and stable recovery are possible without µ2. This reframes the challenge as dictionary design rather than fighting coherence head-on.
- Simple, pragmatic algorithm: RPCA → column-normalized, low-rank dictionary → LRR. Minimal tuning (λ = 1/√n1) and modest extra cost.
- Empirical gains: Clear improvement over RPCA on synthetic coherent setups and in motion segmentation via improved matrix recovery leading to lower clustering error.
- Conceptual clarity: Helps interpret coherence parameters as measures of latent structure rather than mere technical assumptions.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumption gap: The theorems require PUA(U0) = U0 (dictionary spans the true subspace). The unsupervised construction relies on RPCA to supply a good enough estimate; yet the setup of interest is precisely where RPCA is stressed by coherence. A more explicit analysis linking RPCA’s (imperfect) subspace estimate to the success of the subsequent LRR stage would strengthen the contribution.
- Shifted coherence: The bound depends on µ1(A). While µ2(L0) is removed, it would help to discuss when µ1(A) remains benign under the proposed construction and how sensitive results are to dictionary conditioning.
- Experimental scope: 
  - Synthetic experiments appear limited to one family of distributions; it would be valuable to systematically vary the number of clusters at fixed total rank to empirically validate the “µ2-phenomenon” and the claimed immunity of LRR with learned A.
  - The motion experiments are on 11 sequences with artificially imputed ±1 entries; this is small and somewhat stylized. Comparisons on standard robust PCA benchmarks (e.g., video background subtraction, dynamic MRI) and ablations (dictionary rank, number of clusters, corruption level) would improve significance.
  - Baselines: Consider RPCA variants or methods explicitly designed for coherence/union-of-subspaces (e.g., LRR with A = X, weighted/truncated PCP, Outlier Pursuit under relevant corruption models), with careful tuning. LRRx is included for clustering, but not as a recovery baseline.
- Clarity and polish:
  - Some notation/footnote issues and typos; references are inconsistently formatted.
  - Details on optimization (algorithms, stopping criteria, time/memory) are sparse; the claim of 1.2× RPCA runtime would benefit from hardware specs and complexity details.
  - Sensitivity to the threshold 10−3σ1 for rank estimation and to λ would be useful.
- Generality: The theory assumes elementwise sparse corruption; commentary on column-sparse or mixed corruptions would broaden impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a perturbation analysis: If RPCA recovers a subspace within angle θ from U0, give conditions under which the LRR stage succeeds; this would operationalize the pipeline’s advantage.
- Empirically probe dictionary quality: vary the rank truncation, column normalization, and try an iterative RPCA↔LRR refinement; report stability and gains.
- Expand experiments: larger synthetic sweeps (clusters, rank, sparsity, noise), real datasets with natural corruptions, and additional baselines; include wall-clock comparisons.
- Clarify the role of µ3 and formally connect its behavior to µ2 under clustering, possibly via a lemma in the supplement.
- Improve exposition and references; provide code and reproducibility details.

Evaluation
- Quality: Good theoretical advance with some gaps in linking assumptions to the unsupervised construction; empirical evidence promising but limited.
- Clarity: Generally clear, but needs polishing and more experimental/algorithmic detail.
- Originality: Strong; reframes robust recovery under coherence via low-rank dictionaries and provides new guarantees for LRR with general A.
- Significance: Meaningful for robust low-rank modeling and subspace learning; likely to stimulate follow-up on dictionary design for coherent data.

Recommendation: Accept (leaning). The paper proposes a bold and novel direction—escaping coherence via learned low-rank dictionaries—with nontrivial theory and a simple, practical algorithm that improves over RPCA. Despite experimental scope and assumption-link gaps, the conceptual contribution is significant and timely.

Confidence: 3/5
