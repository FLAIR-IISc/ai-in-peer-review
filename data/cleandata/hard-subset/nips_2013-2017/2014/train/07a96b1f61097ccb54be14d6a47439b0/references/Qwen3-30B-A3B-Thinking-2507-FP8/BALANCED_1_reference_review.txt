

# Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper addresses an important limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) when dealing with coherent data (data exhibiting clustering structures), which increases coherence parameters and degrades RPCA's performance. The authors propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary to overcome this limitation, providing theoretical guarantees and an unsupervised algorithm that outperforms RPCA in experiments.

**Summary and Relation to Prior Work:** The paper builds upon RPCA [4] and LRR [13], identifying a specific gap in RPCA's applicability when data has clustering structures that increase coherence parameters (particularly μ2). While RPCA assumes incoherence, the authors demonstrate that clustering structures naturally increase coherence, making RPCA less effective. They extend LRR beyond the special case A=X to show that a low-rank dictionary satisfying U0 ⊂ UA can avoid coherence parameters, providing theoretical guarantees and a practical algorithm for dictionary learning.

**Strengths:**
1. **Novel Insight:** The paper provides a crucial connection between coherence parameters (widely used in RPCA theory) and real-world data structures (clustering), revealing coherence is not merely a theoretical assumption but reflects intrinsic data properties. This is a significant conceptual contribution.

2. **Theoretical Contribution:** The authors prove that LRR with a low-rank dictionary can avoid coherence parameters (Theorem 1), showing a looser rank bound (O(n2/log n1)) compared to RPCA's O(n2/(log n1)2). The theoretical analysis is sound and well-motivated.

3. **Practical Algorithm:** Algorithm 1 is simple, effective, and computationally efficient (only ~1.2x RPCA's runtime), providing a practical solution for handling coherent data without supervision. The unsupervised dictionary learning approach is well-motivated.

4. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments on both synthetic data (showing 47% wider success area) and motion sequences (40% relative improvement in clustering error) convincingly demonstrate the method's superiority over RPCA.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Theoretical Scope:** The theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1-2) require P_UA(U0)=U0, which is challenging to achieve in practice without supervision. The paper doesn't adequately address how sensitive the method is to deviations from this condition or how well the dictionary learning in Algorithm 1 approximates this requirement.

2. **Comparison with Alternative Methods:** The experiments only compare with RPCA, not with other robust PCA methods like Outlier Pursuit [25] or Robust PCA via Outlier Pursuit [24]. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the significance claim.

3. **Practical Dictionary Quality:** The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze how the quality of the dictionary (learned in Step 4 of Algorithm 1) affects performance. In particular, the paper should discuss how the rank estimation (Step 2) impacts the dictionary quality and subsequent recovery.

4. **Scope of Coherence Problem:** While clustering structures are well-demonstrated as increasing coherence, the paper doesn't establish how widespread this problem is across real-world applications. The motion sequence experiment is valuable, but more diverse datasets would better demonstrate the method's general applicability.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical results and well-designed experiments. The claims are well-supported, though the practical implementation details could be more thoroughly addressed.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with consistent notation and logical flow. The theoretical sections are dense but appropriate for the venue. Minor improvements could be made to the presentation of Theorem 1's assumptions.

**Originality:** The connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters is novel and insightful. The extension of LRR to handle coherent data via low-rank dictionary learning represents a meaningful contribution beyond the existing LRR literature.

**Significance:** The work addresses a practical limitation of a widely-used method (RPCA) that is relevant to many applications. The insights about coherence parameters advance our understanding of low-rank recovery methods. The proposed algorithm offers a simple yet effective improvement that could be adopted in practice.

**Recommendation:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the robust matrix recovery literature by identifying and addressing a previously overlooked limitation of RPCA. The theoretical insights and practical algorithm are well-motivated and demonstrated through thorough experiments. While the theoretical guarantees require the dictionary to satisfy specific conditions that may be challenging to achieve in practice, the authors provide a reasonable unsupervised approach to approximate these conditions. The paper makes a strong case for the importance of considering data structures beyond low-rankness in robust recovery methods.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Expand the experiments to compare with other robust PCA methods (e.g., [24, 25]) to better establish the method's relative advantages.
2. Provide a more detailed analysis of how the dictionary learning in Algorithm 1 approximates the theoretical requirement U0 ⊂ UA.
3. Include additional real-world datasets beyond motion sequences to demonstrate broader applicability.
4. Discuss how the method might be extended to handle more complex data structures beyond clustering.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to understanding and improving robust low-rank recovery methods. With minor improvements to address the theoretical-practical gap and expand the experimental comparisons, it would be an excellent fit for NeurIPS. I recommend acceptance.
