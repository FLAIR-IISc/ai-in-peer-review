Summary
The paper addresses a well-known fragility of RPCA/PCP: its recovery guarantees degrade when the low-rank component is coherent, a situation that naturally arises when data lie in a union of subspaces (clustering in the row space). The authors (i) articulate and empirically illustrate a “µ2-phenomenon,” where the second coherence parameter grows with the number of clusters, hurting RPCA; (ii) show theoretically that Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a suitably chosen low-rank dictionary A can avoid dependence on µ2 (and µ3), yielding recovery guarantees that depend only on µ1(A); (iii) propose a simple unsupervised procedure that bootstraps a low-rank dictionary from RPCA’s estimate of L0 and then solves LRR; and (iv) demonstrate improved recovery on synthetic union-of-subspace data and on corrupted motion sequences where improved recovery translates into lower clustering error.

Relation to prior work
RPCA/PCP [Candès et al.] requires incoherence; LRR [Liu et al.] with A = X has been used for subspace clustering but theory for general dictionaries A has been limited. This paper’s main novelty is a recovery analysis for LRR with general low-rank dictionaries showing immunity to µ2, together with a practical way to learn such a dictionary in an unsupervised setting. The work connects coherence to intrinsic clustering structure (union-of-subspaces), clarifying when PCP’s conditions fail and why a low-rank dictionary can help. This provides a principled bridge between robust recovery and subspace segmentation literatures.

Strengths
- Novel conceptual angle: reframes “coherence” as an intrinsic property of clustered data and targets the specific µ2 bottleneck. This is both insightful and practically relevant.
- Theory: Theorems for noiseless and noisy settings show exact/near recovery via LRR with low-rank A under PUA(U0) = U0 and bounds that avoid µ2(L0). The dependence on µ1(A) and the relaxed rank bound O(n2/log n1) versus PCP’s O(n2/(log n1)^2) are appealing.
- Simple, pragmatic algorithm: RPCA → rank trimming → column-normalized dictionary → LRR. Minimal tuning (λ ≈ 1/√n1), easily reproducible, and computationally modest in practice.
- Empirical evidence: On synthetic union-of-subspace data, the success region expands substantially (47% by area). On motion segmentation with missing/corrupted entries, improved recovery yields sizable clustering error reductions across SIM/LRRx/SSC.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Assumption strength/realism: Exact recovery needs U0 ⊂ UA and a low-rank A. In the unsupervised procedure, A is constructed from RPCA’s estimate; if RPCA’s estimate is poor (precisely the regime of high µ2), the assumption may fail. The paper argues PCP is “not brittle” but more quantitative analysis is needed: how accurate must RPCA’s subspace be for LRR to succeed? An error-to-success transfer bound or an analysis of the proposed two-step procedure would strengthen the contribution.
- Algorithmic details: Solvers, stopping criteria, and complexity are only sketched. A more precise runtime comparison to PCP across matrix sizes/ranks would help, as would sensitivity to the conditioning and rank of A, to column normalization, and to λ when A is not exactly unit-norm.
- Experimental breadth: Comparisons are limited to PCP baselines. Please add: Outlier Pursuit/column-sparse variants, robust matrix factorization (e.g., GoDec/AltProj), weighted/tilted RPCA, and LRR with A = X. Ablations varying number of clusters (to directly show the µ2-phenomenon), degree of subspace affinity, dictionary rank, and an iterative version of Algorithm 1 would clarify when gains are largest. Also evaluate with more realistic noise/missingness models beyond Bernoulli ±1 replacements.
- Proof presentation: Many proofs are deferred to a “full paper.” For NeurIPS, the main ideas (dual certificate construction, where µ2 drops out, why µ1(A) suffices) should be sketched sufficiently for readers to assess correctness. Clarify the source of randomness in the theorems (random support Ω? signs?) and constants (ca).

Quality
The theoretical statements are plausible and align with LRR intuition, but the reliance on a strong subspace inclusion and the absence of in-paper proof sketches reduce verifiability. The empirical evidence is positive but limited in scope. Overall technically sound with caveats above.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear notation and motivation. It would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why µ2 disappears when projecting onto a low-rank dictionary subspace, and a more explicit discussion of failure modes of the unsupervised algorithm.

Originality
High. While LRR is known, the specific theoretical guarantee for general low-rank dictionaries that avoids µ2, the interpretation of coherence via union-of-subspaces, and the simple dictionary-learning via PCP are a fresh and bold direction for robust recovery.

Significance
High potential. If the approach reliably mitigates coherence issues, it opens a practical path to robust recovery in clustered data and clarifies when to prefer LRR over PCP. The ideas should be of interest to communities working on robust matrix recovery, subspace clustering, and representation learning.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear new conceptual contribution (tackling µ2/coherence via low-rank dictionaries).
- Theoretical guarantees that push beyond PCP’s standard assumptions.
- Simple, practical algorithm with promising gains on synthetic and real tasks.
- Good fit for NeurIPS audience interested in robustness and representation.

Cons against acceptance
- Strong assumptions and limited theory-to-practice bridge for the unsupervised setting.
- Experimental comparisons and ablations are too narrow; missing stronger baselines.
- Proof details deferred; some ambiguity about constants and probabilistic setup.

Recommendation
Accept (borderline-leaning positive). The paper proposes a meaningful new direction with theory and a usable algorithm. Despite some experimental and presentation gaps, the novelty and potential impact justify acceptance. Constructive improvements as above would strengthen the final version.
