

# Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper addresses a limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) when dealing with coherent data, specifically data exhibiting clustering structures. The authors argue that RPCA's performance degrades as the number of clusters increases due to the corresponding increase in coherence parameters (µ2 and µ3), which are necessary for RPCA's theoretical guarantees. They propose modifying the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework by using a low-rank dictionary matrix A that satisfies U0 ⊂ UA (where U0 is the column space of the true low-rank matrix L0), thereby avoiding dependence on coherence parameters. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for exact and near-exact recovery under specific conditions and proposes an unsupervised algorithm that uses RPCA to construct the dictionary for LRR. The authors demonstrate through experiments on synthetic data and motion sequences that their approach outperforms RPCA.

**Quality:** The paper provides technically sound theoretical contributions with well-structured proofs for Theorems 1 and 2, establishing conditions under which LRR with a low-rank dictionary can recover coherent data without relying on coherence parameters. The experimental design is thorough, including both synthetic data with controlled parameters and real-world motion sequences from the Hopkins155 database. The authors appropriately acknowledge RPCA's limitations and carefully discuss the relationship between clustering structures and coherence parameters. The paper presents a complete piece of work with clear theoretical results, algorithmic implementation, and empirical validation.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively establishes the problem and motivation, while the technical sections provide sufficient detail for experts to understand the mathematical framework. The summary of notations is helpful for readers unfamiliar with the specific matrix norms and coherence parameters. The paper could benefit from slightly more explicit explanation of why clustering structures specifically affect µ2 (and not µ1), as this connection is crucial to the paper's main argument. The algorithm description is clear, though the relationship between the dictionary construction steps and the theoretical guarantees could be more explicitly connected.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by identifying the specific limitation of RPCA with coherent data (particularly due to clustering structures) and proposing a solution using a low-rank dictionary within the LRR framework. This addresses a gap in the literature, as the authors correctly note that while LRR with A = X has been studied extensively, the general dictionary case has not been fully understood in relation to coherent data recovery. The connection between coherence parameters and extra structures (beyond low-rankness) is particularly insightful and represents a meaningful contribution to the theoretical understanding of matrix recovery problems.

**Significance:** The problem addressed has clear practical significance, as many real-world datasets exhibit clustering structures (e.g., motion sequences, image data). The paper demonstrates that their approach provides substantial improvements over RPCA in both synthetic and real-world experiments, with a 47% wider success area on synthetic data and a 40% relative improvement in clustering error rates on motion sequences. The results advance the state of the art in robust matrix recovery by providing a theoretically grounded solution to a previously identified limitation of RPCA.

**Strengths:**
- Clear identification of a specific limitation of RPCA (coherence issues with clustered data)
- Theoretical guarantees that establish conditions for exact recovery without coherence parameters
- Practical unsupervised algorithm that improves upon RPCA without requiring additional supervision
- Comprehensive experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world data
- Insightful connection between coherence parameters and extra data structures (clustering)

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could more explicitly explain why clustering structures specifically affect µ2 (and not µ1) in the main text (this is discussed in the full paper but not sufficiently highlighted in the NIPS submission)
- The computational complexity analysis of Algorithm 1 could be more detailed, particularly regarding how the dictionary construction affects overall runtime
- The paper doesn't fully address whether the proposed dictionary construction is optimal or if there are alternative methods that might yield better results

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with significant theoretical contributions and practical implications. The work builds upon and extends the RPCA and LRR literature while making a novel contribution to understanding the relationship between coherence parameters and data structures. The results demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods in both theory and practice.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery. The theoretical insights are valuable, the experimental validation is thorough, and the practical implications are well-demonstrated. The paper meets all the NIPS quality criteria and would be of interest to the broader machine learning community.
