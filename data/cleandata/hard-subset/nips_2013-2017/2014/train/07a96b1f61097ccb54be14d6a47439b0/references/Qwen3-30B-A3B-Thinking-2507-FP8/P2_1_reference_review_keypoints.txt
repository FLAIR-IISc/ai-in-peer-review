- Identifies RPCA's failure on coherent (clustered) low-rank data due to increased coherence parameters, especially μ2 and μ3.  
- Proposes using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary to avoid the problematic coherence parameters.  
- Proves theoretical recovery guarantees (Theorems 1 and 2) showing LRR with a low-rank dictionary succeeds where RPCA fails.  
- Provides a practical unsupervised algorithm (Algorithm 1) that uses RPCA to construct a suitable low-rank dictionary.  
- Empirical results on synthetic and Hopkins155 motion data show significant improvements over RPCA (47% wider success area, 40% relative error reduction).  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and clearly defines coherence parameters and their relation to clustering ("μ2-phenomenon").  
- Main theorems give clear conditions for success and a looser (better) sample bound for LRR than RPCA.  
- Experimental design is comprehensive, varying rank and corruption support and including real-world sequences.  
- Novel insight: coherence parameters reflect intrinsic data structure (clustering), not just assumptions.  
- Original contribution: identifying the μ2-phenomenon and showing low-rank dictionaries in LRR can mitigate it.  
- Algorithmic contribution: practical unsupervised method bridging theory and application.  
- Clarity: well-organized paper with consistent notation and helpful figures illustrating coherence and clustering.  
- Some theoretical explanations (why low-rank dictionary fixes coherence; why μ2 changes with clustering) lack intuitive exposition for non-experts.  
- Algorithm 1 lacks detailed practical guidance (sensitivity to λ, impact of rank estimation step).  
- Related work: connection to prior mentions of clustering and coherence exists but this work provides more comprehensive analysis and guarantees.  
- Broader impact could be better articulated beyond motion segmentation to other domains (e.g., medical imaging, NLP).  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, significant contribution with strong theory and experiments.  
- Recommendation: accept (pro-acceptance: addresses important limitation, principled solution, strong empirical gains; con-acceptance: some intuition and implementation details need expansion).
