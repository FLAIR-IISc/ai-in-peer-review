This paper addresses the problem of recovering low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse errors, focusing on scenarios where the low-rank component exhibits coherence due to underlying data structures like clustering. The authors argue that standard Robust PCA (RPCA) struggles with such coherent data and propose utilizing a Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework with a carefully chosen dictionary to mitigate this issue. They theoretically analyze the conditions under which LRR avoids the coherence problem and propose an algorithm leveraging RPCA to construct the dictionary for LRR. Experiments on both synthetic and real-world motion data demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear mathematical analysis and justifications for the proposed approach. The proofs, while not included in full due to space constraints, are referenced and the theoretical arguments seem plausible. The connection between coherence and clustering is well-explained and the derivation of conditions for avoiding coherence in LRR is a significant contribution. The algorithm proposed is straightforward and builds upon existing techniques (RPCA and LRR). However, the claim of "exact recovery" needs careful consideration, as the noisy case theorem allows for a bounded error, not perfect recovery. The empirical validation demonstrates improvements over RPCA, but the gains are not always dramatic, and a more detailed ablation study of the dictionary learning process (e.g., the impact of different RPCA parameters) could strengthen the analysis. The authors acknowledge limitations and propose future directions.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. Notation is well-defined, although the use of multiple norms could be slightly overwhelming for readers unfamiliar with the field. The explanation of the µ2-phenomenon is clear, aided by the illustrative figures. The algorithm is clearly presented. A minor improvement would be to provide more intuition behind the choice of λ = 1/√n1.

**Originality:** The problem of robustness to coherence in low-rank matrix recovery is relevant and contributes to the existing body of work on RPCA and related techniques. While LRR itself is not novel, the analysis of how dictionary choice impacts its performance in the presence of coherent data is a significant contribution.  The insight that coherence is linked to underlying data structures and isn't simply an undesirable assumption is a valuable perspective.  The connection to matrix factorization techniques is also a noteworthy observation. The paper adequately references related work, including foundational papers on RPCA and LRR. Checking for closely related work on dictionary learning for robust PCA in specific applications (e.g., video processing) could further strengthen the originality assessment.

**Significance:** The results have the potential to be impactful, particularly in applications where data often exhibits clustering or other coherent structures. Improving the robustness of low-rank matrix recovery algorithms is a valuable contribution, as these algorithms are widely used in various fields like image processing, computer vision, and machine learning. The practical algorithm proposed could be readily adopted by researchers and practitioners. The theoretical insights into the relationship between coherence, data structure, and algorithm performance are valuable for advancing the understanding of low-rank matrix models. The empirical results on motion sequences, while limited, suggest real-world applicability.  The analysis contributes to a deeper understanding of when and why existing methods fail and how to improve them.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem (robustness to coherence) in a well-established field (low-rank matrix recovery).
*   The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the relationship between coherence, data structure, and algorithm performance.
*   The proposed algorithm builds upon existing techniques in a principled way and demonstrates improvements over RPCA in experiments.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical gains over RPCA are not always dramatic and could be strengthened by a more thorough ablation study.
*   The term "exact recovery" is used loosely, as the noisy case allows for a bounded error.
*   The dictionary learning procedure could benefit from further investigation and potentially more sophisticated techniques.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance. This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of robust low-rank matrix recovery. The theoretical analysis and the proposed algorithm offer a practical solution to the problem of coherence, and the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details. While some aspects could be further refined, the overall quality and significance of the work justify acceptance to NIPS.
