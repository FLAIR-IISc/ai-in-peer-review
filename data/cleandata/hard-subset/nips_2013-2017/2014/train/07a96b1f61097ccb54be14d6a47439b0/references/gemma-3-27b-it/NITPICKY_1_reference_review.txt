This paper addresses the problem of low-rank matrix recovery in the presence of corruptions, specifically focusing on the impact of data coherence on the performance of Robust PCA (RPCA) and proposing a Low-Rank Representation (LRR) approach with a learned dictionary to mitigate these effects. While the motivation is sound – that RPCA’s performance degrades with increasing data coherence due to clustering structures – and the proposed approach has the potential to improve recovery, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues ranging from superficial analysis to questionable rigor and formatting inconsistencies, leading me to recommend rejection.

The introduction adequately motivates the problem but is unnecessarily verbose. The repeated emphasis on RPCA's limitations, while correct, feels redundant. The connection made between coherence and clustering (the “µ2-phenomenon”) is presented as a key insight, but the explanation relies heavily on Figure 2 without sufficient analytical depth. The claim that µ1 remains invariant to clustering while µ2 increases is intriguing but lacks a detailed proof, simply referencing the figure. The statement that µ1 *could* be large if row vectors also exhibit clustering is a belated and rather weak qualification. The justification for why LRR avoids coherence parameters is initially unclear and only becomes apparent after delving into the theorems.

The notation section is overly detailed and somewhat disorganized. While thorough, defining six different matrix norms feels excessive for a single paper, and their utility is not fully demonstrated. The repeated abuse of notation (using symbols for both matrices *and* the spaces they span) is confusing and prone to errors.

The theoretical contributions (Theorems 1 & 2) are presented without sufficient context. The conditions for Theorem 1, particularly `rank (A) ≤ ǫ2n2/caµ1(A) log n1`, appear artificially constrained and their practical implications aren’t discussed. The assumption of a column-wisely unit-normed dictionary feels ad-hoc and isn’t convincingly justified. The claim that the proposed algorithm improves upon RPCA’s success probability is stated without comparative analytical bounds—it’s merely asserted based on the logic of Theorem 1. Theorem 2’s noisy recovery guarantee is presented similarly, lacking rigorous error analysis beyond stating that the recovery is “near”.  The significance of the `ε` parameter, representing the noise level, isn’t adequately tied to the other parameters in the theorem.

The experimental section provides some evidence of improvement over RPCA on synthetic data and motion sequences. However, the experimental setup is not described with sufficient detail. The parameter settings (e.g., λ = 1/√n1) are chosen somewhat arbitrarily without justification for their optimality. The 47% wider “success area” in Figure 4 is presented without a clear definition of what constitutes “success”. The results on motion sequences, while positive, rely on comparisons to existing subspace clustering methods *after* applying matrix recovery—a somewhat indirect evaluation of the proposed method. Furthermore, Table 1 is poorly formatted; error rates are presented to an excessive number of decimal places.

**Specific Issues & Minor Flaws:**

*   Equation (3.4) through (3.6) lack clear explanations of what the expectation is over.
*   Figure 1 is referred to but not explicitly labeled with a figure number in the text.
*   The phrase "arguably" in the introduction is weak and unnecessary.
*   The sentence "It is not crucial to determine the exact value of d." is redundant.
*   The statement that "LRR falls back to RPCA when A = I" is technically correct but trivial and doesn’t add value.
*   Numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing throughout the text (e.g., "effects of various extra structures").
*   The Acknowledgement section references support from various grants, but the connection to the specific work is not explained.
*   There are inconsistencies in formatting (e.g., spacing around equations, citation style).
*   Reference list formatting needs to be verified and corrected.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The motivation for addressing coherence in low-rank recovery is valid.
*   The LRR approach, with a learned dictionary, offers a potential improvement over RPCA.
*   The experimental results, though limited, show some promise.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis is superficial and lacks rigorous bounds.
*   The experimental setup is not sufficiently detailed or controlled.
*   The paper suffers from numerous minor flaws in writing, notation, and formatting.
*   The claims of improvement over RPCA are not convincingly substantiated.
*   The paper feels incomplete and lacks a clear, concise presentation of its contributions.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite a potentially interesting idea, the numerous technical flaws, lack of rigorous analysis, and formatting issues significantly detract from the quality of this paper. While the problem addressed is relevant, the proposed solution and its evaluation are not presented with the level of detail and rigor expected for a NIPS publication. I recommend rejection. A major revision addressing the points raised above, with a significantly strengthened theoretical analysis and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, would be required for reconsideration.
