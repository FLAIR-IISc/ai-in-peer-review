### Summary

This paper investigates a key failure mode of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA), namely its performance degradation on "coherent" low-rank data. The authors provide a compelling physical interpretation for this coherence, linking it to the presence of clustering structures within the data's underlying low-dimensional subspace. They demonstrate that as the number of clusters increases, the `µ2` and `µ3` coherence parameters of the low-rank matrix `L0` also increase, violating the conditions required for guaranteed recovery by standard RPCA.

To address this "µ2-phenomenon," the authors propose using the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework with a learned dictionary `A`, i.e., decomposing the data matrix `X` as `X = AZ + S`. Their main theoretical contribution is showing that if the dictionary `A` is itself low-rank and its column space contains the column space of the true low-rank data `L0`, then LRR can recover `L0` and `S0` exactly, with recovery guarantees that are independent of the problematic `µ2` parameter. Based on this insight, they propose a practical two-stage algorithm: first, run standard RPCA to obtain an initial estimate of `L0`; second, use this estimate to construct a dictionary `A`, and then run LRR with this dictionary to obtain a refined, more accurate recovery. Experiments on both synthetic data and real-world motion segmentation data demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms standard RPCA, especially in the challenging coherent regime.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Important Problem Formulation:** The paper provides an excellent diagnosis of a practical weakness in RPCA. While coherence has been a known theoretical quantity, this work gives it a concrete, physically intuitive meaning by linking it to clustering—a ubiquitous structure in real-world data. This insight alone is a valuable contribution that clarifies why and when RPCA might struggle.

2.  **Elegant Theoretical Solution:** The use of LRR with a low-rank dictionary is a clean and well-motivated solution. Theorem 1 formally establishes that this approach circumvents the dependence on `µ2`, providing a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The condition that the dictionary's column space must span the true data's column space (`U0 ⊂ UA`) is an clear and actionable principle for dictionary design.

3.  **Practical and Effective Algorithm:** Algorithm 1 is a simple, intuitive, and demonstrably effective way to implement the theoretical idea in an unsupervised setting. The two-stage refinement process—using RPCA to bootstrap a dictionary for LRR—is a clever heuristic. The claim that this only increases computation time by a factor of ~1.2 is a significant practical advantage.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The phase transition plots on synthetic data (Figure 4) clearly show a substantially larger region of successful recovery compared to RPCA. The application to motion segmentation provides a strong real-world use case, where the improved matrix recovery translates directly to a significant reduction in downstream task error (a 40% relative improvement for SSC).

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Dependence on Initial RPCA Performance:** The success of the proposed algorithm hinges on the initial RPCA step providing a "good enough" estimate of `L0` to construct a useful dictionary. The paper notes that RPCA's performance is "depressed" but not "brittle" on coherent data, but it does not formally characterize the basin of attraction. How poor can the initial RPCA estimate be before the dictionary `Â` becomes useless and the LRR step fails to improve, or even worsens, the result? A discussion on the robustness of Algorithm 1 to the quality of the initial estimate would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Heuristics in Algorithm 1:** The algorithm relies on a heuristic for rank estimation (thresholding singular values at `10^-3 σ1`). The performance of the subsequent steps could be sensitive to this choice. A brief sensitivity analysis or discussion of this parameter choice would be beneficial.

3.  **Dictionary Construction Rationale:** The dictionary `Â` is formed by the column-normalized version of the estimated low-rank matrix `L̃0`. This means the dictionary has `n` atoms. An alternative, more compact dictionary could be constructed from a basis of the estimated column space (i.e., the estimated left singular vectors `Û0`). The paper should briefly justify the choice of using all `n` columns of `L̃0` as the dictionary, as this may not scale well for very large `n`.

4.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** Theorem 1 requires the exact condition `U0 ⊂ UA`. In Algorithm 1, this is approximated by constructing `A` from `L̃0`, which is an estimate of `L0`. There is a gap between the deterministic condition in the theory and its stochastic approximation in the algorithm. While the experiments show this works well, a brief discussion on how the theoretical guarantees might degrade gracefully as the initial estimate moves away from the ground truth would be insightful.

### Detailed Comments

-   The explanation of the "µ2-phenomenon" is a highlight. However, the paper defers the justification of why `µ2` increases with the cluster number to the full version. Given its centrality to the paper's motivation, including a brief, intuitive argument or a simplified derivation in the main text would make the paper more self-contained and impactful.
-   The paper mentions that an iterative version of Algorithm 1 is possible but converges quickly. This is an interesting observation and strengthens the case for the proposed two-step approach.
-   The connection made to matrix factorization is insightful and helps place the work within a broader context.

### Overall Assessment and Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper that makes significant contributions on both theoretical and practical fronts. It identifies a crucial limitation of the widely-used RPCA method, provides a novel and insightful explanation for it, proposes a theoretically sound and elegant solution, and validates it with a practical algorithm and strong empirical results. The weaknesses identified are primarily areas for clarification or future extension and do not undermine the core contribution. The work deepens our understanding of low-rank models and provides a valuable tool for practitioners dealing with complex, clustered data.

-   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound with solid theoretical analysis and compelling experimental support.
-   **Clarity:** Very good. The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation is clear, and the proposed method is explained well.
-   **Originality:** High. The link between coherence and clustering is a novel insight. The proposed two-stage LRR approach to overcome this specific problem is also a new and clever combination of existing ideas.
-   **Significance:** High. Given the ubiquity of RPCA, addressing one of its key failure modes is an important and impactful contribution. Researchers and practitioners in computer vision, signal processing, and machine learning are likely to find this work very useful.

**Recommendation: Accept.**

This is a strong paper that would be an excellent addition to the NIPS program.
