- Overall: Tackles RPCA’s failure on coherent (clustered) data by using LRR with a low-rank dictionary; theoretically and empirically validated; recommends accept with minor revisions.
- Problem/Summary: RPCA degrades with increasing coherence from clustering; a low-rank dictionary in LRR can bypass RPCA’s coherence limitations.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on Candès et al. (RPCA) and Liu et al. (LRR), and connects to matrix factorization literature.
- Strength — Motivation: Clearly defines the coherence issue and links it convincingly to clustering structures in real data.
- Strength — Theory: Provides rigorous theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) showing conditions where LRR with a low-rank dictionary avoids RPCA’s coherence problems.
- Strength — Algorithm & Experiments: Proposes a practical Algorithm 1 (using RPCA as a start) and shows superior results on synthetic and real motion data.
- Strength — Insight: Offers a deeper conceptual understanding of coherence as an inherent data characteristic to be accounted for.
- Strength — Related work: Demonstrates strong awareness and appropriate positioning within related literature.
- Weakness — Conservative theory: Theorem 1’s recovery conditions (especially on dictionary rank) appear restrictive and could be relaxed.
- Weakness — Presentation: Paper is dense; theoretical derivations are verbose, notation occasionally overwhelming, and figures could be clearer.
- Weakness — Dictionary learning: Algorithm 1 is simple (uses RPCA output); limited exploration of alternative dictionary learning methods and missing explanation of why the simple approach works well.
- Weakness — Noisy-case: Theorem 2’s analysis for the noisy case is weaker and less justified than the noiseless results.
- Qualitative scores: Quality 8/10; Clarity 7/10; Originality 8/10; Significance 9/10.
- Arguments for acceptance: Solves an important practical problem, has sound theory and experiments, provides insight into coherence, and offers a computationally efficient algorithm.
- Arguments against acceptance: Conservative theoretical guarantees, clarity/presentation issues, and limited exploration of richer dictionary learning techniques.
- Recommendation & requested revisions: Accept with minor revisions — simplify presentation, discuss/relax theoretical assumptions, explore or discuss alternative dictionary learning methods, and strengthen the noisy-case justification.
