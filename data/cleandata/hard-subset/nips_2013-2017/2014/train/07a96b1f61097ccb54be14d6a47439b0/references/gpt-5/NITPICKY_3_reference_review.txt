Summary
The paper addresses a known failure mode of RPCA/PCP: when the low-rank component L0 exhibits strong coherence (e.g., clustered columns from a union of subspaces), the success conditions degrade via large μ2 (and μ3). The authors propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a prelearned low-rank dictionary A. They prove that if A is low-rank and its column space contains that of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), then exact (noiseless) and near-exact (noisy) recovery are possible without invoking μ2; the bounds depend on μ1(A) instead. They then propose a simple unsupervised heuristic: run RPCA, estimate the rank, normalize columns of the low-rank estimate to form A, and solve LRR. Experiments on synthetic data and motion trajectory data suggest improved recovery and downstream clustering compared to RPCA.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically relevant failure mode of RPCA with a conceptually simple remedy (learn a low-rank dictionary).
- Theoretical results (Theorems 1–2) articulate conditions under which μ2 can be avoided, clarifying the role of coherence and the dependence on μ1(A).
- The heuristic (RPCA → build A → LRR) is easy to implement and can be faster than a second full RPCA if A is sufficiently low-rank.
- Empirical results indicate clear improvements over RPCA on the presented setups.

Major weaknesses and concerns
- Missing assumptions/rigor in theory presentation. Theorems are stated “with probability at least 1−n−10” without specifying the source of randomness (support/sign model for S0, distributional assumptions on Ω, etc.). The necessary randomness and independence conditions are standard in PCP/LRR analyses but are not made explicit here. Proofs are deferred to the “full paper” and absent in the submission.
- Key assumption U0 ⊂ UA is indispensable for exact recovery but is not justified in the unsupervised setting. The proposed algorithm relies on RPCA to construct A. If RPCA is precisely the method that deteriorates on coherent data, there is no guarantee that UA will contain U0 when RPCA under-recovers. The text only provides a heuristic justification (“RPCA is not brittle”) with no analysis of failure modes or conditions under which Algorithm 1 strictly improves RPCA.
- “Avoiding μ2” is qualified: the bounds still depend on μ1(A). In practice μ1(A) can be large (e.g., when columns of A are spiky or poorly conditioned). The paper does not analyze/how to ensure small μ1(A) in the proposed construction. The post-processing (rank truncation, column normalization) addresses conditioning only heuristically.
- Experimental methodology lacks essential detail and ablations. Figures are referenced but not included. Synthetic setup is underspecified (e.g., precise generative model for clustered subspaces, distribution of Ω and S0 signs). No sensitivity analysis to rank threshold (10−3σ1), λ, dictionary normalization, or noise. No experiments demonstrate the Noisy theorem setting (Gaussian noise); instead, missing entries are imputed with ±1, which conflates completion and corruption and biases the problem in favor of sparse recovery. No comparisons to other robust low-rank methods (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, Stable PCP/“noisy PCP”, AltProj/GoDec/IRLS variants, robust matrix completion baselines).
- Claims about complexity are imprecise. The O(n^3) vs O(n^2 rA) statements depend on solver choices and matrix shapes; actual runtime profiles and SVD costs per iteration are not reported. No details on optimization algorithms, stopping criteria, or implementation are given.
- Writing/formatting issues accumulate: numerous grammatical errors; inconsistent notation (ε vs ǫ, “column-wisely”), typos (‖M‖ ,∞), footnote/reference glitches (“PRCA3”), and incomplete/inaccurate references (many missing years/venues; inconsistent capitalization; some author lists appear off). These hinder reproducibility and clarity.
- Novelty vs prior LRR literature is not clearly delimited. The case A=X and its variants have been extensively studied; the claim of “first time” studying coherent low-rank recovery is too strong. The paper should more carefully position the contribution as an analysis showing that low-rank dictionaries can mitigate μ2 dependence, and contrast with existing LRR theory and robust subspace methods.

Quality
- The core idea is reasonable and the theorems, if correct, would be valuable, but the current submission lacks the precise assumptions, proof sketches, and empirical depth needed to evaluate technical soundness. The unsupervised algorithm is heuristic with no guarantees.

Clarity
- Organization is acceptable, but numerous notational/grammatical errors, missing figures, and incomplete references reduce clarity. Important modeling details are missing.

Originality
- Moderate. The focus on coherence and μ2 in clustered data is known; the specific “avoid μ2 with low-rank dictionary containing U0” angle is a useful refinement, but closely tied to known LRR principles.

Significance
- Potentially useful if the conditions can be met in practice, but the reliance on RPCA to build A undercuts robustness gains in the regime of interest. Empirical validation is too limited to assess broad impact.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Specify the probabilistic model (random support, signs, independence, incoherence assumptions on A/L0, etc.) under which Theorems 1–2 hold. Clarify constants and the dependence on m vs n.
- Discuss how the proposed construction ensures small μ1(A) and what happens when μ1(A) is large. Provide diagnostics (measured μ1(A)) in experiments.
- Analyze when Algorithm 1 will or will not satisfy U0 ⊂ UA. Consider an iterative refinement and provide empirical evidence that two iterations suffice.
- Include full figures, solver details, run times, and parameter sensitivity. Evaluate the noisy setting with actual Gaussian noise and missingness models; compare to robust matrix completion baselines.
- Tighten complexity discussion with concrete per-iteration costs and wall-clock timings.
- Thoroughly fix references and notation; unify ε/ǫ; correct typos; provide complete bibliographic entries.

Pros
- Addresses an important practical failure mode of RPCA.
- Clean theoretical statement highlighting μ2 vs μ1.
- Simple, potentially effective heuristic; promising preliminary gains.

Cons
- Incomplete/underspecified theory and experiments; heavy reliance on heuristics.
- Critical assumptions (U0 ⊂ UA) are not justified in the unsupervised setting.
- Numerous writing/formatting issues; missing figures; weak positioning vs prior LRR work.

Recommendation: Reject. The idea is interesting, but the submission falls short on rigor, clarity, and experimental validation. With clarified assumptions/proofs, stronger/complete experiments, and polished writing, a future version could be competitive.
