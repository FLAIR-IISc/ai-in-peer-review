Summary
The paper tackles a documented limitation of RPCA/PCP: its guarantees deteriorate with data coherence, especially when low-rank data exhibit clustered structure (union-of-subspaces), inflating the coherence parameters µ2 and µ3 (“µ2-phenomenon”). The authors propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary A, and develop recovery guarantees showing that if A is low-rank and its column space contains the true column space U0 of L0 (PUA(U0) = U0), the dependence on µ2 can be removed from the success conditions. Concretely, they prove noiseless and noisy theorems for LRR with λ = 1/√n1, requiring rank(L0) ≤ rank(A) ≤ O(n2/(µ1(A) log n1)) and a standard random sparse corruption model with |Ω| ≤ c mn. They then propose a simple unsupervised procedure: run RPCA to estimate L0, shrink to its top r̂0 components, normalize columns to form Â, then solve LRR with A = Â. On synthetic matrices with clustered subspaces and on corrupted motion trajectories, the method improves over RPCA in recovery and downstream clustering.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of the µ2-phenomenon: clustered row-space structure increases µ2 and degrades PCP. This is both empirically demonstrated and conceptually important.
- New recovery guarantees for LRR with general low-rank dictionaries. The conditions remove µ2 (and thus µ3) from the analysis, shifting dependence to µ1(A); the rank bound O(n2/log n1) is slightly looser than PCP’s standard O(n2/(log n1)^2), which is favorable.
- Practical, simple algorithm that leverages RPCA as an initializer to synthesize a dictionary. Empirical improvements are nontrivial (e.g., a 47% larger success region on synthetic data; 40% relative error reduction in motion segmentation).
- Good discussion of the physical meaning of coherence and how extra structures beyond low-rankness (e.g., clustering) drive it.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumption strength: The core guarantees require U0 ⊂ UA and a low µ1(A). In supervised settings this may be plausible, but in unsupervised recovery this is close to assuming access to the column space. The proposed heuristic constructs Â from an RPCA estimate; if RPCA is poor exactly in the problematic regime (high coherence, moderate rank/sparsity), U0 ⊂ UA may fail, and the method may not help. The paper would benefit from an analysis quantifying how approximate containment (angle between U0 and UA) affects recovery.
- Coherence transfer: While LRR avoids µ2(L0), the guarantees hinge on µ1(A). The algorithm builds Â from L̃0’s columns; when data are cluster-coherent in the row-space, what ensures µ1(Â) is small? Empirical reporting of µ1(Â) (vs µ2(L0)) across experiments would substantiate the claim that the dictionary is “well-conditioned.”
- Experimental scope: Corruption is strictly entrywise-sparse with random support. Many realistic settings have structured corruption (block occlusions, nonrandom missingness) or column-sparse outliers; comparisons to Outlier Pursuit (Xu et al.), RASL/TILT-style structured corruptions, or robust matrix completion would strengthen the case. Also, replacing missing entries with ±1 for motion data is unusual; a variant that explicitly handles missing entries (projected constraints) should be compared.
- Solver and complexity details: The claim that LRR costs O(n^2 rA) (assuming m = n) needs more specificity (dimensions of Z are d×n; SVD costs depend on d, n). Provide the actual algorithm (e.g., ADMM) and stopping criteria. Running time comparisons on the reported datasets would be informative.
- Theoretical clarity: The paper alludes to proofs in the full version. A high-level proof sketch indicating the dual certificate construction and where µ2 drops out would help readers assess soundness. Likewise, the precise probabilistic model for the clustered subspaces underpinning the µ2 growth should be described.
- Hyperparameters: The algorithm uses a fixed singular value threshold (10−3 σ1) and fixed ε = 10−6‖X‖F. Sensitivity analyses would help. Similarly, column normalization in A can amplify noise in small-norm columns; how is this handled?

Quality
The theoretical claims are plausible and consistent with prior PCP/LRR analyses, with a clear insight: by constraining L through a low-rank dictionary containing U0, one can eliminate µ2 from the conditions. The empirical results are encouraging, though limited to one corruption model and one real-data domain. The lack of ablations (dictionary rank, quality of RPCA initialization, iteration beyond one pass) and baselines for structured corruptions limits the empirical conclusiveness.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Notation is heavy but standard. Some statements are overly strong (e.g., “avoid the coherence parameters”)—it would be more precise to say “avoid dependence on µ2 under dictionary conditions, while depending on µ1(A).” Please add:
- A brief proof sketch of Theorem 1/2.
- Explicit model and derivation for the µ2-phenomenon with clustered subspaces.
- Practical guidance for d and for handling near-zero columns during normalization.

Originality
LRR is known; the novelty here is the theory and practice of using a low-rank dictionary to mitigate coherence effects, and a concrete two-stage RPCA→LRR pipeline with guarantees conditioned on U0 ⊂ UA. This is a meaningful and nontrivial extension of LRR/PCP theory.

Significance
The work addresses a real and important failure mode of PCP and offers a simple remedy that can be adopted by practitioners. If further validated under more realistic corruptions and with partial observations, it could influence robust subspace learning pipelines.

Pros for acceptance
- Identifies and formalizes an important shortcoming of PCP with clear intuition.
- New theoretical guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries avoiding µ2.
- Simple, practical algorithm with tangible improvements on synthetic and real data.
- Conceptual contribution clarifying the role of coherence and extra structure.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong assumptions (U0 ⊂ UA) with limited analysis of approximate satisfaction in the unsupervised setting.
- Narrow experimental scope; missing comparisons to methods for structured/missing corruptions.
- Some theoretical and algorithmic details deferred to the full version.

Recommendation: Weak accept
Confidence: High

Suggestions to improve
- Add experiments with block occlusion, column-sparse outliers, and nonrandom missingness; include Outlier Pursuit and robust matrix completion baselines.
- Report µ1(Â), µ2(L0), and UA–U0 principal angles before/after dictionary learning.
- Provide sensitivity/ablation on r̂0 thresholding, dictionary rank, and iterative refinement.
- Clarify solver choices and provide runtime comparisons.
