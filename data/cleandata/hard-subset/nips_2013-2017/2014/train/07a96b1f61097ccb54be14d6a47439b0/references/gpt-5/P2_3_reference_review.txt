Summary
The paper addresses robust recovery of a low-rank matrix L0 from grossly corrupted observations X = L0 + S0 in regimes where L0 is coherent, e.g., when data are clustered within a union of subspaces. The authors argue that RPCA’s guarantees degrade with increasing coherence (especially μ2, tied to the row space), a phenomenon they call the μ2-phenomenon. They propose to use Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary A: minimize ||Z||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = AZ + S. The main theoretical result shows that if A is low-rank and its column space contains the true column space (PUA(U0) = U0), then exact recovery is possible without dependence on μ2(L0), with rank bounds scaling like O(n2/log n1) (improving the log factor over RPCA). They extend to a noisy setting with Frobenius error bounds. Practically, they propose an unsupervised two-step algorithm: run RPCA, form a low-rank approximation of its L̂0, column-normalize to construct Â, and then run LRR with Â. Experiments on synthetic union-of-subspaces data and corrupted motion trajectories show larger success regions than RPCA and improved downstream clustering accuracy.

Quality
- Theory: The theorems are clear and technically plausible. The key assumption PUA(U0) = U0 is necessary for exact recovery and the analysis carefully trades μ2(L0) for μ1(A), thereby explaining why a low-rank dictionary that captures the correct column space can neutralize the μ2-phenomenon. The comparison of rank bounds vs. RPCA (log vs. log^2) is a nice touch. The noisy result provides concrete error bounds O(√mn ε).
- However, the main assumption is strong: success hinges on A’s column space containing U0. In supervised settings this is reasonable; in unsupervised settings the proposed heuristic (bootstrapping A from RPCA) lacks formal guarantees. The authors acknowledge this, but the gap between the conditions of Theorem 1 and what Algorithm 1 can ensure is substantial.
- Experiments: The synthetic experiment is well-matched to the claim (union-of-subspaces with increasing coherence) and shows a substantial gain (47% larger success area). The motion segmentation experiment demonstrates practical benefits via downstream clustering error reductions. That said, the empirical section is limited: no ablations (e.g., effect of column normalization or rank truncation), no sensitivity to λ/ε, and a narrow set of baselines. It would strengthen the case to compare against other robust PCA variants (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, alternating/nonconvex PCP), dictionary-learning baselines (e.g., PCA-based A without RPCA), and to evaluate robustness when RPCA’s initial L̂0 is poor.
- Reproducibility: The algorithm is clearly specified, but implementation details (solvers, stopping criteria, computational cost) are sparse beyond a qualitative 1.2× overhead claim.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, with a clear introduction, motivation, and explanation of coherence parameters. The μ2-phenomenon is well motivated and helps demystify “incoherence” assumptions.
- Notation is standard and defined. Some proofs are deferred to the “full paper/supplement,” which is acceptable for NeurIPS.
- Some references are incomplete in the provided list, and the narrative occasionally relies on figures not visible here; ensure all claims tied to figures are echoed in text.

Originality
- Conceptually, replacing the identity in RPCA with a low-rank dictionary that spans the true column space is natural and related to self-expressive subspace methods and matrix factorization. The novelty lies in the theoretical quantification: LRR with an appropriate low-rank dictionary avoids μ2(L0) and yields improved recovery bounds. To my knowledge, this specific avoidance of μ2 and the conditions on A are new and useful.
- The practical two-step heuristic (RPCA → dictionary → LRR) is simple, and while intuitive, it is a neat recipe that practitioners could adopt.

Significance
- Robust recovery under coherent structures is important; many real datasets are clustered. The theoretical insight that coherence can be controlled via dictionary design is valuable and likely to influence follow-up work on dictionary learning for robust recovery and clustering.
- The practical gains shown are promising, though broader empirical validation would solidify impact.

Pros
- Clear identification and explanation of the μ2-phenomenon due to clustering.
- Theoretical results that remove μ2(L0) dependence under explicit and interpretable conditions on A, including a noisy recovery guarantee.
- Simple, practical algorithm that improves over RPCA on synthetic and real data.
- Bridges RPCA and LRR with a principled view of dictionary structure.

Cons
- Key assumption PUA(U0) = U0 is strong; no guarantees for the unsupervised procedure that attempts to meet it.
- Limited experimental breadth: few baselines, no ablations or sensitivity analysis, and nonstandard handling of missing data (replacing with ±1 rather than modeling missingness).
- Some presentation gaps (incomplete references, reliance on deferred proofs/figures) and lack of solver/runtime details.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Can you provide ablations isolating the importance of (i) rank truncation, (ii) column normalization, and (iii) the quality of L̂0? How does performance degrade as RPCA’s initial error grows?
- Compare against additional baselines: Outlier Pursuit, nonconvex RPCA, alternating minimization, and dictionary A from plain PCA on X (or SSC-implied subspaces).
- Discuss or attempt an iterative refinement (RPCA → LRR → updated A → LRR), and report whether it consistently helps and under what conditions.
- Clarify handling of missing data vs. sparse corruptions; can your framework directly accommodate missingness without imputing ±1?
- Provide more details on computational complexity and solver choices.

Recommendation
This is a solid and timely contribution with clear theory and promising empirical evidence. While the unsupervised guarantee gap and limited experiments are weaknesses, the core insight—designing a low-rank dictionary to sidestep coherence—advances understanding and practice. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening experiments and clarifying practical aspects.
