

# Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper proposes to address the limitation of RPCA (Robust Principal Component Analysis) when dealing with coherent data (i.e., data with high coherence parameters), which commonly arises in clustered data structures. The authors argue that RPCA's performance degrades as the number of clusters increases due to the "µ2-phenomenon" (where µ2 increases with cluster count). They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary to avoid dependence on µ2, providing theoretical guarantees and an unsupervised algorithm for dictionary learning.

## Strengths

The paper identifies an important limitation of RPCA that has been overlooked in the literature. The connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters is well-motivated, and the theoretical analysis of the LRR framework with low-rank dictionaries is a meaningful contribution. The unsupervised algorithm (Algorithm 1) is simple and practical, with promising experimental results on synthetic data and motion sequences.

## Major Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent Terminology and Notation**: The paper uses "RPCA" and "PRCA" interchangeably, creating confusion. The paper claims to study "Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit" but consistently refers to LRR (Low-Rank Representation), creating a mismatch between title and content. The paper also uses "coherent data" without a clear definition, while referring to "less incoherent" data without explaining what this means.

2. **Theoretical Inconsistencies**: Theorem 1 claims to avoid µ2, but the condition still depends on µ1(A), a coherence parameter of the dictionary. The paper states "LRR falls back to RPCA when A = I" but doesn't clarify that this requires A to be full rank (which it is), creating unnecessary confusion. The claim "the recovery produced by LRR is the same as RPCA whenever the dictionary A is orthogonal" is mathematically incorrect - orthogonal dictionaries would transform the problem but not yield identical solutions.

3. **Mischaracterization of RPCA**: The paper claims "RPCA cannot be an ultimate solution to Problem 1. Indeed, the method might not produce perfect recovery even when L0 is strictly low-rank." This is factually incorrect - RPCA *does* produce perfect recovery under the standard conditions (e.g., low coherence, sparse corruption), which the paper itself cites in (3.7).

4. **Unsubstantiated Claims**: The paper states "O(n2/log n1) is probably the 'finest' bound one could accomplish in theory" without citing any prior work establishing this as a theoretical limit. The claim about computational time being "merely about 1.2 times as much as RPCA" lacks supporting evidence.

5. **Missing Critical Details**: The paper refers to "Figure 2" and "Figure 3" without providing them, making it impossible to evaluate the empirical validation. The paper claims to "show that it is unnecessary for A to satisfy UA = U0," but doesn't explain why this is sufficient or provide any intuition.

6. **Inadequate Comparison**: The experiments only compare with RPCA and don't include other methods designed for structured data or coherent data (e.g., structured RPCA, GPCA variants), limiting the significance of the results.

## Minor Issues

- The paper uses "n1 = max(m,n) and n2 = min(m,n)" but doesn't consistently apply these definitions in the theorems.
- The paper states "The coherence parameters defined in [3, 4] are excellent exemplars of such quantities" but doesn't clearly define what "such quantities" refers to.
- The paper uses "µ2-phenomenon" without proper definition or explanation.
- The paper claims "LRR (1.3) equipped with proper dictionaries could well handle coherent data" but doesn't provide a clear definition of "proper dictionaries" beyond "low-rank and satisfying U0 ⊂ UA."

## Overall Assessment

While the paper identifies a genuine limitation of RPCA in certain data regimes and proposes a theoretically grounded approach to address it, the paper contains significant technical inconsistencies, misleading claims, and formatting issues that undermine its contribution. The mischaracterization of RPCA's capabilities is particularly problematic, as it misrepresents the state of the art. The theoretical claims are not sufficiently supported, and the experimental validation is incomplete without the referenced figures and proper comparisons.

The paper's core idea has merit, but the execution is flawed. The inconsistent terminology, incorrect mathematical claims, and lack of critical details prevent the paper from being a solid contribution to the field. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to correct these issues before it could be considered for publication.

Given the significant technical errors and misleading claims, I recommend rejection. The paper's contributions are not sufficiently novel or well-supported to overcome these fundamental flaws. The authors should carefully revisit the theoretical foundations of their approach and provide more precise mathematical statements before resubmitting.
