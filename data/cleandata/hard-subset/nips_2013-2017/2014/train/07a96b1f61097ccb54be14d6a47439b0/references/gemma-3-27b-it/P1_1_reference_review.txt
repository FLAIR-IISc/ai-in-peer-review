## Peer Review: Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper addresses an important problem in robust PCA – the limitations of standard RPCA when dealing with data exhibiting inherent coherence, specifically stemming from clustering structures. The authors convincingly argue that coherence isn't merely a technical constraint but reflects underlying data structure. They propose a modification to the LRR framework utilizing a learned dictionary to mitigate the effects of coherence and provide theoretical justification and empirical validation for their approach.  While the theoretical analysis feels a bit dense and could benefit from further clarification, the core idea is strong, well-motivated, and the experimental results are encouraging. I believe this work represents a valuable contribution to the field and recommend **acceptance**, albeit with minor revisions.

**1. Summary of Main Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the established framework of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA), which aims to decompose a data matrix into low-rank and sparse components. The authors identify a key limitation of RPCA: its performance degrades when the low-rank component exhibits coherence – a property often induced by clustering structures in the data. They argue this isn’t a flaw in the method itself, but a consequence of ignoring intrinsic data structure beyond simple low-rankness. 

They revisit the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) model and demonstrate that by employing a suitably constructed dictionary, the effects of coherence can be avoided. The central theoretical contribution is showing that a low-rank dictionary effectively circumvents the issues caused by the coherence parameters, especially µ2, when certain conditions are met. The authors then propose a practical algorithm to learn the dictionary by leveraging the output of RPCA as a starting point.

This work relates to several areas: RPCA ([Candès et al., 2011]), low-rank matrix completion ([Candès & Recht, 2009]), dictionary learning, and subspace clustering. It distinguishes itself by directly addressing the impact of coherence on RPCA, offering a theoretical and practical solution grounded in the LRR framework. It builds on [Liu et al., 2013] by exploring LRR with *general* dictionaries.

**2. Strengths & Weaknesses:**

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of coherence in RPCA and convincingly explains why it arises from common data characteristics like clustering.
* **Theoretical Contribution:**  The theoretical analysis provides a solid justification for using a low-rank dictionary to overcome the limitations of RPCA.  Theorem 1 is a strong result, outlining conditions for successful recovery.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed algorithm is relatively simple and efficient, building upon the widely used RPCA method.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real-world motion data demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, consistently outperforming RPCA.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively situates its contribution within the broader context of robust PCA, matrix factorization, and subspace learning.
* **Insightful Discussion of Coherence:** The paper goes beyond treating coherence as a mere mathematical constraint and argues for its connection to underlying data structure.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Density:** The theoretical analysis, particularly the proof of Theorem 1, is quite dense and requires significant mathematical background to fully grasp. A more intuitive explanation of the key ideas would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions:**  The assumption of a column-wisely unit-normed dictionary in Theorem 1 feels somewhat arbitrary. While likely simplifying the analysis, it's not immediately clear why this constraint is necessary.
* **Limited Exploration of Dictionary Learning:** The algorithm for dictionary learning is relatively straightforward.  Exploring more sophisticated dictionary learning techniques could potentially further improve performance.
* **Noise Model:** Theorem 2 addresses the noisy case, but the discussion of this is brief. More detail on the robustness of the method to different noise levels would be valuable.
* **Comparison to Other Methods:** The experiments primarily compare against RPCA. Including comparisons to more recent or advanced robust PCA methods would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.



**3. Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound, with a rigorous theoretical analysis and well-designed experiments. The claims are generally well-supported, though the theoretical details could be more accessible.
* **Clarity (7/10):**  The introduction and motivation are excellent. However, the theoretical sections require significant effort to follow. Improving the clarity of these sections would greatly enhance the paper's accessibility.
* **Originality (8/10):** While building on existing frameworks (RPCA, LRR), the authors identify a critical limitation of RPCA related to coherence and propose a novel solution. This is a significant contribution to the field.
* **Significance (8/10):**  The problem of robust PCA in the presence of coherent data is highly relevant to numerous applications. The proposed approach has the potential to improve the performance of downstream tasks relying on robust data decomposition.




**4. Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro-Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant limitation of a widely used technique (RPCA).
* Provides a theoretically grounded solution with a practical algorithm.
* Demonstrates empirical superiority over RPCA on both synthetic and real data.
* Offers a new perspective on the role of coherence in low-rank matrix recovery.

**Con-Acceptance:**

* Theoretical analysis could be more accessible.
* The dictionary learning algorithm is relatively simple.
* Limited comparison to other robust PCA methods.



**5. Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** 

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. The core ideas are strong, the theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical results are convincing. The authors should focus on improving the clarity of the theoretical sections and potentially expanding the experimental evaluation to include comparisons with a wider range of robust PCA methods. Addressing the points regarding the unit-normed dictionary assumption would also be beneficial.
