## Peer Review for "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper addresses an important problem in robust PCA – the limitations of standard RPCA when dealing with data exhibiting inherent clustering structures, leading to coherence issues. The authors convincingly argue that coherence isn’t simply a restrictive assumption but is linked to underlying data structure. They propose a Low-Rank Representation (LRR) approach with a carefully chosen dictionary to mitigate these issues, backed by theoretical analysis and experimental validation. While the theoretical contributions are somewhat incremental and rely on existing LRR framework, the insightful connection between coherence, clustering, and dictionary learning provides a valuable contribution. I believe this paper is **above average** and warrants **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary of Main Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established framework of Robust PCA (RPCA), which aims to decompose a data matrix into low-rank and sparse components. The authors identify a crucial limitation of RPCA: its performance degrades when the low-rank component exhibits coherence, often arising from clustering structures within the data.  They argue that standard RPCA effectively ignores these “extra structures” beyond low-rankness. To address this, they revisit the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) model, proposing that using a low-rank dictionary can circumvent the coherence problem. The core idea is that a judiciously chosen dictionary can effectively represent the data, avoiding the need to rely on low coherence of the underlying low-rank matrix itself.

This work relates to several areas: RPCA (Candès et al., 2009), LRR (Liu et al., 2012), and dictionary learning.  The authors correctly position their work within this context, highlighting how they build upon and extend the existing LRR framework. They also connect the work to broader concepts in matrix factorization and the Generalized PCA problem. The reference list is comprehensive and relevant.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Identification:** The authors clearly articulate the limitation of RPCA in the presence of coherent data and convincingly explain why this coherence arises from clustering structures. This is a valuable insight.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides theoretical analysis demonstrating how a low-rank dictionary can address the coherence issue in LRR. Theorem 1 offers a sufficient condition for recovery, relating dictionary properties to data characteristics.
* **Practical Algorithm:** Algorithm 1 provides a practical approach for dictionary learning in an unsupervised setting, leveraging RPCA as an initialization step.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world (motion sequence) datasets provide strong evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The improvement over RPCA in the motion sequence experiment is particularly compelling.
* **Insightful Discussion of Coherence:** The paper moves beyond treating coherence as a mere assumption and interprets it as a signal of underlying data structure. This reframing is a significant contribution.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Theoretical Contribution:** While the theoretical results are sound, they largely build upon existing LRR theory. The main novelty lies in the insightful connection between coherence, clustering, and dictionary design rather than a radical theoretical breakthrough.
* **Dictionary Learning Heuristic:** Algorithm 1 relies on a relatively simple heuristic for dictionary construction (using the RPCA solution). While effective in practice, a more sophisticated dictionary learning method might yield further improvements.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of λ and the noise level ε in the experiments are not discussed in detail. Sensitivity analysis or justification for the chosen values would strengthen the empirical evaluation.
* **Limited Scope of Theoretical Results:** The theorems focus on noiseless or small-noise settings. Extending the analysis to more realistic noise scenarios would be valuable.




**4. Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound, with clear theoretical justifications and well-designed experiments. The results are well-supported.
* **Clarity (9/10):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The notation is clear, and the concepts are explained in a comprehensive manner.
* **Originality (7/10):** The core idea of using a low-rank dictionary to address coherence is novel and insightful, although it builds on existing work on LRR.
* **Significance (8/10):** The paper addresses an important problem in robust PCA and provides a practical solution with demonstrable improvements over existing methods. The insights into the relationship between coherence and data structure are valuable and could influence future research.

**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in robust PCA.
* The authors provide a compelling argument for why coherence arises in practical datasets.
* The proposed approach (LRR with a learned dictionary) is theoretically justified and empirically validated.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical contributions are incremental and largely build upon existing LRR theory.
* The dictionary learning approach is somewhat heuristic.



**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper for publication at NIPS. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field of robust PCA by providing a practical solution to the coherence problem.  I suggest the following minor revisions:

* **Expand discussion on parameter selection:** Provide more justification for the choice of λ and ε in the experiments, or perform a sensitivity analysis.
* **Explore alternative dictionary learning methods:** Briefly discuss the potential benefits of more sophisticated dictionary learning techniques.
* **Discuss limitations of the theoretical results:** Acknowledge the limitations of the theorems (e.g., noise assumptions) and suggest directions for future research.




I believe addressing these points would further strengthen the paper and make it an even more valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
