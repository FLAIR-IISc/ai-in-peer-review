Paper summary
The paper tackles a real shortcoming of RPCA/PCP: when the low-rank component exhibits clustered (union-of-subspaces) structure, coherence (especially µ2) increases and RPCA’s guarantees and performance degrade. The authors (i) formalize and empirically illustrate this “µ2-phenomenon,” arguing coherence is an intrinsic reflection of extra structure, not a mere proof artifact; (ii) study Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a general dictionary and prove that if the dictionary A is itself low-rank and its column space contains the true column space U0, then exact/near recovery is possible without any dependence on µ2 (and with a slightly looser rank bound than PCP); and (iii) propose a simple unsupervised algorithm: run RPCA to estimate L0, form a low-rank, column-normalized dictionary from this estimate, then solve LRR to recover. Experiments on synthetic matrices and corrupted motion trajectories (Hopkins extensions) show improved recovery and downstream clustering over RPCA.

Relation to prior work
This builds on PCP/RPCA (Candès et al.) and LRR (Liu et al.). Prior LRR work has emphasized A=X and subspace segmentation; the present contribution analyzes LRR with general low-rank dictionaries for robust recovery and explicitly connects coherence growth to union-of-subspaces structure. It also provides noisy/noiseless recovery theorems that avoid µ2 under U0⊂UA and a practical two-stage RPCA→LRR pipeline.

Strengths
- Conceptual insight: Clear articulation that coherence parameters capture intrinsic structure (e.g., clustering), explaining when/why PCP falters. The “µ2-phenomenon” is both intuitive and important.
- Novel theoretical angle: Recovery guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that do not depend on µ2 (with rank(L0) ≤ O(n2/log n1) vs O(n2/(log n1)^2) for PCP), under U0⊂UA and random support corruption. Noisy stability bounds are also provided.
- Practicality: The proposed unsupervised recipe is simple, computationally modest (often only ~1.2× PCP wall-clock), and shows sizable gains on synthetic data and real motion segmentation with meaningful downstream benefits (e.g., SSC error reduced from 9.50% after PCP to 5.74% after the proposed method).
- Clarity: The paper is generally well organized; notation and conditions are made explicit; the role of normalization and λ is explained.

Weaknesses and questions
- Strength of assumptions and circularity: The key condition U0⊂UA is necessary for exact recovery but may be hard to ensure unsupervisedly. The proposed method uses PCP to approximate U0 when PCP itself is stressed precisely in the high-coherence regime. Empirically it still helps, but a theoretical perturbation analysis (subspace angle bounds translating to recovery error) would mitigate the circularity concern.
- Experimental breadth: Synthetic experiments appear to use a single clustered regime (5 subspaces) and random ±1 sparse corruption. Please stress-test across: varying number/imbalance of clusters, subspace dimensions, non-square aspect ratios, dense small noise + sparse gross corruption, and adversarial/non-uniform supports. An ablation of Algorithm 1 (e.g., removing rank truncation, varying dictionary rank) and sensitivity to λ/ε would strengthen claims.
- Baselines: Compare against methods designed to handle coherence or robust subspace recovery beyond PCP, e.g., Outlier Pursuit, REAPER/Tyler’s M-estimator variants, IRLS/nonconvex PCP, truncated nuclear norm, and LRR with A=X (reported) plus other dictionary constructions (e.g., spectral denoising of X without solving PCP first).
- Theory details: Proof sketches (e.g., dual certificate construction) are deferred; formal statements quantifying the growth of µ2 under clustering are referenced but not shown here. Clarify the corruption model (random support and arbitrary magnitudes?) and whether the results admit semi-adversarial settings. Also discuss the effect of imperfect column normalization of A and overcomplete dictionaries.
- Presentation: Some references look incomplete; ensure consistency and add recent related robust subspace recovery literature. Minor notation overload; consider a brief table of symbols and a pseudo-code box for Algorithm 1 with default hyperparameters.

Quality
The core analysis appears sound, and the conditions are transparent. The algorithm is coherent with the theory, and the empirical results are promising though limited in scope. Claims are generally measured and supported, with room for stronger baselines and broader stress tests.

Clarity
Clear and readable; figures (not included here) are referenced appropriately. Please tighten the exposition around the µ2-phenomenon with a concrete lemma, and clean up references.

Originality
Moderate-to-strong. LRR is known, but the idea to use a low-rank dictionary to avoid µ2 and the explicit linkage between coherence and clustering provide a fresh theoretical lens and a practical recipe. This goes beyond an incremental tweak to PCP.

Significance
High potential. Many real datasets are clustered; making robust low-rank recovery less sensitive to coherence is impactful for vision, imaging, and recommendation. The simplicity of the approach favors adoption.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, compelling conceptual advance (coherence-as-structure; avoiding µ2 via low-rank dictionaries)
- New recovery guarantees for LRR with general dictionaries; noisy stability
- Simple, practical algorithm with meaningful improvements on synthetic and real data
- Potentially broad impact in subspace modeling and robust recovery

Cons for acceptance
- Strong assumption U0⊂UA; unsupervised construction relies on PCP
- Experimental breadth and baselines are limited
- Missing perturbation/stability analysis for approximate dictionary span
- Some proof and reference details deferred or incomplete

Recommendation
Weak accept. The conceptual novelty and theoretical results point in a valuable direction, and the practical recipe is appealing despite limitations in evaluation. With stronger experiments and discussion of approximate-dictionary robustness, this could be a solid contribution.
