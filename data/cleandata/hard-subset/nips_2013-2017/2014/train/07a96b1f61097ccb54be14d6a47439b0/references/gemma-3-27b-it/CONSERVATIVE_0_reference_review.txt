This paper addresses the problem of robust PCA, specifically focusing on the challenges posed by data exhibiting clustering structures which lead to coherence issues in standard RPCA. The authors argue that coherence isn't an inherent limitation but a consequence of unmodeled structure and propose using a low-rank dictionary within the LRR framework to mitigate this. They provide theoretical analysis demonstrating conditions for recovery and an algorithm leveraging RPCA to learn the dictionary.

The paper is well-motivated. The issue of coherence in RPCA and its relationship to underlying data structure is a valid and important point. The idea of utilizing a learned dictionary to address this issue within the LRR framework is a reasonable approach. The theoretical results, while somewhat constrained by assumptions, provide a formal justification for the proposed method. The experimental validation on both synthetic and real motion data provides some support for the claims.

However, my assessment is largely skeptical. While the motivation is sound, the paper feels incremental and relies heavily on established techniques (RPCA, LRR). The “novelty” lies in recognizing the connection between coherence and clustering, and proposing a somewhat obvious solution (using a dictionary). This connection, while insightful, doesn't fundamentally alter the landscape of robust PCA. 

The theoretical analysis appears overly restrictive. The conditions required for recovery, particularly those concerning the dictionary’s rank and coherence, seem quite strong and may limit the practical applicability of the method. The assumption of column-wise unit-normed dictionary feels artificial. The claim that the algorithm “outperforms RPCA” needs stronger justification. The performance gains reported are not dramatic, and a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art robust PCA methods (beyond just RPCA) is crucial. The experimental setup on the motion data is also somewhat limited; the description lacks detail regarding parameter tuning and the specific subspace clustering methods used. The assertion of a 40% relative improvement feels inflated without more robust statistical analysis.

Furthermore, the paper suffers from a lack of discussion regarding computational complexity. While the authors mention the complexity of LRR, a more detailed analysis, especially in comparison to RPCA, would be helpful. The proposed algorithm, which involves first running RPCA and then LRR, is likely to be more computationally expensive than RPCA alone.

Finally, the writing quality, while adequate, could be improved. The paper is dense and contains a large number of equations and notations, some of which are not fully explained. The connection between the theoretical results and the practical implications is not always clear.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Identifies a valid limitation of RPCA (sensitivity to data coherence).
*   Proposes a reasonable approach (using a dictionary in LRR) to address the issue.
*   Provides theoretical analysis (albeit with strong assumptions) supporting the proposed method.
*   Demonstrates some performance improvement over RPCA on limited datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty; the core idea is not groundbreaking.
*   Theoretical results are heavily constrained and may limit practical applicability.
*   Experimental evaluation is limited and lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
*   Computational complexity is not adequately addressed.
*   Writing quality could be improved.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper falls short of the NIPS acceptance threshold. While the work is technically sound and addresses a relevant problem, it lacks the originality and significance to warrant acceptance. The improvements over existing methods are modest, and the theoretical constraints are significant. The paper feels more like a technical note extending existing work than a substantial contribution to the field. I recommend **rejection**. The authors might benefit from broadening the experimental evaluation, providing a more detailed complexity analysis, and relaxing some of the theoretical assumptions.
