This paper addresses the problem of recovering low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse errors, focusing on the challenges posed by data coherence, specifically when the underlying low-rank structure exhibits clustering. The authors argue that standard Robust PCA (RPCA) struggles with coherent data due to increasing coherence parameters with cluster number, and propose a Low-Rank Representation (LRR) based approach with a carefully designed dictionary to mitigate this issue. They provide theoretical analysis supporting their claims and demonstrate empirical success on both synthetic and real-world motion data.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of RPCA when dealing with coherent low-rank data, a practical concern often overlooked. The connection to clustering structures is well-explained and intuitively illustrated.
* **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides theoretical justification for the proposed LRR approach, demonstrating that avoiding coherence parameters is possible with a well-chosen dictionary.  Theorem 1, particularly, is a strong result.
* **Novelty:**  The investigation of the link between coherence, clustering, and RPCA performance is novel. The proposed dictionary learning approach in LRR, while building on existing work, represents a worthwhile extension.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results on both synthetic and motion data provide convincing evidence of the improved performance of the proposed method compared to RPCA.  The inclusion of a real-world dataset is a plus.
* **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with clear notation and a coherent flow of ideas. The inclusion of an acknowledgement section recognizing support is commendable.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Theoretical Analysis:** While Theorem 1 is a strong result, it relies on specific assumptions about the dictionary A (column-wise unit norm and containment of U0 within UA). The paper doesn't fully explore the practical implications and limitations of these assumptions.  A discussion on how easily these conditions are met in practice would be beneficial.
* **Dictionary Learning Heuristic:** The proposed dictionary learning algorithm (Algorithm 1) relies on RPCA as a first step. If RPCA struggles significantly with the given data (high coherence), the initial estimate might be poor, potentially hindering the performance of LRR. This dependency isn't thoroughly analyzed.
* **Comparison to Related Work:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison to recent advances in robust PCA and low-rank matrix completion. For example, techniques leveraging adaptive weighting schemes or non-convex optimization could be discussed and contrasted.  Specifically, work on dictionary learning beyond the simple normalization step in Algorithm 1 (e.g., learning a dictionary that directly minimizes coherence) could be mentioned.
* **Experimental Details:** The experimental setup could be more detailed.  For the motion data, more information about the parameters used for the subspace clustering methods (SIM, LRRx, SSC) would be helpful for reproducibility.  A sensitivity analysis of the parameter λ would also be beneficial.
* **Overly Strong Claims:** The phrasing "avoiding µ2" is a bit strong. The approach *mitigates* the effect of µ2 by utilizing a specific dictionary structure.  The paper occasionally overstates the impact of its contributions.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and practical problem in robust PCA.
* It provides a novel theoretical insight into the relationship between coherence, clustering, and recovery performance.
* The proposed LRR-based approach demonstrates empirical improvements over RPCA.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis relies on potentially restrictive assumptions.
* The dictionary learning algorithm has a dependency on RPCA, potentially limiting its effectiveness in challenging scenarios.
* The comparison to related work could be more comprehensive.
* Some of the claims are slightly overstated.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper with a clear contribution to the field of robust PCA. The authors identify a relevant problem, provide insightful theoretical analysis, and demonstrate promising empirical results. While there are some weaknesses, they are not severe enough to warrant rejection. The paper would benefit from addressing the limitations of the theoretical assumptions, elaborating on the dictionary learning heuristic, and providing a more thorough comparison to related work.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  Discuss the practical implications of the dictionary assumptions in Theorem 1.
2.  Analyze the sensitivity of the proposed algorithm to the initial RPCA estimate.
3.  Expand the related work section to include more recent advances in robust PCA and dictionary learning.
4.  Provide more detailed experimental setup and a sensitivity analysis of λ.
5.  Moderate the strength of certain claims to reflect the scope of the contributions more accurately.
