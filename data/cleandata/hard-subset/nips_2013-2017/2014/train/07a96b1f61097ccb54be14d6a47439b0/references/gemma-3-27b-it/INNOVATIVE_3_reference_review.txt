This paper tackles a crucial problem – the brittleness of PCA in the face of corrupted data – and proposes a nuanced extension to Robust PCA (RPCA) called Low-Rank Representation (LRR). The core idea, that coherence issues in L0 can be mitigated by a judiciously chosen dictionary *A* in LRR, is a genuinely novel and insightful contribution. While the theoretical analysis isn't exhaustive, the intuition presented regarding the relationship between coherence, clustering structure, and dictionary design is compelling. The experimental results, though not dramatically groundbreaking, support the claims and demonstrate a clear benefit over standard RPCA.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The connection between coherence, clustering, and dictionary learning in the context of low-rank recovery is a fresh perspective. It moves beyond simply addressing sparsity and focuses on the inherent structure *within* the low-rank component. This is a significant conceptual leap.
* **Insightful Analysis:** The explanation of the “µ2-phenomenon” – how clustering increases coherence – is particularly well-articulated and provides a strong motivation for the proposed approach. 
* **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The paper clearly situates itself within the established literature on RPCA, LRR, and matrix factorization.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed algorithm for dictionary construction is simple and leverages existing RPCA solutions, making it easily implementable.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real-world motion data provide supporting evidence for the effectiveness of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Completeness:** The theoretical guarantees, particularly Theorem 1, rely on strong assumptions (e.g., *U0* being a subspace of *UA*).  While these conditions are motivated, their practical verifiability is questionable. A more relaxed analysis, or discussion of how to approximate these conditions in practice, would be beneficial. The noise assumptions in Theorem 2 are also fairly standard, but a more thorough empirical evaluation under varying noise levels is warranted.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental section is somewhat limited in scope. While the results are positive, a more extensive comparison against other state-of-the-art robust PCA methods would strengthen the claims. Specifically, comparing against methods explicitly designed for clustered or coherent data would be valuable.
* **Dictionary Learning Heuristic:** The dictionary learning approach, while simple, feels somewhat heuristic. The authors acknowledge this and suggest iterative refinement as future work, but a more theoretically grounded approach to dictionary learning could be explored.
* **Notational Density:** The paper is quite dense with notation, which, while precise, can make it challenging to follow the arguments at times.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* The core idea is genuinely novel and addresses a significant limitation of existing methods.
* The paper provides a compelling explanation for why standard RPCA fails in certain scenarios.
* The proposed solution, LRR with a carefully chosen dictionary, is a reasonable and practical approach.
* The experimental results, while not overwhelming, support the theoretical claims.

**Con:**

* The theoretical guarantees rely on strong assumptions.
* The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
* The dictionary learning approach is somewhat heuristic.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and insightful approach to robust low-rank matrix recovery. While the theoretical analysis could be more complete and the experiments more comprehensive, the core idea is compelling and the potential impact of this work is significant. The paper introduces a valuable new direction for research in this area. I am willing to overlook the minor flaws and recommend **acceptance** due to the originality and potential of the work. I believe it aligns well with the NIPS focus on novel and impactful research. The authors should be encouraged to further explore the theoretical aspects of dictionary learning and to conduct a more extensive experimental evaluation in future work.
