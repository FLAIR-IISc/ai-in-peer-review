Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies recovery of a low-rank matrix L0 corrupted by sparse gross errors S0 when L0 is “coherent,” i.e., has large coherence parameters due to additional structure such as clustering of points on a union of subspaces. The authors argue that RPCA/PCP, which relies on incoherence, can underperform as the number of clusters increases (the “μ2-phenomenon”). They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a pre-constructed dictionary A, and prove recovery guarantees that purportedly avoid dependence on μ2 if A is itself low-rank and its column space contains the true column space of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA). They provide noiseless and noisy theorems under random support for S0 with bounds depending on μ1(A), not μ2(L0). Practically, they propose a two-stage unsupervised procedure: run RPCA to estimate L0, build a low-rank dictionary by normalizing/truncating this estimate, then solve LRR to obtain a refined recovery. Experiments on synthetic data and corrupted motion trajectories suggest improvements over RPCA.

Quality:
- Theory: The noiseless and noisy theorems are clear in statement and align with known PCP-style analyses; however, the central assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong. In supervised settings it is plausible; in unsupervised settings it is enforced by first running RPCA. There is no theoretical analysis of the two-stage procedure: when RPCA is degraded by high coherence, under what conditions will the RPCA estimate still provide a dictionary whose column space contains U0? As this is the key practical contribution, a formal guarantee (even under simplified models) is missing. The paper also references “the full paper” for proofs; for a conference submission, sufficient proof details or a complete supplement should be included.
- Assumptions: Randomness underlying the probability statements is not fully specified (support distribution of S0, sign/magnitude assumptions, independence from L0). Theorems depend on μ1(A), low rank of A, column normalization, and the necessary U0 ⊂ UA condition. The bound rank(A) ≤ ε^2 n2/(ca μ1(A) log n1) remains coherence dependent via μ1(A); discussion of how the constructed A will achieve small μ1 in practice is limited.
- Algorithms: The heuristic choices (rank threshold 10^-3 σ1, ε = 10^-6||X||F, column normalization) lack sensitivity analysis. Solver details, stopping criteria, and reproducibility information are not provided. The complexity claim (LRR O(n^2 rA) vs RPCA O(n^3)) depends strongly on the solver and d; empirical timing comparisons are only anecdotal.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable and well-organized, and the discussion of coherence and the μ2-phenomenon is helpful. However, reliance on a “full paper” for proofs and some missing details (randomness assumptions, exact solvers) limit reproducibility.

Originality:
The use of LRR with a general, low-rank dictionary to mitigate coherence effects is a natural extension of prior LRR/PCP ideas and relates to matrix factorization with side information. The explicit theoretical avoidance of μ2 under U0 ⊂ UA is novel to my knowledge, though similar themes (side information, partial subspace knowledge) have been explored. The two-stage “RPCA then LRR” pipeline is intuitive but not, in itself, a major algorithmic innovation without accompanying guarantees.

Significance:
The issue of coherent low-rank structures is important and arises in union-of-subspace settings. If the proposed approach could be shown to reliably improve over RPCA under realistic conditions, it would be useful. However, given the strong assumptions and limited experimental breadth, the current evidence is not yet compelling.

Strengths:
- Clear articulation of how clustering inflates μ2 and can harm PCP.
- Theoretical results showing recovery with a low-rank dictionary that contains the true subspace, including a noisy robustness statement.
- Simple practical pipeline that is easy to implement and, in reported tests, improves over RPCA.
- Useful interpretation connecting coherence to data structure and to LRR design.

Weaknesses:
- The key practical algorithm’s success depends on RPCA producing a subspace estimate close enough to U0 precisely in regimes where RPCA is claimed to struggle; no theory supports this bootstrap step.
- The probability model and assumptions underlying the theorems are not fully specified; reliance on a “full paper” for proofs is problematic.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: synthetic experiments use few trials per setting and report a “success area” without error bars; real-data evaluation uses only 11 sequences with artificial corruption (replacing missing entries by ±1) rather than proper handling of missingness; improvements, while present, are modest.
- Baselines are incomplete. Comparisons to Outlier Pursuit, R1-PCA/IRLS-type methods, reweighted PCP, GoDec/AltProj, and LRR with A = X under robust formulations are missing. Timing comparisons are not reported.
- Sensitivity to heuristic choices (rank threshold, λ, ε, normalization) is not studied. The dependence on μ1(A) and how the constructed A achieves low μ1 is not empirically demonstrated.

Questions and suggestions:
- Provide a theoretical analysis (even simplified) of the two-stage procedure: conditions under which RPCA’s estimate ensures U0 ⊂ UA and leads to improved recovery via LRR; quantify improvement in rank/sparsity regimes relative to PCP.
- Clarify the randomness/model assumptions for S0 and noise in the theorems, and include full proofs or a complete supplement.
- Add comprehensive baselines (Outlier Pursuit, reweighted PCP/IRLS, alternating projections) and report statistical significance and runtime.
- Evaluate on larger, standard benchmarks and handle missing entries properly (e.g., masked formulations) rather than imputing ±1.
- Include sensitivity analyses for λ, ε, rank thresholding, and dictionary normalization, and discuss how to choose d in practice.
- Temper the “first to study coherent recovery” claim and cite related work on side information/partial subspace knowledge.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper addresses an important issue and contains interesting theorems, but the core practical contribution rests on a circular assumption without supporting analysis, and the empirical evidence and reproducibility details are not yet strong enough for acceptance. With tighter theory for the two-stage method, clearer assumptions, and broader experiments, this work could become a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
