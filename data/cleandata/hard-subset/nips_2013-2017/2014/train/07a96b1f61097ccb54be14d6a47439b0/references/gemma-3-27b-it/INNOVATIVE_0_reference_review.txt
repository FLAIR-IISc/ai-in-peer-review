This paper tackles a crucial problem in robust PCA – the degradation of performance when the underlying low-rank structure exhibits coherence, specifically clustering. The authors correctly identify that standard RPCA struggles with such coherent data and propose a Low-Rank Representation (LRR) based approach with a carefully constructed dictionary to mitigate this issue. This is a bold and conceptually insightful direction. The core idea of linking coherence to underlying data structure, rather than treating it as a purely mathematical impediment, is a significant contribution. 

The paper is well-structured and builds a logical argument. The theoretical analysis, while not exhaustive, provides a compelling justification for the proposed approach, specifically Theorem 1. The connection to matrix factorization and the discussion around the coherence parameters (µ1, µ2, µ3) are insightful. The experimental validation, demonstrating improved performance on both synthetic and real-world motion data, strengthens the claims. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The central idea of addressing coherence through dictionary learning within the LRR framework is novel and directly addresses a known limitation of RPCA. The framing of coherence not as a necessary evil but as a symptom of underlying data structure is a valuable shift in perspective.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The provided theorems, especially Theorem 1, offer a solid mathematical foundation for the proposed approach.
*   **Practical relevance:** The application to motion sequence recovery demonstrates the practical impact of the work. The improvement over RPCA in this setting is encouraging.
*   **Clear writing:** The paper is generally well-written and the concepts are explained in a clear and understandable manner.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** The distinction between µ1, µ2, and µ3 and the discussion of the µ2-phenomenon is particularly strong.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Algorithm 1’s reliance on RPCA:** The proposed algorithm *starts* with RPCA. While the subsequent dictionary learning step aims to correct for RPCA’s shortcomings, it feels somewhat reliant on the initial, potentially flawed, estimate. Investigating alternative initialization strategies could be valuable.
*   **Limited Theoretical Scope:** Theorem 1 has a number of conditions (e.g. column-wisely unit-normed dictionary). Relaxing these conditions or exploring their impact could broaden the applicability of the theory. The conditions feel fairly strong.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experiments, while solid, are somewhat limited. Expanding the evaluation to a wider range of datasets and comparing against a more comprehensive set of state-of-the-art robust PCA methods would further strengthen the claims.
*   **Discussion of noise:** While Theorem 2 addresses the noisy case, the discussion is comparatively brief. A more in-depth analysis of the algorithm's sensitivity to different noise levels could be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a challenging problem in robust PCA.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a strong justification for the proposed method.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach in practical settings.
*   The insights regarding the relationship between coherence and data structure are valuable and contribute to a deeper understanding of the underlying principles.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on RPCA as a starting point for Algorithm 1 is a potential weakness.
*   The theoretical conditions in Theorem 1 are somewhat restrictive.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and insightful approach to robust PCA. While there are some minor weaknesses, the significant contribution of the work, particularly the conceptual framing of coherence and the proposed dictionary learning strategy, outweighs these concerns. The paper prioritizes a novel idea and provides good justification for it, fitting well with the NIPS emphasis on innovation. I believe this paper deserves acceptance. I would recommend the authors consider expanding the experimental evaluation and further investigating the theoretical limitations in a future work.
