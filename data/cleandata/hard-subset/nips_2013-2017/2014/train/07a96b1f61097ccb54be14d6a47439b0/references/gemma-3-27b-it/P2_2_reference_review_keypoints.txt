- Paper addresses RPCA's failure on coherent (clustered) data and proposes using LRR with a low-rank dictionary to mitigate coherence.  
- Authors prove theoretical guarantees that a low-rank dictionary in LRR can avoid coherence-related failures of RPCA.  
- Proposed practical algorithm builds the dictionary from an RPCA estimate; it is simple and intuitive.  
- Empirical results on synthetic and real motion data show LRR with the learned dictionary outperforms RPCA.  
- Writing and organization are clear, with well-defined notation and motivation.  
- Strength: clearly identifies a practical limitation of RPCA related to data coherence.  
- Strength: provides a rigorous-seeming theoretical analysis supporting the approach.  
- Strength: delivers a simple, effective dictionary construction algorithm.  
- Strength: convincing empirical validation, especially on motion sequence data.  
- Weakness: using LRR itself is not highly novel; novelty is mainly in the dictionary-learning application.  
- Weakness: dictionary is learned from an RPCA estimate, which may inherit RPCA’s coherence issues; robustness of this step is not analyzed.  
- Weakness: theoretical results rely on assumptions (e.g., unit-norm columns, noise levels) that may not hold in practice and lack sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: limited comparison to other recent robust matrix recovery/completion methods beyond RPCA and a basic LRR baseline.  
- Quality score: 8/10.  
- Clarity score: 8/10.  
- Originality score: 6/10.  
- Significance score: 7/10.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a practical RPCA limitation, offers theoretical and empirical support, and provides a simple effective algorithm.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited novelty, reliance on RPCA for dictionary learning, and insufficient comparisons to other methods.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, pending stronger discussion/analysis of dictionary-learning robustness, broader method comparisons, and clearer statement of novelty.
