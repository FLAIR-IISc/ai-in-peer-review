Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses robust recovery of a low-rank matrix L0 from a grossly corrupted observation X = L0 + S0, focusing on the failure mode of RPCA when L0 is coherent. RPCA (Candès et al. 2011) guarantees exact recovery under incoherence assumptions parameterized by µ(L0) = max{µ1, µ2, µ3}. The authors observe and formalize a “µ2-phenomenon”: when data exhibit cluster structure (union-of-subspaces inside the low-dimensional subspace), the second coherence parameter µ2 (row-space coherence) increases with the number of clusters, degrading RPCA performance even when rank(L0) is fixed.

They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned low-rank dictionary A: solve minZ,S ||Z||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = AZ + S. The core theoretical claim is that if A is itself low-rank and its column space contains the true column space (U0 ⊂ UA), then recovery no longer depends on µ2 (and µ3), only on µ1(A). The authors provide two theorems:
- Noiseless recovery: under column-normalized A with U0 ⊂ UA, rank(L0) ≤ rank(A) ≤ c n2/(µ1(A) log n1) and |Ω| ≤ (0.5−ε)mn, the solution is unique and exact with high probability using λ = 1/√n1.
- Noisy near-recovery: analogous conditions with dense noise yield bounds ||AZ* − L0||F ≤ 8√mn ε and ||S* − S0||F ≤ (8√mn+2)ε.

To operationalize this in unsupervised settings, the paper proposes a two-stage heuristic: (1) run RPCA, (2) build a low-rank, column-normalized dictionary A from the truncated SVD of the RPCA low-rank estimate, then (3) run LRR with that A. Experiments on synthetic matrices (200×1000) show a larger exact recovery region than RPCA (reported 47% larger), and motion segmentation on corrupted Hopkins-style sequences shows improved clustering accuracy when using the proposed recovery as preprocessing compared to RPCA preprocessing.

Relation to prior work: RPCA and matrix completion theory ([2–4], [10], [16], [23]) require incoherence. LRR was introduced in [13,14,15], with extensive study of the A = X case; this paper studies general dictionaries, linking performance to dictionary rank and coherence. The approach connects to matrix factorization ideas ([7], [20]) and robust subspace methods ([5], [6], [22], [24]).

Strengths:
- Clear articulation and empirical demonstration of the µ2-phenomenon linking clustering (union-of-subspaces) to elevated row-space coherence, which explains a practical failure mode of RPCA.
- New theoretical guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that avoid dependence on µ2 (and µ3), under the natural condition U0 ⊂ UA. The bounds allow up to roughly 50% sparse corruption and relax the rank bound versus RPCA by a log factor.
- Practical algorithm that is simple, reproducible, and uses RPCA to bootstrap a suitable dictionary; computational cost is close to RPCA and can be lower per iteration due to low-rank A.
- Empirical evidence on both synthetic data and motion segmentation that the method improves over RPCA-based recovery.

Weaknesses:
- The key assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong in unsupervised settings; the proposed construction relies on RPCA recovering a reasonably accurate L̂0. If RPCA fails badly (precisely when coherence is high), there is no formal guarantee that A will contain U0. The paper argues empirically that RPCA is “not brittle,” but no analysis is provided on how inaccurate L̂0 can be while still yielding UA that contains (or approximates) U0 sufficiently for the theorems to be effective.
- The dependence shifts from µ(L0) to µ1(A); the paper would benefit from quantifying how µ1(A) behaves when A is constructed from L̂0 with clustered columns. While µ1 concerns the left singular vectors (column space) and may be less affected by column clustering, this deserves empirical or theoretical support.
- Proof details are deferred (“see the full paper”), and some figures/reasoning (e.g., the µ2-phenomenon derivation) are only referenced. For NeurIPS, the main submission or supplementary should contain full proofs or at least detailed proof sketches.
- Experimental scope is somewhat limited: synthetic setup is convincing, but the real-data evaluation uses 11 additional sequences with synthetic corruption. It would be stronger to include more datasets and comparisons with methods tailored for union-of-subspaces with corruption (e.g., block/group-sparse RPCA variants, Outlier Pursuit [24], or robust matrix factorization baselines).
- The choice λ = 1/√n1 assumes column normalization of A; discussion of sensitivity to scaling violations and of µ1(A) estimation is minimal.

Quality:
- The problem is well-formulated; assumptions and notations are clearly stated. The theorems are plausible extensions of RPCA/LRR analysis and carefully specify conditions and constants. However, the lack of included proofs limits verifiability.
- The algorithm is clearly described and reproducible. Complexity claims are reasonable, though dependent on solver details.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and organized. Definitions of coherence parameters and their roles are clear. A few notational abuses are documented. Several key arguments are deferred to the “full paper,” which should be included/supplemented for review.

Originality:
- The identification of the µ2-phenomenon as linked to clustering is a useful perspective. The main novelty lies in the theoretical guarantee that low-rank dictionaries in LRR can neutralize µ2 dependence, and in the practical two-stage dictionary construction. This extends prior LRR work with general dictionaries.

Significance:
- Addressing RPCA’s incoherence requirement is important in practice. The proposed approach is simple and appears effective. If the theoretical and empirical claims generalize, this could influence robust subspace recovery pipelines. Current experiments suggest moderate but meaningful improvements; broader validation would increase impact.

Specific suggestions for improvement:
- Provide proof sketches for Theorems 1–2 and a formal statement/derivation of the µ2-phenomenon.
- Analyze conditions under which the RPCA-derived dictionary satisfies U0 ⊂ UA approximately, and how deviations affect recovery.
- Empirically study µ1(A) when A is learned from clustered data; include ablations with ground-truth U0-based dictionaries and iterative refinement beyond one iteration.
- Compare against additional baselines (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, robust MF, group-structured RPCA) and include larger real datasets.
- Discuss sensitivity to the rank threshold (10−3σ1), scaling of A, and λ selection when column normalization is imperfect.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a well-motivated and important limitation of RPCA.
- Solid theoretical contribution reducing dependence on µ2 via low-rank dictionaries.
- Simple, practical algorithm with empirical gains and low overhead.
- Clear exposition and reproducible procedure.

Cons against acceptance:
- Reliance on RPCA to build A lacks formal guarantees in hard regimes.
- Limited experimental scope on real data; baselines could be broader.
- Proofs and some key derivations are not included in the submission.

Recommendation: Weak Accept.
