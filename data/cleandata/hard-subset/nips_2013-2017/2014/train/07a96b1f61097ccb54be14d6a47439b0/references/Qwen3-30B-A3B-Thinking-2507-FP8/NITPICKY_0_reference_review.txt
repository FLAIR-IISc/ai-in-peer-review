

# Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper proposes an extension of the LRR framework to handle coherent data in low-rank matrix recovery. The authors argue that when data exhibits clustering structures (which increase coherence parameters), standard RPCA performance degrades, and they propose using a low-rank dictionary in LRR to avoid this issue. The paper provides theoretical guarantees and an unsupervised algorithm for constructing the dictionary.

**Strengths:**
The paper identifies a genuine limitation of RPCA (its sensitivity to coherence) that is well-motivated by the observation that real-world data often exhibits clustering structures. The theoretical analysis is generally sound, and the experimental results on synthetic data and motion sequences demonstrate promising improvements over RPCA.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Technical Inconsistencies:** The paper claims "LRR falls back to RPCA when A = I" but the LRR formulation (1.3) is X = AZ + S, while RPCA is X = L + S. When A = I, LRR becomes X = Z + S, which is not equivalent to RPCA. The authors should clarify this relationship or correct the statement.

2. **Incomplete Sentences:** The phrase "In order for LRR to avoid the coherence parameters which increase with the cluster number underlying" (Section 1) is grammatically incomplete and should be revised.

3. **Theoretical Presentation:** Theorem 1 uses inconsistent notation with "rank (L0) ≤ rank (A) ≤ ǫ2n2 / caµ1(A) log n1" instead of proper LaTeX formatting like "rank(L₀) ≤ rank(A) ≤ (ε²n₂)/(cₐμ₁(A)log n₁)". This inconsistency appears multiple times.

4. **Reference Inconsistency:** The paper cites LRR as [13], but the reference [13] is "Robust recovery of subspace structures by low-rank representation" (Liu et al., TPAMI 2013), while the original LRR paper is Liu et al. (ICML 2010). This could cause confusion for readers.

5. **Figure References:** The paper refers to "Figure 1," "Figure 2," and "Figure 3" multiple times but doesn't include these figures in the provided text, making it impossible to verify the claims about the µ2-phenomenon.

6. **Informal Language:** Phrases like "O(n2/ log n1) is probably the 'finest' bound" and "excellent quantities" are not standard academic language for a theoretical paper.

7. **Incomplete Claims:** The paper states "the variation of µ3 is mainly due to the variation of the row space, which is characterized by µ2" without providing mathematical justification or proof.

8. **Experimental Ambiguity:** The claim that "the success area of our algorithm is 47% wider than that of RPCA" (Section 4.1) lacks definition of "success area" and how it was quantified.

9. **Formatting Issues:** The reference list contains inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Arxiv" instead of "ArXiv" and extra commas in the reference for Borcea et al.).

10. **Theoretical Justification Gap:** The requirement for a column-wise unit-normed dictionary (‖Aei‖₂ = 1, ∀i) is stated as "purely for complying the parameter estimate of λ = 1/√n₁," but the paper doesn't explain why this condition is necessary for the theoretical guarantees.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper identifies a meaningful limitation of RPCA and proposes a theoretically grounded solution, the numerous minor technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and incomplete explanations significantly undermine the paper's quality. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these issues substantial enough to recommend rejection, even though the core idea has merit. The paper would benefit from careful proofreading and clarification of several key technical points before it could be considered for publication.

The authors should address the following before resubmission:
1. Correct the relationship between LRR and RPCA
2. Fix all inconsistent mathematical notation
3. Provide complete sentences and figures
4. Clarify the relationship between coherence parameters and the row space
5. Replace informal language with precise academic terminology
6. Define all ambiguous experimental metrics

These issues are not just cosmetic; they affect the paper's credibility and the ability of readers to properly understand and replicate the work. Given the high standards of NIPS, these minor but pervasive flaws make the paper unsuitable for publication in its current form.
