This paper tackles the important problem of robust PCA in the presence of corrupted data, specifically addressing limitations of standard RPCA when dealing with data exhibiting clustering structures – a common real-world scenario. The core idea – leveraging a low-rank dictionary within the LRR framework to mitigate coherence issues – is a compelling and novel contribution. While the theoretical analysis isn't exhaustive and the experimental validation could be more extensive, the potential impact of this approach warrants strong consideration.

**Summary:** The authors identify that RPCA’s performance degrades as data coherence increases, a phenomenon often linked to clustering within the low-rank component. They propose modifying the LRR framework by incorporating a low-rank dictionary to avoid the problematic coherence parameters. They provide theoretical justification for this approach and demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The connection between data coherence, clustering, and the limitations of RPCA is insightful.  Proposing a dictionary-based approach within LRR to address this issue is a genuinely new idea. This isn’t simply incremental improvement; it reconsiders the assumptions underlying RPCA.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a theoretical basis for why the dictionary-based approach avoids coherence issues. Theorem 1 offers a tangible condition for successful recovery, linking the dictionary's rank to the data characteristics.
*   **Practical Algorithm:** Algorithm 1 offers a straightforward way to implement the proposed approach, utilizing RPCA as a pre-processing step to learn the dictionary. Its relatively low computational overhead is a definite plus.
*   **Demonstrated Improvement:** The experiments, while not overly extensive, clearly show that the proposed algorithm outperforms standard RPCA, both on synthetic data and a real-world motion sequence dataset. The 40% improvement in clustering error on the motion data is a significant result.
*   **Clear Explanation of Coherence:** The authors provide a valuable discussion of the role of coherence and how it relates to data structure, moving beyond treating coherence as a mere mathematical artifact.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Completeness:**  The theoretical analysis, particularly Theorem 2 for noisy data, could be more robust.  The conditions presented seem somewhat restrictive, and a more comprehensive analysis of the impact of noise on performance would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experimental section relies heavily on a single motion dataset. Expanding the evaluation to other domains (e.g., image inpainting, video denoising) would strengthen the claims about the generalizability of the approach.
*   **Dictionary Learning Heuristic:** The use of RPCA to initialize the dictionary is a heuristic. A more systematic exploration of dictionary learning techniques, or an analysis of the sensitivity of the approach to different dictionary initialization methods, would be valuable.
*   **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Robust PCA Alternatives:** The comparison is primarily against standard RPCA.  Including comparisons to more recent and sophisticated robust PCA methods would position the work more effectively within the broader literature.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** Occasionally, the notation is dense and could benefit from clearer explanations. For example, a more intuitive explanation of the significance of parameters like *λ* could enhance understanding.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel perspective on robust PCA, addressing a limitation of existing methods.
*   The theoretical justification, while not exhaustive, provides a solid foundation for the proposed approach.
*   The experimental results, though limited in scope, are encouraging and demonstrate a clear improvement over RPCA.
*   The potential impact of this work is significant, as it offers a way to handle coherent data more effectively, which is common in many real-world applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis lacks complete rigor and generality.
*   The experimental validation could be more comprehensive.
*   The reliance on a heuristic for dictionary learning introduces some uncertainty.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant and novel contribution to the field of robust PCA. While there are some weaknesses, the originality and potential impact of the work outweigh these concerns. The approach of using a low-rank dictionary within the LRR framework is a clever idea that addresses a real limitation of existing methods. I believe this work has the potential to inspire further research in this area and make a valuable contribution to the field.

**Recommendation:** Accept. While further refinement of the theoretical analysis and expansion of the experimental validation would be beneficial, the novelty and potential impact of this work warrant acceptance to NIPS. The authors have identified a significant problem and proposed a promising solution.
