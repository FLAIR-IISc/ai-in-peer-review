## Peer Review: Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of robust low-rank matrix recovery, specifically focusing on the limitations of Robust PCA (RPCA) when dealing with data exhibiting inherent coherence – often caused by clustering structures. The authors argue that RPCA’s reliance on incoherence assumptions can hinder performance in such scenarios. They propose leveraging the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework with a carefully chosen, low-rank dictionary to mitigate the effects of coherence. They provide theoretical analysis demonstrating conditions for successful recovery with LRR and propose a practical algorithm that utilizes RPCA’s output to construct the dictionary.  Experimental results on both synthetic and motion data demonstrate that their approach outperforms standard RPCA.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly on the well-established work of RPCA (Candes et al., 2009) and LRR (Liu et al., 2013). It appropriately situates itself within the broader landscape of robust PCA techniques, referencing relevant literature (e.g., outlier pursuit, spectral methods). The connection to matrix factorization techniques is also noted, placing the work within a historical context.  The authors acknowledge and build upon the challenges identified in previous work regarding the limitations of the incoherence assumption.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** The issue of coherence in low-rank recovery is a practical concern, particularly in data with inherent structures like clustering.  Addressing this limitation is a valuable contribution.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for their approach, demonstrating conditions under which LRR with a carefully constructed dictionary can avoid the issues posed by coherence. Theorem 1 is a particularly strong result.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed algorithm, which leverages RPCA to inform dictionary learning, is a practical and reasonable approach.  The computational considerations are also addressed, showing that the overhead is manageable.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic data and a real-world motion segmentation task convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to RPCA. The results on the motion sequences are particularly compelling.
* **Clear Explanation of Coherence:** The paper does a good job of explaining the concept of coherence and its relationship to data structure (specifically clustering). The “µ2-phenomenon” is a useful insight.
* **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the presented arguments.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Dictionary Learning Heuristic:** The proposed algorithm for dictionary learning relies on the output of RPCA. While this is a reasonable heuristic, the justification for this specific approach is somewhat limited. A more in-depth analysis of the dictionary learning step could strengthen the contribution.  Is RPCA *always* the best starting point?
* **Theoretical Assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on specific assumptions (e.g., column-wise unit-normed dictionary).  The paper acknowledges this, but a discussion of the sensitivity of the results to violations of these assumptions would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison to Alternatives:**  While RPCA is a natural baseline, a comparison to more recent and sophisticated robust PCA methods (beyond the mentioned references in the introduction) could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach.
* **Theorem 2 & Noisy Data:** The discussion on noisy data (Theorem 2) feels less developed than the noiseless case. Further elaboration on the implications of noise and potential extensions to handle more complex noise models would be valuable.
* **Repetitive Arguments:** There's a degree of repetition in the discussion of the coherence parameters and their relation to clustering. Some streamlining could improve readability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in robust low-rank matrix recovery. The theoretical contributions are solid, the proposed algorithm is practical, and the experimental results are convincing. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a real-world limitation of existing robust PCA methods.
* Provides a theoretically grounded solution with demonstrable performance improvements.
* Presents a practical and efficient algorithm.
* Experimental results are compelling and validate the proposed approach.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the underlying concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The dictionary learning heuristic relies on a specific starting point (RPCA) without thorough justification.
* The theoretical assumptions might be restrictive in certain applications.
* Limited comparison to more recent robust PCA techniques.

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  The strengths of this paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses. It represents a valuable contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery and is well-suited for publication at NIPS.  I recommend the authors address the minor weaknesses (particularly the justification for the dictionary learning heuristic and the comparison to alternative methods) in a final revision.



**Confidence:** 8/10
