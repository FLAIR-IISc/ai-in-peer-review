Summary
The paper addresses robust recovery of a low-rank plus sparse decomposition X = L0 + S0 when L0 is “coherent,” notably due to clustered (union-of-subspaces) structure that increases the standard coherence parameters µ2, µ3 and degrades RPCA (PCP). The authors analyze how clustering affects µ2 (“µ2-phenomenon”), then propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a pre-specified dictionary A: minZ,S ||Z||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = AZ + S. They prove that if A is low-rank and its column space contains that of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), then exact (noiseless) or near-exact (noisy) recovery is possible without dependence on µ2, with λ = 1/√n1 and A column-normalized. Practically, they propose an unsupervised two-stage algorithm: first run RPCA to get L̂0, form a low-rank approximation L̃0 and column-normalize it to build Â, then solve LRR to produce ÂZ*. Experiments on synthetic data and a small motion segmentation benchmark suggest improved recovery and downstream clustering errors versus RPCA.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself relative to RPCA/PCP (Candès et al.) and LRR (Liu et al.). Prior work has analyzed LRR with A = X for subspace segmentation; this work focuses on matrix recovery with general (learned) A. The novelty is the theory showing µ2 can be avoided if A is low-rank and aligned with U0, and the simple RPCA-initialized construction of A.

Strengths
- Clear identification of a real failure mode of RPCA under union-of-subspaces models; helpful discussion of how µ2 scales with clustering.
- Theoretical guarantees for LRR with a low-rank dictionary that depend on µ1(A) rather than µ(L0), under explicit assumptions (U0 ⊂ UA), with both noiseless and noisy statements.
- Simple, practical two-stage procedure; empirically shows improvements over RPCA on a coherence-varying synthetic grid and on a motion segmentation setting.
- The exposition of coherence parameters and their roles is valuable and educational.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality (soundness and validation)
- The central assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong and hard to guarantee in unsupervised settings; the proposed construction of A from an RPCA estimate provides no theoretical guarantee that U0 ⊂ UA holds (especially in the regime where RPCA struggles due to coherence). Theorems 1–2 therefore do not apply to Algorithm 1 in the hardest regimes. A perturbation analysis (e.g., principal angles between U0 and UA) or conditions under which the RPCA-initialized Â suffices would strengthen the contribution.
- Dependence on λ = 1/√n1 for general A is justified via column normalization, but the derivation is not fully convincing for arbitrary dictionaries. A sensitivity analysis or a data-dependent policy would help.
- Experimental validation is limited. Synthetic study uses a single setting (200×1000, 5 subspaces) and reports a “47% wider success area” without detailed statistics; error bars and more coverage of cluster counts, subspace dimensions, and support patterns would be useful. The motion experiments use only 11 sequences and an artificial corruption model (missing entries replaced by ±1), which may not reflect realistic noise/missingness. Runtime claims are anecdotal; actual timings and implementation details are missing.
- Baselines are narrow. For matrix recovery: compare to RPCA variants aimed at coherence/structure (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, trimmed/column-sparse PCP, factorization-based robust PCA), and to LRR with A = X. For downstream clustering: compare equally tuned pipelines and report whether ÂZ* improves over simply using L̂0 from RPCA.
- Some statements seem speculative (e.g., “O(n2/log n1) is probably the finest bound”), and constants/probabilities (n−101) are unusual without context.

Clarity
- The manuscript is generally readable, but there are grammatical issues and typos (“PRCA,” “column-wisely,” “equivalently, less incoherent as equal”). The references are incomplete (missing years/venues), and some footnotes interrupt the text flow.
- The “µ2-phenomenon” would benefit from a precise quantitative statement under a union-of-subspaces generative model; figures referenced are not included here.
- Algorithm 1 details need more precision: how to handle near-zero columns when normalizing; robustness of the rank threshold; whether the iterative variant offers consistent gains; stopping criteria.

Originality
- The idea of using a learned dictionary within LRR is not entirely new, and LRR with general A has appeared before; the novelty here is the specific low-rank dictionary condition and µ2-avoidance theorems. The practical two-stage construction via RPCA is straightforward but reasonable.
- The theoretical lens on coherence and cluster-induced µ2 growth is useful, though related insights exist in subspace clustering literature.

Significance
- The problem is important, and a principled way to mitigate coherence effects could have impact. However, the strong assumption U0 ⊂ UA and modest empirical evidence limit immediate practical significance. Broader, more rigorous experiments and weaker conditions (e.g., approximate subspace inclusion bounds) would improve impact.

Questions for the authors
- Under what conditions on the RPCA estimate L̂0 can you guarantee that U0 is well captured by UÂ? Can you provide a theorem in terms of principal angles or perturbation bounds?
- How sensitive is performance to λ and to the rank threshold (10−3σ1)? Please provide ablations.
- How does ÂZ* compare numerically to L̂0 alone (not just qualitatively)? Are gains consistent and substantial?
- Can you report results on the full Hopkins155 dataset with realistic missingness/occlusion handling and on larger synthetic suites?
- How does the method compare to Outlier Pursuit/Trimmed PCP/robust MF baselines designed for coherent or structured corruptions?
- What is the computational complexity and wall-clock time relative to RPCA across problem sizes?

Pros
- Addresses a real limitation of RPCA; insightful discussion of coherence.
- Clear theoretical conditions for LRR with low-rank dictionary avoiding µ2.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with some empirical gains.

Cons
- Strong assumptions (U0 ⊂ UA) not tied to the proposed unsupervised construction.
- Limited and somewhat artificial experimental validation; missing baselines.
- Parameter choices and sensitivity insufficiently justified for general A.
- Presentation and references need polishing.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical direction is interesting and potentially meaningful, but the reliance on a strong assumption not justified by the unsupervised procedure, together with limited empirical validation and missing comparisons, make it premature for acceptance. I encourage the authors to strengthen theory for the learned dictionary, broaden and deepen experiments (including stronger baselines and ablations), and refine the exposition.
