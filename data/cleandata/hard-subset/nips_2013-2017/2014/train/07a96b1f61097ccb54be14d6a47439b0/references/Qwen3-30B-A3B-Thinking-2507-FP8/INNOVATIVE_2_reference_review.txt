

# Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper addresses a critical limitation of the widely used RPCA method: its performance degrades significantly when the low-rank component L0 exhibits clustering structures (which are ubiquitous in real data), as these structures increase coherence parameters (specifically µ2 and µ3) that RPCA depends on. The authors make a bold and novel contribution by showing that coherence parameters are not merely theoretical assumptions but are intrinsically tied to extra structures in the data (like clustering), and they propose a fundamentally new approach to circumvent this issue.

The main novelty lies in their insight that LRR (Low-Rank Representation) can avoid coherence parameters when the dictionary A is low-rank and contains the column space of L0. This leads to Theorem 1, which proves that LRR with such a dictionary can exactly recover L0 and S0 without depending on µ2. The authors further develop a practical unsupervised algorithm (Algorithm 1) that uses RPCA to construct the dictionary for LRR, which outperforms RPCA on both synthetic and real-world motion data. Their experiments show a 47% wider success area for their method compared to RPCA on synthetic data and a 40% relative improvement in clustering error rates on motion sequences.

The paper's most significant contribution is the conceptual breakthrough: coherence parameters are not "assumptions" but rather reflect meaningful data structures (like clustering). This insight fundamentally changes how we understand low-rank recovery problems and provides a new lens to interpret existing work. The authors also make the important point that while RPCA is brittle to coherent data, it's not useless for such data, which informs their practical algorithm design.

The paper builds on prior work (RPCA, LRR) but makes a clear conceptual leap beyond incremental improvements. While the LRR framework was previously explored (Liu et al., 2013), the authors provide a new theoretical understanding of its behavior with coherent data and a novel dictionary learning strategy. Their theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) are significant as they provide a looser rank bound (O(n2/log n1)) than RPCA's O(n2/(log n1)2) requirement.

The experimental validation is thorough, with both synthetic tests (systematically varying rank and sparsity) and real-world motion data from the Hopkins155 database. The results consistently show that their approach outperforms RPCA, particularly when data exhibits clustering structures (which increase coherence parameters).

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: Recognizing coherence parameters as reflecting data structures (not just theoretical assumptions)
- Theoretical contribution: Proving LRR with low-rank dictionaries can avoid coherence parameters
- Practical impact: Developing a simple unsupervised algorithm that outperforms RPCA
- Clear exposition: Well-organized with helpful figures (Figures 1-4) that illustrate key concepts
- Significance: Addresses a real limitation of a widely used method with broad applicability

Weaknesses:
- The paper assumes the dictionary A contains the column space of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), which in unsupervised settings requires the algorithm to implicitly learn this relationship (though Algorithm 1 does address this)
- The theoretical analysis focuses on noiseless and noisy settings but doesn't fully address the computational complexity of dictionary learning
- While the paper shows the coherence-structure relationship, it could better quantify the "clustering" parameter and its relationship to coherence

Despite these minor limitations, this paper makes a significant conceptual contribution that goes beyond incremental improvements to RPCA. The insight that coherence parameters reflect real data structures (not just theoretical constraints) is profound and opens new directions for low-rank matrix recovery. The proposed approach is both theoretically grounded and practically effective, with clear advantages over the current state-of-the-art.

This work is highly novel and addresses a fundamental limitation in a widely used technique. It provides both a new theoretical understanding and a practical algorithm that significantly outperforms RPCA in the presence of coherent data (which is common in real applications). The paper is well-written, theoretically sound, and experimentally validated.

I recommend accepting this paper. The conceptual novelty is substantial, the theoretical contribution is significant, and the practical impact is clear. This work advances the field by revealing a fundamental relationship between coherence parameters and data structures, and provides a principled approach to handling coherent data that was previously unaddressed. This is exactly the kind of bold, novel contribution that NIPS should celebrate.
