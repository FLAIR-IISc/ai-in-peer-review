Summary
The paper studies recovery of a low-rank matrix L0 from grossly corrupted observations X = L0 + S0 (and its noisy variant), focusing on “coherent” L0 where classical RPCA/PCP degrades due to large coherence parameters. The authors empirically and conceptually identify a µ2-phenomenon: when columns of L0 come from a union of multiple subspaces (clustering), the second coherence parameter µ2(L0) grows with the number of clusters, harming RPCA guarantees. They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned low-rank dictionary A: minimize ||Z||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = AZ + S. Their main theoretical result shows that, if A is low-rank and its column space contains that of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), LRR achieves exact (noiseless) or near-exact (noisy) recovery without dependence on µ2(L0), with rank bounds O(n2/log n1) involving only µ1(A). They then give a practical unsupervised algorithm: run RPCA to estimate L0, truncate to estimated rank, column-normalize to make a dictionary Â, and run LRR with A = Â. On synthetic data with clustered subspaces, the method expands the success region over RPCA by 47%. On corrupted motion sequences (Hopkins-style), preprocessing by their recovery improves downstream subspace clustering error further over RPCA.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on RPCA/PCP (Candès et al.), coherence-based conditions (Candès–Recht), and LRR (Liu et al.) which is well-studied for A = X in subspace clustering. The novelty here is analyzing LRR with general dictionaries and showing that a properly chosen low-rank A can “avoid” µ2 dependence in recovery guarantees. The idea connects to matrix factorization and to robust subspace learning. Related robust PCA variants such as Outlier Pursuit/column-sparse models, reweighted/weighted PCP, and nonconvex/alternating methods are relevant baselines for experiments and discussion.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of a real failure mode of RPCA (coherent, clustered data) and its connection to µ2; useful to the community.
- Theoretical results for LRR with general low-rank dictionaries that remove µ2(L0) from the bounds under U0 ⊂ UA, with matching noisy stability bounds.
- A simple, practical two-stage algorithm that leverages existing RPCA implementations; computationally efficient when A is low-rank.
- Empirical evidence on synthetic clustered subspaces and motion data showing consistent improvements over RPCA.
- Insightful discussion about the physical meaning of coherence, relating it to extra structure beyond low-rankness (e.g., clustering).

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumption U0 ⊂ UA: The central guarantees require the dictionary’s column space to contain the true subspace. This is a strong assumption in the unsupervised setting. The proposed algorithm constructs Â from the RPCA estimate, but the theory does not cover this learned Â. If RPCA is inaccurate in the coherent regime (precisely the target), Â may not satisfy U0 ⊂ UÂ. Please quantify how recovery degrades when UA only approximates U0 (e.g., bounds in terms of principal angles between subspaces) and provide empirical sensitivity to subspace error.
- Randomness/identifiability assumptions: Theorems state probability guarantees but the paper does not clearly specify the randomness model (uniform support of Ω? random signs? independence?); this is crucial to interpret the results and compare to PCP theory. Please make the distributional assumptions explicit.
- Role of µ1(A): The recovery bounds depend on µ1(A). When A is formed by normalizing columns of a coherent estimate L̂0, why should µ1(A) be small? Some intuition suggests µ1(A) can still be large for coherent data. Provide guidance or analysis on how the proposed truncation/normalization leads to favorable µ1(A), or propose a dictionary learning step that controls µ1(A) (e.g., column sampling, whitening, or adding spectral regularization).
- Comparisons: Experimental baselines are limited to RPCA. Consider including Outlier Pursuit (column-sparse corruptions), reweighted/weighted RPCA designed for nonuniform sampling, and scalable nonconvex robust PCA (e.g., AltProj/GoDec) to strengthen claims of practical superiority. Also report LRR with A = X as a recovery method (not only as a clustering affinity) for completeness.
- Experimental scope: Synthetic experiments fix k = 5 clusters while varying rank; to support the µ2-phenomenon more directly, sweep the number of clusters k while keeping total rank fixed and report coherence parameters and success rates. The motion experiment uses 11 sequences and a synthetic corruption model; include reconstruction metrics (relative error on known entries), ablations on missing fractions and noise, and runtime comparisons.
- Parameter choices: The paper sets λ = 1/√n1 in all cases. For LRR with a general A, λ also depends on column norms and sparsity fraction. Provide sensitivity studies and practical tuning guidance.
- Algorithmic details: The normalization step could leak sparse corruption from X into Â when RPCA is imperfect. Consider robust column selection (trimmed columns, leverage-score sampling) or projecting onto the estimated column space (ÛÛT X) before normalization, and evaluate the impact.
- Clarity/minor: Some notation/footnotes are truncated; define n1, n2 once and stick to them. Figures referred to (1–4) are essential to understanding but not present; ensure these clearly show µ2 growth and success regions. Provide solver details and stopping criteria for both convex programs to enhance reproducibility.

Quality
Theoretical contributions appear sound and technically nontrivial; however, proofs are deferred. Empirical results are promising but would benefit from broader baselines, clearer protocols, and robustness analyses. The paper is generally careful and honest about the scope.

Clarity
Well-written and organized, with helpful notation and motivation. Please clarify assumptions, add missing figure details, and improve the discussion around µ1(A) and the unsupervised dictionary guarantees.

Originality
The focus on coherent data and the analysis of LRR with low-rank dictionaries that remove µ2 dependence is novel and interesting beyond prior LRR work with A = X.

Significance
Addresses a real and common failure mode in robust low-rank recovery. If strengthened as suggested, the results can influence practice in subspace clustering, vision, and beyond.

Recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on clarifying assumptions and strengthening experiments. Pros: clear problem, meaningful theory, simple and effective algorithm. Cons: reliance on strong dictionary assumptions without end-to-end guarantees for the learned dictionary, limited baselines/analyses. Confidence: high.
