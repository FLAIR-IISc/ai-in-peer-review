This paper addresses the problem of recovering low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse errors, specifically focusing on scenarios where the underlying low-rank matrix exhibits coherence due to clustering structures. The authors argue that standard Robust PCA (RPCA) struggles with such coherent data and propose a Low-Rank Representation (LRR) approach with a carefully designed dictionary to mitigate this issue. They provide theoretical analysis supporting their approach and demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments on both synthetic and real-world motion data.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of RPCA when dealing with coherent low-rank matrices and motivates the need for alternative approaches.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The authors provide a theoretical analysis connecting the coherence of the low-rank matrix to its underlying structure (clustering) and demonstrate conditions under which LRR with a suitable dictionary can avoid the issues associated with coherence. Theorem 1, in particular, is a strong result.
* **Practical Algorithm:**  The proposed algorithm builds on existing techniques (RPCA and LRR) and offers a practical way to leverage the benefits of LRR for coherent data. The algorithm is relatively straightforward to implement.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real motion data provide compelling evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach compared to RPCA. The performance improvement on the motion data is significant.
* **Connection to Related Work:** The paper effectively connects its work to existing literature on RPCA, LRR, and matrix factorization, and highlights the novel contributions. The discussion around the "µ2-phenomenon" is insightful.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Notationally Dense:** The paper employs a substantial amount of notation, which can make it challenging to follow for readers unfamiliar with the field. A more concise notation could improve readability.
* **Theorem Conditions:** The conditions in Theorem 1, while theoretically sound, might be restrictive in practice. The requirement of knowing (or estimating) the rank of the underlying matrix and the condition PUA(U0) = U0 could be difficult to satisfy in truly unsupervised settings.  A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to violations of these conditions would be beneficial.
* **Limited Exploration of Dictionary Learning:**  While the paper proposes using the output of RPCA to construct the dictionary, the discussion on alternative dictionary learning strategies is limited. Exploring other methods for dictionary learning could potentially lead to further performance improvements.  The heuristics used in Algorithm 1 for updating the dictionary could be justified more thoroughly.
* **Discussion of Computational Complexity:** While the paper mentions the computational complexity in relation to RPCA, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed algorithm, particularly the dictionary learning step, would be helpful.
* **Overly Strong Claims:** The phrasing in the abstract and introduction, using terms like "ultimate solution" and "perfect recovery", feels overly strong given the theoretical conditions and practical limitations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in robust matrix recovery.
* The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the relationship between data structure and recovery performance.
* The proposed algorithm offers a practical improvement over existing methods.
* The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
* The paper is well-written (despite some notational density) and makes a clear contribution to the field.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The theoretical conditions for success might be too restrictive for practical applications.
* The dictionary learning strategy is relatively simple and could benefit from further exploration.
* The paper lacks a detailed computational complexity analysis.
* Some of the claims are overly strong.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery. The authors clearly identify a limitation of existing methods, provide a theoretical justification for their proposed approach, and demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding notation, theoretical conditions, dictionary learning, and the strength of the claims. Specifically, the authors should consider clarifying the practical implications of the theorem's conditions and exploring more sophisticated dictionary learning techniques.
