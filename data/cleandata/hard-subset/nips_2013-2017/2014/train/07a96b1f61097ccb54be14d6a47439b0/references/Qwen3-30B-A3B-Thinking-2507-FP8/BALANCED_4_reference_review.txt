

# Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper addresses a significant limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) when applied to coherent data, particularly data with clustering structures. The authors identify that the widely used RPCA method (based on nuclear norm minimization) fails to recover low-rank matrices when the second coherence parameter μ₂ increases with the number of clusters in the data. They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary matrix A (satisfying U₀ ⊂ U_A) as a solution to this problem. The key contribution is the theoretical proof that LRR with a low-rank dictionary can avoid the coherence parameters that degrade RPCA performance, along with an unsupervised algorithm that constructs the dictionary using RPCA's output.

The paper relates well to previous work, particularly the RPCA framework (Candès et al., 2011) and LRR (Liu et al., 2013). It extends these works by identifying a specific scenario (clustering structures) where RPCA's assumptions are violated and providing a solution within the LRR framework. The authors also clarify the physical meaning of coherence parameters, showing they are not arbitrary assumptions but reflect intrinsic data structures.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear identification of a real limitation:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that clustering structures (ubiquitous in real data) lead to high coherence parameters, which degrade RPCA performance. The "μ₂-phenomenon" is well-documented with both theoretical analysis and experiments.

2. **Practical algorithm:** The proposed unsupervised algorithm (Algorithm 1) is simple, effective, and only marginally more expensive than RPCA (1.2x computational cost). The experimental results on synthetic data (Figure 4) and real motion sequences (Table 1) convincingly show 40% relative improvement over RPCA in clustering error rates.

3. **Theoretical contribution:** The theorems (1 and 2) establish that LRR with a low-rank dictionary avoids the coherence constraints that limit RPCA. The analysis of the relationship between dictionary properties and recovery performance is insightful.

4. **Well-structured presentation:** The paper is clearly written with appropriate mathematical notation and well-chosen figures that illustrate key concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental theoretical contribution:** While the connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters is novel, the core solution (using LRR with low-rank dictionaries) builds directly on Liu et al.'s LRR work. The paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison to related approaches like robust subspace clustering methods (e.g., [14, 19]) that also address coherent data.

2. **Limited scope of improvement:** The paper demonstrates significant improvement only when data exhibits clear clustering structures. For data without such structures (where RPCA already performs well), the proposed method offers minimal benefits. The paper doesn't adequately address this limitation or provide guidance on when the proposed method should be used versus RPCA.

3. **Lack of theoretical depth on dictionary learning:** The paper claims the dictionary A can be learned from RPCA's output, but the theoretical justification for this approach is somewhat superficial. The paper doesn't analyze how well the RPCA output approximates the true subspace U₀, which is critical for the dictionary to satisfy U₀ ⊂ U_A.

4. **Experimental scope:** While the Hopkins155 database experiments are well-chosen, the synthetic data experiments could have included more diverse data models (e.g., with different cluster sizes or distributions) to better demonstrate the method's robustness.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to robust matrix recovery by identifying a practical limitation of RPCA and providing an effective solution for a specific but important class of data (coherent data with clustering structures). The theoretical insights about coherence parameters are valuable, and the proposed algorithm is well-motivated and effective in experiments.

I recommend acceptance, but with the following suggestions for improvement:
1. Strengthen the theoretical foundation by providing a more rigorous analysis of the dictionary learning process in Algorithm 1.
2. Expand the experimental section to include comparisons with other robust PCA methods that handle coherent data (e.g., [22, 23]).
3. Provide more detailed guidance on when to use the proposed method versus RPCA, including a discussion of the trade-offs.
4. Clarify the relationship between the clustering structures and the coherence parameters with a more detailed theoretical argument.

The paper addresses a genuine problem in robust matrix recovery with a practical solution that advances the state of the art for a specific but important data regime. While the theoretical contribution is somewhat incremental, the practical impact and clear presentation make this a strong candidate for NeurIPS.
