This paper tackles a crucial problem in robust PCA: the degradation of performance when the underlying low-rank structure exhibits coherence, particularly clustering. The authors correctly identify that standard RPCA struggles in these scenarios due to increasing coherence parameters and propose a solution based on leveraging a low-rank dictionary within the LRR framework. This is a genuinely novel and insightful contribution. While the theoretical analysis feels a bit terse and could benefit from further elaboration, the core idea—that carefully constructed dictionaries can circumvent coherence issues—is compelling and potentially impactful.

The strength of this work lies in its clear identification of a limitation of RPCA and a surprisingly simple, yet effective, approach to mitigate it. The connection drawn between coherence and underlying data structure (clustering) is a significant conceptual leap, moving beyond treating coherence as merely a nuisance parameter. The theoretical result, while presented concisely, establishes a sufficient condition for avoiding coherence issues using a low-rank dictionary. The empirical validation, using both synthetic and real-world motion data, is convincing and demonstrates a clear improvement over RPCA. The algorithm presented is straightforward and builds upon existing methods, making it readily implementable.

However, the paper isn't without its shortcomings. The theoretical treatment, particularly Theorem 1, relies on several conditions that might be restrictive in practice. A more thorough discussion of the practical implications of these conditions, and potential strategies for relaxing them, would be beneficial. The explanation of why *specifically* low-rank dictionaries are effective could be more detailed – while the connection to subspace embedding is implied, explicitly stating it would strengthen the argument. The experimental section, while solid, could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison to other robust PCA variants beyond RPCA. A more detailed ablation study examining the impact of different dictionary construction methods would also be valuable. The paper’s writing, while generally clear, occasionally suffers from density and could be streamlined for better readability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Addresses a significant limitation of RPCA and proposes a genuinely new approach based on dictionary learning. The connection between coherence and data structure is insightful.
*   **Significance:** The problem of handling coherent low-rank data is important and impacts many applications. This work offers a practical solution with demonstrable improvements.
*   **Potential Impact:** The idea of leveraging dictionaries to overcome coherence issues could inspire further research in robust PCA and related areas.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical results, while concise, provide a foundation for the proposed approach, and the empirical results are compelling.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Depth:** The theoretical analysis is somewhat terse and could benefit from further elaboration and discussion of the conditions' practical implications.
*   **Experimental Breadth:** The comparison to other robust PCA methods is limited.
*   **Writing Clarity:** While generally clear, the paper occasionally suffers from density.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor shortcomings, this paper presents a novel and potentially significant contribution to the field of robust PCA. The authors have identified a crucial limitation of existing methods and proposed a surprisingly simple, yet effective, solution. The core idea is compelling, and the empirical results are convincing. I believe the novelty and potential impact of this work outweigh its minor flaws. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the comments regarding theoretical elaboration and experimental breadth. I am prioritizing the originality and potential impact over absolute methodological perfection.
