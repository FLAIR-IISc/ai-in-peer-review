This paper addresses the problem of recovering low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse noise, focusing on the impact of data coherence on the performance of Robust PCA (RPCA) and proposing a Low-Rank Representation (LRR) approach with a learned dictionary to mitigate these issues. While the motivation – the limitations of RPCA with coherent data – is relevant and the proposed idea of leveraging dictionary learning within the LRR framework is conceptually sound, the paper suffers from a multitude of shortcomings in presentation, technical detail, and justification, ultimately leading to a recommendation for rejection.

The introduction is verbose and repetitive. The initial paragraphs rehash well-known concepts of RPCA without adding significant insight. The transition to the problem of coherent data is abrupt, and the explanation of why coherence poses a challenge is superficial. The claim of being the “first” to study coherent low-rank recovery feels overstated; the connection between clustering and coherence, while intuitively plausible, lacks rigorous justification within the introduction itself. The contribution list is overly enthusiastic and self-aggrandizing. Phrases like "elementary criteria" and “promising results” are vague and unsupported.

The notation section, while attempting thoroughness, introduces unnecessary complexity. The abundance of different matrix norms, while potentially relevant, isn't consistently utilized throughout the paper. The abuse of notation (using symbols for both matrices *and* the spaces they span) is confusing and prone to misinterpretation. 

Section 3, the core of the paper, is riddled with issues. The derivation of Theorem 1 is presented without sufficient detail. Crucially, the justification for the column-wise unit norm constraint on the dictionary A feels ad-hoc and lacks a clear theoretical basis. The explanation of why `PUA(U0) = U0` is necessary, while present, is somewhat buried and requires deeper elaboration. The conditions of Theorem 1 appear stringent (specifically the dependency on `log n1`) and their practical implications aren’t adequately discussed. The noise analysis in Theorem 2 is also rather brief; the recovery guarantees seem optimistic given the complexity of the problem. The connection between the µ2-phenomenon and the proposed solution isn't clearly established—the paper asserts that a low-rank dictionary avoids µ2, but doesn’t offer a compelling explanation of *how* the LRR framework achieves this avoidance in terms of the underlying optimization problem. The proposed Algorithm 1 is also not novel, it is essentially a sequential application of RPCA followed by LRR, and the justification for the specific parameter choices (λ = 1/ √ n1) feels arbitrary.

The experimental section is also problematic. The synthetic experiments, while demonstrating an improvement over RPCA, lack a thorough statistical analysis. The improvement of 47% in the success area is a bold claim that needs more detailed validation (e.g., confidence intervals, statistical significance tests). The motion sequence experiments are more compelling, but the reliance on existing subspace clustering methods as evaluation metrics obscures the true impact of the proposed matrix recovery technique. The authors do not evaluate the dictionary learning process itself; a visualization or analysis of the learned dictionary Â would provide valuable insight.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The motivation of addressing the limitations of RPCA with coherent data is relevant.
*   The idea of leveraging dictionary learning within the LRR framework is conceptually reasonable.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The presentation is verbose, repetitive, and lacks clarity.
*   Technical details are often missing or insufficiently justified.
*   The theoretical analysis (Theorems 1 & 2) lacks rigor and relies on ad-hoc assumptions.
*   The experimental evaluation is incomplete and lacks statistical significance.
*   The novelty of Algorithm 1 is limited.
*   The paper overstates its contributions and uses exaggerated language.
*   Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies throughout the manuscript. (e.g., inconsistent use of notation, grammatical errors)

**Overall:**

This paper presents an interesting idea but falls short of NIPS standards due to its lack of technical depth, clarity, and rigorous evaluation. While the authors identify a potential weakness in RPCA, the proposed solution isn’t adequately justified or validated. The numerous minor flaws detract from the overall quality of the work. Therefore, I recommend rejection. A major revision addressing the aforementioned points—with significantly more theoretical rigor, detailed experimental analysis, and a more concise presentation—would be necessary for reconsideration.
