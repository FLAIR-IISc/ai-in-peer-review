This paper addresses the problem of recovering low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse errors, focusing on the challenges posed by data coherence arising from underlying cluster structures. While the motivation is sound – RPCA’s limitations with coherent data are well-known – and the exploration of the relationship between coherence and data structure is potentially valuable, the paper suffers from a multitude of deficiencies, ranging from imprecise definitions and unconvincing theoretical claims to inconsistencies in notation and a lack of rigorous experimental validation. Consequently, I recommend rejection.

**Summary of Main Ideas:** The authors argue that RPCA struggles with data exhibiting clustering structures due to increased coherence. They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully constructed, low-rank dictionary to mitigate this issue. They present theoretical results demonstrating conditions for recovery and an algorithm leveraging RPCA to estimate the dictionary.

**Strengths:**
* **Motivation:** The issue of coherence impacting RPCA performance is a valid and relevant concern.
* **LRR Connection:** Exploring LRR as an alternative framework is a reasonable direction.
* **Algorithm Idea:** The approach of using RPCA to initialize the dictionary for LRR is conceptually straightforward.

**Weaknesses (Extensive):**

1. **Imprecise Definitions & Lack of Rigor:** The definition of “coherence” is overly reliant on existing definitions [3,4] without offering a truly novel perspective. The connection between the three coherence parameters (µ1, µ2, µ3) and the clustering structure is asserted but not rigorously demonstrated. Figure 2, while suggestive, is insufficient as proof and lacks detailed explanation of the parameter settings and experimental setup. The “µ2-phenomenon” is presented as a significant insight, but the analysis lacks depth and doesn’t fully explain *why* µ2 increases with the cluster number beyond a hand-wavy explanation.
2. **Theoretical Claims & Assumptions:** Theorem 1 relies on several restrictive assumptions, including a column-wisely unit-normed dictionary and the condition PUA(U0) = U0. The significance and practical implications of this last condition are not fully discussed. The condition `rank (L0) ≤ O(n2/ logn1)` is stated as looser than RPCA’s condition, but a detailed comparison with the precise RPCA bound from [4] is missing.  Theorem 2, addressing noisy data, introduces an additional parameter ε without fully justifying its role or providing guidelines for its selection.
3. **Notational Inconsistencies & Errors:** The notation is often unclear and inconsistent. For instance, the use of ‘A’ to represent both a dictionary matrix and a general matrix within proofs is confusing.  The definition of the norms is exhaustive but unnecessary, bloating the paper. The statement “SVD is always skinny SVD” is an unnecessary restriction and its impact isn’t explained.
4. **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, while attempting to provide empirical support, are insufficient. The random matrix experiment (Section 4.1) reports a “47% wider success area” without clearly defining the success area or providing confidence intervals. The Hopkins155 dataset experiments (Section 4.2) lack details regarding the parameter tuning for the subspace clustering methods.  Simply stating that the clustering *performs better* is not enough; a quantitative comparison with statistical significance is required.
5. **Related Work:** While a list of references is provided, the discussion of related work is superficial. The connection to matrix factorization (mentioned as an "idea") feels tacked on and doesn't contribute meaningfully to the understanding of the proposed approach.  The work doesn't adequately differentiate itself from other LRR-based methods or recent advancements in robust PCA.
6. **Clarity and Writing Quality:** The writing is often convoluted and verbose.  The arguments are not always logically structured, making it difficult to follow the authors’ reasoning.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**For:**
* Addresses a relevant problem in robust PCA.
* Proposes a potentially viable approach using LRR.
* Includes experimental validation (although weak).

**Against:**
* Theoretical claims are weakly supported and rely on restrictive assumptions.
* Experimental results are insufficient to draw strong conclusions.
* Numerous notational inconsistencies and a lack of clarity.
* Superficial discussion of related work.
* Overall, the paper lacks the technical rigor and polish expected of a NIPS submission.



**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting idea but falls short of delivering a convincing contribution. The theoretical foundations are shaky, the experimental validation is inadequate, and the presentation is marred by numerous flaws. While the motivation is reasonable, the paper, in its current form, does not meet the quality standards for acceptance at NIPS. The authors would need to significantly revise the paper, addressing the weaknesses outlined above, to make it a viable candidate for publication.
