Summary
The paper studies recovery of a low-rank matrix L0 from grossly corrupted observations X = L0 + S0 (and optionally dense noise), focusing on the practically important case where L0 is coherent (i.e., exhibits clustered subspace structure). The authors argue that standard RPCA/PCP succeeds under incoherence and degrades as the number of clusters increases because the second coherence parameter µ2 grows (“µ2-phenomenon”). They advocate Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary A: solve minZ,S ||Z||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = AZ + S. The main theoretical result shows that if A is low-rank and its column space contains that of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), exact recovery is possible without any dependence on µ2 (and only µ1(A) appears), under standard random-sparsity assumptions and column-normalized A. An analogous stability theorem is given for noisy data. To construct A in unsupervised settings, they propose a simple two-stage algorithm: apply RPCA to estimate L0, truncate its SVD to get a well-conditioned low-rank approximation, normalize columns to form A, then run LRR. Experiments on synthetic clustered subspaces with entrywise corruptions, and on corrupted motion trajectories (Hopkins-style sequences), show significant improvements over RPCA as measured by exact recovery on synthetic data and downstream clustering error.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on RPCA/PCP (Candès et al., 2011) and related low-rank recovery literature, which require incoherence (µ) bounds that implicitly penalize clustered subspaces.
- Connects to LRR (Liu et al.), but prior guarantees mainly focus on A = X; this paper analyzes LRR with general low-rank dictionaries, providing conditions that decouple from µ2.
- Related to robust subspace clustering (SIM, SSC, LRRx), matrix factorization/rank minimization, and coherence/incoherence notions in matrix completion. The paper argues the coherence parameters encode real structural properties (clustering) rather than mere proof artifacts.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Coherent/clustered data are ubiquitous (vision, trajectories, documents), and the µ2-phenomenon is a real failure mode for PCP/RPCA.
- Theory: Clear sufficient conditions for exact and stable recovery with LRR using a low-rank dictionary; notably avoids µ2 dependence and gives looser rank bounds than PCP. The requirement U0 ⊂ UA is natural if a good dictionary is available.
- Algorithmic insight: A simple, practical pipeline to learn A from data by bootstrapping off RPCA, with good empirical gains; complexity discussion suggests the second stage can be cheaper than RPCA when A is low-rank.
- Empirical evidence: Extensive synthetic study varying rank and corruption rate, and motion segmentation results demonstrating improved downstream accuracy across multiple clustering methods.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Circularity/robustness of dictionary learning: The unsupervised algorithm critically relies on RPCA to estimate L0 well enough that U0 ⊂ UA holds. When µ2 is large enough to cause RPCA to fail badly, will the learned A still satisfy the required subspace inclusion? Some theoretical or empirical analysis of this failure mode would strengthen the story (e.g., success probability of Algorithm 1 vs RPCA as a function of cluster number and sample density).
- Assumptions on S0: Theorems appear to rely on random, uniformly distributed support with bounded fraction |Ω|/(mn). Many practical corruptions/occlusions are structured (block/missing patterns). Please clarify the stochastic assumptions and discuss robustness to structure (e.g., column-sparse outliers, contiguous occlusions).
- µ2-phenomenon characterization: The increase of µ2 with number of clusters is shown empirically. A formal bound connecting µ2 to the number of subspaces, their dimensions, and sampling per subspace (even under simple random models) would greatly enhance the contribution.
- Noisy setting evaluation: The noisy theorem is presented, but experiments mainly use gross corruptions and missing entries imputed with ±1. Consider evaluating with actual dense noise to support Theorem 2, and/or use projection operators for missing data rather than imputation, which can be adversarial.
- Motion data preprocessing: Replacing missing entries by ±1 is unusual and may bias results. A matrix completion-with-corruptions formulation (using PΩ) would be more standard and fair. Also report exact ranks, subspace counts, and corruption statistics per sequence.
- Sensitivity and ablations: The algorithm fixes λ = 1/√n1 and uses a singular-value threshold of 10^-3σ1. Report sensitivity to these choices, impact of column normalization, and alternatives to one-shot vs iterative refinement. Provide runtime measurements to substantiate the 1.2× claim.
- Comparison baselines: Consider also PCP variants tailored to different corruption models (e.g., Outlier Pursuit for column-sparse outliers) and factorized/nonconvex RPCA to strengthen the empirical comparison.
- Reproducibility: Include proofs in the appendix/supplement, and enough details (code, parameter settings, data splits) to reproduce the figures and table.

Quality
The core theoretical claims are plausible and well-aligned with established analysis techniques for PCP/LRR; the shift from µ(L0) to µ1(A) when U0 ⊂ UA is insightful and technically meaningful. However, important proof details are deferred to the “full paper”; for NeurIPS, these should be in the supplement. The empirical evaluation supports the paper’s main claims but would benefit from more rigorous and standard setups, sensitivity studies, and stronger baselines.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized, with clear notation and good motivation. Minor issues: a few typos (e.g., “PRCA”), footnote formatting, and references missing full publication details. It would help to more explicitly state the randomness assumptions and provide an intuitive discussion of the subspace inclusion condition and its verifiability.

Originality
The identification of µ2 as a bottleneck in clustered settings is known qualitatively, but the paper’s theoretical guarantee for LRR with a general low-rank dictionary that avoids µ2 is, to my knowledge, novel and useful. The simple bootstrapping strategy to learn the dictionary is practical, though conceptually incremental.

Significance
Addressing coherent data is important for robust low-rank recovery and for downstream tasks like subspace clustering. The results can influence both theory (how to leverage dictionaries to relax incoherence) and practice (a simple pipeline that often improves PCP). With stronger empirical backing and clearer analysis of the learned dictionary’s validity, the impact could be substantial.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a formal characterization or bound of µ2 as a function of number of clusters, dimensions, and samples?
- Under what conditions on RPCA’s output does Algorithm 1 guarantee U0 ⊂ UA with high probability?
- How does the method perform with structured (non-i.i.d.) corruptions or missingness modeled via PΩ rather than imputation?
- Sensitivity to λ and the SVD truncation rule?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a real, underappreciated failure mode of RPCA with a clear theoretical advance.
- Simple, actionable algorithm with promising gains.
- Connections between coherence and data structure are insightful.

Cons for acceptance
- Dependence on RPCA for dictionary learning may fail in the hardest coherent regimes; limited theoretical/empirical analysis of this.
- Experimental setup has nonstandard choices; limited baselines and sensitivity analysis.
- Proof details not included in the submission.

Recommendation: Weak accept, conditional on providing full proofs and strengthening the empirical evaluation (especially handling missing data without ad hoc imputation and adding sensitivity/ablation studies).
