This paper addresses a key limitation of the widely-used Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) method for decomposing a data matrix `X` into a low-rank component `L0` and a sparse component `S0`. The authors observe that RPCA's performance, guaranteed by theory, degrades when the low-rank matrix `L0` is "coherent." The paper's first contribution is to provide a clear physical intuition for this coherence: it naturally arises when the data columns in `L0` exhibit clustering structures. This clustering increases the `µ2` (row space) and `µ3` (joint space) coherence parameters, which in turn tightens the conditions under which RPCA can succeed.

To overcome this "µ2-phenomenon," the authors propose leveraging the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) model, which seeks a decomposition `X = AZ + S`, where `A` is a given dictionary and `Z` is a low-rank coefficient matrix. The central theoretical contribution is to show that if the dictionary `A` is itself low-rank and its column space contains the column space of `L0`, the LRR formulation can recover `L0` and `S0` under conditions that are independent of the problematic `µ2` and `µ3` parameters of `L0`. The recovery guarantee instead depends on the coherence `µ1(A)` of the dictionary.

Since constructing such a dictionary is challenging in an unsupervised setting, the paper proposes a practical and intuitive two-stage algorithm. First, standard RPCA is run on the corrupted data `X` to obtain an initial estimate of `L0`. Second, this estimate is used to construct a dictionary `A`, which is then used in the LRR model to obtain a refined and more accurate recovery. Experiments on both synthetic data with controlled clustering structures and real-world motion segmentation data demonstrate that this proposed method significantly outperforms standard RPCA, extending the regime of successful recovery.

### Strengths

1.  **Problem Significance and Motivation:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and significant problem. While RPCA is a cornerstone of modern data analysis, its limitations are important to understand and address. The work provides a clear and insightful connection between a practical data characteristic (clustering) and a theoretical limitation of RPCA (coherence), which is a valuable conceptual contribution in itself.

2.  **Novel Theoretical Insights:** The main theoretical result (Theorem 1) provides a clear prescription for overcoming the coherence issue: use a low-rank dictionary `A` in an LRR framework such that `U0` is a subspace of `UA`. The resulting recovery guarantee's independence from `µ2(L0)` and `µ3(L0)` is a strong and elegant result. This provides a new perspective on the LRR model, showing its potential to handle structured (coherent) data where RPCA might struggle.

3.  **Effective and Practical Algorithm:** The proposed two-stage algorithm is simple, well-justified, and empirically effective. It cleverly uses RPCA's output, even if imperfect, to bootstrap a more powerful model. The demonstration that this approach leads to a 47% larger success area in synthetic experiments and a 40% relative reduction in error on a real-world task is compelling. The claim that the computational overhead is minor (around 1.2x RPCA) makes it a practical alternative.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, the "µ2-phenomenon" is explained with illustrative figures, and the transition from the theoretical requirement to the practical algorithm is logical. The contributions are clearly summarized.

### Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions

1.  **Gap between Theory and Practice:** There is a gap between the conditions required by the theory and what the proposed algorithm provides. Theorems 1 and 2 assume that a suitable dictionary `A` is given, specifically one where `U0` is a subspace of `UA`. However, Algorithm 1 constructs `A` from the output of RPCA, which may be inaccurate precisely in the coherent regime this paper aims to address. The paper does not theoretically analyze the quality of the learned dictionary `A` or how robust the LRR step is to imperfections in `A` (i.e., when `U0` is only approximately contained in `UA`).
    *   **Suggestion:** The paper could be strengthened by adding a discussion on this gap. For instance, providing an analysis on the stability of the LRR solution with respect to perturbations in the dictionary `A`, or empirically studying how the final recovery error relates to the initial RPCA error.

2.  **Sensitivity of Algorithm Components:** The practical algorithm has a few heuristic steps whose sensitivity is not fully explored.
    *   **Rank Estimation:** In step 2, the rank is estimated by thresholding singular values. This is a critical step for constructing the dictionary `A`, and this method can be sensitive to the threshold and noise levels. How does the algorithm's performance vary with this estimated rank?
    *   **Parameter `λ`:** The standard choice `λ = 1/√n1` is used in both the RPCA and LRR stages. While standard for RPCA, it is not obvious that this is the optimal choice for the LRR stage, which uses a data-driven, non-orthogonal dictionary.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors could add a brief discussion or an ablation study in an appendix on the algorithm's sensitivity to the rank estimation and the choice of `λ` in the second stage. This would improve the reproducibility and practical utility of the proposed method.

3.  **Scope of the "Coherence" Problem:** The paper focuses exclusively on the `µ2-phenomenon` which arises from column-wise data clustering. It briefly mentions that a similar issue could affect `µ1` if rows are clustered (e.g., in text data) but defers this to future work. It is worth noting that the proposed solution's theoretical guarantee in Theorem 1 explicitly depends on `µ1(A)`, meaning it might not be suitable for data with large `µ1` coherence.
    *   **Suggestion:** While perfectly acceptable to limit the scope, a short paragraph discussing how one might approach the `µ1` problem would be beneficial. For example, would a symmetric model like `X = ZB + S`, where `B` is a row-dictionary, be a viable approach?

### Summary and Recommendation

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes several valuable contributions. It identifies and provides intuition for a key failure mode of RPCA, offers a novel theoretical solution based on the LRR framework, and translates this theory into a simple and effective practical algorithm. The experimental results are strong and clearly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach. While there is a gap between the assumptions of the theory and the heuristic nature of the proposed algorithm, the paper's core ideas are significant and likely to inspire further research into robust recovery of structured data. The strengths substantially outweigh the weaknesses.

**Decision:** Accept.
