Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses robust recovery of a low-rank matrix L0 corrupted by sparse gross errors S0, with particular focus on “coherent” low-rank matrices where standard RPCA (minimize nuclear norm plus ℓ1 norm) degrades due to large coherence parameters. The authors identify a µ2-phenomenon: when columns of L0 form multiple clusters (union-of-subspaces), the second coherence µ2 (and hence µ3) grows with the number of clusters, tightening RPCA’s rank bound and harming recovery.

To mitigate this, the paper studies Low-Rank Representation (LRR): minimize ‖Z‖* + λ‖S‖1 subject to X = AZ + S, and shows that if the dictionary A is itself low-rank and its column space contains that of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), then exact (noiseless) and near-exact (noisy) recovery guarantees can be proved without dependence on µ2. The rank bound becomes r(L0) ≤ O(n2/log n1)/µ1(A), which is looser than RPCA’s O(n2/(log n1)2)/µ(L0). The authors further propose a simple unsupervised procedure: run RPCA to estimate L̂0, construct a low-rank, column-normalized dictionary Â from L̂0, then run LRR with Â. Experiments on synthetic union-of-subspaces data and corrupted motion sequences (Hopkins155 variants) show improved recovery and downstream clustering performance over RPCA and several subspace clustering baselines.

This builds on RPCA (Candès et al.), LRR (Liu et al.), and subspace clustering (SIM, SSC), and extends LRR theory to general (low-rank) dictionaries for coherent data.

Quality:
- Theory: Theorems 1–2 are technically meaningful: they formalize how a low-rank dictionary with U0 ⊂ UA removes µ2 (and µ3) from the success conditions. The improved rank bound is clearly stated. Assumptions such as column-wise normalization of A and the necessary subspace inclusion are explicit. However, the statements with “with probability at least 1 − n−10” are underspecified about the source of randomness (support Ω and/or signs of S0); clarifying the precise probabilistic model would strengthen rigor. The requirement U0 ⊂ UA is strong; the authors acknowledge this and propose a practical heuristic to approximate it.
- Algorithm: The RPCA→LRR pipeline is simple and sensible as a bootstrap. The claim that the computational overhead is ~1.2× RPCA is plausible given LRR’s complexity with low-rank A, but concrete timing comparisons and solver details (algorithms, stopping criteria) are missing.
- Experiments: Synthetic experiments sweep rank and corruption density and show a substantial increase in the success region (reported as 47% larger). Motion segmentation results on 11 additional sequences show notable error reductions after recovery, and further gains from learned-dictionary LRR. Limitations: relatively small real dataset; missing ablations (e.g., sensitivity to λ, ε; iterative refinements beyond one round; robustness when RPCA’s initial estimate is poor), and comparisons to other robust PCA variants (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, column-sparse corruption variants, nonconvex surrogates) are absent.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized, with clear motivation of the µ2-phenomenon and a good notation summary. Important background on coherence parameters is explained. Some proofs are deferred, which is acceptable if included in the full version. A few references in the list appear incomplete; related work could better situate robust variants beyond RPCA/LRR. It would help to more explicitly state the randomness assumptions in the theorems and provide more experimental details (solvers, hyperparameters, stopping criteria, code availability).

Originality:
The recognition and empirical demonstration of the µ2-phenomenon due to clustering is insightful. The key novelty is the theoretical result that a low-rank dictionary whose span contains U0 eliminates dependence on µ2 in LRR’s guarantees, together with a practical bootstrapping approach to learn such a dictionary in the unsupervised setting. While LRR itself and A=X are known, the analysis for general low-rank dictionaries in the coherent regime appears new, and the algorithmic idea, though simple, is useful.

Significance:
Coherent low-rank data are common in union-of-subspaces settings; improving robustness there is relevant to subspace clustering, vision, and other applications. The theoretical message—that structure-aware dictionaries can neutralize harmful coherence—is valuable and could influence future methods in robust subspace recovery and factorization. The empirical gains, while on modest-scale datasets, are tangible. The work advances understanding of when and why LRR can outperform RPCA.

Pros:
- Clear identification of a realistic failure mode of RPCA (µ2-phenomenon) with intuition and evidence.
- New recovery guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that avoid µ2 and relax rank bounds.
- Simple, practical RPCA→LRR algorithm that improves recovery and downstream clustering.
- Empirical validation on synthetic and motion data.

Cons:
- Strong assumption U0 ⊂ UA in the theory; the unsupervised algorithm relies on RPCA being “good enough,” which may fail in highly coherent/high-corruption regimes.
- The probabilistic model in the theorems is not fully specified; proofs are deferred.
- Experimental scope is limited; missing comparisons to other robust PCA variants and ablations (sensitivity, iterative refinement).
- Some references are incomplete; related work discussion could be broadened.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the randomness assumptions for Theorems 1–2 (support model for Ω, sign distributions).
- Provide solver details, runtimes, and code to enhance reproducibility.
- Add ablations: sensitivity to λ/ε, effect of dictionary rank/conditioning, iterative updates, and robustness when RPCA’s initial estimate is inaccurate.
- Compare to additional robust PCA/subspace recovery baselines (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, column/row-sparse corruption models, nonconvex penalties).
- Include experiments varying the number of clusters to directly demonstrate the µ2-phenomenon and LRR’s immunity.
