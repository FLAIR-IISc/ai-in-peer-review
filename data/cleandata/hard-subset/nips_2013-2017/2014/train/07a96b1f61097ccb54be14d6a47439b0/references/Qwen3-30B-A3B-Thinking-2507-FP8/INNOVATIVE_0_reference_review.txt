

# Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation of Robust PCA (RPCA) when dealing with coherent data, where the low-rank component exhibits clustering structures. The authors make a significant observation that clustering structures lead to high coherence parameters (specifically µ2), which degrades RPCA performance. This "µ2-phenomenon" represents a meaningful gap in the understanding of RPCA's limitations that previous work has overlooked.

The paper's core novelty lies in proposing Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary as a solution to this problem. The key insight is that when the dictionary matrix A is low-rank and satisfies U0 ⊂ UA (where U0 is the column space of the true low-rank matrix L0), LRR can avoid the coherence parameters that plague RPCA. The authors prove this theoretically (Theorems 1 and 2) and propose a practical unsupervised algorithm that uses RPCA to construct the dictionary for LRR.

This work stands out from previous approaches in several ways. While RPCA and LRR have been studied extensively (e.g., [4, 13]), this paper is the first to identify and systematically analyze the relationship between clustering structures and coherence parameters. The authors demonstrate that coherence parameters aren't just "assumptions" for proofs but are intrinsically related to extra structures in data. This conceptual shift is profound and provides new theoretical insights into why certain methods work better than others.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) Identifying the "µ2-phenomenon" as a key limitation of RPCA in practical settings, (2) Establishing the theoretical conditions under which LRR with a low-rank dictionary can overcome this limitation, (3) Proposing a practical algorithm that outperforms RPCA in both synthetic and real-world motion data, and (4) Providing new theoretical insights into the physical meaning of coherence parameters.

The experimental results are convincing, showing that the proposed method achieves a 47% wider success area in synthetic data compared to RPCA, and achieves a 40% relative improvement in motion segmentation error rates. The algorithm is computationally efficient (only 1.2x the time of RPCA) and provides a practical solution to a real-world problem.

The main strength of this paper is its bold conceptual insight about the relationship between clustering structures and coherence parameters. This is not an incremental improvement to existing methods but rather a new perspective that fundamentally changes how we understand the limitations of RPCA. The authors successfully bridge theory and practice by providing both theoretical guarantees and a practical algorithm.

Some minor weaknesses include:
1. The theoretical results don't explicitly address how to construct the low-rank dictionary in unsupervised settings beyond the proposed algorithm
2. The connection to other recent matrix recovery methods could be strengthened
3. The paper could provide more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the dictionary construction

However, these are minor concerns that don't detract from the paper's significant novelty. The authors have clearly identified an important gap in the literature and proposed a novel, theoretically grounded solution that addresses it.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a strong case for its contribution. It directly addresses a meaningful limitation of a widely used technique (RPCA) and provides both theoretical justification and practical implementation for a better approach.

This work represents a significant step forward in understanding and addressing the limitations of robust PCA. The insight about the relationship between clustering structures and coherence parameters is novel and important, and the proposed solution using LRR with a low-rank dictionary is both theoretically sound and practically effective.

I believe this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of matrix recovery and robust statistics. It's not merely an incremental improvement but rather a new direction that could influence future work in robust PCA and related areas. The paper's conceptual novelty, theoretical soundness, and practical impact make it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

The work is well-motivated, properly contextualized within the literature, and provides a clear path forward for future research. The authors have successfully demonstrated that the coherence parameters are not just theoretical constraints but have physical meaning related to data structures, which is a significant conceptual advance.

Given the paper's strong novelty, theoretical contributions, and practical impact, I recommend acceptance. This is not a minor improvement to existing methods but rather a new perspective on a fundamental limitation of RPCA that will likely influence future research in robust matrix recovery.
