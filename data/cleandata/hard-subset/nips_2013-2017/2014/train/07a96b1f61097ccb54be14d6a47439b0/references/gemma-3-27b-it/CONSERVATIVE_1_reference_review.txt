This paper addresses the problem of robust low-rank matrix recovery, specifically focusing on the challenges posed by coherent data – a scenario where the underlying low-rank structure exhibits clustering. While the motivation is relevant, and the problem of improving robustness beyond standard RPCA is valuable, the paper presents incremental contributions built upon established techniques, lacking the novelty and rigorous validation expected of a NIPS submission.

The core idea – leveraging a learned dictionary within the LRR framework to mitigate coherence effects – is not entirely new. The connection between dictionary learning and robustness has been explored previously, and the authors themselves acknowledge the relation to matrix factorization techniques. The novelty lies in formally linking coherence to clustering structures and demonstrating that a low-rank dictionary can alleviate the associated issues. However, the theoretical results (Theorems 1 & 2) feel somewhat constrained by their assumptions (e.g., the column-wise unit norm dictionary and the specific subspace inclusion condition) and the noiseless/low-noise settings. The practical implications of these assumptions, and the sensitivity of the method to violations of them, are not sufficiently discussed.

The experimental section is a mixed bag. The synthetic results demonstrate improvement over RPCA, but the gains are presented visually without clear statistical significance testing. The results on motion sequences are more compelling, showing an advantage over RPCA when combined with subspace clustering. However, the experimental setup relies on existing datasets and relatively standard methods (SIM, SSC, LRRx), and doesn’t explore a broader range of challenging scenarios or competing baselines. Critically, the parameter tuning process for all methods, including the dictionary learning step, is not detailed enough for reproducibility. It is unclear how sensitive the performance is to the choice of dictionary learning parameters. 

The paper’s analysis of coherence parameters (µ1, µ2, µ3) and their relation to clustering is insightful, but remains largely descriptive. While the “µ2-phenomenon” is identified, a deeper understanding of *why* this phenomenon occurs and its implications for different data distributions would strengthen the contribution. The connection between the coherence parameters and data structure is presented but not rigorously formalized beyond the provided theorems.

**Strengths:**

*   Relevant problem: Robust PCA is a crucial area of research.
*   Insightful connection: Linking coherence to data clustering provides a useful perspective.
*   Algorithm 1 provides a practical improvement over RPCA, as demonstrated in experiments.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Incremental novelty: The core idea builds on established techniques without substantial innovation.
*   Theoretical limitations: Assumptions in the theorems restrict their applicability.
*   Insufficient experimental validation: Limited statistical analysis, lack of diverse baselines, and incomplete details hinder reproducibility.
*   Descriptive analysis: While insightful, the analysis of coherence parameters lacks rigorous formalization.
*   The paper feels somewhat lengthy for the amount of genuinely novel contribution.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a clear and well-written analysis of coherence in low-rank matrix recovery.
*   The proposed algorithm offers a practical improvement over RPCA in certain scenarios.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical contributions are limited by their assumptions and scope.
*   The experimental validation is insufficient to demonstrate a significant advance.
*   The paper lacks the level of novelty and rigor typically expected of a NIPS publication.




**Overall assessment:**

I recommend **rejecting** this paper. While the work is technically sound and the problem is important, the contributions are incremental and lack the novelty and comprehensive validation necessary for acceptance at NIPS. The paper would likely be more suitable for a venue that values applied research and practical improvements over fundamental theoretical advances. The authors should consider addressing the limitations outlined above – particularly focusing on more extensive experimental validation with rigorous statistical analysis, relaxing the assumptions in their theoretical results, and providing a more in-depth analysis of the underlying mechanisms driving the observed phenomena – before resubmitting to a similar venue.
