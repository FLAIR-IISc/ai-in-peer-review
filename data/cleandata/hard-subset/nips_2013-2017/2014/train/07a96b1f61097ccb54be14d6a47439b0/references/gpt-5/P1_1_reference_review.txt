Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies recovery of low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse gross errors when the low-rank component is coherent, a regime where classical Robust PCA (RPCA/PCP) can fail. The authors argue and empirically illustrate that clustering structure in the columns (union of subspaces) increases the second coherence parameter µ2 as the number of clusters grows (“µ2-phenomenon”), tightening RPCA’s rank bounds and degrading its performance. They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned low-rank dictionary A: solve minZ,S ||Z||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = AZ + S. They prove that if A is low-rank and its column space contains the true column space (U0 ⊂ UA), then exact (noiseless) and stable (noisy) recovery is possible with bounds that depend on µ1(A) but not on µ2(L0). They then propose a simple unsupervised pipeline: run RPCA to get an estimate of L0, truncate its SVD to get a well-conditioned low-rank approximation L̃0, normalize columns to form A, and run LRR with this A.

This work builds on PCP/RPCA (Candès et al.) and on LRR (Liu et al., 2010–2013). Prior LRR analyses typically focused on A = X for subspace clustering; this paper studies general dictionaries and provides conditions under which LRR can “avoid” µ2. It connects coherence to clustering structure, offering an interpretation of why PCP assumptions can be violated in practice.

Strengths
- Conceptual insight: Clear articulation and empirical illustration of the µ2-phenomenon linking clustering to coherence; useful for understanding failure modes of PCP.
- Theoretical contribution: Noiseless and noisy recovery guarantees for LRR with general low-rank dictionaries, removing dependence on µ2(L0) under U0 ⊂ UA and column-normalization. Bounds allow up to roughly 50% sparsity (noiseless) / 35% (noisy) and r(A) up to O(n2 / (µ1(A) log n1)).
- Practical algorithm: A simple, implementable, and computationally reasonable approach that leverages RPCA to construct a low-rank dictionary; claims of modest extra runtime (≈1.2× PCP).
- Empirical indications: On synthetic data, a larger success region than PCP; on corrupted motion sequences, a meaningful reduction in clustering error when LRR uses the learned dictionary compared to PCP pre-processing.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Strength of the U0 ⊂ UA assumption: Exact recovery theorems require the dictionary to contain the true subspace. In unsupervised settings this is only approximately achieved by RPCA; however, there is no theory for the two-stage procedure when PCP is imperfect (which is exactly the regime of interest). The dependence of recovery on the quality of the initial PCP estimate should be analyzed (e.g., perturbation bounds or stability to subspace misalignment).
- Assumptions on S0: The theorems state high-probability guarantees but the distributional assumptions (e.g., uniformly random support, random signs) are not made explicit here. Please clarify the precise sparsity model and any independence assumptions relative to A and L0.
- Control of µ1(A): Guarantees depend on µ1(A), yet when A is learned from data, there is no analysis or diagnostic to ensure small µ1(A). Column normalization helps but does not control µ1; guidance or a heuristic to reduce µ1(A) (e.g., preconditioning, leverage-score sampling) would strengthen the practical story.
- Experimental scope: Results are promising but limited. The synthetic study shows a success region expansion but lacks quantitative recovery metrics (e.g., relative error on L0 and S0) and statistical variation. The motion experiment evaluates downstream clustering, but more baselines specific to robust low-rank recovery (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, robust matrix factorization, AltProj/fast PCP variants, LRR with A = X for recovery) and sensitivity analyses (choice of λ, rank threshold, ε) would better support significance claims. Also, an ablation on steps 2–4 (rank truncation, normalization) would be valuable.
- Circularity concern: The algorithm depends on PCP to construct A, yet the claimed advantage is when PCP struggles due to high µ2. The paper notes PCP is “not brittle” and often yields a decent estimate; still, a systematic study across µ2 levels showing the probability that the learned A meets the theorem’s conditions would increase confidence.
- Clarity and completeness: Some claims (e.g., “O(n2 / log n1) is probably the finest bound”) are speculative. Figures 1–4 are referenced but not included here; ensure the final paper provides enough detail to reproduce experiments. State exact parameter choices and data generation details (support distribution, noise distribution). There are minor grammar issues.

Quality
The technical claims are plausible and build on established RPCA proof techniques. The main novelty is the dictionary-based avoidance of µ2 with associated bounds. However, the lack of theory for the unsupervised pipeline where A is estimated from imperfect PCP, and incomplete specification of assumptions on S0, reduce the completeness. Experiments support the claims but are limited in breadth.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized with a helpful notation section. Some definitions and assumptions should be made explicit, and experimental details expanded. Editing for grammar/typos would help.

Originality
The connection between clustering-induced coherence and PCP failure is well known informally; formalizing the µ2-phenomenon and proving µ2-free recovery for LRR with low-rank dictionaries is a useful and, to my knowledge, novel angle. The algorithmic pipeline is simple and somewhat incremental but practical.

Significance
If the approach generalizes, it offers a practical way to improve robust low-rank recovery in coherent settings common in vision and other domains. The work could influence how LRR dictionaries are learned for recovery, not just clustering. Broader empirical validation would strengthen impact.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely state the sparsity model for S0 (support distribution, sign model) and any independence from L0/A used in the proofs.
- Provide perturbation analysis when U0 ⊄ UA but principal angles are small.
- How sensitive is performance to the rank threshold (10−3σ1)? Did you try automatic rank selection (e.g., Gavish-Donoho)?
- Can you provide diagnostics or procedures that reduce µ1(A) when constructing A?
- How does the method fare when rows are also clustered (large µ1(L0)) as noted in Sec. 3.1.2?

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros
- Clear theoretical insight connecting coherence to clustering; µ2-free guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries.
- Simple, practical algorithm with encouraging improvements over PCP on synthetic and real data.
- Bridges theory and practice; potentially useful to a broad community working on robust subspace methods.

Cons
- Strong assumptions (U0 ⊂ UA) and missing theory for the unsupervised pipeline.
- Limited experimental scope and baselines; lack of sensitivity/ablation studies.
- Incomplete specification of assumptions and some speculative claims.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contribution and practical potential outweigh the limitations, but the paper would benefit from clarifying assumptions and expanding experiments.
