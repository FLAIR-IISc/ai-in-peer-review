This paper addresses the problem of robust low-rank matrix recovery, specifically focusing on the challenges posed by coherent data – data exhibiting clustering structures that degrade the performance of standard Robust PCA (RPCA). The authors identify a connection between data coherence and underlying data structures, and propose utilizing a low-rank dictionary within the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework to mitigate the impact of coherence. They provide theoretical analysis and experimental validation demonstrating the benefits of their approach.

The motivation for addressing coherence in low-rank recovery is sound. RPCA, while powerful, is known to struggle with highly coherent data, and the paper correctly identifies that this limitation arises from ignoring intrinsic data structures beyond simple low-rankness. The theoretical link established between coherence parameters (specifically µ2) and clustering structures is a valuable contribution. The proposed solution of employing a low-rank dictionary within LRR is a logical extension of existing techniques.

However, despite the reasonable motivation and some positive experimental results, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses. First, the theoretical analysis, while present, feels incomplete. Theorem 1, the core theoretical result, relies on the condition PUA(U0) = U0, which, while stated as necessary, isn’t fully justified regarding its practical attainability without prior knowledge of the underlying subspace U0—effectively requiring supervised learning in some form. The assumption of column-wise unit-normed dictionary feels artificial and is not thoroughly motivated beyond simplifying the parameter estimate for λ.

Second, the experimental validation, while demonstrating improvement over RPCA, lacks sufficient rigor. The comparisons are primarily against RPCA, a known baseline, but a broader comparison against other state-of-the-art robust PCA methods (e.g., those based on outlier pursuit or reweighted nuclear norm minimization) is missing. The choice of parameter λ = 1/ √ n1 is applied consistently without justification beyond consistency with RPCA.  A sensitivity analysis of λ, and other relevant hyperparameters, would strengthen the conclusions. Furthermore, the performance gains on the motion sequences are modest, and the benefit isn’t always clearly demonstrated compared to RPCA after the preprocessing step.

Third, while the paper acknowledges the connection to matrix factorization, it doesn't fully explore the existing literature in this area. The proposed approach is, in essence, a specific instance of matrix factorization, and a more comprehensive discussion of related work would be beneficial.

Finally, the writing, while generally clear, contains some redundancy and lacks conciseness. The use of multiple notations for the same concepts (e.g., various norms) can be confusing.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Identifies a real limitation of RPCA – its sensitivity to data coherence.
*   Provides a theoretically motivated approach for mitigating this limitation.
*   Demonstrates empirical improvements over RPCA.
*   Connects coherence parameters to interpretable data structures.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Theoretical results rely on strong assumptions (PUA(U0) = U0) with limited discussion of practical implications.
*   Experimental validation is insufficient – missing comparisons to more recent robust PCA methods and a lack of hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
*   Limited novelty beyond applying a low-rank dictionary within an existing framework (LRR).
*   Writing could be more concise and clear.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper falls short of the standards for acceptance to NIPS. While the problem addressed is relevant, and the proposed solution is reasonable, the lack of thorough theoretical justification, the limited experimental validation, and the incremental nature of the contribution do not warrant acceptance at this time. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison against existing methods, a deeper exploration of the theoretical implications of the assumptions made, and a more rigorous experimental evaluation. I would recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to address the concerns raised in a revised version for potential consideration in a future workshop or conference.
