Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies robust recovery of a low-rank matrix L0 and a sparse corruption S0 from X = L0 + S0 when L0 is coherent. Classical RPCA via Principal Component Pursuit (PCP) min ||L||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = L + S succeeds under incoherence assumptions (Candès et al., JACM ’11), with guarantees scaling with the coherence parameters μ1, μ2, μ3. The authors argue that clustering/union-of-subspaces structure—ubiquitous in practice—drives up μ2 (row-space coherence), degrading PCP performance (“μ2-phenomenon”).

They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR): min ||Z||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = AZ + S, with a learned dictionary A. The main theoretical result shows that if A is low-rank and its column space spans that of L0 (U0 ⊆ UA), then exact (or near) recovery is possible without dependence on μ2(L0); the bounds depend only on μ1(A), the left-space coherence of A. This yields a way to “avoid” the detrimental μ2. They then propose a practical two-stage algorithm: run PCP to estimate L0, construct a low-rank dictionary by column-normalizing a rank-truncated estimate of L0, then run LRR with this A. Experiments on synthetic coherent data and motion trajectories (Hopkins-style sequences) suggest improved recovery and downstream clustering accuracy over PCP.

Quality
- Theory: The noiseless and noisy theorems are clearly stated, with conditions rank(L0) ≤ rank(A) ≤ O(n2/(μ1(A) log n1)) and |Ω| ≤ 0.5mn (noiseless) or 0.35mn (noisy). The explicit elimination of μ2(L0) is a useful insight, consistent with the intuition that union-of-subspaces inflates row-space coherence but not left-space coherence. However:
  - The key assumption U0 ⊆ UA is strong and functions as a form of supervision/oracle knowledge. The paper argues it is “easy to obey” in supervised settings, but the practical unsupervised algorithm builds A from PCP’s estimate, making A adaptive to X and to S0. The theorems appear to assume A is fixed and independent of Ω. There is no formal analysis showing that the PCP-derived A satisfies U0 ⊆ UA (even approximately) with high probability when PCP is imperfect, nor that adaptivity to Ω doesn’t break the guarantees. This is the main theoretical gap.
  - The near-recovery bounds in the noisy case scale as O(√mn ε), which may be loose; discussion of tightness or whether a dependence on rA is achievable would help.
  - The model for S0 (random support) and the probabilistic statements are only sketched; the paper would benefit from explicitly stating the randomness assumptions (support uniformity, independence from A, magnitude distribution).
- Methodology/algorithm: The two-stage PCP→LRR approach is sensible and simple. The choice λ = 1/√n1 is standard. The normalization/low-rank truncation of the dictionary to improve conditioning is reasonable, but there is no analysis of sensitivity to the rank estimate r̂0 or to the conditioning of A. Complexity claims (LRR cost O(n2 rA)) are plausible if one exploits low-rank structure of A, but the implementation details matter because Z is d×n (often d = n). Please clarify the per-iteration cost and the dependence on d.
- Experiments: The synthetic study demonstrates larger success regions than PCP (claimed +47%), aligned with the theory. However, reporting needs strengthening:
  - Define the exact success criterion and provide confidence intervals across 10 trials.
  - Compare against additional robust PCA variants designed for coherence or structured outliers, e.g., Outlier Pursuit (Xu et al., NIPS ’10), OP/OR-PCA, IRLS/nonconvex surrogates, and LRR with A = X (LRRx) as a recovery method, not only as a clustering post-hoc.
  - Real data: The motion experiment replaces missing entries with ±1, which injects gross nonphysical noise. Consider evaluating on genuine missingness (matrix completion setting) and/or a more realistic corruption model, and also report recovery metrics (relative L-error, support precision/recall) in addition to downstream clustering error. Provide runtime comparisons to support the “1.2× PCP” claim.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized. The exposition on coherence and the μ2-phenomenon is clear and insightful. A few suggestions:
- State the probabilistic model and independence assumptions explicitly where Theorems 1–2 are stated.
- Clarify when A = I reduces LRR to PCP and note that A orthogonal gives equivalence, helping readers situate the proposal relative to RPCA/PCP.
- Move some footnotes/remarks into the main text and fix minor typos.
- Provide a brief proof sketch to convey why μ2 disappears under U0 ⊆ UA, and why μ1(A) remains.

Originality
The focus on coherent data induced by union-of-subspaces and the resulting μ2-phenomenon is a valuable perspective. While LRR is not new, the specific theoretical result showing μ2 can be avoided with a low-rank dictionary spanning U0 appears novel and helps explain when/why LRR with a learned dictionary improves over PCP. The two-stage PCP→LRR pipeline is straightforward but practically useful.

Significance
If the theory–practice gap (adaptive A) is bridged, the contribution could impact robust subspace learning in settings with clustered data (vision, dynamic MRI). The empirical improvements on synthetic and motion data are encouraging, but broader evaluations and stronger baselines would better establish significance.

Pros
- Clear identification and empirical demonstration of the μ2-phenomenon due to clustering.
- Theoretical guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that eliminate dependence on μ2.
- Simple, practical algorithm that often improves PCP in coherent regimes.
- Relevant applications (motion segmentation) with downstream gains.

Cons
- Strong assumption U0 ⊆ UA; no guarantees for the unsupervised, adaptive dictionary learned from PCP.
- Limited baselines and evaluation breadth; some experimental design choices are questionable.
- No analysis of sensitivity to rank estimation, λ, or dictionary conditioning.
- Noisy bounds may be loose; complexity claims need more detail.

Recommendations and questions for the authors
1) Provide a theoretical statement (even under additional conditions) for the two-stage algorithm: if PCP returns an L̂0 within δ of L0, does A learned from L̂0 satisfy an approximate subspace inclusion and yield stable recovery via LRR?
2) Compare against Outlier Pursuit and other robust PCA variants; include LRR with A = X as a recovery baseline.
3) Report standard errors, success criteria, and runtime; analyze sensitivity to r̂0 and to λ.
4) Use realistic missingness without replacing with ±1; add recovery metrics on real data.
5) Discuss scenarios where μ1(A) could be large (e.g., row clustering) and how to mitigate it (e.g., row-wise weighting or alternative dictionary constructions).

Overall: This is a thoughtful and potentially impactful contribution with a compelling idea and supportive evidence, but it needs stronger theoretical justification for the unsupervised procedure and broader empirical validation. I lean toward weak accept if the authors can address the key concerns.
