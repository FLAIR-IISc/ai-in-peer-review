### Summary

This paper addresses a key limitation of the widely-used Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) method: its performance degradation on "coherent" low-rank data. The authors make the crucial observation that such coherence is not an esoteric corner case but arises naturally from common data structures, particularly clustering. They identify a "µ2-phenomenon," where the coherence of the row-space of the low-rank matrix `L0` increases with the number of underlying data clusters, thereby violating a key assumption for RPCA's success.

The core proposal is to move from the RPCA formulation to the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) model, `X = AZ + S`, where `A` is a dictionary. The paper's main theoretical contribution is to show that if the dictionary `A` is itself low-rank and its column space contains the column space of the true low-rank data `L0`, then the recovery guarantees for LRR are independent of the problematic coherence parameters (µ2, µ3). This provides a principled way to handle coherent data. To make this operational in an unsupervised setting, the authors propose a simple yet effective two-stage algorithm: first, run RPCA to get an initial estimate of `L0`, then use this estimate to construct a dictionary for a second, refining LRR step. Experiments on synthetic and motion-capture data demonstrate that this approach significantly outperforms standard RPCA.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its bold reframing of a core problem in low-rank matrix recovery. Instead of treating coherence as a technical nuisance or an assumption to be met, the authors investigate its physical origins, linking it directly to clustering structure. This provides a deep and valuable insight into *why* and *when* methods like RPCA fail. This is a significant conceptual leap that could influence future research in robust subspace learning.

2.  **Elegant Theoretical Foundation:** The main result (Theorem 1) is elegant and powerful. It provides a clear prescription for overcoming the coherence issue: use a low-rank dictionary whose subspace contains the true data subspace. The fact that the resulting recovery bound for LRR completely avoids the problematic µ2 parameter provides a strong theoretical justification for the proposed direction. This is not just an incremental improvement but a fundamental shift in the analysis.

3.  **Simple and Effective Algorithm:** While the theory assumes access to a suitable dictionary, the proposed two-stage algorithm provides a practical and intuitive way to construct one. The idea of using a flawed-but-decent estimate from RPCA to bootstrap a better solution via LRR is clever and pragmatic. The empirical results, showing a 47% increase in the "success area" on synthetic data and substantial gains on real-world motion data, convincingly demonstrate the value of this approach.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out, the "µ2-phenomenon" is explained intuitively, and the progression from the problem to the theoretical solution and the practical algorithm is logical.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1 and 2) are for an idealized setting where a dictionary `A` satisfying the condition `U0 ⊂ UA` is given. However, the proposed Algorithm 1 is a heuristic for finding such an `A`. There is no theoretical analysis guaranteeing that the dictionary constructed from the RPCA output will satisfy this condition, especially when the initial RPCA recovery is poor. While the experiments show this heuristic works well, the theoretical justification for the algorithm *itself* is incomplete.

2.  **Heuristic Components:** The algorithm relies on a heuristic for rank estimation in Step 2. While this is a common practice, it introduces a potential point of failure that is not analyzed. Similarly, the dismissal of a fully iterative procedure as converging in two steps is stated without strong empirical or theoretical backing.

### Recommendation: Accept

I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance. The contributions are significant, novel, and thought-provoking.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper tackles a fundamentally important problem: the failure of standard low-rank models on structured, coherent data, which is arguably more representative of real-world scenarios than incoherent data.
*   The conceptual link it establishes between data clustering and matrix coherence is a major insight that deepens our understanding of these models.
*   The proposed LRR-based solution is not an ad-hoc fix but is grounded in an elegant theoretical result that shows how to sidestep the coherence problem entirely. This is a bold and impressive new direction.
*   The practical algorithm, despite its heuristic nature, is simple, effective, and demonstrates clear superiority over the state-of-the-art RPCA.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theoretical analysis does not cover the proposed practical algorithm, leaving a gap between the guarantees and the implementation.

**Justification:**

In my view, the arguments for acceptance decisively outweigh the arguments against. This paper is a prime example of the kind of research NIPS should champion. It is not an incremental polishing of an existing method; it identifies a fundamental weakness in a major line of work and proposes a genuinely new and principled way forward. The conceptual insight alone is a significant contribution. The fact that this insight leads to a practical algorithm that delivers strong empirical gains makes the paper even more compelling. The gap between the theory and the algorithm is a fair criticism, but for a paper opening up a new direction, it is an acceptable limitation that can be addressed in future work. Prioritizing methodological perfection here would mean rejecting a truly innovative and important idea. This work has the potential to inspire a new line of inquiry into structured robust subspace recovery.
