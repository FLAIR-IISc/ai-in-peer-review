Summary
The paper studies recovery of low-rank plus sparse decompositions when the low-rank component is coherent (i.e., violates standard incoherence assumptions). It argues and empirically illustrates a µ2-phenomenon: when data lie in a union of multiple subspaces within a fixed global low-rank subspace, the right-space coherence µ2(L0) grows with the number of clusters, degrading RPCA guarantees and performance. To address this, the authors analyze Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary A. The main theoretical claims: if A is column-normalized, low-rank, and its column space contains the true column space (U0 ⊂ UA), then solving LRR recovers the decomposition without any dependence on µ2 (and µ3), both in the noiseless and noisy settings, with bounds depending only on µ1(A) and log factors. Practically, they propose a simple unsupervised two-stage algorithm: run RPCA to estimate L0, truncate to estimated rank, normalize to form A, then run LRR to produce the final recovery. Experiments on synthetic union-of-subspaces data show a substantially larger recovery region than RPCA, and motion segmentation on Hopkins-style data shows consistent improvements in clustering error after the proposed recovery compared to using RPCA alone.

Quality
- Theory: The theorems are technically plausible and clearly identify the crux: by using a low-rank dictionary whose span contains U0, LRR shifts the incoherence burden away from µ2(L0), avoiding the growth with the number of clusters. The dependence on µ1(A), rank(A), and log factors mirrors RPCA-style bounds. The assumption U0 ⊂ UA is necessary for exact recovery with AZ = L0 and is stated. The authors provide both noiseless and noisy stability results with explicit error bounds. However, proofs are deferred; for a NeurIPS submission, at least a proof sketch or supplemental proofs are needed to assess tightness and whether technical conditions (e.g., dual certificate construction, handling of column normalization) are standard or require new arguments.
- Algorithm: The two-stage RPCA→LRR heuristic is simple and well motivated: even if RPCA is imperfect under coherence, it often produces a “good-enough” subspace estimate to build a low-rank dictionary that satisfies U0 ⊂ UA approximately, which empirically suffices. The paper claims the success probability is ≥ RPCA’s, but this is argued informally; a formal result under approximate subspace inclusion (angles between U0 and UA) would strengthen the contribution.
- Experiments: Synthetic experiments use a union-of-subspaces model exactly matching the hypothesized µ2-phenomenon and show sizable gains (47% wider “success” area). The real-data motion segmentation experiment shows consistent downstream benefits across clustering methods. Weaknesses: (i) limited baselines focused on RPCA; more comparisons to other robust subspace recovery approaches (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, GoDec/alternating PCP, IRPCA/AltProj, REAPER/Tyler-type methods) would better situate gains; (ii) sensitivity analyses (rank estimation threshold, dictionary conditioning, λ, ε) are absent; (iii) using ±1 to impute missing entries is unusual—better to treat missing data explicitly or use standard masked formulations.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally well written and organized, with clear motivation, definitions of coherence parameters, and algorithm steps. The narrative around the µ2-phenomenon is intuitive and supported by figures (though the submission references figures and “full paper” for details; the NeurIPS version should be self-contained). Some typos (“PRCA3”) and occasional informal phrasing could be cleaned up. It would help to:
- Provide a succinct proof sketch of Theorem 1 highlighting the key deviation from RPCA (e.g., how the dual certificate avoids µ2).
- Clarify how µ3’s behavior is tied to µ2 in the union-of-subspaces setting.
- Explicitly state computational complexity assumptions and solvers used.

Originality
The paper builds on LRR, but the core theoretical novelty is analyzing LRR with general, low-rank dictionaries and showing recovery without µ2 dependence under U0 ⊂ UA, plus formal noisy stability. The identification and analysis of the µ2-phenomenon for clustered data and its practical consequence for RPCA is well motivated. The two-stage dictionary learning heuristic is simple but, to my knowledge, not rigorously analyzed before; the empirical gains are nontrivial. Related work is mostly covered, though comparisons to more recent robust PCA/robust subspace recovery algorithms and masked RPCA for missing data would strengthen positioning.

Significance
Recovering coherent low-rank structures is important in subspace clustering, computer vision, and recommendation. The results give both conceptual insight (why RPCA can fail for clustered data) and a practical fix (learn a low-rank dictionary and use LRR). If the theorems are correct and the approach generalizes, this can influence how practitioners design preprocessing pipelines for union-of-subspaces data. The requirement U0 ⊂ UA limits the immediate generality of the theory, but the heuristic to achieve it unsupervised shows promise. Further validation and broader baselines would increase impact.

Pros
- Clear identification of a realistic failure mode of RPCA via µ2-phenomenon.
- New recovery guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that avoid µ2.
- Simple, computationally reasonable unsupervised algorithm with empirical gains on synthetic and real data.
- Useful insights into the role and meaning of coherence parameters.

Cons
- Strong assumption U0 ⊂ UA; no theoretical analysis for approximate inclusion as induced by the unsupervised procedure.
- Proofs not included; reliance on “full paper” limits assessment.
- Limited experimental baselines and sensitivity analysis; unconventional handling of missing data.
- Some presentation issues (minor typos; figure dependencies).

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you provide analysis under approximate subspace inclusion, e.g., bounds in terms of principal angles between U0 and UA?
- How sensitive is the method to rank estimation and column normalization? Please include ablations.
- Can you compare against additional robust PCA/robust subspace recovery baselines and a masked RPCA/LRR setting for missing data?
- Please include proof sketches or a supplemental with full proofs.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and timely contribution that advances understanding and practice for robust low-rank recovery under coherence induced by clustering. With stronger theoretical exposition (proofs, approximate inclusion) and broader empirical validation, it would be a strong NeurIPS paper. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing clarity and evaluation concerns.
