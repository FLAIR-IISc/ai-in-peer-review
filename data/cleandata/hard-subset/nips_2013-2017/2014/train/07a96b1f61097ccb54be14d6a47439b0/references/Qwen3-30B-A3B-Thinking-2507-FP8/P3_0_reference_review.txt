

# Peer Review: "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) - its performance degradation when the low-rank component exhibits clustering structures, which causes the second coherence parameter (µ2) to increase. The authors identify this "µ2-phenomenon" as a key reason for RPCA's failure on coherent data. They propose an extension of the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework that uses a low-rank dictionary matrix to avoid the coherence parameters that harm RPCA performance. The main theoretical contribution is proving that when the dictionary A is low-rank and satisfies U0 ⊂ UA (where U0 is the column space of the true low-rank matrix L0), LRR can exactly recover L0 and S0 without dependence on µ2(L0). The authors further propose an unsupervised algorithm that uses RPCA to construct the dictionary for LRR, demonstrating improved performance over RPCA on both synthetic and real-world motion data.

This work builds on the established RPCA framework [4] and LRR [13], but makes an important contribution by identifying and addressing a specific failure mode of RPCA that has been largely overlooked in the literature. The paper connects coherence parameters (standard in matrix recovery literature [3, 4]) to real-world data structures (clustering), providing valuable theoretical insights into the nature of these parameters.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant limitation in RPCA**: The paper effectively demonstrates that RPCA's performance degrades when data exhibits clustering structures (Figure 2), which is a common real-world scenario. This is a valuable contribution as most theoretical analyses of RPCA assume incoherence, which is often not satisfied in practice.

2. **Theoretical contribution**: The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution by proving that LRR with a low-rank dictionary can avoid the µ2 parameter (Theorem 1). This is a meaningful extension of existing RPCA theory. The proof technique is sound and builds on established matrix recovery methods.

3. **Practical algorithm**: The proposed unsupervised algorithm (Algorithm 1) is simple and effective, leveraging existing RPCA results to construct a dictionary for LRR. The computational complexity analysis (O(n²r_A) vs. RPCA's O(n³)) is well-justified and shows that the method is scalable.

4. **Strong experimental validation**: The experiments on both synthetic data (Figure 4) and real motion sequences (Table 1) provide convincing evidence of the method's superiority over RPCA. The 40% relative improvement in clustering error rates on real data is particularly compelling.

5. **Insightful connection between theory and practice**: The paper successfully bridges theoretical concepts (coherence parameters) with practical data structures (clustering), providing a deeper understanding of why RPCA fails in certain scenarios.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty**: The paper claims that "for the first time, this paper studies the problem of recovering low-rank, and coherent (or less incoherent as equal) matrices from their corrupted versions." However, the connection between coherence and clustering has been touched on in related work, such as the work on robust subspace clustering [14, 15] and the analysis of coherence in subspace clustering [18]. The paper should more carefully position itself against these related works.

2. **Theoretical limitations**: The theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1 and 2) still depend on µ1(A), the coherence parameter of the dictionary A, rather than eliminating coherence dependence entirely. The paper claims to "avoid the coherence parameters" but actually replaces one coherence parameter (µ2(L0)) with another (µ1(A)). This is a subtle but important distinction that needs clarification.

3. **Sensitivity to assumptions**: The paper assumes U0 ⊂ UA (the column space of L0 is contained in the column space of A), which is a strong assumption. The authors don't adequately address how sensitive their method is to violations of this assumption. In practice, RPCA's estimate L̂0 might not perfectly capture U0, especially when the data is highly coherent.

4. **Limited comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper only compares with RPCA, but there are several more recent robust PCA methods that should be included for a fair comparison (e.g., Outlier Pursuit [25], Robust PCA via Outlier Pursuit [24], and the more recent methods that use structured sparsity or adaptive regularization).

5. **Scalability concerns**: The paper states that LRR has complexity O(n²r_A) compared to RPCA's O(n³), but doesn't address how r_A (the rank of the dictionary) scales with problem size. In practice, r_A could be large, potentially negating the complexity advantage.

## Specific Technical Concerns

1. **Figure 2's explanation of the µ2-phenomenon**: While the figure shows that µ2 increases with cluster number, the paper doesn't provide a clear mathematical explanation for why this happens. A brief derivation of how the coherence parameter changes with clustering would strengthen the argument.

2. **Algorithm 1's step 3**: The step that "forms L̃0 by using the rank-r̂0 approximation of L̂0" is redundant because L̂0 is already the output of RPCA, which produces a low-rank estimate. The rank of L̂0 should already be approximately r0 (the true rank), so this step might not be necessary.

3. **Theoretical bound comparison**: The paper claims that the bound rank(L0) ≤ O(n₂/log n₁) is "looser" than RPCA's bound of rank(L0) ≤ O(n₂/(log n₁)²). However, this is misleading - a looser bound actually means the theoretical guarantee is weaker, not stronger. The paper should clarify that their bound is less restrictive (i.e., allows for higher rank) than RPCA's bound.

4. **Real-world application context**: The motion data experiment (Section 4.2) uses a 10% missing data rate and replaces missing entries with Bernoulli ±1, which is an unusual corruption model. The paper should explain why this model is appropriate for motion sequences.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written, technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the robust matrix recovery literature. The identification of the µ2-phenomenon and its connection to clustering structures is insightful and addresses a real limitation of current RPCA methods. The proposed method is simple, theoretically justified, and shows clear experimental improvements over RPCA.

The paper's main limitations are that the improvement over RPCA might not be dramatic enough to justify the additional complexity in all applications, and that the theoretical guarantees still depend on coherence parameters (just of the dictionary rather than the data). However, these are common challenges in theoretical work on matrix recovery.

The paper meets the quality, clarity, and significance criteria for NeurIPS. The originality is somewhat incremental but still significant, as it addresses a specific failure mode of RPCA that has not been thoroughly explored in the literature. The results are important for practitioners working with real-world data that often exhibits clustering structures.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, and the experimental results are convincing. The main areas for improvement are:

1. Clarify the relationship between the coherence parameters and avoid overstating that coherence is "avoided" (it's replaced, not eliminated).
2. Include comparisons with more recent robust PCA methods beyond RPCA.
3. Address the sensitivity of the method to violations of the U0 ⊂ UA assumption.
4. Provide a clearer explanation of why the rank-r̂0 approximation step (step 3 of Algorithm 1) is necessary.

These revisions would strengthen the paper and make the contribution clearer to readers.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Identifies a significant limitation of RPCA (the µ2-phenomenon) that is overlooked in most theoretical analyses
- Provides a theoretically sound method to address this limitation
- Demonstrates clear experimental improvements over RPCA on both synthetic and real data
- Offers valuable insights into the relationship between coherence parameters and real-world data structures
- The proposed algorithm is simple and practical for implementation

**Con Acceptance:**
- The improvement over RPCA, while statistically significant, might not be dramatic enough for all applications
- The theoretical guarantees still depend on a coherence parameter (of the dictionary)
- The paper doesn't fully address how sensitive the method is to the U0 ⊂ UA assumption
- Limited comparison with more recent robust PCA methods

Overall, the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses, and the contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS.
