Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies recovery of a low-rank matrix L0 and a sparse corruption matrix S0 from a data matrix X = L0 + S0 in regimes where L0 is “coherent.” It argues that classical RPCA (minimize nuclear norm of L and l1 norm of S subject to X = L + S) can fail when the data exhibit extra structure beyond low-rankness, especially clustering of columns within a union of subspaces. The paper characterizes this via coherence parameters µ1, µ2, µ3 and documents a “µ2-phenomenon”: as the number of clusters increases (with fixed overall rank), the second coherence parameter µ2 grows, tightening RPCA’s success condition and hurting performance.

To address this, the paper turns to Low-Rank Representation (LRR): minimize nuclear norm of Z and l1 norm of S subject to X = AZ + S, where A is a pre-specified dictionary. The main theoretical contribution is that if A is itself low-rank and its column space contains the true column space U0 of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), then exact (and stable, in the noisy case) recovery is achievable without invoking µ2 in the success condition; the bounds depend on rank(A), µ1(A), and sparsity level. This suggests learning a low-rank dictionary that spans U0. The paper proposes a simple unsupervised algorithm: run RPCA to estimate L0, truncate to an estimated rank, normalize columns to form A, then run LRR with this dictionary. Experiments on synthetic union-of-subspace data and corrupted motion trajectories (Hopkins-style) show improved recovery regions and better downstream subspace clustering error compared to RPCA and to LRR with A = X.

This work builds on extensive literature on low-rank/sparse decomposition, including RPCA (Candès et al.), Outlier Pursuit (Xu et al., NIPS), and LRR (Liu et al., ICML/TPAMI). Novelty lies in analyzing coherence growth induced by clustering (µ2-phenomenon), showing that an appropriately low-rank dictionary can eliminate dependence on µ2, and proposing a practical two-stage procedure to learn such a dictionary.

Strengths:
- Clear identification and empirical illustration of a key limitation of RPCA due to coherence arising from clustered subspaces.
- Theoretical results (noiseless and noisy) giving exact and stable recovery for LRR with a low-rank dictionary whose column space contains U0, with success conditions independent of µ2. The dependence on µ1(A) and rank(A) is explicit.
- Simple, practical, and computationally reasonable algorithm that leverages RPCA to construct a low-rank dictionary; empirical gains on synthetic data (larger success region) and on motion segmentation (reduced clustering error).
- Conceptual contribution clarifying the role of coherence and how extra structures (e.g., clustering) interact with recovery guarantees.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
Quality (technical soundness):
- Key assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong; exact inclusion is unlikely in unsupervised settings. While the algorithm attempts to approximate this by using an RPCA estimate, no theory is provided to quantify when the learned A satisfies approximate spanning (e.g., bounds in terms of principal angles) and how that affects recovery. Extending Theorems 1–2 to the approximate inclusion case would strengthen the contribution.
- The bounds depend on µ1(A) and rank(A). Guidance or guarantees on how the proposed construction ensures small µ1(A) and suitably low rank(A) are limited to heuristics (rank truncation, normalization). Including analysis (or empirical measurements) of µ1(A) from the learned dictionaries would make the prescriptions more actionable.
- Stability bounds scale as O(√mn ε), which is standard but not compared to RPCA’s stability constants; a discussion of tightness would be useful. The claim that O(n2/log n1) is “probably the finest bound” is speculative without justification or lower bounds.
- The probabilistic model underlying success (e.g., distribution of support Ω and signs/magnitudes in S0) is not fully restated; making these assumptions explicit improves reproducibility.

Clarity:
- The exposition is generally clear with precise notation, but several key proofs are deferred to the “full paper.” Including proof sketches or lemmas that highlight where µ2 is avoided would help readers assess the novelty of the analysis.
- Algorithmic details (e.g., success criterion in synthetic experiments, stopping tolerances, rank thresholding choice 10^-3 σ1, sensitivity to λ and ε) are not discussed. A brief sensitivity analysis would improve clarity.

Originality:
- LRR with A = X is well-studied; using a learned low-rank dictionary and proving guarantees independent of µ2 given U0 ⊂ UA appear novel. The µ2-phenomenon connection to clustering provides a useful perspective.
- The practical two-stage procedure (RPCA → dictionary → LRR) is simple; while intuitive, establishing even partial theoretical justification for this pipeline would improve originality beyond empirical validation.

Significance:
- The problem is important: coherence is a known bottleneck for RPCA, and clustered subspaces are ubiquitous. If the conditions can be met or approximated, the approach could be impactful in subspace clustering, vision, and large-scale data analysis.
- Experimental evidence is promising but limited: synthetic data explore a grid of ranks and sparsity, but real-data evaluation uses only 11 motion sequences with artificial ±1 fills for missing entries. Broader benchmarks, more realistic corruption models, and comparisons against additional robust methods (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, robust matrix factorization/AltProj variants) would strengthen significance claims.
- Reporting runtime and an ablation (RPCA only vs. LRR with learned A vs. LRR with A = X; effect of Steps 2–4) would substantiate the computational and algorithmic claims.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a recognized limitation of RPCA with a principled analysis and a practical method.
- Theoretical guarantees that avoid µ2 under clear (if strong) conditions.
- Empirical improvements on both synthetic and real tasks; method is simple to adopt.

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong dictionary assumption (U0 ⊂ UA) with no theory for the unsupervised construction; the main practical algorithm lacks guarantees.
- Limited empirical scope and baselines; reliance on artificially corrupted small datasets.
- Some claims (e.g., “finest bound”) not rigorously justified; proofs deferred.

Constructive feedback:
- Provide theoretical robustness to approximate spanning (U0 approximately contained in UA), with error bounds depending on subspace angles and dictionary rank.
- Include measurements of µ2(L0) and µ1(A) in experiments to empirically corroborate the µ2-phenomenon and the proposed remedy.
- Expand empirical evaluation: larger and more varied datasets, natural corruption/missingness, additional baselines, and sensitivity/ablation studies.
- Discuss iterative refinement (updating A with ÂZ*) and provide empirical convergence/benefits.
- Clarify assumptions on S0 (support model, magnitudes), detail success metrics, and report computational costs.

Overall, the paper provides a clear perspective on coherence and a promising approach via low-rank dictionaries in LRR, with solid theoretical results under strong assumptions and encouraging preliminary experiments. Further theoretical and empirical strengthening would increase its impact.
