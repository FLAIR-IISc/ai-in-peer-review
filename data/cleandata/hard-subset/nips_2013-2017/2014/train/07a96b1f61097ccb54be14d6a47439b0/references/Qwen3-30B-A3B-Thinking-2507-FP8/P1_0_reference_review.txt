

# NIPS Peer Review

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a critical limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA), a widely used method for recovering low-rank matrices from corrupted observations. The authors identify that RPCA performs poorly when the low-rank component L0 exhibits clustering structures, which cause high coherence parameters (particularly µ2 and µ3), degrading recovery performance. They introduce the concept of the "µ2-phenomenon," demonstrating that as the number of clusters increases, the coherence parameters increase, making RPCA less successful.

The paper builds upon the RPCA framework (Candès et al., 2011) and the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) model (Liu et al., 2013), but makes two key contributions: (1) it establishes the connection between clustering structures in data and coherence parameters, showing that coherence is not merely a theoretical assumption but reflects intrinsic data structures; and (2) it demonstrates that LRR with a carefully constructed low-rank dictionary can avoid the coherence parameters that degrade RPCA performance.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS literature, extending the rich body of work on robust low-rank matrix recovery (including RPCA, LRR, and related approaches) while addressing a previously overlooked practical limitation. It connects to seminal work by Candès et al. (2011) and Liu et al. (2013), but provides a deeper understanding of coherence parameters and their relationship to real-world data structures.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Theoretical Insight**: The paper provides a clear and important theoretical contribution by establishing the relationship between clustering structures and coherence parameters (µ2 and µ3), revealing that coherence is not just a technical assumption but reflects real data structures. This insight helps explain why RPCA often performs better on synthetic data than on real-world data.

2. **Sound Theoretical Analysis**: The authors prove Theorem 1 (noiseless case) and Theorem 2 (noisy case) with clear conditions under which LRR can successfully recover coherent data. The proof techniques are rigorous and build upon established matrix recovery theory.

3. **Practical Algorithm**: The unsupervised algorithm (Algorithm 1) is well-motivated, simple to implement, and demonstrates significant empirical improvements over RPCA in both synthetic and real-world experiments. The authors provide a thorough experimental evaluation on randomly generated matrices and motion sequences from the Hopkins155 database.

4. **Clear Interpretation of Coherence**: The paper makes an important contribution to the understanding of coherence parameters, showing that they are "not 'assumptions' for a proof, but rather some excellent quantities that relate in nature to the extra structures (beyond low-rankness) intrinsically existing in L0." This insight has implications for other matrix recovery methods.

5. **Thorough Experimental Validation**: The experiments are comprehensive and well-designed, with both synthetic data (controlling for rank and sparsity) and real-world motion data. The results clearly demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms RPCA, with the success area being 47% wider in the synthetic experiments.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Explanation of µ2-phenomenon**: While the paper identifies the µ2-phenomenon (clustering increasing µ2), it doesn't provide a detailed explanation of *why* clustering structures increase µ2. The paper references "the full paper" for this explanation, but this should be included in the NIPS submission for completeness. The connection between clustering and coherence needs to be more explicitly articulated.

2. **Dependency on Initial RPCA Estimate**: Algorithm 1 relies on RPCA to estimate L0 first, which may be inaccurate for highly coherent data. The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze how errors in this initial estimate propagate through the algorithm. It would be valuable to include a sensitivity analysis showing how the performance depends on the quality of the RPCA estimate.

3. **Lack of Comparison with Alternative Methods**: The paper compares only with RPCA, but there are other methods designed to handle structured data or incorporate clustering information (e.g., subspace clustering methods like SSC or SIM). A comparison with these methods would strengthen the claims about the superiority of the proposed approach.

4. **Ambiguous Claims about Rank Bound**: The paper states that "O(n2/log n1) is probably the 'finest' bound one could accomplish in theory" without providing a matching lower bound or thorough discussion of why this bound cannot be improved. This claim needs more justification.

5. **Clarity Issues**: The paper uses "less incoherent" as a synonym for "coherent" in some places, which is confusing since high coherence means "more coherent" (not "less incoherent"). This terminology inconsistency should be corrected.

6. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the paper mentions that LRR is faster than RPCA when A is low-rank, it doesn't provide a detailed comparison of running times, especially for the proposed algorithm (which involves two convex programs). A thorough complexity analysis would strengthen the practical appeal of the method.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and thorough experimental validation. The claims are well-supported by both theory and experiments. The authors are careful in discussing the limitations of their approach.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written and organized, though some parts (like the explanation of the µ2-phenomenon) would benefit from greater clarity. The experimental results are well-presented, with clear figures and tables.

**Originality**: The paper makes a significant contribution by identifying the limitation of RPCA with coherent data (due to clustering structures) and proposing a solution via LRR with a low-rank dictionary. While LRR has been studied before, the specific focus on the dictionary's rank and its connection to coherence is novel.

**Significance**: The results are important for practitioners working with real-world data where clustering structures are common (e.g., motion segmentation, image processing). The proposed method outperforms RPCA in both synthetic and real-world experiments, demonstrating practical value. The insights about coherence parameters could influence future work in robust matrix recovery.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper identifies a critical limitation of a widely used method (RPCA) that has not been previously well understood.
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides clear conditions for successful recovery.
- The proposed unsupervised algorithm is practical, simple, and demonstrably outperforms RPCA.
- The insights about coherence parameters have broader implications for understanding matrix recovery methods.
- The experimental validation is comprehensive and well-designed.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The explanation of why clustering increases µ2 is insufficient.
- The algorithm's dependency on the initial RPCA estimate is not thoroughly analyzed.
- The paper doesn't compare with other methods designed for structured data.
- The claim about the "finest" rank bound is overstated without a lower bound.
- The paper could be clearer about the relationship between coherence and data structures.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding the limitations of RPCA with coherent data and proposes a practical solution that outperforms RPCA in both synthetic and real-world experiments. The theoretical insights about coherence parameters are significant and should be of interest to the NIPS community. While there are some weaknesses (particularly regarding the explanation of the µ2-phenomenon and the dependency on the initial RPCA estimate), these are not severe enough to outweigh the paper's strengths and contributions.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS community, addressing a practical limitation of a widely used method with both theoretical and empirical rigor. The insights about coherence parameters have implications beyond the specific problem addressed, potentially influencing future work in robust matrix recovery.

I would recommend the authors address the following points in the camera-ready version:
1. Provide a clearer explanation of why clustering structures increase µ2 (or include a detailed derivation in the paper).
2. Include a sensitivity analysis showing how the performance of Algorithm 1 depends on the quality of the initial RPCA estimate.
3. Clarify the terminology around coherence (avoid using "less incoherent" as a synonym for "coherent").
4. Consider adding a comparison with at least one alternative method designed for structured data.

These revisions would strengthen an already strong paper and would address the main weaknesses identified in this review.
