This paper addresses the problem of robust PCA in the presence of corrupted data, specifically focusing on the impact of data coherence on the performance of standard RPCA. The authors identify a “µ2-phenomenon” where clustering structures within the low-rank component lead to increased coherence, degrading RPCA’s recovery guarantees. They propose using a low-rank dictionary within the LRR framework to mitigate this issue, providing theoretical justification and empirical validation.

The core idea – that coherence, often treated as an assumption, is actually tied to underlying data structure – is a potentially valuable insight. The analysis connecting clustering to coherence parameters is interesting, although the explanation could be more intuitive and detailed. The proposed solution, leveraging a low-rank dictionary in LRR, is a reasonable approach to address the identified problem.

However, several aspects of the paper raise concerns. First, the theoretical analysis feels incomplete. Theorem 1 establishes recovery under specific conditions on the dictionary ‘A’ (U0 ⊂ UA), but the practical implications of enforcing this condition in unsupervised settings are not fully explored. The algorithm proposed (Algorithm 1) relies on RPCA as a first step, meaning the performance gain is contingent on RPCA's initial ability to provide a reasonable estimate. If RPCA fails significantly due to high coherence *before* dictionary learning, the subsequent LRR step may not recover well. The connection to related matrix factorization techniques is acknowledged but remains superficial. The experiments, while demonstrating improvement over RPCA, lack a comprehensive comparison to other robust PCA variants, particularly those addressing coherence directly (e.g., methods employing reweighting or more sophisticated regularization). The performance improvement on motion sequences is encouraging, but the experimental setup needs more detail – what are the precise corruption levels, and how were the error rates calculated for each clustering method?  The impact of the parameter λ isn’t thoroughly investigated. Furthermore, the analysis focuses predominantly on the noiseless case. While a theorem is presented for the noisy scenario, the impact of noise on the dictionary learning step isn’t adequately addressed.

**Strengths:**

*   Identifies a connection between data clustering and coherence in low-rank matrix recovery.
*   Proposes a reasonable approach (LRR with a low-rank dictionary) to mitigate the issue.
*   Provides theoretical analysis to support the claims (though limited).
*   Demonstrates empirical improvements over RPCA on synthetic and real data.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Theoretical analysis is incomplete and lacks practical guidance for unsupervised dictionary learning.
*   Algorithm heavily relies on the initial performance of RPCA.
*   Limited comparison to other robust PCA methods.
*   Insufficient detail in the experimental setup.
*   The noisy case analysis lacks detail regarding the impact of noise on dictionary learning.
*   The explanation of the µ2 phenomenon could be more intuitive.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper identifies a subtle but potentially important issue regarding coherence and data structure in robust PCA.
*   The proposed solution offers a practical way to improve performance in certain scenarios.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical contributions are incremental and lack the rigor expected for NIPS.
*   The experimental evaluation is not comprehensive enough to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method.
*   The reliance on RPCA as a starting point limits the overall impact.
*   The presentation could be more clear and detailed, particularly regarding the practical implications of the theoretical results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I am leaning towards **reject** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The paper addresses an interesting problem and proposes a reasonable solution, but the theoretical analysis and experimental evaluation are not strong enough for acceptance at NIPS. The authors need to significantly strengthen the theoretical justification, provide a more thorough comparison to existing methods, and provide more detailed experimental results. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would substantially improve the paper’s quality and increase its potential impact. This work feels more appropriate for a conference with less stringent requirements.
