

# Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper proposes an extension to Robust PCA (RPCA) called Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit (LRR) for handling coherent data, which is defined as data with clustering structures that increase coherence parameters (particularly µ2) and degrade RPCA performance. The authors claim that by using a low-rank dictionary matrix A in the LRR framework, they can avoid the coherence parameters that limit RPCA.

The paper makes a valid observation that RPCA can struggle with coherent data, particularly when data exhibits clustering structures (as demonstrated in Figure 2). The authors provide theoretical analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) showing that LRR with a low-rank dictionary A can recover low-rank matrices without relying on coherence parameters. Their proposed Algorithm 1 uses RPCA to construct a dictionary for LRR, with experimental results showing improved performance over RPCA on synthetic data and motion sequences.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper identifies a genuine limitation of RPCA that is acknowledged in the literature (e.g., Candès et al. [4] discuss coherence requirements).
2. The theoretical analysis is reasonably sound for the noiseless and noisy cases they consider.
3. The experimental results on synthetic data demonstrate a clear improvement over RPCA in the coherent regime (Figure 4 shows a 47% wider success area).
4. The algorithm is practical and computationally efficient (1.2x the cost of RPCA).

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of rigorous validation against established baselines:** The paper compares only to RPCA, but fails to benchmark against other state-of-the-art methods for robust low-rank recovery (e.g., Outlier Pursuit [25], LRR with other dictionaries [13, 14], or recent methods like [24] which also address coherence issues). This is a significant oversight for a paper claiming to improve upon RPCA.

2. **Theoretical claims lack sufficient empirical validation:** The paper claims that LRR with a low-rank dictionary "avoids" coherence parameters, but the experiments don't clearly demonstrate this. The success of Algorithm 1 depends on the quality of the dictionary constructed from RPCA's output, which may not be reliable when RPCA itself fails (which is precisely when coherence is high). The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of when their dictionary construction succeeds or fails.

3. **Overstated novelty:** The paper claims to be the "first to study" coherent data recovery, but this problem has been addressed in several prior works. For instance, Xu et al. [25] proposed "Robust PCA via Outlier Pursuit" which specifically addresses the case when the sparse component has large entries (which is related to coherence). The paper also fails to reference recent work on structured low-rank recovery (e.g., [15, 22]) that directly addresses the relationship between data structure and recovery performance.

4. **Lack of clarity on "coherence" in practical applications:** The paper demonstrates that clustering increases coherence (µ2), but doesn't explain how to detect coherence in real-world data or how to determine when their method should be applied. This is a significant gap for practitioners who need to decide when to use this approach versus RPCA.

5. **Unjustified claims about "coherence parameters being related to extra structures":** The paper states that coherence parameters "are not assumptions for a proof, but rather some excellent quantities that relate in nature to the extra structures," but provides no new evidence for this beyond what's already established in [4]. This claim appears to be a rephrasing of existing theory rather than a new insight.

**Significance and Originality:**
The paper makes a minor improvement to an established framework (LRR) rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach. While the problem of coherent data is valid, the solution proposed is incremental and not sufficiently validated against competing methods. The paper's claim that this is a "new" approach to coherent data recovery is overstated, as similar ideas have been explored in prior work on structured low-rank recovery.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a reasonable extension to existing work that addresses a genuine limitation of RPCA, but it falls short of NIPS standards for methodological rigor and validation. The experimental validation is insufficiently comprehensive, and the paper fails to adequately compare against relevant state-of-the-art methods. The theoretical claims are not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence, and the novelty is overstated relative to the existing literature.

I recommend rejection, as the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of improvement over established methods, and the contribution appears to be incremental rather than transformative. The authors should significantly strengthen the experimental validation (including comparison to more recent methods) and clarify the practical utility of their approach before reconsidering for publication.
