

# Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper proposes an extension to Robust PCA (RPCA) that addresses what the authors call "coherent data" - data with clustering structures that increase coherence parameters (particularly μ2), which they claim degrades RPCA's performance. The authors introduce a method using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary matrix A, arguing that when A is low-rank and satisfies U0 ⊂ UA (column space of L0 is contained in column space of A), LRR can avoid the coherence parameters that limit RPCA.

The paper makes several theoretical claims (Theorems 1 and 2) showing that LRR with proper dictionaries can recover low-rank and sparse components under certain conditions. They then propose Algorithm 1, which uses RPCA to construct the dictionary matrix A for LRR. The authors claim their method outperforms RPCA on synthetic data (47% wider success area) and on motion sequences from the Hopkins155 dataset (40% relative improvement in clustering error).

**Strengths:**
- The paper identifies an interesting theoretical limitation of RPCA related to coherence parameters, which is a valid concern in the robust PCA literature.
- The connection between clustering structures and increased coherence parameters (the "μ2-phenomenon") is well-documented with examples.
- The proposed algorithm is computationally efficient (1.2x the time of RPCA) under the assumption that the dictionary matrix is low-rank.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of rigorous empirical validation:** The paper's claims about superior performance rely on limited experiments. The synthetic data experiments use a single generation method for L0 (5 subspaces with ranks 1-20), which doesn't capture the diversity of real-world coherent data. The Hopkins155 experiments use only 11 sequences with 10% missing data, which is insufficient to establish generalizability. Crucially, the paper doesn't compare to other established methods for handling coherent data or clustering structures (e.g., subspace clustering methods that explicitly account for clusters).

2. **Circularity in the proposed algorithm:** Algorithm 1 uses RPCA to construct the dictionary for LRR. However, RPCA is precisely the method that the authors claim fails on coherent data. If RPCA fails to accurately recover L0 (which is exactly the problem they're trying to solve), then the dictionary construction will be flawed, making their method unreliable in the very scenarios where it's needed.

3. **Overstated theoretical claims:** The theoretical guarantees (Theorem 1) require U0 ⊂ UA, which is a strong assumption that's not clearly satisfied in practice. The paper claims this condition can be approximately achieved in unsupervised settings, but provides no evidence or analysis supporting this claim. The paper also doesn't address how often this condition holds in real-world data.

4. **Insufficient comparison to relevant literature:** The paper doesn't adequately compare to recent advances in robust PCA that handle structured data. For instance, works like "Robust PCA via Outlier Pursuit" (Xu et al., NIPS 2010) or "Robust Subspace Clustering" (Liu et al., CVPR 2010) address structured noise and could provide more relevant baselines.

5. **Mischaracterization of RPCA's limitations:** The paper claims RPCA "becomes brittle" with coherent data, but RPCA's theoretical guarantees (Candès et al., JACM 2011) already acknowledge that coherence parameters are necessary for recovery. The authors' proposed solution essentially requires the same conditions RPCA already requires, but with additional constraints (low-rank dictionary with U0 ⊂ UA).

**Critical assessment:**
While the paper identifies a genuine theoretical limitation of RPCA, it fails to provide rigorous evidence that this limitation is significant in practice or that their proposed solution reliably addresses it. The theoretical guarantees require conditions that are not practically verifiable, and the empirical validation is insufficient to support the claimed improvements. The algorithmic approach is also circular, as it relies on RPCA (which fails on the very data it's designed to handle) to construct the dictionary.

The paper's main contribution appears to be a minor variation on existing LRR methods rather than a substantial advancement in robust low-rank recovery. The authors fail to demonstrate that their method provides meaningful improvements over existing approaches in realistic settings, which is a critical requirement for NIPS.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper identifies an interesting theoretical point about coherence parameters, it fails to provide sufficient evidence that this point is practically significant or that their proposed solution effectively addresses the problem. The theoretical claims are not adequately supported by empirical validation, and the proposed algorithm has fundamental limitations that undermine its practical utility. The paper does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way, and the experiments do not provide convincing evidence of the claimed improvements over established methods. For NIPS, the paper would need to substantially strengthen its empirical validation, address the circularity in the proposed algorithm, and provide more convincing evidence that the proposed method solves a meaningful problem in real-world applications.
