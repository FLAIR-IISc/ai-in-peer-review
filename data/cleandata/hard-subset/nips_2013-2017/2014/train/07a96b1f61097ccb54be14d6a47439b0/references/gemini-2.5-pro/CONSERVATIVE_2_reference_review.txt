**Summary:**
This paper investigates a key limitation of the widely-used Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) method for decomposing a matrix `X` into a low-rank component `L0` and a sparse component `S0`. The authors correctly identify that RPCA's performance guarantees degrade when `L0` is "coherent," a situation they convincingly argue arises naturally when the data exhibits clustering structures (the "µ2-phenomenon"). To address this, they propose shifting from the RPCA formulation (`X = L + S`) to the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework, where the low-rank component is modeled as `L = AZ`. The paper's central theoretical contribution is to show that if one uses a pre-specified, low-rank dictionary `A` whose column space contains that of the true `L0`, the recovery guarantees of LRR are independent of the problematic coherence parameters of `L0`. For the practical unsupervised case, they propose a two-stage algorithm: first, run RPCA to get an initial estimate of `L0`, and second, use this estimate to construct the dictionary `A` for a final LRR recovery step. Experiments on synthetic and motion-capture data show this approach outperforms standard RPCA.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Problem Motivation:** The paper addresses a well-defined and important problem. The limitations of RPCA in the face of coherent data are known, and the authors provide a clear and intuitive explanation for why this coherence arises in practical scenarios like clustered data. This "µ2-phenomenon" is a nice conceptual contribution.
2.  **Theoretical Insight:** The main theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) are the strongest part of the paper. They provide a sound mathematical justification for why the LRR framework, under the assumption of a suitable dictionary `A`, can circumvent the coherence issue that plagues RPCA. This provides a clear principle for dictionary design: the dictionary should be low-rank and its column space should span the column space of the ground truth low-rank matrix.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out, the connection between coherence and clustering is well-explained, and the proposed approach is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Disconnect between Theory and Practice:** There is a significant gap between the conditions required by the theory and what the proposed practical algorithm can guarantee. The theorems assume the existence of a dictionary `A` where `U0 ⊂ UA`. However, Algorithm 1 constructs `A` from the output of an initial RPCA run. The entire premise of the paper is that RPCA performs poorly on coherent data. If RPCA yields a poor estimate `L̂0`, the resulting dictionary `Â` will also be poor, and the condition `U0 ⊂ UÂ` is unlikely to hold. The LRR step's theoretical guarantees would then be void. The paper does not provide any analysis of the conditions under which Algorithm 1 is expected to work, i.e., when the initial RPCA step is "good enough" to produce a useful dictionary but not so good that the second step is redundant. This makes the proposed algorithm a heuristic whose success is not rigorously established.
2.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** While the experiments show positive results, the validation against the primary baseline (RPCA) is not as thorough as it should be.
    *   The comparison is only against the standard RPCA formulation from Candès et al. (2011). Several other variants of robust PCA exist (e.g., Outlier Pursuit by Xu et al., 2010), which might be more robust to certain data structures. A comparison against a wider range of established methods is needed.
    *   Both RPCA and the proposed method are run with a single, fixed parameter setting (`λ = 1/√n1`). The performance of RPCA can be sensitive to this parameter. It is possible that a better-tuned RPCA could close the performance gap shown in Figure 4. A more rigorous comparison would involve a parameter sweep for the baseline method to ensure the comparison is against a strong, well-tuned competitor.
3.  **Limited Novelty of the Algorithmic Approach:** The core idea of using an initial estimate to refine a solution in a second stage is a common strategy in optimization and signal processing. While its application here within the LRR framework is novel, the practical algorithm itself is an intuitive heuristic. The main contribution lies in the theoretical justification for the *ideal* case, not in the robustness of the proposed two-step procedure itself.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Identifies and provides insight into an important failure mode of RPCA.
*   Provides a sound theoretical result (Theorems 1 & 2) that establishes a new principle for recovering coherent low-rank data using the LRR framework, assuming a suitable dictionary.
*   The connection between clustering and coherence is a useful contribution to the field's understanding.
*   The empirical results, though limited, are promising and demonstrate a clear benefit on both synthetic and real-world tasks.

**Arguments against Rejection:**
*   The main practical contribution, Algorithm 1, is a heuristic that lacks theoretical guarantees. Its success relies on the initial RPCA step performing reasonably well, which appears to contradict the paper's core motivation.
*   The empirical evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous. The comparison to a single baseline with a fixed parameter does not provide conclusive evidence that the proposed method is superior to the state of the art.
*   The paper oversells the algorithm by grounding it in a theoretical result that relies on an assumption (a given, good dictionary) that the algorithm itself cannot guarantee.

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents an interesting analysis of a known weakness in RPCA and offers a theoretically elegant potential solution based on LRR. The identification of the "µ2-phenomenon" is insightful. However, the work is let down by a significant methodological gap between its theory and its proposed practical algorithm. The algorithm is a heuristic whose performance is not theoretically justified, and the experimental validation is not comprehensive enough to convincingly demonstrate its superiority over well-established and properly tuned baselines. While the theoretical ideas are valuable, the paper as a whole does not meet the high bar of methodological soundness required for publication at NIPS. I lean towards rejection but would reconsider if the authors could provide either a theoretical analysis of their two-stage algorithm or a much more extensive and rigorous empirical evaluation.
