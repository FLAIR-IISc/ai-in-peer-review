

# Peer Review: "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) when dealing with coherent data - specifically, data exhibiting clustering structures. The authors demonstrate that when data points lie on a union of subspaces (rather than being uniformly distributed), the coherence parameters of the low-rank component increase, degrading RPCA's performance. The paper's key insight is that by using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a properly constructed dictionary matrix A (which is itself low-rank and contains the column space of the low-rank component), this coherence dependence can be avoided. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for both noiseless and noisy settings, and propose a practical unsupervised algorithm (Algorithm 1) that uses RPCA to construct the dictionary before applying LRR. The approach is validated on both synthetic data and motion sequences from the Hopkins155 database, showing significant improvements over RPCA.

This work builds upon and extends the RPCA framework established by Candès et al. (2011), while also connecting to the LRR framework introduced by Liu et al. (2013). It addresses an important gap in the literature by identifying the connection between coherence parameters and data structures, which has been largely overlooked in previous robust PCA work.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a novel theoretical analysis showing that coherence parameters (specifically μ2) can be avoided when using LRR with a low-rank dictionary satisfying U0 ⊂ UA. Theorems 1 and 2 are well-proven and provide clear recovery conditions that are more relaxed than RPCA's requirements.

2. **Important Practical Insight**: The paper reveals the physical meaning of coherence parameters, demonstrating they are not merely theoretical assumptions but are intrinsically related to extra data structures (like clustering). This insight significantly advances our understanding of RPCA's limitations.

3. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The experiments on both synthetic data (Figure 4) and real motion sequences (Table 1) provide compelling evidence of the method's superiority over RPCA. The 40% relative improvement in clustering error rates on motion sequences is particularly impressive.

4. **Practical Algorithm**: Algorithm 1 is simple, effective, and computationally efficient (only ~1.2x more expensive than RPCA). The unsupervised nature of the approach makes it applicable to real-world scenarios without requiring labeled data.

5. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper effectively motivates the problem by demonstrating how ubiquitous clustering structures in real data lead to increased coherence parameters, which degrades RPCA's performance (the "μ2-phenomenon").

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison to Recent Methods**: While the paper compares to RPCA, it doesn't compare with more recent robust PCA methods like Robust PCA via Outlier Pursuit (Xu et al., 2010) or the more recent Robust PCA via L1 Minimization (Liu et al., 2012). This weakens the significance claim as the comparison is not with the current state-of-the-art.

2. **Insufficient Explanation of μ2-phenomenon**: The paper demonstrates that μ2 increases with cluster number (Figure 2), but doesn't provide a rigorous mathematical explanation for why this occurs. A more detailed analysis of how clustering affects the row space and thus μ2 would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

3. **Dictionary Construction Details**: The paper states that the dictionary construction in Algorithm 1 is "heuristic" but doesn't thoroughly analyze how sensitive the performance is to the specific steps (Steps 2-3). It would be valuable to understand the margin of error in the dictionary construction.

4. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the paper mentions that LRR with low-rank A is faster than RPCA, it doesn't provide a detailed complexity analysis showing how the computational advantage scales with problem size.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the robust PCA literature by addressing a critical limitation of RPCA that has been largely overlooked. The connection between coherence parameters and data structures is insightful and well-supported by both theory and experiments. The proposed method provides a practical solution to a real-world problem with demonstrated improvements over the current standard.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The main weaknesses are the limited comparison to more recent methods and insufficient explanation of the μ2-phenomenon. However, these do not undermine the paper's core contribution.

**Recommendation: Accept**

## Final Comments

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses an important problem in robust statistics with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The work advances our understanding of coherence parameters in low-rank matrix recovery and provides a practical solution that outperforms existing methods on real-world data. The paper's theoretical insights about the relationship between coherence parameters and data structures are particularly valuable and should be of interest to the broader machine learning community.

I recommend this paper for acceptance at NIPS. The authors have made a solid contribution that advances both the theoretical understanding and practical application of robust matrix recovery. With minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above (particularly adding a comparison to more recent robust PCA methods), this paper would be an excellent contribution to the conference.
