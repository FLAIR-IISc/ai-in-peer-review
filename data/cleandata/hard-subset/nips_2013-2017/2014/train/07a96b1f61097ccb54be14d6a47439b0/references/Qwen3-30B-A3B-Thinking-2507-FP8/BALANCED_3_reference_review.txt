

# Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper addresses an important limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) when dealing with coherent data (data exhibiting clustering structures), which causes RPCA's performance to degrade. The authors propose a novel approach using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully constructed dictionary matrix that is low-rank and contains the column space of the true low-rank matrix. The paper provides theoretical guarantees showing that LRR with such a dictionary can avoid coherence parameters that harm RPCA, and proposes an unsupervised algorithm to learn the dictionary.

The paper builds upon the RPCA literature [4] and LRR framework [13], but makes a significant contribution by identifying the connection between clustering structures in data and coherence parameters (particularly µ2), which had not been previously recognized as a fundamental limitation of RPCA. The authors demonstrate that when data exhibits clustering structures, the second coherence parameter µ2 increases with the number of clusters, directly impacting RPCA's performance. This insight is valuable for understanding the limitations of existing robust PCA methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Insight:** The paper makes a crucial observation about the relationship between clustering structures in data and coherence parameters, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The authors clearly demonstrate that coherence parameters are not merely "assumptions" but are intrinsically related to data structures.

2. **Theoretical Contributions:** Theorems 1 and 2 provide rigorous theoretical guarantees for LRR with a low-rank dictionary, showing it can avoid coherence parameters. The conditions for exact recovery are well-motivated and provide clear design principles for dictionary construction.

3. **Practical Algorithm:** Algorithm 1 provides a simple, effective unsupervised method to construct the dictionary using RPCA's output. The experimental results on synthetic data and motion sequences show clear improvements over RPCA (47% wider success area on synthetic data, 40% relative error reduction on motion segmentation).

4. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The connection between clustering, coherence, and RPCA's limitations is effectively explained, and the notation is well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical Conditions:** The conditions for exact recovery (U0 ⊂ UA and A being low-rank) are restrictive. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how to verify or enforce U0 ⊂ UA in unsupervised settings, which is critical for practical application. The claim that "U0 ⊂ UA can be viewed as supervision information" is not fully developed.

2. **Experimental Comparison:** The experiments compare only to RPCA, but don't include more recent robust PCA variants (e.g., [24, 25, 26]) that might address some of the same limitations. A comparison to these methods would strengthen the claims.

3. **Computational Complexity:** The claim that LRR is "much lower than RPCA" is not supported with concrete complexity analysis. The paper states that "the complexity of solving the LRR problem (1.3) is O(n²rA) (assuming m = n), which is much lower than that of RPCA (which requires O(n³))" but doesn't provide evidence that rA is typically small enough to make this true in practice.

4. **Clustering-Coherence Relationship:** While the paper shows that clustering increases µ2 (Figure 2), it lacks a formal proof of this relationship. The connection between clustering and coherence parameters needs more rigorous mathematical justification.

**Assessment:**

*Quality:* The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical contributions and well-designed experiments. The theoretical guarantees are rigorous, and the empirical results support the claims. However, the restrictive conditions for exact recovery and lack of comparison to recent methods slightly weaken the quality assessment.

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, making complex concepts accessible. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproduction.

*Originality:* The connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters is novel and significant. While LRR has been studied before, the specific focus on coherent data and the low-rank dictionary condition represents a meaningful advance.

*Significance:* The problem addressed is highly relevant to real-world applications where data often exhibits clustering structures. The proposed method provides practical improvements over RPCA in these scenarios, making it potentially valuable for practitioners.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of robust PCA and presents a practical method for handling coherent data. The core insight about coherence parameters and data structures is significant and well-supported. While the theoretical conditions are somewhat restrictive, the unsupervised algorithm (Algorithm 1) provides a practical solution that works well in experiments.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Strengthen the connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters with a formal mathematical proof.
2. Include comparisons to more recent robust PCA methods in the experiments.
3. Provide more detailed computational complexity analysis to support the claim that LRR is significantly faster than RPCA.
4. Discuss how the condition U0 ⊂ UA can be verified or approximated in unsupervised settings.
5. Consider including a discussion of the relationship between the proposed method and other dictionary learning approaches for robust PCA.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. With the suggested improvements, it would be an excellent fit for NeurIPS. I recommend acceptance.
