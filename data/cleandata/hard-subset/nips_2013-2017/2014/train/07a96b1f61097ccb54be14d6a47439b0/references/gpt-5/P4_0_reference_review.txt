Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses robust low-rank recovery when the low-rank component exhibits coherence induced by extra structure (e.g., clustering). While RPCA recovers a low-rank L0 and sparse S0 from X=L0+S0 under incoherence assumptions, the authors argue that in many practical settings the low-rank component is coherent, especially as the number of underlying clusters increases. They formalize this as the “µ2-phenomenon”: when data points come from a union of subspaces whose span has fixed rank, the second coherence parameter µ2 (and consequently µ3) grows with the number of clusters, tightening RPCA’s success condition.

To address this, the paper analyzes Low-Rank Representation (LRR): minimize ||Z||* + λ||S||1 subject to X=AZ+S. The main theoretical contribution is that if the dictionary A is low-rank and its column space contains that of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), then exact (noiseless) and near-exact (noisy) recovery are possible without dependence on µ2 (and µ3), with a rank condition depending on µ1(A) and log n. This provides a prescription for choosing A to handle coherent data. The paper proposes a simple unsupervised two-stage algorithm: (1) run RPCA to estimate L0, (2) form a low-rank dictionary A by normalizing columns of a rank-truncated estimate of L0, then (3) solve LRR with this A.

The work builds on a large literature on RPCA/PCP [Candès et al. 2011], LRR [Liu et al. 2010–2013], outlier-robust PCA [Xu et al., NIPS 2010; T-IT 2012], and related matrix recovery and subspace methods. The novelty here is the analysis of coherence due to clustering (µ2-phenomenon) and theoretical guarantees for LRR with general low-rank dictionaries that avoid µ2. Empirically, they compare RPCA vs. their two-stage LRR pipeline on synthetic clustered data and on motion segmentation sequences (Hopkins155 variants), showing improved recovery and clustering accuracy.

Quality
- Theory: Theorem 1 (noiseless) and Theorem 2 (noisy) provide recovery guarantees under (i) low-rank A with U0 ⊂ UA, (ii) a rank condition rank(L0) ≤ rank(A) ≤ O(n2/(µ1(A) log n1)), (iii) sparsity |Ω| ≤ (0.5−ε)mn (noiseless) or 0.35−ε (noisy), and λ=1/√n1. The avoidance of µ2 and µ3 in the conditions is clear. The U0 ⊂ UA condition is necessary for exactness since AZ=L0 implies this containment. The proofs are deferred to the full paper; the claims align with prior proof strategies in PCP/LRR analyses, but the paper should clearly state any randomness assumptions on Ω used to obtain high-probability statements (currently only its cardinality is specified).
- Algorithm: The unsupervised procedure is a pragmatic heuristic. The only theoretical link is that if RPCA already exactly recovers L0, then the follow-up LRR will also be exact—this does not explain why the method improves over RPCA when RPCA is imperfect on coherent data. Nonetheless, the prescription (low-rank A approximating U0) follows from the theory.
- Experiments: Synthetic experiments vary rank and sparsity and show a 47% larger “success area” for the proposed method compared to RPCA. Real-data experiments on corrupted motion sequences report lower subspace clustering error after recovery, with additional gains when using the learned dictionary in LRR compared to RPCA preprocessing. However, the real-data evaluation is limited in scope (11 sequences), uses an atypical corruption model for missing entries (filling with ±1), and does not include ablations (e.g., different dictionary constructions, mismatch U0 ⊄ UA, effect of dictionary rank/conditioning), parameter sensitivity, or comparisons to other robust PCA variants (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, GoDec).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written with clear notation, definitions of coherence parameters (µ1, µ2, µ3), and a coherent narrative from the µ2-phenomenon to the LRR-based remedy. Some details are deferred (“see the full paper”), including proofs and the precise probabilistic model for S0’s support; making these explicit would improve reproducibility. Minor typographical issues aside, the organization is adequate.

Originality
- Conceptually, the identification and empirical illustration of the µ2-phenomenon for clustered data, and the observation that LRR with a suitable low-rank dictionary can avoid µ2, are valuable and, to my knowledge, not covered in prior LRR work that mostly focused on A=X or orthonormal dictionaries.
- The specific two-stage algorithm is simple and resembles known practices (using an initial low-rank estimate to build a dictionary or basis), but the theoretical insight guiding the choice of dictionary is novel.

Significance
- Theoretical significance: Provides conditions under which coherent data can be recovered without µ2 dependence, clarifying the role of coherence and offering guidance for dictionary design in LRR.
- Practical significance: The two-stage method shows consistent improvements over RPCA in presented settings. Given the prevalence of clustered subspace data, these insights are likely to influence follow-up work on robust subspace recovery and segmentation.
- The current empirical validation is limited; broader benchmarks and stronger ablations would increase impact.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of why RPCA can fail on coherent clustered data; formalization via µ2 behavior.
- Recovery guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that depend only on µ1(A), not µ2/µ3.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with demonstrated improvement over RPCA on synthetic and motion data.
- Connection to and clarification of the role of coherence in matrix recovery.

Weaknesses
- Strong assumption U0 ⊂ UA in theory; unsupervised algorithm relies on RPCA to approximate U0, which may be unreliable precisely in the coherent regimes of interest; no theoretical guarantees for the unsupervised case beyond the trivial “no worse than RPCA when RPCA is exact.”
- Experimental scope is narrow; real-data corruption model may not reflect practical missingness; limited baselines and lack of ablations/sensitivity analyses.
- Some theoretical details (randomness of Ω, constants, and proof sketches) are deferred and should be included or summarized.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Clarify the probabilistic model for S0: is Ω assumed uniformly random for the high-probability bounds?
- Provide a robustness analysis when UA only approximately contains U0 (e.g., principal angle bounds) and when A’s rank exceeds rank(L0) by different margins.
- Include ablations: vary dictionary rank/conditioning, compare alternative dictionary constructions (e.g., from a small clean subset, learned bases), and measure performance vs. number of clusters at fixed rank to empirically support the µ2-phenomenon and LRR’s immunity.
- Compare against additional robust PCA baselines (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, AltProj/GoDec) and against LRR with A=X on the recovery task.
- Report sensitivity to λ and ε; justify fixed choices.
- Consider a more realistic handling of missing data (masking rather than replacing with ±1), or use formulations that explicitly model missingness.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a well-motivated gap: robust recovery under coherent low-rank structure.
- Provides new theoretical insight and guidance (avoidance of µ2 via low-rank dictionaries).
- Empirically improves over a strong baseline with a simple method.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theory relies on a strong dictionary condition; unsupervised algorithm lacks supporting guarantees and depends on RPCA’s initial success.
- Experimental validation is limited and misses important baselines and ablations.
- Some technical details are deferred, reducing verifiability.

Overall, the work offers a useful theoretical contribution and a practically effective heuristic, but would benefit from stronger empirical validation and theoretical treatment of the unsupervised setting.
