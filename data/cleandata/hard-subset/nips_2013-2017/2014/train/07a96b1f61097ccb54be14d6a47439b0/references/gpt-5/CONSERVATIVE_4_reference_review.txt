Summary
The paper studies recovery of low-rank plus sparse matrices when the low-rank component is “coherent,” i.e., has large coherence parameters due to extra structure such as clustering (union of subspaces). The authors argue that RPCA’s guarantees degrade with increasing coherence (notably µ2), and propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary A to “avoid” dependence on µ2. They provide recovery theorems in noiseless and noisy settings under the conditions that (i) the dictionary A is low-rank with column-wise normalization, (ii) the column space U0 of L0 is contained in the column space of A (U0 ⊂ UA), and (iii) sparse corruptions are not too dense and have random support. They then propose a two-stage unsupervised algorithm: run RPCA to get L̂0, construct A by normalizing columns of a rank-truncated L̂0, and finally run LRR with this A. On synthetic data (union-of-subspaces with Bernoulli ±1 corruptions) and a small set of motion sequences (Hopkins-style), this approach improves over RPCA and yields better downstream clustering error with SIM/LRRx/SSC.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant and well-studied problem (robust low-rank recovery) with a focus on a practically important failure mode (coherent data/clustered subspaces).
- Clear articulation of the “µ2-phenomenon” and its connection to union-of-subspaces structure.
- Theorems for LRR with a general dictionary A that remove dependence on µ2(L0) under U0 ⊂ UA, shifting the condition to µ1(A). Noisy and noiseless statements are provided.
- Simple, computationally reasonable two-stage algorithm and empirical improvements over RPCA on synthetic and motion data.

Weaknesses and concerns (with a conservative stance)
- Strong and circular assumptions for guarantees: The main theorems require U0 ⊂ UA. The proposed unsupervised procedure obtains A by first running RPCA, which is precisely the method that may fail when coherence is high. There is no analysis of how dictionary estimation errors (when RPCA is imperfect) affect U0 ⊂ UA approximately, nor a guarantee for the two-stage procedure under realistic failure modes. As written, the theoretical results do not support the claimed practical gains of Algorithm 1 unless RPCA already succeeds.
- Limited experimental validation: 
  - Synthetic experiments rely on Bernoulli ±1 corruptions and random supports; missing sensitivity analyses (e.g., varying number of clusters, cluster imbalance, nonuniform sampling on subspaces, different corruption models such as structured outliers or missingness).
  - Real data evaluation is small (11 added sequences), and the “corruption” is artificially injected (±1 replacements). It is unclear whether parameters were tuned fairly across baselines. Only a narrow set of baselines is considered (RPCA and LRRx). Robust subspace recovery methods (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, other RPCA variants, nonconvex factorization methods) are not compared.
  - The downstream evaluation focuses on clustering accuracy; direct recovery metrics (e.g., subspace angle error, rank accuracy, S/S0 support F1) are not reported.
- Theoretical novelty vs. prior LRR work: While the shift to a general low-rank dictionary is interesting, the condition “U0 ⊂ UA and A low-rank” is a form of supervision/side information. The manuscript acknowledges this and suggests supervised settings are feasible, but the core interest is the unsupervised case. Without guarantees for learning A from data, the contribution risks being mainly conditional.
- Clarity of proofs and dependence on “full paper”: Several statements defer proofs; it is essential that a NeurIPS submission includes complete proofs in the main/supplement. The paper would benefit from a precise, formal statement of the µ2-phenomenon (not just figures) to underpin the narrative.

Quality
- The theoretical statements appear technically sound under the stated assumptions, but the key assumption (U0 ⊂ UA) is hard to ensure in the unsupervised algorithm, and there is no perturbation analysis (e.g., sin-Theta bounds) quantifying how close UA must be to U0 for near-optimal recovery.
- Empirical evidence is too limited to substantiate strong claims of practical superiority.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with helpful notation. However, some arguments are deferred and several figures/tables are crucial. Please include complete proofs and ensure all figures are legible and self-contained.
- Algorithm 1 is clear, but the rationale for choices (rank threshold 10−3σ1, ε, λ) and sensitivity are not discussed.

Originality
- The link between coherence growth and clustering is known but usefully emphasized. Using a low-rank dictionary in LRR to mitigate coherence is a reasonable idea with connections to matrix factorization and dictionary learning. The precise theoretical bounds under general A add some novelty, though the reliance on side information reduces originality in the unsupervised setting.

Significance
- Potentially useful if a practical and theoretically justified method for learning A is provided. As it stands, impact is limited by the gap between the assumptions in the theorems and the proposed unsupervised procedure, and by modest experimental scope.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a perturbation analysis: replace U0 ⊂ UA with a subspace angle bound and quantify recovery error as a function of that angle and µ1(A).
- Analyze the two-stage algorithm theoretically under imperfect RPCA output; give conditions under which it improves over RPCA.
- Expand experiments: larger and more diverse datasets; different corruption processes (missing at random, block occlusions, real noise); more baselines (Outlier Pursuit, nonconvex RPCA/factorization, robust subspace recovery).
- Ablations: effect of dictionary rank; alternative dictionary learners; sensitivity to λ, ε, and rank thresholding.
- Fair parameter tuning across methods; report recovery metrics in addition to downstream clustering.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important failure mode of RPCA with a clear idea and some theory.
- Simple method that can be adopted by practitioners of LRR.

Cons for acceptance
- Key assumption (U0 ⊂ UA) is unrealistic in unsupervised settings; no guarantees for the proposed learning procedure.
- Experimental validation is limited and lacks strong baselines and sensitivity analyses.
- Theoretical novelty is conditional and does not yet translate into a robust, validated practical algorithm.

Recommendation: Weak reject

Confidence: Moderate
