

# Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper addresses an important limitation in robust PCA methods when dealing with coherent data (data with clustering structures), which degrades the performance of RPCA. The authors identify that when data exhibits clustering, the second coherence parameter µ2 increases with the number of clusters, causing RPCA to fail even when the low-rank component is perfectly recoverable. They introduce a novel approach using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a carefully constructed low-rank dictionary matrix A that avoids the coherence parameters, providing theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1 and 2) and a practical algorithm (Algorithm 1) that outperforms RPCA in experiments.

The key novelty lies in recognizing that coherence parameters are not just mathematical artifacts but are fundamentally related to intrinsic data structures (like clustering). This insight transforms our understanding of coherence from a "problem to be assumed" to a "property to be explained," which is a significant conceptual contribution. The authors' main theoretical contribution is showing that when the dictionary A is low-rank and satisfies U0 ⊂ UA, LRR can avoid the coherence parameters that plague RPCA, with the recovery bound being looser than RPCA's (O(n2/log n1) versus O(n2/(log n1)2)).

The paper's strength lies in its clear identification of a real limitation in a widely used method (RPCA) that has been overlooked in the literature. The "µ2-phenomenon" they describe is compelling and well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experiments. The proposed algorithm is simple yet effective, using RPCA's output to construct the dictionary matrix A, with computational complexity only 1.2x that of RPCA. The experimental results on synthetic data (showing 47% wider success area) and motion sequences (reducing clustering error rates by 40% relative improvement) convincingly demonstrate the practical value of the approach.

The paper makes an important contribution to our understanding of low-rank recovery methods by revealing the physical meaning of coherence parameters. This is a significant theoretical insight that goes beyond the typical incremental improvements seen in many papers on matrix recovery. The connection between coherence and clustering structures provides a new lens through which to view data, which is valuable for both theoretical understanding and practical applications.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical notation and a logical flow from problem identification to theoretical analysis to practical implementation. The authors appropriately reference related work, including the seminal RPCA paper by Candès et al. and the LRR framework by Liu et al.

The main weaknesses are:
1) The paper doesn't fully address how to choose the dictionary size d (mentioned as "not crucial" but not explained).
2) The experiments are limited to synthetic data and motion sequences; additional applications would strengthen the case.
3) The paper doesn't compare with other methods that might handle coherent data (e.g., other RPCA variants or clustering-aware approaches).
4) The theoretical guarantees assume the dictionary A satisfies U0 ⊂ UA, which requires some knowledge of the underlying subspace structure.

Despite these limitations, the paper makes a significant contribution by identifying a fundamental limitation in RPCA and providing a theoretically grounded, practical solution. The insight that coherence parameters are related to data structures (not just mathematical assumptions) is particularly valuable and novel. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach.

In terms of novelty, this paper stands out in the crowded field of low-rank recovery methods. It doesn't just propose another variant of RPCA or LRR but fundamentally rethinks the role of coherence parameters in the recovery process. The paper's main contribution is conceptual rather than purely algorithmic, which is exactly the kind of bold, novel contribution NeurIPS values.

The significance of this work is substantial. It addresses a real limitation in a widely used method, provides theoretical insights that change our understanding of coherence parameters, and offers a practical solution that improves performance in real applications. The paper advances the state of the art by bridging theoretical understanding with practical implementation.

I believe this paper makes a strong contribution to the field. The novelty of the conceptual insight about coherence parameters being related to data structures is particularly compelling, and the practical algorithm that leverages this insight works well in experiments. The theoretical guarantees provide a solid foundation for the approach.

While the paper has some minor weaknesses in experimental scope and theoretical completeness, these do not outweigh the significant novelty and practical impact of the work. The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem with a novel perspective.

I recommend acceptance for NeurIPS. This paper represents a meaningful advancement in understanding low-rank recovery methods and offers a practical solution to a real limitation in current approaches. The conceptual insight about coherence parameters is particularly valuable and likely to inspire future work in the field.
