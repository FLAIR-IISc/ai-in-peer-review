**Summary**

This paper investigates a limitation of the popular Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) method for decomposing a data matrix `X` into a low-rank component `L0` and a sparse component `S0`. The authors argue that RPCA's performance degrades when `L0` is "coherent," a situation that arises naturally when the data columns exhibit clustering structures. This coherence heightens specific parameters (`µ2`, `µ3`) that appear in RPCA's performance guarantees. To address this, the authors propose using the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) model, where `L` is represented as `AZ` for a pre-defined dictionary `A`. They prove that if `A` is itself low-rank and its column space contains that of `L0`, the recovery guarantees for LRR are independent of the problematic coherence parameters. For the practical unsupervised setting, they propose a two-stage algorithm: first, run RPCA to get an initial estimate of `L0`; second, use this estimate to construct a dictionary `A` and then run LRR for a refined recovery. Experiments on synthetic and motion-capture data show that this approach improves upon standard RPCA.

**Strengths**

1.  **Important Problem:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and practical limitation of RPCA. The assumption of incoherence is a known requirement for RPCA's success, and investigating scenarios where it is violated, such as in clustered data, is a valuable contribution.

2.  **Novel Conceptual Link:** The connection drawn between the abstract concept of coherence (specifically, the `µ2` parameter) and the concrete data structure of clustering (the "µ2-phenomenon") is insightful. It provides a clear physical intuition for why and when RPCA might struggle.

3.  **Simple and Effective Algorithm:** The proposed two-stage algorithm is intuitive and easy to implement. It leverages RPCA as a starting point and uses LRR as a refinement step, which is a sensible heuristic. The empirical results, particularly the 40% relative improvement in clustering error on the motion data, suggest the method is effective in practice.

4.  **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) that formally support the core idea—that LRR with a suitable dictionary can circumvent the dependence on `µ2(L0)`.

**Weaknesses**

The paper, while containing promising ideas, suffers from a substantial number of issues related to clarity, technical precision, and methodological detail.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript is marred by numerous notational and formatting problems that undermine its professionalism and clarity.
    *   **Notation:** The notation is frequently clumsy and inconsistent. For example, `‖UT0 ei‖22` should be `‖U_0^T e_i‖_2^2`. The SVD is written as `UMΣMV T M`, which is non-standard and difficult to read. The symbol `n1` is defined but then a typo appears in theorems as `n−101` which is likely meant to be `n_1^{-10}` or similar. Equation (3.7) has `crn2` instead of `c_r n_2`.
    *   **Technical Inaccuracy:** There is a significant error in the notation section. The Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse for a matrix `M` with SVD `UΣV^T` is `VΣ^+U^T`. The paper defines it as `VMΣ −1 M U T M` which appears to be a typo for `V_M Σ_M^{-1} U_M^T`, but even so, the use of `U^T_M` at the end is incorrect. This is a fundamental definition, and its incorrect statement is concerning.
    *   **Formatting:** The reference list is poorly formatted. Many entries are incomplete (e.g., missing conference years, as in [14]) or inconsistently styled (e.g., [1] has "Arxiv," with trailing commas). This suggests a lack of careful preparation.
    *   **Phrasing:** The language is sometimes awkward, for example, "the coherence of L0 keeps heightening" or "defend the possible infections raised by coherent data."

2.  **Methodological Gaps:** Several key details are either glossed over or non-standard.
    *   **Experimental Model:** The synthetic data is generated as `X = PΩ⊥(L0) + PΩ(S0)`. This is not the standard additive model `X = L0 + S0`. This formulation implies that the entries of `L0` at the locations of the sparse errors are zeroed out. This needs to be justified, as it might create an easier problem than the one RPCA is typically designed for.
    *   **Algorithm Details:** Algorithm 1 has potential failure points. Step 2 uses a heuristic threshold (`10^{-3}σ_1`) to estimate rank, but the theory relies on this rank. The gap between the heuristic and the theory is not discussed. Step 4, column normalization, does not specify how to handle columns of `L̃0` that might be zero.
    *   **Dictionary Condition:** The core theoretical requirement is `PUA(U0) = U0`, meaning the column space of the true `L0` must be a subspace of the dictionary's column space. The algorithm approximates this by building `A` from an estimate `L̃0`. There is no analysis of how sensitive the LRR step is to errors in this approximation, which is crucial since `L̃0` will be imperfect precisely when RPCA is struggling.

3.  **Originality and Significance:** While the core idea is interesting, the claimed contributions may be slightly overstated.
    *   The improvement of the recovery bound from `O(n_2/(\log n_1)^2)` to `O(n_2/\log n_1)` is a minor theoretical improvement (a single log factor).
    *   The idea of using a dictionary or factorization `L=AZ` to incorporate prior knowledge is not new and is related to a large body of work on matrix factorization and dictionary learning. The paper acknowledges this but could position its contribution more precisely within that context.

**Recommendation**

While the paper addresses an interesting problem with a sensible approach and promising results, the significant number of minor flaws—ranging from typos and notational inconsistencies to a fundamental error in the definition of the pseudoinverse and unclear experimental methodology—collectively undermine the work's credibility. A paper submitted to a top-tier venue like NIPS should be polished and technically precise. The current manuscript does not meet this standard.

I recommend **rejection**. The core ideas are valuable, but the paper requires a thorough revision to correct the numerous errors, clarify the methodology, and strengthen the connection between the theory and the practical algorithm before it can be considered for publication.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a clear and important limitation of a widely used method (RPCA).
*   The proposed algorithm is simple, intuitive, and demonstrates strong empirical performance.
*   The "µ2-phenomenon" provides a useful conceptual link between theory and data structures.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   A high density of notational errors, typos, and awkward phrasing detracts from readability and professionalism.
*   A serious technical error in a basic definition (Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse).
*   Unclear and potentially non-standard experimental setup for synthetic data.
*   The gap between the strong assumptions of the theory (e.g., `PUA(U0) = U0`) and the heuristics in the algorithm is not adequately addressed.
*   Sloppy and incomplete reference list.
