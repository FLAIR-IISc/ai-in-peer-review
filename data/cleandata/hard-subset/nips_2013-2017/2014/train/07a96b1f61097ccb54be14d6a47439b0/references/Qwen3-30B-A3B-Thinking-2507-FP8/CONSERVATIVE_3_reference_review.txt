

# Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper proposes an extension to Robust PCA (RPCA) by introducing a low-rank dictionary in the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework to handle coherent data. The authors argue that RPCA performs poorly when data exhibits clustering structures (which increase coherence parameters µ2 and µ3), and propose that using a low-rank dictionary A that satisfies U0 ⊂ UA (where U0 is the column space of the low-rank matrix L0) can circumvent this issue. They present theoretical results for noiseless and noisy cases, along with an unsupervised algorithm to construct such a dictionary.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a clear connection between clustering structures and increased coherence parameters, which is a valid observation. The theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) provide formal guarantees under specific conditions, and the algorithmic proposal (Algorithm 1) is straightforward. The experimental results on synthetic data and motion sequences show modest improvements over RPCA, particularly in the context of clustering structures.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core idea of using dictionaries in LRR was introduced in [13] (Liu et al., 2013), and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contribution from this prior work. The authors acknowledge that "LRR falls back to RPCA when A = I" and "the LRR model (1.3) with general dictionaries is not fully understood yet," yet they present their work as a significant advancement without clearly establishing what is truly novel.

2. **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims RPCA "cannot be an ultimate solution" due to coherence issues, but this is already well-known in the literature (e.g., [4, 25]). The coherence parameters are not "assumptions" but rather characteristics of the data, as the authors themselves acknowledge. The paper doesn't address the fundamental limitation of RPCA (requiring incoherence) but instead proposes a method that requires the very condition (U0 ⊂ UA) that RPCA lacks.

3. **Theoretical Assumptions:** The theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1 and 2) require U0 ⊂ UA, which is essentially a supervision condition. The paper acknowledges this as "supervision information" but then claims their unsupervised algorithm works well. However, Algorithm 1 constructs A from RPCA results, which may not satisfy U0 ⊂ UA, making the theoretical guarantees inapplicable to their proposed method. The experiments don't verify whether the constructed dictionary actually satisfies the required condition.

4. **Experimental Limitations:** The experiments are limited to synthetic data and a single motion database (Hopkins155). The comparison is primarily against RPCA, but the paper fails to compare against other established methods that handle coherent data, such as the "outlier pursuit" approach [25] or the robust PCA variant that uses non-convex penalties [24]. The improvement over RPCA (40% relative reduction in error rates on Hopkins155) is modest and not convincingly demonstrated to be statistically significant.

5. **Practical Relevance:** The paper claims their method "outperforms RPCA in our experimental study," but the computational cost (1.2x that of RPCA) is not justified by the marginal gains. In practice, RPCA is already highly efficient for many applications, and the added complexity of dictionary learning may not be worth the modest improvement.

**Critical Assessment:**

The paper's central claim—that LRR with a low-rank dictionary can avoid coherence parameters—is theoretically interesting but lacks practical significance. The authors fail to demonstrate that their dictionary learning approach reliably satisfies the critical condition U0 ⊂ UA in unsupervised settings, which is essential for their theoretical results. Without this, the proposed method is essentially RPCA with an extra step that doesn't address the fundamental issue.

The paper makes a good case for the connection between clustering structures and coherence, but the proposed solution does not adequately resolve the problem it identifies. The coherence issue is not a limitation of RPCA per se, but a reflection of data characteristics that RPCA was never designed to handle. The authors' approach requires additional knowledge (the column space of L0) that is not available in unsupervised settings.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a theoretically interesting but practically limited extension of existing methods. The novelty is overstated, the theoretical guarantees have strong assumptions that are not verified in practice, and the experimental validation is insufficient. The marginal improvements over RPCA do not justify the added complexity for most real-world applications. The paper would benefit from more rigorous comparison against established methods that handle coherent data, a clearer demonstration of the dictionary construction satisfying the theoretical conditions, and a more thorough discussion of the practical limitations.

Given these concerns and the fact that the paper does not present a substantial advancement over existing RPCA/LRR frameworks, I recommend rejection. The contributions are incremental and do not meet the high standards expected for NIPS, particularly regarding methodological soundness and practical significance.
