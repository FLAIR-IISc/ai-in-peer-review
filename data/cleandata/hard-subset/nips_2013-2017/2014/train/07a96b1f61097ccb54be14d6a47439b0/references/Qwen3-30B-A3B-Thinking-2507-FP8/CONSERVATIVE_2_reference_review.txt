

# Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

This paper addresses a limitation of Robust PCA (RPCA) when dealing with coherent data, specifically data exhibiting clustering structures that increase coherence parameters (µ2, µ3), degrading RPCA's performance. The authors propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary to avoid these coherence parameters, providing theoretical guarantees and an unsupervised algorithm that outperforms RPCA in their experiments.

The paper correctly identifies a valid limitation of RPCA, as noted in earlier works (e.g., Candès et al. [4] and Xu et al. [24] discuss coherence as a factor in recovery conditions). The authors build upon established LRR [13], extending it to handle coherent data through a low-rank dictionary constraint. The paper demonstrates that when the dictionary A is low-rank and contains the column space of L0, LRR can avoid the coherence parameters that degrade RPCA.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a clear connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters, providing valuable insights into the physical meaning of coherence parameters (µ2-phenomenon).
2. The theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) are sound and extend the RPCA theory to the LRR framework, showing that the rank condition is looser than RPCA's (O(n2/log n1) vs. O(n2/(log n1)2)).
3. The experimental results on synthetic data and motion sequences are promising, showing consistent improvement over RPCA (47% wider success area on synthetic data and 40% relative improvement in clustering error rates on motion sequences).
4. The proposed Algorithm 1 is simple, practical, and computationally efficient (only ~1.2x the time of RPCA), making it feasible for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper's main claim—that coherence is a significant problem for RPCA in real-world applications—lacks sufficient empirical justification. The authors demonstrate the coherence issue theoretically and on synthetic data but fail to provide compelling evidence that real-world datasets frequently exhibit high coherence due to clustering structures. Without this, the problem's significance is questionable.
2. The proposed solution (LRR with low-rank dictionary) is not sufficiently novel. LRR with general dictionaries has been studied in [13], and the specific approach of using RPCA to construct the dictionary is a heuristic without strong theoretical grounding. The authors cite LRR with A=X extensively but don't clearly differentiate their approach from existing LRR variants.
3. The experimental evaluation is limited. The synthetic data experiments use a narrow range of parameters (rank from 5-100, sparsity from 2.5%-50%), and the motion sequence experiment uses only one dataset (Hopkins155). A more comprehensive comparison against state-of-the-art robust PCA methods (e.g., [24, 25]) would strengthen the claims.
4. The theoretical conditions (e.g., rank(L0) ≤ O(n2/log n1)) are overly optimistic for practical applications. In real-world scenarios, the rank of L0 is often large relative to n2, making the theoretical guarantees less applicable.
5. The paper lacks comparison with other methods designed to handle coherent data, such as those based on structured sparsity or subspace clustering. The connection to GPCA [22] is mentioned but not explored in depth.

**Critical Assessment:**
While the paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to understanding coherence in low-rank recovery, the practical significance is questionable without stronger evidence that coherent data is a common problem in real applications. The proposed solution is a minor variation of existing LRR approaches, not a fundamentally new method. The experimental validation, while promising, is insufficient to demonstrate that the method is generally superior to established baselines. The authors claim their algorithm "outperforms RPCA in our extensive experiments," but the experiments are limited to two datasets, and the improvement (47% wider success area) is not compared against other robust PCA methods.

The paper's main contribution is insightful but incremental. It provides a useful perspective on coherence parameters and a practical heuristic for dictionary construction, but it doesn't present a novel method that fundamentally advances the state of the art. The theoretical results are valuable but don't address the core question: How often do real-world datasets actually exhibit the coherence parameters that degrade RPCA?

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper is technically sound and makes a valid observation about coherence parameters, the problem addressed is not sufficiently significant for NIPS, and the proposed solution lacks sufficient novelty and validation. The authors would need to provide more compelling evidence of the prevalence of coherent data in real-world applications and demonstrate clear superiority over multiple established baselines in diverse settings before this work could be considered for publication at NIPS. The paper's contribution is interesting but incremental, not transformative, and the experimental validation is insufficient for the claims made.
