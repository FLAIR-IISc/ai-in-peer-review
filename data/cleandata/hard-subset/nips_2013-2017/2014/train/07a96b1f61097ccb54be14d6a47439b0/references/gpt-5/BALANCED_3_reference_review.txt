Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles robust recovery of low-rank matrices in the presence of gross, sparse corruptions, focusing on cases where the low-rank part is coherent (e.g., due to clustering/union-of-subspaces structure). It argues that RPCA/PCP’s guarantees degrade with increasing coherence via the “µ2-phenomenon” (second coherence parameter grows with number of clusters), a behavior illustrated empirically. To address this, the authors advocate Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary A and prove recovery guarantees that avoid dependence on µ2(L0), depending instead on µ1(A) and the condition U0 ⊂ UA. They present noiseless and noisy theorems, then propose a practical two-stage unsupervised algorithm: run RPCA to estimate L0, form a low-rank approximation, normalize columns to build A, then solve LRR to refine the recovery. Experiments on synthetic union-of-subspaces data and corrupted motion sequences show gains over RPCA and improvements in clustering accuracy (SIM, LRR with A=X, SSC).

This work builds on a well-developed literature on RPCA/PCP (Candès et al., JACM 2011) and LRR (Liu et al., ICML 2010; TPAMI 2013). Prior LRR analyses emphasized A=X or orthonormal dictionaries; here the focus is on general low-rank dictionaries and their role in mitigating coherence. The link between union-of-subspaces and increasing µ2 aligns with intuition in subspace clustering and coherence-based analyses but is formalized/empirically demonstrated here.

Strengths:
- Clear identification of a practical failure mode for RPCA with clustered data and a crisp connection to coherence parameters (“µ2-phenomenon”).
- New recovery guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that avoid µ2(L0) under U0 ⊂ UA, both in noiseless and noisy settings, with bounds comparable or slightly looser than PCP but with different dependence.
- Simple, practical unsupervised algorithm that leverages RPCA to construct a viable dictionary; empirical results show consistent gains over RPCA on synthetic data and motion segmentation.
- Insightful perspective on the role of dictionaries/factorization in robust recovery and on the meaning of coherence beyond being a technical assumption.

Weaknesses:
- The key theoretical assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong and may be unrealistic without supervision; the proposed unsupervised construction depends on RPCA being good enough precisely in regimes where coherence hurts it. The paper acknowledges this but provides no formal analysis of the two-stage procedure (e.g., perturbation bounds when UA only approximately contains U0).
- The dependency in the theorems shifts to µ1(A), but the paper gives limited guidance/analysis on how to ensure small µ1(A) in practice. The “well-conditioned” dictionary post-processing is heuristic; no ablation or theoretical characterization is provided.
- Experimental evaluation, while indicative, is limited. The synthetic setup fixes k=5 clusters and varies rank and sparsity; broader sweeps over cluster number and direct measurement of µ2 vs. performance would strengthen the µ2-phenomenon claims. Comparisons exclude several robust low-rank methods (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, robust matrix factorization variants), and the motion dataset is small; statistical significance and runtime comparisons are not reported.
- Clarity/polish issues: some typos (“PRCA”), footnote/formatting oddities, and incomplete references. Some statements (e.g., “LRR falls back to RPCA when A=I”) deserve a precise discussion of equivalence conditions.

Quality:
- The problem is well-motivated and the core idea is sound. Theorems are plausible and consistent with LRR theory, but the most impactful practical contribution (the two-stage algorithm) lacks theoretical guarantees. Assumptions such as column normalization and the exact inclusion U0 ⊂ UA should be discussed more critically. Parameter choices (λ=1/√n1) are ported from PCP without sensitivity analysis.

Clarity:
- Generally readable with helpful notation and definitions. However, the manuscript has typographical errors, missing or incomplete citations, and occasionally informal phrasing. Some proofs are deferred; more intuition on why µ2 disappears with low-rank A would help.

Originality:
- The link between coherence growth and union-of-subspaces is a useful synthesis, and the nontrivial theoretical guarantee for general low-rank dictionaries distinguishes this from prior LRR work (mostly A=X). The practical two-stage approach is simple but not entirely novel in spirit (using a preliminary denoising to construct a representation), yet its application and empirical validation here are valuable.

Significance:
- Potentially impactful for applications where data are clustered (vision, recommendation, bioinformatics). If the two-stage method can be theoretically underpinned and shown robust across broader regimes, it could influence robust subspace learning practice. As is, the contribution feels promising but somewhat preliminary.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a perturbation analysis quantifying recovery when UA only approximately contains U0, as a function of the RPCA estimation error and rank(A).
- Analyze how µ1(A) depends on the column normalization and the conditioning of L̂0; propose a principled way to regularize/learn A to control µ1(A) and rank(A).
- Expand experiments: vary number of clusters and measure µ2(L0); compare against additional robust methods (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, factorization-based robust PCA); include sensitivity to λ, ε, and to the conditioning threshold in rank estimation; report runtimes.
- Clarify when A=X or A orthonormal recovers RPCA, and the limits of the “dictionary errors tolerance.”
- Fix typos/formatting and complete references.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a real and under-discussed weakness of RPCA with a principled approach.
- Provides new theoretical insights for LRR with low-rank dictionaries avoiding µ2.
- Simple algorithm with empirical gains in both synthetic and real tasks.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Strong assumptions (U0 ⊂ UA) and lack of guarantees for the practical unsupervised pipeline.
- Limited experimental breadth and missing comparisons.
- Some incremental feel relative to prior LRR work and presentation polish issues.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The ideas are interesting and potentially impactful; with stronger theoretical support for the two-stage method, broader experiments, and improved clarity, this could be a solid contribution.
