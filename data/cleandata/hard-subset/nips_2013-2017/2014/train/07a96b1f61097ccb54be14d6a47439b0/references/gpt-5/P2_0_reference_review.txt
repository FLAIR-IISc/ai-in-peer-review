Summary
The paper studies robust recovery of a low-rank matrix L0 corrupted by sparse gross errors S0 when L0 exhibits “coherent” structure, notably clustering of columns across multiple low-dimensional subspaces. The authors argue and empirically illustrate a µ2-phenomenon: the standard RPCA recovery guarantees degrade as the number of clusters grows because the row-space coherence µ2(L0) increases, tightening RPCA’s rank bound. To mitigate this, they analyze Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a general dictionary A, X = AZ + S, and prove that if A is low-rank and its column space contains the true column space (U0 ⊂ UA), then exact (noiseless) or near-exact (noisy) recovery is possible with bounds that do not depend on µ2 (only on µ1(A)). They then propose a practical unsupervised two-stage algorithm: run RPCA to get an initial low-rank estimate, construct a column-normalized low-rank dictionary A from it, and re-solve via LRR. On synthetic clustered data, they report a substantially larger phase of exact recovery than RPCA, and on corrupted motion trajectories from Hopkins155, they demonstrate lower clustering error after recovery, improving over both raw data and RPCA pre-processing.

Quality
- Theory: The main theorems are clearly stated, with assumptions and scaling regimes aligned with the literature. The condition U0 ⊂ UA is necessary for exact recovery and the bounds remove the µ2 dependence, addressing the central failure mode for RPCA with clustered data. The role of column normalization is explained (to use λ = 1/√n1). The claims that LRR reduces to RPCA for orthogonal A are correct by nuclear norm invariance under orthogonal transforms.
- Assumptions: The central assumption—availability or learnability of a low-rank A whose column space contains U0—is strong in an unsupervised setting. The proposed heuristic (RPCA → dictionary → LRR) is plausible, and empirically effective, but lacks theoretical guarantees when RPCA is inaccurate precisely due to high coherence. The paper notes this but could analyze conditions under which RPCA still provides a column-space estimate good enough for UA to contain U0 (or approximate containment).
- Experiments: The synthetic study varies both rank (via number of subspaces) and corruption fraction with multiple trials; the phase diagram shows substantial gains. The motion segmentation application demonstrates downstream utility with credible baselines (SIM, LRRx, SSC) and a meaningful reduction in clustering error. However, real-data experiments are limited in number and do not include ablative analyses (e.g., sensitivity to rank estimation threshold, λ, ε, or dictionary conditioning), nor comparisons to alternate initializations beyond RPCA.
- Reproducibility: The algorithm is described clearly. Details on solvers, stopping criteria, runtime, and code availability would improve reproducibility. Complexity discussion is helpful; empirical timing would strengthen it.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized. The introduction motivates the µ2-phenomenon and situates the work relative to RPCA and LRR. Notation is precise. Some minor issues:
- A few typos/consistency: “PRCA” should be “RPCA”; “column-wisely”→“column-wise”; probability lower bound n^(-101) should be formatted clearly; footnotes are slightly awkward inline.
- The discussion of why µ2 increases with number of clusters is deferred; a brief intuition (nonuniform sampling of the row space concentrates leverage scores) would help.
- Provide more details on rank estimation and normalization choices in Algorithm 1, and clarify solver settings.

Originality
- The µ2-phenomenon for clustered low-rank data is well known qualitatively but is articulated here with targeted experiments and used to motivate new theory.
- LRR was previously introduced; the novelty here is the theoretical analysis for general low-rank dictionaries that avoid µ2, and the simple unsupervised pipeline that leverages RPCA to learn such a dictionary.
- The connection to factorization and orthogonal-dictionary equivalence to RPCA is appropriate. Related work is broadly cited (RPCA, Outlier Pursuit, matrix completion, subspace clustering). It would be useful to discuss other robust subspace recovery methods that address coherence (e.g., leverage-score aware methods) if applicable.

Significance
- The work addresses a real limitation of RPCA in subspace-clustered data, common in vision and other domains. The theoretical insight that a low-rank dictionary with U0 ⊂ UA removes µ2 is valuable and may guide future dictionary learning strategies for robust recovery.
- The proposed two-stage algorithm is simple, practical, and yields tangible gains on both synthetic and real tasks. The impact could extend to robust GPCA and subspace clustering pipelines.

Pros
- Clear motivation and identification of a key failure mode (µ2-phenomenon).
- New recovery guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that avoid µ2.
- Practical, simple algorithm with consistent empirical improvements.
- Good connection to and differentiation from prior LRR/RPCA work.

Cons
- Strong assumption (U0 ⊂ UA) is hard to ensure unsupervised; no guarantees for the proposed heuristic when RPCA underperforms because of coherence.
- Limited ablation and sensitivity analyses; modest real-data scope.
- Some proofs and quantitative details deferred; clarity could be improved in places.

Questions for authors
- Can you quantify conditions under which RPCA’s column-space estimate suffices to ensure UA contains U0 (or an approximation that still yields good recovery)?
- How sensitive is Algorithm 1 to the rank threshold, column normalization, and λ? Could adaptive λ help with nonuniform column norms?
- Have you compared alternative dictionary initializations (e.g., PCA on trimmed columns, leverage-score sampling) or an iterative RPCA↔LRR refinement?
- Can you report solver details and runtime comparisons?

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers solid theoretical advances tailored to a meaningful limitation of RPCA and a practical method that shows clear gains. Strengthening the analysis of the unsupervised dictionary learning step and expanding empirical evaluation would further improve the contribution.
