

This paper addresses a limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) when dealing with coherent data, specifically data exhibiting clustering structures. The authors identify that when data has clustering structures, the second coherence parameter μ2 increases with the number of clusters, which degrades RPCA's performance. They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary that contains the column space of the true low-rank matrix L0. The main theoretical contribution is proving that LRR with such a dictionary can avoid the coherence parameters that affect RPCA's performance. The authors also provide a practical algorithm (Algorithm 1) that uses RPCA to construct the dictionary for LRR, demonstrating improved performance on synthetic data and motion sequences compared to RPCA.

Strengths:
The paper identifies a meaningful limitation of RPCA (the μ2-phenomenon) that has not been thoroughly explored in the literature, providing valuable insights into the relationship between data structures (clustering) and coherence parameters. The theoretical analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) is sound and well-motivated, providing clear conditions for exact and near-exact recovery. The practical algorithm (Algorithm 1) is simple, well-specified, and supported by computational complexity analysis showing it's only slightly more expensive than RPCA. The experimental validation on both synthetic data and real-world motion sequences provides convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness, with a 47% wider success area on synthetic data and a 40% relative improvement in motion segmentation error rates.

Weaknesses:
The connection between clustering structures and coherence parameters could be more thoroughly explained. While the paper shows that μ2 increases with cluster number (Figure 2), the mathematical justification is somewhat brief and would benefit from more detailed analysis. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how to construct the dictionary in a truly unsupervised setting. Algorithm 1 uses RPCA to construct the dictionary, but it's unclear how robust this approach is to errors in the RPCA estimate. The comparison to other methods beyond RPCA is limited, particularly with respect to more recent robust matrix recovery approaches.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- The paper identifies a genuine limitation of a widely used method (RPCA) that impacts its performance in real-world applications with clustered data.
- The theoretical analysis provides clear conditions under which the proposed method outperforms RPCA.
- The practical algorithm is simple, well-motivated, and demonstrates significant improvements in both synthetic and real-world experiments.
- The insights about coherence parameters and their relationship to data structures advance understanding of fundamental limitations in matrix recovery.

Con arguments against acceptance:
- The theoretical contribution might be considered incremental, as it builds directly on existing LRR work (Liu et al., 2013).
- The paper doesn't sufficiently address the challenges of implementing the method in truly unsupervised settings where the column space of L0 is unknown.
- The experimental section would be strengthened by comparing against more recent methods and providing more detailed analysis of failure cases.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and properly contextualized within the existing literature. The authors appropriately cite relevant work on RPCA (Candès et al., 2011) and LRR (Liu et al., 2013), though they could have more clearly differentiated their contribution from previous LRR work with different dictionaries.

The paper makes a significant contribution to understanding the limitations of RPCA and proposing a practical solution for data with clustering structures. It advances the state of the art in matrix recovery for coherent data, which is a significant problem in applications like motion segmentation. The theoretical insights are valuable for understanding the relationship between data structures and coherence parameters, and the empirical results demonstrate clear practical advantages.

The paper is within the scope of NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning (robust matrix recovery) with both theoretical and practical contributions. The quality is high, with sound technical analysis and convincing experimental validation. The novelty lies in connecting clustering structures to coherence parameters and proposing a solution using LRR with a low-rank dictionary, which is not widely explored in previous work. The significance is demonstrated through improved performance on both synthetic and real-world data.

Overall, this paper makes a solid contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery. It provides valuable theoretical insights, a practical algorithm, and convincing empirical results. While the theoretical contribution might be somewhat incremental, the practical insights and improvements for real-world data are significant. I recommend acceptance.
