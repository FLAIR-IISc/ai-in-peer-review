Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper investigates recovery of low-rank matrices from grossly corrupted observations when the low-rank component is coherent (i.e., has large coherence parameters). It first revisits RPCA/PCP, which solves minL,S ||L||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X=L+S, and is known to succeed under incoherence assumptions on L0 (Candes et al., JACM 2011). The authors analyze how intrinsic data structure—specifically, clustering of data points drawn from a union of subspaces—elevates the second coherence parameter µ2 (and µ3), thereby tightening RPCA’s rank bound and degrading performance (“µ2-phenomenon”).

To address this, the paper studies Low-Rank Representation (LRR), minZ,S ||Z||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X=AZ+S, with a general dictionary A (previous LRR analyses largely focused on A=X; see ICML’10 and related PAMI/JMLR works by the same authors). The central theoretical result shows that if A is itself low-rank and its column space contains the true column space (U0 ⊂ UA), then exact (noiseless) or near-exact (noisy) recovery is possible without conditions involving µ2(L0); the bounds depend instead on µ1(A), the sparsity level |Ω|, and rank(A). This provides a way to “avoid” the harmful dependence on the row-space coherence of L0 by choosing an appropriate A.

Building on this, the paper proposes an unsupervised two-stage algorithm: (1) run RPCA to get a preliminary low-rank estimate L̂0; (2) form a rank-truncated, column-normalized dictionary Â from L̂0; (3) run LRR with Â. The idea is that even if RPCA is degraded by coherence, its estimate may suffice to learn a low-rank dictionary whose span contains U0, enabling LRR to improve recovery.

Experiments on synthetic data (union-of-subspaces with varying rank and sparsity) and on corrupted motion segmentation sequences (Hopkins-like) show improvements over RPCA in recovery success regions and in downstream clustering error when coupled with SIM/LRRx/SSC.

Quality:
- Theory: The noiseless and noisy theorems are clearly stated with assumptions and recovery guarantees. The requirement U0 ⊂ UA and low rank(A) is explicit, and dependence on µ2(L0) is indeed removed. The bounds are comparable or slightly looser in rank scaling than RPCA’s (O(n2/log n1) vs O(n2/(log n1)2)), and the sparse corruption fractions are reasonable (≤ 0.5−ɛ or ≤ 0.35−ɛ). Uniqueness and correctness (Z*=A+L0, S*=S0) are stated. Full proofs are deferred; inclusion in the supplement would be important.
- Algorithm: The unsupervised scheme is simple and practical. The rationale for column normalization and rank truncation is provided. However, there is no formal guarantee for the two-stage algorithm; its improvement is argued empirically and heuristically (“success probability ≥ RPCA”) rather than proven.
- Experiments: Synthetic experiments follow a relevant model (union-of-subspaces with entrywise sparse corruptions). Results show a substantially larger success region. Real data uses motion trajectories with injected missing entries; matrix recovery improves downstream clustering, with further gains from the learned dictionary. Runtime claims (≈1.2× RPCA) are plausible given LRR complexity, but detailed timing and solver settings are not reported.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Notation is carefully defined; coherence parameters µ1, µ2, µ3 are reviewed and their roles distinguished. The µ2-phenomenon is explained conceptually and with references to figures. The algorithm is presented step-by-step with hyperparameter defaults. Some statements refer to “see the full paper” for proofs; for NeurIPS, proofs should be in the supplementary material. Minor editorial issues do not impede understanding.

Originality:
- Conceptual: The identification and empirical demonstration of the µ2-phenomenon connecting clustering to increased row-space coherence, and its impact on RPCA, is a useful articulation.
- Theoretical: Guarantees for LRR with general low-rank dictionaries that avoid µ2(L0) under U0 ⊂ UA appear novel relative to prior LRR analyses that focused on A=X. The shift of coherence dependence from L0 to A (µ1(A)) under a side-information-like assumption is a meaningful contribution.
- Methodological: The two-stage RPCA→LRR dictionary learning is simple and pragmatic; while related in spirit to factorization and to prior uses of LRR, this particular pipeline and its analysis are, to the best of my knowledge, not previously formalized.

Significance:
The results provide a principled way to mitigate coherence-induced failures of RPCA, a well-known issue in practice when data cluster on subspaces. The theoretical insight that a properly chosen low-rank dictionary can eliminate µ2(L0) dependence may influence future robust subspace recovery methods. The proposed algorithm is easy to adopt and shows gains on both synthetic and motion data. The dependence on U0 ⊂ UA is strong; the unsupervised approach addresses this empirically, but its guarantees remain an open direction. Overall significance is moderate to high.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Clear identification of a practically important failure mode of RPCA due to row-space coherence induced by clustering.
- New recovery guarantees for LRR with general low-rank dictionaries that avoid µ2(L0), under explicit and interpretable conditions.
- Simple, practically useful algorithm leveraging RPCA to construct a suitable dictionary, with consistent empirical improvements.
- Good alignment with NeurIPS topics in robust learning, matrix methods, and subspace clustering; relates to prior NeurIPS work (e.g., Outlier Pursuit; subspace clustering).

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- The key assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong; exact satisfaction is unrealistic in unsupervised settings. The paper lacks theoretical guarantees for the proposed two-stage method when this assumption holds only approximately.
- Experimental comparisons omit other robust subspace recovery baselines that can handle coherence or structured outliers (e.g., Outlier Pursuit for column-sparse outliers, RANSAC-type multibody factorization, l1-factorization), and do not study sensitivity to degree of clustering or to dictionary rank/mismatch.
- Proof details are deferred; for a conference paper, full proofs (or at least detailed sketches) should be provided in the supplemental material.
- Limited analysis of parameter sensitivity (λ, rank truncation threshold), solver choices, and runtime.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include full proofs (noiseless and noisy theorems) and a robustness analysis for the case when dist(U0, UA) > 0, quantifying recovery degradation versus subspace mismatch and rank(A).
- Add ablations on dictionary rank, rank-truncation threshold, and column normalization; study sensitivity to the amount of clustering (k) to directly connect to the µ2-phenomenon.
- Compare against additional baselines tailored to coherent data or structured corruption (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, l1-factorization, RANSAC-based multibody methods), and report runtimes and solver details.
- Discuss strategies to learn A without relying on RPCA (e.g., spectral initializations, self-expressive models) and potential iterative refinement with convergence evidence.

Overall assessment:
The paper offers a well-motivated theoretical advance and a practical algorithm addressing coherent data in low-rank recovery. With stronger empirical baselines and theoretical robustness to imperfect dictionaries, it would be a strong contribution. As is, it is a solid submission with meaningful insights and promising results.
