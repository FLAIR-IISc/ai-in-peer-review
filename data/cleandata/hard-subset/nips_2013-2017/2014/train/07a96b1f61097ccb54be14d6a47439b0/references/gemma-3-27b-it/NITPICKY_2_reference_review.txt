## Review of “Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit”

This paper addresses the problem of robust low-rank matrix recovery, specifically focusing on scenarios where the underlying low-rank matrix exhibits coherence due to clustering structures. The authors argue that standard RPCA struggles with such coherence and propose leveraging the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework with a carefully chosen dictionary to mitigate this issue. They provide theoretical analysis linking dictionary properties to recovery guarantees and propose an algorithm based on initializing the dictionary with an RPCA solution. 

While the core idea – utilizing a tailored dictionary in LRR to handle coherence – is promising and potentially valuable, the paper suffers from a multitude of shortcomings, ranging from imprecise terminology and incomplete literature review to a lack of rigorous experimental validation and questionable presentation. 

**Strengths:**

* **Identifies a relevant problem:** The issue of coherence impacting RPCA performance is a valid concern, particularly in the presence of data clustering.
* **Connects coherence to data structure:** The paper attempts to link coherence parameters to the underlying structure of the low-rank matrix (specifically, clustering), offering a potentially insightful perspective.
* **Proposes a practical algorithm:** The proposed algorithm, utilizing RPCA to initialize the LRR dictionary, is relatively straightforward and could be readily implemented.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Imprecise Terminology & Notation:** The definition of "coherent data" lacks clarity. The entire paper hinges on this concept, yet it’s never rigorously defined beyond referencing the coherence parameters. The frequent switching between referring to the low-rank matrix as L0 and Z, without sufficient explanation, is confusing. Notation is inconsistent (e.g., using both U and U0 for related but distinct matrices).
* **Incomplete Literature Review:** While the introduction cites a number of relevant papers, it misses crucial work on dictionary learning and its connection to low-rank matrix recovery. The claim of being the “first” to study coherent matrix recovery is demonstrably false – numerous papers explore robust PCA variations specifically designed for clustered data (e.g., work on subspace clustering with outliers). The discussion of RPCA is overly simplistic and doesn't acknowledge more recent advancements in its theory and practice.
* **Theoretical Weaknesses:** Theorem 1 relies on the assumption that the dictionary is column-wise unit-normed, which is an artificial constraint not justified by the theory. The condition PUA(U0) = U0 is overly restrictive and impractical in most real-world scenarios.  The theoretical analysis, while present, feels incomplete.  The connection between the theoretical results and the practical algorithm is not clearly articulated. Theorem 2's discussion of near recovery is vague and lacks quantitative guarantees beyond stating error bounds.
* **Experimental Deficiencies:** The experiments on randomly generated matrices are not convincing. The improvement over RPCA, while reported, is not substantial enough to justify the added complexity of the proposed algorithm.  The experimental setup lacks detail (e.g., specific parameters used for RPCA, details of subspace generation). The results on motion sequences, while promising, are based on a very specific dataset and task. The comparison to baseline methods is limited. There’s no ablation study to assess the contribution of different components of the proposed algorithm. The claim of a "40% relative improvement" needs more precise definition.
* **Presentation Issues:** The paper is poorly written and contains numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing. The figures (particularly Figure 2) are difficult to interpret. The excessive use of symbols without clear explanations makes the paper unnecessarily dense. The "Acknowledgement" section is awkwardly worded and seems out of place.
* **Minor Errors:** Equation (3.4) is missing a squared term in the denominator. The statement about O(n2rA) complexity is imprecise without defining the constants involved.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The problem addressed is relevant to a significant area of research (robust matrix recovery).
* The proposed approach has the potential to improve upon existing methods in specific scenarios.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper suffers from significant technical flaws and a lack of rigor.
* The theoretical contributions are limited and rely on overly restrictive assumptions.
* The experimental validation is insufficient to support the claims made.
* The paper is poorly written and difficult to follow.
* The literature review is incomplete and misrepresents the state-of-the-art.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend **rejection**. While the idea of using a learned dictionary to address coherence in LRR is potentially valuable, this paper falls far short of NIPS standards. The numerous technical flaws, incomplete literature review, weak experimental validation, and poor presentation outweigh the limited contributions of the work. A substantial revision addressing the identified weaknesses would be necessary before reconsideration. The paper, in its current form, does not represent a significant advance in the field. It feels more like a preliminary exploration of an idea than a complete and well-supported scientific contribution.
