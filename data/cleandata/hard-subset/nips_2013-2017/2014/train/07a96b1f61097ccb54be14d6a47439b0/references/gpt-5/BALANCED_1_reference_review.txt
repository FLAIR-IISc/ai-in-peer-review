Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses robust low-rank recovery when the low-rank component L0 is “coherent,” e.g., when data exhibit clustering/union-of-subspaces structure. While RPCA (Candes et al., 2011) exactly recovers L0 + S0 under incoherence (bounded coherence parameters µ1, µ2, µ3) and random sparsity, the authors show that clustering inflates µ2 (row-space coherence), degrading RPCA’s guarantees and empirical performance (“µ2-phenomenon”). To mitigate this, they study Low-Rank Representation (LRR, Liu et al., ICML’10; TPAMI’13) with a general dictionary A: minimize nuclear norm of Z and ℓ1 norm of S subject to X = AZ + S. Their main theoretical result: if A is low-rank and its column space contains that of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), then exact (noiseless) or near-exact (noisy) recovery is possible without any dependence on µ2 (and µ3), with rank and sparsity conditions depending on µ1(A) only. They propose a simple unsupervised algorithm: (1) run RPCA to estimate L0; (2) estimate rank and form a truncated SVD approximation; (3) normalize columns to form a low-rank dictionary A; (4) run LRR with A; (5) output AZ*. Experiments on synthetic data (union-of-subspaces plus sparse corruptions) and corrupted Hopkins155 sequences show improved recovery and downstream clustering compared to RPCA preprocessing.

The work builds directly on RPCA (Candes et al., 2011) and LRR (Liu et al., 2010/2013), and connects to subspace clustering (Elhamifar & Vidal, 2013; SIM), outlier-robust PCA (Xu et al.), and GPCA (Vidal et al.). The contribution here is a new coherence-aware analysis of LRR with general low-rank dictionaries and a practical two-stage implementation.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: union-of-subspaces structure naturally increases µ2, explaining RPCA’s degradation; coherence parameters are interpreted as capturing latent structure rather than mere proof artifacts.
- Theoretical contribution: recovery guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that remove dependence on µ2, addressing exactly the scenario where RPCA struggles. The rank bound is slightly looser than RPCA’s, and the noisy case is also handled.
- Practical algorithm: simple, easy to implement, and computationally modest (≈1.2× RPCA in authors’ simulations). The idea of using an initial RPCA to learn a low-rank dictionary is intuitive and empirically effective.
- Empirical evidence: on synthetic data, a noticeably larger success region than RPCA; on corrupted motion trajectories, substantial improvements in subspace clustering error when using the proposed recovery as preprocessing.

Weaknesses
- Strong assumption–practice gap: The main theorem requires U0 ⊂ UA and column-wise unit norm A. The unsupervised algorithm constructs A from an RPCA estimate of L0. When µ2 is large (the target setting), RPCA can be substantially biased; it is unclear when the estimated UA will contain U0 or be close enough for the guarantees to apply. No theory is provided for approximate containment or for perturbation tolerance (e.g., angles between subspaces), which is crucial to justify the two-stage procedure.
- Limited modeling clarity for S0: The success probability statements (1 − n^−101) imply randomness in the support Ω, but the theorem statements only bound |Ω|. Please make the distributional assumptions explicit (e.g., random support, arbitrary signs/magnitudes) and discuss to what extent structured corruptions violate guarantees.
- Experimental scope: Synthetic comparisons are only to RPCA. Missing are comparisons to other robust low-rank recovery methods known to help with coherence/structure (e.g., Outlier Pursuit/column-sparse variants, reweighted RPCA, ALT-PROJ, GoDec, etc.), or to LRR with alternative dictionaries (A = X, or dictionaries learned by other means). An ablation on the dictionary construction (with/without truncation, rank selection threshold, normalization) and sensitivity to λ would strengthen the empirical case.
- Algorithmic circularity: If RPCA already recovers L0 exactly, the second stage is guaranteed to succeed, but then the practical benefit is marginal. The paper’s value hinges on regimes where RPCA is imperfect yet still good enough to seed A so that LRR improves recovery. This regime is plausible and hinted at empirically, but should be better characterized (e.g., measure principal angle between U0 and UA from RPCA across coherence levels and correlate with LRR gains).
- Clarity and reproducibility: Some implementation details (solvers, stopping criteria, runtime/memory usage as n and rA vary) are missing. The additional corrupted sequences to Hopkins155 are not standard; please indicate whether they and the code will be released.

Quality
The analysis is technically sound as far as presented (full proofs deferred). Removing µ2 from LRR’s success conditions under low-rank dictionaries is a meaningful theoretical advance. However, the gap between assumptions and the unsupervised algorithm is not closed theoretically. Empirical results are promising but could be broader and more diagnostic.

Clarity
Overall well-written, with clear motivation, notation, and setup. The discussion of coherence and the µ2-phenomenon is insightful. Suggestions:
- State the randomness assumptions on Ω explicitly alongside Theorems 1–2.
- Provide intuition sketches for why low-rank A eliminates µ2 dependence.
- Detail the solver settings and rank-thresholding sensitivity (10^−3σ1 is ad hoc).

Originality
Moderately original. LRR is established, but analyzing LRR with general low-rank dictionaries to avoid µ2 in coherent settings, and the simple RPCA→LRR pipeline, are novel and well-motivated. The connection made between coherence and data clustering is a helpful perspective.

Significance
Potentially significant. Many real datasets are coherent due to clustering; providing a simple, theoretically motivated way to mitigate RPCA’s limitations can impact robust subspace learning and associated applications (e.g., motion segmentation). The practical gains reported are meaningful, though broader validation and stronger theory for the unsupervised case would increase impact.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses a practically important failure mode of RPCA with a theoretically principled fix.
- New guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries removing µ2 dependence.
- Simple, effective algorithm; promising improvements on synthetic and motion data.

Cons:
- Theoretical assumptions (U0 ⊂ UA) are not justified for the proposed unsupervised construction; no perturbation analysis.
- Empirical comparisons and ablations are limited; lack of baselines beyond RPCA in synthetic tests.
- Some implementation and data release details missing.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add perturbation/approximation theorems quantifying recovery when dist(U0, UA) > 0 (e.g., bounds in terms of principal angles and rank(A)), and when A is only approximately low-rank.
- Expand experiments: compare against Outlier Pursuit/column-sparse variants, reweighted RPCA, ALT-PROJ/GoDec; include sensitivity to cluster number and dictionary rank/threshold; report runtimes.
- Clarify assumptions on S0; test non-random/structured corruptions.
- Release code and the corrupted motion sequences.

Recommendation: Weak accept
Confidence: Medium-high
