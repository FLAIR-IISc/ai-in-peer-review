Summary
The paper studies recovery of low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse gross errors when the low-rank component is coherent (e.g., due to clustering). The authors argue RPCA’s guarantees degrade with increasing coherence (µ2-phenomenon). They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary A and prove that, under conditions including U0 ⊂ UA (the true column space contained in the dictionary’s column space) and bounded µ1(A), LRR can exactly (noiseless) or approximately (noisy) recover the components without dependence on µ2(L0). Practically, they construct A by first running RPCA on X, truncating to an estimated rank, column-normalizing, and then solving LRR. On synthetic clustered data and a small set of corrupted motion sequences (Hopkins-style), the method reportedly improves over RPCA.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on RPCA/PCP (Candès et al.) and LRR (Liu et al.). The novelty claimed is analyzing coherence effects and showing that with a properly chosen low-rank dictionary, LRR can avoid µ2, plus a simple unsupervised procedure to learn A from an RPCA estimate. The general idea of using a learned/structured dictionary in LRR has appeared in the LRR line of work; here the theoretical angle targets µ2 specifically.

Strengths
- Problem motivation is clear: clustered data induce coherence that can hurt RPCA.
- Theoretical results (Theorems 1–2) articulate conditions under which LRR with a low-rank dictionary avoids µ2, providing noiseless and noisy bounds.
- The practical algorithm is simple and aligns with existing toolchains (RPCA then LRR).
- Empirical results show improvements over RPCA on synthetic clustered matrices and some motion sequences, with consistent λ choices.

Weaknesses
- Strong assumptions for theory: Exact recovery requires U0 ⊂ UA and a low-rank A with bounded µ1(A). This is essentially supervision (dictionary must already span the true subspace). The unsupervised algorithm that builds A from RPCA is not analyzed; there is no guarantee that the RPCA-estimated subspace contains U0 when RPCA underperforms due to coherence—the very regime of interest.
- Circularity and lack of robustness analysis: The method depends on RPCA to obtain A. If RPCA fails or is biased, UA may miss parts of U0. There is no perturbation analysis quantifying how subspace error in A affects LRR recovery (e.g., bounds in terms of principal angles or coherence of A with the missing components).
- Empirical evaluation is limited:
  - Synthetic setup lacks detail on success criteria and statistical variability; only one type of corruption (entrywise ±1) is considered.
  - Motion experiments use 11 sequences with ad hoc handling of missing entries (replacing missing with ±1 rather than treating them as missing), which biases the corruption model and does not test methods designed for missing data.
  - Baselines are narrow. Missing are robust PCA variants for coherent data or structured outliers (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, AltProj/nonconvex RPCA, GoDec, RPCA/PCP with missing data), and LRR with other dictionary choices. No ablations (e.g., effect of Step 2–4, dictionary rank/conditioning, sensitivity to λ/ε).
  - No runtime comparisons despite claims; complexity arguments are informal.
- Clarity and rigor:
  - Some references are incomplete; several constants and probability expressions (e.g., 1 − n−101) are opaque.
  - The comparison “looser than RPCA” mixes different coherence dependencies (µ1(A) vs. µ(L0)) without discussing how µ1(A) relates to the data when A is learned from X.
  - Statement that LRR equals RPCA for orthogonal A is asserted without proof or conditions.
- Scope of contribution relative to prior LRR work is not fully disentangled. Much of the insight (benefit of dictionary factoring and subspace-preserving structure) is known; the main novelty is the µ2-centric theory under U0 ⊂ UA, which is a strong precondition.

Quality
Partially sound. The theoretical statements are plausible under their assumptions but do not cover the proposed unsupervised algorithm—the main practical contribution. Experimental evidence is encouraging but insufficiently thorough, with questionable corruption modeling and limited baselines.

Clarity
Generally readable, but with notation overload, missing details/definitions, and incomplete references. The experimental section needs more methodological transparency.

Originality
Moderate. The µ2-focused analysis with low-rank dictionaries adds a perspective, but the algorithmic idea (learn dictionary then LRR) and use of LRR are not fundamentally new.

Significance
Potentially useful if the unsupervised procedure can be backed by theory and validated more extensively. As is, impact is limited by reliance on RPCA to construct A and by a narrow empirical study.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide perturbation guarantees for Algorithm 1: If the RPCA estimate has subspace error θ, give recovery/approximation bounds for LRR in terms of θ, µ1(A), and corruption level.
- Replace the ad hoc ±1 imputation with a principled missing-data model; compare against RPCA/matrix completion variants that handle missing entries and sparse corruptions.
- Add baselines: Outlier Pursuit, AltProj/nonconvex RPCA, GoDec, RPCA with column/row-wise outliers, LRR with A=X and with other dictionary learning schemes.
- Include ablations on dictionary construction (rank threshold, normalization), sensitivity to λ/ε, and runtime.
- Tighten presentation: fully specify constants/probabilities, clarify when LRR equals RPCA, fix references.

Pros
- Clear articulation of coherence (“µ2-phenomenon”) affecting RPCA.
- Theoretical results (under strong assumptions) indicating how a low-rank dictionary can mitigate µ2.
- Simple, practical pipeline; promising improvements over RPCA in reported settings.

Cons
- Key practical algorithm lacks theoretical backing in the regime where RPCA is weak.
- Strong supervision-like assumptions for theory; limited generality.
- Narrow and partly unrealistic experimental evaluation; few baselines; no ablations.
- Presentation and referencing issues.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is interesting and potentially valuable, but the evidence and rigor (especially for the unsupervised setting) are insufficient for NeurIPS standards. Strengthening the theoretical link between the proposed dictionary-learning procedure and the recovery guarantees, along with a broader, fair empirical evaluation, would significantly improve the case.

Confidence: moderate.
