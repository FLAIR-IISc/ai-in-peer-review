**Summary**

This paper addresses a potential limitation of the well-established Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) method for decomposing a matrix `X` into a low-rank component `L0` and a sparse component `S0`. The authors argue that RPCA's performance degrades when the low-rank component `L0` is "coherent," a property they compellingly link to the presence of clustering structures within the data. High coherence, particularly the `µ2` parameter related to the row space, tightens the theoretical recovery guarantees for RPCA. To overcome this, the authors propose a different model based on Low-Rank Representation (LRR), `X = AZ + S`. Their main theoretical contribution is to show that if the dictionary `A` is itself low-rank and its column space contains that of `L0`, the recovery guarantees for LRR do not depend on the problematic `µ2` parameter. Based on this insight, they propose a practical, unsupervised two-stage algorithm: first, run standard RPCA to get an initial estimate of `L0`, and second, use this estimate to construct a dictionary for a subsequent LRR step to refine the recovery. Experiments on synthetic and motion data show that this proposed method expands the regime of successful recovery compared to standard RPCA.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Identification:** The paper provides a valuable contribution by identifying and characterizing a specific failure mode for RPCA. The connection drawn between the abstract concept of coherence (`µ2`) and the concrete data property of clustering (the "µ2-phenomenon") is insightful and helps build intuition about the limitations of existing methods.

2.  **Sound Theoretical Result:** Theorem 1 provides a clean theoretical justification for the LRR approach. It clearly states the conditions on the dictionary `A` (low-rank, and `U0` is a subspace of `UA`) under which the recovery guarantee is independent of `µ2(L0)`. This is a solid and well-defined theoretical finding.

3.  **Promising Empirical Performance:** The experiments, particularly the phase transition plot in Figure 4, demonstrate a clear and significant improvement over the standard RPCA baseline. The 47% increase in the success region is substantial, and the improved performance on the motion segmentation task provides evidence of the method's practical utility.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection)**

1.  **Significant Methodological Gap between Theory and Practice:** The primary weakness of this work lies in the disconnect between the theoretical results and the proposed algorithm. The theory (Theorem 1) assumes access to a "good" dictionary `A` that satisfies `PUA(U0) = U0`. However, the proposed algorithm constructs a dictionary `Â` from the output of RPCA (`L̂0`), which is the very method the paper argues is unreliable for coherent data. There is no analysis or guarantee that if RPCA produces a moderately inaccurate estimate `L̂0`, the resulting dictionary `Â` will be good enough to satisfy the conditions for the LRR stage to succeed. The algorithm is a heuristic, and its success relies on the assumption that RPCA does not fail completely, which seems to contradict the initial motivation. The paper would be much stronger if it included analysis on the quality of the learned dictionary or the robustness of the LRR stage to imperfections in `Â`.

2.  **Insufficient Validation of the Proposed Algorithm:** The algorithm itself contains heuristic steps that are not well-justified. For example, the rank estimation threshold (`10^-3 * σ1`) is arbitrary. The claim that the post-processing in steps 2-4 "encourage[s] well-conditioned dictionary" is not substantiated. More importantly, the experimental comparison is limited. The sole baseline is RPCA with a fixed `λ = 1/√n1`. While this is a standard choice, it is possible that a more carefully tuned RPCA could perform better on this class of problems. The paper does not explore alternative established methods or variations of RPCA that might also be robust to such structures.

3.  **Potential Overstatement of Contribution:** While the problem is well-posed, the paper frames the proposed method as a definitive solution for coherent data. However, the algorithm is fundamentally a refinement or boosting technique for RPCA, not a replacement. It succeeds only when RPCA provides a reasonable starting point. The experiments show an improvement in a specific regime, but RPCA remains effective in many cases, and the proposed method still fails when the problem is sufficiently difficult (high rank or high corruption). The significance of the work should be framed more cautiously as an effective heuristic for improving RPCA in a specific, well-characterized regime.

**Questions and Suggestions for Authors**

*   Could you provide some analysis on the robustness of the LRR recovery (Theorem 1) when the condition `PUA(U0) = U0` is only approximately met? This would help bridge the gap between your theory and the practical algorithm.
*   What happens in the regime where RPCA fails badly? Does Algorithm 1 still provide a meaningful improvement, or does it fail as well? A discussion of the failure modes of the proposed two-step process would be valuable.
*   The rank estimation in Step 2 of Algorithm 1 is a critical heuristic. How sensitive are the final results to the choice of the threshold? Have you considered more principled methods for rank estimation in this context?
*   Have you considered comparing your method against other robust subspace recovery methods that might also be applicable to data with clustering structures? The comparison only to a single configuration of RPCA feels somewhat narrow.

In conclusion, the paper presents an interesting idea and a promising direction for improving upon the foundational RPCA method. The identification of the `µ2-phenomenon` is a nice contribution. However, the work in its current form feels incomplete. The core of the proposed practical algorithm is a heuristic that lacks theoretical support, creating a significant disconnect with the paper's main theoretical results. While the empirical gains are notable, the lack of rigorous justification for the algorithm's central step makes it difficult to endorse as a methodologically sound contribution for a venue like NIPS.
