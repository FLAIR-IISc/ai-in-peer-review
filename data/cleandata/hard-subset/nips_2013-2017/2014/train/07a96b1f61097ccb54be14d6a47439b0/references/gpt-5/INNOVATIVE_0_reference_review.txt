Summary
The paper tackles a real and underappreciated weakness of RPCA/PCP: its reliance on incoherence. The authors articulate and empirically illustrate a µ2-phenomenon: when the low-rank component exhibits clustering (union-of-subspaces) structure, µ2 grows with the number of clusters and RPCA’s guarantees (and performance) degrade. They propose using LRR with a low-rank dictionary A, and prove recovery guarantees that remove dependence on µ2 (and thus the cluster-count-driven degradation), relying instead on µ1(A) and the structural condition U0 ⊂ UA. They provide noiseless and noisy theorems, and an unsupervised two-stage algorithm: run RPCA to estimate L0, construct a low-rank, column-normalized dictionary from it, then solve LRR. On synthetic data, the success region expands substantially vs RPCA; on corrupted motion trajectories, downstream clustering errors drop further compared to RPCA preprocessing.

Relation to prior work
This work builds on RPCA (Candès et al.) and LRR (Liu et al.), but departs in two ways: (i) it explicitly analyzes coherence through the lens of clustering and shows µ2 rises with cluster count; (ii) it shows that using a low-rank dictionary containing the true column space allows LRR to sidestep µ2 in the recovery bounds. Prior LRR analyses focused largely on A = X or on segmentation settings; this paper addresses general dictionaries and their role in handling coherent data. The paper also connects conceptually to matrix factorization and robust subspace segmentation (SSC, SIM), and to Outlier Pursuit/robust PCA variants.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Clear identification of the µ2-phenomenon and its connection to clustering; reframing coherence as a manifestation of extra structure beyond low-rankness.
- Theoretical contribution: Noiseless and noisy guarantees for LRR with a low-rank dictionary that avoid µ2, with explicit conditions (column-normalized A, U0 ⊂ UA, rank bounds). The bounds are at least as permissive as RPCA and in some regimes looser.
- Practical algorithm: A simple, unsupervised, two-stage method that learns a suitable dictionary from RPCA’s output; low overhead and an intuitive pipeline.
- Empirical indications: Substantial improvement on synthetic data (larger success region) and meaningful gains on Hopkins-based motion segmentation when used as preprocessing for SIM/LRRx/SSC.
- Clarity of the new direction: The paper provides an actionable prescription for designing A (low-rank, “covering” the column space), with tolerance to dictionary imperfections.

Weaknesses
- Strength of assumption: Exact recovery theorems require U0 ⊂ UA. In unsupervised settings, this hinges on RPCA’s estimate being sufficiently accurate; if RPCA fails substantially due to high µ2, Algorithm 1 may not meet the required condition. The paper asserts success probability ≥ RPCA but does not provide a formal analysis of when the two-stage scheme strictly improves over RPCA.
- Limited experimental breadth: Synthetic setup uses a single family (5 subspaces; varying dimension and sparsity). No sensitivity analysis to cluster number, dictionary rank, RPCA failure regimes, or the threshold used to estimate rank. No ablation isolating the contribution of column normalization or rank truncation.
- Baselines and alternatives: Missing comparisons to other robustness-enhancing approaches (e.g., weighted PCP, reweighted nuclear norm/ℓ1, structured PCP variants), or to dictionary/factorization-based robust methods that could also mitigate coherence.
- Clarity/details: Several figures/tables are referenced but not included; some probabilistic assumptions (random support/signs for S0) are implicit but not stated clearly alongside theorems. Constants and parameter choices (ε, λ) could be better motivated, and sensitivity is not explored.
- Notational heaviness and minor typos; the exposition sometimes defers to a “full paper.”

Quality
Technically sound overall, with meaningful theoretical results and reasonable proofs (as described). The unsupervised algorithm is heuristic but well motivated. Empirical evidence is promising but not exhaustive.

Clarity
Generally clear, though the presentation would benefit from tighter statements of assumptions in the theorems, explicit inclusion of referenced figures/tables, and a brief proof sketch highlighting how µ2 is eliminated.

Originality
High on the conceptual front: isolating µ2 as the culprit for clustered data and showing how a low-rank dictionary removes it in LRR is a fresh angle. The two-stage RPCA→LRR pipeline is simple but, to my knowledge, not previously formalized in this way with guarantees tied to dictionary design.

Significance
Moderate to high. The idea that suitable dictionaries can immunize against coherence-driven failures is broadly useful for robust low-rank recovery and subspace learning, and may influence how practitioners construct dictionaries or precondition data.

Questions for the authors
- Can you formalize conditions under which Algorithm 1 strictly improves the success probability over RPCA (beyond the “no worse” claim)?
- What precise randomness assumptions are required for Theorems 1–2 (support distribution, sign/magnitude of S0)? Please state alongside the theorems.
- How sensitive are results to the rank thresholding in Step 2 and to λ and ε? Any guidance for practice?
- What happens when RPCA’s estimate is biased (e.g., misses small clusters)? Can you quantify A’s tolerance to missing subspaces?
- Could alternative dictionary constructions (e.g., learned via column subset selection or spectral methods) further improve performance?

Minor suggestions
- Add ablations varying number of clusters, cluster sizes, and ranks; include failure cases where RPCA under-recovers.
- Report wall-clock times to substantiate the 1.2× claim.
- Clarify whether column normalization is essential in practice or primarily for analysis.

Overall recommendation: Accept
This paper introduces a compelling and novel direction—using low-rank dictionaries in LRR to neutralize coherence effects—supported by nontrivial theory and a practical, low-overhead algorithm. Despite some experimental and presentation gaps, the conceptual contribution is strong and likely to stimulate further work.

Reviewer confidence: Moderate-high.
