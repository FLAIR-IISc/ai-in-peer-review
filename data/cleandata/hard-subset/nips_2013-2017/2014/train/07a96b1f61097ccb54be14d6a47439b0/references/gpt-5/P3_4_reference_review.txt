Summary
The paper studies robust recovery of a low-rank matrix L0 in the presence of gross sparse corruptions S0 (X = L0 + S0), focusing on the case where L0 is coherent due to structure such as clustering. The authors argue and empirically illustrate a “µ2-phenomenon”: when columns are drawn from a union of multiple subspaces inside a fixed r0-dimensional subspace, the second coherence parameter µ2(L0) increases with the number of clusters, degrading RPCA’s guarantees. They then analyze Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a general dictionary A: minZ,S ||Z||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = AZ + S. The main theoretical result shows that if A is low-rank and its column space contains the true column space of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), then exact recovery can be achieved without dependence on µ2 (and with looser rank scaling than RPCA), under standard random support assumptions and with λ = 1/√n1 after column normalization of A. A corresponding noisy theorem provides near-recovery with Frobenius error bounded by O(√mn ε). Based on these insights, the authors propose a practical two-stage unsupervised algorithm: (i) run RPCA to estimate L0, (ii) form a low-rank, column-normalized dictionary A from the estimate, and (iii) run LRR with A. On synthetic data and corrupted motion sequences (Hopkins155 variants), the method improves recovery success region and reduces downstream subspace-clustering errors relative to RPCA and to clustering methods applied directly to corrupted data.

Quality
- Theory: The noiseless and noisy guarantees are technically plausible and valuable. Replacing dependence on µ(L0) by dependence on µ1(A) under U0 ⊂ UA clarifies when coherence on the row space ceases to be a bottleneck. The conditions rank(L0) ≤ rank(A) ≤ ε^2 n2 /(c μ1(A) log n1) and |Ω| ≤ (0.5−ε)mn (noiseless) are interpretable and slightly looser in rank than RPCA’s O(n2/(log n1)^2). The choice λ = 1/√n1 with column-normalized A is reasonable, though it would help to discuss whether a more general scaling by ||A|| is needed and how sensitive the result is to imperfect normalization.
- Assumptions: The key assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong but necessary for exactness (since AZ* = L0 implies that inclusion). This restricts the scope of the formal guarantees. The paper is honest about using RPCA to approximately realize this in practice, though no theory is provided for the two-stage estimator’s error propagation (RPCA → A → LRR), which would be a valuable addition.
- Empirics: The synthetic experiments are clear and show a meaningful enlargement of the success region. The motion experiments show useful downstream impact. However, the empirical section is somewhat limited: it does not include recovery metrics on real data, ablations on dictionary quality, or comparisons to other robust decompositions (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, robust matrix factorization variants). The protocol for dealing with missing entries by replacing with ±1 noise is nonstandard and may bias the corruption model; masked formulations (PΩ constraints) or matrix completion baselines would be more appropriate.

Clarity
The paper is mostly well written, with careful notation and helpful intuition on coherence. The separation of µ1, µ2, µ3 is well motivated. Still, some parts could be improved:
- Provide a quantitative plot or table explicitly showing µ2 vs. number of clusters and relate it to failure rates of RPCA in the same setting (the text mentions Figure 2 but details are limited here).
- Clarify whether the success probability is over random Ω only, and whether any randomness in A (when built from noisy RPCA output) is assumed.
- Discuss how column normalization of A affects UA (it should not change UA), and conditioning implications for A+.
- Complexity claims would benefit from empirical runtimes and discussion of dependence on rank(A).

Originality
- The idea that coherence arises from clustering is known qualitatively; making the µ2-phenomenon explicit and measuring it is useful.
- LRR is established, especially with A = X; this work’s novelty is in proving exact/near recovery with general low-rank dictionaries that contain U0, thereby avoiding µ2. This is a meaningful theoretical refinement of the LRR framework.
- The two-stage RPCA→LRR heuristic is simple but practically motivated. While conceptually related to factorization and self-expressive models, the specific theoretical lens (avoid µ2 via a low-rank dictionary) is original to my knowledge.

Significance
- The contribution advances our understanding of when and why RPCA fails on coherent data, and how LRR with a suitable dictionary can remedy it. This matters for many applications with unions of subspaces (vision, recommendation, bioinformatics).
- The practical algorithm is easy to adopt and shows notable gains in simulated and motion data settings.
- The lack of guarantees for the unsupervised pipeline and limited empirical breadth temper the overall impact, but the theoretical insight is likely to be influential and spur follow-up on dictionary learning for robust recovery.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear theoretical advance: recovery with general A avoiding µ2 under explicit, interpretable conditions.
- Insightful connection between clustering structure and coherence, with practical implications.
- Simple algorithm with promising empirical improvements and minimal overhead.
- Relevance to robust subspace learning and subspace clustering, topics of interest at NeurIPS.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong assumption U0 ⊂ UA in theory; no analysis of approximation effects when the dictionary is learned.
- Empirics are limited: missing baselines (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, robust MF), no masked formulations for missing data, no robustness studies to mis-specified A, and limited runtime evaluation.
- Some aspects of the setup (replacing missing entries by ±1) are atypical and could distort conclusions.

Suggestions and questions
- Provide an ablation varying the quality of A (e.g., add controlled noise to L̃0) to show robustness and quantify how dictionary errors affect recovery.
- Compare against: RPCA variants for missing data (matrix completion), Outlier Pursuit, and LRR with A = X in the recovery task.
- Analyze parameter sensitivity (λ, rank threshold 10−3σ1) and the effect of column normalization on conditioning.
- If possible, give a perturbation-style guarantee for the two-stage algorithm under approximate U0 ⊂ UA.
- Use masking rather than ±1 imputation for missing entries, or justify the chosen corruption model.

Overall evaluation: Weak accept. The theoretical contribution is solid and clarifies an important limitation of RPCA, and the proposed fix via low-rank dictionaries within LRR is both principled and practical. Strengthening the empirical section and adding some analysis of the learned-dictionary regime would make the paper much stronger.
