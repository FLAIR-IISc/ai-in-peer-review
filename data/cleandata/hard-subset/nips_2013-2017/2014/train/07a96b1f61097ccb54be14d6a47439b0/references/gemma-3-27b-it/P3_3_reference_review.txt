## Review of "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper addresses an important and practical problem: the limitations of Robust PCA (RPCA) when dealing with data exhibiting inherent structure, specifically clustering. The authors identify the connection between data coherence and clustering, and propose a modification to the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework to mitigate the issue. While the technical contributions are incremental, the analysis is sound, the presentation is clear, and the experimental results are convincing. I believe this paper represents a worthwhile contribution to the field and recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary of Main Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established framework of RPCA for low-rank matrix recovery in the presence of corruption. It correctly points out that RPCA’s performance degrades when the underlying low-rank matrix (L0) exhibits high coherence, often stemming from inherent data structure like clustering. The core insight is that the coherence parameter isn’t an unavoidable assumption, but a consequence of underlying structure. The authors propose utilizing a learned dictionary (A) within the LRR framework, where the dictionary itself is low-rank, to circumvent the limitations imposed by high coherence. This builds upon prior work in LRR [13] and matrix factorization [20, 23] and connects it to the robustness concerns of RPCA [4]. The paper bridges the gap between theoretical guarantees in RPCA and practical limitations when the underlying data departs from random incoherence.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of coherent data and its impact on RPCA's performance. The connection to clustering is well-explained with a helpful visual example (Figure 1).
* **Sound Theoretical Analysis:**  The authors provide theoretical justification for their approach in Theorem 1, demonstrating that a low-rank dictionary can effectively avoid the coherence issue.  The noisy case analysis in Theorem 2 adds to the robustness of the claims.
* **Practical Algorithm:** Algorithm 1 is straightforward and leverages the strengths of both RPCA and LRR. The authors acknowledge that it's not a radical departure from existing methods but shows a clear improvement.
* **Convincing Experimental Validation:**  The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world motion sequence data are compelling and support the theoretical claims. The comparison to RPCA and other subspace clustering methods is well-structured and demonstrates a consistent advantage for the proposed approach.
* **Well-Written & Organized:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The notation is clearly defined, and the mathematical derivations are presented with sufficient detail.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Contribution:** While the theoretical insights are valuable, the core idea of using a learned dictionary within LRR isn’t entirely novel. The key contribution lies in *connecting* the coherence parameter to inherent data structure and providing theoretical justification for using a low-rank dictionary to address it. This feels like a refinement of existing ideas rather than a revolutionary new approach.
* **Dictionary Learning Heuristic:** The dictionary learning strategy in Algorithm 1 (using the RPCA result as initialization) is a reasonable heuristic, but a more rigorous analysis of its convergence and sensitivity to the initial RPCA solution would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are well-executed, they focus primarily on synthetic data and motion sequences. Exploring other application domains where clustering is prevalent (e.g., image/video processing, document analysis) would strengthen the claims.
* **Theorem 1 Assumptions:** The assumption of a column-wisely unit-normed dictionary in Theorem 1 feels somewhat restrictive. It's unclear how much this impacts practical performance and whether the theoretical results can be extended to more general dictionaries.



**4. Detailed Comments & Suggestions:**

* **Page 3 (µ2-phenomenon):** The explanation of why µ2 increases with cluster number could be more intuitive.  Perhaps a simplified geometric illustration would help.
* **Page 6 (Theorem 1):** Clarify the practical implications of the condition PUA(U0) = U0.  How easy is it to verify this condition in practice?  Are there alternative conditions that could be used?
* **Page 7 (Algorithm 1):**  Discuss the choice of λ = 1/ √n1. Is this a standard practice, or is there a specific justification for this value in the context of LRR?
* **Page 8 (Experiments):**  Provide more details on the parameter tuning for the subspace clustering methods used in the motion sequence experiments.
* **Discussion:** Expand the discussion section to address potential limitations of the proposed approach and suggest directions for future research (e.g., exploring alternative dictionary learning strategies, extending the theory to noisy scenarios with more general dictionaries).

**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important practical issue in robust PCA.
* The theoretical analysis is sound and provides a justification for the proposed approach.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a clear improvement over RPCA.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The contribution is incremental rather than revolutionary.
* The dictionary learning strategy is a heuristic and lacks a rigorous analysis.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.

**6. Final Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting this paper. The identified weaknesses are not critical, and the strengths of the paper outweigh them. Addressing the suggestions for clarification and expanding the discussion section would further strengthen the contribution.
