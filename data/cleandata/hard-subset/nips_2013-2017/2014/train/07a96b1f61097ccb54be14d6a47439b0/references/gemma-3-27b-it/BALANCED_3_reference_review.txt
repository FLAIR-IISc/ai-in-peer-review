This paper addresses the problem of recovering low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse noise, specifically focusing on scenarios where the underlying low-rank matrix exhibits coherence due to clustering structures. The authors argue that standard Robust PCA (RPCA) struggles in such cases due to its sensitivity to coherence, and propose a Low-Rank Representation (LRR) approach with a carefully chosen dictionary to mitigate this issue. They provide theoretical analysis demonstrating the benefits of their approach and propose an algorithm utilizing RPCA to learn the dictionary. Experimental results on both synthetic and real-world motion sequence data demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** The paper tackles a practical limitation of RPCA, namely its sensitivity to coherence arising from common data structures like clustering. This is a valuable contribution, as real-world data often deviates from the idealized assumptions of RPCA.
* **Clear motivation and problem statement:** The authors clearly articulate the issue of coherence and its impact on RPCA performance, grounding the problem in a realistic context. The µ2-phenomenon is well-explained and visually demonstrated.
* **Theoretical analysis:** The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the proposed LRR approach, showing that a low-rank dictionary can alleviate the issues caused by coherence. While the assumptions are somewhat strong, the analysis provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed algorithm is relatively simple and leverages the existing RPCA framework, making it potentially easy to implement and use. The idea of utilizing RPCA's output for dictionary learning is insightful.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach, showing improvements over RPCA.
* **Well-written and organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with clear explanations of the concepts and methods. The use of mathematical notation is appropriate and consistent.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Limited novelty of LRR:** The use of LRR itself is not novel. The core contribution lies in the analysis of how to choose the dictionary *A* to address coherence, rather than the LRR model itself. This distinction could be emphasized more clearly.
* **Strong Assumptions:**  The theoretical results rely on assumptions like a column-wisely unit-normed dictionary and the condition PUA(U0) = U0, which may be difficult to satisfy in practice. The practical implications of these assumptions are not thoroughly discussed.
* **Dictionary Learning Heuristic:** The proposed algorithm for dictionary learning is a heuristic and lacks theoretical guarantees beyond those for LRR with a well-chosen dictionary.  It's unclear how robust the performance is to the quality of the initial RPCA recovery.
* **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are solid, the comparison is primarily against RPCA.  Comparing against other dictionary learning approaches, or more recent robust PCA variants might provide a more comprehensive evaluation. The experimental setup for the motion sequences lacks details (e.g., parameter tuning for competing methods).
* **Discussion of computational complexity:** While the paper mentions the complexity is about 1.2 times that of RPCA, a more rigorous analysis with respect to the dimensions of the matrix and the rank would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a practical limitation of a widely used method (RPCA).
* Provides theoretical justification for the proposed approach.
* Demonstrates empirical improvements over RPCA on both synthetic and real data.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* Incremental contribution, as the LRR model is not new.
* Strong theoretical assumptions that may limit practical applicability.
* The dictionary learning algorithm is a heuristic with limited theoretical support.
* Experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of robust PCA, addressing an important practical issue related to data coherence. While the novelty lies more in the analysis of dictionary selection for LRR than in the LRR model itself, the theoretical insights and empirical results are compelling. The strong theoretical assumptions and the heuristic nature of the dictionary learning algorithm are concerns, but do not necessarily outweigh the positive aspects of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept** with minor revisions. 

**Suggested Revisions:**

* **Clarify the novelty:** Emphasize that the primary contribution is the analysis of dictionary selection for LRR to handle coherence, rather than simply applying LRR.
* **Discuss the limitations of the assumptions:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the strong theoretical assumptions.  Consider exploring variations that relax these assumptions.
* **Expand the experimental evaluation:** Compare against other relevant dictionary learning methods or robust PCA variants.  Provide more details on the experimental setup, including parameter tuning for competing methods.
* **Provide a more detailed complexity analysis:** Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm more rigorously.
* **Explore potential iterative refinement of the dictionary:** While the authors mention the possibility of iteration, a brief exploration of its practical benefits and drawbacks would be valuable.
