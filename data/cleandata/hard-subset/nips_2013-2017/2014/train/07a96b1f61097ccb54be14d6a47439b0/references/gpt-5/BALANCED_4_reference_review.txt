Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies recovery of a low-rank matrix L0 corrupted by sparse gross errors S0 when L0 is coherent, i.e., exhibits large coherence parameters (especially µ2) due to structured, clustered sampling of the underlying subspace(s). While RPCA/PCP recovers L0+S0 under incoherence assumptions (Candès et al., 2011), the authors argue and empirically illustrate a “µ2-phenomenon”: as the number of clusters increases (with fixed overall rank), µ2 grows and RPCA’s guarantees and performance degrade.

To address this, they analyze Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary A: minZ,S ||Z||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = AZ + S. Their main theoretical insight is that if A is itself low-rank and its column space contains that of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), the recovery guarantees can avoid any dependence on µ2 (and µ3). They provide noiseless and noisy theorems that establish exact or near-exact recovery with high probability when rank(A) is bounded by O(n2/(µ1(A) log n1)) and the sparse support is not too dense (up to 50% in the noiseless, ~35% in the noisy case), with λ = 1/√n1 and column-normalized A.

In practice, the authors propose an unsupervised two-stage algorithm: run RPCA to get an estimate of L0, truncate it to estimated rank, normalize columns to form A, and solve LRR. On synthetic data with clustered subspaces, this expands the empirical “success region” over RPCA by ~47%. On corrupted motion sequences (Hopkins155 extension), using the recovered matrices as preprocessing improves downstream subspace clustering error (e.g., SSC error drops from 9.50% with RPCA preprocessing to 5.74% with LRR and learned A). This work builds on and extends RPCA (Candès et al., 2011), LRR (Liu et al., 2010–2013), and related matrix recovery literature by explicitly tying coherence to clustered structures and showing how a low-rank dictionary can immunize LRR to µ2.

Strengths
- Clear identification and articulation of the µ2-phenomenon linking coherence to clustering—a useful perspective that helps explain RPCA’s brittleness in common settings.
- Theoretical results for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that remove dependence on µ2 (and µ3), with explicit conditions and noisy/noiseless guarantees.
- A simple, practical, and computationally modest algorithm to learn a suitable dictionary that empirically improves over RPCA.
- Solid application results in motion segmentation showing that better recovery materially helps downstream tasks.

Weaknesses
- The key assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong and is central to the theory. The proposed unsupervised algorithm relies on RPCA to construct A, but the cases where RPCA performs poorly are precisely those where ensuring U0 ⊂ UA is hardest. The paper states tolerance to “errors” in A but does not provide formal robustness bounds for approximate inclusion.
- Experimental evaluation, while suggestive, is limited in scope: one synthetic setting (fixed m,n, number of clusters) and one application. There is little sensitivity analysis (e.g., varying number of clusters, rank(A), µ1(A), amount of dictionary rank truncation, iterative refinement), ablation (effect of normalization, rank estimation threshold), or runtime comparisons beyond anecdotal statements.
- Comparisons to related methods are somewhat sparse. For fairness, baselines such as LRR with A=X (analyzed) are reported only in clustering, not in recovery success plots. Other robust factorization or nonconvex RPCA variants are not considered. The connection to GPCA is mentioned but not experimentally explored.
- Some assumptions and probabilistic statements are underspecified in the main text (e.g., randomness model for Ω, distributional assumptions on S0 nonzeros), and proofs are deferred.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The stated theorems appear plausible and consistent with prior analyses of RPCA/LRR, and the proposed conditions are interpretable. However, the central practical gap is the lack of theoretical guarantees for the learned A when RPCA is imperfect. Clarifying the precise probabilistic model and offering a stability result for approximate U0 ⊂ UA would strengthen the work.
- Empirical support: Positive but not comprehensive; broader stress tests and baseline comparisons are needed.

Clarity
- Generally clear exposition with helpful discussion of coherence and the µ2-phenomenon. Algorithm 1 is easy to follow. Some notation and assumptions could be tightened (e.g., specify randomness assumptions in the main text; define the probability space for the theorems; report solver details, stopping criteria, and parameter choices beyond λ and ε).

Originality
- Novel framing of coherence as induced by clustering and novel recovery guarantees for LRR with low-rank dictionaries that avoid µ2. The algorithmic idea is simple but useful. Relation to prior LRR work is acknowledged and extended.

Significance
- Addresses a practically important failure mode of RPCA; results are likely to influence how practitioners approach robust recovery in clustered data and how dictionaries are chosen in LRR. Significance is moderate to high, contingent on stronger empirical validation and robustness analysis.

Arguments for acceptance
- New theoretical guarantees that eliminate µ2 dependence under interpretable conditions.
- Clear identification of a common real-world regime where RPCA falters.
- Simple, practical algorithm with demonstrated gains on both synthetic and real data.
- Potential impact on robust subspace learning and downstream tasks.

Arguments against acceptance
- Strong assumption U0 ⊂ UA with no quantitative robustness analysis for approximate inclusion.
- Limited empirical breadth and ablations; missing comparisons to stronger baselines.
- Some clarity issues regarding assumptions and proofs deferred.

Suggestions for improvement
- Prove a stability result: if dist(U0, UA) ≤ δ and A has rank rA, derive bounds on recovery error versus δ and µ1(A); formalize “tolerance to errors in A.”
- Provide sensitivity analyses: vary the number of clusters, rank of A, rank estimation threshold, normalization, and explore iterative refinement beyond a single LRR pass.
- Expand baselines: LRR with A=X in recovery plots; reweighted PCP, robust matrix factorization, and other RPCA variants if feasible; report runtime and memory.
- Clarify randomness assumptions and the probability space in the main text. Detail solver choices and stopping criteria.
- Explore semi-supervised dictionary construction if a small clean subset exists, and discuss how to estimate µ1(A) or monitor dictionary quality.

Recommendation: Weak accept (6). The theoretical insight and practical utility outweigh the current empirical and robustness gaps; with the suggested improvements, this could be a strong NeurIPS contribution.

Reviewer confidence: 3.5/5.
