This paper addresses the problem of robust PCA, specifically tackling the issue of coherence in low-rank data and its impact on the performance of standard RPCA. The authors identify a “µ2-phenomenon” where clustering structures increase coherence, leading to degraded RPCA performance. They propose a low-rank representation (LRR) approach with a learned dictionary to mitigate this, and provide theoretical justification and empirical validation.

The motivation for addressing coherence is valid; RPCA’s sensitivity to coherence is a known limitation. The identification of the µ2-phenomenon, linking clustering to increased coherence, is a potentially useful observation. The LRR approach, leveraging a learned low-rank dictionary, is a reasonable strategy to address this issue. However, the paper’s presentation and justification raise several concerns.

The theoretical contributions feel incremental and rely heavily on existing results. While Theorem 1 provides conditions for avoiding µ2, the requirement that the dictionary A satisfies PUA(U0) = U0 is quite strong and, while discussed, doesn’t seem easily achievable in practice without substantial prior knowledge. The theorem’s noise robustness (Theorem 2) doesn't appear to significantly advance the state of the art in robust matrix completion. The link between the coherence parameters and extra structures, while intuitively plausible, lacks a more rigorous and novel treatment than what is already present in the cited literature (specifically, [4]).

The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive, doesn’t offer overwhelming evidence of superiority. The improvements shown on randomly generated data are modest and the gains on the motion sequence data, while positive, are presented without a clear comparison to other state-of-the-art robust PCA methods besides RPCA. The algorithm presented (Algorithm 1) is essentially a two-step procedure that relies on first running RPCA to *estimate* L0, and then using that estimate to construct the dictionary for LRR. This feels like a somewhat ad-hoc approach. The claim that this doesn’t "double" the computational work is potentially misleading; solving two convex optimization problems will invariably take longer than solving one.

The writing, while generally clear, contains some awkward phrasing and lacks conciseness. The extensive notation section is necessary but adds to the density of the paper. The acknowledgements are unusually detailed and feel somewhat out of place.

**Strengths:**

*   Identifies a potentially important connection between data clustering and coherence in low-rank recovery.
*   Proposes a reasonable approach (LRR with learned dictionary) to address coherence issues.
*   Provides theoretical justification for the proposed method.
*   Demonstrates empirical improvements over RPCA on both synthetic and real datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Theoretical contributions are incremental and heavily rely on existing work.
*   The condition PUA(U0) = U0 is strong and may be difficult to satisfy in practice.
*   Experimental results, while positive, are not overwhelmingly convincing, and comparisons to other SOTA methods are limited.
*   The proposed algorithm is a two-step process relying on an initial RPCA solution.
*   Writing could be more concise and polished.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in robust PCA.
*   The proposed method shows some empirical improvement over RPCA.
*   The theoretical analysis, while incremental, provides some justification for the approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical contributions are not particularly novel.
*   The experimental validation lacks sufficient comparison to other state-of-the-art methods.
*   The proposed algorithm is relatively ad-hoc and relies on an initial RPCA solution.
*   The strong requirement on the dictionary construction may limit practical applicability.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the problem addressed is relevant and the approach is reasonable, the paper falls short of making a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical results are incremental, the experimental validation is limited, and the proposed algorithm feels somewhat ad-hoc. The reliance on RPCA as a first step significantly diminishes the claimed novelty. This work feels like a natural extension of existing RPCA and LRR research rather than a breakthrough innovation. More compelling experimental results, a more thorough comparison to alternative methods, and a more rigorous theoretical justification would be necessary for acceptance. I believe the paper, as it stands, does not meet the NIPS quality bar for originality and significance.
