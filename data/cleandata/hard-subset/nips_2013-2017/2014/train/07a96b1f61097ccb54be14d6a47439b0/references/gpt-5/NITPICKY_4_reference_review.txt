Summary
The paper studies robust recovery of a low-rank matrix L0 from a grossly corrupted observation X = L0 + S0 when L0 is coherent (e.g., due to clustering/union-of-subspaces structure). The authors argue that RPCA’s guarantees degrade with the coherence parameters µ1, µ2, µ3, and empirically highlight a “µ2-phenomenon” where the second coherence parameter grows with the number of clusters. They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned dictionary A:
minZ,S ||Z||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = AZ + S.
They claim that, if A is low-rank and U0 ⊂ UA, LRR can “avoid” dependence on µ2 (and µ3) in the recovery conditions. Theoretical results (noiseless and noisy) are stated showing exact/near recovery with conditions involving rank(A), µ1(A), and the sparsity of S0. Practically, they propose an unsupervised two-stage algorithm: (1) apply RPCA to get L̂0, (2) build a low-rank, column-normalized dictionary Â from a truncated SVD of L̂0, then (3) solve LRR with Â. On synthetic data and corrupted motion sequences, the method appears to outperform RPCA and improves downstream subspace clustering error rates.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on RPCA (Candes et al.), LRR (Liu et al.), and coherence-based analyses used in matrix completion/recovery. The idea of using a learned/side-information dictionary for robust low-rank recovery is closely related to factorization approaches and “inductive” variants of robust PCA (feature-aware/side-information frameworks), although such connections are not explicitly discussed. The empirical setting touches standard datasets (Hopkins motion sequences) and baselines (SIM, SSC, LRRx).

Strengths
- Clear articulation of why clustering yields coherence that hurts RPCA, and an intuitive remedy via a dictionary that captures the subspace.
- Theorems (stated) suggest recovery guarantees for LRR without explicit µ2(L0)/µ3(L0) dependence, under additional structure on A.
- Simple, practical algorithm: use RPCA to initialize a low-rank dictionary and re-solve via LRR.
- Promising empirical improvements over RPCA on synthetic and motion data; downstream subspace clustering error rates substantially reduced.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical assumptions and gaps
- Core guarantees require U0 ⊂ UA and a low-rank A with column normalization. This is a strong supervision/side-information assumption. In the unsupervised setting, Algorithm 1 does not provide a guarantee that Â satisfies U0 ⊂ UÂ, nor that rank(Â) and µ1(Â) meet the stated bounds. The paper claims improved success probability but offers no theorem linking Algorithm 1 to Theorem 1/2 conditions.
- Although µ2(L0) is avoided, the bounds depend on µ1(A), thus shifting coherence assumptions from L0 to A. There is no characterization of µ1(Â) produced by Algorithm 1 or how it scales with clustering.
- Probability statements (e.g., 1 − n^−101) and constants are unusual and unexplained; the randomness model (only Ω randomness? S0 values?) is not clearly stated here. Claims like “O(n2/log n1) is probably the ‘finest’ bound” are speculative without proof/citations.
- No proofs in the submission (referred to “full paper”); for NeurIPS, proofs should be in the main or supplementary material for evaluation.

Methodology and empirical evaluation
- The two-stage algorithm hinges on RPCA already producing a decent subspace estimate; when RPCA fails due to high coherence (the motivation), it is unclear when the initialization is good enough to make Â usable.
- The choice of rank threshold (σi > 10^-3 σ1) is ad hoc; no sensitivity analysis.
- Column normalization of L̃0 may distort subspace geometry and is not theoretically justified vis-à-vis µ1(A) or solver behavior.
- Missing data handling is atypical: replacing missing entries with Bernoulli ±1 injects adversarial-like noise rather than modeling missingness; this confounds the setting (robust recovery vs completion vs corruption). It is unclear whether results depend on this particular imputation.
- Experiments lack ablations: (i) effect of dictionary rank; (ii) effect of closeness of UÂ to U0; (iii) sensitivity to λ and ε; (iv) number of clusters vs performance; (v) comparisons to other dictionary/feature-aware robust PCA methods or subspace clustering methods that explicitly handle unions of subspaces with corruption.
- Runtime/scalability claims (Algorithm 1 only 1.2× RPCA, LRR complexity O(n^2 rA)) are asserted without reporting solver, stopping criteria, wall-clock times, or memory usage. Problem sizes are modest (200×1000).
- The synthetic setup is under-specified: distribution of subspaces, sampling per subspace, S0 model beyond Bernoulli ±1, dependence across trials, and how “success area” is computed (thresholds/metrics).
- On motion sequences, only 11 sequences are used, and the corruption model is artificial. Reporting variance/CI would help.

Clarity and presentation
- Many formatting/typo issues: “column-wisely” (should be column-wise), “heightening”, “looser”, inconsistent notation (n1, n2), dangling footnotes in text (“1”, “2”), and informal claims without references.
- Some definitions and assumptions are repeated or ambiguous (e.g., A orthogonal vs arbitrary, assumptions on S0 support distribution).
- References are incomplete and inconsistently formatted (missing years, venues, pages; some arXiv-only entries without IDs). Key related work (feature-aware/inductive RPCA variants; robust union-of-subspaces with coherence-aware analyses) is not discussed.

Originality
- The observation that coherence harms RPCA is well-known; formalizing µ2 growth with clustering is useful but not fully developed here. Using a learned low-rank dictionary within LRR to mitigate coherence is sensible but close in spirit to existing factorization/feature-aware paradigms. The theoretical angle of removing µ2(L0) under strong assumptions on A is interesting but, without proofs and a bridge to the proposed unsupervised algorithm, the novelty is blunted.

Significance
- If the theory were fully substantiated and connected to a practical, provably-correct dictionary-learning procedure, the contribution would be significant. As it stands, the empirical gains are promising but limited in scope, and the theoretical claims are not verifiable from the submission.

Questions for the authors
- Provide proofs (or supplement) for Theorems 1–2 and precisely state the randomness model.
- Can you quantify conditions under which RPCA initialization ensures U0 ⊂ UÂ (approximately), and bound µ1(Â)?
- Why the particular rank threshold 10^-3 σ1? Sensitivity?
- How do results change with proper missing data handling (masking + completion) instead of ±1 imputation?
- Report solver details, stopping criteria, and runtimes. How does performance scale?

Minor comments and nits
- Fix numerous grammatical issues and dangling footnotes; define all symbols when introduced; ensure consistent use of n1, n2.
- Avoid speculative statements without citations. Justify constants (e.g., 0.5−ε, 0.35−ε).
- Complete and standardize references.

Pros
- Addresses an important practical failure mode of RPCA.
- Conceptually simple method with encouraging empirical improvements.
- Attempts to decouple guarantees from µ2(L0) via a dictionary.

Cons
- Strong, impractical assumptions on A with no guarantee for the proposed unsupervised construction.
- Missing proofs; unclear probabilistic model; theoretical-to-practical gap.
- Limited, under-specified experiments; atypical handling of missing data.
- Presentation and referencing need substantial polishing.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the current submission has substantial theoretical, methodological, empirical, and presentation shortcomings that prevent a confident acceptance. I encourage the authors to include full proofs, establish guarantees for the unsupervised algorithm, expand and clarify experiments (with proper missing data modeling, ablations, and runtime reporting), and significantly improve clarity and references.
