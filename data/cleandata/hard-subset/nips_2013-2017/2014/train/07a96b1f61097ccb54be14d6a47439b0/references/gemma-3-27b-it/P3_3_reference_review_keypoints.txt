- Addresses RPCA’s failure on coherent (clustered) data and proposes a low-rank dictionary within LRR to mitigate it.  
- Core insight: coherence is a consequence of underlying structure (e.g., clustering) and can be alleviated by a learned low-rank dictionary.  
- Builds on RPCA and LRR literature and connects theoretical RPCA guarantees to practical structured-data limitations.  
- Theoretical contributions: Theorem 1 shows a low-rank dictionary can avoid coherence issues; Theorem 2 analyzes the noisy case.  
- Proposes a practical Algorithm 1 that combines RPCA initialization with LRR using a learned dictionary.  
- Experimental validation on synthetic and real motion-sequence data convincingly supports improved performance over RPCA and other methods.  
- Presentation is clear, well-organized, and mathematically detailed with good notation.  
- Strength: clear problem motivation and intuitive link between clustering and increased coherence (Figure 1).  
- Strength: sound theoretical analysis that justifies the proposed approach.  
- Strength: practical, straightforward algorithm showing consistent empirical gains.  
- Strength: experiments are convincing and comparisons are well-structured.  
- Weakness: contribution is incremental—refinement and connection of existing ideas rather than a radically new approach.  
- Weakness: dictionary-learning strategy (using RPCA output) is a heuristic lacking rigorous convergence or sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: experiments limited mainly to synthetic data and motion sequences; broader application domains are not explored.  
- Weakness: Theorem 1 assumes a column-wise unit-norm dictionary, which may be restrictive and its practical impact is unclear.  
- Suggestion: clarify µ2 increase with cluster number using a simpler geometric illustration.  
- Suggestion: explain practical implications and verifiability of the condition PUA(U0)=U0 and possible alternative conditions.  
- Suggestion: justify the λ = 1/√n1 choice for Algorithm 1 and discuss parameter tuning details used in experiments.  
- Suggestion: expand discussion of limitations and future directions, including alternative dictionary-learning strategies and broader noisy-dictionary theory.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important practical RPCA issue, provides sound theory, and shows compelling empirical improvements.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty, heuristic dictionary learning without rigorous analysis, and somewhat limited experimental scope.  
- Final recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, asking for clarifications and expanded discussion as noted.
