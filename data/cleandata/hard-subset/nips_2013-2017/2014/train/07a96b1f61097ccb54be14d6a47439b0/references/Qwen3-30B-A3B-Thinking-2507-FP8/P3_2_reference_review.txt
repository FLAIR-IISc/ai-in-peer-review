

# Peer Review: "Recovery of Coherent Data via Low-Rank Dictionary Pursuit"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses a critical limitation of Robust Principal Component Analysis (RPCA) when dealing with "coherent" data - data that exhibits clustering structures, which commonly occur in real-world applications. The authors identify that when data points cluster within a low-dimensional subspace (as illustrated in Figure 1), the second coherence parameter μ₂(L₀) increases with the number of clusters, degrading RPCA's performance (the "μ₂-phenomenon"). Building on the Low-Rank Representation (LRR) framework, the authors propose that using a low-rank dictionary matrix A that satisfies U₀ ⊂ U_A (where U₀ is the column space of the true low-rank matrix L₀) can avoid the coherence parameters that undermine RPCA. They provide theoretical guarantees for exact and near-exact recovery under these conditions and propose an unsupervised algorithm (Algorithm 1) that constructs the dictionary using RPCA results.

The paper extends the well-established RPCA framework by introducing a new perspective on coherence parameters, revealing they are not merely theoretical assumptions but relate to intrinsic data structures (clustering). This connects to prior work on RPCA [4] and LRR [13], but provides a novel approach to handling coherent data.

## Quality Assessment

The paper presents technically sound theoretical results, with Theorem 1 providing a recovery guarantee for the noiseless case and Theorem 2 extending this to the noisy case. The theoretical conditions (3.8) and (3.10) are reasonable, and the bound on rank(L₀) is looser than RPCA's requirement, which is a positive contribution.

However, I have several concerns about the quality:

1. **Theoretical completeness**: The paper refers to "the full paper" for the explanation of why μ₂ increases with cluster number (as mentioned in Section 3.1.2), which is unacceptable for a conference submission. The key explanation of the "μ₂-phenomenon" should be included in the paper, not relegated to a full version.

2. **Theoretical justification**: The claim that "the variation of μ₃ is mainly due to the variation of the row space, which is characterized by μ₂" lacks mathematical justification. The relationship between μ₂ and μ₃ should be explicitly derived.

3. **Algorithmic details**: The post-processing steps in Algorithm 1 (Steps 2 and 3) are not well-explained. The paper states they "encourage well-conditioned dictionary," but doesn't specify how this is achieved or why it matters for the theoretical guarantees.

4. **Experimental validation**: The experiments on motion sequences (Table 1) are interesting but limited. The paper doesn't compare with other dictionary learning methods for LRR, nor does it investigate how the quality of the dictionary constructed by RPCA affects the final performance. The improvement from 9.50% to 5.74% error rate is significant, but without understanding why this improvement occurs, the result is less informative.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with good mathematical notation. The key concepts (coherence parameters, clustering structures, LRR framework) are well-defined.

However, some areas need improvement:

1. The relationship between clustering structures and coherence parameters could be explained more clearly. A simple example demonstrating how μ₂ increases with cluster number would help readers understand the "μ₂-phenomenon."

2. The paper uses "coherent data" and "less incoherent data" interchangeably, which is confusing. The standard terminology in the field is "incoherent" (low coherence) versus "coherent" (high coherence), so the paper should be consistent with this.

3. The paper claims that "the coherence parameters are not 'assumptions' for a proof, but rather some excellent quantities that relate in nature to the extra structures," but doesn't provide concrete examples showing this relationship beyond the "μ₂-phenomenon."

## Originality Assessment

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of coherence parameters in low-rank matrix recovery. While LRR with general dictionaries has been studied before, the insight that a low-rank dictionary can help avoid coherence issues is novel. The paper's main contribution is showing that coherence parameters are intrinsically related to extra data structures (like clustering), rather than being arbitrary theoretical constraints.

However, I note that:
- The idea of using a dictionary to improve recovery is related to other matrix factorization approaches (e.g., [20, 23] mentioned in the paper).
- The proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) is straightforward and may not be as novel as claimed, as it essentially uses RPCA to initialize LRR.

The paper is sufficiently different from previous NIPS papers on RPCA and LRR, particularly in its focus on the coherence parameters and their relationship to data structures.

## Significance Assessment

The problem addressed is significant because many real-world datasets (e.g., motion sequences, image data) exhibit clustering structures that lead to coherent data, which degrades RPCA's performance. The proposed method has the potential to improve applications like motion segmentation, image processing, and computer vision.

However, the significance is somewhat limited by:
- The paper doesn't quantify how common coherent data is in real-world applications.
- The improvement over RPCA is demonstrated but not thoroughly analyzed (e.g., how much better is the method in different scenarios?).
- The paper doesn't discuss potential limitations or scenarios where the proposed method might not work as well.

## Strengths

1. The paper identifies a genuine limitation of RPCA that has been noted in the literature but not thoroughly addressed: the performance degradation with coherent data.
2. The theoretical results provide a clear condition (low-rank dictionary with U₀ ⊂ U_A) under which coherence parameters can be avoided.
3. The proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) is simple and practical, with only a modest computational overhead (1.2x) compared to RPCA.
4. The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world data support the theoretical claims.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper relies on "the full paper" for key explanations (e.g., the μ₂-phenomenon), which is unacceptable for a conference submission.
2. The relationship between clustering structures and coherence parameters lacks mathematical justification.
3. The post-processing steps in the algorithm are not well-explained.
4. The experimental comparison with other dictionary learning methods for LRR is missing.
5. The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze why the proposed method works better than RPCA, beyond the theoretical guarantees.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The paper addresses a meaningful problem with a novel perspective on coherence parameters, and the theoretical results are sound. However, the paper has several weaknesses that need to be addressed before it can be considered for publication.

The most critical issue is the reliance on "the full paper" for key explanations. The paper should include a self-contained explanation of the μ₂-phenomenon, showing how clustering structures increase μ₂. Without this, the theoretical contribution is incomplete.

The authors should also:
1. Provide a mathematical justification for the relationship between μ₂ and μ₃.
2. Clarify the post-processing steps in Algorithm 1 and explain how they affect the dictionary's quality.
3. Include a comparison with other dictionary learning methods for LRR.
4. Analyze the practical implications of the theoretical guarantees.

With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field and would be suitable for NIPS.

## Final Comments

This paper makes an important contribution to understanding the limitations of RPCA with coherent data and proposes a practical solution using low-rank dictionaries. While the paper has some significant weaknesses that need to be addressed, the core idea is valuable and well-motivated. The authors should focus on making the theoretical explanations self-contained and providing more detailed experimental analysis to support their claims.

I believe this paper has the potential to be a strong NIPS submission if the authors address the concerns raised in this review. The theoretical insight that coherence parameters are related to intrinsic data structures (like clustering) is particularly valuable for the machine learning community.
