This paper addresses the problem of recovering low-rank matrices corrupted by sparse errors, focusing on scenarios where the underlying low-rank structure exhibits coherence – a phenomenon often observed in clustered data that degrades the performance of standard Robust PCA (RPCA). The authors identify that the coherence is linked to the intrinsic structures within the low-rank matrix and propose a Low-Rank Representation (LRR) approach with a carefully chosen dictionary to mitigate this issue. They provide theoretical analysis and empirical validation demonstrating the effectiveness of their method, especially compared to RPCA.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Relevant Problem:** The problem of handling coherent low-rank data is indeed important. RPCA’s sensitivity to coherence limits its applicability in many real-world scenarios. Addressing this limitation is a valuable contribution.
* **Clear Motivation & Insight:** The paper clearly articulates the connection between clustering structures, coherence, and the performance degradation of RPCA. The intuition behind avoiding coherence by leveraging dictionary learning is well explained.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical results, particularly Theorem 1, provide a solid foundation for understanding the conditions under which LRR can avoid the coherence issue. Establishing these conditions and connecting them to the dictionary structure is a key strength.
* **Algorithm & Implementation:** The proposed algorithm, which utilizes RPCA to initialize the dictionary for LRR, is a practical and efficient approach. Its computational complexity analysis is also provided.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both randomly generated matrices and motion sequence data demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to RPCA and other baseline approaches. The results provide convincing evidence of the improvement.
* **Well-written & Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are presented clearly. The inclusion of a detailed introduction and related work section provides context and establishes the paper’s contribution.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Dictionary Learning Heuristic:** While the algorithm works well in practice, the method for constructing the dictionary from the RPCA result feels somewhat heuristic. The paper does not rigorously explore other dictionary learning techniques or provide a strong justification for this specific approach. It would be helpful to analyze the properties of the dictionary learned via this method and how these properties contribute to the improved performance.
* **Limited Scope of Theoretical Results:** The theoretical results (Theorems 1 & 2) are based on strong assumptions, such as knowing the rank of the low-rank matrix and a column-wise unit-normed dictionary. The practical implications of these assumptions and their impact on the method’s robustness in more realistic scenarios could be further discussed. Specifically, how sensitive is the method to errors in rank estimation?
* **Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The comparison with existing methods is limited. While the experiments demonstrate improvement over RPCA, a more comprehensive comparison with recent advances in robust PCA and low-rank matrix completion would strengthen the paper. For example, methods based on reweighted nuclear norm minimization or adaptive regularization could be considered.
* **Discussion of Noise Sensitivity:** While Theorem 2 addresses noisy data, a more in-depth discussion of the method's sensitivity to different types of noise (e.g., Gaussian noise vs. impulsive noise) and varying noise levels would be beneficial.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** The notation can be dense at times. Providing a more concise summary of the key notations in the main body of the paper might improve readability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in robust matrix recovery.
* The proposed method offers a practical and effective solution to mitigate the coherence issue in RPCA.
* The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the underlying mechanisms of the method.
* The empirical results demonstrate the method's effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the problem, proposed solution, and experimental results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The dictionary learning approach relies on a heuristic and lacks a more rigorous justification.
* The theoretical results are based on strong assumptions that may not hold in all practical scenarios.
* The comparison with state-of-the-art methods is limited.
* The discussion of noise sensitivity could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of robust matrix recovery. The authors have identified a significant limitation of RPCA and proposed a practical and effective method to address it. The theoretical analysis and empirical validation are convincing, and the paper is well-written and organized. While there are some weaknesses, they are not critical and can be addressed in future work. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  Provide a more detailed justification for the dictionary learning approach and explore alternative techniques.
2.  Discuss the sensitivity of the method to violations of the theoretical assumptions.
3.  Expand the comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
4.  Provide a more thorough analysis of the method's performance under different noise conditions.
5.  Consider streamlining the notation for improved readability.
