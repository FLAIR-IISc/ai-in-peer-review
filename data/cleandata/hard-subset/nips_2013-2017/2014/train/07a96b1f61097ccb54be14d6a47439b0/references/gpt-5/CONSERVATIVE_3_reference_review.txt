Summary
The paper addresses a well-known weakness of Principal Component Pursuit (RPCA): its reliance on incoherence, which is violated when data lie in unions of subspaces (clustered columns), leading to large µ2 and degraded recovery. The authors argue that coherence, particularly µ2, naturally grows with the number of clusters and is tied to intrinsic structure rather than a mere technical assumption. They propose using Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a low-rank dictionary A to “avoid” the dependence on µ2. Theoretically, they show exact and near-exact recovery guarantees for LRR when A is column-normalized, low-rank, and its column-space contains the true column-space (U0 ⊂ UA), with bounds scaling as rank(A) ≤ O(n2/(µ1(A) log n1)) and sparsity up to a constant fraction. Practically, they propose a two-stage unsupervised algorithm: run RPCA to estimate L0, build a normalized, rank-truncated dictionary from it, then run LRR. Synthetic and motion segmentation experiments indicate gains over RPCA.

Relation to prior work
This work builds on PCP/RPCA (Candès et al.), on LRR and its use in subspace segmentation (Liu et al.), and on the role of coherence in matrix completion and recovery (Candès–Recht, Candès–Plan). The “µ2-phenomenon” is closely related to classical coherence effects in matrix completion and to known failure modes of RPCA for data that are not uniformly spread in a subspace. The use of a low-rank factorization/dictionary connects to matrix factorization and subspace learning traditions. The specific theoretical claim of avoiding µ2 by choosing a low-rank dictionary that contains U0 is a useful perspective on why dictionary-based models can help under clustering.

Strengths
- Clearly identifies and explains an important practical failure mode of RPCA due to clustered data; the empirical “µ2-phenomenon” is plausible and aligns with prior theory.
- Provides recovery guarantees for LRR with general (low-rank) dictionaries that do not invoke µ2, clarifying how dictionary properties (µ1(A), U0 ⊂ UA) control success.
- Simple unsupervised algorithm with small computational overhead (RPCA then LRR) and consistent improvements over RPCA on tested datasets.
- Bridges RPCA/LRR theory with GPCA intuition and factorization viewpoints; the paper is generally well organized.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Core theoretical assumption (U0 ⊂ UA) is very strong in unsupervised settings. The guarantees essentially require the dictionary to already contain the true subspace. The proposed algorithm achieves this by using RPCA’s output, which is precisely the object that fails in the coherent regime the paper targets. There is a circularity: when RPCA is inaccurate due to high µ2, the assumption needed for the subsequent theorem likely fails. The paper does not provide bounds under approximate containment or quantify how much RPCA error is tolerable.
- Practical validation is limited:
  - Synthetic experiments use a single size (200×1000) and do not systematically sweep cluster numbers to directly connect performance to measured µ2. No reporting of reconstruction error, support recovery, or sensitivity to λ and rank thresholding. No ablation to isolate whether improvements come from rank truncation/column normalization alone (i.e., RPCA + low-rank projection) versus the LRR stage.
  - Motion segmentation setup replaces missing entries with ±1, which is unrealistic and conflates corruption types. Evaluation is via downstream clustering error using three methods, which introduces confounding; reconstruction metrics are not reported. The gain over RPCA is promising but modest and not analyzed for statistical significance or robustness to parameter choice.
  - Baselines are thin. There is no comparison against other robust PCA variants designed for structured coherence or relaxed incoherence assumptions (e.g., reweighted PCP, factorization-based robust PCA/GoDec/AltProj), nor to column/row-wise robust methods (Outlier Pursuit) when applicable. LRR with alternative, simpler dictionaries (e.g., PCA basis of X, random low-rank projections) is not tested.
- The claim of “avoiding µ2” is contingent on being able to build a low-µ1 dictionary that contains U0. The paper does not provide a principled dictionary learning method with guarantees in the unsupervised setting; the current heuristic depends on RPCA’s success. The role of µ1(A) is not explored empirically; how to keep µ1(A) small when columns are normalized but clustered is unclear.
- Theoretical presentation relies on “full paper” for proofs; constants and probability statements are opaque. Practical choices (ε setting, 10−3σ1 threshold) are ad hoc and not justified.
- Writing issues: some typos (PRCA vs RPCA), nonstandard terms (“column-wisely”), and several incomplete references.

Quality
Technically, the main theorems under exact subspace containment are plausible and align with LRR analyses, but the gap between the assumptions and the proposed unsupervised use is substantial and not bridged by analysis. Experimental support is not sufficient to validate the method across regimes where RPCA fails strongly.

Clarity
Generally readable, but the empirical section lacks necessary details (data generation, parameter sensitivity, metrics) and the proofs are deferred. References need polishing.

Originality
Moderate. The identification of the µ2 issue in RPCA for clustered data is not new per se, but the formalization within LRR with low-rank dictionaries and the specific “avoid µ2” guarantee are useful. The algorithm is a straightforward combination of known tools (RPCA → dictionary → LRR).

Significance
Potentially useful in practice if the dictionary can be learned reliably, but the dependence on RPCA and limited validation reduce confidence. The theoretical perspective may spur further work on robust dictionary learning for coherent data.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and practical limitation of RPCA.
- Provides clear, interpretable conditions under which LRR can succeed without µ2.
- Simple algorithm with some empirical gains on both synthetic and motion data.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong assumptions for theory; unsupervised algorithm lacks theoretical support.
- Limited, confounded, and somewhat unrealistic experiments; weak baselines and no ablations.
- Claims about avoiding coherence not convincingly demonstrated in the regimes where RPCA truly struggles.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide analysis under approximate subspace containment (e.g., principal angle bounds) induced by imperfect RPCA, and derive stability guarantees depending on that misalignment.
- Expand experiments: sweep number of clusters and measure µ2; add ablations (RPCA + rank truncation only; LRR with random/PCA dictionaries); compare against stronger baselines; report reconstruction/support metrics and parameter sensitivity; include realistic missing/corruption models and larger datasets; measure runtime.
- Explore principled unsupervised dictionary learning strategies that promote low rank and low µ1 without relying on RPCA.
- Include proof sketches and clean up writing and references.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is interesting and the perspective on coherence is valuable, but the current submission does not provide sufficient theoretical or empirical evidence to justify the claims in the unsupervised setting where the contribution would matter most.

Confidence
High.
