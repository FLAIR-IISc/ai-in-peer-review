Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses recovery of a low-rank matrix L0 and a sparse corruption S0 from X = L0 + S0 when L0 is coherent, i.e., violates standard incoherence conditions. It revisits Robust PCA (RPCA), which solves min ||L||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = L + S, and highlights its sensitivity to coherence. The authors analyze coherence via three parameters µ1, µ2, µ3 and identify a µ2-phenomenon: when data lie in a union of multiple subspaces (common in clustering), µ2 (row-space coherence) increases with the number of clusters, degrading RPCA’s performance (per Candès et al., JACM 2011).

To mitigate this, the paper considers Low-Rank Representation (LRR): min ||Z||* + λ||S||1 s.t. X = AZ + S for a pre-constructed dictionary A. The main theoretical result shows that if A is low-rank and its column space contains the true column space of L0 (U0 ⊂ UA), then exact recovery via LRR does not require bounds on µ2 (and µ3), only on µ1(A), with rank(L0) ≤ rank(A) ≤ O(n2/(µ1(A) log n1)) and |Ω| ≤ (0.5−ε)mn. A noisy analogue provides near-recovery with errors scaling as √mn ε. These guarantees are contrasted with RPCA’s bounds that depend on µ(L0) = max{µ1, µ2, µ3} and have an extra log factor.

Because in practice U0 is unknown, the paper proposes an unsupervised two-step heuristic: (1) run RPCA to estimate L0, rank-truncate and column-normalize to form a low-rank dictionary Â; (2) run LRR with Â. The claim is that when RPCA is already exact, the proposed algorithm is also exact, and in practice often improves over RPCA when data are coherent.

Experiments include: (a) synthetic 200×1000 matrices with five subspaces and varying subspace dimensions and sparsity of S0, showing a 47% larger “success area” than RPCA; (b) motion segmentation on 11 corrupted sequences added to Hopkins155, where matrix recovery improves clustering error for SIM, LRRx, SSC; the proposed method further reduces SSC error from 9.50% (after RPCA) to 5.74%.

This work builds on and relates to: RPCA (Candès et al., JACM 2011), matrix completion (Candès & Recht; Gross), outlier pursuit (Xu et al.), and LRR (Liu et al., PAMI 2013; ICML 2010) where the A = X case is well-studied. The novelty here is the analysis for general low-rank dictionaries and the “avoidance” of µ2 under U0 ⊂ UA, plus a practical dictionary-construction heuristic.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of the failure mode of RPCA under clustered data via the µ2-phenomenon; useful conceptual link between coherence and union-of-subspaces structure.
- Theoretical results for LRR with general low-rank dictionaries that remove dependence on µ2 (and thus µ3), under explicit and interpretable conditions (U0 ⊂ UA, rank bounds, column normalization).
- Practical algorithm that is simple (RPCA → dictionary → LRR), with plausible computational advantages when the learned A is low-rank.
- Empirical evidence on synthetic and motion data demonstrates meaningful gains over RPCA and improves downstream clustering performance.

Weaknesses
- The key assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong; the unsupervised algorithm provides no formal guarantee that Â will satisfy it unless RPCA is already (near-)correct. This introduces a potentially circular dependency on RPCA’s performance.
- Experimental scope is limited: one synthetic setup and one application domain (motion segmentation, small added set). Lack of systematic studies varying the number of clusters, cluster imbalance, or more real-world datasets.
- No comparison to other methods designed for coherent or structured low-rank recovery (e.g., column/row-sparse corruption models, reweighted RPCA variants, GoDec), or to iterative dictionary-learning approaches for LRR.
- Some methodological choices in experiments could be better justified: replacing missing entries with ±1 rather than treating them as missing (masking) may not reflect realistic noise; fixed ε = 10−6||X||F irrespective of noise level; thresholding singular values at 10−3σ1 without sensitivity analysis.
- Clarity issues: the main proofs are deferred; some notational footnotes are truncated; algorithmic solver details and runtime measurements are minimal.

Evaluation per NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The theoretical statements are precise, with conditions and probability guarantees; the removal of µ2 dependence is well-motivated. However, guarantees for the proposed unsupervised pipeline are heuristic, relying on RPCA’s initial success. Experiments support claims but are not exhaustive.
- Clarity: Generally well-organized with clear definitions of coherence parameters and model setup. The µ2 phenomenon is explained and empirically illustrated. Some presentation aspects (complete proofs, solver details, sensitivity studies) could be expanded.
- Originality: The link between coherence growth due to clustering and RPCA failure is insightful. The analysis of LRR with low-rank dictionaries avoiding µ2 under U0 ⊂ UA appears novel relative to prior LRR (mostly A = X). The practical dictionary-construction procedure is straightforward but useful.
- Significance: Addressing coherent data is important for robust subspace methods. The theoretical criterion for dictionary design is valuable and may inform future methods. Empirical gains are promising, though broader validation would strengthen impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Provides new theoretical insight: LRR with low-rank dictionaries can circumvent µ2-driven failures of RPCA.
- Simple, practical algorithm with demonstrated improvements on coherent data.
- Clear conceptual contribution connecting coherence to union-of-subspaces structure.

Arguments against acceptance
- Reliance on a strong and unverifiable assumption (U0 ⊂ UA) in practice; no guarantees for the unsupervised step beyond trivial cases where RPCA is already exact.
- Limited experimental breadth and comparisons; unclear robustness across settings and datasets.
- Some methodological choices (handling missing data, parameter selection) are ad hoc.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a proof sketch and intuition for Theorems 1–2 in the main text; include full proofs in the appendix.
- Add experiments systematically varying number of clusters, cluster sizes, and show µ2 growth and recovery performance; include ablations on dictionary quality (e.g., perturbed Â, different normalization).
- Compare against additional baselines (e.g., Outlier Pursuit, reweighted RPCA, GoDec, robust matrix factorization) and report wall-clock times and memory.
- Handle missing data via masking rather than imputation with ±1, and tune ε to actual noise; include sensitivity analyses for λ and the rank threshold.
- Discuss extensions to row-clustered data (large µ1) and whether analogous dictionary conditions could mitigate µ1.

Scope
The paper is within NeurIPS scope (machine learning theory and methods for high-dimensional, robust estimation). The contribution is relevant to practitioners and researchers working on subspace methods and robust matrix recovery.
