Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles robust recovery of a low-rank matrix L0 and a sparse corruption S0 from observations X = L0 + S0 when L0 is coherent, i.e., exhibits non-uniform sampling on its subspace (e.g., clustered columns). Standard RPCA/PCP [Candès et al. 2011] succeeds under incoherence conditions characterized by coherence parameters µ1, µ2, µ3; in practice, clustered data tends to inflate µ2 (the “µ2-phenomenon”), degrading RPCA performance. The paper studies this phenomenon and proposes a remedy based on Low-Rank Representation (LRR) with a learned low-rank dictionary A. The central theoretical claim is that if A is low-rank and its column space contains the true column space U0 (U0 ⊂ UA), then solving LRR with A avoids dependence on µ2 and can exactly (or near) recover L0 and S0 under sparsity up to a constant fraction and a rank bound that depends on µ1(A) rather than µ(L0). This is formalized via Theorems 1 (noiseless) and 2 (noisy). Practically, the paper proposes a simple two-stage unsupervised algorithm: run RPCA to estimate L0, build a normalized low-rank dictionary from that estimate, and then run LRR to refine the recovery. Empirically, on synthetic data with multiple subspaces and on corrupted motion trajectories (Hopkins155 variants), the method improves success regions and downstream clustering error compared to RPCA and to LRR with A=X.

Strengths:
- Clear articulation and empirical illustration of the µ2-phenomenon: as the number of underlying subspaces grows, µ2 increases while µ1 remains stable, explaining RPCA degradation on clustered data.
- Theoretical contribution: recovery guarantees for LRR with a low-rank dictionary that span U0, removing µ2 from conditions and slightly loosening rank scaling (O(n2/log n1) vs O(n2/(log n1)^2) in PCP-style results). The noisy result gives explicit Frobenius error bounds.
- Practical algorithm is simple, computationally reasonable, and empirically effective on both synthetic and motion data; the idea of leveraging an RPCA estimate to construct a well-conditioned low-rank dictionary is intuitive and useful.
- The work clarifies the role of coherence as reflecting intrinsic structure (e.g., clustering) rather than merely a technical artifact, and it connects LRR with low-rank dictionary learning/factorization intuitions.

Weaknesses:
- The key assumption U0 ⊂ UA is strong and central to the theorem; in the unsupervised setting, A is derived from RPCA, which may be inaccurate exactly when µ2 is large. The paper shows empirical robustness, but lacks a quantitative analysis for approximate inclusion (e.g., when principal angles between U0 and UA are small but nonzero).
- The improvement in rank scaling is modest and hinges on properties of A (µ1(A), rank(A)), shifting the coherence constraint rather than completely removing coherence requirements. Discussion of how µ1(A) behaves for the constructed dictionary would strengthen the claims.
- Empirical evaluation, while positive, is limited in scope: synthetic experiments consider a specific generative model and one “success region” plot; real data experiments focus on motion segmentation with missing entries replaced by ±1 rather than modeling true missingness (matrix completion), and downstream performance is reported rather than direct recovery error on L0/S0.
- The algorithm’s dependency on heuristics (rank threshold at 10−3σ1, normalization, single iteration) is not ablated; sensitivity to these choices and to λ is not analyzed. The potential iterative variant is mentioned but not studied.
- Clarity/organization: a number of crucial proofs are deferred; some notation is heavy, and a few claims (e.g., complexity O(n2 rA) vs O(n3)) depend on solver specifics and would benefit from careful justification. Some references and prior art (e.g., nonconvex robust PCA/AltProj, IRLS/GoDec, coherence-aware formulations) could be discussed.

Quality:
The paper is technically sound at a high level, with reasonable conditions and standard proof techniques in this area (though proofs are deferred). The empirical methodology is standard; claims are supported by experiments, but broader baselines and robustness analyses would improve confidence. The connection between dictionary design and avoidance of µ2 is insightful but predicated on a strong inclusion condition.

Clarity:
Generally well-written, with thorough background on coherence parameters and clear problem motivation. The presentation would benefit from:
- A short, self-contained sketch of the proof ideas for Theorem 1/2 to illuminate where µ2 vanishes and how µ1(A) enters.
- A precise statement of solver choices, stopping criteria, and runtime comparisons; and a clearer account of the noisy setting experiments (since ε is set but noise is not systematically varied).

Originality:
Moderately novel. LRR with general dictionaries has been explored, but the specific theoretical avoidance of µ2 via a low-rank dictionary spanning U0, and the simple two-stage construction using RPCA, are interesting contributions. The identification and empirical study of the µ2-phenomenon in clustered data is useful.

Significance:
Practical significance is plausible for applications with clustered subspaces (vision, trajectories, recommender systems with communities). However, the theoretical conditions are somewhat restrictive, and the unsupervised algorithm’s reliance on RPCA limits the extent to which it fundamentally transcends RPCA. With stronger robustness theory and broader experiments, impact would increase.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide robustness guarantees when UA only approximately contains U0, e.g., bounds in terms of principal angles or subspace distance, and how this affects recovery error and the role of µ2.
- Empirically vary the number of clusters, cluster imbalance, and subspace affinity to show how performance scales with µ2; report direct recovery metrics (‖L̂−L0‖F/‖L0‖F, support precision/recall for S0).
- Compare against additional baselines: LRR with A=X, nonconvex/alternating robust PCA (e.g., AltProj), GoDec/IRLS, Outlier Pursuit variants if appropriate, and matrix completion-based methods for missing data rather than imputing ±1.
- Study sensitivity to rank estimation threshold and λ; include an ablation on the iterative variant (one vs two iterations).
- Analyze properties of the learned A (rank, condition number, µ1(A)) and relate them to theory; justify the complexity claims with solver details.
- Improve clarity: streamline notation, fix minor typos, and include brief proof sketches.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a real and underexplored weakness of RPCA with coherent/clustered data.
- Provides a principled and simple method with clear empirical gains.
- Contributes theoretical insight linking dictionary design to coherence avoidance.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Core theoretical assumption (U0 ⊂ UA) is strong; lack of approximate-inclusion analysis.
- Empirical evaluation is relatively narrow and lacks broader baselines and robustness/sensitivity studies.
- Incremental nature of the algorithm (RPCA → LRR) and modest theoretical improvement may limit impact.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper is promising and well-motivated, but would benefit from robustness theory for approximate dictionaries and a more comprehensive empirical and comparative evaluation.
