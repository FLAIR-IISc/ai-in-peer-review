This paper presents a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP). Learning the unconstrained kernel is a notoriously difficult non-convex problem, and prior work has typically focused on more restricted, convex settings, such as learning weights for fixed kernels or for rows of the kernel. The authors’ approach cleverly reparameterizes the kernel via its eigendecomposition and derives an effective optimization procedure using a Jensen's inequality lower bound on the likelihood, which is then optimized via coordinate ascent for the eigenvalues and Stiefel manifold optimization for the eigenvectors.

The primary strength of this paper is its originality and technical quality. The proposed EM framework is a well-founded and meaningful contribution to the DPP literature, tackling a significant, open problem. The theoretical derivation appears sound and offers a new way to approach the challenge of learning a full DPP kernel.

However, the paper suffers from two major weaknesses in its current form: experimental validation and clarity. First, the experiments are limited in scope. While the proposed method shows favorable performance against a simple K-Ascent baseline, this is not sufficient to demonstrate its practical significance. The introduction mentions several alternative subset selection methods, but no comparisons are made against them. Without this broader context, it is difficult to assess the practical benefits of this DPP learning approach over the state of the art in diverse subset selection.

Second, the paper's exposition is a significant barrier to understanding and requires substantial revision. The writing is often sloppy, with notation introduced after its first use (e.g., marginal kernel K and eigenvectors V). The overall structure is convoluted, interleaving the core method derivation with side remarks and alternatives, making the main technical thread difficult to follow. I would strongly recommend presenting the final, complete method first in a clear, linear fashion before discussing alternatives or extensions. Specific phrasing is also imprecise (e.g., “minimises the change to Frobenius norm”), and references to external work (e.g., [26]) are used as a substitute for explaining the necessary concepts, hindering reproducibility. The rationale behind certain modeling choices, such as the initialization in equation (23), is also inadequately explained.

In summary, the core technical contribution is valuable and original. However, the paper is not yet ready for publication due to its weak empirical evaluation and significant lack of clarity. I would encourage the authors to thoroughly revise the writing and substantially strengthen the experimental section with a wider range of competitive baselines.
