This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP). DPPs are a powerful tool for modeling diversity and subset selection, but learning their kernel parameters via direct maximum likelihood estimation is challenging due to the non-convexity of the problem. The authors circumvent this by reparameterizing the kernel in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors, and then applying an EM approach with a carefully constructed lower bound on the likelihood.

The work builds upon a substantial body of prior research on DPPs, referencing key papers on their theoretical foundations [10, 21] and applications to various machine learning tasks [8, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 19, 2, 3, 20]. The authors correctly identify the limitations of previous approaches, such as learning only row weights [2] or employing fixed kernel matrices [3], and position their work as a method for learning the full kernel without restrictive parameterizations. The connection to the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix and its relation to elementary DPPs is a clever insight that underpins the proposed algorithm. The paper clearly articulates the difficulty of directly optimizing the likelihood and frames the EM approach as a solution to avoid problematic projection steps inherent in gradient ascent methods.

The strengths of this paper lie in its technical novelty and the potential for improved learning of DPPs. The formulation of the EM algorithm is well-motivated, and the theoretical derivations appear sound. The experiments on the baby registry dataset demonstrate that the proposed EM method consistently outperforms a projected gradient ascent approach (KA), particularly when initialized naively. The analysis of the runtime performance is also valuable, showing that EM can be significantly faster than KA in practice. The provided code and data enhance reproducibility.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge the approximations within the M-step, the discussion feels somewhat cursory. The complexity of the M-step update, and the reliance on single gradient steps and the Stiefel manifold update, detract from the claimed "elegance" of the method. Furthermore, the paper lacks an investigation into the interplay between the EM and KA algorithms. It would be beneficial to explore initializing EM from solutions obtained via KA, to assess whether the EM algorithm can further refine a good starting point.

The use of "relative log-likelihood" as the primary evaluation metric is a minor point, but reporting log-probability differences would likely provide more interpretable results. While not a major issue, the stylistic choices of overly enthusiastic wording (e.g., describing the algorithm as "elegant") and unnecessary bolding in the introduction detract from the overall professionalism of the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel EM framework for learning DPP kernels, addressing a challenging optimization problem.
*   Demonstrated empirical improvements over a baseline gradient ascent method.
*   Clear explanation of the underlying theory and connection to elementary DPPs.
*   Availability of code and data for reproducibility.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Complexity of the M-step and reliance on approximations.
*   Lack of analysis of initialization strategies (e.g., using KA solutions as a starting point for EM).
*   Use of "relative log-likelihood" which hinders interpretability.
*   Minor stylistic issues.

**Overall:**

This is a solid paper with a promising contribution to the field of DPP learning. The proposed EM algorithm offers a viable alternative to existing methods and demonstrates empirical gains on a real-world dataset. While the complexity of the M-step and the lack of exploration of initialization strategies are weaknesses, they do not fundamentally undermine the value of the work. The strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses, and I believe it represents a worthwhile contribution to NIPS.

**Rating:** Accept (with minor revisions – specifically, encouraging the authors to report log-probability differences and briefly discuss initialization from KA solutions).
