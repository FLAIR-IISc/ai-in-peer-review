This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from data. DPPs are probabilistic models that capture diversity in subset selection tasks, and are parameterized by a positive semi-definite (PSD) kernel matrix, L. The authors correctly note that learning the full matrix L via maximum likelihood is a non-convex problem, conjectured to be NP-hard. Previous work, such as Kulesza and Taskar (UAI 2011, ICML 2011), has circumvented this difficulty by learning more restricted parameterizations, such as a single weight per item or weights for a linear combination of fixed kernels. While these approaches are convex, they either fail to fully capture the item-item similarities that drive diversity or lose the attractive computational properties of a single DPP.

The main contribution of this paper is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) style algorithm for learning the full, unconstrained kernel matrix. The key idea is to reparameterize the kernel in terms of its eigendecomposition (eigenvalues and eigenvectors). This formulation naturally introduces a latent variable corresponding to a selection of eigenvectors, which enables the use of an EM approach. The resulting algorithm cleverly avoids the projection steps required by a naive projected gradient ascent baseline, which the authors argue can lead to poor local optima (e.g., near-diagonal kernels that fail to model diversity).

### Strengths

**Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and difficult open problem in the application of DPPs. Providing an effective method to learn the full kernel from data is a significant step forward that could greatly expand the practical utility of DPPs. The proposed EM algorithm, based on the eigendecomposition, is a novel approach to this problem. It moves beyond the restricted, convex settings of prior work and allows for learning a much richer model of quality and diversity. The results are likely to be built upon by other researchers and used by practitioners in areas like recommendation systems, summarization, and search.

**Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the derivation of the algorithm is rigorous. Key technical contributions include:
1.  The insightful reparameterization of the learning problem in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors, which is the cornerstone of the EM approach.
2.  The derivation of the EM lower bound using Jensen's inequality, which is standard but well-executed.
3.  A crucial finding (Lemma 1) that the posterior distribution over the latent variables needed for the E-step is itself a k-DPP, which allows for its efficient computation.
4.  The derivation of a closed-form update for the eigenvalues in the M-step and an efficient gradient computation for the eigenvectors. The use of optimization on the Stiefel manifold to handle the orthogonality constraint on the eigenvectors is particularly elegant and avoids the problematic projection step that plagues the baseline method.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the authors provide an intuitive explanation for why the projected gradient ascent baseline can fail. The step-by-step derivation of the EM algorithm is easy to follow, with each component (E-step, M-step for eigenvalues, M-step for eigenvectors) explained logically. The connection between the eigendecomposition and the generative view of a DPP is used effectively to motivate the introduction of the latent variable.

**Experimental Validation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the superiority of the proposed EM algorithm over the projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) baseline. The choice of the baby registry dataset provides a compelling and intuitive real-world application. The results show that EM achieves higher test log-likelihood, particularly when initialization is less informed (Wishart) or when data is scarce. The qualitative example—where the baseline method recommends three different baby monitors while EM produces a more diverse set—is a powerful illustration of the practical benefit of the proposed method. Furthermore, the demonstration that EM is also computationally faster than the baseline adds to its practical appeal.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The paper only compares against projected gradient ascent on the full kernel. While this is the most direct baseline, the paper's motivation rests on the limitations of prior work with restricted parameterizations [2, 3]. It would strengthen the paper's claims to include a comparison against these methods on the same dataset. This would help quantify not only that the EM approach is a better way to learn the full kernel, but also how much is gained by learning the full kernel compared to the restricted models.

2.  **Scope of Datasets:** The experiments are conducted on a single type of real-world dataset (product recommendation). While the application is well-chosen, demonstrating the algorithm's effectiveness on other common DPP application domains, such as document summarization or image search, would make a stronger case for its general applicability.

3.  **Conclusion:** The conclusion feels somewhat brief. It accurately summarizes the main contribution but could have done more to highlight the key technical insights (e.g., the posterior as a k-DPP, the Stiefel manifold optimization) and to discuss potential future work or limitations.

### Summary of evaluation

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound, with solid theoretical derivations and well-designed experiments that support its claims.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is very well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow.
*   **Originality:** High. The paper presents a novel and non-trivial approach to a known hard problem that has been a barrier to the wider application of DPPs.
*   **Significance:** High. The ability to learn a full DPP kernel effectively is an important contribution. The proposed algorithm is more robust and faster than the natural baseline, making it a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners interested in modeling diversity.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution. It presents a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound algorithm for an important problem, and backs it up with clear writing and convincing experiments. The weaknesses are minor and relate mostly to the scope of the experimental evaluation. I recommend this paper for acceptance.
