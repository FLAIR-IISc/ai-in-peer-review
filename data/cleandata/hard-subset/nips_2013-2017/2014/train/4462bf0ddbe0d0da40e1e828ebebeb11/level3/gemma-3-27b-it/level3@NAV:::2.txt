## Review of “Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes”

This paper presents a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP), addressing a known NP-hard problem. The approach cleverly reparameterizes the kernel in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors, utilizes a Jensen lower bound, and employs a Stiefel-manifold optimization step. This work builds upon a growing body of research leveraging DPPs for subset selection, attempting to overcome limitations of prior methods that focused on restricted learning scenarios like learning scalar weights or mixtures of fixed DPPs. 

The core idea – leveraging eigendecomposition and an EM framework – is original and represents a meaningful contribution to the field. The theoretical foundation appears sound, and the authors correctly identify the shortcomings of existing approaches, such as the projection issues encountered during naive gradient ascent.  The proposed algorithm avoids this pitfall and offers potential advantages in both speed and robustness.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The write-up is disorganized, interleaving method derivation with tangential discussions. Notation is often introduced *before* formal definition (e.g., marginal kernel K and variable V), hindering comprehension.  Phrasing is imprecise (e.g., “minimises the change to Frobenius norm” should be clearer), and references to external work (specifically [26] regarding Stiefel manifold optimization) are unnecessarily burdensome, requiring readers to consult the cited source to understand the method. Ambiguous terms, like “weight” on page 4, require clarification. Moreover, the rationale behind certain choices, such as excluding *m<sub>ij</sub>* in equation (30) and the initialization strategy, is not adequately explained.

The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating improved test log-likelihood compared to a K-Ascent baseline, is limited in scope. Crucially, the paper lacks comparisons to other relevant subset-selection methods mentioned in the introduction (e.g., Bayesian approaches or those using alternative kernel parameterizations). Without such comparisons, it’s difficult to assess the true practical benefits of the proposed DPP-based approach. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Technically sound, original, addresses an important problem, and demonstrates improvements over a simple baseline.
**Arguments against Acceptance:** Poor clarity, insufficient experimental validation, and lacks a thorough comparison to existing methods.

**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable technical contribution, but requires substantial revision to improve clarity and strengthen the experimental evaluation before it can be considered for acceptance. I recommend rejection in its current form, with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the aforementioned concerns.
