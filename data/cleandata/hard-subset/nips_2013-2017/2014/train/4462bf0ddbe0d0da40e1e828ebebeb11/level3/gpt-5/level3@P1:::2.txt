This paper proposes an expectation–maximization (EM) algorithm for maximum-likelihood learning of determinantal point processes (DPPs), parameterized via the marginal kernel K’s eigendecomposition. The key idea is to reparameterize K with eigenvalues and eigenvectors, introduce the latent index set J of active eigenvectors from the mixture view of DPPs (elementary DPPs), and form a Jensen lower bound on the log-likelihood. The E-step sets q(J|Y) to the exact posterior pK(J|Y), which the authors show is a k-DPP conditioned on |J| = |Y| and for which efficient marginal computations are available. The M-step yields a closed-form update for eigenvalues via k-DPP marginals, and a gradient step for eigenvectors on the Stiefel manifold using a multiplicative update. This avoids the projection pathologies of naive projected gradient ascent on K’s entries (KA) and yields better asymptotic complexity O(nNk^2) per EM iteration, where k is the maximum set size. Experiments on an Amazon baby registry recommendation task show consistent test log-likelihood gains over KA (up to 16.5%) and 2.1× speedups, with larger gains in categories where off-diagonal structure matters. The work relates to earlier DPP learning that restricts parameterization—per-row weights [2], mixtures of fixed kernels [3], Bayesian parametric approaches [8]—and advances toward learning a full, nonparametric kernel.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The EM construction is principled: the mixture view (elementary DPPs), hidden variable J, and Jensen bound are standard. The E-step as a k-DPP is well-motivated and enables polynomial-time marginals used in the eigenvalue update. The eigenvector update on a Stiefel manifold is also appropriate. However, convergence guarantees are not fully established: the algorithm uses only one gradient step for V per M-step and selects step sizes by checking likelihood after re-running the E-step. Please clarify whether L is guaranteed to increase monotonically under this inexact M-step and whether F(q,V,Λ) remains a valid lower bound throughout. Discuss stationary-point conditions and any safeguards when eigenvalues approach 1 (numerical stability of R = diag(√λ/(1−λ))).
- Baselines: The KA baseline is very weak; many of its failure modes are due to naive projection and step size. Stronger baselines (e.g., parameterizing K = V diag(σ(θ))V⊤ and using manifold optimization; factorized K = BB⊤ with spectral squashing of eigenvalues; Riemannian trust-region or proximal methods) would better isolate the benefit of the EM formulation.
- Experiments: The single-domain evaluation and use of test log-likelihood alone make it hard to assess practical impact for recommendation. Additional metrics (e.g., precision/recall/NDCG@k, intra-list similarity, coverage/diversity) and MAP set quality would strengthen the case. Include synthetic recovery experiments to test identifiability and robustness to initialization, and sensitivity to set size k and N. Compare against alternative subset-selection methods mentioned in the introduction (e.g., submodular facility-location/diversity models, mixture-of-DPPs [3], “quality-only” learning [2]) to support claims of practical advantage.

Clarity
- Organization: The derivation is interleaved with side remarks, which obscures the main thread. Consider reorganizing to: (1) preliminaries and the mixture/eigendecomposition view; (2) EM lower bound and E-step; (3) M-step eigenvalue update; (4) M-step eigenvector update on the Stiefel manifold; (5) complexity and convergence discussion; (6) experiments.
- Notation: Several symbols are used before being defined (e.g., K and V in early sections). Define all matrices (U, R, ZYi) the first time they appear, and keep consistent subscripts/superscripts. Clearly state that k = max|Yi|.
- Language: Tighten phrasing and avoid ambiguous terms. For example, replace “minimizes the change to Frobenius norm” with “projects to the closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix.” Specify what “weight” refers to in the mixture pK(J) (product of λj and 1−λj factors).
- Stiefel manifold step: The update references [26] but is hard to follow without derivation. Please provide a self-contained description of the simplified multiplicative update, define the matrix exponential, and detail how you compute the skew-symmetric direction V⊤G−G⊤V and its cost.
- Initialization by moment matching: Equation (23) sets K̃ij = sqrt(max(K̃ii K̃jj − mij, 0)). Provide the rationale: for a DPP, P({i,j}⊆Y)=det(K{ij})=Kii Kjj−Kij^2, so this choice approximately matches first- and second-order marginals. Discuss the sign ambiguity of Kij, why the positive root is chosen, and why empirical covariance isn’t used (it generally won’t satisfy DPP constraints).

Originality
- The EM approach that leverages the mixture over elementary DPPs to derive tractable E- and M-steps for the full kernel is novel relative to prior restricted-learning approaches ([2], [3], [8]). The Stiefel-manifold optimization within this EM framework is also a thoughtful design. The contribution is incremental in the sense that it builds on known properties of DPPs, but it addresses a long-standing practical gap (full-kernel learning).

Significance
- If robust and scalable, this could notably impact learning for diverse subset selection across domains (summarization, sensor placement, search, recommendation). The current empirical scope (one dataset, one weak baseline) limits the demonstrated significance. Stronger baselines and broader evaluations would likely elevate the impact.

Pros
- Principled EM derivation exploiting the DPP mixture/eigendecomposition.
- Avoids harmful PSD projections and is asymptotically faster for small k.
- Clear empirical evidence of KA’s projection pathology and EM’s robustness.
- Practical algorithmic details (e.g., eigenvalue updates via k-DPP marginals).

Cons
- Convergence and monotonicity under inexact M-steps are not established.
- Experimental scope is narrow; baselines are limited.
- Exposition/notation are sloppy in places; reliance on external references for key steps.
- Initialization choices and their rationale need clearer justification and analysis.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a convergence proposition (monotonic increase of L or F) under the implemented inexact M-steps, or clearly state limitations.
- Add comparisons to (i) submodular diversification models, (ii) mixture-of-fixed-DPPs [3], (iii) per-item weight learning [2], and stronger constrained optimization baselines.
- Evaluate application-relevant metrics (diversity/quality trade-offs, MAP set quality), ablations (with/without eigenvector updates), and sensitivity to initialization and data sparsity.
- Improve notation and structure as noted; include a succinct, self-contained description of the Stiefel update.

Recommendation: borderline accept. The method is original and technically interesting, addresses an important problem, and shows promising gains, but the paper needs clearer exposition and substantially stronger experimental comparisons to support its significance. Confidence: moderate.
