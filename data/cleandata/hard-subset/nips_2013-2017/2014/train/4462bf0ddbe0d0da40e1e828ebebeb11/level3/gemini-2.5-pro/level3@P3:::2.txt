This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from data. The authors correctly note that maximizing the log-likelihood is a non-convex problem, and prior work has focused on more restricted, convex settings, such as learning weights for kernel rows or for a mixture of fixed DPPs. The main contribution is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the unconstrained kernel. The key idea is to re-parameterize the marginal kernel K via its eigendecomposition, K = VΛVᵀ. This allows the authors to view the DPP as a mixture of "elementary" DPPs, where the mixture components are determined by a latent variable J indicating a subset of eigenvectors. By applying Jensen's inequality to this latent variable formulation, they derive an EM algorithm that alternates between computing the posterior over J (E-step) and updating the eigenvalues Λ and eigenvectors V (M-step). The update for V is handled via optimization on the Stiefel manifold.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental, difficult, and open problem in the DPP literature: learning the full kernel matrix. The proposed EM framework is a novel and principled approach that moves beyond the restrictive assumptions of prior work. By enabling the learning of a full, non-parametric kernel, this work has the potential to significantly advance the application of DPPs to real-world subset selection tasks where modeling complex diversity patterns is crucial. The conceptual contribution is strong.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method appears to be technically well-founded. The derivation of the EM algorithm by introducing a latent variable for the eigenvector basis and applying a Jensen lower bound is a standard and sound technique. The formulation is clever, and the use of Stiefel manifold optimization for the eigenvector updates is appropriate for the constraints of the problem.

### Weaknesses

*   **Clarity and Exposition:** The paper's greatest weakness is its clarity. The write-up is often sloppy and difficult to follow, which obscures the novel contributions.
    *   **Structure:** The organization of Section 3 is convoluted. The core derivation of the algorithm is frequently interleaved with side remarks, justifications for alternative choices (like why projection is bad), and implementation details. This makes the main thread of the algorithm hard to trace. A much clearer structure would be to present the final EM algorithm cleanly upfront, and then dedicate subsequent subsections to deriving each step.
    *   **Vague Language and Notation:** The prose is sometimes imprecise. For instance, in Algorithm 1's description, "chooses the closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix to Q" is much clearer than the paper's "minimises the change to Frobenius norm." Notation is sometimes used before it is clearly contextualized; for example, the variables `K` and `V` are central to the work, but their roles could be established more clearly in the introduction before they appear in equations.
    *   **Lack of Self-Containment:** The eigenvector update step relies on a method from [26], but the paper provides little intuition, stating only that they use a simplified version of the algorithm. To improve reproducibility and understanding, the authors should briefly outline the update logic they are using rather than requiring readers to consult an external text for a core component of their method.

*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is insufficient to demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed method.
    *   **Limited Baselines:** The experiments only compare the proposed EM algorithm to a projected gradient ascent method (K-Ascent). While this comparison shows that EM is more robust and efficient than this simple baseline, it is a weak point of comparison. K-Ascent is shown to be sensitive to initialization and prone to failure, which may exaggerate the relative performance of the EM approach.
    *   **Missing Comparisons:** Crucially, the paper fails to compare against the very methods it motivates its work against in the introduction, namely the restricted DPP learning settings from Kulesza & Taskar ([2], [3]). It also lacks comparisons to any non-DPP subset selection methods. Without these comparisons, it is impossible to judge whether the significant increase in model complexity and the difficulty of non-convex optimization are justified by a meaningful performance gain in practice. Does this complex method offer a real-world advantage over simpler, convex alternatives? The experiments do not provide an answer.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   Could you clarify the initialization strategy in Eq. (23)? The derivation from second-order moments makes sense, but have you considered alternative initializations, such as deriving K from an initial L set to the empirical covariance of the item indicator vectors? This seems like a natural starting point.
*   The term "weight" on p.4 (line 189 in the original submission, referring to the mixture weight in Eq. 7) could be made more specific for clarity.
*   The experimental results on the data-poor setting (n=2N) are interesting and suggest EM is more robust. This is a strong point that could be emphasized more, but it still requires stronger baselines to be fully convincing.
*   The qualitative example of the baby registry items in Figure 2a is helpful, but would be stronger if compared to the output from a model trained using the convex method of [2] as well.

### Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically solid and original contribution to a significant problem in machine learning. The EM-based approach to learning a full DPP kernel is a valuable research direction. However, the paper is held back by a poor presentation and an experimental section that, while showing promise against a weak baseline, fails to establish the practical utility of the method compared to existing, simpler alternatives. The work is interesting and the core ideas are strong, but the paper needs substantial revision to improve its clarity and a much more thorough experimental evaluation to justify its claims of significance.
