

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper presents an EM algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a problem previously considered NP-hard. The authors make a key insight by changing the parameterization from matrix entries to eigenvalues and eigenvectors, which enables a more robust optimization procedure compared to projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent). This work builds on prior DPP research (e.g., Kulesza & Taskar's work on DPPs as probabilistic models for subset selection [14, 15]) but differs by avoiding restrictive parameterizations of the kernel matrix, which was a limitation in previous approaches [2, 3, 8].

**Quality:** The technical content is sound with a well-motivated approach. The EM framework with eigendecomposition avoids the problematic projection steps required by gradient ascent, which the authors convincingly demonstrate can lead to degenerate solutions (near-diagonal kernels that fail to model diversity). The asymptotic runtime analysis showing EM's advantages over K-Ascent is plausible. However, the experimental evaluation is severely limited. The paper only tests on one dataset (baby registry product recommendations) and compares exclusively to K-Ascent, omitting comparisons to other subset selection methods mentioned in the introduction (e.g., greedy submodular maximization, other probabilistic models for diversity). Without these comparisons, it's impossible to assess the practical benefits of the DPP approach over established techniques.

**Clarity:** The paper suffers from significant clarity issues. Notation is often introduced after use (e.g., marginal kernel K and variable V are used before definition), and phrasing is imprecise ("minimises the change to Frobenius norm" should state "closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix to Q"). The paper structure interweaves method derivation with side remarks, making the main thread difficult to follow. Section 3.5's reference to external work [26] is particularly problematic as it forces readers to look up the original paper rather than explaining the simpler approach directly. The term "weight" in p.4 line 189 is ambiguous without context. The rationale for equation (30) is questionable—why not initialize with the empirical covariance matrix?

**Originality:** The paper makes a meaningful contribution by presenting a novel method for learning the full kernel matrix without restrictive parameterizations. The use of eigendecomposition, mixtures of elementary DPPs, and Stiefel-manifold optimization in an EM framework is original. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from Affandi et al.'s work on learning DPP kernels [8], which also uses Bayesian methods to learn unconstrained kernels, though with a different approach.

**Significance:** The significance is limited by the narrow experimental scope and lack of comparison to alternative subset selection methods. While the paper demonstrates 16.5% relative gains in log-likelihood over K-Ascent in specific settings, it fails to show how DPPs compare to other approaches for subset selection that have been established in the literature (e.g., greedy algorithms for submodular functions). The claim that "modeling negative interactions between items is important" lacks empirical support without comparative experiments. The paper would be significantly stronger with experiments on standard subset selection benchmarks.

**Overall:** This is technically sound and original work that makes a meaningful contribution to the DPP literature. However, the writing is sloppy and the experimental evaluation is inadequate. The paper would benefit greatly from clearer exposition (fixing notation issues, restructuring to present the method before side remarks), and substantially stronger experimental comparisons (to other subset selection methods beyond K-Ascent).

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept. The technical contribution is solid and novel, but the paper needs significant revisions to address clarity issues and substantially strengthen the experimental evaluation before it can be considered for publication. The current version does not sufficiently demonstrate the practical advantages of the DPP approach over other subset selection methods, which is crucial for establishing significance.
