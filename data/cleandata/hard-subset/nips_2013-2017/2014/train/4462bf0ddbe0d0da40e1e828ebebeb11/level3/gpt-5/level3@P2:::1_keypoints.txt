- Proposes learning full DPP kernel by EM in the eigenbasis, treating DPP as a mixture over elementary DPPs.  
- E-step computes q(J|Yi)=pK(J|Yi), a k-DPP with closed-form kernel from V and λ.  
- M-step gives closed-form λ updates via k-DPP marginals and a gradient update for V on the Stiefel manifold (practically one step).  
- Method avoids PSD/eigenvalue projection and aims to preserve non-diagonal kernel structure.  
- Per-iteration complexity O(n N k^2) for E/M plus O(N^3) for manifold updates; can be faster than KA's O(n N^3) inverses.  
- Experiments on Amazon baby registries show EM improves median test log-likelihood (~3.7% with Wishart init) and performs much better in low-data regimes (up to 16.5%).  
- EM is about 2.1× faster wall-clock than KA in the tested settings.  
- Derivations are technically sound and consistent with DPP theory, but the M-step is approximate (single gradient step) and weakens EM monotonicity/convergence guarantees.  
- Convergence to critical points or of the practical algorithm is not established.  
- Empirical evaluation is on realistic data and isolates initialization effects, but baselines are limited (missing per-item, mixture, Bayesian, and simple quality models).  
- Reporting “relative” log-likelihood is unconventional; absolute/additive differences and CIs would be clearer.  
- Paper is well-written and clear on k-DPP posterior and computational costs; minor style issues and a need to clarify convergence guarantees.  
- The approach is original in applying EM over eigenvectors with a k-DPP E-step and Stiefel updates.  
- Significant for applications needing full-kernel learning (diverse recommendation, summarization), though average gains are modest and scalability to large N is unclear.  
- Strengths: principled EM formulation, closed-form eigenvalue update, empirical gains in off-diagonal/low-data settings, and competitive runtime.  
- Weaknesses: approximate M-step with no theoretical guarantees, limited baselines, unclear scalability, and awkward “relative” metric reporting.  
- Suggested improvements: add baselines (per-item, fixed-L mixtures, Bayesian), report additive log-likelihoods and CIs, study initialization sensitivity, discuss convergence/monotonicity, and explore regularization/low-rank scaling.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel principled solution to a hard problem, empirical robustness and speed, clear algorithms and public code.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited theory for the approximate M-step, narrow empirical comparisons, and unknown scalability to large ground sets.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — promising and technically grounded but needs broader empirical validation and stronger convergence/scalability analysis.
