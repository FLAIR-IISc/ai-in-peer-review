- Tackles maximum-likelihood learning for DPPs without restricting kernel parameterization, optimizing full kernel eigenvalues and eigenvectors.  
- Proposes an EM procedure derived from a Jensen lower bound on the DPP log-likelihood.  
- E-step identifies a conditional distribution over eigen-index sets that is a k-DPP, enabling efficient computation of sufficient statistics.  
- M-step updates eigenvalues in closed form and updates eigenvectors via gradient steps on the Stiefel manifold, preserving PSD and spectral constraints.  
- Avoids harmful spectral-projection steps used by naive projected gradient ascent (KA), which can cause degeneracy.  
- Situates work clearly against prior DPP learning approaches (convex/restricted and Bayesian parametric), highlighting novelty of unconstrained kernel learning.  
- Derivation is technically sound and insightful, with elegant use of k-DPP structure and appropriate manifold optimization.  
- Computational complexity analysis is convincing and the projection-degeneracy observation is well-motivated.  
- Caveat: M-step does not fully maximize the bound (single V step with line search), so monotonicity/convergence guarantees are not established.  
- Writing, proofs, and EM derivation are clear and reproducible; conclusion is abrupt and understates manifold optimization and k-DPP results.  
- Originality: high — novel EM scheme exploiting eigendecomposition for unrestricted L learning.  
- Significance: empirical gains shown on synthetic data and one product recommendation task, demonstrating improved test likelihood and more diverse selections.  
- Evaluation is limited to one real-world domain and lacks comparisons to stronger baselines (e.g., Bayesian parametric DPP learning).  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, well-motivated EM formulation; solid technical contributions; empirical improvements and practical relevance.  
- Rejection arguments: limited real-world evaluation, partial M-step raising convergence/initialization concerns, and some implementation details (e.g., handling λ ≈ 1) need expansion.
