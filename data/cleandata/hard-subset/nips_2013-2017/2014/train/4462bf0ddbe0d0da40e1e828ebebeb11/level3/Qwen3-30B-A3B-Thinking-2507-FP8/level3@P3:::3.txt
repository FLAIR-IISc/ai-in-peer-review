

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper addresses a significant challenge in DPP learning: the NP-hard problem of learning the full kernel matrix L without restrictive parameterization. Previous work had limited approaches: [2] learned only scalar weights per row (focusing on quality but not diversity), [3] learned weights for linear combinations of fixed DPPs (losing DPP properties), and [8] assumed fixed parametric forms for L. The authors propose a novel EM-based method that avoids these restrictions by parameterizing L through its eigenvalues and eigenvectors, eliminating the need for problematic projection steps in gradient ascent.

The key insight is to exploit the eigendecomposition of L and apply Jensen's inequality to lower-bound the log-likelihood, creating an EM framework. The E-step identifies a hidden variable J (the set of selected eigenvectors) and computes a k-DPP distribution. The M-step updates eigenvalues in closed form (no projection needed) and uses a Stiefel manifold optimization for eigenvectors, avoiding projection that harms diversity. The authors prove that the inverse transformation from K to L preserves DPP properties, and provide a complete EM derivation.

The paper demonstrates strong technical contributions: mapping the optimization to the Stiefel manifold, showing the inverse distribution remains a DPP, and providing a clear EM derivation. Experiments on 13 Amazon baby registry categories show EM achieves 16.5% higher log-likelihood than the KA baseline (K-Ascent) in data-poor settings, while better preserving diversity (e.g., KA incorrectly recommended three video monitors, while EM recommended more diverse products).

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with solid theoretical foundations. The EM derivation is rigorous, and the experiments adequately support claims about diversity preservation. The runtime analysis shows asymptotic advantages over gradient-based methods. However, the conclusion is somewhat hasty, omitting the significance of the Stiefel manifold optimization contribution.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear exposition of the EM procedure. The connection between DPP properties and eigenvalue/eigenvector parameterization is well-motivated. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-2) are precise and reproducible. The supplementary material provides valuable technical details.

**Originality**: This work significantly advances the state of DPP learning by removing the restrictive parameterization assumptions of prior work. The EM approach on eigenvalues/eigenvectors is novel, and the avoidance of projection steps represents a fundamental methodological shift from previous approaches. The paper clearly differentiates from prior work in the introduction and related work.

**Significance**: The method achieves demonstrable improvements in both quality and diversity on real-world recommendation tasks. The 16.5% log-likelihood gain in data-poor settings is substantial. While the paper focuses on product recommendation, the approach could benefit many DPP applications requiring negative interactions modeling (e.g., document summarization, sensor placement). The method's computational advantages (2.1x faster on average) enhance practical applicability.

**Strengths**: 
- Novel EM approach that avoids projection steps harming diversity
- Complete theoretical derivation with clear connection to DPP properties
- Strong empirical results demonstrating diversity preservation
- Comprehensive runtime analysis showing asymptotic advantages
- Well-structured and clearly written paper

**Weaknesses**: 
- Conclusion omits key technical contributions (Stiefel manifold optimization)
- Limited to one real-world dataset (Amazon baby registries)
- Lacks experiments on additional real-world datasets to demonstrate broader practical importance
- Does not explore how the method scales to extremely large N (beyond N=71 in experiments)

**Recommendation**: Accept. This paper makes a significant contribution to the DPP learning literature with a novel, theoretically sound approach that addresses a fundamental limitation of prior work. The method's ability to preserve diversity while improving quality is well-demonstrated, and the technical contributions are substantial. While additional experiments on more diverse datasets would strengthen the practical impact, the paper's core contribution is clear and significant. The writing is excellent, and the results are well-supported. The paper advances the state of the art in DPP learning in a demonstrable way that will be valuable to researchers and practitioners working with diverse set selection problems.
