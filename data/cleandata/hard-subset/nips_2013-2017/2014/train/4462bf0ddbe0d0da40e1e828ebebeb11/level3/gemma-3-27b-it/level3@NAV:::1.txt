## Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix in a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) model. The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework, reformulating kernel learning to bypass the difficulties of direct gradient-based optimization (referred to as KA). The core idea leverages the eigendecomposition of the kernel, allowing for a more stable and potentially efficient learning process. 

The work builds upon the established literature on DPPs, acknowledging previous limitations like the need for restrictive parameterizations (e.g., learning scalar weights) or the computational cost of maintaining a large ensemble of kernels. The authors correctly identify the NP-hardness of full kernel learning and position their EM approach as a viable alternative. The decomposition into elementary DPPs, and the use of a variational distribution updated as a k-DPP in the E-step, are well-motivated and technically sound.

The empirical evaluation on a product recommendation task demonstrates a clear advantage of the EM approach, particularly when compared to a naive KA initialization. The modest gains even with improved KA initialization suggest a fundamental benefit to the EM formulation. However, the M-step’s complexity, requiring approximations and caveats, detracts somewhat from the claimed elegance of the method. The reliance on a single gradient step in the M-step feels like a potential bottleneck and a point for future investigation. 

A significant weakness is the lack of analysis on the effect of initialization. Specifically, it’s unclear whether initializing the EM algorithm with solutions obtained from KA would yield further improvements, or if the EM algorithm consistently surpasses KA regardless of the starting point. Moreover, the use of “relative log likelihood” as the primary metric is questionable; reporting log-probability *differences* would be more intuitive and facilitate easier comparison with other models. 

Finally, the writing contains minor stylistic issues – the repeated assertion of the algorithm’s “elegance” feels unnecessary, and the bolding in the introduction is distracting.

**Overall:** This is a promising and interesting approach to DPP kernel learning. While the empirical gains are modest, the method demonstrates robustness and potential for further optimization. The experiments could be strengthened with an initialization analysis and a clearer metric choice. I recommend **acceptance**, contingent on the authors addressing these points in a revised version.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
* Novel approach to a difficult problem.
* Technically sound with a clear theoretical justification.
* Demonstrates empirical gains over baseline methods.
* Addresses a key limitation in existing DPP learning techniques.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
* Modest empirical gains.
* Complex M-step with approximations.
* Missing initialization analysis.
* Questionable metric choice.
* Minor stylistic concerns.
