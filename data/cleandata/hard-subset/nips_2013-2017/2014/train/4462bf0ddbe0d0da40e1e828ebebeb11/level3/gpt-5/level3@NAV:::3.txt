This paper tackles maximum-likelihood learning for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without restricting the kernel parameterization. Prior work typically learns only scalar or low-dimensional parameters (e.g., per-item weights or mixtures of fixed kernels), sacrificing the ability to model diversity through off-diagonals. The authors instead optimize directly over the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the DPP kernel via an EM procedure derived from a Jensen lower bound on the log-likelihood. The E-step identifies a conditional distribution over eigen-index sets that is a k-DPP, enabling efficient computation of sufficient statistics. The M-step updates eigenvalues in closed form and takes a gradient step on eigenvectors with a Stiefel manifold update, avoiding the harmful projection steps that plague naive projected gradient ascent (KA). The method thus preserves diversity structure while maintaining PSD and spectral constraints.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself clearly with respect to DPP modeling and learning (e.g., Kulesza & Taskar and follow-ups), contrasting with convex but restricted learning and Bayesian parametric approaches. The EM view based on the mixture of elementary DPPs is classical for inference, but its use for unconstrained learning of the full kernel is novel.

Quality: The derivation is technically sound and insightful. Using k-DPP structure to compute marginals for eigenvalue updates is elegant, and mapping the orthogonality constraint to optimization on the Stiefel manifold is appropriate. The analysis of computational complexity and the observation about projection-induced degeneracy are convincing. A caveat is that the M-step does not fully maximize the bound (single step on V with line search), so monotonicity/convergence guarantees are not fully established.

Clarity: The writing, proofs, and step-by-step EM derivation are clear and reproducible. The conclusion is somewhat abrupt and omits emphasizing the manifold optimization and conditional k-DPP result.

Originality: High. Unrestricted learning of L with an EM scheme that exploits the eigendecomposition is novel relative to previous constrained formulations.

Significance: The experiments on synthetic data and a real product recommendation task show improved test likelihood and more diverse selections; even when KA’s top-10 differs in only two items, it suggests redundant products (three baby monitors), underscoring the diversity advantage. Broader real-world benchmarks and comparisons to stronger baselines (e.g., Bayesian parametric DPP learning) would strengthen impact.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, well-motivated EM formulation that avoids spectral projections and preserves diversity.
- Solid technical contributions (k-DPP E-step, Stiefel manifold update) with coherent derivations.
- Empirical gains in both quality and diversity; practical relevance.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited real-world evaluation beyond one domain; missing comparisons to alternative learning schemes.
- Partial M-step on V leaves open questions about convergence guarantees and sensitivity to initialization.
- Conclusion undersells contributions; some implementation details (e.g., handling λ ≈ 1) could be expanded.
