## Review of “Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes”

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix *L* for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), a task known to be NP-hard. Existing approaches either partially learn *L* (e.g., learning scalar weights) or impose restrictive parameterizations, sacrificing the ability to model full diversity. This work proposes a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that learns the full kernel matrix without such restrictions, cleverly re-parameterizing *L* in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors. 

The core contribution lies in the derivation of an EM procedure that avoids problematic projection steps inherent in naive gradient ascent methods, which can lead to degenerate solutions (near-diagonal *L* matrices, hindering diversity). The authors achieve this by lower-bounding the likelihood using Jensen’s inequality and exploiting the eigendecomposition of *L*.  Mapping the constraint to optimization on the Stiefel manifold and demonstrating the inverse distribution is a DPP are also strong technical contributions. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the mathematical derivations, particularly the step-by-step EM procedure. The authors acknowledge limitations and provide a thorough discussion of related work, correctly positioning their contribution within the existing DPP literature.  Experimental results on synthetic data and a product recommendation task demonstrate that the proposed method preserves both quality and diversity, outperforming a baseline Kernel Ascent (KA) method. The comparison to KA, even with its limited ability to incorporate diversity, effectively highlights the benefits of the full-kernel learning approach.

However, the conclusion feels somewhat rushed and doesn't fully emphasize the significance of the technical contributions. While the experiments are promising, they are limited to a single real-world task. Expanding the evaluation to other datasets and application domains would strengthen the claims about the practical importance of the learned diversity.  The KA baseline, while reasonable, only replaces a small number of products, potentially understating the advantage of the proposed method. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Addresses a fundamental challenge in DPP learning with a novel approach.
*   **Technical Soundness:**  The EM derivation is rigorous and well-explained.
*   **Significance:** Offers a more flexible and potentially more effective way to learn DPPs, enabling better modeling of diversity.
*   **Clarity:** Exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Real-World Evaluation:**  Experiments are confined to a single task.
*   **Conservative Baseline:** The KA baseline may not fully showcase the method’s potential.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of DPP learning. Despite the minor weaknesses, the technical novelty, clarity, and promising experimental results warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to expand the experimental evaluation and strengthen the conclusion.
