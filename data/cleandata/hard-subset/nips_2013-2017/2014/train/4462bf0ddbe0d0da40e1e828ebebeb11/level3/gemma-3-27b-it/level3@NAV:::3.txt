## Review of “Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes”

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix *L* for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), a task known to be NP-hard. Previous approaches have been limited by either partially learning *L* (scalar weights or restricted forms) or suffering from issues like projection steps in gradient-based learning, which can hinder diversity. The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) approach that avoids these limitations by operating on the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of *L*, leveraging Jensen’s inequality to derive a tractable objective.

The core contribution is a learning method that doesn’t restrict the parameterization of *L*, a significant step forward. The paper elegantly maps the constrained learning problem onto optimization on the Stiefel manifold and demonstrates the resulting distribution is indeed a DPP. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is particularly insightful and well-explained, showcasing a strong theoretical understanding. 

Experiments on synthetic data and a product recommendation task demonstrate that the proposed method effectively preserves both quality and diversity, surpassing the K-Ascent (KA) baseline.  The comparison to KA, while perhaps a simplified baseline (limiting replacements to the top-10 products), clearly highlights the benefits of the EM approach, particularly in maintaining diversity.  The writing is generally clear and coherent, with proofs appearing sound. 

However, the conclusion feels somewhat rushed and doesn’t fully emphasize the contributions. The authors could benefit from further highlighting the practical implications of their work.  A significant weakness is the limited scope of real-world datasets. While the product recommendation task is a good start, further evaluation on diverse datasets would strengthen the claim of broader applicability and demonstrate the practical significance of the improved diversity.  It would be valuable to see if the method exhibits similar improvements in other domains where DPPs are utilized, such as document summarization or image search.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a challenging problem with strong theoretical grounding.
*   Clear and well-explained derivation of the EM algorithm.
*   Empirical results demonstrate improved performance in maintaining both quality and diversity.
*   Solid technical contributions (Stiefel manifold mapping, inverse distribution proof).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of real-world experiments.
*   Conclusion feels incomplete.
*   Baseline comparison (KA) is somewhat restrictive.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of DPP learning. The theoretical soundness and empirical results warrant acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns regarding the limited real-world evaluation and bolstering the conclusion. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
