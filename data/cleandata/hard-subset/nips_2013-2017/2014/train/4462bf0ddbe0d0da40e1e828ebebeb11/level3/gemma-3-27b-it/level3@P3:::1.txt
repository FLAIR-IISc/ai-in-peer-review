This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework for learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP), offering an alternative to direct gradient-based kernel parameter learning (KA). DPPs are valuable for modeling diversity in subset selection, and learning the kernel is crucial for applying them effectively. Prior work has often been limited to learning subsets of kernel parameters due to the NP-hardness of full kernel learning. This paper cleverly reformulates the problem, leveraging the eigendecomposition of the kernel to facilitate an EM approach.

The core idea is well-motivated. The paper correctly points out the limitations of existing methods – the convexity restrictions of [2] and [3], and the potential for degenerate solutions with gradient ascent as discussed in [1]. The decomposition into elementary DPPs, and the use of a variational distribution (k-DPP) in the E-step are logical and build upon established DPP theory [21]. The theoretical justification for the M-step, while complex, is presented adequately, and the connection to existing work on Stiefel manifolds is appreciated.

The empirical evaluation on the baby registry dataset is a good starting point. The reported gains of up to 16.5% compared to naive KA initialization are encouraging, and the modest improvements even with smarter initialization are noted. The discussion relating performance gains to the data’s underlying structure (through the 'd' metric) is insightful. However, the experiments lack a critical analysis of initialization effects. Specifically, testing whether initializing the EM algorithm *from* the solution obtained by KA would improve performance is a significant omission. It’s unclear if the EM method genuinely offers a superior optimization trajectory, or if it merely benefits from escaping poor local optima resulting from the KA initialization.

One significant weakness lies in the complexity of the M-step. While the authors acknowledge it, the extent of approximations and caveats diminishes the claimed “elegance” of the method. The reliance on a single gradient step for the eigenvectors and the practical difficulties of properly implementing the Stiefel manifold update suggest the M-step is far from trivial. A more thorough discussion of the impact of these approximations would be beneficial.

Furthermore, the use of “relative log likelihood” as the primary evaluation metric is questionable. While mathematically convenient, ratios of log probabilities are harder to interpret than log-probability differences. Reporting absolute log-probability differences would provide a clearer understanding of the actual magnitude of the improvements.

The writing style is generally clear, but the repeated use of the adjective “elegant” to describe the algorithm feels unjustified given the practical complexities of the M-step. The bolding in the introduction is also unnecessary and distracting.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an interesting and promising paper. The EM framework offers a viable alternative to gradient-based kernel learning for DPPs, and the theoretical grounding is solid. The empirical results, while not overwhelming, demonstrate a benefit over naive gradient ascent. However, the M-step’s complexity and the lack of initialization analysis weaken the overall impact. The paper is well-written overall, despite minor stylistic quirks.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a challenging problem (DPP kernel learning).
*   Solid theoretical foundation based on DPP eigendecomposition.
*   Demonstrated empirical gains over a naive baseline.
*   Addresses limitations of existing methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Complexity of the M-step and reliance on approximations.
*   Lack of analysis of initialization effects (KA as a warm start).
*   Questionable choice of evaluation metric (relative log likelihood).
*   Stylistic issues (overuse of "elegant", unnecessary bolding).

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the initialization analysis, consider reporting log-probability differences, and refine the discussion around the M-step’s complexity. Removing the subjective adjective “elegant” would also improve the paper’s overall tone. Addressing these points would strengthen the paper’s contribution and make it a more compelling addition to the NIPS proceedings.
