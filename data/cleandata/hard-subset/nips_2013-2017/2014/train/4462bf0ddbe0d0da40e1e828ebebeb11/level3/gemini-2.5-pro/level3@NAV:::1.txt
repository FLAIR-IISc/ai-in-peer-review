This paper addresses the challenging non-convex problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from data. While prior work, such as Kulesza & Taskar (2010, 2011), has focused on more restricted convex learning settings, this work proposes a novel method for learning the unconstrained kernel. The main contribution is the reformulation of kernel learning within an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework. This is achieved by parameterizing the kernel by its eigendecomposition and leveraging the insight that any DPP can be seen as a mixture over elementary DPPs. The algorithm introduces a latent variable representing this mixture, where the E-step updates a variational posterior (a k-DPP) and the M-step performs gradient-based updates on the eigenvalues and eigenvectors.

The paper's primary strength lies in its originality. It presents a principled and creative approach to a difficult, long-standing problem in the DPP literature. The empirical results are promising, showing that the proposed EM algorithm substantially outperforms a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) when using a naive initialization, demonstrating greater robustness. The gains are more modest with a stronger, moment-matching initialization, but the approach remains competitive and is shown to be asymptotically faster, which is a significant practical advantage.

However, the work could be improved in several areas. While the EM formulation is appealing, its claimed elegance is somewhat undermined by the complexity of the M-step, which requires approximations and caveats, such as taking only a single gradient step for the eigenvectors. The experimental evaluation, though promising, feels incomplete. A key missing experiment is an analysis of initialization effects; for instance, does initializing the EM algorithm with the solution from the well-initialized KA baseline yield further improvements? This is critical for a complete comparison. Furthermore, the use of "relative log likelihood" (ratios of log probabilities) as a metric is unconventional; presenting log-probability differences would be more standard and interpretable. Minor stylistic issues include the self-characterization of the algorithm as "elegant" and unnecessary bolding in the introduction.

Overall, this is a strong paper introducing an interesting and valuable approach. Despite the modest empirical gains in some settings and the need for a more thorough experimental analysis, the method is a significant contribution to learning DPPs and is likely to inspire follow-up work. I recommend acceptance.
