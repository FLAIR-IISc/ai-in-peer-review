

This paper addresses a significant limitation in the learning of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), a model widely used for subset selection tasks requiring both quality and diversity. Previous work has focused on restricted learning settings due to the NP-hardness of learning the full kernel matrix L [1], either learning scalar weights for each row [2] or weights for linear combinations of DPPs with fixed kernels [3]. The authors propose a novel expectation-maximization (EM) approach that avoids the problematic projection steps required by gradient ascent methods, instead parameterizing the kernel in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors. This method directly addresses the key limitation in prior work—learning only partial information about L, which sacrifices the diversity dimension captured by off-diagonal elements.

The paper's technical contribution is strong. By leveraging the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K and applying Jensen's inequality to lower-bound the likelihood, the authors develop a principled EM algorithm that avoids the need for projection steps that often produce degenerate (nearly diagonal) solutions. The derivation is thorough and well-motivated, with careful attention to the mathematical details of the E-step and M-step updates. The authors correctly identify that the projection step in gradient ascent (K-Ascent) leads to poor local optima by forcing the kernel toward diagonal form, which fails to capture negative interactions between items essential for diversity. The paper provides an elegant solution by mapping the constraint to optimization on the Stiefel manifold, using a multiplicative update rule that preserves the orthogonality constraint without projection.

The experimental validation on a real-world product recommendation task (Amazon baby registries) demonstrates the practical value of the approach. The EM method achieves relative gains of up to 16.5% in test log-likelihood over the K-Ascent baseline, with the advantage being most pronounced in categories where items exhibit strong negative interactions (d > 0.025). The paper provides insightful analysis showing that K-Ascent's poor performance in these cases stems directly from its projection step. The runtime analysis further supports the practicality of the method, with EM being 2.1x faster on average than K-Ascent despite the theoretical asymptotic complexity advantage.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with the mathematical derivations presented in a logical sequence that builds toward the EM algorithm. The supplementary material provides necessary technical details for reproducibility. The authors appropriately cite relevant prior work, including key NIPS papers on DPPs [1, 2, 3, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20], and clearly differentiate their contribution from previous approaches.

However, the paper has a few limitations. The experimental validation, while strong for the product recommendation task, is limited to a single application domain. The authors acknowledge this by suggesting more experiments on diverse real-world datasets would better illustrate the practical importance of their method. The conclusion's claim that the method "will similarly have a significant advantage" in other applications is somewhat hasty without broader validation. Additionally, while the paper demonstrates improvements in log-likelihood, it does not directly measure how these improvements translate to downstream task performance (e.g., user engagement in recommendation systems), which would strengthen the significance argument.

The strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The technical contribution is novel and addresses a well-documented limitation in DPP learning. The method is both theoretically sound and practically effective, as demonstrated by the experiments. The paper advances the state of the art in DPP learning by providing a principled approach to learn the full kernel matrix without restrictive assumptions, which is a meaningful step beyond previous work that focused on partial learning.

Pro: Novel EM approach that avoids projection steps, preserving diversity modeling capabilities
Pro: Theoretical derivation is rigorous with clear connection to DPP properties
Pro: Strong experimental validation on a real-world dataset showing clear improvements
Pro: Well-written with clear explanations of the technical contributions
Con: Limited to a single application domain for experimental validation
Con: Claims about broader applicability could be better supported

This paper makes a solid contribution to the DPP literature and advances the state of the art in learning these models. It addresses a significant gap in the literature while providing a method that is both theoretically sound and practically effective. The work is well-positioned for NIPS as it demonstrates a meaningful improvement in a well-established model with clear theoretical insights and practical validation. The method's ability to preserve diversity while learning the full kernel matrix represents an important step forward in DPP applications. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limited experimental scope.
