Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles learning the full kernel of a determinantal point process (DPP), a model widely used for subset selection with diversity (document summarization, sensor placement, image search, recommendation). Rather than optimizing the entries of the marginal kernel K (or L), the authors reparameterize via the eigendecomposition K = V diag(λ) V^T and introduce a latent variable J indexing subsets of eigenvectors. Exploiting the interpretation of a DPP as a mixture over elementary DPPs, they apply Jensen’s inequality to derive a lower bound on the log-likelihood, leading to a generalized EM procedure:
- E-step: compute q(J | Y) = pK(J | Y), which is a k-DPP with a closed-form kernel constructed from V and λ. This step uses k-DPP marginalization.
- M-step: update eigenvalues in closed form via posterior marginals of q; update eigenvectors with a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold using a matrix exponential retraction.

They compare this EM approach to naïve projected gradient ascent on K with eigenvalue clipping to [0,1]. The paper argues projection can trap optimization near diagonal matrices, losing the negative correlations crucial for DPPs. Complexity is reduced from O(T1 n N^3 + T1 T2 N^3) for K-ascent to O(T1 n N k^2 + T1 T2 N^3) for EM, where k = max |Yi|. Experiments on Amazon baby registry data show consistent improvements in held-out log-likelihood and runtime versus the K-ascent baseline, especially when off-diagonal structure matters and under data scarcity.

This contributes beyond prior work that learned only per-item quality weights [2], mixtures with fixed kernels [3], or Bayesian parametric models [8], by proposing a method to learn an unconstrained kernel with preserved DPP structure.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The EM derivation via a latent subset J is well-motivated; the E-step identification of a conditional k-DPP and the closed-form eigenvalue update are elegant. The Stiefel manifold step is standard and appropriate for V. The complexity analysis is plausible and aligns with the implemented steps. The approach is effectively a generalized EM (single gradient step for V) with monotonicity enforced via step-size search on the true likelihood.
- Empirical evaluation: Results support the main claims (robustness to initialization, less prone to degeneracy, faster), but the scope is narrow. Only one real dataset from a single domain and only one baseline (K-ascent) are used. The evaluation uses held-out log-likelihood; no task-level metrics (e.g., recommendation relevance/diversity) are reported.
- Concerns/questions:
  - Initialization Eq. (23): The choice Kij = sqrt(max(Kii Kjj − mij, 0)) is nonstandard. Why not use the empirical covariance (with shrinkage) or a low-rank similarity estimate? Please justify this mapping and its effect on PSD-ness.
  - Baseline strength: Could K-ascent be strengthened via alternative parameterizations (e.g., K = B (B^T B + I)−1 B^T, low-rank factorization with bounded spectrum, or log-det barriers) to reduce projection-induced degeneracy?
  - Regime where k is large: When k ≈ N the purported runtime advantage diminishes; please report performance in such regimes or clarify applicability.
  - Convergence details: Please state stopping criteria, handling of λ near 1 (practical epsilon), and any safeguards for numerical stability in Eq. (21).
  - Evaluation metrics: For recommendation, report MAP or greedy MAP subset quality, hit/diversity metrics, or user-meaningful proxies alongside likelihood.

Clarity
- The core idea is strong but the exposition is hard to follow in places. Specific suggestions:
  - Define all notation before first use. In a few places K, V, or IY appear before being introduced.
  - Tighten the narrative: present the modeling background, then the latent-variable interpretation and lower bound, then the E- and M-step derivations, followed by complexity. Avoid interleaving side remarks that interrupt the main thread.
  - Be precise in language. For example, instead of “minimizes the change to Frobenius norm,” say “projects to the closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix to Q.”
  - The manifold optimization subsection currently defers to [26]; please provide a self-contained description of the chosen retraction-based update (which you already mostly do in Eq. (21)) and explain why a single first-order step per M-step suffices in practice.
  - Clarify ambiguous terms: e.g., “weight” should be consistently defined (mixture weight in Eq. (7) vs. per-row weights in prior work).
  - Ensure all symbols in Lemma 1 and Eqs. (16–21) are defined at first appearance (e.g., U = V^T, BYi).

Originality
- High. Using a latent subset over eigenvectors to derive a tractable generalized EM with closed-form eigenvalue updates for full-kernel DPP learning is, to my knowledge, novel relative to prior DPP learning approaches that either restrict parameterization or forgo a true DPP. The k-DPP perspective in the E-step is particularly insightful.

Significance
- Potentially high for learning DPPs, since full-kernel learning has been a bottleneck. Preserving the DPP structure maintains attractive inference properties. However, the demonstrated impact is limited by the experimental breadth. Stronger baselines (mixture-of-DPPs [3], Bayesian learning [8], submodular alternatives), more datasets (e.g., image search, summarization), and task-oriented evaluation would strengthen the case for adoption.

Pros
- Conceptually elegant EM via eigendecomposition and latent subsets; closed-form eigenvalue updates.
- Addresses known pitfalls of projected gradient on K and offers a plausible runtime advantage.
- Clear empirical wins over a standard baseline; robustness to initialization; code provided.

Cons
- Experimental scope and baselines are limited; no task-level utility evaluation.
- Exposition is sloppy in places; notation and structure hinder readability.
- Some design choices (initialization Eq. (23)) are ad hoc and insufficiently justified.

Questions for the authors
- Can you compare against (i) per-row weight learning [2], (ii) mixtures of fixed kernels [3], and (iii) a simple submodular baseline (e.g., facility location with learned weights) in terms of likelihood and MAP quality?
- How sensitive is performance to taking only one V-update per M-step? Do multiple steps materially help?
- Could you provide ablations: eigenvalues-only vs. full EM; effect of step-size search; different initializations (covariance-based, low-rank)?
- What happens when the cardinalities |Yi| are large (k close to N)?

Minor comments/typos
- Replace vague phrasing as noted; ensure consistent equation numbering.
- Explicitly state that the method is a generalized EM and clarify monotonicity guarantees.
- Clarify the meaning of “weight” when first introduced.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a technically solid and original approach to a challenging and relevant problem in DPP learning, with clear improvements over a reasonable baseline. With clearer exposition and stronger experimental comparisons, it could have substantial impact.
