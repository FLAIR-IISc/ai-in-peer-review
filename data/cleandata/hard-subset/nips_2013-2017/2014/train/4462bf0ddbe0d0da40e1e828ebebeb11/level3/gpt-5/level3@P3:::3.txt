Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles maximum-likelihood learning for determinantal point processes (DPPs), models that assign higher probability to diverse subsets and are parameterized by a positive semidefinite (PSD) kernel L (or equivalently the marginal kernel K). While DPP inference is tractable, learning L is non-convex and conjectured NP-hard, so prior work has restricted the parameterization: convex learning of per-item quality weights (scalars per row/diagonal) [Kulesza & Taskar], mixtures or linear combinations of fixed kernels [Lin & Bilmes], or Bayesian learning with strong parametric assumptions [Affandi et al.]. These approaches either only learn quality and not inter-item similarity, or restrict the diversity structure.

The paper proposes an EM-style algorithm that learns the full kernel without imposing a particular parametric form and avoids the problematic PSD/eigenvalue projection that plagues naïve projected gradient ascent (KA). The key idea is to reparameterize K via its eigendecomposition K = V diag(λ)V^T, introduce the hidden index set J of selected eigenvectors from the standard DPP sampling view, and apply Jensen’s inequality to derive a tractable lower bound. The E-step yields q(J|Y), which the paper shows is a k-DPP with a closed-form kernel; the M-step updates eigenvalues in closed form using k-DPP marginals and updates eigenvectors by gradient ascent on the Stiefel manifold using a multiplicative (matrix-exponential) retraction that exploits full-rank V and obviates eigenvector projection. This addresses the degeneracy observed with KA: projections that zero or cap eigenvalues tend to produce near-diagonal K’s, degrading learned diversity.

Experiments on synthetic data (in the supplement) and a large-scale product recommendation task (Amazon baby registries) show that the method improves test log-likelihood and preserves both quality and diversity compared to KA, with up to 16.5% relative gain in low-data settings and 2.1× runtime speedups. Qualitative analyses show that KA tends to overemphasize item quality and under-penalize similarity (e.g., recommending multiple baby monitors), while the EM learner better maintains diversity.

Strengths
- Technical novelty: A clean EM formulation in the eigenbasis with a well-motivated hidden variable. The Jensen bound is used appropriately to derive updates.
- Solid algorithmic contributions: closed-form eigenvalue updates via k-DPP marginals; mapping eigenvector updates to optimization over the Stiefel manifold using a multiplicative update that avoids projection; exploiting full-rank V reduces overhead.
- Insightful derivation: step-by-step EM development (E-step as a k-DPP, efficient marginals, complexity analysis O(nN k^2) per outer iteration), with care for practical issues (λ < 1, step-size selection).
- Empirical evidence: On product recommendation, EM is more robust to initialization and data scarcity, and better preserves diversity. The safety category example is compelling: KA replaces two items in the top-10 with additional monitors, whereas EM maintains variety.
- Clarity and coherence: The paper and proofs are generally well written; the connection to sampling, conditional DPPs, and manifold optimization is explained clearly.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Limited baselines: The main comparison is to KA. Including the convex per-item-weight learner and mixture-of-kernels baselines would strengthen the empirical case, even if they sacrifice DPP properties.
- EM monotonicity: Because step-size is chosen by checking the true likelihood and an E-step is rerun for each trial, the traditional EM monotonicity on the bound is not guaranteed nor analyzed. A brief discussion of convergence properties (e.g., monotone increase of L under the implemented updates) would help.
- Real-world breadth: Results center on one application domain. Additional real-world datasets (e.g., document/image summarization, sensor placement) and user-centric diversity metrics would better illustrate practical gains beyond likelihood.
- Conclusion: Slightly terse; it omits some contributions (e.g., conditional k-DPP characterization for q(J|Y) and the Stiefel-manifold perspective).

Quality
The paper is technically sound, with careful derivations, correct use of k-DPP marginals, and appropriate complexity analysis. The observed failure mode of KA due to projection is convincingly diagnosed and addressed. Claims are supported by experiments; however, broader baselines and datasets would further solidify significance.

Clarity
Well organized and clearly written. The EM derivation is accessible and detailed. Minor suggestions: summarize the Stiefel update intuition inline and provide a short convergence discussion in the main text.

Originality
The approach of learning an unconstrained DPP kernel by EM over eigenvalues/eigenvectors, leveraging a conditional k-DPP E-step and Stiefel manifold updates, is novel relative to prior DPP learning work that restricted parameterization. Showing that the conditional posterior over eigen-index sets is itself a DPP variant is a neat and useful observation.

Significance
Learning unrestricted DPP kernels is important for applications where diversity matters. Avoiding projection-induced degeneracy makes this practically impactful. The method should be of interest to researchers building diverse subset selection systems; the algorithmic ideas (E-step as k-DPP; Stiefel updates) are likely to be reused.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a long-standing challenge: learning full DPP kernels without restrictive parameterizations.
- Strong, well-motivated algorithmic design that avoids harmful projections while maintaining PSD/eigenvalue constraints.
- Clear technical contributions (Jensen-based EM, k-DPP E-step, Stiefel-manifold M-step).
- Empirical gains in likelihood, diversity preservation, and runtime; robust to initialization and data scarcity.
- High clarity and reproducibility.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation relies primarily on KA; lacks comparisons to other learning paradigms (e.g., diagonal scaling, mixtures).
- Limited diversity of real-world datasets; more evidence would bolster generality.
- Convergence properties of the practical EM variant are not analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines: convex diagonal scaling, mixture-of-fixed L, and parametric Bayesian approaches where applicable.
- Broaden experiments to established DPP tasks and include diversity-oriented metrics beyond likelihood.
- Provide an ablation on step-size/E-step coupling and a brief theoretical discussion of convergence guarantees or observed behavior.
- Expand the conclusion to recap all contributions and limitations.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper provides a principled, well-executed method for learning unconstrained DPPs, with clear technical insights and demonstrated benefits in retaining both quality and diversity over prior methods. With stronger baselines and broader empirical scope, it would be an unequivocal accept.
