- Proposes a novel EM algorithm for learning DPP kernels by re-parameterizing via eigenvalues and eigenvectors to avoid NP-hard direct likelihood maximization.  
- Uses Jensen’s inequality to derive a lower bound and applies coordinate ascent for effective optimization.  
- Builds on extensive DPP literature and frames the method as advancing full-kernel learning beyond prior constrained parameterizations.  
- Technical novelty: eigendecomposition-based approach is clever and well-motivated.  
- The EM derivation is theoretically grounded and connects to mixture-of-elementary-DPPs theory.  
- Empirically outperforms a projected gradient baseline (K-Ascent) on a product recommendation task.  
- Performance gains are more pronounced when modeling strong negative interactions between items.  
- Asymptotic runtime analysis suggests potential efficiency advantages for the EM method.  
- Major clarity and presentation problems: convoluted writing and interleaved derivations hinder readability.  
- Notation is sometimes ambiguous (symbols used before definition) and several phrases are unclear or imprecise.  
- Important details are deferred to external references, making the paper hard to follow independently.  
- Some terms (e.g., “weight”) and implementation choices lack clear definition or justification.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited and omits comparisons to other relevant subset-selection methods.  
- Comparison only to K-Ascent does not convincingly show advantages over simpler alternative methods.  
- Initialization choices (e.g., not using m_ij) and the effectiveness of moment-matching vs other strategies are underexplored.  
- Overall quality: technically sound with robust theory but weakened by limited experiments and clarity issues.  
- Clarity rating: poor — needs major improvements in organization, notation, and phrasing.  
- Originality: high — the eigendecomposition + EM approach is a novel contribution.  
- Significance: potentially important for diverse subset selection but impact is limited by lack of comparative validation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel and sound method, empirical improvement over a baseline, strong theory, and potential application impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: poor presentation, limited experiments and comparisons, ambiguous statements, and inconsistent notation.  
- Overall recommendation: promising work but recommend rejection now with encouragement to resubmit after substantial revisions to clarity and expanded experimental comparisons.
