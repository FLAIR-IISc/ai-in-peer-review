## Review of “Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes”

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), a task known to be NP-hard. The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework as an alternative to direct gradient-based kernel learning (KA). The core idea is to reparameterize the kernel in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors, allowing for a more tractable optimization procedure by exploiting the DPP’s decomposition into elementary DPPs. 

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing approaches – primarily convex relaxations or fixed kernel parameterizations – and positioning the EM method as a more general solution. The method itself is logically presented, with a clear breakdown of the E-step (using a k-DPP approximation) and the M-step. The connection to existing DPP theory, particularly the eigendecomposition and marginal kernel representation, is effectively leveraged. 

The empirical evaluation on a product recommendation task demonstrates a substantial improvement over naive KA initialization, and a modest gain even with more informed initialization. This provides initial evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. However, the M-step, while theoretically sound, appears complex and relies on approximations, somewhat diminishing the claimed “elegance” of the method. The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed discussion of the impact of these approximations would be beneficial.

A significant weakness is the lack of analysis regarding initialization effects. Specifically, investigating whether initializing the EM algorithm with solutions obtained from KA could accelerate convergence or improve performance would be valuable. Furthermore, the use of “relative log likelihood” as the primary evaluation metric is somewhat opaque. Reporting log-probability *differences* would facilitate easier interpretation and comparison. 

The writing style, while generally clear, suffers from occasional unnecessary embellishments (e.g., calling the algorithm “elegant” and the bolding in the introduction). These stylistic choices detract from the paper’s overall professionalism.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a challenging problem.
*   Sound theoretical foundation and clear presentation.
*   Demonstrated empirical gains over a baseline method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Complexity of the M-step and reliance on approximations.
*   Missing analysis of initialization effects.
*   Unclear metric choice (relative log likelihood).
*   Minor stylistic issues.

**Overall:** This is a promising and interesting paper with modest empirical gains. While the experiments could be improved, and the method’s complexity acknowledged more thoroughly, the contribution is valuable and warrants acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
