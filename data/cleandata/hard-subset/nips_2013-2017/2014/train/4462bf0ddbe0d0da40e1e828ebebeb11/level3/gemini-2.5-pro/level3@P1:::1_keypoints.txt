- Problem & contribution: Proposes a novel EM-style algorithm that learns the full, unconstrained DPP kernel by reparameterizing via eigendecomposition and treating eigenvector selection as a latent variable.

- Key insight: Viewing a DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs over eigenvectors makes the E-step tractable and enables closed-form eigenvalue updates in the M-step.

- Empirical claim: EM is faster, more robust to naive initialization, and outperforms projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) on a real-world product recommendation task.

- Strength — originality/significance: Learning the full kernel is a significant advance over prior restricted parameterizations and avoids problematic projection steps that can collapse diversity.

- Strength — technical quality: The derivation is sound; the E-step posterior is a k-DPP and eigenvalue updates are elegant and closed-form.

- Strength — empirical demonstration: Experiments on the baby registry dataset show EM avoids near-diagonal solutions, correlates gains with off-diagonal importance, and is computationally faster.

- Weakness — M-step complexity: The M-step does not fully maximize the objective; eigenvectors are updated by a single gradient step and require expensive E-steps during line search, relying on heuristics.

- Weakness — experimental incompleteness: Lacks experiments to disentangle whether EM finds better optima or simply follows a better trajectory (e.g., initializing EM from K-Ascent solutions).

- Weakness — presentation/metrics: Reporting ratios of log-likelihoods is confusing; absolute or normalized differences would be clearer; some stylistic distractions in writing.

- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses an important, difficult problem with a principled, novel algorithm that is robust and computationally favorable; well-written with sound derivations.

- Arguments against acceptance: The approximate/heuristic M-step weakens the EM framing, empirical gains can be modest with good initialization, and experimental/presentation gaps remain.

- Overall assessment: Strong, valuable contribution with practical impact; recommend acceptance despite M-step and experimental limitations.
