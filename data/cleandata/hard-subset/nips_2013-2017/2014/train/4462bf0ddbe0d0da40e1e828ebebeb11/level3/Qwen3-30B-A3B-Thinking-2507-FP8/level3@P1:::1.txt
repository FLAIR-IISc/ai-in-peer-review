

This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework for learning the full kernel matrix of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a key limitation in prior work that only learned restricted forms of the kernel. The authors note that while DPPs have gained popularity for modeling set diversity in applications like product recommendation, document summarization, and sensor placement, learning the full kernel matrix has been challenging due to the non-convexity of the log-likelihood objective and the NP-hard conjecture for this problem. Previous approaches either learned only scalar weights per row [2] or linear combinations of fixed DPPs [3], sacrificing the DPP's attractive computational properties.

The main contribution is a novel EM-based learning algorithm that reparameterizes the kernel using eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The key insight is recognizing that a DPP can be viewed as a mixture over elementary DPPs (with eigenvalues set to 1), where the hidden variable J represents the subset of eigenvectors used to generate a sample. The authors derive an EM algorithm that alternates between an E-step (computing a k-DPP distribution over subsets) and an M-step (updating eigenvalues via closed-form expressions and eigenvectors via gradient steps). This approach avoids the problematic projection steps required by gradient ascent methods (K-Ascent), which often lead to degenerate diagonal kernels that fail to capture item diversity.

The paper makes several strong contributions. Theoretical insights about the eigendecomposition of DPPs are well-explained and extend prior work [1, 21]. The authors provide a clear derivation of the EM algorithm, including the key Lemma 1 that connects the E-step to k-DPPs. The empirical evaluation on 13 product recommendation categories from Amazon baby registries demonstrates that the proposed method achieves substantial improvements (up to 16.5% relative gain in test log-likelihood) over K-Ascent with naive Wishart initialization. The analysis showing that EM's advantage correlates with the strength of negative item interactions (d > 0.025) is particularly compelling. The paper also establishes that EM is asymptotically faster than K-Ascent, with empirical runtime improvements (2.1x average speedup) that validate this claim.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that merit consideration. The M-step is surprisingly involved, requiring multiple approximations and caveats (e.g., the need to handle eigenvalues near 1, the use of a single gradient step instead of full optimization, and the complex Stiefel manifold update for eigenvectors). This complicates the claimed elegance of the approach. The authors acknowledge these approximations but could have more thoroughly addressed their impact on convergence guarantees or theoretical properties. The empirical analysis would also benefit from a deeper investigation of initialization effects—particularly whether initializing EM from K-Ascent solutions yields further improvements, which the authors note is a promising direction but do not explore. The use of "relative log likelihood" (ratios of log probabilities) as the primary metric is questionable; reporting absolute log-probability differences would make the improvements more interpretable for readers.

The paper is well-written overall, though the authors' repeated use of the word "elegant" in the introduction feels unnecessary and slightly overconfident. The writing is clear and well-organized, with sufficient detail to reproduce the experiments. The related work section appropriately cites key NIPS papers on DPPs [8, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 19, 2, 3, 20], though it could better distinguish how this work differs from recent advances in DPP learning [e.g., Affandi et al., ICML 2013].

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a meaningful gap in DPP learning that has been noted in prior work [1]. The method has clear practical value for applications requiring set diversity, such as the product recommendation task demonstrated. While the empirical gains are modest in some settings (e.g., 0.4% average gain with moment-matching initialization), the consistency of the improvements across categories and the theoretical grounding make this a valuable contribution. The paper advances the state of the art in DPP learning by enabling unconstrained kernel learning without sacrificing computational efficiency.

The quality of the work is strong, with careful theoretical development and thorough empirical validation. The paper is technically sound, with claims well-supported by both analysis and experiments. The authors are appropriately honest about the limitations of their approach and the need for approximations.

Pro: Novel EM framework for unconstrained DPP kernel learning; clear theoretical derivation; substantial empirical gains with naive initialization; good runtime analysis; well-motivated real-world application.

Con: M-step involves multiple approximations that weaken the elegance claim; lacks analysis of initialization effects; questionable use of relative log likelihood metric; some writing style choices (e.g., "elegant" claims) are unnecessary.

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to DPP learning with solid theoretical insights and practical results. While the empirical gains are modest in some settings, the method's robustness to initialization and avoidance of degenerate solutions are significant advantages. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the initialization analysis and metric concerns, but as is, it represents a valuable advance in the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the metric issue and clarify the M-step's limitations.
