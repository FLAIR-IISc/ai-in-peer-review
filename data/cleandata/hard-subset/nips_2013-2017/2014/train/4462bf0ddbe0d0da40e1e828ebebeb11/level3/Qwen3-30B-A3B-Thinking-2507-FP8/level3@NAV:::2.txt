

This paper proposes an EM algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a significant limitation in prior work that focused on restricted learning settings. The authors cleverly exploit the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K and develop a Jensen lower bound to formulate an EM-style optimization procedure, avoiding the problematic projection steps required by naive gradient ascent (K-Ascent). The method is technically sound, building on DPP theory while introducing novel elements like Stiefel-manifold optimization for eigenvector updates and leveraging mixtures of elementary DPPs.

The paper's main contribution is original and meaningful, as it enables full kernel learning without restrictive parametric assumptions, which is a significant advancement over prior work (Kulesza & Taskar, UAI 2011; Affandi et al., ICML 2013). The theoretical development is well-founded, and the algorithm achieves up to 16.5% relative gains in test log-likelihood compared to K-Ascent on a real-world product recommendation task.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues that undermine its impact. The write-up is poorly organized, with method derivation interleaved with side remarks that disrupt the main narrative. Critical notation (K, V) is used before definition, and ambiguous terms like "weight" (p.4 line 189) require clarification. The phrasing "minimises the change to Frobenius norm" (p.3) is unclear and should be rephrased as "closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix to Q." Section referencing [26] forces readers to consult external work rather than providing a self-contained explanation.

Most critically, the experimental evaluation is limited in scope. The paper compares only to K-Ascent (the naive baseline), omitting comparisons to alternative subset-selection methods mentioned in the introduction (e.g., k-DPPs, DPP ensembles). This omission makes it unclear whether the DPP approach offers practical advantages over established methods. The rationale for excluding m_{ij} in equation (30) and the choice of initialization (not using empirical covariance) also lacks justification.

While the method is technically solid and original, the paper requires substantial improvements in exposition and experimental validation to be considered for publication. The authors should restructure the paper to present the method clearly first, define all notation upfront, and include comprehensive comparisons to relevant subset selection approaches.
