This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP). DPPs are powerful models for capturing diversity, parameterized by a positive semi-definite (PSD) matrix L. As the authors correctly note, learning the full matrix L via maximum likelihood is NP-hard. Consequently, prior work has focused on restricted, convex learning settings, such as learning only scalar weights or fixed linear combinations of kernels. This paper makes a significant contribution by proposing a novel expectation-maximization (EM) style algorithm that learns the full, unconstrained kernel by parameterizing it via its eigenvalues and eigenvectors. This approach cleverly avoids the problematic projection step required by standard projected gradient ascent, which can prematurely collapse the kernel's eigenvalues and thus harm its ability to model diversity.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The proposed method is a novel and important step towards fully learning DPPs. By moving beyond restricted parameterizations, it unlocks the model's full potential to jointly learn item quality and similarity, advancing the state of the art. The approach of using an EM algorithm on the eigendecomposition is both elegant and effective.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the EM procedure, using Jensen’s inequality to lower-bound the objective, is insightful and clearly explained. Further solid contributions include mapping the eigenvector update to an optimization problem on the Stiefel manifold and proving that the intermediate conditional distribution is also a DPP. The empirical evaluation on a product recommendation task effectively supports the main claim that the EM method preserves diversity better than the projected gradient ascent baseline, especially in data-poor settings or with uninformative initializations.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, coherent, and organized. The technical details are presented with sufficient clarity for an expert to understand the derivations.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are well-executed, their scope could be broadened. The practical impact of the method would be more convincingly demonstrated with evaluations on additional, larger-scale real-world datasets from other domains where DPPs are applied, such as document summarization or image search.
*   **Conclusion:** The conclusion feels somewhat hasty and undersells the paper's contributions. It could be strengthened by reiterating some of the insightful technical details, such as the properties of the derived auxiliary distribution.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with strong technical contributions that addresses a key limitation in the literature on learning DPPs. The proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated.
