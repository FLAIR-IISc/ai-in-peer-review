### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the challenging non-convex problem of learning the full kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. While DPPs are powerful models for capturing diversity in subset selection tasks, learning their parameters via maximum likelihood has been conjectured to be NP-hard. Previous work has circumvented this by focusing on more restricted, convex learning settings, such as learning weights for each item or for a linear combination of fixed kernels.

The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to tackle the full learning problem. The key idea is to re-parameterize the DPP's marginal kernel matrix `K` in terms of its eigendecomposition (`K = VΛVᵀ`). The algorithm treats the selection of a subset of eigenvectors as a latent variable, leveraging the view of a DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs. By applying Jensen's inequality, the authors derive a tractable lower bound on the log-likelihood. The optimization then proceeds via coordinate ascent: the E-step involves computing the posterior over the latent eigenvector selections, while the M-step involves updating the eigenvalues (in closed form) and the eigenvectors. The update for the eigenvectors is handled elegantly as an optimization problem on a Stiefel manifold to preserve their orthogonality.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the EM algorithm, including the use of a latent variable representation, the Jensen's inequality bound, and the optimization on the Stiefel manifold, appears correct and is a well-founded approach to this difficult optimization problem.

However, the experimental validation is a significant weakness. The authors compare their EM algorithm against a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) on a product recommendation dataset. While the results demonstrate that EM is faster and more robust (especially with poor initializations or limited data), this comparison is internal and only shows that EM is a better optimization strategy than a simple alternative for the DPP log-likelihood objective. The experiments fail to demonstrate the broader utility of learning a full DPP kernel. The paper would be much stronger if it included comparisons to other state-of-the-art subset selection or recommendation methods that are not based on DPPs. Without such context, it is difficult to assess whether the learned DPP model offers a practical advantage over other approaches.

Additionally, the justification for the moment-matching initialization in Eq. (23) is unclear. Why are the off-diagonal terms `K̃ij` computed using `sqrt(max(K̃ii*K̃jj - m_ij, 0))` rather than a more direct use of the empirical pairwise frequency `m_ij`? An explanation of this choice, perhaps in relation to ensuring positive semi-definiteness, or a comparison to initializing with the empirical covariance matrix, would strengthen the paper.

**Clarity**

The clarity of the manuscript is its most significant weakness and presents a major barrier to comprehension. The organization and writing are confusing, making the novel technical contributions difficult to follow.

1.  **Structure and Flow:** The derivation of the algorithm in Section 3 is convoluted. The main steps are interspersed with side remarks, justifications, and runtime analyses, which disrupts the logical flow. It would be far clearer to first present the complete EM algorithm (Algorithm 2) and then dedicate separate, clearly delineated subsections to the derivation of the E-step, the M-step for eigenvalues, and the M-step for eigenvectors.

2.  **Undefined Notation:** Several key variables are used before they are formally defined. For instance, the marginal kernel `K` is used in the log-likelihood objective (Eq. 4) but is only properly defined in relation to `L` at the end of Section 2. The eigenvector matrix `V` appears in equations (e.g., in the text describing sampling) before its role in the eigendecomposition `K = VΛVᵀ` is stated.

3.  **Lack of Self-Containment:** The explanation for the eigenvector update is difficult to follow. The authors refer to a general algorithm for optimization on Stiefel manifolds from [26] and then state they use a simplified version (Eq. 21) without sufficient explanation. A brief, intuitive derivation of this update step would greatly improve the paper's accessibility.

4.  **Vague Phrasing:** The manuscript contains imprecise language. For instance, the description of the projection step in Algorithm 1 should be clarified from "chooses the closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix" directly in the text, rather than relying on a vague statement and a citation.

**Originality**

The paper is highly original. To my knowledge, this is the first work to propose a practical algorithm for learning the full, unconstrained kernel matrix of a DPP. The approach of re-parameterizing via the eigendecomposition and formulating the problem within an EM framework is novel and creative. It represents a significant departure from previous, more restrictive learning formulations and is a valuable contribution to the literature on DPPs.

**Significance**

The work is potentially very significant. The ability to learn a DPP's parameters directly from data is crucial for its widespread adoption in machine learning applications. By providing an effective algorithm for this task, this paper could enable the use of DPPs in many domains where modeling diversity is important, such as recommendation, summarization, and search. However, the current weaknesses in the experimental evaluation and clarity of presentation limit its immediate impact. If the practical benefits of the learned DPPs were demonstrated more convincingly against other methods, the significance would be undeniable.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, difficult, and well-motivated problem: learning the full kernel for a DPP.
*   Proposes a novel and technically sophisticated EM algorithm that is well-suited to the problem's structure.
*   The work represents a clear conceptual advance over prior, more constrained methods for learning DPPs.
*   The experiments, though limited, show the proposed algorithm is superior to a natural baseline (projected gradient ascent).

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The paper is poorly written and organized, making the technical contributions extremely difficult to understand and verify.
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient. It lacks comparisons to alternative non-DPP subset selection methods, making it hard to judge the practical value of the learned model.
*   Key methodological choices (e.g., the moment-matching initialization) are not adequately justified.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a novel and technically impressive EM algorithm for learning the full kernel of a DPP. The core idea is strong and represents a significant contribution to the field. However, the manuscript is severely hampered by poor clarity and an experimental section that fails to demonstrate the practical advantages of the learned model over competing methods. While the technical contribution is valuable, the paper in its current form is not ready for publication. I would be enthusiastic about a revised version that substantially improves the clarity of the exposition and includes a more comprehensive experimental evaluation.
