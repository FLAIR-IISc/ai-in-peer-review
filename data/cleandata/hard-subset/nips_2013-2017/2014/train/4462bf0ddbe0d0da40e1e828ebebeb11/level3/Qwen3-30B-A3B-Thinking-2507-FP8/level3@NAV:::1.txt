

This paper proposes an expectation-maximization (EM) framework for learning the full kernel matrix in Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a key limitation of previous work that focused on restricted learning settings. The authors leverage the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K to reformulate the learning problem, representing DPPs as mixtures over elementary DPPs. The EM approach alternates between an E-step (computing a k-DPP variational distribution) and an M-step (updating eigenvalues via closed-form and eigenvectors via single gradient steps), avoiding the problematic projection steps required by naive gradient ascent (KA).

The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing a principled alternative to direct kernel parameter learning, which often leads to degenerate solutions. Empirical results on a product recommendation task demonstrate that EM substantially outperforms KA with naive Wishart initialization (average 3.7% relative gain), with even larger improvements (up to 16.5%) in data-poor settings. The authors correctly identify that KA's projection step frequently produces nearly diagonal kernels that fail to capture item diversity, a key advantage of DPPs.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The M-step is notably complex, requiring approximations and caveats (e.g., handling λj = 1, Stiefel manifold updates) that undermine the claimed elegance of the method. The analysis of initialization effects is incomplete—particularly whether initializing EM from KA solutions would yield further improvements. The use of "relative log likelihood" (ratios of log probabilities) is questionable; reporting absolute log-probability differences would facilitate clearer interpretation. The writing contains stylistic issues, including unsubstantiated claims of "elegance" and unnecessary bolding in the introduction.

Strengths include a technically sound approach, clear exposition of the DPP background, and meaningful empirical results demonstrating the method's robustness. Weaknesses involve the M-step complexity, missing initialization analysis, and questionable metric choice. The work is original in its EM reformulation of DPP kernel learning, though it builds on established DPP theory. The significance is modest but meaningful for applications requiring high-quality diverse subsets.

Pro: Novel EM framework, strong empirical demonstration of KA's limitations, clear theoretical foundation. Con: Overstated elegance claims, M-step complexity, incomplete initialization analysis, questionable metric choice. Overall, this is a promising contribution with solid empirical results, though the method's practical implementation challenges and missing analyses slightly temper its impact. The paper is acceptable for NIPS with minor revisions addressing the metric and initialization concerns.
