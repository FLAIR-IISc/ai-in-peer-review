This paper addresses the challenging non-convex problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. Previous work has tackled this problem by either learning a restricted, convex version of the problem (e.g., learning only a single weight per item) or by learning weights for a mixture of DPPs with fixed kernels. In contrast, this work proposes a novel algorithm to learn the full, unconstrained kernel matrix. The key idea is to reparameterize the kernel via its eigendecomposition and view the DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs defined by subsets of these eigenvectors. This perspective allows the authors to introduce the selection of eigenvectors as a latent variable, leading to an Expectation-Maximization (EM) style learning algorithm. The proposed EM algorithm iteratively updates a variational distribution over the latent variables (E-step) and then updates the kernel's eigenvalues and eigenvectors (M-step). The authors compare their method to a baseline of projected gradient ascent on the kernel matrix entries (K-Ascent) on a real-world product recommendation task. The results show that the EM algorithm is faster and more robust, outperforming the baseline significantly when a naive initialization is used, and still showing modest gains in data-poor settings even with a more informed initialization.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel and clever approach to a fundamentally important problem in the application of DPPs. Learning the full kernel allows DPPs to model complex diversity and quality trade-offs, a task for which prior methods are insufficient. Framing the problem in an EM framework by treating eigenvector selection as a latent variable is a creative and insightful contribution. This method provides a principled alternative to projected gradient ascent, and the paper makes a convincing case that the projection step in the baseline method can be problematic, leading to poor local optima (e.g., near-diagonal kernels that fail to capture diversity). The proposed algorithm could be highly valuable for practitioners who wish to apply DPPs without relying on restrictive kernel parameterizations.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development of the algorithm is sound. The derivation of the EM lower bound is standard, but the insight that the posterior distribution `p(J | Y_i)` in the E-step is itself a k-DPP is a key technical contribution that makes the E-step tractable. The closed-form update for the eigenvalues in the M-step is also an elegant result.

3.  **Empirical Demonstration:** The experiments on the baby registry dataset clearly demonstrate the weaknesses of the K-Ascent baseline, particularly its sensitivity to initialization. When started from a Wishart-initialized kernel, the proposed EM algorithm shows substantial improvements. The analysis correlating EM's gains with a measure of off-diagonal importance (`d`) provides a compelling explanation for *why* EM's ability to avoid collapsing to a near-diagonal solution is crucial. The paper also demonstrates that EM is asymptotically and practically faster than the baseline.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Complexity of the M-step:** While the EM framework is appealing, the M-step is not as clean as the presentation might suggest. It does not perform a full maximization of the objective. The update for the eigenvectors `V` consists of only a single gradient step, and the objective function for this step is itself intractable. The authors' solution—performing an E-step for each trial step size during the line search—is a practical workaround but adds complexity and moves the algorithm away from a conventional EM procedure. This reliance on approximations and heuristics in the M-step somewhat detracts from the overall elegance of the proposed framework.

2.  **Incomplete Experimental Analysis:** The experiments could be strengthened. A key question left unanswered is how the two algorithms interact. For instance, does the EM algorithm find a better local optimum, or does it simply have a better optimization trajectory from certain initializations? A valuable experiment would be to initialize the EM algorithm with the converged solution from K-Ascent (especially the moments-matching version). If EM can still make significant progress, it would provide much stronger evidence that it is finding qualitatively better solutions. Without this, it's hard to disentangle the benefits of the optimization procedure itself from its robustness to initialization.

3.  **Clarity of Metrics and Presentation:** The reporting of "relative test log-likelihood differences" in Figure 1 is confusing. Log-likelihoods are logarithmic quantities, so taking their ratio is not standard and makes the magnitude of the improvement difficult to interpret. It would be much clearer to report the absolute difference in average test log-likelihood (`LL_EM - LL_KA`) or a normalized difference like `(LL_EM - LL_KA) / |LL_KA|`. Minor stylistic points: the use of bolding in the introduction is distracting, and self-characterizations of the algorithm as "elegant" could be removed to let the work speak for itself.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   Addresses the important and difficult problem of learning an unconstrained DPP kernel.
    *   The proposed EM-style algorithm is novel, principled, and avoids the problematic projection step inherent in naive gradient-based methods.
    *   The method is shown to be more robust to initialization and computationally faster than the projected gradient ascent baseline.
    *   The paper is well-written and the technical derivations are sound.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    *   The M-step is more complex and approximate than is typical for an EM algorithm, which slightly undermines the conceptual framing.
    *   The empirical gains over a well-initialized baseline are modest, and the experimental analysis could be more thorough to better isolate the benefits of the algorithm.
    *   The presentation of results could be clearer.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution. The novel formulation of DPP kernel learning as an EM problem is a significant conceptual advance. While the M-step has some practical complexities and the empirical gains are not always dramatic, the work successfully identifies a key failure mode of a natural baseline and provides a more robust and efficient alternative. The paper is well-executed and addresses a problem of high interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
