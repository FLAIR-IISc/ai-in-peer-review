This paper presents a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a known NP-hardness in directly maximizing the log-likelihood of the kernel matrix *L*. Prior work has circumvented this by learning restricted parameterizations of *L*, sacrificing the ability to fully model diversity. This paper’s key contribution is a method that learns *L* without such restrictions, leveraging an eigendecomposition and Jensen's inequality to formulate an EM procedure that avoids problematic projection steps inherent in gradient ascent.

The work builds upon a strong foundation of prior DPP research, citing key papers from Kulesza & Taskar (UAI’09, ICML’12) establishing DPPs for subset selection and their computational properties. It acknowledges the limitations of scalar weighting approaches [2] and L-ensemble methods [3], positioning its contribution as a more general and effective learning framework. The connection to related work on sensor placement [6, 7] and document summarization [4, 5] highlights the broad applicability of DPPs and the relevance of improved learning methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the EM algorithm is well-motivated and clearly explained. The use of Jensen’s inequality and the mapping to optimization on the Stiefel manifold demonstrate a solid theoretical understanding. The insightful step-by-step derivation is a significant strength.
* **Novelty:** The method addresses a challenging problem – full kernel matrix learning – without restrictive parameterizations. The shift to eigenvalue/eigenvector parameterization is a clever approach to sidestep the difficulties of directly optimizing the kernel entries.
* **Performance:** The experiments on synthetic data and a product recommendation task provide empirical support for the claim that the method retains both quality and diversity, outperforming K-Ascent (KA). The comparison to KA, even with its limited replacement strategy, showcases the method’s advantage.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise.
* **Solid Technical Contributions:** Mapping the constraint to optimization on the Stiefel manifold and demonstrating the inverse distribution is a DPP are strong technical achievements.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conclusion is Hasty:** The conclusion briefly summarizes the work but doesn’t fully emphasize the significance of the achieved results. It downplays the core contribution of avoiding projection steps and their impact on diversity.
* **Limited Real-World Datasets:** While the product recommendation task is relevant, more extensive evaluation on a wider range of real-world datasets would strengthen the claims about practical importance and demonstrate the generalizability of the diversity gains. The categories used, while representative, might not fully capture the diversity of DPP applications.
* **KA Baseline Limitations:** The description of the KA baseline's replacement strategy (only two of the top-10) feels somewhat contrived. While it highlights the proposed method's advantage, a more thorough KA implementation with a more extensive search for replacements might offer a stronger comparison.
* **Step Size Search in EM:** The paper mentions the step size search during the eigenvector update, but details regarding its implementation are sparse. A more detailed explanation of the step size selection process would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant theoretical and practical challenge in DPP learning.
* The proposed EM algorithm offers a clear advantage over existing methods in terms of both performance and robustness.
* The technical contributions are solid and well-explained.
* The experiments provide compelling evidence supporting the claims.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The limited number of real-world datasets and the somewhat constrained KA baseline might raise concerns about the generalizability of the results.
* The conclusion could be strengthened by more explicitly highlighting the significance of the contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPP learning. The proposed EM algorithm is a significant improvement over existing methods and offers a practical solution to a challenging problem. While some additional experiments and a more comprehensive comparison to KA would be beneficial, the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2014. The work is technically sound, novel, and clearly presented. The potential impact on applications relying on DPPs is substantial.
