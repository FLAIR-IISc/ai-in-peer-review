This paper proposes an EM-style algorithm for learning full DPP kernels, offering an alternative to direct gradient-based learning on K (KA). The key idea is to exploit the mixture representation of a DPP over elementary DPPs indexed by eigenvector subsets J, introduce q(J|Y) as a variational distribution, and lower-bound the likelihood. The E-step sets q(J|Y)=p(J|Y;K), which is shown to be a k-DPP; the M-step updates eigenvalues via closed-form marginals and takes a single gradient step for eigenvectors with a Stiefel-manifold update. Empirically, EM substantially outperforms KA with naive initialization and is modestly better in some settings with a stronger initializer, while being faster asymptotically. This relates well to prior work that either learns only item weights or mixtures of fixed DPPs, and to k-DPPs and eigendecompositions used in DPP inference (e.g., Kulesza & Taskar; Affandi et al.).

Quality: The E-step derivation and eigenvalue updates are sound and efficiently implemented via k-DPP marginals. However, the M-step is only partially optimized (single gradient step), requires repeated E-steps for step-size selection, and uses approximations (e.g., λ capped below 1), so the algorithm departs from a monotone EM with a guaranteed bound improvement. The runtime comparison is plausible but would benefit from reporting the number of expensive eigendecompositions per iteration and total wall-clock with variance.

Clarity: Generally well-written, but the “elegant” self-assessment and bolding in the introduction are unnecessary. Please clarify whether improvements are measured on the true log-likelihood or the lower bound; define “relative log-likelihood” precisely and avoid ratios of log-probabilities—report absolute log-likelihood differences (per example or per item) for interpretability.

Originality: Reformulating kernel learning for DPPs via an EM framework with a k-DPP E-step and eigenvalue/eigenvector separation is a novel and interesting contribution compared to prior convex surrogates and mixtures.

Significance: The approach addresses an important problem and shows modest to substantial empirical gains and better robustness to poor initializations; it is likely to be useful to practitioners training DPPs.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually novel EM reformulation leveraging mixture-of-elementary DPPs
- Principled E-step and efficient eigenvalue updates
- Consistent empirical gains, especially with naive initialization; reasonable speedups
- Relevance to many NIPS topics (probabilistic modeling, subset selection)

Cons for acceptance:
- M-step complexity and approximations undercut theoretical elegance and monotonicity
- Missing analysis of initialization effects (e.g., EM initialized from KA, or vice versa)
- Metric choice (relative log-likelihood) hinders interpretation
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses (e.g., effect of |Y| distribution, k)
