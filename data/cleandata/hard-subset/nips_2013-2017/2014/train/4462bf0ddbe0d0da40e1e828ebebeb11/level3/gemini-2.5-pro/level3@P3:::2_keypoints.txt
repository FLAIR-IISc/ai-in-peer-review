- Main contribution: novel EM algorithm to learn an unconstrained DPP kernel by reparameterizing K = VΛVᵀ and treating the DPP as a mixture over subsets of eigenvectors.  
- Key idea: introduce latent subset J of eigenvectors, apply Jensen’s inequality to derive E-step (posterior over J) and M-step (update Λ and V).  
- Eigenvector updates: handled via optimization on the Stiefel manifold.  
- Strength: original and significant — tackles the open problem of learning a full, non-parametric DPP kernel beyond restrictive convex settings.  
- Strength: technically sound derivation using a latent-variable EM formulation and appropriate manifold optimization.  
- Weakness: clarity and exposition are poor, with sloppy writing that obscures novel contributions.  
- Weakness: Section 3 poorly organized; core derivation is interleaved with side remarks and implementation details, making the algorithm hard to follow.  
- Weakness: vague language and notation usage (e.g., unclear phrasing in Algorithm 1, variables introduced without sufficient context).  
- Weakness: lack of self-containment — relies on external method [26] for eigenvector updates without sufficient intuition or description.  
- Experimental weakness: insufficient evaluation — comparisons only to projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent), a weak baseline.  
- Experimental weakness: missing comparisons to the restricted DPP learning methods from Kulesza & Taskar and to non-DPP subset selection methods.  
- Empirical note: EM shows robustness in data-poor settings (n = 2N), but this claim needs stronger baselines to be convincing.  
- Request for clarification: describe the initialization strategy in Eq. (23) and consider alternatives (e.g., deriving K from empirical covariance).  
- Minor clarity request: make the term “weight” in Eq. 7 more specific.  
- Suggestion: the qualitative baby-registry example would be stronger if compared against convex methods like [2].  
- Overall assessment: technically solid and original work with strong potential, but hampered by poor presentation and an inadequate experimental evaluation that require substantial revision.
