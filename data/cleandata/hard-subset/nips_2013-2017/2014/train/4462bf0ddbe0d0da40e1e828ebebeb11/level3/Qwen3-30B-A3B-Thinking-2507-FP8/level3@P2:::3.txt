

**Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"**

**Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) from example subsets. DPPs have gained popularity in machine learning for modeling set diversity, but learning the kernel matrix L from data has been a significant challenge. Prior work has focused on restricted learning settings: Kulesza and Taskar (2011) showed learning scalar weights for each row of L is convex, but this only captures item quality without diversity. Affandi et al. (2013) proposed learning weights for a linear combination of DPPs with fixed kernels, but this loses the DPP structure and its computational advantages. The authors correctly note that learning the full kernel matrix is conjectured to be NP-hard, making the proposed EM approach a significant contribution to the field.

The key insight of this paper is to change the parameterization from matrix entries to eigenvalues and eigenvectors, then apply an EM framework by introducing a hidden variable J that represents the selection of eigenvectors. By using Jensen's inequality to lower-bound the log-likelihood, they derive an EM algorithm that avoids the problematic projection steps required in naive gradient ascent approaches. The algorithm handles the non-convex eigenvector constraint by leveraging optimization on the Stiefel manifold, which is a sophisticated technical contribution.

**Strengths**

*Quality*: The paper is technically sound and well-justified. The authors provide a clear theoretical derivation of their EM algorithm, including the necessary mathematical details for the E-step and M-step. The proof that the E-step corresponds to a k-DPP is elegant and well-motivated. The computational complexity analysis is thorough, showing that the proposed method is asymptotically faster than the gradient ascent baseline (KA). The experiments on a real-world product recommendation task are well-designed and support the theoretical claims.

*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with DPPs, and the derivation of the EM algorithm is presented in a logical, step-by-step manner. The use of Algorithm 2 to summarize the EM procedure is particularly helpful. The authors clearly explain the intuition behind each step, making the technical content accessible to readers.

*Originality*: This work represents a significant step beyond prior learning approaches for DPPs. While previous work either restricted the kernel parameterization or used approximate methods, this paper develops a method that does not assume fixed values or restrictive parameterizations for L. The EM approach with eigenvalue/eigenvector parameterization is novel and addresses a key limitation of prior methods. The authors properly contextualize their contribution against previous NIPS papers on DPPs (Kulesza & Taskar, 2012; Affandi et al., 2013; Gillenwater et al., 2013).

*Significance*: The results have clear practical significance for applications where set diversity is important, such as product recommendation, document summarization, and sensor placement. The experiments show consistent improvements over the baseline KA method, with up to 16.5% relative gains in test log-likelihood. The paper demonstrates that the proposed method better preserves the diversity properties of DPPs, which is crucial for their intended applications.

**Weaknesses**

*Limited Experimental Scope*: While the experiments on the baby registry dataset are convincing, the paper could have included additional real-world applications to better demonstrate the broader significance of the approach. For instance, showing results on document summarization or sensor placement would strengthen the case for the method's applicability.

*Hasty Conclusion*: The conclusion section briefly mentions the method's advantages but could better emphasize the specific contributions and limitations. A more thorough discussion of when the EM approach would be particularly beneficial (e.g., when diversity is more important than item quality) would be helpful.

**Arguments Pro and Con**

*Arguments For Acceptance*: Novel EM approach for learning full DPP kernel matrices; avoids problematic projection steps that degrade diversity modeling; theoretically and empirically validated; well-written and clear exposition; demonstrated improvements over baseline method.

*Arguments Against Acceptance*: Limited experimental validation on additional real-world applications; conclusion section could better articulate specific contributions and limitations; more detailed discussion of practical implementation challenges would be beneficial.

**Recommendation**

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The contribution is significant and well-executed, addressing a key limitation in DPP learning that has been previously recognized as NP-hard. The EM approach is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and validated through comprehensive experiments. While additional experiments on different application domains would strengthen the paper further, the current results are sufficient to demonstrate the method's value. The paper is clear, well-written, and represents a solid contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling for set diversity.

The authors have made a meaningful contribution to the DPP literature by providing a method that preserves the diversity properties of DPPs while learning the full kernel matrix, which has been a longstanding challenge in the field. This work will be valuable to researchers and practitioners working with DPPs in various applications.
