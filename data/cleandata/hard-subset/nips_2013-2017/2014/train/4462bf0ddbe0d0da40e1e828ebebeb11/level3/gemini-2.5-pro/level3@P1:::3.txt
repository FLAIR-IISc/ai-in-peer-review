This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from data. DPPs are powerful probabilistic models for capturing diversity in subset selection tasks, parameterized by a positive semi-definite kernel matrix L. The main difficulty, as the authors note, is that maximizing the log-likelihood of observed subsets is a non-convex problem, conjectured to be NP-hard. Previous work has circumvented this by learning restricted versions of the kernel, such as learning a single weight per item [2] or learning weights for a mixture of fixed kernels [3]. These approaches, however, limit the model's ability to learn complex, non-parametric similarity relationships between items, which is fundamental to the diversity-promoting nature of DPPs.

The core contribution of this paper is a novel algorithm for learning the full, unconstrained kernel matrix. The key idea is to move away from optimizing the matrix entries directly. Instead, the authors parameterize the kernel by its eigendecomposition (eigenvalues and eigenvectors). By viewing the selection of eigenvectors as a hidden variable in the DPP's generative process, they are able to derive an Expectation-Maximization (EM) style algorithm. This approach cleverly avoids the problematic projection step required by naive projected gradient ascent, which can prematurely truncate eigenvalues to zero and lead to poor local optima that fail to model diversity.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a significant advance in the area of learning with DPPs. To my knowledge, this is the first practical algorithm for learning the full, non-parametric kernel matrix. This opens the door to using DPPs in a much wider and more flexible range of applications, where the underlying notions of quality and similarity are not known beforehand and must be learned from data. The proposed method directly tackles the central difficulty of the learning problem, rather than simplifying the model.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical derivation of the EM algorithm is elegant and sound. There are several strong technical contributions:
    *   The re-parameterization and introduction of a hidden variable (the set of active eigenvectors J) is insightful.
    *   A key result (Lemma 1) shows that the posterior distribution over this hidden variable, `q(J | Yi)`, is itself a k-DPP. This is a crucial insight that makes the E-step tractable, as efficient inference algorithms for k-DPPs can be leveraged.
    *   The M-step updates are particularly well-designed. The update for the eigenvalues is a closed-form solution that automatically satisfies the necessary constraints, completely avoiding a projection step. For the eigenvectors, the authors correctly identify the update as an optimization problem on a Stiefel manifold and employ an appropriate multiplicative update rule, again avoiding a naive and potentially harmful projection. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the optimization geometry.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide clear motivation, carefully walk the reader through the background of DPPs, and present the step-by-step derivation of their EM algorithm in a logical and understandable manner. The connection between the failure mode of the projected gradient method and the proposed EM solution is articulated persuasively.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on the Amazon baby registry dataset effectively support the paper's claims. The comparison with projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) shows that the proposed EM method is not only faster but also more robust, achieving significant gains in test log-likelihood, especially in data-poor settings or with less-informed initializations. The qualitative example—where K-Ascent selects three different baby monitors while EM chooses a more diverse set—is a compelling illustration of the practical benefits of the proposed learning algorithm in capturing negative interactions.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Experimental Scope:** While the product recommendation task is a good fit, the paper's claims would be significantly strengthened by evaluating the algorithm on at least one other real-world dataset from a different domain where DPPs are commonly used, such as document summarization or image search. This would provide a more compelling case for the general utility and practical importance of the method.

2.  **Conclusion Section:** The conclusion is somewhat brief and undersells the paper's contributions. It could be expanded to re-emphasize the key technical innovations (e.g., the k-DPP posterior, the Stiefel manifold optimization) and to discuss potential future work, such as scaling the method to even larger N or exploring different optimization strategies within the EM framework.

3.  **Baseline Comparison:** The main comparison is against K-Ascent, which is a very reasonable baseline. However, it might have been instructive to also include a comparison against a restricted model (e.g., the method of [2]) to empirically quantify how much is gained by learning the full kernel matrix versus a simpler, convex parameterization on this dataset.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a strong and novel contribution to the literature on determinantal point processes. The proposed EM algorithm is technically sophisticated, well-motivated, and addresses a key open problem in the field. The experimental results, while limited to a single domain, clearly demonstrate the advantages of the approach. The weaknesses are minor and relate mostly to the scope of the evaluation and the conciseness of the conclusion. This work is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers working with DPPs and related models of diversity.
