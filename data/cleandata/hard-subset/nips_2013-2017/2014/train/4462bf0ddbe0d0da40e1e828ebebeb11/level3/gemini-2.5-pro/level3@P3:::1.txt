### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the challenging non-convex problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from data. Previous work has typically circumvented this difficulty by focusing on more restricted, convex learning settings, such as learning weights for rows of a fixed kernel or for a mixture of fixed DPPs. The main contribution of this paper is a novel algorithm for learning the full kernel based on an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework.

The key idea is to reformulate the DPP likelihood by leveraging the kernel's eigendecomposition. A DPP can be viewed as a mixture over "elementary DPPs," each corresponding to a subset of the kernel's eigenvectors. The authors treat this subset choice as a latent variable. This formulation naturally leads to an EM-style algorithm where the E-step involves computing the posterior distribution over these latent subsets (which turns out to be a k-DPP), and the M-step updates the kernel's eigenvalues and eigenvectors to maximize the expected log-likelihood. The authors compare their method (EM) to a baseline of projected gradient ascent on the kernel matrix (K-Ascent or KA) on a product recommendation task.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a creative and principled new approach to the difficult problem of full kernel learning for DPPs. Casting the problem within an EM framework by introducing latent variables based on the eigendecomposition is a novel idea that opens up a new direction for research in this area. It provides a meaningful alternative to direct gradient-based optimization.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development of the EM algorithm is sound. The insight that a DPP can be represented as a mixture of elementary DPPs is leveraged effectively, and the derivation of the E-step, which results in a tractable k-DPP, is a key technical contribution.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experiments, conducted on a real-world baby registry dataset, demonstrate that the proposed EM algorithm is a viable and often superior alternative to projected gradient ascent (KA). The results are particularly compelling when using a naive Wishart initialization, where EM substantially outperforms KA. The finding that EM holds an advantage in data-poor settings even with a better "moments-matching" initialization is also an important result, highlighting the robustness of the proposed method. The qualitative example of the learned product recommendations nicely illustrates the practical benefit of the improved model.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Critique of the M-step:** While the EM formulation is conceptually elegant, the practical implementation of the M-step is less so. The update for the eigenvectors is particularly complex. The authors note that fully optimizing the eigenvectors in the M-step is intractable and resort to a single gradient step on the Stiefel manifold. Furthermore, the derivative used is only valid for the initial gradient, and the step-size search requires re-running the E-step. This, combined with the need for special handling of eigenvalues approaching 1, makes the M-step feel somewhat ad-hoc and weakens the paper's claim of having a simple and elegant algorithm.
*   **Missing Experimental Analysis:** The experimental comparison could be strengthened by a more thorough analysis of initialization. The paper shows that EM is better than KA with a poor start (Wishart) and comparable with a good start (moments-matching). A crucial missing experiment is to initialize the EM algorithm with the converged solution from KA. If EM can further improve upon KA's solution, it would provide much stronger evidence that the EM updates offer benefits beyond simply being more robust to poor initialization. This would help disentangle the optimization properties of the algorithm from its initialization sensitivity.
*   **Clarity of Evaluation Metrics:** The primary evaluation metric used is "relative test log-likelihood," which appears to be a ratio of log-likelihoods. This metric is non-standard and difficult to interpret. A small improvement in this ratio could correspond to a large or small improvement in probability, but the scale is unclear. It would be much more transparent and informative to report the *difference* in average test log-likelihoods. A difference of log(2) ≈ 0.69, for instance, has a clear interpretation: the model is twice as likely on average. This would allow for a much better assessment of the practical significance of the reported gains.
*   **Minor Writing/Stylistic Issues:** The authors repeatedly describe their own algorithm as "elegant." While the conceptual framework is clever, this self-assessment feels unnecessary. It is better to let the quality and simplicity of the method speak for itself. Additionally, the use of bolding in the introduction for emphasis is stylistically jarring and does not add to the clarity of the text.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a very good paper that introduces an interesting and promising approach for learning DPP kernels. The reformulation of the problem into an EM framework is a novel and valuable contribution. The method is shown to be empirically effective, outperforming a standard baseline in several important settings, particularly those involving poor initialization or limited data.

The main weaknesses lie in the somewhat complex M-step, which tempers the elegance of the overall framework, and in an experimental evaluation that could be more comprehensive. However, these points do not undermine the core contribution. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a significant problem in the field. It is likely to be of interest to researchers working on probabilistic models, diversity modeling, and subset selection, and I recommend its acceptance.
