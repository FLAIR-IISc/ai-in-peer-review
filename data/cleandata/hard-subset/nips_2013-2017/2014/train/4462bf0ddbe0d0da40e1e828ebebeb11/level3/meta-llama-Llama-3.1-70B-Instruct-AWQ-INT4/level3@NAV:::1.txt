This paper presents a novel algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework. The main contribution of this work is reformulating kernel learning for DPPs as an EM problem, which provides an alternative to direct gradient-based kernel parameter learning.

The method is based on the observation that any DPP can be represented as a mixture over elementary DPPs, and uses a variational distribution over subsets to update the kernel parameters in an EM-style optimization algorithm. The E-step updates the variational distribution as a k-DPP, and the M-step performs single gradient steps to update the kernel parameters.

The empirical results show that the EM algorithm outperforms the naive kernel ascent (KA) method substantially with naive KA initialization, and modestly in some settings with smarter KA initialization. The results also demonstrate that the EM algorithm is more robust to initialization and dataset changes, and is asymptotically faster than the KA method.

The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the EM algorithm and its advantages over the KA method. The experimental results are convincing, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the results, including an investigation of the effect of initialization on the performance of the EM algorithm.

Strengths:

* The paper presents a novel and interesting approach to learning DPPs using an EM framework.
* The EM algorithm is shown to outperform the KA method in several settings, and is more robust to initialization and dataset changes.
* The paper provides a clear and thorough explanation of the EM algorithm and its advantages over the KA method.
* The experimental results are convincing, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the results.

Weaknesses:

* The M-step of the EM algorithm is fairly involved and requires caveats/approximations, which weakens the claimed elegance of the method.
* The paper lacks an analysis of initialization effects, such as whether initializing the EM algorithm from KA solutions yields improvements.
* The use of "relative log likelihood" as a metric is questionable, and the authors could consider reporting log-probability differences for easier interpretation.
* The writing style is generally clear, but the authors could avoid using bolding in the introduction, and refrain from calling the algorithm "elegant".

Overall, this is a good paper that presents a novel and interesting approach to learning DPPs using an EM framework. The experimental results are convincing, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the results. However, the paper could be improved by addressing the weaknesses mentioned above.
