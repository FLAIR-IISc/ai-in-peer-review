This paper proposes a novel algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) using eigendecomposition, Stiefel-manifold optimization, mixtures of elementary DPPs, and a Jensen lower bound. The authors present an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of a DPP, which is a significant contribution to the subset selection literature.

The paper starts by providing a clear introduction to DPPs and their applications in subset selection tasks. The authors then describe the challenges of learning DPPs, including the non-convexity of the log-likelihood objective and the need for a projection step to maintain positive semi-definiteness of the kernel matrix. They propose an EM algorithm that overcomes these challenges by changing the kernel parameterization from matrix entries to eigenvalues and eigenvectors.

The EM algorithm is well-founded and original, and the authors provide a clear derivation of the algorithm. They also provide experimental results that show the favorable behavior of the EM algorithm compared to a simple K-Ascent baseline. However, the experiments are limited in scope, and the authors do not provide comparisons to alternative subset-selection methods mentioned in the introduction. Therefore, the practical benefits of the DPP approach are unclear.

The write-up of the paper is sometimes sloppy and hard to follow. Notation is sometimes undefined or introduced after use, and the paper structure interleaves method derivation with side remarks and alternatives, making the main thread convoluted. The authors should present the method clearly first and then discuss alternative approaches and side remarks.

Specific notation issues include the use of the marginal kernel K and variable V before definition. The language and phrasing are also sometimes unclear, such as "minimises the change to Frobenius norm" which should state "closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix to Q". The section referencing external work ([26]) is hard to follow, and the authors should outline the simpler approach directly rather than forcing readers to look up the original.

Ambiguous terms need clarification, such as what "weight" refers to in p.4 line 189. The rationale in equation (30) is also questionable, and the authors should explain why m_{ij} is not used and why not initialize with the empirical covariance matrix.

In terms of the quality criteria, the paper scores well on originality, as the EM algorithm is a novel contribution to the subset selection literature. The paper also scores well on significance, as DPPs have many applications in real-world tasks. However, the paper scores poorly on clarity, due to the sloppy write-up and notation issues. The paper also scores poorly on experimental comparisons, as the experiments are limited in scope and do not provide a clear understanding of the practical benefits of the DPP approach.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The EM algorithm is a novel and original contribution to the subset selection literature.
* The paper provides a clear introduction to DPPs and their applications.
* The experimental results show the favorable behavior of the EM algorithm compared to a simple K-Ascent baseline.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The write-up is sometimes sloppy and hard to follow.
* The notation is sometimes undefined or introduced after use.
* The paper structure is convoluted, and the main thread is hard to follow.
* The experiments are limited in scope, and the practical benefits of the DPP approach are unclear.
* The paper lacks comparisons to alternative subset-selection methods mentioned in the introduction.

Overall, the paper is technically solid and original, but it needs clearer exposition and substantially stronger experimental comparisons. With revisions to address these issues, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the subset selection literature.
