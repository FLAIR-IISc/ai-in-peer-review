

This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the kernel matrix of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a significant gap in the literature where previous work has been limited to restricted learning settings due to the NP-hardness of full kernel learning. The authors recognize that gradient ascent methods require problematic projection steps that often result in degenerate solutions, and they develop a novel approach by leveraging the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K, using a Jensen lower bound on the likelihood, and employing Stiefel manifold optimization. This differs from prior work by [2] (learning row weights), [3] (learning weights for linear combinations of fixed DPPs), and [8] (parametric Bayesian methods), as it directly learns an unconstrained, non-parametric kernel matrix without losing the computational benefits of DPPs.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with a well-motivated methodological approach. The EM framework elegantly transforms the learning problem by introducing a hidden variable J representing a set of eigenvectors, and the authors successfully derive closed-form updates for eigenvalues and a multiplicative update for eigenvectors. The theoretical foundation is solid, with appropriate use of Jensen's inequality and careful handling of the Stiefel manifold constraint. The experimental results on a baby registry product recommendation task demonstrate clear advantages over the K-Ascent baseline, with EM achieving up to 16.5% relative gains in test log-likelihood and being asymptotically faster. The analysis of why EM outperforms K-Ascent (particularly in settings with strong negative item interactions) is insightful and well-supported by the d metric analysis.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues that undermine its presentation. The writing is often convoluted, with method derivation interleaved with side remarks and alternative approaches rather than presenting the core algorithm clearly first. Critical notation (K, V, J) is introduced without definition or used before formal introduction, making the paper difficult to follow. For instance, the phrase "minimises the change to Frobenius norm" (p.4, line 189) is unclear and should specify "closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix to Q." The reference to [26] for the Stiefel manifold update is problematic as it forces readers to consult external material rather than providing a self-contained description of the simplified approach.

The experimental evaluation has important limitations. While the comparison to K-Ascent is useful, the paper fails to compare with other subset selection methods mentioned in the introduction (e.g., [4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9]), making it unclear whether the DPP approach itself provides practical advantages over alternative frameworks. The "moments-matching" initialization (Equation 23) appears questionable since the authors don't explain why m_ij is not used directly, and why they take the square root of max(K̃_iiK̃_jj - m_ij, 0) rather than using empirical covariance. The dataset is limited to a single application domain (baby registry recommendations), and the paper doesn't demonstrate whether the method generalizes to other DPP applications like document summarization or sensor placement.

The paper's significance is somewhat diminished by these limitations. While the learning algorithm is technically novel and well-founded, the lack of broader experimental validation and comparison to competing approaches makes it difficult to assess the practical impact of the method. The paper also doesn't sufficiently clarify how the learned DPP kernel translates to better real-world performance beyond log-likelihood metrics.

In terms of quality, the technical content is sound with appropriate theoretical grounding and experimental validation. The paper is original in its approach to learning the full kernel matrix without restrictive assumptions. However, the clarity issues and limited experimental scope prevent it from making a fully compelling contribution to the field.

Strengths:
- Novel EM-based learning algorithm that avoids problematic projection steps
- Theoretically grounded with careful derivation of updates
- Clear empirical advantages over K-Ascent in specific settings
- Asymptotic runtime advantage over gradient-based approaches

Weaknesses:
- Poorly written with unclear notation and convoluted structure
- Limited experimental scope (only one application domain)
- No comparison to alternative subset selection methods
- Questionable initialization rationale and lack of justification for design choices
- Fails to demonstrate practical advantages of DPPs over other approaches

Pro acceptance: Strong technical contribution to a well-motivated problem, with clear advantages over a baseline method in specific settings.
Con acceptance: Insufficient experimental validation to demonstrate broader significance, and poor presentation that obscures the contribution.

Overall, this is a technically solid paper that makes a meaningful methodological contribution to DPP learning, but its impact would be substantially greater with clearer exposition, more comprehensive experimental evaluation, and comparison to alternative subset selection approaches. The core idea is promising, but the current presentation limits its accessibility and impact.
