This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from data. The authors correctly point out that this is a non-convex optimization problem, conjectured to be NP-hard, and that prior work has focused on more restricted, convex learning settings. The main contribution is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the unconstrained kernel. The key idea is to change the parameterization from the kernel matrix entries to its eigenvalues and eigenvectors. By viewing a DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs (defined by subsets of eigenvectors), the authors introduce a latent variable indicating which elementary DPP generated a given data point. This allows them to derive a Jensen's inequality-based lower bound on the log-likelihood. The algorithm then alternates between an E-step, which computes the posterior over the latent variables, and an M-step, which updates the eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The eigenvector update is handled via an optimization on the Stiefel manifold to maintain orthogonality. The proposed EM method is compared against a projected gradient ascent baseline on a product recommendation task using a baby registry dataset, showing improvements in test log-likelihood and runtime.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a well-known and difficult problem in the DPP literature: learning the full kernel matrix. The proposed EM algorithm is a novel and non-trivial contribution. If effective, this method would significantly broaden the applicability of DPPs by allowing them to be learned directly from data without restrictive assumptions on the kernel structure. This is a meaningful step forward for probabilistic modeling of diverse subsets.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the EM algorithm appears to be technically sound. The formulation of the problem using the mixture of elementary DPPs as a latent variable model is clever and elegant. The subsequent application of Jensen's inequality and the derivation of the E- and M-steps are standard but well-executed. The use of Stiefel manifold optimization for the eigenvector update is appropriate for the constraints of the problem.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Exposition:** The paper's primary weakness is its clarity. The writing is often dense and the structure makes the core ideas difficult to follow.
    *   The derivation in Section 3 interleaves the main steps of the algorithm with justifications, runtime analysis, and discussions of alternatives. This makes the main algorithmic thread convoluted and hard to piece together. It would be much clearer to present the final algorithm and its derivation first, and then discuss these other aspects.
    *   Notation is sometimes undefined or introduced after its first use. For example, the marginal kernel `K` is used throughout Section 2, but its relationship to `L` is only defined at the end of the section in Eq. (1). Similarly, `V` is used in Section 3.2 without being explicitly defined as the matrix of eigenvectors.
    *   The description of the projected gradient ascent baseline is unclear. The phrase "chooses the closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix to Q" would be much more precise than the current text.
    *   The section on the M-step update for eigenvectors (Sec 3.5) refers to an algorithm from [26] but then describes a simplified version without clearly outlining the steps of that simplification. This forces the reader to consult external material to fully understand the update rule.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is another significant weakness.
    *   The comparison is limited to a single, relatively simple baseline: projected gradient ascent on the kernel entries (K-Ascent). While the EM algorithm is shown to be more robust, especially with a naive initialization, the baseline itself is shown by the authors to have fundamental flaws (e.g., getting stuck at diagonal solutions). A stronger baseline would make the results more convincing.
    *   More importantly, the paper does not compare the learned DPP model against any alternative subset selection methods. The introduction mentions applications like document summarization and product recommendation where other diversity-aware methods exist. Without such comparisons, it is difficult to assess the practical significance of the proposed learning algorithm in a broader context. The experiments demonstrate that EM is a better way to optimize DPP likelihood than K-Ascent, but they do not demonstrate that a DPP learned via EM is better than other state-of-the-art recommendation or summarization models.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   **Structure:** I strongly recommend that the authors restructure Section 3. A clear, top-down presentation of the final EM algorithm first, followed by the detailed derivations of the E-step and M-step, would greatly improve readability.
*   **Initialization Rationale:** Regarding the moment-matching initialization in Eq. (23), could the authors clarify the rationale? Why is the second term under the square root, and why is the empirical pairwise frequency `m_ij` subtracted from the product of marginals? Have the authors considered a more direct initialization, for instance, using the empirical covariance matrix of the item indicator vectors?
*   **Ambiguity:** On page 4, the text mentions that the update in Eq. (21) uses `η` as the step size. This seems to conflict with Algorithm 2, where `η` is initialized to 1 and then halved until the likelihood improves, but after a successful step, it is doubled (`η ← 2η`). This doubling strategy is unusual for line searches and warrants explanation.
*   **Minor Fixes:** The sentence beginning "The first part of the projection step..." could be rewritten for clarity, for example: "The projection `V diag(max(λ, 0)) V^T` finds the closest PSD matrix to Q in Frobenius norm [24]".

### Overall Assessment

In summary, this paper presents a technically solid, original, and potentially significant contribution to learning DPPs. The EM-based approach is novel and well-founded. However, the paper is severely hampered by a lack of clarity in its exposition and by an experimental evaluation that is too limited in scope to convincingly demonstrate the practical impact of the method. While the core technical ideas are strong, the weaknesses in presentation and validation are significant. I believe the paper has high potential but requires a major revision to address these issues before it can be accepted.
