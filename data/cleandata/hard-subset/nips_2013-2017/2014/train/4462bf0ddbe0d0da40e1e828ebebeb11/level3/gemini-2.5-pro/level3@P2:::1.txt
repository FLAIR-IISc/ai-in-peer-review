This paper addresses the challenging non-convex problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from data via maximum likelihood. Previous work has largely avoided this problem by focusing on more restricted, convex learning settings, such as learning weights for each item [2] or learning weights for a linear combination of fixed DPPs [3]. The main contribution of this paper is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full kernel. The key idea is to reparameterize the kernel by its eigenvalues and eigenvectors and leverage the representation of a DPP as a mixture of "elementary" DPPs. This introduces a latent variable corresponding to the selection of a subset of eigenvectors. The proposed EM algorithm then alternates between an E-step, which computes the posterior distribution over this latent variable (shown to be a k-DPP), and an M-step, which updates the eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The M-step for eigenvalues is a closed-form update, while the update for the eigenvectors is a single gradient step on the Stiefel manifold. The authors compare their EM approach to a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) on a product recommendation task, showing that EM is more robust, achieves higher test log-likelihood (especially with naive initializations or limited data), and is computationally faster.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel and creative approach to a significant and difficult problem in the DPP literature. Applying the EM framework by introducing a latent variable over the eigenspace is an insightful way to tackle the non-convexity of the full kernel learning problem. A robust method for this task is highly desirable and would greatly expand the practical utility of DPPs. This work is a solid step in that direction and moves beyond the more constrained learning scenarios previously studied.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated. The core idea of avoiding the projection step inherent in naive gradient ascent is compelling. As the authors argue, this projection can prematurely collapse the eigenvalue spectrum, leading to poor, near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture the diversity-modeling power of DPPs. The EM formulation elegantly sidesteps this issue for the eigenvalues. The derivation is clear, and the identification of the posterior in the E-step as a tractable k-DPP is a nice result.

3.  **Empirical Results:** The experiments, while focused on one task, effectively demonstrate the advantages of the EM approach. It outperforms the K-Ascent baseline, with relative gains of up to 16.5% in a low-data setting. The results convincingly show that EM is more robust to initialization than projected gradient ascent and is also asymptotically and practically faster. The qualitative example of the learned baby registry nicely illustrates the failure mode of the baseline and the superiority of the EM-trained model.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Complexity of the M-Step:** While the EM framing is elegant, the resulting M-step is somewhat involved and relies on approximations. Specifically, the eigenvectors are updated via a single gradient step rather than a full maximization, making this a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm. Furthermore, this step requires specialized optimization techniques on the Stiefel manifold and a line search that re-evaluates the full likelihood, which slightly detracts from the simplicity of the overall framework. While these are reasonable practical choices, the procedure is less straightforward than a classic EM algorithm with closed-form updates.

2.  **Incomplete Experimental Analysis:** The experiments could be strengthened with additional analysis. A key question left unanswered is how the EM algorithm performs when initialized from the solution found by the K-Ascent baseline. Would EM be able to further refine this solution and escape the local optimum? This experiment would provide deeper insight into the optimization landscape and better characterize whether EM is simply a better initializer/optimizer or if it consistently finds qualitatively different solutions.

3.  **Clarity of Evaluation Metric:** The use of "relative test log-likelihood differences" (i.e., `(L_EM - L_KA) / |L_KA|`) is non-standard for reporting log-likelihood results. A ratio of logarithms is difficult to interpret. A much clearer and more standard metric would be the absolute difference in average test log-likelihood per sample, `(L_EM - L_KA) / n_test`. This value has a direct interpretation related to the improvement in the model's predictive probability.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   The paper introduces a novel and technically interesting algorithm for the important and difficult problem of learning a full DPP kernel.
*   The method successfully avoids the problematic projection step of naive gradient-based methods, leading to a more robust learning procedure.
*   The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over a natural baseline in terms of model quality (especially with poor initializations) and computational efficiency.

### Arguments against Acceptance

*   The empirical gains are modest when the baseline is well-initialized with sufficient data.
*   The M-step is not a true maximization and is complex to implement, which makes the algorithm less clean than a standard EM procedure.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive to better understand the interplay between the two algorithms.

In summary, this is a very good paper that presents a creative and promising approach to a challenging problem. The method is well-motivated, and the experiments demonstrate its value. Despite some weaknesses in the experimental analysis and the complexity of the M-step, the strengths in originality and significance are substantial. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field and is likely to inspire follow-up work. I recommend acceptance.
