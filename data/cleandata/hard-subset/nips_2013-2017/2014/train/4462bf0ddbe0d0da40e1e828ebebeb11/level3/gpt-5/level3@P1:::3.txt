Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles maximum-likelihood learning of determinantal point processes (DPPs), which model distributions over subsets favoring both item quality and diversity via a PSD kernel L (or equivalently marginal kernel K). While DPP inference is efficient, learning a full unconstrained kernel from sets is conjectured NP-hard, so prior work has restricted the parameterization: per-item scalings on a fixed similarity matrix (convex; Kulesza & Taskar UAI’11), or mixtures/linear combinations of fixed kernels (Lin & Bilmes UAI’12; Gillenwater et al. NIPS’12), or Bayesian learning under parametric forms (Affandi et al. ICML’13/NIPS’12). These approaches often capture “quality” but underexploit “diversity,” or sacrifice exact DPP structure.

This paper contributes an EM-style algorithm that learns the full kernel by reparameterizing K through its eigendecomposition K = VΛV^T and introducing a hidden variable J indexing eigenvectors. Using the known representation of a DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs, the authors derive a Jensen lower bound, yielding a tractable E-step and partially tractable M-step:
- E-step: q(J|Y) = pK(J|Y) is shown to be a k-DPP with a closed-form kernel (via an explicit construction using U = V^T and R = diag(√(λ/(1−λ)))).
- M-step: eigenvalues admit closed-form updates via k-DPP marginals; eigenvectors are updated by a single gradient step on the Stiefel manifold (orthogonality constraint), using a multiplicative matrix-exponential update.

This avoids the projections required by naive projected gradient ascent on K (“KA”), which can zero eigenvalues and trap the optimizer near diagonal kernels, thereby destroying diversity modeling. Experiments on a large-scale product recommendation task (baby registry categories) show improved test log-likelihood and faster runtime compared to KA; gains are most pronounced in settings where negative interactions matter and in lower-data regimes.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The EM derivation is careful and leverages well-established DPP identities (mixtures of elementary DPPs) and k-DPP marginalization. Showing q(J|Y) is a k-DPP and providing an efficient eigenvalue update via k-DPP marginals are solid contributions. The Stiefel-manifold update for V is appropriate and avoids problematic projections. The algorithm is a generalized EM: one V-step per M-step with acceptance based on likelihood increase, which preserves monotonic improvement in L.
- Algorithmic complexity is analyzed: O(T1 n N k^2 + T1 T2 N^3), which is advantageous over KA when set sizes k are small relative to N. Practical safeguards (keeping λ < 1) are documented.
- Empirical evaluation: On the baby registry dataset (13 categories, average N≈71, n≈8.6k), EM improves median test log-likelihood over KA with Wishart initialization and retains an edge with moment-matching initialization, especially in data-poor settings (up to 16.5% gain). EM is ~2.1× faster. A qualitative example illustrates reduced redundancy in recommendations.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written. The probabilistic and matrix calculus preliminaries are concise and accurate. The EM lower bound, E/M-steps, complexity, and the manifold update are explained with sufficient detail to reproduce (supplement covers proofs/derivations). The conclusion is brief and could better highlight the broader applicability and limitations.

Originality
Recasting DPP learning via eigenvalue/eigenvector EM with a k-DPP E-step and a manifold-aware V-update is, to my knowledge, novel within the DPP learning literature. Prior works either constrain parameterization or use Bayesian/posterior sampling under parametric forms. The observation that projection-based KA can collapse diversity due to eigenvalue truncation, and the demonstrated fix via EM in the eigenbasis, is insightful.

Significance
Learning full DPP kernels is important for applications where diversity is essential (summarization, search, sensor placement, recommendation). The method retains the DPP’s attractive properties and demonstrates both robustness and speed. Although experiments focus on one domain, the approach is general and likely to influence subsequent work on scalable and unconstrained DPP learning.

Strengths
- Principled EM formulation grounded in DPP structure; elegant use of the mixture over elementary DPPs.
- Closed-form eigenvalue updates via k-DPP marginals; efficient E-step construction.
- Manifold optimization for eigenvectors avoids harmful projections; practical line-search with monotonic L.
- Clear advantage over KA in likelihood, robustness to initialization, and runtime; convincing qualitative example.
- Good complexity characterization; practical details (λ < 1, step-size strategy) provided.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Empirical scope: Results are from a single real-world domain. Adding at least one other application (e.g., document/image summarization) or public datasets would strengthen claims of generality.
- Baselines: KA is a reasonable strawman for full-K learning, but comparisons to alternative parameterizations (e.g., low-rank factorization with box-constrained eigenvalues, Cholesky with soft constraints, quasi-Newton on the Stiefel manifold, or Bayesian methods under flexible kernels) would contextualize gains.
- Optimization details: The eigenvector update uses a single gradient step per M-step; an ablation showing sensitivity to the number of V-updates and step-size would be informative. Discuss numerical cost and stability of the matrix exponential for larger N.
- Scalability: While asymptotically favorable when k ≪ N, the method still incurs O(N^3) eigendecompositions during line-search. Discussion/experiments with approximations (e.g., Nyström, randomized SVD, low-rank DPPs) would be valuable for larger N.
- Evaluation metrics: Complement log-likelihood with task metrics that reflect diversity/coverage and user utility in recommendation; the qualitative example is useful but limited.
- Theory: Convergence guarantees are empirical; a brief discussion of generalized EM convergence properties in this setting would help.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated algorithm that directly addresses a long-standing bottleneck in DPP learning.
- Solid technical contributions (k-DPP E-step, efficient eigenvalue updates, manifold treatment of V).
- Clear empirical gains and runtime benefits; compelling qualitative behavior.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited diversity of datasets and baselines; unclear performance on larger N without approximations.
- Some optimization choices (single-step V-update, reliance on matrix exponential) merit deeper analysis.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive is performance to the λ cap below 1 and to initialization? Any heuristics for setting the cap?
- Can the V-update be implemented with a Cayley transform or first-order retraction to avoid matrix exponentials?
- Have you explored low-rank variants or Nyström approximations within the EM framework?
- How does the method behave when observed sets have widely varying sizes (large k)?

Overall evaluation
A strong paper that advances learning of full DPP kernels with a principled and practical EM approach. With broader empirical validation and additional baselines, it would be even more compelling. I lean toward acceptance.
