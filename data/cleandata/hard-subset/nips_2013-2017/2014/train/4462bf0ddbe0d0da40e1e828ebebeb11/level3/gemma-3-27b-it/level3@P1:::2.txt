This paper proposes a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP). DPPs are a valuable tool for modeling diverse subset selection, finding applications in areas like recommendation systems. While previous work has largely focused on constrained learning settings due to the NP-hardness of full kernel learning, this paper cleverly utilizes eigendecomposition and a Jensen’s inequality-based lower bound to develop a practical optimization procedure. The core idea – shifting the parameterization from kernel entries to eigenvalues and eigenvectors – is a significant contribution.

The paper builds on a substantial body of work in DPPs, referencing key papers from Kulesza & Taskar (2012), Kulesza et al. (2014), and others. The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature, acknowledging prior attempts at DPP learning, such as learning scalar weights [2] or ensembles of DPPs [3].  The connection to related models and the identified limitations of previous approaches (e.g., the potential for degenerate solutions with gradient ascent due to the positive semi-definiteness constraint) demonstrates a solid understanding of the field.  The reference to foundational work on DPPs as models of fermions [10] provides necessary context.

The strengths of this paper are its technical soundness and originality. The derivation of the EM algorithm appears rigorous, and the application of the Stiefel manifold optimization for the eigenvector update is a sophisticated technique. The use of a Jensen’s inequality lower bound is a standard approach in EM algorithm development and appropriately applied here. The experimental results, while limited, do demonstrate that the proposed EM algorithm outperforms a baseline K-Ascent method, particularly when initialized with a moments-matching approach. The observation that EM performs better when negative interactions between items are strong – supported by the analysis of the 'd' metric – is insightful.

However, several weaknesses detract from the overall impact of the paper. The most significant is the lack of comparison to other, established subset selection methods. While the paper effectively compares against K-Ascent, it fails to benchmark the DPP approach against alternatives like greedy algorithms, submodular maximization techniques (referenced in [4, 5]), or other diversity-promoting models. This makes it difficult to assess the true significance and practical benefits of using DPPs in the first place.  Do the gains justify the added complexity?  The absence of this comparative analysis weakens the argument for the method’s adoption.

The clarity of the paper is also a concern. The writing is often convoluted and lacks precision.  Notation is occasionally introduced *after* its use, and certain terms (e.g., “weight” on page 4) are ambiguous. The paper frequently interrupts the flow of the method derivation with tangential discussions, making it difficult to follow the main thread.  For example, the discussion of projected gradient ascent feels somewhat disjointed from the main EM algorithm. A more structured presentation, prioritizing a clear, step-by-step explanation of the EM method *before* delving into alternatives or justifications, would greatly improve readability.  Specific phrasing issues, such as “minimises the change to Frobenius norm,” should be revised for clarity.  Referencing external work, like [26], without adequately explaining the relevant technique places an undue burden on the reader.  

Furthermore, the initialization strategy, while improved by the moments-matching approach, raises questions.  The rationale behind not utilizing m_{ij} in the initialization, and why an empirical covariance matrix wasn't considered, is unclear.  Finally, the experiments are limited in scope; more extensive evaluation on a wider range of datasets would strengthen the conclusions.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound EM algorithm for learning DPP kernels.
*   Addresses a challenging problem (full kernel learning) previously considered intractable.
*   Demonstrates improvement over a baseline method (K-Ascent).
*   Potential for applications in diverse subset selection tasks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of comparison to relevant alternative subset selection methods.
*   Poor clarity and organization of the writing.
*   Limited scope of the experimental evaluation.
*   Ambiguous notation and phrasing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically interesting contribution to the field of DPPs. However, its impact is diminished by its lack of broader comparative evaluation and its convoluted presentation. While the algorithm itself is well-motivated and appears to function correctly, the paper needs significant improvement in clarity and experimental validation to justify acceptance into NIPS. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to address the concerns raised in this review and resubmit a revised version in the future.
