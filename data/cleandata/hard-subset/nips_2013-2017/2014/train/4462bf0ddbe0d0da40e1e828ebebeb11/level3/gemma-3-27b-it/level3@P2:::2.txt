This paper presents a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing the NP-hardness of directly maximizing the log-likelihood of the kernel matrix. The authors cleverly re-parameterize the kernel in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors, enabling an effective optimization procedure leveraging a Jensen’s inequality lower bound and a coordinate ascent approach. The work builds upon a substantial body of research on DPPs, initially developed as models of fermions [10] and recently gaining traction in machine learning for tasks requiring balanced quality and diversity [8, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 19, 2, 3, 20].  Prior work has focused on constrained learning settings [2, 3], and this paper offers a significant step towards learning the full kernel matrix without restrictive parameterizations.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its technical novelty and theoretical grounding. The use of eigendecomposition to circumvent the non-convexity of the kernel learning problem is a clever and well-motivated approach. The derivation of the EM algorithm appears sound, and the connection to existing DPP theory (particularly the mixture of elementary DPPs [21]) is clearly articulated. The experiments demonstrate that the proposed EM algorithm consistently outperforms a simple projected gradient ascent method (K-Ascent) on a product recommendation task, and the analysis suggests that the performance gains are more pronounced when modeling strong negative interactions between items. The asymptotic runtime analysis highlights the potential efficiency improvements of the EM approach.

**Weaknesses:**

While technically strong, the paper suffers from significant clarity and presentation issues. The writing is often convoluted, interleaving method derivation with tangential discussions and alternative approaches. A more streamlined presentation, first clearly outlining the method and then discussing related work and alternatives, would greatly improve readability. The notation is occasionally ambiguous, with symbols like *K* and *V* being used before their definitions. Several phrasing issues exist (e.g., “minimises the change to Frobenius norm”), and specific references ([26]) are presented in a way that requires readers to consult external sources for crucial details, hindering understanding. Ambiguous terms, such as “weight” on page 4, require clarification. 

Crucially, the experimental evaluation is limited. The paper lacks comparisons to other relevant subset selection methods mentioned in the introduction (e.g., those in [4, 5, 6, 7, 9]), making it difficult to assess the practical benefits of using DPPs over alternative approaches. While the comparison to K-Ascent is valuable, it doesn't demonstrate a compelling advantage over existing, potentially simpler, methods for subset selection. The rationale behind certain implementation choices, such as not using *m<sub>ij</sub>* in the initialization, is not fully explained. The choice of initialization based on moment matching is a step in the right direction, but its effectiveness compared to other initialization strategies could be explored further.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the theoretical analysis appears robust. However, the lack of thorough experimental validation and the aforementioned clarity issues prevent it from being considered a fully complete piece of work.

**Clarity:** The paper is poorly written and difficult to follow. Significant improvements in organization, notation, and phrasing are needed.

**Originality:** The approach of using eigendecomposition and an EM algorithm for DPP learning is novel and represents a significant contribution to the field. The method offers a new perspective on addressing the challenging problem of kernel learning for DPPs.

**Significance:** The results are potentially important, as they provide a more effective method for learning DPPs and could lead to improved performance in various applications requiring diverse subset selection. However, the lack of comparison to alternative methods limits the demonstrable impact of this work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to DPP learning.
*   Demonstrated improvement over a baseline (K-Ascent).
*   Potential for significant impact in applications requiring diverse subset selection.
*   Well-grounded in existing DPP theory.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Poor clarity and presentation.
*   Limited experimental evaluation and lack of comparison to alternative methods.
*   Several ambiguous statements and unclear rationales.
*   Sloppy writing and inconsistent notation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising approach to learning DPPs, but its impact is currently limited by its presentation and experimental validation. While the technical contributions are significant, the clarity issues and lack of comprehensive comparisons to existing methods hinder its overall quality. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to resubmit after substantial revisions addressing the clarity concerns and expanding the experimental evaluation. The authors should prioritize a clearer presentation of the method, more thorough experimental comparisons, and a more detailed justification of their implementation choices.
