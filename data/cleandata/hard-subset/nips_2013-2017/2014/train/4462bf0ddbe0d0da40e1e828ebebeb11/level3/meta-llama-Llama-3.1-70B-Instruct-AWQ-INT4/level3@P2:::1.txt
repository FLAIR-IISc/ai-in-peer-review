This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), a probabilistic model of set diversity. The main contribution of the paper is reformulating kernel learning for DPPs using an EM framework as an alternative to direct gradient-based kernel parameter learning (K-Ascent, or KA).

The authors provide a clear and concise background on DPPs, their applications, and the challenges associated with learning their kernel parameters. They explain how the EM algorithm works by representing any DPP as a mixture over elementary DPPs and using a variational distribution over subsets. The E-step updates this distribution as a k-DPP, and the M-step performs single gradient steps.

The empirical results show that the EM algorithm outperforms KA substantially with naive KA initialization and modestly in some settings with smarter KA initialization. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a product recommendation task, where they achieve relative gains of up to 16.5% in test log-likelihood compared to KA.

However, the M-step is fairly involved and requires caveats/approximations, which weakens the claimed elegance of the method. The authors could have provided more insight into the trade-offs between the EM algorithm and KA, particularly in terms of computational complexity and convergence rates.

One notable omission is the analysis of initialization effects. It would be interesting to see whether initializing the EM algorithm from KA solutions yields improvements in performance. Additionally, the authors could have explored other initialization strategies to better understand the robustness of their approach.

The use of "relative log likelihood" as a metric is also concerning. While it provides a sense of the relative performance of the two algorithms, it can be difficult to interpret. Reporting log-probability differences would provide a more straightforward comparison.

The writing style is clear and concise, but there are some minor issues. The authors' enthusiasm for their approach is evident, but phrases like "elegant" could be avoided. The bolding in the introduction is also unnecessary and detracts from the overall readability of the paper.

Overall, this is an interesting and promising approach to learning DPPs. While the empirical gains are modest, the authors demonstrate the potential of their method in a real-world application. With some additional analysis and refinement, this work could make a significant contribution to the field.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel EM algorithm for learning DPPs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The authors provide a clear and concise background on DPPs and their applications.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the EM algorithm in a real-world application.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The M-step is fairly involved and requires caveats/approximations, which weakens the claimed elegance of the method.
* The paper lacks analysis of initialization effects and other initialization strategies.
* The use of "relative log likelihood" as a metric is concerning and could be replaced with log-probability differences.

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns mentioned above, particularly the analysis of initialization effects and the use of a more interpretable metric. With some refinement, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
