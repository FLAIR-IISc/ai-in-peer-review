This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from data. DPPs are powerful probabilistic models for diverse subsets, parameterized by a positive semi-definite (PSD) kernel matrix L, where learning L via maximum likelihood is NP-hard. Previous work (e.g., Kulesza & Taskar, UAI 2011; ICML 2012) has sidestepped this difficulty by learning only restricted parameters of L, such as scalar weights for each item or weights for a linear combination of fixed kernels. While these approaches are convex, they fundamentally limit the model's ability to learn the rich, item-to-item similarity structure that governs diversity. The key contribution of this paper is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that enables learning of the full, unconstrained kernel matrix. The authors achieve this by re-parameterizing the kernel into its eigenvalues and eigenvectors and introducing a latent variable corresponding to the selection of eigenvectors, which facilitates an effective EM-style optimization procedure.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a significant advancement in the area of learning DPPs. To my knowledge, this is the first practical algorithm for learning the full, unconstrained kernel matrix from data. This directly addresses a major limitation of prior work and allows DPPs to capture both item quality and item similarity in a fully data-driven manner. The ability to learn the eigenvectors, which encode the notion of similarity/diversity, is a crucial step towards realizing the full potential of DPPs in applications.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical contributions are sound and elegant.
    *   The core idea of re-parameterizing the kernel into its eigendecomposition and framing the problem in an EM framework is highly innovative. This reframing circumvents the non-convexity of the original problem in a principled way.
    *   The use of Jensen's inequality to derive a tractable lower bound on the log-likelihood is standard for EM but very well-executed here. The derivation is clear and insightful.
    *   A key technical strength is the method for updating the eigenvectors. Instead of using a naive projection step, which the authors correctly argue can destroy the learned structure, they map the orthogonality constraint (VᵀV = I) to an optimization problem on the Stiefel manifold. This is a sophisticated and appropriate solution that preserves the integrity of the learned representation.
    *   The paper contains another solid contribution in proving that the posterior distribution over the latent variables, q(J|Yi), is itself a conditional DPP (a k-DPP). This insight allows for efficient computation during the E-step.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated in the introduction, and the background on DPPs is concise yet sufficient. The step-by-step derivation of the EM algorithm in Section 3 is a model of clarity, making a complex optimization procedure easy to follow. The connection between the eigendecomposition, the generative view of DPPs, and the introduction of the latent variable J is explained intuitively.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation provides strong support for the proposed method. The comparison against projected gradient ascent ("K-Ascent") is a natural and effective baseline.
    *   The experiments demonstrate that the EM algorithm is more robust, especially when starting from a generic initialization (Wishart) or in data-poor settings. It consistently achieves better test log-likelihoods.
    *   The qualitative example from the baby registry dataset is particularly compelling. The fact that the baseline method suggests three different baby monitors while the EM-trained model produces a more diverse and practical set clearly illustrates the real-world value of learning a better diversity model. This highlights that the proposed method successfully balances quality and diversity, whereas the baseline collapses towards a quality-only model.
    *   The analysis showing that the EM algorithm is also asymptotically and empirically faster than K-Ascent is another significant practical advantage.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**

1.  **Scope of Experiments:** While the product recommendation experiment is well-executed and provides a convincing case study, the paper's claims about the general applicability of the method would be stronger if supported by experiments on a wider range of real-world datasets. The introduction mentions document summarization and image search as key applications for DPPs; demonstrating the method's effectiveness on at least one other domain would substantially bolster its perceived impact.

2.  **Conclusion:** The conclusion is somewhat brief and feels a bit hasty. It effectively summarizes the main findings but misses an opportunity to reiterate some of the key technical innovations (e.g., the Stiefel manifold approach) or to discuss potential limitations and interesting avenues for future research. A slightly more detailed conclusion would make the paper feel more complete.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental and important contribution to the literature on determinantal point processes. It tackles the core, difficult problem of learning the full DPP kernel and proposes a novel, technically sound, and empirically validated solution. The strengths—originality, technical depth, clarity, and strong experimental results—far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The proposed EM algorithm is likely to be widely used by researchers and practitioners who want to apply DPPs without being constrained by restrictive, pre-specified kernel structures. The paper is a clear advancement of the state of the art and is well-suited for publication at this conference.
