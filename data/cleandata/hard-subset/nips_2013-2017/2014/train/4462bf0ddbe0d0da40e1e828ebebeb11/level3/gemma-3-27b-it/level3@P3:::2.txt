This paper presents an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) by focusing on eigendecomposition and avoiding direct optimization of the kernel matrix entries. The approach is a valuable contribution to subset selection, addressing a known NP-hard problem by cleverly leveraging the DPP’s structure. However, the paper suffers from clarity issues and a limited experimental evaluation, hindering a full assessment of its practical impact.

The authors correctly identify the difficulty of directly learning the kernel matrix *L* of a DPP and build upon previous work that focused on restricted learning settings (scalar weights or ensembles of fixed kernels). Their key innovation lies in parameterizing *L* through its eigenvalues and eigenvectors, allowing for an EM-style optimization that bypasses the problematic projection steps inherent in naive gradient ascent. This is a conceptually sound approach, and the derivation of the algorithm appears mathematically well-founded. The use of a Jensen lower bound is a standard technique for developing EM algorithms and is appropriately applied here.

The strengths of the paper are its technical novelty and theoretical justification. The idea of switching to an eigendecomposition to sidestep the positive semi-definiteness constraint during optimization is insightful. The potential asymptotic runtime improvement over gradient ascent is also a significant point. The paper clearly demonstrates an understanding of the DPP literature and positions its contribution effectively within that context.

However, several weaknesses detract from the overall impact. First, the writing is often sloppy and difficult to follow. Notation is frequently introduced before definition (e.g., marginal kernel *K* and variable *V*), disrupting the reader’s flow. The paper interleaves method derivation with tangential discussions and alternative approaches, obscuring the core algorithm. A more structured presentation, prioritizing a clear explanation of the EM algorithm *before* delving into alternatives, would greatly improve readability. Specific phrasing issues exist, such as “minimises the change to Frobenius norm,” which should be clarified as “closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix to Q.”

Second, the experimental evaluation is limited in scope. While the comparison to a K-Ascent baseline is positive, it doesn’t sufficiently demonstrate the DPP approach’s benefits in a broader context. The paper lacks comparison to other relevant subset-selection methods mentioned in the introduction (e.g., methods based on submodularity), making it difficult to ascertain the practical advantages of the DPP approach. The datasets used, while real-world, are limited to a specific domain (baby registries).

Third, the paper includes sections that are hard to follow without referencing external work. For example, Section 3.1 references [26] regarding the Stiefel manifold optimization without providing a sufficient explanation of the approach. The authors should either outline the simpler aspects of the method directly or provide a more detailed explanation to avoid burdening the reader.

Fourth, ambiguous terms are present. The meaning of “weight” on p.4, line 189, is unclear and requires further clarification. Furthermore, the rationale behind equation (30) is questionable – why is *m<sub>ij</sub>* not used, and why not initialize with the empirical covariance matrix? The potential benefits of such an initialization should be discussed.

In summary, this is a technically sound and original paper with a potentially significant contribution to the field. However, its impact is diminished by clarity issues and a limited experimental evaluation. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the underlying theory, but the presentation needs significant refinement. The lack of comparison to a wider range of competing methods leaves the practical benefits of the proposed approach unclear.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and original approach to DPP learning.
*   Addresses a challenging NP-hard problem.
*   Potential for improved asymptotic runtime.
*   Demonstrated improvement over a simple baseline (K-Ascent).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Poor clarity and organization of the writing.
*   Limited experimental evaluation and lack of comparison to relevant methods.
*   Ambiguous terminology and questionable rationale in certain sections.
*   Reliance on external references without sufficient explanation.



I recommend acceptance with major revisions, contingent on addressing the clarity and experimental evaluation issues. Specifically, the authors should: (1) restructure the paper for improved readability, (2) expand the experimental comparisons to include more relevant subset selection methods, (3) clarify ambiguous terminology, (4) justify key design choices, and (5) provide a more accessible explanation of referenced external work.
