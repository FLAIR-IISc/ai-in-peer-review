Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses learning full-kernel determinantal point processes (DPPs), where maximizing likelihood over the kernel entries is non-convex and conjectured NP-hard. Prior learning work has largely focused on restricted convex settings, e.g., learning per-item quality weights or mixtures/linear combinations of fixed DPP kernels, or parametric Bayesian models. The paper’s main contribution is to reformulate kernel learning in terms of eigenvalues/eigenvectors and to derive a generalized EM procedure. The key observation is that any DPP can be represented as a mixture over elementary DPPs (eigenvalues set to 1 for a selected subset of eigenvectors). Introducing the latent index set J of selected eigenvectors yields a tractable E-step: q(J | Y) equals the posterior p(J | Y) and is a k-DPP with a computable kernel. The M-step updates eigenvalues in closed form via posterior marginals over J and performs a single Stiefel-manifold gradient step for the eigenvectors with line search. As a baseline, the paper contrasts to projected gradient ascent on K (KA), which often stalls due to eigenvalue clipping that induces near-diagonality. On a baby registry dataset, EM substantially outperforms KA with naive Wishart initialization and modestly outperforms it with a smarter moment-matching initialization; EM is also faster asymptotically and in practice for the reported sizes.

Quality
Technical soundness: The derivation of the latent-variable view and the E-step as a k-DPP is solid and connects to standard DPP theory (mixture over elementary DPPs). The eigenvalue update follows cleanly from the lower bound, and the use of k-DPP marginals to avoid exponential sums is a nice computational insight. The eigenvector update is the weakest link: only the initial gradient is tractable, so the M-step uses a single gradient step on the Stiefel manifold with a line search that requires recomputing the E-step. This is a reasonable generalized EM strategy, but the number of caveats (recomputing q for step sizes, capping eigenvalues < 1 to avoid infinities in R, reliance on first-step gradient formula) undermines the claimed elegance and may complicate reproducibility and stability. The complexity analysis is persuasive: O(nNk^2) for the main per-iteration costs is attractive when k ≪ N; the N^3 term arises in the manifold update’s eigen computations.

Empirical evaluation: The baby registry experiments are relevant and well executed for a medium-scale setting. With Wishart initialization, EM consistently beats KA, particularly in categories with stronger off-diagonal structure (as quantified by the proposed statistic d). With moment matching, the gap narrows; in data-poor settings EM regains a clear advantage. Reporting that EM is ~2× faster than KA for these sizes strengthens the case. Missing, however, is a study of initialization effects across methods: e.g., does initializing EM from a good KA solution further improve performance? Conversely, does KA benefit from EM warm-starts?

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful algorithm boxes and complexity comparisons. Some presentation issues detract: calling the algorithm “elegant” in the text feels self-congratulatory; and boldface in the introduction is unnecessary. It would help to explicitly label the method as a generalized EM and to separate the exact steps (E-step, eigenvalue update) from the approximations (single-step eigenvector update, line search with repeated E-steps). Please also clarify numerical stability when λ approaches 1 (how tight is the cap? any observed failures?) and provide implementation details (tolerances, stopping criteria, step-size schedule, number of line-search E-steps).

Originality
The latent-variable formulation via mixtures of elementary DPPs is known in DPP theory, but deploying it as a practical EM learning procedure for full unconstrained kernels is new to my knowledge and clearly differentiated from prior convex/restricted learning approaches and from parametric Bayesian treatments. The k-DPP E-step and the eigenvalue update are particularly neat uses of known results; the Stiefel update for V in this context is a judicious choice.

Significance
Learning full DPP kernels is important for applications requiring nuanced diversity modeling. The work provides a viable and often superior alternative to naive gradient ascent on K, especially when off-diagonals matter and when initializations are not carefully engineered. The empirical gains are modest in some settings, but the method’s robustness and speed advantages make it a useful tool that others can build on. Broader impact would be strengthened by more diverse datasets and larger-scale tests.

Strengths
- Clear reformulation of DPP learning as a generalized EM in the eigenbasis.
- E-step as a k-DPP with efficient marginal computations; closed-form eigenvalue updates.
- Thoughtful complexity analysis and demonstrated runtime benefits.
- Empirical evidence that EM is more robust than KA, especially with naive initializations and limited data.
- Practical manifold optimization for eigenvectors.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- M-step for eigenvectors is involved, approximate, and requires caveats (single gradient step, repeated E-steps in line search, eigenvalue capping). This softens the “elegant” claim.
- Missing experiment on initialization interplay: EM initialized from KA (both Wishart- and moment-matching–initialized KA), and KA initialized from EM.
- Metric choice: “relative test log-likelihood” (ratios of log probabilities) is hard to interpret. Please report per-example log-likelihood differences (nats or bits), and ideally also perplexity-like measures.
- Limited domain diversity and scale; consider additional datasets (e.g., summarization, image search) and sensitivity to N, n, and k.
- Ablations: effect of multiple M-step gradient steps; cost/benefit of line search; how performance varies with the cap on λ.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the cap on λ near 1? Did you observe instabilities as λ approaches 1?
- What happens if you take multiple manifold steps per M-step? Is there a sweet spot?
- Can you cache or approximate the E-step during line search to reduce recomputation?
- Have you tried initializing EM from the moment-matching KA solution?

Writing/style issues
- Avoid calling the algorithm “elegant”; let evidence speak for itself.
- Remove bolding in the introduction.
- Be explicit that this is generalized EM and enumerate the approximations.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled EM framework tailored to DPPs with practical efficiency.
- Demonstrated robustness and speed over a common baseline.
- Potentially impactful for applications needing full-kernel DPP learning.

Cons for acceptance
- Approximate and somewhat intricate M-step undermines simplicity.
- Empirical gains are modest under good initialization; missing analyses on initialization effects and metrics hamper interpretability.
- Evaluation limited to one domain and moderate scales.

Overall assessment
An interesting and promising approach that advances full-kernel DPP learning. Despite an involved M-step and some experimental gaps, the paper is technically solid, generally clear, and shows modest to substantial empirical gains alongside better robustness and speed. I lean toward acceptance, with suggestions to strengthen the empirical analysis and tone down stylistic overstatements.
