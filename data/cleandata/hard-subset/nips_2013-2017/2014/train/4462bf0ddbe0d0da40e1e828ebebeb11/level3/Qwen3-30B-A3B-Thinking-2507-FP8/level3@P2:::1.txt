

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in Determinantal Point Process (DPP) learning: the difficulty of learning the full kernel matrix by maximizing likelihood, which is known to be NP-hard. Previous work has focused on restricted learning settings, such as learning a single weight per row of the kernel matrix or learning weights for linear combinations of fixed DPPs. The authors propose a novel EM-based approach that reformulates the learning problem by exploiting the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix. They show that a DPP can be viewed as a mixture over elementary DPPs, allowing them to develop an EM algorithm that avoids the problematic projection steps required in naive gradient ascent methods. The approach is evaluated on a real-world product recommendation task, showing significant improvements in log-likelihood over the baseline method, particularly with naive initialization.

This work builds upon the foundational DPP literature (Kulesza & Taskar, NIPS 2011, 2012; Affandi et al., ICML 2013) while addressing a key limitation of previous approaches. Unlike prior work that either restricted the parameterization of the kernel or required fixed kernel matrices, this paper offers a method for learning an unconstrained, non-parametric kernel matrix.

## Strengths
1. **Originality**: The EM reformulation of DPP learning is genuinely novel and addresses a significant limitation in the field. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior NIPS work on DPPs, particularly from Affandi et al. (2013) and Kulesza & Taskar (2012).

2. **Technical Soundness**: The theoretical development is rigorous, with detailed derivations of the E-step and M-step. The paper demonstrates that the EM approach avoids the degenerate solutions that plague gradient-based methods (KA), as evidenced by the experimental results.

3. **Significance**: The method has clear applicability to important real-world problems requiring diversity modeling (product recommendation, document summarization, etc.). The empirical results on the baby registry dataset demonstrate meaningful improvements (up to 16.5% relative gain in log-likelihood), and the paper provides a compelling case for why diversity modeling matters in practice.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and generally clear. The mathematical derivations are thorough, and the connection between the DPP mixture perspective and the EM algorithm is well-motivated.

## Weaknesses
1. **M-step Complexity**: The M-step is quite involved, requiring multiple approximations and caveats (e.g., handling the constraint V^TV = I, the need for single gradient steps rather than full optimization). This somewhat undermines the elegance the authors claim for their method.

2. **Experimental Design**: The paper lacks analysis of initialization effects—specifically, whether initializing EM from KA solutions would yield further improvements. This is a notable gap given the importance of initialization in non-convex optimization.

3. **Metric Interpretation**: The use of "relative log likelihood" (ratios of log probabilities) is problematic for interpretation. The authors should report absolute log-probability differences instead, which would be more meaningful to readers.

4. **Writing Style**: The paper contains unnecessary stylistic elements, such as bolding in the introduction, which detracts from the professionalism of the work. The authors' claim that their method is "elegant" is somewhat overstated given the complexity of the M-step.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**For Acceptance**:
- The paper addresses a significant gap in DPP learning literature
- The EM approach is theoretically sound and provides meaningful empirical improvements
- The method has clear applicability to important real-world problems
- The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior NIPS work on DPPs
- The writing is generally clear and well-organized

**Against Acceptance**:
- The M-step requires several approximations that weaken the claimed elegance
- The experimental design lacks analysis of initialization effects
- The use of relative log likelihood is misleading
- The paper makes an overstatement about the elegance of the method

## Overall Assessment
This is an interesting and promising paper that makes a valuable contribution to DPP learning. The method is novel, technically sound, and demonstrates meaningful improvements over the baseline approach on a real-world task. While the M-step complexity and experimental design have some weaknesses, these do not substantially undermine the core contribution. The paper is well-written and makes a clear case for the importance of learning the full kernel matrix for DPPs.

The paper addresses a significant challenge in DPP learning that has been acknowledged in prior work (Kulesza & Taskar, 2011, Conjecture 4.1), and the proposed EM approach provides a practical solution that avoids the degeneracy issues of previous methods. The empirical results are convincing, particularly for categories with strong negative interactions between items (d > 0.025), which aligns with the theoretical motivation.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the metric interpretation issue and to remove the overstated claim about elegance. The paper is a strong contribution to the field that advances our understanding of DPP learning while providing a practical algorithm with demonstrable improvements over existing methods.
