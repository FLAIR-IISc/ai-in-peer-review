

This paper addresses a significant challenge in learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the NP-hard problem of learning the full kernel matrix L without restrictive parameterizations. Previous work has focused on learning only scalar/vector parameters for L (e.g., [2]) or restricting L's parametric form (e.g., [3, 8]), which sacrifices the diversity dimension. The authors propose a novel EM-based learning method that parameterizes L through its eigenvalues and eigenvectors, avoiding the problematic projection steps required in gradient ascent approaches.

The main contribution is a theoretically sound EM procedure derived using Jensen's inequality, which avoids the need for projections that often lead to degenerate solutions (nearly diagonal L matrices that fail to model item diversity). The paper makes several strong technical contributions: the insightful EM derivation, the mapping of constraints to optimization on the Stiefel manifold, and the demonstration that the inverse distribution remains a DPP. The authors provide a clear, step-by-step explanation of their optimization procedure.

Experiments on synthetic data and a product recommendation task demonstrate that the proposed method achieves up to 16.5% relative gains in test log-likelihood compared to the K-Ascent baseline, while better preserving diversity. The method is also asymptotically faster than gradient ascent. The paper is well-written, with clear proofs and a coherent presentation of the technical content.

Strengths include the novel approach to the learning problem, solid theoretical contributions, and strong experimental validation. The paper's significance is demonstrated through its practical application to a real-world recommendation task where diversity preservation matters. A minor weakness is that the conclusion is somewhat hasty and omits some key contributions, and the authors could better highlight how their method differs from related work on Bayesian DPP learning [8].

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in DPP learning. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it's technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the field with clear implications for practical applications requiring diverse subset selection.
