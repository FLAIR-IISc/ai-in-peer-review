This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), a probabilistic model for diverse subset selection. The core contribution is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that circumvents the non-convexity issues inherent in directly maximizing the log-likelihood of the kernel entries, a method previously attempted via projected gradient ascent (KA). By cleverly changing the parameterization to eigenvalues and eigenvectors, and leveraging the relationship between DPPs and mixtures of elementary DPPs, the authors derive an effective optimization procedure. They demonstrate empirical improvements over KA on a product recommendation task, showcasing the algorithm's robustness and potential.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research utilizing DPPs in machine learning, including applications in recommendation systems [8, 11], document summarization [4, 5], and image search [3]. The authors correctly identify the key bottleneck in applying DPPs – learning the kernel matrix L – and note prior work's limitations in either restricting the learning process (e.g., learning a scalar weight per row [2]) or sacrificing the full DPP structure (e.g., learning weights for a linear combination of fixed kernels [3]). This paper represents a significant step towards overcoming this limitation. The connection to related work is well-established, and the problem framing is clear. 

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the EM algorithm is well-motivated and appears theoretically sound. The decomposition into eigenvalues and eigenvectors is a smart move to avoid the problematic projection step in gradient ascent. The use of a variational distribution and Jensen’s inequality is a standard and appropriate technique.
* **Novelty:** The approach of reformulating the learning problem through eigendecomposition and applying EM to the resulting structure is novel. It provides a new perspective on DPP learning and opens avenues for further research.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the baby registry dataset demonstrate that the proposed EM algorithm outperforms the baseline KA method, particularly with a naive initialization.  The comparison shows practical benefit, even if the gains aren’t massive.
* **Clear Identification of Challenges:** The authors are upfront about the limitations of the M-step and acknowledge the need for approximations, demonstrating intellectual honesty. They also point out the sensitivity to initialization and the potential for KA to get stuck in poor local optima.

**Weaknesses:**

* **M-step Complexity:** The description of the M-step and the required approximations feels somewhat complex and obscures the potential "elegance" claimed in the introduction. The reliance on approximations to handle the gradient computation and the need for a Stiefel manifold update add significant implementation complexity.
* **Limited Experimentation:** While the results are positive, the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.  The authors do not explore initializing EM from the output of KA. Assessing whether EM can refine a solution obtained by KA would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's capabilities.
* **Evaluation Metric:** The use of "relative log-likelihood" is slightly awkward.  Reporting the actual log-probability differences (rather than ratios) would be more intuitive and easier to interpret.
* **Writing Style:** The introduction contains some stylistic choices that detract from the paper's overall professionalism. The use of boldface font for emphasis is unnecessary and the claim of an "elegant" algorithm feels somewhat subjective.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles an important and challenging problem in the DPP literature.
* The proposed EM algorithm represents a significant technical contribution and offers a viable alternative to existing methods.
* The empirical results demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness.
* The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach and suggest avenues for future work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The complexity of the M-step and the approximations required may limit the practical applicability of the algorithm.
* The experimental evaluation could be more extensive.
* The writing style is somewhat uneven.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper that presents an interesting and promising approach to learning DPPs. While there are some weaknesses, the technical contribution is significant, and the empirical results are encouraging. The paper is a valuable addition to the DPP literature and addresses a key challenge in applying these models to real-world problems. I believe the strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and I **recommend acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding the writing style and potential expansion of the experimental evaluation (specifically, testing EM initialization from KA solutions).  The authors should also consider reporting log-probability differences in their results for better interpretability.
