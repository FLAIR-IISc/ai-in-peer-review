This paper proposes a novel algorithm for learning determinantal point processes (DPPs) using an expectation-maximization (EM) framework. The main contribution of this work is the reformulation of kernel learning for DPPs as an EM problem, providing an alternative to direct gradient-based kernel parameter learning. This approach is interesting and promising, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness on a real-world product recommendation task.

The method is based on the observation that any DPP can be represented as a mixture over elementary DPPs. The authors use a variational distribution over subsets and update this distribution as a k-DPP in the E-step. The M-step performs single gradient steps to update the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the kernel matrix. This approach allows the authors to avoid the problematic projection step required for naive gradient ascent to maintain positive semi-definiteness of the kernel matrix.

The empirical results show that the EM algorithm outperforms the naive gradient ascent approach (KA) substantially with naive initialization, and modestly in some settings with smarter initialization. The authors also demonstrate that the EM algorithm is asymptotically faster than KA, with a runtime of O(T1nNk2 + T1T2N3) compared to KA's O(T1nN3 + T1T2N3).

However, the M-step is fairly involved and requires caveats and approximations, which weakens the claimed elegance of the method. Specifically, the authors need to tighten the constraint on the eigenvalues to keep them slightly below 1 to avoid infinite values in the QYi matrix. Additionally, the authors do not fully optimize the eigenvectors in each M-step, instead taking a single gradient step on V. This may not be optimal, and the authors could consider using more sophisticated update techniques, such as those employed for optimization over a Stiefel manifold.

Another limitation of the paper is the lack of analysis of initialization effects. The authors only consider two initialization options, a Wishart distribution and a moments-matching initializer, and do not explore the impact of different initializations on the performance of the EM algorithm. It would be interesting to see how the EM algorithm performs with different initializations, such as initializing from KA solutions.

Furthermore, the authors use the "relative log likelihood" metric, which is the ratio of log probabilities, to evaluate the performance of the EM algorithm. However, this metric may not be the most informative, as it can be difficult to interpret. The authors could consider reporting log-probability differences instead, which may provide a more intuitive understanding of the performance of the EM algorithm.

In terms of writing and style, the authors could avoid using bolding in the introduction, as it is not necessary and can be distracting. Additionally, the authors should avoid calling the algorithm "elegant," as this is a subjective term and may not be universally agreed upon.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that proposes an interesting and promising approach to learning DPPs. While there are some limitations and areas for improvement, the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a real-world task, and the paper is a valuable contribution to the field.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and interesting approach to learning DPPs using an EM framework.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a real-world product recommendation task.
* The EM algorithm is asymptotically faster than the naive gradient ascent approach.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The M-step is fairly involved and requires caveats and approximations.
* The authors do not fully optimize the eigenvectors in each M-step.
* The paper lacks analysis of initialization effects.
* The authors use a potentially uninformative metric to evaluate the performance of the EM algorithm.

Rating: 8/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the limitations mentioned above, including providing more analysis of initialization effects and considering alternative metrics for evaluating the performance of the EM algorithm.
