## Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper presents a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP). The approach cleverly utilizes eigendecomposition to circumvent the non-convexity issues inherent in directly maximizing the log-likelihood, employing a Jensen lower bound and Stiefel-manifold optimization. The problem addressed – efficient DPP learning – is a significant one in subset selection, and the proposed method represents a meaningful contribution to the field, offering a potential alternative to existing approaches that rely on restrictive parameterizations or partial learning of the kernel.

The core idea is well-founded and the technical development appears sound. The use of eigendecomposition and the resulting simplification of the optimization problem are particularly noteworthy. The paper correctly identifies the limitations of previous work, such as the convex constraints imposed by [2] and the storage requirements of [3], and positions its contribution as a more general and flexible learning framework. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating gains over a simple K-Ascent baseline, is limited in scope. Crucially, it lacks comparison to other relevant subset selection methods mentioned in the introduction (e.g., Bayesian approaches [8], or methods based on submodular optimization). Without these comparisons, the practical benefits of the proposed DPP approach remain unclear. 

Furthermore, the write-up is surprisingly sloppy and difficult to follow. Notation is often introduced before being defined (e.g., marginal kernel K and variable V), and phrasing is occasionally unclear ("minimises the change to Frobenius norm"). The paper’s structure is also problematic, interleaving method derivation with side remarks and alternative approaches, obscuring the main line of reasoning. Section 3.1, detailing the K-Ascent baseline, relies heavily on a citation ([26]) without adequately explaining the referenced technique, forcing the reader to consult the original source. Ambiguous terms, like “weight” on page 4, require clarification. Finally, the rationale behind certain choices, such as the omission of *m<sub>ij</sub>* in equation (30) and the lack of initialization with the empirical covariance matrix, is not adequately explained.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Technically sound, original contribution to DPP learning, addresses a challenging problem.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Limited experimental evaluation, poor clarity of exposition, lack of comparison to relevant baselines, sloppy writing.

**Overall:** This paper presents a technically solid and original contribution, but requires substantial revisions to improve its clarity, strengthen its experimental validation, and address the identified weaknesses. I recommend rejection in its current form, but encourage resubmission after addressing these concerns.
