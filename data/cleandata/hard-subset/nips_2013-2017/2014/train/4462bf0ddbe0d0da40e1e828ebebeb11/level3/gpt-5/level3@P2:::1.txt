Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the long-standing problem of learning the full kernel of a determinantal point process (DPP) from observed subsets by maximum likelihood. Direct optimization over kernel entries is nonconvex and conjectured NP-hard, and naive projected gradient ascent (KA) often collapses to nearly diagonal solutions due to PSD/eigenvalue constraints. Prior work has largely avoided full-kernel learning by: (i) learning per-item scalar weights while holding similarities fixed (convex but limited) [e.g., Kulesza & Taskar, UAI 2011], (ii) learning mixtures/linear combinations of fixed DPPs [e.g., Lin & Bilmes, UAI 2012], or (iii) Bayesian learning under parametric assumptions [e.g., Affandi et al., NIPS/ICML]. This paper proposes an alternative parameterization and optimization: express the marginal kernel K via its eigendecomposition (eigenvalues λ and eigenvectors V), view a DPP as a mixture over elementary DPPs (indexed by a latent set J of eigenvectors), and apply an EM-style lower-bounding procedure.

Concretely, the E-step sets q(J|Yi) to the posterior pK(J|Yi), which the authors show is a k-DPP with a closed-form (non-marginal) kernel built from V and λ. The M-step yields a closed-form update for λ via k-DPP marginals and a gradient step for V on the Stiefel manifold using a first-order multiplicative update; in practice only a single gradient step is taken and step sizes are chosen by checking improvements in the true likelihood. The method avoids PSD/eigenvalue projection, aims to preserve non-diagonal structure, and has per-iteration complexity O(n N k^2) for the E/M computations plus O(N^3) for manifold updates (with k = max set size), which can be favorable to KA’s O(n N^3) inverses.

Experiments on an Amazon baby registry dataset across 13 categories compare EM to KA under two initializations: a Wishart-based random PSD kernel and a moment-matching initializer (using empirical first- and second-order marginals). With the Wishart initializer, EM improves median test log-likelihood by about 3.7% on average and substantially more on categories with stronger off-diagonal structure; with moment matching, EM and KA are similar on average, but in a data-poor regime (n ≈ 2N) EM shows notable gains (up to 16.5%). EM is also about 2.1× faster wall-clock than KA in these settings.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivations are clear and consistent with DPP theory: the mixture interpretation, the k-DPP posterior in the E-step, the eigenvalue update via marginals, and the Stiefel-manifold update for V. However, the M-step is approximate: only a single gradient step is taken for V and step sizes are chosen by checking the true likelihood, so standard EM monotonicity guarantees on the lower bound are weakened. Convergence properties (to critical points of the likelihood or lower bound) are not established.
- Empirical evaluation: The dataset is real and relevant, and the study isolates the effect of initialization. That said, baselines are limited to KA; missing are comparisons to prior DPP learning approaches (per-item weighting, fixed-L mixtures, Bayesian parametric learning) and to simple quality-only models. The analysis of when EM helps (strong off-diagonals) is insightful. Reporting “relative test log-likelihood” is unconventional; absolute log-likelihood differences would be easier to interpret.
- Reproducibility: The algorithm is specified with pseudo-code and released code/data are promised. Details on stopping criteria, step-size heuristics, and the λ < 1 safeguard are provided at a high level.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with helpful equations and algorithms. The exposition around the k-DPP posterior and computational costs is clear. Minor writing/style issues: avoid subjective language (e.g., calling the algorithm “elegant”) and unnecessary bolding in the introduction. Clarify the definition of “relative” log-likelihood and report additive differences. A short discussion of convergence behavior (what is guaranteed to increase: lower bound vs. true likelihood) would help.

Originality
Learning the full DPP kernel via an EM procedure in the eigenbasis, with an E-step that is a k-DPP and a Stiefel-manifold update for eigenvectors, is novel to my knowledge. The work builds meaningfully on known DPP structure (elementary DPP mixture, k-DPP marginals) and connects it to variational optimization. This differs from prior NIPS/UAI/ICML work that either constrained the kernel or used mixtures/parametric Bayesian models.

Significance
Being able to learn full DPP kernels is important for applications where negative correlations drive performance (diverse recommendation, summarization, sensor placement). The empirical gains are modest on average but substantial in some categories and in low-data regimes. The method is also faster than KA under the tested settings. Limitations include unclear scalability to very large N (due to repeated O(N^3) manifold updates), the lack of stronger theoretical guarantees, and missing baselines.

Strengths
- Conceptually neat EM formulation leveraging DPP structure; avoids problematic PSD/eigenvalue projection.
- Closed-form eigenvalue update via k-DPP marginals; principled manifold update for eigenvectors.
- Empirical gains in realistic setting, especially where off-diagonal structure matters and with limited data.
- Competitive or better runtime than KA; clear analysis of when KA fails (near-diagonal trapping).

Weaknesses
- M-step is approximate; no convergence guarantees beyond empirical checks; reliance on multiple E-steps for step-size search.
- Limited baselines; no comparison to prior constrained learning methods or Bayesian approaches.
- Metric/reporting: “relative log-likelihood” is hard to interpret; absolute differences preferred.
- Scalability to larger N not demonstrated; experiments are moderate-scale (N≈71).
- Missing analysis of initialization effects across methods (e.g., initializing EM from a KA solution and vice versa).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines: per-item weight learning, mixtures of fixed L, and a parametric Bayesian learner.
- Report additive test log-likelihood differences and confidence intervals; include per-category ablations.
- Study initialization: start EM from converged KA and start KA from converged EM; analyze sensitivity.
- Provide a brief convergence discussion and, if possible, a monotonicity guarantee for the practical variant.
- Explore regularization (e.g., spectrum or sparsity penalties) and larger-N settings with low-rank structure to reduce O(N^3) costs.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and principled approach to a hard, relevant problem; leverages DPP theory elegantly.
- Demonstrated robustness and speed gains; meaningful improvements in realistic and low-data regimes.
- Clear algorithms and public code/data facilitate impact.

Arguments against acceptance
- Approximate M-step with limited theory; unclear convergence guarantees.
- Comparatively narrow empirical study and baselines; unclear advantage over other established DPP learners.
- Scalability to large ground sets not shown.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. Interesting and promising method with a solid theoretical foundation and encouraging results; broader empirical comparisons and clearer convergence/scalability analysis would strengthen the paper.
