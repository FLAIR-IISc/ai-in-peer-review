

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework for learning the full kernel matrix of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a key limitation in previous work. The authors recognize that while DPPs are powerful for modeling diversity, learning the kernel matrix from data has been challenging due to the non-convexity of the log-likelihood objective and the NP-hardness conjecture. Previous approaches either restricted learning to single weights per row (Kulesza and Taskar, 2011) or learned weights for linear combinations of fixed DPPs (Affandi et al., 2013), both of which sacrificed either flexibility or computational properties. The authors' key insight is that any DPP can be represented as a mixture over elementary DPPs, enabling an EM formulation that avoids the problematic projection steps required by naive gradient ascent (referred to as K-Ascent or KA).

The method reformulates the learning problem using the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel matrix K, introducing a hidden variable corresponding to the set of eigenvectors used to generate a sample. The E-step computes a k-DPP distribution over subsets, while the M-step updates the eigenvalues in closed form and performs a single gradient step on the eigenvectors. The authors demonstrate this approach on a real-world product recommendation task using baby registry data, showing EM outperforms KA substantially with naive initialization (Wishart) and modestly with improved initialization (moment matching).

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear theoretical foundation. The EM framework elegantly avoids the projection issues that plague gradient-based methods. The authors provide sufficient theoretical details and algorithmic descriptions, though the M-step's reliance on approximations (single gradient steps rather than full optimization) and the need for careful handling of the Stiefel manifold constraint somewhat weaken the claimed elegance. The empirical results are well-conducted, with appropriate baselines and meaningful analysis of initialization effects. However, the paper lacks an analysis of whether initializing EM from KA solutions (with better initializations) yields further improvements, which would strengthen the comparison.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, though the authors' claim of "elegance" feels overstated given the M-step's complexities. The introduction contains unnecessary bolding that distracts from the content. The use of "relative log likelihood" (ratios of log probabilities) is confusing; reporting absolute log-probability differences would make the results more interpretable. The algorithms are clearly presented, though the derivation of Equation (20) and its computational complexity could be more transparent.

**Originality**: The paper makes a significant contribution by extending DPP learning beyond restricted settings. While the EM framework for DPPs builds on standard techniques, the specific application to unconstrained kernel learning represents a novel approach to a well-known problem. The connection between DPPs and mixtures of elementary DPPs is well-motivated and not previously exploited for learning. The work clearly differentiates itself from prior NIPS contributions on DPPs (e.g., Affandi et al., 2013; Kulesza and Taskar, 2012).

**Significance**: The method addresses an important practical limitation in DPP applications, particularly where modeling negative item interactions is crucial (e.g., product recommendation). The empirical gains, while modest in some settings, are meaningful (up to 16.5% relative improvement), and the runtime advantage (2.1x faster on average) is practically significant. The paper provides a useful alternative to existing approaches, particularly for datasets where KA fails due to poor initialization.

**Strengths**: The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging learning problem. The empirical analysis is thorough and well-motivated, with insightful analysis of when EM's advantages manifest (correlating with the importance of off-diagonal kernel entries). The runtime analysis and practical demonstration on real-world data strengthen the contribution.

**Weaknesses**: The M-step's complexity undermines the elegance claim. The paper lacks a critical experiment on whether initializing EM from KA solutions (with better initializations) would further improve results. The metric choice ("relative log likelihood") is confusing, and the authors' self-description as "elegant" is unwarranted given the method's implementation details.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that advances the state of the art in DPP learning. While the empirical gains are modest in some settings, the method provides a more robust and theoretically sound alternative to gradient-based approaches. The paper's technical quality is strong, and the contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS. With minor revisions to address the metric choice and remove the unsupported "elegant" claim, this would be an excellent paper. The current version, while not perfect, represents a meaningful contribution to the literature on probabilistic models for diverse subset selection.
