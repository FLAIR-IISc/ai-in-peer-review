This paper tackles a fundamental problem in subset selection – learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) – offering a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) approach. The core idea of leveraging the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix *L* to circumvent the problematic projection step inherent in naive gradient ascent is compelling and represents a significant departure from previous work. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing methods (scalar weighting, L-ensemble, Bayesian approaches) and convincingly argue that their EM algorithm avoids the pitfalls of these approaches, particularly the tendency to learn nearly diagonal kernels which fail to capture crucial diversity-inducing negative interactions.

The technical presentation is largely sound, with clear definitions and derivations. The connection to existing DPP theory is well-established, and the use of the marginal kernel *K* simplifies the analysis. The step-by-step development of the EM algorithm, including the derivation of the update rules for both eigenvalues and eigenvectors, is commendable. The theoretical justification for the asymptotic runtime advantage over gradient ascent is also a strong point.

However, while the novelty is high, the validation feels somewhat limited. The experiments, centered around baby registry data, provide a reasonable proof-of-concept but lack broader generalization. The comparison to K-Ascent is valuable, but the experimental design feels slightly skewed – initializing KA with a Wishart distribution inherently disadvantages it, and the moments-matching initialization largely closes the performance gap. A more rigorous comparison, perhaps including a wider range of datasets and initialization strategies, would strengthen the claims. Furthermore, while the authors acknowledge the limitations of simplifying the eigenvector update, a more detailed exploration of the impact of this simplification would be beneficial. The discussion of the Stiefel manifold update feels somewhat brief; elaborating on the practical challenges and potential alternatives would improve the paper.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The EM algorithm represents a genuinely new approach to learning DPPs, avoiding the limitations of prior methods.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivations are clear and well-justified, demonstrating a strong understanding of DPP theory.
*   **Problem Importance:** Learning DPPs is a core problem with applications across a wide range of domains.
*   **Clear Explanation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Identified Key Issue:** Correctly diagnoses the problem with gradient ascent and proposes a solution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Validation:** Experiments are focused on a single domain (baby registries).
*   **Biased Comparison:** Initialization strategies for K-Ascent appear to be chosen to favor the EM algorithm.
*   **Simplification of Eigenvector Update:** The impact of simplifying the eigenvector update is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Brief Discussion of Stiefel Manifold:**  The complexities of the Stiefel update are glossed over.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for learning DPPs.
*   The approach addresses a significant limitation of existing methods.
*   The experiments, while limited, provide a promising proof-of-concept.
*   The algorithm’s potential for scalability (asymptotic runtime advantage) is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental validation is not comprehensive enough to fully support the claims.
*   The comparison to K-Ascent is potentially biased.
*   The simplification of the eigenvector update could affect performance.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. While the experimental validation is not exhaustive, the technical soundness and theoretical justification of the EM algorithm are compelling. The authors have identified a key issue in DPP learning and proposed a clever solution. I lean heavily towards acceptance, recognizing that further validation is desirable but not essential given the significance of the conceptual advance. I would encourage the authors to expand the experimental section in a future iteration of this work.



**Rating:** Accept.
