Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses maximum likelihood learning of determinantal point processes (DPPs) without restrictive parameterizations on the kernel. DPPs are used for subset selection tasks balancing item quality and diversity, with applications in summarization, sensor placement, image search, and recommendations. Prior learning approaches have optimized limited parameterizations (e.g., per-item quality weights) or mixtures of fixed DPPs, or used Bayesian estimation for parametric kernels, none of which provide full, unconstrained learning of the DPP kernel while retaining DPP tractability.

The authors propose an EM-style algorithm that works in the eigendecomposition space of the marginal kernel K, leveraging the mixture-of-elementary-DPP representation where a subset of eigenvectors J acts as a latent variable. The E-step sets q(J|Y) to the posterior and shows it is a k-DPP with a closed-form kernel, enabling efficient marginal computations. The M-step updates eigenvalues in closed form via posterior marginals and updates eigenvectors with a single gradient step constrained to the Stiefel manifold using a matrix exponential update. This approach avoids the PSD/eigenvalue projection step required by naive projected gradient ascent on K, which the authors argue can induce near-diagonal degeneracy.

They compare the proposed EM to projected gradient ascent (KA) on real-world baby registry data (13 categories), evaluating test log-likelihood and runtime. EM shows median test log-likelihood gains of ~3.7% with a Wishart initialization and ~0.4% with a moment-matching initialization; in a data-poor regime (n≈2N), EM’s advantage increases (average 4.5%). EM is on average about 2.1× faster. They correlate EM’s gains with a measure of off-diagonal strength in data co-occurrence statistics, supporting the claim that EM better preserves negative correlations.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The derivation of the EM lower bound via Jensen’s inequality is standard. The E-step q(J|Y) = pK(J|Y) is correct and the identification of q as a k-DPP is useful and computationally enabling. The eigenvalue update reduces to posterior inclusion probabilities q(j∈J|Y), computable in O(Nk^2) per example via k-DPP marginalization, avoiding exponential sums. The eigenvector update uses a first-step gradient formula valid at q=p and a Stiefel manifold update; in practice, the algorithm performs a single gradient step with backtracking line search and recomputes the E-step to ensure the likelihood increases. This is consistent with generalized EM; while a formal convergence proof is not provided, the monotonicity check on L with E-steps at each trial step supports convergence to a stationary point under standard conditions.
- Complexity analysis is plausible: KA requires O(nN^3) per iteration for matrix inverses and projections; the EM method reduces per-iteration data-dependent cost to O(nNk^2) plus O(N^3) for eigendecompositions in step-size searches, advantageous when k ≪ N.
- Experimental methodology: The dataset is realistic and sufficiently large across multiple categories. Comparisons are against a well-defined baseline (KA) under two initializations. Results include both accuracy (test likelihood) and speed. However, the evaluation is limited to a single domain and baseline. There is no comparison to alternative learning formulations (e.g., Bayesian parametric DPP learning or mixtures of DPPs), and no synthetic recovery experiments to validate estimation quality. Some claims (e.g., KA projection causing near-diagonal solutions) would benefit from quantitative diagnostics (spectra, off-diagonal norms).
- Assumptions and limitations are acknowledged: numerical care for λ close to 1; gradient for V uses the initial derivative; proofs and some derivations are deferred to the supplement.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear, with a logical progression from background to algorithms and experiments. Notation is dense but standard for DPPs. Key formulas (e.g., for q’s kernel, eigenvalue updates, and eigenvector gradients) are stated explicitly, though some derivations and proofs are deferred. Suggestions:
- Move at least the statement and intuition for Lemma 1 and the derivations of Equations (19) and (20) into the main text, or provide more intuition.
- Clarify the construction of IY and sign conventions around log|det|.
- Detail stopping criteria, line search parameters, and how λ is kept below 1 in practice to aid reproducibility.

Originality:
The EM algorithm for learning a full, unconstrained DPP kernel via the eigen-decomposition with a latent selection of eigenvectors is, to the best of my knowledge, novel relative to prior work on per-item weights, mixtures of fixed DPPs, and Bayesian parametric approaches. The use of k-DPP structure for efficient E-step computations and closed-form eigenvalue updates is an elegant contribution. The Stiefel-constrained update for eigenvectors is a standard tool applied appropriately here.

Significance:
Learning full DPP kernels is a long-standing challenge due to nonconvexity and PSD constraints. The proposed method offers a practical alternative to projected gradient with better robustness to initialization, improved runtime for small k, and empirical gains on a real dataset. Given the wide applicability of DPPs, this has potential impact for practitioners and researchers. The significance would be strengthened by broader evaluations and comparisons to other learning strategies.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Novel EM formulation leveraging the mixture-of-elementary DPP view and k-DPP structure.
- Avoids problematic PSD/eigenvalue projections; uses principled manifold-constrained updates.
- Improved asymptotic and observed runtime when k ≪ N.
- Empirical gains in test likelihood and robustness across realistic tasks; analysis linking gains to off-diagonal structure.
- Clear algorithms and practical considerations; code availability indicated.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Empirical evaluation limited to a single application domain and a single baseline (KA); no comparison to Bayesian/parametric or mixture-based alternatives.
- No synthetic recovery experiments or ablations (e.g., effect of the V-step approximation, step size strategy).
- Some technical details deferred to the supplement; no formal convergence guarantees beyond generalized EM monotonicity checks.
- Sensitivity to initialization and hyperparameters not deeply analyzed; numerical stability near λ≈1 only briefly addressed.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add comparisons to parametric Bayesian DPP learning and mixture-of-DPP approaches on likelihood and downstream tasks.
- Include synthetic experiments to assess recovery of known kernels and sensitivity to k, N, and sample size.
- Provide diagnostics of learned kernels (eigenvalue spectra, off-diagonal norms) and an ablation on the V-step.
- Expand discussion on convergence properties and numerical stability, including conditioning of HY and computational constants.

Overall recommendation:
A solid, technically sound and novel contribution to DPP learning with practical benefits. I recommend acceptance, contingent on strengthening the empirical comparisons and adding more diagnostic analysis.
