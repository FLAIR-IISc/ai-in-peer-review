Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an EM-style algorithm for maximum likelihood learning of determinantal point processes (DPPs) without restrictive parameterizations of the kernel. Instead of optimizing entries of the marginal kernel K directly (which needs eigenvalue projections to maintain 0 ≤ K ≤ I and often degenerates to near-diagonal matrices), the authors reparameterize K via its eigen-decomposition K = VΛV^T and introduce a latent variable J indexing subsets of eigenvectors. Leveraging the mixture interpretation of DPPs as mixtures over elementary DPPs (all eigenvalues equal to 1 on J), they derive a lower bound on the log-likelihood via Jensen and perform coordinate ascent.

E-step: q(J|Y) is set to the true posterior pK(J|Y), which the authors show is a k-DPP with a closed-form kernel QY. This enables efficient computation of marginals q(j ∈ J | Y) using k-DPP marginalization in O(Nk^2).

M-step: Eigenvalues admit a closed-form update λj = (1/n) Σi q(j ∈ J | Yi), self-normalizing into [0,1]. Eigenvectors are updated by a single gradient step on the Stiefel manifold (V^TV=I), using a simplification valid at q=p (the initial gradient), with a line search that enforces likelihood improvement. The overall complexity is O(T1 n N k^2 + T1 T2 N^3), where k is the maximum set size, T1 iterations and T2 line-search steps.

The method is compared to a projected gradient ascent baseline on K, showing better robustness to initialization and data scarcity, and faster runtime in practice. Prior work largely learned item-qualities only (diagonal scaling) or mixtures of fixed DPPs, or used Bayesian inference under parametric forms; this work aims to learn a full, nonparametric K while preserving DPP structure and tractable operations.

Strengths
- Sound exploitation of the eigenstructure of DPPs: the hidden-variable construction via J and the k-DPP posterior are elegant and computationally beneficial.
- Clear advantage over direct projected gradient on K: avoids degeneracy to near-diagonal kernels that suppress diversity modeling; better robustness to poor initializations and small-data regimes.
- Computational contribution: reduces the dominant cost from repeated N×N inverses to O(Nk^2) operations via k-DPP structure, with empirical speedups (~2×) on moderate N.
- Practical empirical evaluation: real-world baby-registry dataset across 13 categories; evaluation via held-out test log-likelihood; analysis relating performance gains to off-diagonal signal (diversity strength).
- Careful engineering choices: Stiefel manifold update for eigenvectors; self-normalizing eigenvalue updates; reasonable initialization strategies (Wishart, moment-matching).

Weaknesses
- M-step for eigenvectors uses a gradient valid only at q=p; beyond the first step, the exact gradient of the EM lower bound is intractable and replaced by a generalized EM step with a likelihood-increasing line search. While practical, theoretical convergence guarantees (e.g., to a stationary point of the true likelihood) are not provided.
- Evaluation breadth: a single application/domain; no comparisons to stronger learning baselines (e.g., parametric L-learning or Bayesian methods) or recent structured/low-rank DPP learners; no large-scale experiments (N in the thousands).
- Some notation and derivations are terse, with proofs deferred to the supplement. In particular, dimensions and construction of QY, and the conditions under which Eq. (20) holds, could be clarified.
- The reported average log-likelihood gains over a good moment-matching initialization are small (0.4%) in the data-rich setting; the method’s main advantage emerges particularly under poor initialization or scarce data.
- Computational analysis underestimates the cost of repeated E-steps during line search; and the N^3 cost of matrix exponentials for Stiefel updates may be limiting for larger N unless more efficient retractions are used.

Quality
The technical development is largely sound, with correct use of DPP mixture structure and k-DPP marginals. The eigenvalue update is clean and efficient, and the E-step characterization appears correct. The eigenvector update is a practical generalized-EM step; however, the paper would benefit from a brief convergence discussion (e.g., monotone improvement of the likelihood ensured by line search, conditions for convergence to a stationary point). Empirical results support claims of robustness and speed against a sensible baseline; broader baselines would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Suggestions:
- Clarify matrix dimensions and indexing in Lemma 1 (U, UY, ZY) and in Eq. (20) (BY, VY) to avoid ambiguity.
- Explicitly state that the algorithm is generalized EM and describe any monotonicity guarantee used in practice (checking L after each line-search step).
- Provide more detail on the k-DPP marginal computation and its O(Nk^2) cost.

Originality
Introducing a hidden-variable EM formulation based on the mixture of elementary DPPs and exploiting k-DPP posteriors for efficient updates is novel relative to prior nonparametric DPP learning approaches. The Stiefel-manifold update in this context is a thoughtful adaptation.

Significance
Learning full DPP kernels is important for applications requiring explicit diversity modeling. The method can influence practitioners who currently rely on restricted parameterizations or unstable projected gradients. The gains in small-data regimes and robustness to initialization are practically meaningful. Demonstrating effectiveness on additional domains and scales would increase impact.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you provide a brief convergence statement for the generalized EM procedure you use (e.g., Zangwill-type arguments), or at least empirical monotonicity plots?
- Could you adopt a cheaper retraction (e.g., QR/Cayley) instead of a full matrix exponential for the Stiefel update to reduce the N^3 factor?
- How sensitive is performance to the number of eigenvector update steps per M-step? Would a few steps meaningfully improve results?
- Can you compare to parametric L-learning (e.g., feature-based kernels) or Bayesian approaches on at least one domain?
- Please clarify the shapes in Lemma 1 and Eq. (20), and include pseudo-code for computing q(j ∈ J | Y) via the rank-k eigen-decomposition.

Pros
- Novel EM formulation leveraging DPP eigenstructure and k-DPP posteriors
- Robust and faster than projected gradient on K; avoids degeneracy
- Solid derivations for eigenvalue updates; practical and principled manifold updates
- Real-data evaluation with clear analysis of when it helps most

Cons
- Generalized EM without theoretical guarantees presented; eigenvector step uses an approximation
- Limited empirical scope and baselines; small gains in data-rich settings
- Potential scalability concerns due to N^3 operations in V updates

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a new and practical algorithmic approach to a longstanding challenge in DPP learning, with clear empirical benefits and a solid technical foundation. Strengthening the convergence discussion and broadening empirical validation would further solidify the contribution.
