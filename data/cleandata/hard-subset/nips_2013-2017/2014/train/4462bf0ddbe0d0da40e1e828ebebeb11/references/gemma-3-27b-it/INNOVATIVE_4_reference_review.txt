This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), specifically addressing the difficulties associated with maximizing likelihood when the kernel matrix L is unconstrained. The authors propose an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that cleverly exploits the eigendecomposition of L, circumventing the problematic projection steps required by naive gradient ascent and offering potential asymptotic speedups. This is a genuinely novel approach; while DPPs have seen increasing interest, learning L remains a major hurdle, and the EM formulation presented here provides a compelling alternative to existing methods like scalar weighting or parametric assumptions. The connection to hidden variables, framing the learning process through a generative model involving eigenvector selection, is particularly insightful.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The core idea – leveraging the eigendecomposition within an EM framework – is elegant and well-motivated. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of gradient ascent and demonstrate how their approach avoids those pitfalls. The theoretical justification for the algorithm is solid, and the analysis suggesting asymptotic speedup is promising. The experimental results, though not overwhelmingly decisive, show that the EM algorithm is more robust than K-Ascent, particularly in scenarios with limited data or strong item interactions. The inclusion of both Wishart and moment-matching initialization strategies is thoughtful, and the runtime comparison provides valuable practical insight.

However, the paper isn’t without its weaknesses. While the theoretical advantage of EM is appealing, the practical gains observed in the experiments are moderate. The discussion regarding the initialization strategies feels somewhat incomplete; the conditions under which each initialization performs best aren't fully elucidated. The complexity analysis, while present, could benefit from a more detailed breakdown of the computational costs involved in each step of the EM algorithm, especially concerning the Stiefel manifold update. The experiments are limited to a single application (baby registries), and it’s unclear how well the proposed method will generalize to other domains with different data characteristics. The presentation in sections 3.4 and 3.5 becomes quite dense, making it difficult to follow the derivations without significant effort. While the authors mention neglecting second-order information in the Stiefel manifold update, the implications of this simplification aren't thoroughly discussed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The EM algorithm for learning DPPs is a significant contribution, offering a new perspective and a potentially powerful solution to a longstanding problem.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical foundation of the algorithm is well-established, and the connection to eigendecomposition and hidden variables is insightful.
*   **Potential Impact:** If the asymptotic speedup holds true for larger datasets, this algorithm could become a standard tool for DPP learning.
*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of learning DPPs and motivates the need for a more robust and efficient algorithm.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Moderate Experimental Gains:** The improvements over K-Ascent, while statistically significant, aren’t dramatic in the presented experiments.
*   **Limited Generalizability:** The experiments are restricted to a single domain, raising questions about the algorithm’s broader applicability.
*   **Dense Presentation:** The technical details in sections 3.4 and 3.5 are difficult to follow, potentially hindering reproducibility.
*   **Incomplete Discussion of Simplifications:** The impact of neglecting second-order information in the Stiefel manifold update isn't fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its minor flaws, I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of DPP learning. The novelty of the proposed EM algorithm, coupled with its strong theoretical foundation, outweighs the limitations in the experimental results and presentation. The authors have identified a significant problem and proposed a promising solution. The potential for improved efficiency and robustness makes this work worthy of acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper, with a minor recommendation for the authors to expand the experimental evaluation to include more diverse datasets and provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational complexity of the algorithm. I also recommend they improve the clarity of sections 3.4 and 3.5 to facilitate reproducibility and understanding.
