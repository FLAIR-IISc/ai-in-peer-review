This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), circumventing the difficulties associated with directly maximizing the likelihood via gradient ascent, which necessitates problematic projection steps to maintain positive semi-definiteness. The approach leverages the eigendecomposition of the DPP kernel and introduces a hidden variable corresponding to the eigenvectors, leading to a more stable and potentially faster optimization procedure.

The core idea – utilizing the eigendecomposition and formulating an EM algorithm – is conceptually sound and addresses a known challenge in DPP learning. The paper is structured logically, building from background on DPPs to the proposed algorithm and its theoretical justification, culminating in experimental validation. The detailed derivations within the supplementary material are appreciated, enhancing reproducibility. However, a meticulous examination reveals numerous shortcomings, particularly regarding implementation details, clarity, and experimental thoroughness.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a real problem:** The instability of gradient ascent for DPP learning is a recognized issue, and the proposed EM algorithm offers a viable alternative.
*   **Sound theoretical foundation:** The utilization of eigendecomposition and the EM framework are mathematically grounded and well-explained.
*   **Clear presentation of background:** The background section provides a good overview of DPPs and their properties.
*   **Identifies a weakness of gradient ascent:** The observation that gradient ascent often leads to near-diagonal kernels is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Insufficient experimental detail:** The experimental section lacks crucial details. The choice of step size selection mechanisms in KA and EM is glossed over ("average of T2 iterations"). The precise parameter settings, hardware used, and statistical significance tests are absent. The reported gains are medians over 25 trials, but variances are not reported, hindering assessment of reliability.
*   **Implementation ambiguity:** The phrase "we simply tighten the constraint on λ to keep it slightly below 1" is vague and requires precise quantification. How is this tightening implemented? A value close to 1 might still cause numerical instability.
*   **Overly complex initialization:** The moment-matching initialization, while attempting to provide a good starting point, appears unnecessarily complex. A simpler approach, such as a scaled identity matrix, might suffice and improve clarity. The benefit of the complex initialization is not convincingly demonstrated.
*   **Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art:** The paper primarily compares against a naive gradient ascent (KA). More relevant comparisons would include other DPP learning methods, such as variational inference approaches or methods based on kernel alignment.
*   **Minor technical inaccuracies & inconsistencies:** Equation (1) states `K = N∑ j=1 λjvjv > j ⇔ L = N∑ j=1 λj 1− λj vjv > j `. The left-to-right implication is not generally true; the summation should start from 1. While minor, it demonstrates a lack of careful attention to detail. Similarly, the explanation of Equation (20) uses terms without prior definition, such as “H Yi”.
*   **Formatting issues:** The text includes instances of inconsistent spacing (e.g., around equations) and poorly formatted algorithm descriptions (e.g., inconsistent indentation). These detract from readability.
*   **Ambiguous Notation:** The use of the same variable name ‘η’ for both the step size in KA and in the V update of EM is highly confusing.
*   **Missing Proofs:** The claim of asymptotic runtime improvement is made without a detailed proof or justification, making it difficult to assess.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The proposed EM algorithm offers a promising alternative to gradient ascent for DPP learning, addressing a known challenge in the field.
*   The theoretical foundation is sound, and the paper provides a clear explanation of the algorithm.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental section is insufficiently detailed and lacks thorough comparisons to related work.
*   The implementation details are ambiguous, and the paper contains minor technical inaccuracies and formatting issues.
*   The overall presentation lacks the level of polish expected for a NIPS publication. The excessive number of small errors indicates a lack of thorough review by the authors.
*   The gains, while present, are modest, especially when using the improved initialization, leading to questions about the practical impact of the proposed algorithm.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the approach and its theoretical soundness, I recommend **rejection** due to the significant number of shortcomings in the experimental evaluation, implementation details, and presentation. The paper feels incomplete and lacks the rigor expected of a NIPS publication. While the core idea is valuable, the lack of thoroughness and detail prevents me from confidently endorsing its acceptance. The authors should address the issues raised above and provide a more comprehensive and well-documented evaluation of their method before resubmitting.
