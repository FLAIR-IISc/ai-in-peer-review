This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a known NP-hardness of maximum likelihood estimation. The core idea is to exploit the eigendecomposition of the DPP kernel matrix, avoiding the problematic projection step inherent in naive gradient ascent approaches. The paper demonstrates through experiments on a product recommendation task that the EM algorithm outperforms gradient ascent, particularly when negative interactions between items are strong.

The paper is generally well-written and addresses a relevant problem in DPP learning. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the connection to prior work is reasonably well established. However, a meticulous examination reveals numerous shortcomings, ranging from minor formatting inconsistencies to questionable methodological choices and a lack of thoroughness in the experimental evaluation. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The EM approach, leveraging eigendecomposition, offers a potentially valuable alternative to existing DPP learning methods. The avoidance of the problematic projection step is a definite plus.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The paper provides a clear derivation of the EM algorithm, grounding it in established concepts like Jensen’s inequality and auxiliary variable introduction.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the EM algorithm’s superiority over gradient ascent in certain scenarios.
*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly defines the challenge of learning DPPs and its relevance to subset selection tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Formatting & Notation:** Throughout the paper, formatting is inconsistent. For example, equations are not consistently numbered, and the notation for set differences (e.g., A ∧ B) is unconventional and jarring. The use of “Y`” is particularly confusing.
*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** While the paper highlights the issues with naive gradient ascent initialization, the impact of *different* initialization schemes on the EM algorithm’s performance isn't thoroughly investigated. The comparison of Wishart vs. moment-matching initialization is somewhat superficial. The claim that moment matching is a "perfect fix" is demonstrably false given the results in Figure 1b.
*   **Computational Complexity Claims:** The claimed asymptotic runtime advantage of the EM algorithm, O(T1nNk2 +T1T2N3), over gradient ascent, O(T1nN3 + T1T2N3), relies on the assumption that k << N. While this is plausible in some scenarios, it’s not universally true and should be explicitly stated as a condition. A more detailed analysis, perhaps with empirical timings for different values of k and N, is needed.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation lacks depth. The use of only one dataset (Amazon baby registries) limits generalizability. The reported gains of EM over KA are often small (e.g., 3.7% average gain), and the statistical significance isn’t rigorously assessed. The justification for selecting categories based on the ‘d’ parameter feels *post-hoc*. Moreover, the paper does not compare its method against other DPP learning approaches (e.g., Bayesian methods [8]) or more recent advancements.
*   **Incomplete Gradient Step:** The simplification of the eigenvector update in the M-step—using only the initial gradient—is a significant limitation. Justification for this simplification is weak, and its potential impact on convergence and accuracy needs to be discussed.
*   **Lack of Discussion on Convergence:** The paper provides limited details about the convergence criteria used for both EM and KA. What constitutes “convergence” (δ < c)? What are typical values for c? This lack of detail hinders reproducibility.
*    **Equation (23):** The clamping of the eigenvalue is done after the normalization. This normalization is not part of the equation and is not properly mentioned. 
*   **Missing Details in Lemma 1:** The proof for Lemma 1 is relegated to the supplement. While this is acceptable, the lemma itself is crucial for understanding the E-step and deserves more prominence in the main text.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The proposed EM algorithm represents a novel approach to DPP learning.
*   The paper provides a clear and theoretically sound derivation of the algorithm.
*   Experiments demonstrate improved performance compared to a baseline gradient ascent method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Numerous minor formatting and notation issues detract from the paper’s clarity.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited in scope and lacks statistical rigor.
*   The computational complexity claims require further justification.
*   Simplifications in the M-step raise concerns about convergence and accuracy.
*   The paper fails to adequately compare with existing DPP learning techniques.
*   The writing is, at times, imprecise and lacks polish.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite presenting a novel idea, the paper suffers from a multitude of flaws – both substantive and superficial – that significantly diminish its overall quality. The lack of thorough experimental evaluation, questionable methodological choices (e.g., incomplete gradient step), and numerous minor inconsistencies suggest that the work is not yet ready for publication in a top-tier conference like NIPS. 

**Recommendation:** **Reject**. The paper requires substantial revisions, including a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, a more rigorous theoretical analysis, and a thorough polishing of the writing and formatting. A comparison with other DPP learning methods is also crucial. While the core idea has merit, its presentation and validation are currently insufficient.
