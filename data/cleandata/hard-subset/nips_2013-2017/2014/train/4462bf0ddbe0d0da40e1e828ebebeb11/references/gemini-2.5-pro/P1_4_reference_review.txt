### Summary

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. Learning the full, unconstrained kernel via maximum likelihood is known to be a difficult, non-convex problem. The authors first describe a baseline approach using projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent), and insightfully diagnose its primary failure mode: the projection step required to maintain the kernel's positive semi-definite (PSD) constraints can truncate eigenvalues to zero, leading to poor, near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture the diversity-promoting nature of DPPs.

The paper's main contribution is a novel expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for this learning problem. The key idea is to re-frame the DPP as a mixture of "elementary" DPPs, which introduces a latent variable corresponding to a selection of the kernel's eigenvectors. This allows the authors to derive an EM procedure where:
1.  The E-step involves computing the posterior distribution over the latent eigenvector selections for each observed subset. The authors show this posterior is itself a tractable *k*-DPP.
2.  The M-step updates the kernel's eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The eigenvalue update has a closed-form solution that avoids any projection. The eigenvector update is performed with a single gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, which also sidesteps the problematic projection of the baseline method.

This work builds upon a line of research in learning DPPs [2, 3, 8], but it is distinct in that it tackles the full, non-parametric kernel learning problem without the restrictive assumptions of prior work (e.g., learning only quality scores [2], using L-ensembles [3], or assuming a parametric form [8]). The authors demonstrate empirically on a real-world product recommendation dataset that their EM algorithm is more robust to initialization and more accurate than projected gradient ascent, particularly when items have strong negative interactions or when data is limited. Furthermore, they show it is asymptotically and empirically faster.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The application of an EM framework to DPP kernel learning is a significant and novel contribution. The core insight of using the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs representation to introduce a latent variable is clever and transforms the difficult constrained optimization problem into a more manageable one.
2.  **Addresses a Core Problem:** The paper provides a compelling diagnosis of the failure mode of a standard and intuitive approach (projected gradient ascent). The proposed EM algorithm is not just an alternative method; it is specifically designed to overcome the identified weakness (the projection step), making the contribution highly significant.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments on the Amazon baby registry dataset are convincing.
    *   The comparison clearly demonstrates the robustness of EM over the K-Ascent baseline, especially with a random initialization.
    *   The analysis correlating EM's performance gain with a measure of off-diagonal importance (`d`) provides strong evidence for the authors' hypothesis about K-Ascent's failings.
    *   The data-poor setting experiment shows that EM is superior even when K-Ascent is given a strong, data-driven initialization.
    *   The qualitative example (three baby monitors) is an effective illustration of the practical difference between the models.
    *   The runtime comparison confirms the theoretical asymptotic advantage of the EM algorithm.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the technical development is logical, and the connection between the theory and the experiments is strong.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The only baseline is a "naive" projected gradient ascent. While the authors' critique of this method is valid, it would strengthen the paper to discuss or compare against more sophisticated constrained non-convex optimization techniques. For example, methods using more advanced line searches or second-order information might also mitigate the issues with the simple projection step. The claims of superiority are strong but are made relative to a somewhat simple competitor.
2.  **Approximation in M-step:** The M-step for the eigenvectors `V` is not a full maximization but a single gradient step (making this a Generalized EM algorithm). The authors state that the gradient formula (Eq. 20) is only exact at the beginning of the M-step. While the line search checks for an increase in the true likelihood `L`, a brief discussion of the theoretical implications of this approximation on convergence would be beneficial.
3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The overall algorithm has a complexity term of O(N³), arising from eigendecompositions and the matrix exponential update for the eigenvectors. This is acceptable for the moderate `N` (~71) in the experiments but limits the method's direct applicability to problems with very large ground sets (N >> 1000). While this is a common challenge for kernel-based methods, the paper could be more explicit about this limitation in the conclusion.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant EM-based solution to the important and difficult problem of learning a full DPP kernel.
*   Provides a clear and insightful diagnosis of the failure modes of the more straightforward projected gradient ascent approach.
*   The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated.
*   Empirical evaluation is thorough, performed on a real-world dataset, and strongly supports the claims of improved robustness, accuracy, and speed.
*   The paper is very well-written and will likely be influential for practitioners and researchers working with DPPs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The primary baseline (K-Ascent) is relatively simple, and the paper could be strengthened by comparison to more advanced constrained optimization methods.
*   The scalability of the method to large `N` is a limitation that is not fully discussed.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is an excellent paper. It identifies a key problem, proposes a novel and technically sound solution, and validates it with strong empirical results. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. This work represents a clear advance in the practical application of DPPs. I strongly recommend acceptance.
