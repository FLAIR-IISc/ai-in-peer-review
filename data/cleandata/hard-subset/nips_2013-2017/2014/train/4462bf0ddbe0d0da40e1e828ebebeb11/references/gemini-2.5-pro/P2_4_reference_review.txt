### Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

**Summary**

This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. The standard maximum likelihood objective for this problem is conjectured to be NP-hard. The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to learn the unconstrained kernel matrix, which contrasts with prior work that has focused on more restricted learning scenarios (e.g., learning weights for rows, combining fixed kernels, or using specific parametric forms).

The key idea is to leverage the eigendecomposition of the DPP kernel `K = VΛVᵀ`. By viewing a DPP as a mixture of "elementary DPPs" (each corresponding to a subset of eigenvectors), the authors introduce the eigenvector index set `J` as a hidden variable. This framing allows for the application of the EM algorithm. The E-step involves computing the posterior distribution over `J` given an observed subset `Y`, which the authors show is itself a tractable k-DPP. The M-step then involves updating the eigenvalues `Λ` and eigenvectors `V`. The eigenvalue update has a closed-form solution, while the eigenvector update is handled via a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, which elegantly enforces the orthogonality constraint. The authors compare their EM algorithm to a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) and demonstrate experimentally on a real-world product recommendation dataset that their method is more robust, less prone to poor local optima, and computationally faster.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a novel and principled approach to a difficult and highly relevant problem. Learning an unconstrained DPP kernel is a fundamental task for leveraging the full expressive power of DPPs. The proposed EM algorithm is a significant contribution that moves beyond the restrictive assumptions of previous methods. The idea of using the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs formulation to motivate the hidden variable is elegant and insightful.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical derivation of the EM algorithm is sound. The key insight that the posterior distribution in the E-step, `p(J|Y)`, is also a DPP is a non-trivial result (Lemma 1) that makes the entire approach computationally feasible. The M-step is handled carefully, with a clean, closed-form update for the eigenvalues and a well-justified gradient-based update on the Stiefel manifold for the eigenvectors. This avoids the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent, which the paper convincingly argues can lead to degenerate, near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture diversity.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the background on DPPs is concise and sufficient, and the step-by-step derivation of the EM algorithm (from introducing the hidden variable to the E and M-steps) is logical and well-explained. The algorithms are presented clearly.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the Amazon baby registry dataset provide compelling evidence for the authors' claims. The comparison with K-Ascent is well-designed, showing that EM is not only faster (as predicted by the complexity analysis) but also significantly more robust, particularly with a less-informed initialization (Wishart) or in a data-poor setting. The qualitative example of the selected "safety" items nicely illustrates the practical benefit of EM's learned model over the one from K-Ascent, which selects a less diverse set.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Approximation in the M-step:** The M-step for the eigenvectors `V` is not a full maximization but rather a single gradient step. The gradient formula used (Equation 20) is derived under the assumption that `q=p`, which is only true at the beginning of the M-step. This makes the algorithm a form of Generalized EM (GEM). While this is a common and often effective practice, the paper could be more explicit about this approximation and briefly discuss why this is a reasonable choice (e.g., by noting that each step is still guaranteed to not decrease the likelihood, as checked in the line search).

2.  **Scalability Concerns:** While the EM algorithm is shown to be asymptotically faster than the K-Ascent baseline, the `N³` complexity of the eigenvector update (due to the matrix exponential in the Stiefel manifold update) remains a bottleneck for applications with very large ground sets (`N`). The paper focuses on the comparison to KA, which is fair, but a brief discussion of this limitation and potential avenues for future work (e.g., using approximations for the matrix exponential or stochastic gradient methods) would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Limited Comparison to Other Methods:** The experimental comparison is focused solely on the K-Ascent baseline. While this is a logical and effective baseline to demonstrate the core advantages of the EM approach, it would have been interesting to see a comparison or at least a discussion of how this method relates to other state-of-the-art approaches, such as the Bayesian method of Affandi et al. (2013). This would help to better position the work within the broader literature.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The claims are well-supported by both the theoretical development and the experimental results. The authors are honest about the failings of the baseline method and provide a clear analysis of why their proposed method overcomes them.

*   **Clarity:** The writing is excellent. One minor point: the derivation of several key equations (19, 20) is deferred to the supplement. While this is necessary for space, it does mean that a full verification of the work depends on the supplementary material.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. To my knowledge, this is the first application of an EM-style algorithm for learning the full, unconstrained kernel of a DPP. The formulation is novel and provides a new and powerful tool for the DPP community. The work clearly differentiates itself from prior contributions.

*   **Significance:** The results are important and likely to be influential. DPPs are a popular model for tasks requiring diversity, and the difficulty of learning the kernel has been a major practical obstacle. This paper provides an effective and efficient algorithm that significantly advances the state of the art in this area. Researchers and practitioners working on product recommendation, document summarization, and other subset selection tasks are likely to use and build upon these ideas.

**Conclusion**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated algorithm for a fundamental problem in the literature on determinantal point processes. The work is technically solid, clearly presented, and addresses a significant limitation of prior methods. Despite minor points for improvement regarding the M-step approximation and experimental comparisons, the contributions are substantial. I recommend this paper for acceptance.
