

# Review: Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes

This paper introduces a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) approach for learning the kernel of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a fundamental limitation in existing methods. The authors identify that gradient ascent approaches for learning DPP kernels require projection steps that often produce degenerate, near-diagonal matrices, failing to capture the diversity-inducing negative interactions between items. By leveraging the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K, they develop a principled EM algorithm that avoids these problematic projections.

The paper's key insight is recognizing that DPPs can be viewed as mixtures of elementary DPPs (via their eigendecomposition), which naturally suggests an EM framework. The authors derive the E-step as a k-DPP distribution and provide closed-form updates for eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The theoretical development is sound, with clear connections to DPP properties (e.g., Equation 1 linking L and K) and proper application of Jensen's inequality for the lower bound. The computational complexity analysis shows asymptotic runtime advantages over gradient ascent methods, which is a significant practical contribution.

The experiments on a real-world baby registry dataset demonstrate clear advantages of the EM approach over the projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent). EM achieves higher test log-likelihoods (average 3.7% gain from Wishart initialization, 4.5% in data-poor settings) while being asymptotically faster (2.1x faster on average). The analysis linking the relative performance to the importance of off-diagonal kernel entries (d = 1/N ||M||_F/||diag(M)||_2) is particularly insightful, showing EM's advantage is most pronounced when negative interactions matter most.

This work stands out from previous DPP learning approaches in several ways. Unlike [2] which only learns scalar weights for rows of L, or [3] which uses L-ensembles (losing DPP properties), or [8] which uses Bayesian methods with parametric assumptions, this paper learns an unconstrained, non-parametric kernel. The EM approach is the first to avoid the problematic projection step that plagues gradient ascent methods.

The paper's strengths are its clear novelty, strong theoretical foundation, practical significance, and convincing experimental results. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., initialization sensitivity), and the experiments demonstrate the algorithm's robustness to different initializations and data conditions. The writing is clear and well-organized, with detailed pseudocode and thorough explanations of the technical developments.

Minor weaknesses include the need for a good initialization (though the authors provide two reasonable approaches), and the fact that the EM algorithm is still sensitive to the choice of step size. However, these are well-documented limitations rather than fundamental flaws.

The significance of this work is substantial. DPPs are widely used in applications requiring diverse subset selection (recommendation systems, document summarization, etc.), and this paper provides a more robust, principled method for learning them. The approach is not just an incremental improvement but represents a fundamentally different and more effective optimization strategy for DPP learning. This is particularly valuable given that learning DPPs has been recognized as a challenging problem [1, Conjecture 4.1].

Compared to recent NIPS papers on DPPs (e.g., [16, 17, 20]), this work makes a clear conceptual advance by introducing an EM framework that avoids the critical limitation of previous methods. The connection between DPPs and mixture models via eigendecomposition is novel and well-executed.

In summary, this paper makes a significant contribution by introducing a novel learning algorithm for DPPs that addresses a fundamental limitation of existing approaches. The theoretical development is sound, the experiments are convincing, and the approach has clear practical advantages. The novelty and significance of this work strongly outweigh the minor methodological limitations.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Novel EM framework for DPP learning, avoiding problematic projection steps
- Theoretical development is sound and well-motivated
- Clear experimental advantages over baseline methods in both log-likelihood and runtime
- Addresses a fundamental limitation in DPP learning previously recognized as NP-hard to optimize
- Provides a more principled approach to learning unconstrained DPP kernels

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Initialization sensitivity (though acknowledged and mitigated with two reasonable approaches)
- Slightly higher implementation complexity compared to gradient ascent
- Limited to the specific problem of learning DPP kernels (though this is a core challenge)
