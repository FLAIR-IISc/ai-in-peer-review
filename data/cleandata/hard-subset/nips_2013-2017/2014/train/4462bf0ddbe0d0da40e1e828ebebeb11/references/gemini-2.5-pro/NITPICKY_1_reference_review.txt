This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from observed subset data. Standard maximum likelihood estimation via projected gradient ascent is known to suffer from poor local optima, often collapsing to near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture the diversity-promoting properties of DPPs. The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to overcome this issue. The key idea is to leverage the eigendecomposition of the DPP kernel and view the DPP distribution as a mixture of elementary DPPs. This introduces a latent variable corresponding to a selection of eigenvectors, enabling the use of the EM framework. The authors derive E- and M-steps for this formulation, resulting in an algorithm that avoids the problematic projection step. Experiments on a baby registry product recommendation task show that the proposed EM algorithm is more robust and computationally faster than a projected gradient ascent baseline.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles a central and difficult problem in the application of DPPs: learning the full kernel from data. The proposed EM-based approach is, to my knowledge, novel and provides an elegant alternative to existing methods, which either learn restricted kernel forms or rely on problematic projection steps. This is a significant contribution that could enable wider and more effective use of DPPs.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The formulation of the learning problem within the EM framework is clever. Using the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs perspective to define a latent variable is a well-motivated and natural way to structure the optimization problem. The resulting algorithm directly optimizes eigenvalues and eigenvectors, avoiding the numerical issues associated with direct optimization of the kernel matrix entries.

3.  **Empirical Demonstration:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the proposed method's advantages. The comparison against projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) clearly shows that EM is more robust to initialization and achieves better test log-likelihoods, especially in data-poor settings. The runtime analysis further demonstrates a practical advantage, with EM being significantly faster on the tested datasets. The qualitative example in Figure 2a is also illustrative of the practical difference in model quality.

### Weaknesses

Despite the strong core contribution, the paper is marred by a substantial number of minor flaws, ranging from presentation issues to points of technical unclarity, which collectively undermine its quality.

1.  **Lack of Polish and Clarity:** The paper appears to have been hastily prepared.
    *   The reference section contains numerous formatting errors (e.g., "[1] A. Kulesza, PhD thesis, University of Pennsylvania,, , ," contains extraneous commas).
    *   A timestamp ("Tuesday, August 5, 14") is inexplicably present at the bottom of the first page.
    *   The M-step update for the eigenvectors V is not clearly explained. The paper states that Eq. (20) is for "the particular case of the initial gradient, where we have q = p". This is confusing. The M-step objective is an expectation over the posterior `q(J|Yi)` computed in the E-step. It is unclear if this formula is used in every M-step, and if so, why it remains valid when `q` is updated. This is a critical detail of the algorithm that requires significant clarification.
    *   The algorithm performs a single gradient step for V in the M-step, making it a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm. This standard terminology is not used, which would have added precision.

2.  **Missing Technical Details:**
    *   The moments-matching initialization (Eq. 23) is presented without citation or derivation. The origin of this specific formula is not obvious and its properties are not discussed.
    *   In the M-step, the paper mentions that to avoid `λ_j = 1`, they "simply tighten the constraint on λ to keep it slightly below 1." This is an ad-hoc fix; the amount by which it is tightened could affect performance and stability, and should be specified.
    *   The line search for the eigenvector update step size η is unconventional, as it requires re-evaluating the likelihood `L`, which involves re-running part of the E-step logic for each trial step. The computational implications of this expensive inner loop are not fully discussed, beyond being captured by the `T2` term in the complexity analysis.

3.  **Incomplete Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The paper's central claim of being asymptotically faster hinges on `k << N`. However, the characteristic subset size `k = max_i |Y_i|` for the experimental dataset is never reported. This is a crucial missing value needed to contextualize the runtime results.
    *   The baseline K-Ascent algorithm uses a very simple step-size reduction scheme. The authors themselves note that "employing more complex step-size selection mechanisms could alleviate this problem" for KA, but do not explore this, potentially creating an unfair comparison.
    *   Experimental results in Figure 1 lack any indication of variance (e.g., error bars or shaded regions), even though results are medians over 25 trials. While quartile numbers are mentioned to be in the supplement, this information is essential for interpreting the plots in the main paper.
    *   The evaluation is limited to one dataset and one metric (log-likelihood). A comparison to the prior, more restrictive learning methods discussed in the introduction (e.g., [2], [8]) would have provided a much stronger case for the benefits of learning a full, unconstrained kernel.

### Recommendation

While the core idea of this paper is strong and the problem it addresses is important, the execution and presentation are not at the level expected for NIPS. The numerous minor errors in formatting, lack of clarity on critical algorithmic details, and missing information in the experimental section detract significantly from the work. The accumulation of these flaws suggests a rushed submission that has not undergone sufficient internal review. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. I believe that after a thorough revision to address the issues of clarity, detail, and experimental reporting, this work would represent a very strong contribution.
