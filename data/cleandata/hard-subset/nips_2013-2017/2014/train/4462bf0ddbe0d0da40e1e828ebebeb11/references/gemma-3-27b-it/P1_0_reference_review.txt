## Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix *L* for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) from observed subsets. Existing methods either partially learn *L* (e.g., learning item quality scores) or rely on restrictive parameterizations. The authors propose an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that avoids the pitfalls of naive gradient ascent (specifically, the need for a problematic projection step to maintain positive semi-definiteness) and offers potential asymptotic speedups. The key insight is to leverage the eigendecomposition of *L* and formulate the learning problem in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors, introducing a hidden variable representing the underlying DPP mixture components. The authors demonstrate the algorithm’s robustness and efficiency through experiments on a baby registry dataset.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions its contribution within the existing literature on DPP learning. It highlights the limitations of previous approaches: learning scalar weights ([2]), learning ensembles of DPPs ([3]), and Bayesian methods with parametric *L* ([8]). The authors clearly articulate how their EM algorithm overcomes these limitations by offering a fully nonparametric learning approach without the need for restrictive assumptions or problematic projection steps. The connection to existing work on k-DPPs ([3]) is also appropriately noted.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed EM algorithm presents a genuinely novel approach to DPP learning, avoiding the limitations of previous methods.  Formulating the problem in terms of the eigendecomposition of *L* is a clever and effective strategy.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the EM algorithm, including the use of Jensen’s inequality and the closed-form updates for eigenvalues, appears sound. The authors clearly explain the theoretical basis for their approach.
* **Practical Advantages:** The authors convincingly demonstrate that the EM algorithm avoids the degeneracy issues encountered with gradient ascent and offers potential runtime benefits.  The analysis of the runtime complexity is insightful.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the baby registry dataset support the claims of improved robustness and efficiency. The comparison to K-Ascent provides a valuable baseline.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The explanations of the underlying concepts are accessible, and the mathematical notation is used effectively.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors have made code and data available, which is commendable and fosters reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While theoretically elegant, implementing the EM algorithm appears non-trivial, particularly the Stiefel manifold update for the eigenvectors. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of practical implementation considerations.
* **Sensitivity to Initialization:** Although the EM algorithm is presented as more robust, the experiments demonstrate a clear dependence on initialization. The comparison between Wishart and moment-matching initialization reveals significant performance variations. More investigation into initialization strategies would be beneficial.
* **Limited Dataset:** The experiments are limited to a single domain (baby registries). While the results are encouraging, the generalizability of the algorithm to other applications remains unclear.  Exploring other datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Step Size Selection:** The process for selecting the step size (η) in the coordinate ascent step is not fully detailed. A more thorough discussion of this aspect, including its impact on convergence, would be valuable.
* **Discussion of k-DPP marginalization:** The authors mention using k-DPP marginalization for the eigenvalue update, but the connection is somewhat cursory. Expanding on this point with greater detail, and perhaps a figure illustrating the marginalization process, would improve clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of DPP learning. The proposed EM algorithm offers a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem. The empirical results are encouraging, and the paper is well-written and clearly organized. While the algorithm’s implementation complexity and sensitivity to initialization are concerns, they are outweighed by the potential benefits.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in DPP learning.
* The proposed EM algorithm is a novel and theoretically sound solution.
* The empirical results demonstrate the algorithm's robustness and efficiency.
* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
* The availability of code and data promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The algorithm’s implementation may be complex.
* The results are limited to a single domain.
* The dependence on initialization requires further investigation.



**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS, contingent on minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the practical implementation considerations, particularly the Stiefel manifold update.
2.  Investigate and discuss alternative initialization strategies.
3.  Consider experimenting with the algorithm on additional datasets to demonstrate its generalizability.
4.  Elaborate on the step size selection process and its impact on convergence.
5.  Provide a more detailed explanation of the k-DPP marginalization process.
