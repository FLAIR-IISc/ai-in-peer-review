Summary
The paper proposes an expectation–maximization (EM) algorithm for learning unconstrained determinantal point process (DPP) kernels directly in the marginal parameterization K. The key idea is to reparameterize K via its eigendecomposition (eigenvalues λ and eigenvectors V) and to exploit the well-known mixture view of DPPs over elementary DPPs indexed by a hidden subset J of eigenvectors. Using this hidden-variable formulation, the authors derive a lower bound via Jensen’s inequality. The E-step produces a conditional k-DPP q(J|Y), for which they give an explicit kernel; the M-step yields a closed-form update for eigenvalues using k-DPP marginals and a gradient step for eigenvectors on the Stiefel manifold. This avoids the degeneracy often induced by PSD projections in naive gradient ascent on K, and is asymptotically faster. Experiments on an Amazon baby registry dataset compare the EM method against projected gradient ascent (KA), showing better test likelihoods, greater robustness to initialization, and practical speedups, especially when off-diagonal structure (negative interactions) matters.

Relation to prior work
Previous learning approaches for DPPs either learned only per-item quality weights (diagonal scaling), learned mixtures of fixed L’s, or assumed parametric kernels with Bayesian posterior inference. Those methods either constrain expressivity or lose DPP structure. The work here tackles unconstrained, nonparametric learning of K by leveraging the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs view to derive an EM algorithm, together with k-DPP machinery for efficient marginals, and manifold optimization for V. To my knowledge, using the J-indexed mixture as an explicit latent variable to derive an EM procedure for full K learning is novel and addresses a long-standing pain point (PSD projections) in DPP training.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: The hidden-variable formulation and resulting EM algorithm are elegant and open a new direction for full DPP learning without restrictive parameterizations.
- Technical contributions: E-step as a k-DPP with a concrete kernel; closed-form eigenvalue updates via k-DPP marginals; practical Stiefel-manifold update for eigenvectors; complexity analysis showing O(n N k^2) scaling for the main per-iteration computations.
- Practical impact: Avoids projection-induced degeneracy that can make K nearly diagonal; empirically faster than KA; more robust across initializations and datasets.
- Empirics: Sensible evaluation on held-out likelihood, correlation analysis with a simple data statistic (d) indicating when off-diagonal structure is important; qualitative MAP example consistent with diversity modeling.

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines: Comparisons are only to KA. It would strengthen the case to include parametric/Bayesian methods (e.g., Affandi et al.) and “diagonal only” or mixture-of-DPP baselines, even if those lose some DPP properties.
- Convergence guarantees: The M-step uses a single gradient step for V and an empirical line search with updated q, so classical EM monotonicity is not established. The paper checks likelihood improvements in practice, but a brief discussion of convergence properties or a monotonic variant would be valuable.
- Scalability and scope: Experiments are on categories with N ≈ 25–100 and moderate k; no results for larger N (where DPPs are often applied) or for other domains (summarization, image search). Discussion of low-rank constraints or Nyström approximations in conjunction with this EM would be helpful.
- Regularization: No explicit regularization (e.g., spectral or trace constraints) is used. This could matter in data-poor settings and to prevent λ → 1 issues beyond the practical clipping mentioned.
- Evaluation breadth: Gains in data-rich settings are modest with the stronger initializer; adding recommendation-style utility/diversity metrics (beyond likelihood) and ablations (number of V updates, effect of step-size scheduling) would clarify practical benefits.

Quality
Overall technically sound with clear derivations for key steps; some derivations are deferred to the supplement (reasonable). The lack of theoretical convergence analysis for the generalized EM variant is the main technical gap. Complexity claims are plausible and matched by wall-clock comparisons, but scalability to larger N remains to be demonstrated.

Clarity
Well-written and well-structured. Algorithms and notational choices are mostly clear; minor notation could be streamlined (e.g., toggling between U = V^T and VYi). The rationale behind the manifold update is succinct and useful.

Originality
High. Framing full DPP learning as EM over the eigenvector-subset latent variable and operationalizing it via k-DPP marginals and manifold optimization is a novel and bold approach that advances DPP learning beyond prior partial or parametric methods.

Significance
High potential. Robust, unconstrained DPP training is important for many applications where diversity matters. Even with modest empirical scope here, the algorithmic ideas are likely to be used and extended (e.g., to low-rank, mini-batch, or approximate settings).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a short discussion of convergence: conditions under which the likelihood is guaranteed to improve, or a monotone variant with a provable bound.
- Add baselines: parametric/Bayesian DPP learning and simpler non-diverse models, to contextualize likelihood and utility/diversity tradeoffs.
- Explore scalability: experiments with larger N using low-rank or Nyström approximations integrated into the EM steps.
- Include regularization and sensitivity analyses: spectral penalties, effects of clipping λ, and number of V updates per M-step.
- Broaden experimental domains and metrics (e.g., summarization F-scores/diversity, image search diversity).

Decision
Accept. The paper presents a genuinely novel, well-motivated EM framework for DPP learning that addresses a core bottleneck and shows practical benefits. Despite some incomplete validation and missing theoretical convergence guarantees, the contribution is bold and likely to stimulate further research.
