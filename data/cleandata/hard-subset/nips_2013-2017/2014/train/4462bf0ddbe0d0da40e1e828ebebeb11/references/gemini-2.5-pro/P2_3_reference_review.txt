### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. While DPPs are powerful models for capturing diversity in subset selection tasks, learning their full, unconstrained kernel via maximum likelihood is conjectured to be NP-hard. Previous approaches have either learned restricted versions of the kernel (e.g., only scaling factors for rows), combined fixed kernels, or used Bayesian methods for parametric forms.

The main contribution of this work is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for maximum likelihood estimation of the DPP kernel. The key idea is to perform optimization over the kernel's eigendecomposition (eigenvalues and eigenvectors). The authors leverage the interpretation of a DPP as a mixture of "elementary DPPs," where each component corresponds to a subset of eigenvectors. This subset of eigenvectors is treated as a latent variable, enabling the EM framework. The E-step involves computing the posterior distribution over this latent variable for each observed subset, which the authors show is itself a tractable k-DPP. The M-step then updates the eigenvalues in closed form and the eigenvectors via a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, cleverly avoiding the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent.

The proposed EM algorithm is compared against a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) on a real-world product recommendation dataset (Amazon baby registries). The results demonstrate that the EM method is more robust, particularly with uninformative initializations or in data-poor settings, and is also computationally faster.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical development of the EM algorithm is sound and elegant. The insight to frame the problem in terms of a latent eigenvector selection variable (`J`) is clever and unlocks the EM approach. The derivation relies on established properties of DPPs and standard optimization techniques (Jensen's inequality, manifold optimization). The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The choice of a real-world dataset makes the results compelling, and the analysis goes beyond simple performance metrics to diagnose *why* and *when* the proposed method outperforms the baseline (e.g., the analysis of the off-diagonal importance via the `d` metric). The authors are honest about the limitations of the baseline and the conditions under which their method provides the most benefit.

*   **Weaknesses:** The M-step for the eigenvectors (`V`) is not a full maximization but rather a single gradient step. The paper notes that the gradient formula (Eq. 20) is only exact at the start of the M-step and that fully optimizing `V` is intractable. This makes the algorithm a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm, which is a perfectly valid approach, but this could be stated more explicitly. Furthermore, the line search for the eigenvector update step size (`η`) requires re-evaluating the E-step, which adds a potentially significant computational cost that is somewhat glossed over in the main complexity analysis.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

*   **Strengths:** The motivation for the problem is established clearly in the introduction. The background on DPPs is concise but sufficient. The progression from the naive baseline (K-Ascent) to the proposed EM algorithm is logical and easy to follow. The authors provide strong intuition for the failure mode of the baseline (getting stuck in near-diagonal local optima due to projection), which effectively motivates their projection-free approach. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, and the qualitative example (recommending one vs. three baby monitors) is a powerful and memorable illustration of the algorithm's practical benefit.

*   **Weaknesses:** Some key derivations and proofs are deferred to the supplement (e.g., Lemma 1 proof, derivation of Eqs. 19 and 20). While this is standard practice for conference papers due to space constraints, it makes the paper slightly less self-contained.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.

*   **Strengths:** To my knowledge, this is the first EM-style algorithm for learning a full, unconstrained, non-parametric DPP kernel. It presents a fundamentally new way to approach the optimization problem by moving from the space of kernel entries to the space of its eigen-components. This change of variables and the introduction of the latent eigenvector selection process is a significant conceptual contribution that neatly sidesteps the major issues with direct gradient-based methods. The work is well-situated within the context of previous literature, clearly articulating how it differs from and improves upon prior attempts at DPP learning.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is very significant.

*   **Strengths:** This paper addresses a core bottleneck in the practical application of DPPs. By providing a more robust and efficient algorithm for learning the fundamental kernel matrix from data, it makes DPPs significantly more accessible and useful for a wide range of applications (e.g., recommendation, summarization, search). The problem is important, and the proposed solution is practical and effective. Researchers and practitioners looking to apply DPPs are very likely to use this algorithm or build upon its ideas. The insight of optimizing over the eigendecomposition may also inspire new approaches for other problems involving PSD matrices. The work represents a clear advance in the state of the art for learning DPPs.

### Justification for Rating

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** It presents a novel and elegant EM algorithm for a fundamental, difficult, and important problem in machine learning: learning an unconstrained DPP kernel.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The method is well-founded theoretically, combining concepts from DPPs and manifold optimization in a sophisticated way.
3.  **Strong Empirical Evidence:** The experiments are conducted on a realistic, large-scale dataset and provide compelling evidence for the proposed method's superiority over a strong baseline in terms of both robustness and speed.
4.  **Clarity and Insight:** The paper is exceptionally clear and provides deep insights into the failure modes of previous approaches and the reasons for its own success.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The M-step for the eigenvectors is a single gradient ascent step rather than a full maximization, making it a GEM algorithm. While not a major flaw, a more thorough discussion of the implications would be welcome.
2.  The algorithm still depends on a non-diagonal initialization to learn diversity, a common challenge in this space that is not fully solved here (though the algorithm is more robust to the quality of that initialization).

The strengths of this paper overwhelmingly outweigh the minor weaknesses. It is a complete, high-quality piece of work that makes a significant contribution to an active area of research. It is well-written, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. I expect this work to be highly influential.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
