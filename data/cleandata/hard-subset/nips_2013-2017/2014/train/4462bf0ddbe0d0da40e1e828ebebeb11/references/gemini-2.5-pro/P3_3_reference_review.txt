**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. While DPPs are powerful models for capturing diversity, learning their parameters via maximum likelihood is conjectured to be NP-hard, and prior work has been limited to learning restricted or parametric forms of the kernel. The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to learn an unconstrained, non-parametric kernel.

The key idea is to work with the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel `K = VΛVᵀ` and to treat the selection of eigenvectors `J` as a latent variable in the DPP's generative process. This recasts the log-likelihood as a sum over these latent variables, making it amenable to EM. In the E-step, the algorithm computes the posterior distribution over the latent variable `J` given an observed subset `Y`. The authors provide an important result here, showing this posterior is itself a k-DPP whose kernel can be computed efficiently. In the M-step, this posterior is used to find updated parameters `V` and `Λ`. The update for the eigenvalues `Λ` is derived in closed form and cleverly avoids the projection step that plagues gradient-based methods. The update for the eigenvectors `V` is handled by taking a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, a principled approach to maintaining the orthogonality constraint `VᵀV = I`. The authors compare their EM algorithm to a projected gradient ascent baseline and demonstrate its superior robustness and speed on a real-world product recommendation task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a fundamental and difficult problem in the DPP literature. Learning a full, non-parametric kernel is a significant advance over prior methods that learn restricted versions [2] or ensembles that lose DPP properties [3]. The proposed EM algorithm is novel, elegant, and well-motivated by the underlying generative structure of DPPs. This work significantly pushes the state-of-the-art for DPP learning.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical development is sound and insightful. The formulation of the problem in terms of the eigendecomposition and the identification of the eigenvector indices `J` as a latent variable is very clever. A key technical contribution is Lemma 1, which characterizes the posterior `p(J|Y)` as a k-DPP, enabling an efficient E-step. The M-step updates are also well-derived. The projection-free update for the eigenvalues and the use of Stiefel manifold optimization for the eigenvectors are more principled and robust than the naive projection used in the baseline, as convincingly argued by the authors and supported by the experiments.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are thorough and provide strong evidence for the claims. The choice of a real-world baby registry dataset is an excellent and natural application for DPPs.
    *   The comparison against projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) is a strong and appropriate baseline.
    *   The paper demonstrates the EM algorithm's robustness, particularly with a generic (Wishart) initialization and in a data-poor setting, where the baseline's projection step leads to degenerate, near-diagonal solutions that fail to model diversity.
    *   The qualitative example in Figure 2a, which shows EM selecting a diverse set of products while KA selects three redundant baby monitors, is a powerful illustration of the practical impact of the improved learning algorithm.
    *   The runtime analysis confirms the asymptotic advantage of the EM approach.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the background on DPPs is concise, and the algorithmic development is presented in a logical, step-by-step manner.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors**

1.  **M-step for Eigenvectors (V):** The authors state they take only a single gradient step for `V` in the M-step, as the gradient in Eq. (20) is only valid at the start of the M-step. This suggests the algorithm is technically a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm, which should be mentioned for clarity. Could the authors comment on the practical trade-offs of this single-step approach versus a more complex M-step that might try to further optimize `V`?

2.  **Step-size Selection:** The line search for the eigenvector update seems potentially expensive. The paper states that it requires "performing an E-step for each trial step size" to check for improvement in the true log-likelihood `L(V,Λ)`. This seems to deviate from the standard EM framework of optimizing the auxiliary function `F` and could add significant computational cost. While the complexity analysis includes a `T2` factor for this search, could the authors provide more detail on how this worked in practice? For instance, how many trial steps were typically needed?

3.  **Local Optima:** While the paper impressively shows that EM is more robust to initialization than K-Ascent, as an EM algorithm, it is still susceptible to converging to local optima. The experiments report median performance over 25 trials. It would strengthen the results to provide more detail on the variance of the outcomes (e.g., via boxplots or interquartile ranges in the main paper, rather than just the supplement) to give a clearer picture of this sensitivity.

4.  **Scalability:** The algorithm's complexity contains an `O(N^3)` term due to the eigendecomposition and matrix exponential, which limits its application to problems with a moderately sized ground set `N`. This is a common challenge for methods that learn a full `N x N` kernel. While not a flaw of this specific work, a brief discussion of potential avenues for scaling, for example by combining the EM framework with low-rank kernel approximations, would be a welcome addition to the conclusion or future work section.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant contribution to the machine learning community. It proposes a novel, principled, and effective EM algorithm for the challenging problem of learning a full DPP kernel. The technical ideas are elegant, and the experimental results are strong and convincing. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily requests for clarification. The paper is of high quality, originality, and significance, and I strongly recommend its acceptance.
