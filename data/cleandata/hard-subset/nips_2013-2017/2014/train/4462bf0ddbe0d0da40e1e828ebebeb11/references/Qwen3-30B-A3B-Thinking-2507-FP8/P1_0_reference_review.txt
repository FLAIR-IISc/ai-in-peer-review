

# Peer Review: "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant challenge in Determinantal Point Process (DPP) learning: maximizing the likelihood of observed subsets without restrictive assumptions about the kernel matrix K. The authors recognize that previous approaches either impose parametric constraints (e.g., [3] using L-ensembles, [8] using Bayesian methods) or use gradient ascent with problematic projection steps that often lead to degenerate solutions (near-diagonal K matrices that fail to capture diversity). The paper proposes an EM algorithm that exploits the eigendecomposition of K, introducing a hidden variable J (representing a subset of eigenvectors) to avoid the need for projection steps. This approach is theoretically grounded in the relationship between DPPs and their marginal kernels, and the authors provide a detailed derivation of the E-step and M-step updates.

The paper builds on foundational DPP work by Kulesza and Taskar [5, 20] and relates to broader EM literature (e.g., [25]). It makes a clear contribution to the growing body of work on DPP learning, addressing a practical limitation in existing methods.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and well-motivated technical contribution**: The key insight—using eigendecomposition to avoid projection steps—solves a real problem in DPP learning that has been noted in prior work (e.g., [1, Conjecture 4.1] on the NP-hardness of likelihood maximization). The derivation of the EM algorithm is thorough, with clear mathematical justification for each step.

2. **Strong experimental validation**: The experiments on the Amazon baby registry dataset (13 categories, ~8,500 training examples) convincingly demonstrate EM's advantages over gradient ascent (K-Ascent). The analysis showing that EM outperforms KA particularly when the true kernel has significant off-diagonal components (d > 0.025) is insightful and well-supported.

3. **Clear theoretical analysis**: The paper carefully explains why gradient ascent with projection fails (leading to near-diagonal matrices that lose diversity information), which is a significant contribution to understanding the limitations of prior methods.

4. **Practical runtime advantage**: The paper provides a compelling theoretical and empirical argument for EM's asymptotic runtime advantage (O(T1nNk² + T1T2N³) vs. O(T1nN³ + T1T2N³) for K-Ascent). The empirical results (2.1x average speedup) are particularly valuable for practitioners.

5. **Well-organized and clearly written**: The paper is logically structured, with helpful mathematical derivations and intuitive explanations of key concepts (e.g., the k-DPP in Lemma 1).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited experimental scope**: The paper only evaluates on the baby registry dataset. While this is a good test case, it would be stronger to include experiments on other DPP applications (e.g., document summarization, sensor placement) to demonstrate broader applicability. The paper mentions these domains in the introduction but doesn't validate the method in them.

2. **Incomplete comparison to prior methods**: The paper compares only to K-Ascent (gradient ascent with projection), but doesn't compare to other recent DPP learning methods. For instance, [8] proposes a Bayesian approach that learns parameters of a parametric DPP form, and [3] uses a mixture of DPPs. A comparison to these methods would better establish the paper's contribution.

3. **Vague computational complexity analysis**: The paper claims EM has "asymptotically faster" runtime but doesn't fully justify the complexity reduction from N³ to Nk². The term k (max |Yi|) is used without explaining how it typically compares to N in practical DPP applications. For small k, the advantage is clear, but for larger k, the benefit might diminish.

4. **Lack of convergence guarantees**: The paper doesn't provide theoretical guarantees about the convergence of the EM algorithm (e.g., to a local optimum). While this is common in EM-based methods, it's worth noting as a limitation.

5. **Insufficient analysis of initialization sensitivity**: While the paper acknowledges initialization is important, it doesn't thoroughly analyze how sensitive EM is to different initialization strategies. The moment-matching initialization helps KA, but does it also help EM significantly more than Wishart initialization? This would strengthen the paper's claims about EM's robustness.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The technical contribution is significant, the experiments are well-executed and convincing, and the paper makes a clear, novel contribution to the field of DPP learning. While the experimental scope could be broader and the comparison to other methods more comprehensive, these are relatively minor weaknesses that don't undermine the core contribution.

The paper addresses a practical limitation in DPP learning that has been noted in prior work [1, 8], and provides a theoretically sound and empirically superior alternative to existing methods. The authors have clearly demonstrated that their approach avoids the degenerate solutions that plague gradient ascent methods, which is a major step forward for practical DPP applications.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include experiments on at least one additional DPP application domain (e.g., document summarization) to demonstrate broader applicability.

2. Add a comparison to [8] (Bayesian method) and [3] (L-ensemble method) in the experiments to better establish the paper's relative contribution.

3. Provide a more detailed explanation of the computational complexity claim, particularly the relationship between k and N in practical settings.

4. Conduct a more thorough analysis of initialization sensitivity for both EM and KA, including a comparison of how much each method benefits from the moment-matching initialization.

5. Add a brief discussion of convergence guarantees for the EM algorithm (even if it's standard for EM methods).

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPP learning. With the minor improvements suggested above, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The combination of theoretical insight, practical implementation, and empirical validation makes it a noteworthy contribution to the literature on determinantal processes.
