

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without the need for projection steps required by gradient ascent methods. The authors address a key limitation in existing DPP learning approaches, where gradient ascent methods require projection to maintain positive semi-definiteness, often resulting in degenerate solutions that fail to model diversity. The paper builds on the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K, leveraging the DPP's generative process with hidden variables to develop a more robust learning algorithm.

The paper relates its work to key NIPS contributions on DPPs, including Kulesza and Taskar's foundational work on DPPs in machine learning [5, 16, 20], Affandi et al.'s work on learning DPP parameters [8], and the recent advances in k-DPPs [3]. It clearly differentiates itself from prior approaches: unlike [2] which learns scalar weights for rows of L (focusing only on item quality), and [3] which uses L-ensembles (losing DPP properties), the authors develop an unconstrained, non-parametric learning method. The paper also acknowledges the NP-hardness conjecture for DPP likelihood maximization [1], positioning their work as a practical approach to this challenging problem.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound EM algorithm with thorough theoretical development. The authors derive the E-step and M-step carefully, with proper mathematical justification using Jensen's inequality and the DPP's generative properties. The computational complexity analysis is well-executed, showing that the EM method has better asymptotic runtime than gradient ascent (O(T1nNk² + T1T2N³) vs. O(T1nN³ + T1T2N³)). The experimental evaluation on Amazon baby registry data (13 categories with 71 products each on average) demonstrates that the EM approach consistently achieves higher test log-likelihoods than gradient ascent (K-Ascent) across various initialization methods. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of when the EM method provides the most significant gains (correlating with the "d" metric measuring off-diagonal importance), showing careful consideration of the method's limitations.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The background section concisely explains DPPs and their marginal kernel representation, establishing the necessary foundation. The algorithmic development (Sections 3.1-3.5) progresses logically from the problem statement to the complete EM formulation, with each step well-motivated. The experimental setup is clearly described, and the results are presented effectively through both quantitative comparisons (Figures 1-2) and qualitative examples (Figure 2a). The supplementary material provides additional theoretical details, making the paper sufficiently complete for replication.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by developing an EM-based learning method that avoids the projection step required in gradient ascent approaches. While the general EM framework is standard, its application to DPP learning through eigendecomposition is novel. The paper clearly articulates how this differs from previous work, particularly by avoiding the limitations of [3] (loss of DPP properties) and [8] (parametric assumptions). The authors effectively position their work as addressing a key gap in the literature, as noted in the introduction where they state that "learning an unconstrained, non-parametric L" has been challenging.

**Significance:** The results demonstrate clear practical value, with the EM method achieving 3.7% average log-likelihood improvement over K-Ascent on real-world product recommendation data. The runtime analysis shows EM is 2.1 times faster on average, which is significant for practical applications. The paper's focus on modeling negative interactions between items (critical for diversity in subset selection) directly addresses a core challenge in applications like product recommendation, document summarization, and sensor placement. The method could be broadly applicable to any DPP-based application where modeling item diversity is important, advancing the practical utility of DPPs.

**Strengths:**
1. The EM algorithm effectively overcomes the projection problem in gradient ascent, leading to more robust learning of DPP parameters.
2. The theoretical development is rigorous and well-justified.
3. The experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate clear advantages on real-world data.
4. The paper provides meaningful analysis of when the method is most beneficial (via the d metric).
5. The runtime analysis shows practical advantages beyond just accuracy.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper could provide more detailed analysis of convergence properties of the EM algorithm, including guarantees or empirical convergence rates.
2. The initialization methods (Wishart and moment matching) are somewhat limited; exploring more sophisticated initialization strategies could strengthen the comparison.
3. The paper doesn't directly compare with other recent DPP learning methods beyond K-Ascent, though the focus is on overcoming a specific limitation of gradient-based methods.
4. The theoretical analysis doesn't explore the relationship between the EM algorithm's performance and the structure of the true DPP kernel.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of DPP learning. The paper addresses a clear limitation in existing methods, presents a technically sound solution with thorough theoretical and empirical validation, and demonstrates practical significance. The work advances the state of the art in DPP learning and provides a more robust and efficient approach for applications requiring subset selection with diversity constraints. The paper meets all the key criteria for acceptance at NeurIPS, with high quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
