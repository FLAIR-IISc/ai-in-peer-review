**Summary**

The paper tackles the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. While DPPs are powerful models for capturing diversity in subset selection tasks, learning the full, unconstrained kernel matrix via maximum likelihood is conjectured to be NP-hard. Previous work has typically circumvented this by learning restricted or parametric forms of the kernel. This paper introduces a novel and elegant Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to tackle the full learning problem. The key insight is to re-parameterize the DPP kernel via its eigendecomposition and to introduce the set of "active" eigenvectors as a latent variable. This reframing allows the authors to derive an EM-style algorithm that alternates between computing the posterior over these latent variables (E-step) and updating the kernel's eigenvalues and eigenvectors (M-step). This approach cleverly avoids the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent, which can lead to degenerate, near-diagonal solutions that fail to model diversity.

**Strengths**

*   **Novelty and Originality:** The central contribution of this paper is highly novel. The application of an EM framework to learn an unconstrained DPP kernel is, to my knowledge, a completely new approach. The formulation, which leverages the generative interpretation of DPPs via a mixture of elementary DPPs and treats the selection of eigenvectors as a latent variable, is both insightful and theoretically elegant. It transforms a difficult constrained optimization problem into a more manageable one. This is precisely the kind of bold, conceptual advance that pushes the field forward, moving beyond incremental improvements on existing learning paradigms for DPPs.
*   **Significance and Impact:** The problem of learning a DPP from data is of fundamental importance, with direct applications in recommender systems, document summarization, and many other areas where diverse subset selection is critical. By providing a practical and robust algorithm for learning the full kernel, this work significantly advances the state-of-the-art. The proposed method allows practitioners to learn the crucial negative interactions (i.e., similarity) between items directly from data, rather than relying on hand-crafted features or restrictive parametric forms. This has the potential to be widely adopted and built upon by other researchers.
*   **Technical Quality:** The derivation of the algorithm is clear and well-motivated. The connection made in the E-step, showing that the posterior over the latent variables is itself a k-DPP, is a particularly nice result that allows for efficient computation. While the M-step for the eigenvectors involves a gradient step rather than a full maximization (making it a Generalized EM), the authors correctly identify the non-convexity issue and employ a principled update on the Stiefel manifold, which is a sound and sophisticated choice.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while focused on a single domain, are well-designed and convincing. The comparison against projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) is the most natural and important baseline. The results clearly demonstrate that the proposed EM algorithm is not only faster but, more importantly, more robust. Its superior performance in the data-poor setting and with a less-informed initialization highlights its key advantage: it is less prone to getting stuck in poor local optima (like near-diagonal solutions) that fail to capture the diversity-seeking nature of the data. The qualitative example from the baby registry dataset provides a compelling, intuitive illustration of the practical benefits.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The empirical validation is conducted on a single type of dataset (baby registries). While this is a good real-world test case, demonstrating the algorithm's effectiveness on other canonical DPP tasks, such as document summarization or image search, would have strengthened the paper's claims of general applicability.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The algorithm's complexity scales with `O(N^3)`, which is typical for methods involving eigendecompositions of an `N x N` matrix. This will be a bottleneck for applications with very large ground sets (large `N`). While the authors correctly point out that their method is asymptotically faster than the baseline for `k << N`, this inherent scaling issue is a practical limitation for the broader use of unconstrained DPPs. This is less a flaw of the specific algorithm and more a challenge for this class of models, but it is worth noting.

**Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. Its primary strength lies in its conceptual novelty. The authors have introduced a genuinely new and powerful way to think about and solve the difficult problem of learning a full DPP kernel. This is a significant theoretical and practical contribution. The proposed EM algorithm is elegant, well-motivated, and empirically shown to be more robust and efficient than the standard gradient-based alternative. The weaknesses are minor; the experimental scope is sufficient to demonstrate the core value of the idea, and the scalability limitations are inherent to the problem rather than a specific flaw in the proposed solution. This work opens up a new and promising direction for learning DPPs and is likely to have a substantial impact on future research in this area.
