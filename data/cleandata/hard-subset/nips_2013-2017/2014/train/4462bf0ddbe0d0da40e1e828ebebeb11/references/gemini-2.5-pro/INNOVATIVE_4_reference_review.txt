### Summary of the Paper
This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning the full, unconstrained kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. The authors note that maximum likelihood estimation for the DPP kernel is conjectured to be NP-hard and that a naive projected gradient ascent approach suffers from a critical flaw: the projection step required to maintain positive semi-definiteness can lead to degenerate, near-diagonal solutions that fail to model diversity.

To overcome this, the paper introduces a novel and elegant Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The core idea is to leverage the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix, `K = VΛV^T`. The authors reframe the DPP generative process as a mixture of elementary DPPs, where the selection of a subset of eigenvectors, `J`, is treated as a hidden variable. Based on this formulation, they derive an EM procedure:
- **E-step:** Computes the posterior distribution over the latent eigenvector selections `J` given an observed data subset `Y_i`. The authors show this posterior is itself a k-DPP, for which efficient marginalization is possible.
- **M-step:** Updates the kernel parameters (eigenvalues `Λ` and eigenvectors `V`). The eigenvalue update has a simple, closed-form solution that does not require projection. The eigenvector update is handled by taking a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, which also cleverly avoids a problematic projection.

The proposed EM method is compared against the projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) on a real-world product recommendation dataset (Amazon baby registries). The results show that EM is more robust, particularly with uninformative initializations or in data-poor settings, and is also computationally faster.

### Strengths
The primary strength of this paper lies in its exceptional novelty and the boldness of its approach. The problem of learning a full DPP kernel is fundamental, and prior work has largely sidestepped its difficulty by using restricted parameterizations. This paper confronts the problem head-on with a completely new algorithmic paradigm for this setting.

1.  **Conceptual Leap:** The idea of introducing the eigenvector selection `J` as a latent variable to enable an EM algorithm is a brilliant conceptual contribution. It provides a new lens through which to view the DPP learning problem, moving beyond direct optimization on the matrix entries. This kind of creative reframing is precisely what pushes a field forward.

2.  **Elegant Solution to a Real Problem:** The authors do an excellent job of motivating their work by first identifying a specific, critical flaw in the most direct baseline approach (projected gradient ascent). The tendency of the projection step to destroy off-diagonal information and collapse the model's diversity-modeling capacity is a crucial insight. The proposed EM algorithm is not just a new method for its own sake; it is a carefully constructed solution that directly remedies this specific failing. The projection-free updates for both eigenvalues and eigenvectors are a testament to the elegance of the formulation.

3.  **Strong Empirical Demonstration:** The experiments on the baby registry dataset provide compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness. The comparison using a Wishart initialization clearly shows the failure mode of K-Ascent and the robustness of EM, especially on categories where diversity is key. The qualitative example of the recommended "safety" registry is particularly telling—EM produces a sensible, diverse set, while KA suggests a redundant set with three different baby monitors, a classic failure of models that don't capture negative correlations.

4.  **Efficiency:** The fact that the proposed algorithm is not only more robust but also asymptotically and practically faster than the baseline is a significant practical advantage that will encourage adoption.

### Weaknesses
The paper is a strong piece of work, and its weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core contribution.

1.  **Approximation in M-step:** The M-step does not fully optimize the eigenvectors `V` in each iteration; instead, it takes a single gradient step. This makes the algorithm a form of Generalized EM. While this is a standard and perfectly reasonable practical choice, it is a slight deviation from the classic EM framework. The authors are transparent about this, and the line search on the true likelihood ensures convergence, but it's a detail worth noting.

2.  **Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are convincing but are focused on a single, albeit very appropriate, application domain. Demonstrating the algorithm's utility on a different task, such as document summarization or sensor placement, would have broadened the paper's empirical claims. However, the chosen dataset is non-trivial and serves as a strong proof of concept.

### Recommendation
I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It presents a novel, elegant, and effective solution to a fundamental and difficult problem in the study of determinantal point processes. The core idea is a significant conceptual advance over previous approaches, moving away from restricted parameterizations or flawed direct optimization schemes. While methodological purists might point to the use of a single gradient step in the M-step, I see this as a pragmatic choice that does not diminish the novelty of the core framework. The paper's value lies in its bold new direction for DPP learning, and this contribution substantially outweighs any minor limitations in the experimental scope. This is precisely the kind of innovative and insightful work that NIPS should champion.
