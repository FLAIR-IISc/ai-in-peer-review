

# Peer Review: "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

## Summary
This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the parameters of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without restrictive assumptions about the kernel structure. Previous work on DPP learning has either imposed strong constraints on the kernel (e.g., learning only scalar weights for rows [2], or learning ensembles of fixed DPPs [3]) or relied on gradient ascent methods that require problematic projection steps to maintain positive semi-definiteness [8]. The authors propose an EM algorithm that exploits the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K, avoiding the need for projection steps that often lead to degenerate solutions. The key insight is to treat the selection of eigenvectors as a hidden variable, allowing for a natural EM formulation. The paper provides theoretical derivations of the E-step and M-step updates, with closed-form solutions for eigenvalue updates and an efficient eigenvector update procedure. The authors evaluate their method on a baby registry dataset from Amazon, demonstrating superior performance and runtime compared to projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent).

## Strengths

**Originality**: The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a fundamental limitation in DPP learning. The use of eigendecomposition to develop an EM algorithm is novel in this context, and the authors clearly differentiate their approach from prior work. While previous papers have explored DPP learning (e.g., [8, 3]), none have proposed an EM-based approach that avoids the projection issues inherent in gradient methods. The paper's focus on unconstrained learning of the marginal kernel K represents a meaningful advance over previous approaches.

**Technical Quality**: The theoretical development is rigorous and well-executed. The paper provides clear derivations of the EM updates, including the key Lemma 1 for the E-step and the closed-form eigenvalue updates (Equation 18). The runtime analysis (O(T₁nNk² + T₁T₂N³)) is thorough and demonstrates the asymptotic advantage of the EM approach over gradient ascent (O(T₁nN³ + T₁T₂N³)). The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the need for a good initialization and the computational complexity of eigenvector updates.

**Significance**: DPPs are widely used for subset selection tasks requiring diversity (e.g., recommendation systems, document summarization), and efficient learning of their parameters is a critical problem. The paper demonstrates clear empirical advantages over the baseline method (K-Ascent) on a real-world dataset, with EM showing up to 16.5% improvement in log-likelihood for certain categories. The runtime advantage (EM being 2.1× faster on average) further enhances the practical significance of the contribution.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The background section provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with DPPs, and the mathematical derivations are presented logically. The experimental setup is well-motivated, and the results are presented with appropriate statistics and visualizations (Figures 1-2). The supplement provides additional details on derivations and computational complexity, which is helpful for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

**Initialization Sensitivity**: While the paper acknowledges that both EM and K-Ascent require a reasonable initialization (with diagonal kernels being problematic), the experimental results show that EM's advantage is most pronounced when starting from a Wishart initialization. When using a moments-matching initializer, the performance gap narrows significantly (only 0.4% average gain), suggesting that the initialization method is crucial. The paper could provide more guidance on how to obtain good initializations for practical applications.

**Computational Complexity of Eigenvector Updates**: The eigenvector update procedure (Equation 21) requires repeated evaluation of the M-step objective during step size search, which incurs a significant computational cost (O(T₁T₂N³)). While the authors acknowledge this, they could more clearly articulate when this overhead is justified compared to the potential gains in log-likelihood.

**Limited Theoretical Guarantees**: The paper does not provide theoretical guarantees about convergence or local optima for the EM algorithm. While this is common for EM algorithms, the authors might consider adding a brief discussion about the convergence properties of their specific EM variant.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPP learning by proposing a novel EM algorithm that avoids the problematic projection steps of gradient ascent. The theoretical development is sound, the experiments are well-designed and meaningful, and the results clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach. The work is technically rigorous, well-motivated, and addresses a significant problem in DPP learning.

The main weaknesses are the sensitivity to initialization and the computational cost of eigenvector updates, but these are reasonable limitations that the authors acknowledge. The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, as it makes a significant contribution to probabilistic modeling with applications to important machine learning problems.

## Pro/Con for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Novel EM approach that avoids projection steps required in gradient ascent
- Clear theoretical development with rigorous derivations
- Strong empirical results demonstrating superior log-likelihood and runtime
- Well-motivated real-world application on Amazon baby registry data
- Comprehensive comparison with the baseline K-Ascent method

**Con:**
- Limited discussion of theoretical convergence guarantees
- Performance advantage is most pronounced with Wishart initialization, suggesting initialization sensitivity
- Eigenvector update procedure has significant computational overhead

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to DPP learning. The advantages of the proposed method over previous approaches are clearly demonstrated, and the paper is technically sound. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.
