Summary
This paper proposes an EM algorithm for learning full, nonparametric DPP kernels by working in the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K. The key idea is to view a DPP as a mixture over elementary DPPs indexed by a subset J of eigenvectors; treating J as a hidden variable enables an EM formulation. The E-step sets q(J|Y)=pK(J|Y) and shows this distribution is a k-DPP with an explicit kernel derived from V and the eigenvalues λ, enabling efficient computation of its marginals via low-rank structure. The M-step gives a closed-form update for eigenvalues using these marginals and performs a Stiefel-manifold gradient step for eigenvectors with a matrix-exponential retraction. The method avoids the problematic PSD projection of naive projected gradient ascent (KA), which often collapses to near-diagonal solutions, and enjoys better asymptotic complexity O(T1 n N k^2 + T1 T2 N^3) when set sizes are small. On a real baby registry dataset across 13 categories, the EM method improves test log-likelihoods (especially in data-poor or strongly interacting regimes) and is about 2.1× faster than KA.

Relation to prior work
Prior DPP learning typically learned only item qualities (diagonal scaling) or mixtures of fixed kernels (sacrificing DPP structure), or relied on Bayesian inference with parametric kernels. This paper’s EM over the eigenspace with nonparametric L is, to my knowledge, new in the DPP literature. The use of k-DPPs to make the E-step tractable (and to compute marginals efficiently) is particularly elegant and leverages known DPP/k-DPP identities. The paper situates itself among foundational DPP work, partial learning schemes, and sampling/marginalization algorithms.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Casting nonparametric DPP learning as EM over elementary DPPs is a clean, bold idea. Identifying p(J|Y) as a k-DPP with a computable kernel is a neat contribution that unlocks tractable updates.
- Methodological soundness: The closed-form eigenvalue update and the Stiefel-manifold update for eigenvectors are principled and avoid the degeneracies of PSD projection in KA.
- Practical advantages: The algorithm is more robust to initialization and dataset changes, and asymptotically faster when k << N; the empirical runtime benefits are demonstrated.
- Clarity of derivations: The main steps are well presented, with complexity analyses and algorithmic details. The paper provides code.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Experimental scope: Results are limited to a single application domain with moderate N (avg 71). It would strengthen the case to include additional standard DPP benchmarks (e.g., document summarization, image search, sensor placement) and larger N.
- Baselines: The main baseline is KA. A comparison to alternative learning strategies (parametric Bayesian DPPs, mixtures of L-ensembles) would clarify relative merits and practical trade-offs.
- EM monotonicity and approximations: The M-step for V is a single gradient step and the step size is chosen by checking the likelihood L rather than the lower bound F, so classical EM’s monotonic guarantee is not preserved. This is reasonable in practice, but a discussion of convergence behavior or a lightweight guarantee (e.g., using a bound-preserving line search on F) would be welcome.
- Sensitivity: The method requires keeping λ strictly below 1 to avoid blow-ups in R; some discussion of regularization or damping would help. It would also be useful to show sensitivity to initialization and to the k distribution of training sets.
- Gains magnitude: While EM is clearly more robust and often better, median gains over KA can be modest with a good initializer; highlighting cases where modeling negative interactions is essential is helpful, but broader empirical evidence would increase significance.

Quality
The technical development is careful and sound overall. The use of k-DPP identities to compute marginals and gradients at O(N k^2) is convincing. The manifold update is standard. The main caveat is the approximate M-step and the reliance on L rather than F during line search; in practice this seems fine, but theory-minded readers will notice.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. The motivation for avoiding projection is clear and empirically supported. A few typos and incomplete references exist; polishing would help.

Originality
High. The EM formulation with a hidden variable over eigenvectors for nonparametric DPP learning is, to my knowledge, a novel and elegant direction.

Significance
Moderate to high. DPPs are widely used, and an effective, robust learning algorithm for full kernels is valuable. The main limitations are experimental scope and baselines; nevertheless, the conceptual advance is likely to influence follow-on work.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add at least one more application domain and a larger N regime; include comparisons to Bayesian parametric learning and L-ensemble mixtures.
- Provide an ablation on step-size selection and number of eigenvector updates per M-step; report sensitivity to initialization.
- Consider a retraction-based update (e.g., Cayley transform) as a cheaper alternative to matrix exponentials; discuss practical costs.
- Discuss regularization of λ (e.g., logit parameterization with priors) and strategies when some λ approach 1.
- If possible, provide a mild convergence guarantee (e.g., majorization-minimization step for V increasing F) or empirical convergence diagnostics.

Pros
- Novel EM formulation leveraging k-DPP structure
- Avoids projection degeneracy and improves robustness
- Better asymptotic and observed runtimes
- Clear derivations; code available

Cons
- Limited experimental breadth and baselines
- Approximate M-step without monotonicity guarantee
- Modest gains in some regimes; scalability to much larger N not shown

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a fresh, principled direction for learning DPPs that is both elegant and practically useful. Despite some experimental limitations, the conceptual novelty and the demonstrated robustness and efficiency warrant inclusion.
