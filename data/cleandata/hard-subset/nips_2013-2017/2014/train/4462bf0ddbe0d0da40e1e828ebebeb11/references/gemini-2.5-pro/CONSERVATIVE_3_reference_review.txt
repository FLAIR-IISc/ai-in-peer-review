**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. The authors correctly identify that the standard maximum likelihood approach via projected gradient ascent is problematic, as the projection step required to enforce the positive semi-definite (PSD) constraint can lead to degenerate, near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture the diversity-promoting properties of DPPs.

The central contribution is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for this learning problem. The authors re-parameterize the DPP kernel in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors. By leveraging the representation of a DPP as a mixture of "elementary" DPPs, they introduce a latent variable corresponding to a selection of eigenvectors. This formulation allows for the standard EM machinery to be applied. The resulting algorithm features a closed-form, projection-free update for the eigenvalues and a gradient-based update for the eigenvectors on the Stiefel manifold, which also avoids projection. The proposed EM method is evaluated against projected gradient ascent on a real-world product recommendation dataset, where it is shown to be more robust to initialization, more effective in data-poor settings, and computationally faster.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The paper's main strength lies in its elegant formulation of the DPP learning problem within the EM framework. Applying EM is a standard technique, but its application here, through the eigendecomposition and the introduction of a well-motivated latent variable, is novel and clever. It directly targets a clear and known weakness of the more straightforward gradient ascent approach—the problematic projection step. The resulting algorithm, particularly the closed-form update for the eigenvalues which inherently satisfies the constraints, is a notable methodological contribution.

The experimental evaluation is reasonably well-executed. The use of a large, real-world dataset is commendable. The authors provide a nuanced analysis by testing two different initialization schemes (random vs. data-informed) and considering both data-rich and data-poor scenarios. The investigation into the correlation between EM's performance gains and the importance of off-diagonal kernel entries provides a convincing explanation for the observed results. The finding that EM is also computationally more efficient is a significant practical advantage.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that temper its contributions.

1.  **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The central claim of the paper rests on the superiority of EM over projected gradient ascent (KA). While KA is a natural baseline, it is also a relatively simple one. The failure mode described—projection leading to poor local optima—is a known issue for this class of algorithms. A more compelling case would require comparison against more sophisticated constrained optimization methods. For example, interior-point methods or log-det barrier methods are standard for handling PSD constraints and may not suffer from the same pathologies as simple projection. Without this comparison, it is difficult to ascertain whether the proposed EM algorithm is truly state-of-the-art or simply better than a weak baseline.

2.  **Methodological Rigor of the M-Step:** The optimization of the eigenvectors in the M-step is not a full maximization but rather a single gradient step. This makes the proposed algorithm a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm, a detail that should be stated and discussed. More critically, the line search procedure described for this step is unorthodox. Instead of evaluating the M-step objective (the lower bound F), the authors check for improvement in the true log-likelihood (L) for each trial step size, which requires re-running the E-step. This procedure blurs the line between a principled EM algorithm and a direct gradient-based optimization of the likelihood itself, and its convergence properties are not clear. Furthermore, the validity of the gradient expression in Equation (20) across all iterations is not clearly established in the main text, which could be a critical omission.

3.  **Generalizability of Results:** The empirical evidence is drawn from a single application domain (product recommendation). While the results are positive, DPPs are used in a variety of other areas such as document summarization and sensor placement. Demonstrating the method's effectiveness in these other domains would be necessary to substantiate the broad claims of robustness and superiority.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and elegant EM-based formulation for learning an unconstrained DPP kernel.
*   The method directly addresses a known flaw in the standard projected gradient ascent approach.
*   The resulting algorithm is shown to be more robust and computationally faster than the baseline on a realistic, large-scale task.
*   The paper is clearly written and the core ideas are well-motivated.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental comparison is limited to a single, simple baseline. The lack of comparison to more advanced constrained optimization methods weakens the claims of superiority.
*   The M-step for the eigenvectors relies on a single-step, generalized EM approach with an unconventional line search, raising questions about its theoretical grounding and convergence.
*   Empirical validation is confined to a single dataset and application, limiting the generality of the conclusions.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the literature on learning DPPs. The proposed EM algorithm is well-motivated and demonstrates clear practical advantages over the most direct baseline. However, my enthusiasm is moderated by the limited scope of the experimental comparison and some questions regarding the methodological rigor of the M-step optimization. While the approach is promising, the claims of its general superiority are not yet fully substantiated. Nevertheless, the novelty of the EM formulation and the positive results on a non-trivial problem make it a worthwhile contribution to the community.
