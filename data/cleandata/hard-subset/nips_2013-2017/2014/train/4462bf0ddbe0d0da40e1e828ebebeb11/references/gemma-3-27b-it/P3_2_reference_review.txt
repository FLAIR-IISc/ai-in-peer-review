## Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The problem of learning DPPs is known to be challenging, and the authors effectively address limitations of existing methods (gradient ascent and partial learning). The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and offers a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of learning the kernel matrix *L* (or its equivalent *K*) of a DPP from observed subsets. Learning *L* directly is computationally difficult, prompting previous work to focus on learning partial parameters or assuming specific kernel forms. The authors propose an EM algorithm that leverages the eigendecomposition of *L* to circumvent the need for problematic projection steps encountered in naive gradient ascent. This eigendecomposition allows them to introduce a hidden variable representing the eigenvectors contributing to the observed subsets, enabling a more stable and potentially faster optimization process.

The authors accurately position their work within the existing literature. They clearly articulate the drawbacks of previous approaches – the convexity limitations of scalar weight learning [2], the scalability issues and loss of DPP properties in L-ensemble methods [3], and the parametric constraints of Bayesian approaches [8].  The connection to the work on k-DPPs [3] is well highlighted. The inclusion of references to foundational DPP work [10, 21] and applications [4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9] provides excellent context.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The EM approach based on eigendecomposition is a significant contribution. It addresses a core challenge in DPP learning and offers a principled alternative to existing methods.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the EM updates, particularly the closed-form eigenvalue update and the gradient for the eigenvectors, appears correct and well-motivated.  The connection to k-DPPs and the use of marginalization algorithms are clever and efficient.
* **Practical Advantages:** The paper highlights the avoidance of projection steps that plague gradient ascent, leading to more robust learning and potentially faster convergence. The empirical results support this claim.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are intuitive. The inclusion of algorithmic details (Algorithms 1 & 2) is highly beneficial.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the baby registry dataset demonstrate the efficacy of the EM algorithm compared to gradient ascent, especially in settings with stronger item interactions and limited data. The runtime analysis provides further support for the practical advantages of the proposed approach.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While theoretically elegant, the implementation of the EM algorithm, particularly the Stiefel manifold update for the eigenvectors, might be non-trivial.  The authors acknowledge this and provide references, but a deeper discussion of implementation challenges would be valuable.
* **Step Size Tuning:** The paper mentions the need for step size tuning during the eigenvector update but does not provide explicit guidance on how this should be done in practice. Further detail on a practical step size selection strategy would strengthen the paper.
* **Initialization Sensitivity:** While EM is shown to be more robust than gradient ascent, the performance still appears dependent on the initialization strategy. The impact of different initialization schemes could be explored further.
* **Scalability beyond N=71:** The experiments are conducted on datasets with a moderate number of items (N ~ 71). The scalability of the EM algorithm to significantly larger datasets (e.g., N > 1000) remains an open question and could be discussed.



**4.  Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a fundamental problem in DPP learning.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound solution.
* Demonstrates empirical advantages over existing methods.
* Clear and well-written paper.
* Significant potential impact on applications using DPPs.

**Con:**

* Implementation might be complex.
* Scalability to very large datasets is not fully explored.



**5.  Review Criteria Breakdown:**

* **Quality:** Excellent. The paper is technically sound, the derivations are correct, and the experiments are well-designed.
* **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.
* **Originality:** High. The EM approach is a novel and significant contribution to the field.
* **Significance:** High. The work has the potential to advance the state of the art in DPP learning and to enable new applications.



**6.  Final Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.**

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The authors have presented a strong and compelling contribution to the field of DPP learning. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above – providing more details on step size tuning, implementation complexities, and scalability concerns – would further strengthen the paper.  I believe this work will be of significant interest to the NIPS community.
