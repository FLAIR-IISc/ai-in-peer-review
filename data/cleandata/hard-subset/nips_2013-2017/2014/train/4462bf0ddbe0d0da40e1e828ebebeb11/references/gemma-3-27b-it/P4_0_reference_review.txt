This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing the challenge of learning the kernel matrix L from example subsets. Existing methods either partially learn L, assume restrictive parameterizations, or suffer from optimization issues like degeneracy due to projection steps when using gradient ascent. The authors leverage the eigendecomposition of L to formulate an EM algorithm that avoids the problematic projection, potentially leading to more robust and efficient learning, especially when negative interactions between items are important.

The paper is well-structured, starting with a clear introduction to DPPs and the problem of kernel learning. The background section provides necessary mathematical foundations, including the relationship between L and the marginal kernel K. The core of the paper details the EM algorithm, including projected gradient ascent as a baseline, the variable change to eigendecomposition, lower-bounding the objective, and the coordinate ascent procedure. The experimental section focuses on a product recommendation task using baby registry data, comparing the proposed EM algorithm to gradient ascent.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the EM algorithm appears mathematically sound. The use of eigendecomposition and Jensen’s inequality are standard techniques for developing EM algorithms, and the authors clearly articulate the steps and motivations. The handling of the M-step, particularly the updates for eigenvalues and eigenvectors, seems well-considered, with appropriate references to relevant optimization techniques.
*   **Novelty:**  While DPPs have been studied extensively, the proposed EM algorithm offers a novel approach to learning the kernel without restrictive assumptions or problematic projections. The use of the eigendecomposition to circumvent the projection step is a significant contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details. Equations are well-defined and the flow of the derivation is easy to follow. The inclusion of pseudocode for the algorithms (KA and EM) is helpful.
*   **Significance:** The problem addressed is relevant to several applications involving subset selection and diversity. If the EM algorithm indeed provides improved robustness and efficiency, it could have a significant impact on the practical use of DPPs. The experimental results on the baby registry dataset provide some evidence of this improvement.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation is limited to a single application (baby registries) and dataset. While the results are promising, it’s unclear how well the algorithm generalizes to other domains and datasets with different characteristics. Additional experiments with diverse datasets would strengthen the claims.
*   **Comparison Details:**  While the paper compares EM to K-Ascent (KA), the details of the hyperparameter tuning (step sizes, convergence criteria) for both algorithms could be more explicit.  A more thorough comparison, potentially including other DPP learning methods, would be beneficial.
*   **Complexity Discussion:** The paper mentions the asymptotic runtime complexity of EM and KA but doesn’t thoroughly explore the practical implications of these complexities.  A more detailed analysis of the computational cost, especially for large-scale datasets, would be valuable.
*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** Although the authors address initialization issues, a deeper investigation of the impact of different initialization strategies on the performance and convergence of the EM algorithm would be useful.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound EM algorithm for learning DPPs.
*   The algorithm addresses a significant challenge in DPP learning—avoiding problematic projections during optimization.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the algorithm’s effectiveness in a practical application.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
*   The comparison to other DPP learning methods is not comprehensive.
*   The discussion of computational complexity could be more detailed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of DPP learning. The proposed EM algorithm is a clever and effective solution to a challenging problem. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the results are encouraging and demonstrate the potential of the algorithm. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the suggestions for expanding the experimental evaluation and providing more details on hyperparameter tuning and computational complexity.
