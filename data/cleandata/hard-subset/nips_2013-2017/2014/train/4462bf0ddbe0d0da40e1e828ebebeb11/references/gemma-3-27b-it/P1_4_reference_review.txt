## Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix *L* (or its marginal form *K*) for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) from observed subsets. The authors argue that directly maximizing the likelihood via gradient ascent suffers from issues with maintaining positive semi-definiteness of *L* and often leads to degenerate solutions (near-diagonal *L*).  They propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that, by operating in the eigendecomposition space of *L*, avoids the problematic projection step of gradient ascent and allows for learning a more expressive, non-diagonal *L*. The core idea is to introduce a hidden variable representing the eigenvectors contributing to each observed subset, and then perform EM updates on the eigenvalues and eigenvectors.  The paper provides theoretical justification for the approach and demonstrates its efficacy on a product recommendation task using a baby registry dataset.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper positions itself within the existing literature on DPP learning. It correctly identifies prior approaches – learning scalar weights for rows of *L* [2], learning weights for a linear combination of DPPs [3], and Bayesian methods with parametric *L* [8] – as incomplete or limiting.  The proposed EM algorithm distinguishes itself by learning a fully non-parametric *L* without restrictive assumptions. The work builds upon the known eigendecomposition properties of DPPs [21] and leverages them for efficient optimization.  The connection to k-DPPs [3] is also well established and used for efficient computation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The EM approach for learning DPPs without restrictive parameterizations is a significant contribution. The use of the eigendecomposition and introduction of the hidden variable for eigenvectors are clever and well-motivated.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the EM updates appears rigorous and is backed by appropriate references to matrix calculus and optimization theory. The explanation of the advantages over gradient ascent (avoiding projection) is convincing.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on the baby registry dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of the EM algorithm, showing it outperforms gradient ascent, especially when negative interactions between items are crucial.  The ablation study examining the impact of initialization is insightful.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the algorithm is presented in a digestible format. The inclusion of the runtime analysis is helpful.
* **Practical Significance:** Learning DPPs effectively has implications for various applications, including recommendation systems, document summarization, and sensor placement. The results suggest the proposed algorithm could improve performance in these domains.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors claim asymptotic runtime improvements, the runtime analysis focuses on idealized conditions. The practical overhead of the EM algorithm, particularly the step size search, might not always translate to faster execution in real-world scenarios, particularly for very large *N*.  A more detailed runtime comparison with gradient ascent on larger datasets would be beneficial.
* **Sensitivity to Initialization:** While the paper highlights the importance of initialization, the provided initialization strategies (Wishart and moment matching) may not be optimal for all datasets. Exploring other initialization techniques or adaptive initialization strategies could further improve performance.
* **Limited Experimentation:** The evaluation is primarily focused on one dataset and one application (product recommendation).  Exploring other datasets and applications would strengthen the generalizability claims.
* **Handling of Convergence:** The convergence criterion (δ < c) is somewhat vague.  Providing more details on the choice of ‘c’ and the stopping behavior of the algorithm would increase transparency.
* **Step Size Selection:** While the paper acknowledges the need for step size selection, the description of this process could be more detailed.  How is the “average of T2 iterations” used to determine the best step size?

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a challenging and important problem in DPP learning.
* The proposed EM algorithm is a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The empirical results demonstrate the algorithm’s effectiveness and advantages over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The runtime complexity analysis could be more nuanced and detailed.
* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.
* The algorithm’s sensitivity to initialization warrants further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of DPP learning. The proposed EM algorithm is a clever and effective solution to a challenging problem, and the empirical results are promising.  While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more detailed discussion of runtime performance on larger datasets, a clearer explanation of the step size selection process, and consideration of additional initialization strategies.



**Confidence:** 8/10
