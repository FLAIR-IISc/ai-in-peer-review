Summary
The paper addresses maximum likelihood learning of unconstrained determinantal point processes (DPPs). Rather than optimizing entries of the marginal kernel K with projected gradient (which can lead to degenerate, near-diagonal solutions), the authors parameterize K via its eigendecomposition K = VΛV^T and derive a generalized EM algorithm that treats the subset of active eigenvectors J as a latent variable. Using the known mixture view of DPPs as combinations of elementary DPPs, the E-step computes the posterior over J given each observed set Yi. The authors show this posterior is a k-DPP (k = |Yi|) with a kernel that admits low-rank structure, enabling efficient computation of marginals p(j ∈ J | Yi). The M-step yields: (i) a simple, closed-form update for eigenvalues λj using these marginals; and (ii) a single gradient step for eigenvectors on the Stiefel manifold using a specialized gradient expression valid when q = pK(J | Yi). The resulting algorithm avoids PSD/eigenvalue clipping, is claimed to be asymptotically faster than projected gradient when observed sets are small (k ≪ N), and is empirically more robust. Experiments on a large Amazon baby registry dataset show higher test likelihoods and roughly 2× speedups over projected gradient with a Wishart initializer, and comparable performance with a stronger moments-matching initializer; EM performs better in data-poor regimes.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself against: (a) learning only item-quality weights (diagonal scaling) which is convex but cannot capture diversity/similarity; (b) mixtures of fixed DPPs (losing DPP structure and expensive storage); and (c) Bayesian parametric forms for L. Using the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs view for sampling is standard, but leveraging it for EM over the full spectrum (V, Λ) with k-DPP posteriors and Stiefel updates is, to my knowledge, novel relative to prior ML-focused DPP learning work.

Quality and correctness
- The EM formulation is sound. The Jensen lower bound and coordinate-ascent structure are standard; the E-step choice q = pK(J | Yi) is correct, and identifying it as a k-DPP is an elegant observation that enables tractable posterior marginals via low-rank structure.
- The eigenvalue update λ′j = (1/n) Σi p(j ∈ J | Yi) is intuitive and removes the need for eigenvalue projection; using k-DPP marginals to compute these expectations in O(n N k^2) appears plausible given the rank-k structure of QYi (details delegated to supplement).
- The eigenvector update is more heuristic: the gradient expression (Eq. 20) is valid only when q = p and only for the “first” step. The algorithm accordingly takes a single Stiefel-manifold step per M-step and line-searches on step size. This is a generalized EM; while monotonic increase in the lower bound F is not guaranteed, the authors check improvements in likelihood L at each trial, which in practice should prevent degradation. A short discussion or guarantee (even empirical) of monotonicity in L across EM iterations would strengthen the work.
- Complexity claims: KA is O(T1 n N^3 + T1 T2 N^3), dominated by per-example inverses and projections. EM is O(T1 n N k^2 + T1 T2 N^3), with the first term from eigenvalue updates and gradient computation; the second from repeated Stiefel updates. It would be helpful to clarify the cost of evaluating the likelihood during line search (which requires determinants for each Yi) and whether it contributes an additional O(n k^3) or O(n N^3) term in practice. The claimed empirical speedups suggest the overhead is manageable for N ≈ 70.
- Numerical stability: since QYi involves R = diag(√(λ/(1−λ))), keeping λ bounded away from 1 is important; the pragmatic clipping mentioned is reasonable but may bias updates. A safer parameterization (e.g., logit transform) could be discussed.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-structured. A few notational points could be improved:
- In Lemma 1, clarify that UYi denotes the submatrix of U = V^T consisting of columns indexed by items Yi (so ZYi = UYi UYi^T is N×N and rank ≤ |Yi|), to avoid dimension confusion.
- Make explicit how p(j ∈ J | Yi) is computed in O(N k^2) (e.g., via eigendecomposition of a k×k matrix in the span of R UYi).
- The algorithmic description sometimes alternates between checking L for line search and F for convergence; clarify the exact criterion used to guarantee progress.

Originality
The key novelty is the EM formulation over the full spectral parameterization of K with a k-DPP E-step and a manifold-constrained M-step for V. Prior learning methods either constrain L/K or parameterize L in specific forms. Framing learning through the elementary-DPP mixture latent variable and making it computationally viable is an original and insightful contribution.

Significance
Learning unconstrained DPPs is important for applications where modeling repulsion matters. The proposed method addresses a practical pain point (degeneracy of projected gradient) and shows improved robustness and speed on a sizable real dataset. While N here is moderate, the method should be of interest to practitioners and researchers working with DPPs, and it opens the door to more principled learning beyond diagonal scaling or parametric assumptions.

Strengths
- Elegant EM formulation leveraging known DPP structure; closed-form eigenvalue updates without projection.
- Efficient use of k-DPP posteriors; good computational scaling when k ≪ N.
- Principled handling of V with Stiefel manifold updates; avoids eigenvector projection artifacts.
- Solid empirical evaluation with both naive and data-informed initializations; analysis tying gains to off-diagonal strength; code/data released.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- The V-update is heuristic (single step with a gradient valid only at q = p). Consider:
  - Reporting likelihood vs iterations to empirically demonstrate monotonic increases.
  - Exploring alternative Riemannian optimization (e.g., retraction-based methods) that avoid repeated matrix exponentials and allow multiple steps while maintaining feasibility.
- Limited domains: validate on additional tasks (image search, summarization) and larger N, exploring low-rank parameterizations to mitigate O(N^3) costs.
- Baselines: compare to parametric Bayesian learning [Affandi et al.] and to stronger non-projection optimizers (e.g., Riemannian trust-region on K with spectral constraints).
- Practicalities: discuss numerical stabilization for λ near 1 via constrained parameterization; provide more detail on computing P(Y) efficiently during line search.
- Clarify complexity accounting for likelihood evaluations in practice.

Minor comments
- The motivation for rescaling the Wishart draw is good; include statistics of learned set-size distributions to show calibration.
- The metric d used to correlate gains with off-diagonality is insightful; consider including it systematically in the experiments.

Recommendation
Overall, this is a strong, well-motivated paper with a novel and practically useful learning algorithm for DPPs. Despite some heuristic elements in the M-step and limited breadth of experiments, the contribution is significant and likely to influence future work on DPP learning. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifications and minor additions suggested above.
