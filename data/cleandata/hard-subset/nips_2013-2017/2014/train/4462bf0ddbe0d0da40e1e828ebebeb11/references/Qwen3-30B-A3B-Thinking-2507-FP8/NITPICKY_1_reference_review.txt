

# Review: Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes

This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the marginal kernel K of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) without assuming fixed values or restrictive parameterizations. The authors argue that their approach overcomes degeneracy issues inherent in projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) methods, which require projection steps that often produce nearly diagonal matrices failing to model diversity. The paper develops a theoretically grounded EM framework that exploits the eigendecomposition of K, and provides experimental evidence on a baby registry dataset showing improved robustness and faster runtime compared to K-Ascent.

**Strengths:** The paper addresses a meaningful limitation in DPP learning literature. The theoretical development is sound, with clear connections between the eigendecomposition of K and the EM algorithm. The experimental results are well-designed, demonstrating consistent improvements across multiple categories, with a particularly strong advantage in settings with strong negative interactions between items. The runtime analysis is well-motivated and matches the empirical observations.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the strong contributions, the paper contains several technical inconsistencies and formatting issues that undermine its technical rigor:

1. **Parameterization confusion:** The paper frequently switches between learning L (the kernel matrix) and K (the marginal kernel) without clear justification. While they state in Section 3 that they "learn the marginal kernel K" and note "This is equivalent to learning L, as Equation (1) can be applied to convert from K to L," the paper inconsistently refers to "learning L" in the introduction and "learning K" in later sections. This confusion is particularly problematic when discussing the projection step, as the projection applies to K (not L) but the paper states "a projection step can sometimes lead to learning a nearly diagonal L."

2. **Technical detail inconsistencies:** In Section 3.1, the authors state "the computational complexity of KA is dominated by the matrix inverses of the L derivative," but the derivative is with respect to K, not L (the derivative is with respect to K, not L). This is a critical technical error that undermines their runtime analysis.

3. **Formatting and typo issues:** There are several typos and formatting inconsistencies throughout the paper. For example, "EM g in an average of 3.7%" (Section 4.1) should be "EM gains." The references contain inconsistent punctuation (e.g., double commas in some entries), and the paper inconsistently refers to "k-DPPs" versus "k-DPP" in the text.

4. **Missing details:** The paper claims the EM algorithm is "asymptotically faster" but fails to provide a clear comparison of the constants in the runtime complexity. The analysis states the EM runtime is O(T1nNk^2 + T1T2N^3), but doesn't clarify how this compares to KA's runtime in terms of the actual constants, which is crucial for understanding the magnitude of the improvement.

5. **Methodological gap:** The paper mentions that the moments-matching initializer "alleviates KA's projection problem" but doesn't provide a theoretical explanation for why this works better than the Wishart initialization. The experimental results show only a small improvement (0.4% average gain) with this initializer, yet the paper doesn't explore whether this is sufficient to fully overcome KA's limitations.

**Additional concerns:** The paper claims the EM algorithm "is asymptotically faster than gradient ascent," but the runtime analysis assumes the same number of iterations (T1) for both algorithms, which is questionable. In practice, the EM algorithm might require more iterations to converge than gradient ascent, potentially offsetting the per-iteration speed advantage.

**Conclusion:** While the paper presents a theoretically sound approach with empirical validation that addresses a meaningful limitation in DPP learning, the technical inconsistencies, parameterization confusion, and minor formatting errors are significant enough to undermine the paper's overall quality. The switching between L and K without clear justification is particularly problematic for a paper claiming to be meticulously developed. The runtime analysis contains a critical technical error that affects the paper's main claim about efficiency.

The paper has a clear contribution to the DPP learning literature, but these technical flaws prevent it from meeting NeurIPS's standards for a well-executed scientific contribution. The authors should carefully revise the paper to resolve these inconsistencies before considering it for publication.

**Recommendation:** Reject.
