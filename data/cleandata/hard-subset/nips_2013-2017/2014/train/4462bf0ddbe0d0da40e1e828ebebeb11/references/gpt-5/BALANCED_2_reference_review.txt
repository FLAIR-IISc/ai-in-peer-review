Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses maximum-likelihood learning of determinantal point processes (DPPs) in the unconstrained, nonparametric setting. Instead of optimizing the entries of the marginal kernel K directly with projected gradient ascent (KA), the authors exploit the eigendecomposition K = VΛV^T and derive a generalized EM algorithm. They introduce a hidden variable J indexing a subset of eigenvectors, leveraging the well-known mixture-of-elementary-DPP representation: a DPP can be seen as a mixture over all subsets J with mixture weights determined by eigenvalues and component likelihoods given by elementary DPPs. Using Jensen’s inequality yields a lower bound whose E-step sets q(J|Y) = pK(J|Y), shown to be a k-DPP. The M-step separates: eigenvalues admit a closed-form update via expected inclusion probabilities (computable using k-DPP marginals), and eigenvectors are updated by a single gradient step on the Stiefel manifold (matrix exponential update). The authors argue this avoids the degeneracy induced by eigenvalue projection in KA (which can drive many eigenvalues to zero and trap the optimizer near diagonal K). They analyze computational complexity, claiming O(T1 n N k^2 + T1 T2 N^3) versus KA’s O(T1 n N^3 + T1 T2 N^3) (k = max set size), and show empirical speedups. Experiments on an Amazon baby registry dataset compare EM vs KA under two initializations (Wishart and moment-matching), showing EM is more robust (notably when off-diagonal structure matters and in low-data regimes), with median test log-likelihood gains and ~2× runtime advantage.

This builds on prior DPP work used in ML [Kulesza & Taskar; Affandi et al.; Gillenwater et al.], where learning has often been restricted to item-quality weights, mixtures of fixed kernels, or Bayesian parametric forms. The contribution is an unconstrained, nonparametric learner that maintains DPP structure and leverages eigendecomposition, an idea not explored in earlier works to my knowledge.

Strengths
- Technical novelty: A clean EM formulation via the mixture-of-elementary DPP view, with a k-DPP E-step and efficient, interpretable eigenvalue updates. The Stiefel-manifold update for V is appropriate and avoids ad-hoc projection.
- Addresses a practical pain point: elucidates why KA can collapse to near-diagonal K after projection, and demonstrates improved robustness to initialization and data scarcity.
- Complexity insight: For problems with small k relative to N, the eigenvalue/M-step computations scale better than KA’s repeated N×N inverses; the empirical speedup corroborates this.
- Empirical relevance: Real-world dataset (baby registries) with a natural need for diversity and negative interactions; improvements in held-out log-likelihood and a qualitative example illustrate practical benefits.
- Reproducibility: Algorithms are clearly presented; code/data promised.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
Quality
- Convergence guarantees: The method is a generalized EM with a partial M-step (single gradient step for V) and line search on the true likelihood L rather than the lower bound F. Monotonic improvement of the bound and global convergence properties are not established. Clarify that this is generalized EM and discuss convergence guarantees (e.g., to a stationary point under mild conditions), or provide empirical monotonicity plots.
- Gradient for V: Eq. (20) is stated to hold only at the initial point where q = pK. The algorithm uses only one such step per EM iteration; that’s reasonable, but the text could better justify why this is sufficient and how step-size selection with re-evaluated q maintains stability.
- Computational details: The claim that eigenvalue updates are O(n N k^2) hinges on efficient k-DPP marginalization; the main text defers key derivations to the supplement. A brief sketch (or explicit reference to the exact algorithm) would aid clarity. Also, the Stiefel update involves matrix exponentials and repeated eigendecompositions, which dominate at O(N^3). In practice, for what N does the first term outweigh the second?
- Regularization/generalization: Learning a full K without regularization risks overfitting, especially in low-data regimes. The paper demonstrates better test likelihoods, but a discussion and/or experiments with regularization (e.g., trace/nuclear norm, shrinkage priors on λ, graph sparsity) would strengthen the story.
- Baselines: Comparisons are limited to KA. Including parametric DPP learners (e.g., featureized L with learned weights, Bayesian methods [Affandi et al.]) or mixtures of fixed kernels would better situate performance and robustness trade-offs.
- Scale and domains: Experiments are on moderate N (~71) and a single domain. Additional datasets (e.g., document summarization, image search) and larger N would increase significance.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but several important steps are in the supplement (Lemma 1, Eq. (19), Eq. (20)). A compact intuition in the main text would help.
- Minor issues: Typographical errors (e.g., “negelecting”, ambiguous “η ← η2” likely intended as η ← η/2) in algorithms; please correct. Define IY precisely when used in Eq. (3).

Originality
- The EM view using the elementary DPP mixture and hidden J appears original, as is the practical Stiefel update within this context. The identification and analysis of projection-induced degeneracy in KA is insightful.

Significance
- Practical: The method addresses a real need (robust fully nonparametric DPP learning), with speed/robustness advantages that practitioners can use.
- Scientific: Introduces a useful lens (hidden-variable EM) that could inspire further advances (e.g., structured DPPs, featureized kernels, scalable approximations).

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide a convergence statement for your generalized EM (e.g., monotonicity of L under your line search; convergence to a stationary point)?
2. How sensitive is performance to step-size choices and to λ clipping near 1 (numerical stability of R = diag(√(λ/(1−λ))))?
3. What is the precise algorithmic cost of computing q(j∈J|Yi) in Eq. (19) without a full eigendecomposition of QYi? A brief pseudo-code or reference would help.
4. Could you include a synthetic experiment with known ground-truth K to assess recovery and the impact of KA’s projection degeneracy?
5. Have you explored simple regularizers or sparsity priors on K (or Λ) to improve generalization?

Pros
- Novel EM formulation with efficient updates and principled handling of constraints
- Addresses real limitations of projected gradient; improved robustness and speed
- Clear algorithms; real-data evaluation; code available

Cons
- Limited baselines and domains; modest gains in some settings
- Missing theoretical convergence discussion for generalized EM
- Heavy reliance on supplement for key derivations; minor typos
- No regularization or scalability study to larger N

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper presents a principled and practically useful advancement in DPP learning with clear algorithmic ideas and demonstrated benefits. Strengthening convergence discussion, adding broader baselines, and fixing minor clarity issues would make it even stronger.

Confidence: High (familiar with DPP literature and optimization on manifolds).
