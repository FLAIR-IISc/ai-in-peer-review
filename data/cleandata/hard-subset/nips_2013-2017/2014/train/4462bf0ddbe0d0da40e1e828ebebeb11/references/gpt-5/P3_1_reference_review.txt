Summary
The paper tackles maximum likelihood learning of determinantal point processes (DPPs) without restricting the kernel to a parametric form. The key idea is to exploit the eigendecomposition K = VΛV^T and the known mixture representation of a DPP as a mixture over elementary DPPs indexed by a subset J of eigenvectors. Introducing J as a latent variable leads to an EM-style algorithm: the E-step computes the exact posterior q(J|Y) = pK(J|Y), which the authors show is a k-DPP with kernel QY = R ZY R (R = diag(√λ/(1−λ)), ZY = UY UY^T, U = V^T). The M-step updates eigenvalues in closed form via λ′j = (1/n) Σ_i p(j ∈ J | Yi) and takes a single gradient step for V on the Stiefel manifold using a first-step gradient formula derived when q = p. The method is compared to projected gradient ascent on K with spectral clipping. On an Amazon baby-registry dataset across 13 categories, the proposed method achieves higher test log-likelihoods, is more robust to initialization (especially when off-diagonal structure matters), and is about 2x faster asymptotically and in practice.

Relation to prior work
- Prior DPP learning typically restricts L/K: per-item quality weights (convex) or mixtures/parametric kernels; Bayesian approaches sample parameter posteriors. The paper aims to learn a full, nonparametric kernel.
- The mixture representation over elementary DPPs and k-DPP machinery are established; using them to construct an EM procedure that decouples eigenvalues (closed-form) and eigenvectors (manifold update) appears novel.
- The baseline (projected gradient on K) is standard but prone to degeneracy due to spectral clipping.

Strengths
- Methodological novelty: clever use of the DPP mixture over eigenvector subsets to derive an EM-like procedure. The E-step as a k-DPP with a concrete kernel (Lemma 1) is elegant and practically useful.
- Solid algorithmic design: closed-form eigenvalue updates via k-DPP marginals; a principled Stiefel-manifold update for V; and an argument explaining why projection on K can lead to near-diagonal solutions that cannot recover off-diagonals.
- Complexity analysis and practical acceleration: O(n N k^2) per iteration for key steps (with k = max |Yi|), plus N^3 terms for line search, compares favorably to naive projected gradient.
- Empirical evaluation on a realistic dataset with substantial n and moderate N, with evidence of robustness to initialization and data scarcity. The diagnostic relating EM’s advantage to off-diagonal strength in empirical marginals is insightful.
- Reproducibility: code and data made available.

Weaknesses and questions
- EM guarantees/monotonicity: The algorithm is effectively a generalized EM. However, there is some confusion and lack of guarantees:
  - The paper states F(q,V,Λ) is intractable to evaluate exactly, yet Algorithm 2 reports ΔF for stopping. Please clarify what is actually computed. If the line search uses L for acceptance (as stated), then the method ensures non-decreasing likelihood, not a lower bound. Make the generalized EM nature explicit and either remove references to ΔF or provide an estimable surrogate with bounds.
  - The gradient for V (Eq. 20) is derived for q = p (i.e., at the start of the M-step). Subsequent steps do only one update per iteration; this is reasonable but the effect on convergence should be discussed.
- Complexity details: The stated O(n N k^2) omits the cost of inverting HYi (k×k) per example, which is O(k^3) each, leading to O(n k^3) terms. For moderate k this is fine, but please include this term in the complexity and discuss the regimes where it is negligible versus dominant.
- Scalability: The method still incurs O(N^3) costs (eigendecompositions and matrix exponentials on the Stiefel manifold) per step-size search iteration. This limits applicability when N is in the thousands. A discussion of low-rank or Nyström variants (e.g., Affandi et al.) and whether your EM can be adapted would improve the paper.
- Baselines: The experimental comparison is only to the naive projected gradient method. Given prior work on:
  - parametric/Bayesian DPP learning (e.g., Affandi et al.), and
  - mixtures/linear combinations of fixed kernels,
it would strengthen the case to either compare to a strong parametric baseline (e.g., features for quality and similarity with learned weights) or explain why such comparisons are infeasible/unfair in your setting.
- Generality: All experiments are on one domain (baby registries). It would help to include at least one additional domain (e.g., document summarization or image search) to demonstrate generality, or provide more detailed analysis (e.g., MAP set quality, diversity metrics) beyond log-likelihood.
- Numerical stability: R = diag(√λ/(1−λ)) becomes ill-conditioned as λ→1. You mention capping λ below 1; please quantify the ε used and its impact on likelihood and stability.
- Initialization sensitivity: While EM is more robust than KA, absolute sensitivity to initialization is not fully characterized. Report variance across runs and whether moment-matching initialization helps EM further.
- Minor clarity issues:
  - The definition of IY in Eq. (3) (identity with zeros on Y) is unusual; a short derivation or pointer would help.
  - BYi notation is introduced late; consider integrating its meaning earlier to ease reading Eq. (20).
  - Some derivations are deferred to the supplement; providing at least intuition for Lemma 1 and Eq. (19) would aid readability.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled use of DPP structure to enable learning of unconstrained kernels.
- Clear algorithmic contributions with practical speedups and robustness.
- Empirically relevant problem with a convincing real-world dataset.
- Techniques (k-DPP posteriors, manifold updates) likely to be adopted by others working with DPPs.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited baselines and domains; empirical scope could be broader.
- Convergence analysis and objective tracking (F vs L) need clarification.
- Scalability to larger N remains challenging and is not addressed experimentally.

Suggestions
- Clarify the generalized EM nature and what objective is monitored; ensure monotone increase of L is reported per iteration.
- Include the O(n k^3) term in complexity and discuss practical k.
- Add at least one more baseline (parametric DPP learning) or an ablation showing the benefit of updating V vs only Λ.
- Provide an additional dataset or more downstream task metrics.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The methodological contribution is solid and relevant, the paper is clearly written, and the empirical evidence is supportive. Addressing the clarity and experimental scope concerns would make it stronger.
