**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. Maximum likelihood estimation is known to be difficult due to the non-concave objective and the positive semi-definite (PSD) constraint on the kernel. The authors first introduce a baseline method, projected gradient ascent on the marginal kernel `K` (termed K-Ascent or KA), and highlight its primary weakness: the projection step can truncate eigenvalues, leading to near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture the diversity-promoting nature of DPPs. The main contribution is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning `K`. This algorithm is derived by working with the eigendecomposition of `K` and introducing a latent variable corresponding to a selection of eigenvectors, based on the view of a DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs. The E-step computes the posterior over this latent variable, which is shown to be a k-DPP, while the M-step involves a closed-form update for the eigenvalues and a gradient-based update on the Stiefel manifold for the eigenvectors. Experiments on a baby registry dataset suggest that the proposed EM algorithm is more robust to initialization and computationally faster than the KA baseline.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The problem of unconstrained DPP kernel learning is fundamental and has been a significant bottleneck for the broader application of DPPs. This paper presents a novel and elegant EM-based approach that directly addresses this problem without resorting to the restrictive parameterizations common in prior work. The idea of leveraging the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs formulation to define latent variables for an EM algorithm is a substantial conceptual contribution.

2.  **Technical Approach:** The derivation of the EM algorithm is clever, particularly the identification of the posterior in the E-step as a k-DPP (Lemma 1), which allows for efficient computation. The use of Stiefel manifold optimization for the eigenvector update is principled and avoids the problematic projection step that plagues the baseline method.

3.  **Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is generally well-written and provides a clear motivation for the problem. The limitations of the projected gradient ascent baseline are cogently explained, setting the stage effectively for the proposed EM solution.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the novelty of the core idea, the paper suffers from a substantial number of technical inaccuracies, unclear derivations, and formatting errors that undermine its overall quality.

1.  **Fundamental Formulation Issues:**
    *   The likelihood function in Equation (3), `P(Y) = |det(K − I_Y)|`, appears to be incorrect or at least non-standard and poorly explained. Standard literature (e.g., [1, Eq 3.69]) gives the probability as `det( (I-K)_{Y^c} )`. The formulation used here, involving subtracting a partial identity matrix from `K`, is confusing.
    *   This issue propagates to the gradient calculation in Equation (5). The derivative of `log(|det(X)|)` is not simply `(X^{-1})`, it involves a `sign(det(X))` term that is ignored. Furthermore, since the matrix `K - I_Y` is not guaranteed to be positive or negative definite, its determinant could be zero, making the log-likelihood and its gradient undefined. This potential technical error in the core objective function for both the baseline and the proposed method is a major concern.

2.  **Lack of Rigor in Algorithm Description:**
    *   The M-step for the eigenvectors `V` is not a true maximization but a single gradient step, making the algorithm a Generalized EM (GEM) scheme. This distinction is not made, and convergence properties are not discussed.
    *   The gradient for `V` in Equation (20) is stated to be valid only when `q=p`, i.e., at the beginning of the M-step. Using this single, potentially stale, gradient for the entire `V` update and line search is a crude approximation whose impact is not analyzed.
    *   The line search procedure for `V` is described ambiguously. The text states, "we update q's distribution to match the updated V and Λ... and then determine if the current step size is good by checking for improvement in the likelihood L." This implies performing a full E-step for every trial step size, which is computationally intensive and blurs the lines between the E and M steps.
    *   In Algorithm 2, the step size update at the end of the main loop is `η ← 2η`. This is a highly aggressive and unusual schedule for a learning rate, which is typically decreased or adapted more cautiously. This choice is not justified and seems likely to cause instability.
    *   In Algorithm 1, the line search appears to have a typo: `η ← η2` should presumably be `η ← η/2`.

3.  **Clarity and Formatting Deficiencies:**
    *   A stray date, "Tuesday, August 5, 14", appears at the bottom of page 3.
    *   The notation `vjv > j` in Equations (1) and (6) is non-standard; it should be `v_j v_j^T`.
    *   The variable `k` is defined as `maxi |Yi|`, which is missing the subscript `i` on the `max`.
    *   The definition of the diversity measure `d = 1N ||M ||F ||diag(M)||2` in Section 4.1 is peculiar. The squaring of only one norm in the ratio is not standard and is not justified.
    *   The reference list is improperly formatted, with missing venue information and stray commas (e.g., `[1]`, `[2]`). This suggests a lack of care in the manuscript's preparation.
    *   Figure 1 is missing a y-axis label.
    *   The formula for moment matching initialization (Eq. 23) is presented without derivation, though it can be inferred from the second-order marginal probability of a DPP. A brief justification would improve clarity.

4.  **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The empirical validation is confined to a single type of dataset. While interesting, its specificity makes it difficult to assess the general applicability of the proposed method. Testing on other benchmark tasks for DPPs (e.g., document summarization) would be necessary to establish broader utility.
    *   The only baseline is a straightforward projected gradient ascent method. A comparison to other existing learning techniques, such as the Bayesian method of [8] mentioned in the introduction, is conspicuously absent.
    *   Key claims about KA learning a near-diagonal matrix are supported only by an indirect correlation. A direct analysis, for example, by plotting the eigenvalue spectra or a diagonality metric for the learned kernels from KA and EM, would provide much stronger evidence.

**Decision**

While the paper introduces a promising and elegant EM-based approach for a crucial problem in the DPP literature, the execution is marred by a significant number of flaws. These range from minor notational and formatting issues to potentially critical errors in the core mathematical formulation of the likelihood function. The algorithmic description lacks precision, and the experimental evaluation, while encouraging, is too narrow. The sheer volume of minor issues, combined with the major questions about the technical correctness of the objective function, prevents me from recommending acceptance in its current form. The core idea has merit, but the paper requires a thorough revision to address these numerous and substantial weaknesses.

**Recommendation: Reject**
