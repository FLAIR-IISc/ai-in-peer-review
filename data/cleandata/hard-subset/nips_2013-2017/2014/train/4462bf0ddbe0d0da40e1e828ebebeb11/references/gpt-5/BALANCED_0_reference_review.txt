Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles maximum-likelihood learning for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without restrictive parameterizations of the kernel. The authors propose an EM-style algorithm that works in the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K, introducing a latent subset J of eigenvectors (the “elementary DPP” mixture view). The E-step sets q(J|Y) = pK(J|Y), which the authors show is a k-DPP with a computable kernel. The M-step yields a closed-form update for eigenvalues via k-DPP marginals and a gradient step for eigenvectors on the Stiefel manifold. They argue this avoids the degeneracy and inefficiency of projected gradient ascent on K (which tends to collapse to near-diagonal matrices after eigenvalue clipping), and they provide a runtime analysis showing O(T1 n N k^2 + T1 T2 N^3) vs O(T1 n N^3 + T1 T2 N^3) for a KA baseline, favorable when k ≪ N.

This builds on classic DPP machinery (marginal and L-ensembles), the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs view, and prior work on learning restricted DPPs: diagonal scaling [Kulesza & Taskar], mixtures of fixed L-ensembles [Gillenwater/Kulesza/Taskar], Bayesian parametric learning [Affandi et al.], and Nyström/approximate inference. To my knowledge, using EM over the eigen-subset latent J to learn a full, unconstrained kernel has not been previously proposed, and the algorithm leverages k-DPP marginalization techniques.

Empirically, on a real-world Amazon baby registry dataset across 13 categories (N≈71, n≈8.6k), EM improves test log-likelihood over projected gradient ascent (KA) with Wishart initialization (median gains ~3.7%), remains more robust in data-poor settings (n≈2N, ~4.5% gains), and is ~2.1× faster in wall-clock. With a moments-matching initialization, EM and KA are comparable on full data, but EM retains an edge when data are scarce. The paper also illustrates qualitative gains in diversity (avoiding multiple redundant baby monitors).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: an EM formulation using the elementary DPP mixture with J as a latent variable; clean E-step and tractable M-step for eigenvalues using k-DPP marginals.
- Practicality: avoids the problematic PSD/eigenvalue projection steps that can induce near-diagonal collapse in K-based gradient methods; uses Stiefel manifold updates for eigenvectors.
- Computational advantage when k ≪ N, which is common in subset selection.
- Empirical evidence on a sizable real dataset with reproducibility (code/data), and clear analysis of when EM helps most (high off-diagonal/interaction regimes and low-data regimes).
- Clear link to prior DPP work and the NP-hardness conjecture for unrestricted MLE.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- The eigenvector M-step is heuristic: Eq. (20) is stated to be valid only for the initial gradient (q = p), yet the algorithm takes a single gradient step per M-step and uses line search on the true likelihood. Monotonicity of the EM lower bound is not guaranteed, and convergence properties are not theoretically established. A discussion of convergence (e.g., to stationary points of L) would strengthen the claims.
- Some notational/dimensional ambiguities: In Lemma 1, QYi = R ZYi R with ZYi = UYi(UYi)>. As written, ZYi is |Yi|×|Yi|, R is N×N, so QYi’s dimensions do not align. Please clarify the exact construction (e.g., embeddings via selection matrices BYi, or QYi = R U> BYi> BYi U R).
- Pseudocode clarity: the line “η ← η2” appears in both Algorithm 1 and 2—presumably this means halving or doubling. Please disambiguate and standardize the backtracking strategy.
- Baselines: The KA baseline is simple projected gradient. It would be fair to compare against (i) projected L-BFGS, (ii) parameterizations that avoid projection (e.g., optimize eigenvalues via logistic reparameterization and eigenvectors on Stiefel), and (iii) prior learning schemes (e.g., Affandi et al. Bayesian parametrics, mixtures with learned weights), to contextualize EM’s advantages.
- Scalability: Experiments are limited to N up to ~100. Given the N^3 dependence in the manifold step, a study of scaling to larger N (possibly with Nyström approximations) would enhance significance.
- Evaluation breadth: Results focus on log-likelihood. Adding downstream task metrics (e.g., MAP set quality, diversity measures, or simulated recommendation performance) and sensitivity to initialization would make the empirical case stronger.

Quality
Overall technically sound, with a thoughtful use of DPP structure and k-DPP marginalization. The eigenvector update is a pragmatic heuristic without full theoretical backing; the paper compensates by checking improvements in true likelihood during line search. Proof and derivation details (especially Lemma 1 and Eq. 20) should be carefully clarified to avoid dimensional confusion.

Clarity
Generally well-written, with good intuition and motivation. However, there are several notational inconsistencies (dimensions of QYi; ambiguous η updates). The supplement is cited for key derivations; summarizing the most critical steps in the main text would aid readability.

Originality
High. The EM perspective for full-kernel DPP learning via the eigen-subset latent variable is, to my knowledge, novel and nontrivial.

Significance
Moderate to high. Learning unconstrained DPPs robustly and efficiently is important across applications. The empirical gains are modest on average but meaningful in regimes where diversity interactions matter or data are scarce. With broader baselines and larger-scale tests, this could be a go-to method.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Novel EM formulation and algorithmic insight leveraging DPP/k-DPP structure.
- Avoids degeneracy of projected gradients; practical manifold update.
- Measurable improvements in likelihood, robustness, and speed; qualitative diversity benefits.
- Relevance to many NeurIPS topics (probabilistic modeling, discrete subset selection, structured distributions).

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Lack of convergence guarantees for the full EM procedure; eigenvector update is heuristic.
- Some notational and algorithmic clarity issues.
- Limited baselines and scale; evaluation restricted to log-likelihood on one domain.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a careful derivation with correct dimensions for Lemma 1 and Eq. (20); fix pseudocode for η.
- Discuss convergence and monotonicity; if possible, prove that the chosen manifold step with line search yields ascent in L, or bound improvement under reasonable conditions.
- Add stronger baselines (projected L-BFGS, reparameterized eigen optimization) and one additional domain (e.g., summarization or sensor placement).
- Report sensitivity to initialization and ablations on k, T2, and the effect of the λ<1 constraint.
- Consider integrating Nyström or low-rank variants to demonstrate scalability.

Recommendation: Weak accept
Confidence: Medium-high
