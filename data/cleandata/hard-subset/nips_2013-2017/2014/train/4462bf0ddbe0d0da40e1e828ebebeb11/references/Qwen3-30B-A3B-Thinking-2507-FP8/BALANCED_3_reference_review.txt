

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper proposes an EM-based algorithm for learning the marginal kernel K of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without assuming fixed values or restrictive parametric forms. The authors identify a key limitation of previous gradient ascent approaches: they require projection steps that often result in degenerate, near-diagonal K matrices that fail to model the negative interactions between items crucial for diversity. By exploiting the eigendecomposition of K, they develop an EM algorithm that avoids these problematic projections and achieves better log-likelihoods and faster runtime compared to projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent).

The paper builds on foundational work in DPPs (Kulesza & Taskar, 2012; Affandi et al., 2013) while addressing a gap in learning methods. Previous approaches either restricted the learning to scalar weights per row (Kulesza & Taskar, 2011), used L-ensembles (Kulesza & Taskar, 2012), or employed Bayesian methods with parametric assumptions (Affandi et al., 2013). The authors correctly position their work as providing a non-parametric learning approach that maintains the attractive computational properties of DPPs.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear derivation of the EM algorithm (E-step and M-step) and careful analysis of the optimization problem. The theoretical runtime analysis showing asymptotic improvement over gradient ascent is solid. The experimental results on the baby registry dataset demonstrate consistent improvements in log-likelihood (3.7% average gain) and runtime (2.1x faster on average) over K-Ascent. However, the evaluation is limited to a single dataset and lacks comparison to other state-of-the-art DPP learning methods, which weakens the claim of significant improvement. The paper also doesn't adequately address potential failure cases or limitations of the proposed method.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations are clear and well-justified, with sufficient detail for reproduction. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are precise and easy to follow. The experimental setup is thoroughly described, including data preprocessing and initialization methods.

**Originality:** The EM approach leveraging eigendecomposition is novel in the context of unconstrained DPP learning. The authors effectively distinguish their contribution from prior work, particularly in their discussion of the projection problem in gradient ascent. While EM for DPPs has been explored in other contexts (e.g., for sampling), the specific application to learning the full kernel matrix without restrictions is a meaningful contribution to the DPP literature.

**Significance:** The problem of learning DPPs is important for applications requiring diversity in subset selection (recommendation systems, document summarization, etc.). The paper demonstrates practical improvements in log-likelihood and runtime, which are significant for applications where DPPs are used. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the narrow experimental scope and lack of demonstration of how these improvements translate to better performance in downstream applications beyond log-likelihood.

**Strengths:**
- Well-motivated problem addressing a clear limitation of previous methods
- Theoretical justification for avoiding projection steps
- Solid runtime analysis showing asymptotic improvements
- Excellent writing and organization
- Clear experimental comparison showing consistent improvements

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited experimental evaluation (only one dataset, no comparison to state-of-the-art methods)
- Insufficient analysis of how log-likelihood improvements translate to practical application benefits
- No discussion of failure cases or limitations of the proposed method
- Initialization methods not thoroughly evaluated across different settings

**Pro/Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Provides a theoretically sound alternative to gradient ascent for learning DPPs
- Avoids the problematic projection steps that cause degenerate solutions
- Demonstrates consistent improvements in log-likelihood and runtime
- Well-written with clear mathematical derivations
- Addresses an important limitation in current DPP learning methods

*Con:*
- Experimental evaluation is too limited to a single dataset
- Lacks comparison to other state-of-the-art DPP learning methods
- Doesn't demonstrate how improved log-likelihood translates to better application performance
- No discussion of potential limitations or failure cases

**Recommendation:** The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the DPP learning literature by providing a theoretically sound EM algorithm that avoids the projection issues of gradient ascent. While the technical content is strong and well-presented, the limited experimental evaluation weakens the significance claim. I would recommend acceptance with major revisions to address the experimental limitations.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Include comparisons to other state-of-the-art DPP learning methods (e.g., Affandi et al., 2013; Kulesza & Taskar, 2012)
2. Expand the experimental evaluation to multiple datasets across different application domains
3. Add a section discussing limitations and potential failure cases of the proposed method
4. Include a more thorough analysis of how the log-likelihood improvements translate to practical application benefits (e.g., recommendation quality metrics)
5. Consider adding a comparison to the recent work on MAP inference for DPPs (Gillenwater et al., 2012) to further contextualize the contribution

The paper's core technical contribution is sound and valuable, and with expanded evaluation, it would make a strong contribution to the field.
