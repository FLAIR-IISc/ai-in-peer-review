### Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. This is a known hard problem, as direct maximum likelihood estimation is conjectured to be NP-hard. Previous approaches have either learned restricted versions of the kernel (e.g., only quality terms) or used methods that lose the computational benefits of the DPP structure. The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to learn the full, unconstrained kernel. The key idea is to work with the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix, K = VΛVᵀ, and to introduce a latent variable J representing a subset of the eigenvectors. This allows the log-likelihood to be lower-bounded and optimized via alternating E and M steps. The proposed EM algorithm is compared against a projected gradient ascent baseline on the kernel entries (termed K-Ascent). Experiments on a real-world product recommendation dataset show that the EM method is more robust, particularly with uninformative initializations or limited data, and is also computationally faster than the baseline.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Formulation:** The paper presents an elegant and novel application of the EM algorithm to the difficult problem of DPP kernel learning. The choice of the eigenvector index set J as the latent variable is clever, leveraging the known mixture-of-elementary-DPPs representation of a DPP to create a tractable lower bound on the likelihood.

2.  **Addresses a Key Limitation:** The work directly tackles the problem of learning the full DPP kernel, which is crucial for capturing the diversity-promoting (repulsive) interactions between items. This is a significant step beyond prior work that focused on more restricted learning scenarios.

3.  **Methodological Soundness:** The derivations for the EM updates appear correct. The E-step is solved exactly by showing the posterior over the latent variable is itself a k-DPP. The M-step update for the eigenvalues is a simple, closed-form expression. The use of optimization techniques on the Stiefel manifold for the eigenvector update is appropriate for the orthogonality constraint.

4.  **Identifies a Plausible Failure Mode of Baselines:** The authors provide a compelling intuition for why simple projected gradient ascent might fail. The argument that the projection step can prematurely truncate eigenvalues to zero, leading to a near-diagonal kernel from which it is difficult to escape, is a valuable insight and effectively motivates the need for an alternative approach like EM.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Baseline Simplicity:** The primary comparison is against a relatively straightforward projected gradient ascent method (K-Ascent). While this is a reasonable first baseline, it may not represent the strongest possible established approach. The described failure mode (getting stuck in a diagonal-like local optimum) is a known issue for simple projected gradient methods. It is unclear if the performance gap would persist against more sophisticated constrained optimization methods, such as those employing second-order information or more advanced line search and projection techniques. The paper's claims would be stronger if compared against a more robust baseline.

2.  **Incomplete M-step:** The M-step does not fully maximize the objective with respect to the eigenvectors V; instead, it takes a single gradient step. This is a form of a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm. While this is a common and valid practice, the paper presents it as a standard M-step, which is slightly imprecise. The practical implications of this approximation are not discussed.

3.  **Dependence on Initialization:** The experimental results show that the advantage of EM over K-Ascent is most pronounced with a random Wishart initialization. When a more informed "moments-matching" initialization is used, the performance of the two methods becomes comparable in the data-rich setting. This suggests that the main advantage of the proposed EM algorithm may be its robustness to poor initialization rather than its ability to find a superior solution in general. While robustness is a valuable property, this nuance is important.

4.  **Scalability Concerns:** The computational complexity of the algorithm is dominated by O(N³) operations for eigendecomposition and the matrix exponential in the eigenvector update. This effectively limits the applicability of the method to problems with a moderately sized ground set N (the experiments use N ≈ 71). This is a common limitation in DPP literature but it is worth noting that the proposed method does not fundamentally overcome this scaling barrier.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper tackles a central and difficult problem in the DPP literature: learning the full kernel.
*   The proposed EM algorithm is novel, technically sound, and well-motivated.
*   The analysis of the failure mode of projected gradient ascent is insightful.
*   The experimental results, though limited to one domain, demonstrate a clear advantage for the proposed method in terms of robustness and speed under specific, realistic conditions (poor initialization, sparse data).

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The comparison is made against a potentially weak baseline (K-Ascent), which may overstate the benefits of the proposed method.
*   The performance gains are heavily dependent on the quality of the initialization, nearly disappearing with a good data-driven start.
*   The M-step for the eigenvectors is an approximation (a single gradient step), a detail that is not fully elaborated upon.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the literature on learning DPPs. The EM formulation is elegant and provides a principled way to avoid the problematic projection step inherent in a naive gradient-based approach. The paper is well-written and the experiments, while not exhaustive, support the main claims of improved robustness and efficiency. Despite some concerns about the strength of the baseline and the dependence on initialization, the novelty of the approach and the importance of the problem it addresses merit its acceptance. The insights regarding the failure modes of projected gradient methods for this problem are themselves a useful contribution.
