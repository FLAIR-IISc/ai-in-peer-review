**Summary:**
This paper proposes a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. The key idea is to work with the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K. By viewing the DPP sampling procedure as a mixture of elementary DPPs, the authors introduce a latent variable corresponding to the selection of a subset of eigenvectors. This allows for the formulation of an EM-style algorithm that alternates between computing the posterior over this latent variable (E-step) and updating the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of K (M-step). This approach is presented as an alternative to projected gradient ascent on the entries of K, which the authors argue can suffer from poor local optima due to the projection step required to maintain positive semi-definiteness. The proposed EM method is shown to be more robust and asymptotically faster, with experiments on a product recommendation task demonstrating its advantages, particularly with uninformative initializations or in data-poor settings.

**Strengths:**
1.  The paper tackles a central and difficult problem in the application of DPPs: learning the full, unconstrained kernel matrix from data. This is a significant step beyond prior work that focused on more restricted learning settings (e.g., learning weights for rows, or for a fixed ensemble of kernels).
2.  The formulation of the problem in the eigenspace and the use of the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs interpretation to derive an EM algorithm is clever and elegant. This is a non-trivial and original contribution to the literature on learning DPPs.
3.  The experimental results on the baby registry dataset provide compelling evidence for the proposed method's superiority over the projected gradient ascent baseline. The analysis showing EM's particular advantage in settings where diversity (off-diagonal kernel elements) is important is insightful. The practical speed-up is also a valuable result.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the novelty of the core idea, the paper is marred by a substantial number of technical ambiguities, methodological gaps, and presentational flaws.

1.  **Major Technical Concern in the M-step:** The most critical weakness lies in the update for the eigenvectors V. The authors derive a gradient expression in Equation (20), but then state: "Equation (20) is only valid for the first gradient step, so in practice we do not bother to fully optimize V in each M-step; we simply take a single gradient step on V." This is a significant issue. If the gradient formula is only correct for the very first iteration (when q=p), using it for subsequent updates within the EM loop is not justified and potentially incorrect. The algorithm's derivation and convergence properties are therefore cast into serious doubt. Is this an approximation? If so, its validity and impact on the optimization must be analyzed. As presented, this appears to be a major technical flaw.

2.  **Clarity and Technical Precision:**
    *   The paper claims to develop an "EM algorithm." However, since the M-step for V does not fully maximize the objective but merely takes a single gradient step, the algorithm is more accurately described as a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm. This distinction is important for technical correctness.
    *   The notation is occasionally confusing. In Section 3.5, `V_Yi` is defined as `(U_Yi)^T` where `U = V^T`. This is ambiguous. A clearer definition, perhaps specifying that `V_Yi` is the submatrix formed by the rows of V indexed by the set `Y_i`, would greatly improve readability.
    *   Equation (3) presents the probability of a set Y in terms of K as `|det(K - I_Y)|`. This formulation is unusual. While it references Kulesza's thesis, it is less common than other forms and presented without any derivation or intuition, which could confuse readers familiar with DPPs.
    *   The metric `d` used in Section 4.1 to measure the importance of off-diagonal elements is not clearly defined or justified. The definition `d = (1/N) * ||M||_F / ||diag(M)||_2` is opaque. What is the L2-norm of a diagonal matrix in this context—the operator norm (`max|M_ii|`) or the vector norm of the diagonal elements? Why this specific combination of norms? This ad-hoc metric weakens the argument it is meant to support.

3.  **Minor Methodological and Algorithmic Details:**
    *   In Algorithm 2, after a successful line search for the eigenvectors, the step size is updated via `η ← 2η`. This is a very aggressive and unconventional heuristic. Typically, one might reset the step size for the next major iteration. This choice lacks justification and could lead to instability.
    *   The claim that the EM algorithm "negates the need for the problematic projection step" is a slight overstatement. While it avoids the eigenvalue clipping of the baseline, the eigenvector update requires its own sophisticated procedure (the update on the Stiefel manifold via matrix exponential) to handle the `V^T V = I` constraint, which is itself a form of projection. The phrasing could be more precise.

4.  **Formatting and Presentation:**
    *   The manuscript contains numerous signs of a hasty preparation. There is a stray timestamp ("Tuesday, August 5, 14") at the bottom of page 5.
    *   There is a typo in Section 3.5 ("negelecting").
    *   The formatting of the references is highly inconsistent and appears incomplete (e.g., references [1] and [2] end with several superfluous commas). This lack of attention to detail detracts from the overall quality of the submission.

**Recommendation:**
**Reject.**

**Justification for Recommendation:**
The paper presents a promising and original idea for learning DPP kernels. If the methodology were sound and clearly presented, this would be a strong candidate for acceptance. However, the current manuscript suffers from a critical technical flaw concerning the gradient used for the eigenvector updates in the M-step. This issue is severe enough to question the correctness and convergence of the proposed algorithm. Furthermore, this major flaw is compounded by a large number of smaller issues, including unclear notation, unjustified methodological choices, and sloppy presentation and formatting. While the perfectionist reviewer's eye catches even minor formatting issues, the sheer quantity of them here, combined with the major technical ambiguity, makes the paper fall well short of the standard expected for NIPS. The work has significant potential, but it requires a major revision to fix the technical gap in the M-step, clarify the exposition, and polish the manuscript to a professional standard.
