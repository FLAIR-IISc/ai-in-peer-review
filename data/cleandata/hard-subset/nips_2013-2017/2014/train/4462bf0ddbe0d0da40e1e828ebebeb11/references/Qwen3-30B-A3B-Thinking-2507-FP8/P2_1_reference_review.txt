

# Review: "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

## Summary
This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the marginal kernel K of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a key limitation in prior work where gradient ascent approaches require problematic projection steps that often result in degenerate solutions. The authors exploit the eigendecomposition of K to develop an EM-based learning method that avoids these projections, making it more robust to initialization and dataset changes. The paper provides theoretical derivations, a detailed algorithm, and extensive experiments on a real-world baby registry dataset from Amazon, demonstrating that their method outperforms gradient ascent approaches in log-likelihood and runtime.

The paper situates its work well within the literature on DPP learning, distinguishing itself from prior approaches that either learned only partial parameters (e.g., [2]), used L-ensembles (e.g., [3]), or assumed parametric forms for L (e.g., [8]). The authors correctly identify that these approaches fail to learn unconstrained, non-parametric L matrices.

## Strengths

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical development. The derivation of the EM algorithm (Sections 3.2-3.5) is well-motivated and clearly presented. The authors provide explicit formulas for both the E-step (Lemma 1) and M-step updates (Equations 18-21), with computational complexity analysis showing asymptotic runtime improvements over gradient ascent (O(T1nNk² + T1T2N³) vs. O(T1nN³ + T1T2N³)). The experimental design is strong, with careful analysis of initialization strategies (Wishart vs. moments-matching), thorough comparison of log-likelihood performance across 13 categories, and meaningful runtime analysis. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the sensitivity to initialization when starting from a diagonal kernel.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background on DPPs (Section 2) is concise yet comprehensive, providing exactly the right level of detail for readers familiar with machine learning. The transition from problem statement to proposed solution is logical, and the mathematical derivations are presented with clear explanations. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are precise and implementable. The supplementary material (referenced throughout) appears to contain the necessary proofs and additional details for reproducibility.

**Originality**: The core contribution—applying EM to DPP learning via eigendecomposition—is novel and well-motivated. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from prior work, particularly in avoiding the problematic projection steps required by gradient ascent. This is not merely a minor modification but a fundamentally different optimization strategy that leverages the special structure of DPPs. The paper effectively demonstrates that this leads to meaningful improvements in practice, not just theory.

**Significance**: DPPs are widely used in applications requiring subset selection with diversity constraints (e.g., document summarization, product recommendation, sensor placement). The paper demonstrates significant practical improvements in a real-world application (baby registry recommendations), showing that their method achieves better log-likelihoods (up to 16.5% gains in some categories) and is asymptotically faster (2.1x faster on average). These results are likely to be valuable to practitioners working with DPPs, and the method could be adopted in many existing DPP-based applications.

## Weaknesses

The paper is strong overall, but I have two minor concerns:

First, while the paper correctly identifies the limitation of gradient ascent methods (Section 3.1), the explanation of why projection leads to degenerate solutions could be slightly more detailed. Specifically, the connection between the gradient being diagonal when K is diagonal and the resulting inability to learn off-diagonal elements is clear, but a brief example with a small matrix might further clarify this for readers less familiar with the problem.

Second, the paper focuses on learning the marginal kernel K rather than the L-matrix. While the authors correctly note that these are equivalent (via Equation 1), the paper would benefit from a brief discussion of why they chose to work with K rather than L, especially since the DPP literature often uses L as the primary parameterization. This is a minor point, but it would strengthen the connection to prior work.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to the learning of Determinantal Point Processes. The EM algorithm they propose is theoretically sound, practically effective, and represents a meaningful improvement over the state-of-the-art gradient ascent approach. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides both strong theoretical and empirical justification for their method.

The quality of the work is high, the clarity is excellent, the originality is clear, and the significance is substantial. The minor weaknesses noted above do not detract from the paper's overall contribution.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper at NIPS. The work addresses a meaningful problem in a novel way, provides strong theoretical and empirical evidence for its claims, and has clear potential for impact in both research and practice. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it advances the state of the art in probabilistic modeling with clear connections to real-world applications.

The paper meets all four review criteria at a high level, with particular strength in quality and significance. It provides a clear, robust, and efficient method for learning DPPs that should be of interest to the NIPS community working on probabilistic models and subset selection problems.
