### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the challenging non-convex problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. While DPPs are powerful models for capturing quality-diversity tradeoffs, learning the full kernel via maximum likelihood is conjectured to be NP-hard. Previous work has circumvented this by learning restricted or parametric forms of the kernel, or by using Bayesian methods.

The authors' key contribution is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full, unconstrained DPP kernel. The approach is based on the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix `K = VΛV^T`. By viewing a DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs (each corresponding to a subset of eigenvectors), the authors introduce a latent variable `J` representing the chosen subset of eigenvectors. This formulation naturally leads to an EM algorithm:
-   **E-step:** Compute the posterior distribution over the latent variable `J` given an observed subset `Y_i`. The authors show this posterior is itself a tractable conditional DPP (a k-DPP).
-   **M-step:** Update the eigenvalues `Λ` and eigenvectors `V` to maximize the expected complete-data log-likelihood. The eigenvalue update has a closed form, while the eigenvector update, which involves optimization on a Stiefel manifold, is handled with a single multiplicative gradient step to avoid a problematic projection.

The proposed EM method is compared against a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) on a real-world product recommendation task using a baby registry dataset. The experiments show that the EM algorithm is more robust to initialization and data scarcity, and is empirically faster than the baseline.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel and elegant approach to a fundamental problem in the DPP literature. Learning the full kernel is a known bottleneck, and previous methods have significant limitations. Framing the problem in an EM framework by leveraging the eigendecomposition and the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs interpretation is a highly original and insightful contribution. This work provides a new, principled optimization algorithm that is likely to be of significant interest to both researchers and practitioners using DPPs.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The derivation of the EM algorithm is technically sound. The identification of the posterior `p(J|Y)` as a k-DPP (Lemma 1) is a key result that makes the E-step tractable. The derivation of the M-step updates is well-reasoned, and the use of methods for optimization on Stiefel manifolds to update the eigenvectors is appropriate for handling the orthogonality constraint. This avoids the projection step required by naive gradient ascent, which the authors convincingly argue is a major source of poor local optima.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established, the background on DPPs is concise yet sufficient, and the step-by-step derivation of the algorithm is logical. The inclusion of pseudocode for both the proposed method and the baseline is very helpful for understanding the implementation details.

4.  **Thorough Experimental Analysis:** The experiments, while focused on a single dataset, are well-designed and provide compelling evidence for the authors' claims. The comparison against a natural baseline (projected gradient ascent) is fair. The analysis of different initialization strategies (random Wishart vs. data-driven moment matching) and data regimes (full vs. data-poor) provides a nuanced understanding of where the proposed EM algorithm offers the greatest advantages. The qualitative example of the learned "safety" registry in Figure 2 effectively illustrates the failure mode of the baseline and the superiority of the EM-trained model.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity of the Baseline's Complexity:** The runtime analysis claims that K-Ascent (KA) has a complexity of `O(T1*n*N^3 + ...)`, where the `n*N^3` term arises from computing `n` matrix inverses for the gradient. This seems potentially inefficient. Standard DPP likelihood gradient computations (e.g., Kulesza & Taskar, 2011) typically involve inverting a matrix of size `|Y_i| x |Y_i|`, which would lead to a much faster gradient computation of `O(n * k^3)`, where `k` is the maximum subset size. If the authors' implementation indeed computes `n` full `N x N` inverses, the runtime comparison might be unfairly favorable to the EM method. I would urge the authors to clarify this point. Is there a reason this specific gradient form was used? If a more efficient gradient calculation for KA exists, the runtime comparison should be revised or at least discussed.

2.  **Approximation in the M-step:** The M-step for the eigenvectors `V` is approximated by a single gradient step rather than being optimized to convergence. This makes the algorithm a form of Generalized EM (GEM). This is a reasonable and common practical choice, but it would be good to state this explicitly and briefly comment on whether it affects theoretical convergence guarantees (which typically rely on the M-step fully maximizing the objective). The current line search on the true likelihood `L` ensures monotonic improvement, which is a good practical safeguard.

3.  **Scope of Experiments:** While the baby registry dataset is a great real-world test case, the empirical conclusions would be stronger if validated on at least one other standard DPP application domain, such as document summarization or video analysis. This would demonstrate the general applicability of the algorithm and its robustness properties across different data types.

4.  **Context for Complexity:** The complexity of the EM algorithm depends on `k`, the maximum subset size. It would be helpful to report the statistics of `k` in the experimental dataset. This would give readers a better sense of the practical computational cost and how the `O(n*N*k^2)` term compares to the `O(N^3)` term in a real-world setting.

### Decision

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, elegant, and technically sound EM algorithm for the important problem of learning a full DPP kernel.
*   Provides a clear diagnosis of a critical failure mode (collapsing to diagonal solutions) in the standard projected gradient ascent approach and offers a principled solution.
*   Demonstrates superior robustness to initialization and data scarcity on a real-world task.
*   The paper is well-written, and the contribution is significant for the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The runtime comparison with the baseline may be based on an inefficient implementation of the baseline's gradient calculation.
*   Empirical validation is limited to a single dataset.
*   The performance gains over a well-initialized baseline are modest in the full data regime.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. The originality of the EM formulation and its demonstrated robustness represent a clear and significant advance for learning DPPs. The weaknesses are minor and can be readily addressed in a final version. I recommend this paper for acceptance.
