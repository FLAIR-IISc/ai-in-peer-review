This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) – a crucial task for subset selection with quality and diversity considerations – without restrictive assumptions on the kernel L. The authors propose an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm leveraging the eigendecomposition of L, cleverly avoiding the problematic projection steps inherent in naive gradient ascent methods. This is a significant and novel contribution. The core idea of switching the optimization target to the eigenvalues and eigenvectors is insightful and well-motivated, building upon the known properties of DPPs. The connection to hidden variables (the index set J) and the subsequent application of Jensen's inequality are hallmarks of a well-constructed EM approach.

The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The background section provides sufficient context for understanding the DPP formalism, and the derivation of the EM algorithm is presented clearly, though dense. The experiments are well-designed, comparing the EM approach against projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) on a real-world dataset (baby registries). The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the EM algorithm, particularly in scenarios with strong item interactions and limited training data. The inclusion of runtime comparisons further strengthens the argument for the proposed method.

However, the paper isn’t without its shortcomings. While the theoretical justification for the EM algorithm is solid, a more detailed analysis of its convergence properties would be beneficial. The authors mention asymptotic runtime but lack a tight theoretical bound. The discussion of the Stiefel manifold update (Equation 21) is somewhat terse; a more thorough explanation or reference to relevant literature would be helpful. Moreover, the initialization strategy, even with moment matching, seems potentially sensitive. A more robust initialization scheme, or a broader exploration of initialization techniques, could be considered. Finally, while the experiments are convincing, exploring datasets beyond baby registries would broaden the generalizability claims.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The EM algorithm for unconstrained DPP kernel learning is a genuinely novel approach, circumventing limitations of prior work.
*   **Significance:** Learning DPPs without restrictive assumptions has broad implications for applications requiring diverse subset selection.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation is solid, leveraging established DPP properties and EM principles.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Convergence Analysis:** A more detailed convergence analysis would strengthen the theoretical foundation.
*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** The reliance on a specific initialization strategy might limit robustness.
*   **Limited Dataset Exploration:** Experiments are primarily focused on a single dataset.
*   **Stiefel Manifold Detail:** The discussion of the Stiefel manifold update is brief.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite minor flaws, this paper presents a significant and novel contribution to the field of DPP learning. The EM algorithm offers a promising solution to a challenging problem, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its advantages. The authors have successfully addressed a key limitation of previous DPP learning methods. I prioritize the novelty and potential impact of this work, and am willing to overlook the relatively minor shortcomings. The potential benefits of being able to learn DPPs without imposing constraints are substantial.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper deserves to be presented at NIPS, even with the slight imperfections. The bold approach and strong results make it a valuable addition to the literature.
