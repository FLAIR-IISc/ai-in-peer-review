Summary
The paper tackles maximum-likelihood learning of full, unconstrained DPP kernels by exploiting the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K. It introduces an EM-style algorithm that treats the subset of active eigenvectors J as a latent variable, leveraging the known mixture-of-elementary-DPPs interpretation. The E-step sets q(J|Y)=pK(J|Y) and shows this conditional is a k-DPP with a computable kernel; the M-step separates eigenvalues and eigenvectors: eigenvalues have a closed-form update via k-DPP marginals, while eigenvectors are updated by a gradient step constrained on the Stiefel manifold. The method avoids PSD/eigenvalue clipping projections required by naive projected gradient ascent (KA), which the paper argues can induce degeneracy (near-diagonal kernels) and poor local optima. The algorithm’s per-iteration complexity is O(n N k^2) for the E/M computations plus O(N^3) for the manifold step-size search; this can be better than the O(n N^3) cost of KA when k ≪ N. Experiments on a large Amazon baby registry dataset show consistent test log-likelihood improvements over KA (especially when negative interactions matter), faster runtime, and qualitatively better diverse recommendations.

Quality
- Theory: The EM derivation is sound and aligns with standard latent-variable treatments. Using the mixture representation to define a tight lower bound and generalized EM (single gradient step on V with step-size search and likelihood monitoring) is reasonable; monotonic ascent in the true likelihood is enforced by checking L after each update, which robustifies the inexact M-step.
- Key lemmas: The k-DPP characterization of p(J|Y) and the efficient marginal computations underpin the practicality of the eigenvalue update; while proofs are deferred to the supplement, the constructions are consistent with prior DPP results.
- Optimization details: The Stiefel manifold update for V is appropriate and avoids problematic projections. The caveat around λ=1 and numerical clipping is acknowledged. The eigenvector gradient (Eq. 20) is only exact for the first gradient; in practice the algorithm relies on repeated E-steps during line search and checks L, which is a standard generalized EM tactic.
- Complexity claims: The contrast between O(n N k^2) and O(n N^3) is plausible for sparse/small-set regimes. The extra O(T2 N^3) for line search is transparent.
- Empirics: Experiments are careful in comparing two initializations, include a low-data regime, provide an interpretable diagnostic (d) correlating with gains, and include runtime comparisons. However, baselines are limited to KA; comparisons to other learning approaches (e.g., diagonal reweighting [2], mixtures [3], Bayesian approaches [8]) are not reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The use of eigenvalue/eigenvector parameterization, hidden variable J, and the link to k-DPPs are clearly explained. Algorithmic steps are summarized concisely. Some heavy steps (derivations for Eq. 19 and Eq. 20) rely on the supplement; a brief sketch in the main text would help. More implementation detail (numerical stabilization for λ near 1, cost breakdown of E-step) would aid reproducibility.

Originality
The core contribution—an EM algorithm for full DPP learning via the latent J and eigen-parameterization—is novel relative to prior work that learned only item qualities or linear mixtures, or adopted parametric/Bayesian forms. Using k-DPPs in the E-step and Stiefel manifold updates for V is a neat and principled combination of known tools in a new learning setting.

Significance
Learning full DPP kernels is important for applications where diversity/negative interactions matter (summarization, recommendation, sensor placement). The method removes a major practical obstacle (projection-induced degeneracy), is faster in relevant regimes, and demonstrably improves test likelihood and qualitative diversity. Given the increasing interest in DPPs, this work is likely to be used and extended (e.g., low-rank settings, regularization).

Empirical evaluation
- Strengths: Real-world dataset with many categories; both random and moment-matching initializations; analysis of data regimes; runtime study; qualitative example that highlights diversity benefits.
- Limitations: No synthetic recovery tests to assess identifiability or sensitivity; N is modest (~70), leaving open scalability to larger ground sets; no comparisons to Bayesian or structured/parametric DPP learning; overfitting/regularization not discussed; the robustness claim to initialization could be quantified more systematically.

Pros
- Conceptually clean EM derivation exploiting the DPP mixture structure.
- Avoids problematic projections; principled handling of orthogonality via manifold optimization.
- Efficient eigenvalue updates using k-DPP marginals; clear complexity advantages when k is small.
- Solid improvements in test likelihood and runtime; compelling qualitative diversity example.
- Clear exposition and useful discussion of when EM helps most (strong off-diagonal signal).

Cons
- Limited baselines; missing comparisons to other established DPP learning approaches.
- Scalability not demonstrated beyond N≈100; no low-rank variant or memory/runtime analysis at larger N.
- The eigenvector update uses only a single gradient step per M-step; convergence properties are empirical.
- Heavy reliance on supplement for key derivations; some implementation details omitted.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Can you report results against Bayesian kernel learning [Affandi et al., ICML] and diagonal reweighting [Kulesza & Taskar]?
- How does the method scale with larger N (e.g., N≥1k)? Would a low-rank parameterization of V improve scalability?
- Do you use any regularization on λ or V to prevent overfitting? How sensitive is performance to the λ cap <1?
- Please provide more details on computing Eq. (19) efficiently and numerical stabilizations for Eq. (21).
- A synthetic study where the ground-truth K is known would help assess recovery and the benefits of EM vs KA.

Overall
A strong and timely contribution that advances practical learning of DPPs with a principled EM approach, yielding empirical gains and better robustness than projected gradient methods. With broader baselines and some scaling discussion, this would be even stronger. I lean toward acceptance.
