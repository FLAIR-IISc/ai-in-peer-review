This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), specifically addressing the difficulties of directly maximizing the likelihood function due to its NP-hardness. The authors propose an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm leveraging the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix, a clever idea that avoids the problematic projection step inherent in naive gradient ascent. The core contribution—an EM algorithm for unconstrained DPP learning—is a significant and potentially impactful development.

The strengths of this work are considerable. First, the problem itself is important. DPPs are a powerful tool for modeling diversity and quality tradeoffs, with applications across many domains. Second, the EM approach is well-motivated and theoretically sound, building on the established connections between DPPs and their eigendecompositions. The authors convincingly argue why previous methods fall short, highlighting the limitations of partial learning and restrictive parameterizations. The analysis demonstrating the asymptotic runtime advantage over gradient ascent is also a valuable contribution. Finally, the experimental validation, while not exhaustive, provides evidence supporting the effectiveness and robustness of the EM algorithm, particularly in scenarios where negative interactions between items are crucial. The inclusion of comparisons to K-Ascent and the analysis of the initialization impact are helpful.

However, the paper isn’t without its weaknesses. While the theoretical justification for the EM algorithm is strong, some aspects of the derivation could benefit from further clarity and detail. Specifically, the leap from Equation (13) to the gradient computation in Section 3.5 feels somewhat abrupt and relies on an unspecified simplification. The experimental section, while demonstrating improvements over K-Ascent, could be strengthened. The dataset used is relevant, but the comparisons are largely limited to a single application (baby registries). Exploring additional datasets or tasks would bolster the generalizability claims.  More thorough ablation studies, perhaps examining the sensitivity of the algorithm to different initialization strategies or step sizes, would also be beneficial. The discussion of the Stiefel manifold update (Equation 21) feels cursory; a more detailed explanation of the practical implementation and potential challenges would be appreciated.  Furthermore, the connection between the ‘d’ value and the EM gain, while interesting, is presented as an observation rather than a rigorously established relationship.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The EM algorithm for unconstrained DPP learning is a truly novel approach to a difficult problem. It circumvents the limitations of existing methods and offers a more flexible and potentially powerful solution.
* **Significance:** Successful learning of DPPs unlocks a wider range of applications and allows for more nuanced modeling of diverse datasets.  The theoretical runtime improvement is also significant.
* **Soundness:**  The algorithm is based on solid mathematical principles and a clear understanding of DPP theory.
* **Potential Impact:** This work could inspire further research into efficient DPP learning algorithms and broaden the adoption of DPPs in various machine learning tasks.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while positive, are somewhat limited in scope.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** Some parts of the theoretical derivation could benefit from further elaboration.
* **Missing Ablation Studies:** A more thorough investigation of the algorithm’s sensitivity to hyperparameters and initialization would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a novel and potentially significant contribution to the field of DPP learning. While there are some minor weaknesses in terms of clarity and empirical validation, the originality and potential impact of the proposed EM algorithm outweigh these concerns. The authors have identified a crucial challenge and offer a compelling solution that, despite not being fully polished, deserves to be shared with the NIPS community. I am inclined to accept this paper, recognizing that further refinement and validation could lead to even greater impact. I would encourage the authors to address the clarity issues and expand the experimental evaluation in future work.
