### Paper Summary

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. While DPPs are powerful models for capturing diversity in subset selection, learning their parameters via maximum likelihood is conjectured to be NP-hard. The authors critique the standard approach of projected gradient ascent, highlighting a key failure mode: the projection step required to maintain positive semi-definiteness can prematurely truncate eigenvalues to zero, leading to degenerate, near-diagonal kernel matrices that fail to model diversity.

To overcome this, the paper proposes a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The core idea is to re-parameterize the DPP kernel `K` in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors and to introduce a latent variable `J` representing the subset of chosen eigenvectors in the DPP's generative process. This formulation allows for the development of an EM procedure where:
1.  The E-step involves computing the posterior distribution over the latent variable `J` given an observed subset `Y`. The authors prove an elegant result (Lemma 1) showing that this posterior is itself a k-DPP, which can be represented compactly by a new kernel matrix.
2.  The M-step updates the parameters (eigenvalues and eigenvectors). The update for the eigenvalues is a closed-form, projection-free expression derived from the marginals of the k-DPP computed in the E-step. The eigenvectors are updated via a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, which correctly handles the orthogonality constraint without a problematic projection.

The authors empirically validate their method on a real-world baby registry dataset. The results show that their EM algorithm is more robust than projected gradient ascent, particularly with uninformative initializations or in data-poor settings. It consistently learns better models that capture negative correlations between items and is also computationally faster.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The application of EM to learning DPP kernels is a significant contribution. The key insight of treating the set of active eigenvectors `J` as a latent variable is both clever and theoretically sound. This reframing of the problem is non-obvious and unlocks a much more stable optimization procedure. The result in Lemma 1, which characterizes the posterior `p(J|Y)` as a k-DPP, is a beautiful theoretical finding that makes the entire approach computationally feasible.

2.  **Clear Diagnosis of a Key Problem:** The paper provides an excellent and insightful critique of the projected gradient ascent baseline. The explanation of how the projection step can interact poorly with the gradient calculation to trap the optimization in a poor local optimum (a near-diagonal kernel) is clear and compelling. This provides strong motivation for seeking an alternative optimization strategy.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and thoroughly support the paper's claims.
    *   The choice of a real-world product recommendation task is highly appropriate for demonstrating the value of DPPs.
    *   The comparison against K-Ascent with different initializations (Wishart vs. moments-matching) clearly demonstrates the superior robustness of the proposed EM method. The finding that EM's advantage is most pronounced in settings where diversity is important (high `d` value) and when data is scarce provides strong evidence for its utility.
    *   The qualitative example of the three baby monitors selected by the KA-trained model versus the more diverse set from the EM-trained model is an intuitive and powerful illustration of the practical impact of the improved learning algorithm.
    *   The runtime comparison confirms the theoretical asymptotic advantage of the EM approach.

4.  **High Clarity and Quality of Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The background is concise, the motivation is clear, and the derivation of the EM algorithm is presented in a logical, step-by-step manner. The authors do a great job of explaining complex concepts, such as optimization on a Stiefel manifold, in an accessible way.

### Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Local Optima of EM:** While the paper convincingly demonstrates that the EM approach avoids the specific pathological local optima that plague projected gradient, EM itself is still a local optimization algorithm. The discussion could be slightly more nuanced by acknowledging this. For instance, how sensitive is the EM algorithm to initialization compared to K-Ascent? While it is shown to be *more robust*, a brief discussion of its own potential to converge to different local optima would make the analysis more complete.

2.  **M-step for Eigenvectors:** The M-step for the eigenvectors `V` is not a full maximization but a single gradient step, which technically makes this a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm. This is a perfectly valid and common practical choice, but explicitly framing it as such would be more precise. Furthermore, the line search for the step size `η` involves re-evaluating the E-step to check for an improvement in the true likelihood `L`. This could be computationally intensive. A comment on the practical cost of this line search (e.g., how many steps `T2` are typically needed) would be helpful.

3.  **Scalability to Large N:** The algorithm's complexity includes an O(N^3) term due to the eigendecomposition and matrix exponential. This will be a bottleneck for applications with a very large ground set `N` (e.g., thousands of items). The experiments are on moderately sized problems (N ≈ 70), where this is acceptable. While outside the direct scope of this work, a brief mention of this limitation and potential future directions, such as combining this EM framework with kernel approximation techniques (e.g., Nyström, which the authors have worked on elsewhere), would strengthen the paper.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on learning probabilistic models for subset selection.

*   **Quality & Originality:** The work is of high technical quality. The proposed EM algorithm for learning DPPs is novel and addresses a well-motivated and important flaw in existing methods. The theoretical underpinnings are sound.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is written with exceptional clarity, making a complex topic accessible.
*   **Significance:** The problem of learning a DPP kernel is fundamental to its practical application. This paper provides a robust, efficient, and well-justified algorithm that is very likely to become the standard method for this task. Researchers and practitioners looking to apply DPPs will find this work invaluable.

The strengths of this paper far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, elegant, and practical solution to an important problem, supported by strong theoretical motivation and compelling empirical results. I strongly recommend acceptance.
