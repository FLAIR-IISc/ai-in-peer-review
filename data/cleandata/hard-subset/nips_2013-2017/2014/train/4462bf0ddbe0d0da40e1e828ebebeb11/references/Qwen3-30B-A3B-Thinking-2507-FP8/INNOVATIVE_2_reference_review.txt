

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper presents a novel approach to learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) by leveraging their eigendecomposition properties to develop an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors identify a fundamental limitation in existing DPP learning methods: gradient ascent approaches require projection steps to maintain positive semi-definiteness, which often results in degenerate, nearly diagonal kernel matrices that fail to model the diversity-seeking nature of DPPs. 

The core insight is to exploit the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K (equivalent to the L matrix) to develop an EM algorithm that avoids these problematic projection steps. The authors transform the learning problem by introducing the set of eigenvectors as a hidden variable, then derive an efficient EM procedure with closed-form updates for eigenvalues and a gradient-based update for eigenvectors. The method is theoretically grounded, with derivations showing the algorithm is asymptotically faster than gradient ascent (O(T1nNk² + T1T2N³) vs. O(T1nN³ + T1T2N³)).

The authors evaluate their approach on a baby registry dataset (13 categories with N=71 items on average), comparing against gradient ascent (K-Ascent). They demonstrate that their EM approach consistently achieves higher test log-likelihoods, particularly in categories where diversity is important (e.g., safety, carseats, strollers), with average gains of 3.7% from Wishart initialization and 4.5% in data-poor settings. The paper also provides a concrete example showing EM's superior modeling of negative interactions between items.

**Strengths:**

1. **High Novelty:** This is the paper's strongest aspect. The authors identify a fundamental problem in DPP learning that has been overlooked in prior work and propose a genuinely novel solution using EM with eigendecomposition. The approach differs substantially from all prior work (Kulesza & Taskar, 2010, 2011; Affandi et al., 2013), which either restricted learning to scalar weights, used parametric assumptions, or relied on projection-based gradient ascent.

2. **Significant Practical Impact:** The method directly addresses the core challenge of DPP learning—modeling negative interactions between items—and provides a clear, efficient algorithm that outperforms the naive gradient approach. The runtime analysis and empirical results demonstrate both theoretical and practical advantages.

3. **Clear Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides thorough derivations of the EM updates and connects them to the underlying DPP properties. The connection between the eigendecomposition and the DPP sampling process is elegantly leveraged.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments are confined to a single dataset (baby registry categories) with modest N (average 71 items). While sufficient to demonstrate the core idea, additional experiments on larger or more diverse datasets would strengthen the case for broader applicability.

2. **Initialization Sensitivity:** The paper acknowledges that both methods perform poorly with diagonal initializations, but the analysis of initialization strategies could be more comprehensive. The moment-matching initialization helps KA but doesn't fully resolve its limitations, suggesting the EM approach is fundamentally better.

3. **Theoretical Convergence Guarantee:** While the paper derives the EM updates, it doesn't provide a convergence guarantee for the algorithm, which is a common expectation for such methods.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a significant conceptual contribution to DPP learning by introducing a novel EM framework that overcomes a fundamental limitation of existing approaches. The core idea is bold, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear practical advantages. While the experimental validation is somewhat limited, the theoretical novelty and strong empirical results on the presented dataset are compelling.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper to be an excellent fit for NIPS. The authors have identified a meaningful gap in the literature and provided a creative solution that advances the field. The paper clearly articulates how it differs from previous work and offers a new direction for DPP learning.

I recommend acceptance. This work represents a significant conceptual advance that will likely influence future research on DPPs and similar probabilistic models. The novelty and significance of the approach outweigh the minor weaknesses in experimental scope and the absence of a convergence proof.
