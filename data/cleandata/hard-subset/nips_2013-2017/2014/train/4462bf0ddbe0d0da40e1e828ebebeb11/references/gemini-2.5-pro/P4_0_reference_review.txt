### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets via maximum likelihood estimation. The authors note that learning a full, unconstrained kernel matrix is conjectured to be NP-hard and that prior work has circumvented this by learning restricted or parametric forms of the kernel. A straightforward approach using projected gradient ascent on the kernel matrix entries is introduced as a baseline, but the authors highlight its primary weakness: the projection step required to maintain positive semi-definiteness can lead to poor local optima, particularly near-diagonal solutions that fail to model diversity.

The main contribution of this work is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for this learning problem. The key insight is to leverage the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel `K = VΛV^T` and the interpretation of a DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs. This mixture formulation naturally introduces a latent variable `J`, which is a subset of indices corresponding to a selection of eigenvectors. The EM algorithm then alternates between two steps:
1.  **E-step:** Computing the posterior distribution over the latent variable `J` for each observed subset `Y_i`. The authors show that this posterior is itself a k-DPP, which can be compactly represented and efficiently manipulated.
2.  **M-step:** Maximizing the expected log-likelihood with respect to the kernel's eigenvalues `Λ` and eigenvectors `V`. The update for the eigenvalues has a closed-form solution that is computed using marginal probabilities from the k-DPP found in the E-step. The update for the eigenvectors is handled by taking a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold to respect the orthogonality constraint, thus avoiding a problematic projection step.

The paper provides an asymptotic runtime analysis and presents empirical results on a product recommendation task using a baby registry dataset. These experiments demonstrate that the proposed EM algorithm is more robust to initialization and faster than the projected gradient ascent baseline.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality: The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work.**

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical development of the EM algorithm is well-founded. The use of the DPP mixture model to introduce a latent variable is an elegant way to reframe the optimization problem. A key technical contribution is the characterization of the posterior `p(J|Y_i)` as a k-DPP (Lemma 1), which makes the E-step tractable. The derived M-step updates are appropriate for the problem structure: the closed-form update for eigenvalues avoids the projection issues of the baseline, and the use of optimization on the Stiefel manifold for the eigenvectors is the correct approach for handling the orthogonality constraint. The claims of improved robustness and speed are supported by experiments on a real-world dataset, with a clear analysis of the failure modes of the baseline method.
*   **Weaknesses:** The M-step optimization for the eigenvectors `V` is a practical approximation. The authors state that they take a single gradient step rather than fully optimizing `V` within each M-step, using the true likelihood to guide a line search. While this is a reasonable heuristic, it is a deviation from a strict coordinate ascent on the EM lower bound. The experimental validation is based on a single type of dataset (product registries), and demonstrating the algorithm's effectiveness in another domain would further strengthen the paper's claims.

**Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.**

*   **Strengths:** The paper provides a clear motivation for the problem and a concise background on DPPs. The description of the baseline projected gradient ascent method and its shortcomings effectively sets the stage for the proposed EM algorithm. The derivation of the algorithm is presented in a logical, step-by-step manner that is easy to follow. The experimental setup and results are described clearly, and the figures effectively communicate the main findings. The paper provides enough detail for an expert to understand the method's mechanics.
*   **Weaknesses:** There are no major clarity issues. A minor point is that the simplification of the Stiefel manifold update algorithm could be briefly elaborated upon for completeness, but this does not detract significantly from the paper's readability.

**Originality: The proposed approach is novel and addresses the DPP learning problem from a new perspective.**

*   **Strengths:** To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to propose an EM algorithm for learning an unconstrained DPP kernel. Previous works, such as [2, 3, 8], focused on learning restricted versions of the kernel. The conceptual leap of using the subset of eigenvectors `J` as a latent variable is the core novel idea that enables the EM formulation. This represents a significant departure from prior approaches and provides a new, effective tool for DPP learning.
*   **Weaknesses:** The individual components of the algorithm (EM framework, Jensen's inequality, Stiefel manifold optimization) are standard machine learning techniques. However, their synthesis and application to this specific problem are highly original.

**Significance: The work is likely to be impactful for both researchers and practitioners using DPPs.**

*   **Strengths:** The paper tackles a fundamental problem in the practical application of DPPs. A robust and efficient algorithm for learning the full kernel from data is a critical step towards making DPPs more widely usable. The demonstrated advantages over a natural baseline method suggest that the proposed EM algorithm will become a valuable tool. By enabling the learning of complex, non-parametric diversity models, this work has the potential to advance the state of the art in applications where diverse subset selection is important, such as product recommendation, document summarization, and search results diversification.
*   **Weaknesses:** The algorithm's runtime includes `O(N^3)` steps, which may limit its scalability to problems with very large ground sets (`N`). While the paper shows it can be faster than the baseline for moderate `N`, this complexity remains a practical constraint.

### Arguments for Acceptance

1.  The paper presents a novel and elegant EM-based algorithm for the important and challenging problem of learning unconstrained DPP kernels from data.
2.  The method is technically sound, and its derivation is clear and well-motivated. It successfully avoids the projection-related pitfalls of a naive gradient-based baseline.
3.  Empirical results on a real-world dataset demonstrate that the proposed algorithm is more robust and computationally faster than the baseline, representing a tangible advance.
4.  The paper is exceptionally well-written and will be a valuable contribution to the NIPS proceedings.

### Arguments for Rejection

1.  The M-step for the eigenvectors is a heuristic (single gradient step) rather than a full optimization of the EM lower bound, which is a minor theoretical compromise.
2.  The experimental evidence is drawn from a single application domain, though the results within that domain are convincing.
