Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles maximum-likelihood learning of unconstrained determinantal point processes (DPPs) without restricting L/K to a parametric family. The key idea is to move from optimizing entries of K to optimizing its eigendecomposition K = VΛV^T via an EM-like procedure that introduces a latent subset J of eigenvectors, leveraging the elementary DPP mixture view. The E-step sets q(J|Y) = pK(J|Y), which is shown to be a k-DPP with a computable kernel; the M-step has a closed-form update for eigenvalues λj via k-DPP marginals and performs a gradient step for eigenvectors V on the Stiefel manifold. Compared to projected gradient ascent on K (KA), the proposed method avoids the projection-induced degeneracy that can push K towards near-diagonal matrices, and it is argued to be asymptotically faster: O(T1 n N k^2 + T1 T2 N^3) vs. O(T1 n N^3 + T1 T2 N^3).

Relative to prior work: (i) learning item-quality scalings [Kulesza & Taskar, UAI’11] and mixtures of fixed L [Kulesza & Taskar, ICML’11] only partially learn L or abandon the DPP form; (ii) Bayesian parameter learning with assumed kernel families [Affandi et al., ICML’14] adds flexibility but is still parametric; (iii) submodular optimization-based systems (e.g., Lin & Bilmes) address related objectives but not DPP likelihood learning. The proposed EM provides, to my knowledge, the first general-purpose MLE algorithm operating directly on the full eigendecomposition without constraining L.

Quality
Strengths:
- Sound use of the elementary DPP mixture representation to introduce a latent variable enabling an EM lower bound.
- E-step reduces to computing a k-DPP posterior; the resulting kernel QY has a compact expression. This is elegant and leads to efficient marginals for the λ-update.
- The eigenvalue M-step is closed-form and self-normalizing (no projection needed). The eigenvector update uses a principled Stiefel manifold step.
- Analyses of runtime show favorable scaling for moderate k relative to naive GA on K.
- Empirical evaluation on a real catalog dataset demonstrates improvements in test log-likelihood (notably when off-diagonal interactions matter) and faster wall-clock time. The diagnostic d correlating EM gains with off-diagonal strength is thoughtful, and the qualitative example is compelling.

Concerns and weaknesses:
- Convergence guarantees: The method is a generalized EM. Only a single gradient step is taken for V, and the step size is selected by checking improvement in the true likelihood L rather than the bound F, with a fresh E-step each time. This breaks the standard EM monotonicity proof; the paper should clarify whether L is guaranteed to increase at each outer iteration and under what conditions. If not, please characterize convergence (e.g., to a stationary point) or provide empirical monotonicity plots.
- Computational details and complexity: The claim of O(n N k^2) for computing the λ-updates hinges on efficient computation of k-DPP marginals q(j ∈ J | Y). Please include or move from supplement a clear derivation and the precise routine used (eigendecomposition of a k×k matrix, then projecting back to N?). Also discuss constants and the cost of repeatedly forming QY and HY for all Y. The V-update contains an O(N^3) matrix exponential per line search step—how many such steps are needed on average? For larger N (e.g., 500–5000 common in vision/NLP), the O(N^3) cost may dominate; any strategies (low-rank factorization, Nyström, or tying V to features) should be discussed.
- Baselines: KA on K is a reasonable strawman, but a stronger baseline would optimize directly over (V,λ) with projected gradient/Manifold optimization on the Stiefel manifold and simple clipping of λ in [0,1], without the EM lower bound. This would separate the effect of working in eigenspace from the EM formulation. Comparison to parametric/Bayesian approaches (even if mismatched) would help position practical benefits.
- Regularization and numerical stability: The method relies on “tightening” the λ ≤ 1 constraint to avoid infinities in R = diag(sqrt(λ/(1−λ))). This is an ad hoc fix. Consider adding an explicit barrier or penalty discouraging λ → 1 (e.g., ∑j −α log(1−λj) or a trace/Frobenius penalty on L) and evaluating its effect on generalization. Also comment on conditioning of HY inverses, and whether damping is used.
- Evaluation breadth: Experiments are limited to a single application domain with N ≈ 71. Synthetic experiments showing recovery of known kernels (including off-diagonal structure) would substantiate identifiability and robustness to initialization. Reporting sensitivity to n, k, and initialization would be valuable. While improvements of 3–5% in test log-likelihood can be meaningful, the variance across runs and categories should be presented more fully.
- Objective formulation: The use of |det(K − IY)| is inherited from DPP theory; a short clarification of the absolute value (sign behavior and whether it ever changes for feasible K) in the main text would aid readers.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The mixture interpretation and the k-DPP posterior are explained clearly. However, critical derivations (Lemma 1, eqs. for marginals and V-gradient) are deferred to the supplement. Given their centrality, please include at least proof sketches and algorithmic details in the main paper. Notation for IY (identity with entries zeroed on Y) is unusual; a brief illustrative example would help.

Originality
The EM formulation leveraging the eigenvector-subset latent variable and k-DPP posteriors for full, unconstrained DPP learning appears novel and nontrivial. The eigenvector update on the Stiefel manifold in this context is also a useful contribution.

Significance
Learning full DPP kernels is important for diversity-aware selection. The proposed method advances the state of the art beyond partial or parametric learning by providing a practical, faster, and more robust alternative to projected gradient on K. With additional clarity on convergence and improved scalability (e.g., low-rank/feature-based variants), this could become a standard approach.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually elegant EM leveraging DPP structure; novel and technically sound
- Practical algorithm with closed-form λ-update; avoids projection pathologies
- Empirical improvements on real data; faster than KA
- Clear potential impact on DPP learning practice

Cons for acceptance
- Limited convergence guarantees and reliance on generalized EM
- Scalability concerns for larger N; O(N^3) steps remain
- Baseline comparisons could be stronger; evaluation limited in scope
- Some ad hoc numerical fixes; limited theoretical analysis of consistency

Suggestions
- Provide convergence discussion and, if possible, monotonicity guarantees or empirical evidence
- Add a baseline optimizing over (V,λ) without EM and compare
- Include synthetic recovery experiments; report sensitivity analyses
- Consider explicit regularization to handle λ near 1; discuss numerical damping
- Discuss and/or implement a low-rank/feature-parameterized variant to improve scalability

Overall: A solid, novel contribution to DPP learning with clear practical benefits. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing convergence clarity and expanding experimental validation.
