## Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix *L* (or its marginal *K*) for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) from data. Existing approaches either partially learn *L* (e.g., learning item quality scores), rely on restrictive parameterizations, or suffer from computational issues like needing to store multiple kernels or requiring problematic projection steps during gradient ascent. The authors propose an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that leverages the eigendecomposition of *L* to overcome these limitations. The core idea is to introduce a hidden variable representing the eigenvectors of *K*, allowing for a more stable and efficient optimization process. The paper demonstrates both theoretical advantages (asymptotic runtime) and empirical improvements over a gradient ascent baseline on a product recommendation task.

**Relationship to Prior Work:**

The authors accurately position their work within the existing DPP learning literature. They clearly articulate the drawbacks of previous approaches, including: the partial learning of *L* by Kulesza & Taskar (2012), the ensemble method of Affandi et al. (2013), and the Bayesian approach of  Affandi et al. (2015). This paper builds upon the understanding of DPPs’ properties through eigendecomposition, a direction explored by Kulesza and Taskar (2012) but taken to a novel algorithmic conclusion with the EM framework. The comparison to projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) is particularly insightful, highlighting the issues with maintaining positive semi-definiteness without the EM approach. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The EM algorithm presented is a genuinely novel approach to DPP learning, circumventing the difficulties of direct kernel optimization. The use of the eigendecomposition and the introduction of a hidden variable are clever and enable a more stable optimization process.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a clear theoretical justification for their method, demonstrating its advantages over gradient ascent in terms of asymptotic runtime and avoiding problematic projection steps.  The derivation of the update equations appears sound.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on baby registry data show a consistent improvement over the K-Ascent baseline, especially in settings where the true kernel is more complex (high *d* value). The results are statistically significant and presented clearly.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The background section effectively introduces DPPs and their relevant properties. The algorithmic descriptions are clear and concise.
* **Practical Considerations:** The authors address practical challenges like initialization and the need to avoid exactly singular kernels, demonstrating a thoughtful approach to implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While theoretically appealing, the EM algorithm appears complex to implement. The need for Stiefel manifold optimization, although addressed with simplifications, adds to the implementation burden. The authors should provide more details on the practical challenges encountered during implementation and optimization.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are limited to a single dataset and task (product recommendation).  While the results are compelling, it would be beneficial to see the algorithm’s performance on other datasets or tasks to assess its generalizability.  Experiments with different dataset sizes and dimensionality would also be valuable.
* **Heuristic Step Size Selection:** The paper mentions a step-size search but lacks detail on the specific method used. A more detailed description of this process would enhance reproducibility and allow for a better understanding of the algorithm’s sensitivity to hyperparameters.
* **Discussion of k-DPPs:** The introduction of k-DPPs is somewhat abrupt.  More context on their properties and relevance beyond computational efficiency would be beneficial.  Specifically, how does choosing 'k' impact the learned DPP, and how sensitive are the results to this choice?
* **Computational Complexity Nuance:** While the paper claims asymptotic runtime improvement, the stated complexity involves 'k' (the size of the largest set in the training data). In some real-world scenarios, 'k' could be substantial, potentially diminishing the performance benefit over gradient ascent. This nuance could be further elaborated.



**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem.
* Empirical evidence demonstrates improvement over existing methods.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Addresses practical considerations of implementation.

**Con:**

* Implementation complexity might be a barrier to adoption.
* Limited scope of experiments.
* Lack of detail in heuristic step size selection.
* Dependence on 'k' in complexity analysis.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of DPP learning. The proposed EM algorithm is a clever and well-justified approach that addresses the limitations of existing methods. While the implementation complexity and limited scope of experiments are potential concerns, the theoretical and empirical results are compelling. I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS conference and recommend **acceptance**. 

**Recommendation:** Accept.

**Minor Suggestions:**

* Provide more implementation details, particularly regarding the Stiefel manifold optimization and step-size search.
* Expand the experimental evaluation to include additional datasets and tasks.
* Elaborate on the choice of 'k' in the k-DPP framework and its impact on the learned DPP.
* Clarify the practical implications of the 'k' dependence in the asymptotic runtime analysis.
