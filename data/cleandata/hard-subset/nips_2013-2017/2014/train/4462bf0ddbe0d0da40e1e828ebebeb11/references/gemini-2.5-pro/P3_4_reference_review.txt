### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. The maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) of the full, unconstrained kernel matrix is conjectured to be NP-hard. Previous work has circumvented this difficulty by either learning a restricted form of the kernel—for instance, by only learning item-specific quality scores (Kulesza & Taskar, UAI 2010)—or by learning mixtures of DPPs with fixed kernels (Kulesza & Taskar, ICML 2011), which sacrifices the attractive computational properties of a single DPP. More recent Bayesian methods (Affandi et al., ICML 2013) have allowed for learning kernel parameters, but still rely on a specific parametric form for the kernel.

The main contribution of this paper is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for the non-parametric MLE of the DPP kernel. The key insight is to leverage the generative interpretation of a DPP, where a subset is sampled by first selecting a random subset of the kernel's eigenvectors (`J`) and then sampling from the corresponding elementary DPP. The authors treat this set of selected eigenvectors `J` as a latent variable. This formulation allows them to derive an EM algorithm that operates on the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel `K`, optimizing its eigenvalues (`Λ`) and eigenvectors (`V`) separately. This approach cleverly avoids the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent, which can lead to poor, near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture diversity.

### Strengths

1.  **High Originality and Elegance:** The formulation of DPP learning as an EM problem is highly novel and insightful. Identifying the set of active eigenvectors `J` as the latent variable is a clever theoretical step that connects the generative process of DPPs directly to a powerful optimization framework. The resulting algorithm is elegant and well-motivated.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the EM algorithm is solid.
    *   The E-step is particularly neat: the authors show that the posterior `p(J | Y)` is itself a k-DPP (Lemma 1), which makes the required expectations tractable.
    *   The M-step provides a closed-form, projection-free update for the eigenvalues (`λ_j`), which is a major advantage over projected gradient methods.
    *   The handling of the eigenvector matrix `V` update is sophisticated, correctly identifying the optimization as occurring on a Stiefel manifold and employing an appropriate multiplicative update rule, which is superior to a naive projection.

3.  **Significant Contribution:** This work represents a substantial advance in learning DPPs. By providing a practical algorithm for learning an unconstrained kernel, it opens the door to applying DPPs in domains where the underlying similarity/diversity structure is not known a priori and cannot be easily parameterized. This is a fundamental problem in the DPP literature, and this paper offers the most compelling solution to date.

4.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed EM algorithm over a strong baseline (projected gradient ascent, KA).
    *   The choice of a real-world baby registry dataset is compelling and clearly illustrates the need for modeling diversity.
    *   The authors astutely identify the failure mode of the baseline—its tendency to collapse to near-diagonal solutions after projection—and even propose a metric (`d`) that correlates with the performance gap between EM and KA.
    *   The comparison across different initializations and in a data-poor setting effectively showcases the robustness of the EM approach.
    *   The qualitative example of the three baby monitors selected by the KA model versus the more diverse set from the EM model is a powerful illustration of the practical impact of the improved learning algorithm.
    *   The runtime analysis confirms the claimed asymptotic advantages of EM.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Scalability:** The primary weakness is the computational complexity, which contains an `O(N^3)` term due to the eigendecomposition and the Stiefel manifold update for `V`. While the paper shows EM is faster than the baseline, this complexity still limits the applicability to problems with a large ground set size `N` (e.g., `N > 1000`). The paper would be strengthened by explicitly acknowledging this limitation and perhaps briefly discussing potential avenues for future work, such as incorporating low-rank approximations (as in Affandi et al., AISTATS 2014) into the EM framework.

2.  **M-step for Eigenvectors:** The paper notes that the gradient for `V` (Eq. 20) is only exact at the beginning of the M-step. The practical solution—taking a single gradient step and using the true likelihood `L` for a line search—is a form of Generalized EM (GEM). While this is a standard and perfectly reasonable approach, framing it explicitly as such would add a bit more formal precision to the description of the M-step.

3.  **Local Optima:** As with all EM and gradient-based algorithms, the method is susceptible to local optima. The experiments show it is much more robust to poor initialization than the KA baseline, which is a key strength. However, the quality of the final solution still depends on the starting point (as evidenced by the performance difference between Wishart and moments-matching initializers). This is an inherent property of the problem's non-convexity and not a flaw of the paper, but merits a brief mention.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the machine learning literature on probabilistic subset selection. The problem it addresses is fundamental, the proposed EM algorithm is both novel and technically sound, and the experimental results are thorough and convincing. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to the inherent computational challenges of the problem domain than to flaws in the proposed method. This work is highly likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers working with DPPs.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and elegant EM formulation for a difficult, non-convex learning problem.
*   Solves a key practical issue with the naive gradient ascent approach (the destructive nature of the projection step).
*   Represents a significant step towards learning unconstrained, non-parametric DPP kernels from data.
*   Strong, convincing experimental validation on a real-world task.
*   Very high quality of writing and presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The `O(N^3)` complexity remains a bottleneck for scalability to very large `N`.

The arguments for acceptance are compelling and far outweigh the single, well-understood limitation.

**Recommendation: Accept**
