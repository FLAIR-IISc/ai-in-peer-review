

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper proposes an EM-based algorithm for learning the marginal kernel K of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) without restrictive assumptions about its structure. The authors address the key limitation of previous DPP learning approaches (which either use gradient ascent with problematic projection steps or make restrictive assumptions about the kernel structure) by exploiting the eigendecomposition of K. The paper provides a thorough derivation of the EM algorithm, including the E-step (which involves k-DPPs) and M-step (with updates for eigenvalues and eigenvectors), and demonstrates through experiments that their approach outperforms gradient ascent with projection in terms of log-likelihood and runtime on a real-world product recommendation dataset.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness (Quality):** The paper provides rigorous mathematical derivations for the EM algorithm, including the use of Jensen's inequality to lower-bound the likelihood objective. The theoretical analysis of computational complexity is sound, showing that the EM algorithm is asymptotically faster than gradient ascent. The experimental validation on a large baby registry dataset demonstrates the practical advantages of the proposed method.

2. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical derivations are presented in a logical sequence, and the algorithms (1 and 2) are clearly described. The experiments are well-designed, with appropriate statistical analysis (medians from 25 trials) and insightful visualizations (Figures 1 and 2).

3. **Originality:** The key insight of using the eigendecomposition of K to develop an EM algorithm is novel in the context of DPP learning. Previous work on DPP learning has largely focused on gradient-based methods with projection steps [1, 3, 8] or restrictive parametric forms. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from these prior works.

4. **Significance:** The problem of learning DPPs is important for many applications requiring subset selection with diversity (e.g., recommendation systems, document summarization). The paper demonstrates practical significance through experiments on a real-world dataset, showing that the EM approach outperforms gradient ascent in both log-likelihood and runtime.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** While the paper compares its method to gradient ascent with projection (KA), it does not sufficiently compare with other recent DPP learning approaches. For instance, [8] proposes Bayesian methods for learning DPPs, and [3] explores learning weights for linear combinations of DPPs. The paper should clarify how the EM approach differs from and improves upon these methods.

2. **Initialization Sensitivity:** The paper acknowledges that the EM algorithm is sensitive to initialization, noting that both EM and KA fail when starting from a diagonal kernel. However, it doesn't provide a comprehensive analysis of this sensitivity or propose strategies to mitigate it. The moments-matching initialization works well in some cases but not all (as shown in Figure 1b), and the paper doesn't discuss how to handle cases where the data is too sparse for moment matching.

3. **Theoretical Justification:** While the paper demonstrates empirically that the EM algorithm avoids the degeneracy issues of gradient ascent, it could provide stronger theoretical justification for why the EM approach is more robust. For example, it could analyze the landscape of the likelihood function and show that the EM approach is less likely to get stuck in poor local optima.

4. **Broader Applicability:** The paper focuses on a single application (baby registry recommendation), which limits the demonstration of broader significance. The authors should discuss how their method could be applied to other DPP applications (e.g., document summarization, sensor placement) and whether the advantages observed in the baby registry dataset generalize to these domains.

**Constructive Suggestions:**
1. Expand the comparison section to include more recent DPP learning methods, particularly those that use Bayesian approaches or other non-gradient-based optimization techniques.

2. Provide more detailed analysis of initialization strategies, including how to handle cases where the data is too sparse for moment matching or where the initial kernel is degenerate.

3. Include a theoretical analysis of why the EM approach avoids the degeneracy issues observed in gradient ascent, perhaps by examining the landscape of the likelihood function.

4. Discuss broader applications of the proposed method and provide results on at least one additional DPP application domain to demonstrate wider significance.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the problem of learning DPPs. The EM-based approach is novel, technically sound, and demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods. The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions. While there are some limitations in the scope of the comparison and the application domain, these are relatively minor given the paper's solid technical content and experimental validation.

The paper meets the high standards for NIPS in terms of quality and significance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding the comparison with other state-of-the-art methods and the discussion of broader applicability. The work is important for the machine learning community as it provides a more robust and efficient method for learning DPPs, which are increasingly used in applications requiring subset selection with diversity.
