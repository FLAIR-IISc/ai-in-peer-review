**Summary**
This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. Learning the full, unconstrained kernel is conjectured to be NP-hard, and previous methods have resorted to restrictive parameterizations, learning only item quality scores, or using ensembles that lose the beneficial properties of a single DPP. The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to maximize the data likelihood. The key idea is to leverage the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K and treat the selection of eigenvectors as a latent variable. This allows the authors to construct a tractable lower bound on the log-likelihood and perform coordinate ascent. The E-step involves computing the posterior over the latent eigenvector selections, which is shown to be a k-DPP itself. The M-step provides a closed-form update for the eigenvalues and a gradient-based update for the eigenvectors on the Stiefel manifold. The paper compares this EM approach to a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) on a real-world product recommendation task, demonstrating that the proposed method is more robust, less prone to poor local optima, and computationally faster.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The application of EM to this problem is highly novel. The choice of the eigenvector index set J as the hidden variable is a clever and natural way to decompose the complex likelihood objective. It builds elegantly on the known generative interpretation of DPP sampling. The theoretical result in Lemma 1, which shows the posterior distribution over J is a k-DPP, is a beautiful and insightful connection that makes the E-step tractable.

2.  **Sound Technical Approach:** The derivation of the EM algorithm is solid. The paper correctly identifies a major weakness of the naive projected gradient ascent approach: the projection step can squash eigenvalues to zero, leading to a near-diagonal kernel from which it is difficult to escape, thus failing to model diversity. The proposed EM algorithm circumvents this issue by design. The M-step update for eigenvalues is a closed-form solution, and the update for eigenvectors correctly identifies the problem as optimization on a Stiefel manifold and employs a standard and appropriate technique (multiplicative update via matrix exponential) to handle the orthogonality constraint.

3.  **Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and strongly support the paper's claims.
    *   The choice of a real-world baby registry dataset provides a compelling use-case where modeling diversity is crucial.
    *   The comparison against K-Ascent is the appropriate baseline.
    *   The analysis is insightful, particularly the correlation between EM's performance gains and the "diversity importance" metric `d`. This provides a clear explanation for *why* EM is superior, tying the experimental results back to the methodological motivation (i.e., KA's projection problem).
    *   Showing that EM maintains an advantage in a data-poor setting further highlights its robustness.
    *   The qualitative example of the selected "safety" items is effective in illustrating the practical difference between the models learned by EM and KA.
    *   The runtime analysis confirms the claimed asymptotic advantages of the EM approach for the problem sizes tested.

**Weaknesses and Questions**
1.  **Approximation in the M-step for V:** The paper states that "we do not bother to fully optimize V in each M-step; we simply take a single gradient step on V." This means the algorithm is not performing a true coordinate ascent on the lower bound `F`. This is a practical and reasonable choice, but it technically makes the algorithm a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm. The authors should acknowledge this and briefly discuss the implications. The guarantee of monotonic improvement in the likelihood `L` seems to come from the line search, which checks for improvement in `L` directly, rather than from the standard EM convergence properties. This should be made clearer.

2.  **Scalability Concerns:** The overall runtime includes an `O(N^3)` term for the eigenvector updates (due to the matrix exponential in the Stiefel manifold update). While the experiments show a speedup over the baseline for `N` up to 100, this term will dominate for larger `N`. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the algorithm's limitations for high-dimensional problems (e.g., `N > 1000`) and potential avenues for scaling it up, perhaps through low-rank approximations of `K` or approximations to the matrix exponential.

3.  **Local Optima:** While the paper excellently demonstrates that its EM algorithm avoids the specific poor local optima that plague projected gradient ascent, EM itself is only guaranteed to find a local optimum. The experiments use two initializations, but a more thorough discussion of the sensitivity of the final learned kernel to the initial eigenvectors in `V` would be valuable. The Wishart initialization is agnostic to any structure, but how would the algorithm perform if initialized with eigenvectors that are very far from the optimal ones?

**Critique based on NIPS criteria**
*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound. The proposed algorithm is well-motivated and its derivation is clear. The claims are strongly supported by a well-executed set of experiments on a real-world dataset.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides the necessary background on DPPs, clearly explains the pitfalls of a naive approach, and logically develops the proposed EM algorithm step-by-step. The notation is consistent and the figures are informative.
*   **Originality:** High. This work presents a novel and non-trivial application of the EM framework to a difficult learning problem. While the components (DPPs, EM, Stiefel manifold optimization) are known, their combination to solve the problem of learning an unconstrained DPP kernel is new and insightful. It clearly advances the state of the art beyond previous methods that required restrictive assumptions on the kernel.
*   **Significance:** High. Learning DPP kernels is a fundamental problem with wide-ranging applications in subset selection tasks (recommendation, summarization, etc.). This paper provides the first practical and robust algorithm for learning a full, non-parametric DPP kernel. The method is more robust and faster than the natural baseline, making it highly likely that researchers and practitioners will adopt and build upon this work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant EM algorithm for the challenging problem of learning a full DPP kernel.
*   Clearly identifies and solves a critical failure mode of the naive projected gradient ascent baseline.
*   Provides strong and convincing empirical evidence of the method's superiority in terms of robustness, solution quality, and speed on a real-world task.
*   The work is well-written, technically sound, and represents a significant advance in the field of learning probabilistic models for subset selection.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The M-step for the eigenvectors is a single-step approximation (a GEM step), which could be stated more explicitly.
*   The scalability of the `O(N^3)` eigenvector update step to problems with very large `N` is not addressed.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on learning determinantal point processes. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core novelty and impact of the work.
