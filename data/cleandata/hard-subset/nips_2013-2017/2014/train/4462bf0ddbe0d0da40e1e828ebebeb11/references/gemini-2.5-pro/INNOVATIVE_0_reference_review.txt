### **Summary**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. Maximum likelihood estimation for the full DPP kernel is conjectured to be NP-hard, and prior work has typically resorted to learning restricted parameterizations or using ensembles of fixed kernels. The main contribution of this paper is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full, unconstrained kernel.

The key insight is to work with the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel `K`. The authors reframe the DPP generative process as a mixture of "elementary" DPPs, where each component in the mixture corresponds to a specific subset of eigenvectors. This formulation naturally introduces the selection of eigenvectors (`J`) as a latent variable. The paper then derives an EM algorithm to optimize the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of `K`. The E-step involves computing the posterior over `J`, which is shown to be another k-DPP. The M-step provides a closed-form, projection-free update for the eigenvalues and uses a principled gradient step on the Stiefel manifold for the eigenvectors, cleverly avoiding the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent. The proposed EM method is compared against a projected gradient ascent baseline on a real-world product recommendation task, demonstrating superior robustness and speed.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper is its novelty and the elegance of its approach. Instead of tackling the constrained optimization problem with standard, often problematic, techniques like projection, the authors have reframed the problem in a way that makes the constraints much easier to handle.

1.  **High Originality and Conceptual Advance:** The idea of using the eigendecomposition to define a latent variable (`J`, the set of active eigenvectors) and subsequently derive an EM algorithm is a significant conceptual leap. It provides a completely new and powerful perspective on the DPP learning problem. This is a bold approach that moves the field beyond incremental improvements on existing methods and is likely to inspire similar techniques for other problems involving PSD matrix learning.

2.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Analysis:** The paper does a fantastic job of motivating its approach by first analyzing the failure modes of a more straightforward baseline, projected gradient ascent. The insight that the projection step can prematurely force eigenvalues to zero and trap the optimization in a poor, near-diagonal local optimum is critical. This analysis provides a strong and clear justification for why a new method is needed.

3.  **Principled Optimization:** The resulting EM algorithm is well-derived. The closed-form update for the eigenvalues, which automatically respects the [0, 1] constraint without any projection, is a major advantage. Furthermore, handling the orthogonality constraint on the eigenvectors via optimization on the Stiefel manifold is far more principled than a projection-based alternative. This methodological rigor is a significant strength.

4.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. Using a real-world baby registry dataset is a great choice, as it is a natural application for DPPs where diversity is key. The results clearly demonstrate that the proposed EM algorithm is not only faster but, more importantly, more robust than projected gradient ascent, especially in challenging settings (e.g., less-informed initialization or limited data). The qualitative example of the recommended "safety" items is particularly illustrative, showing a clear, practical advantage for the EM-trained model.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper is technically sound and the core ideas are excellent. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall contribution.

1.  **Generalized EM Implementation:** The M-step for the eigenvectors `V` is not a full maximization but rather a single gradient step. This makes the algorithm a form of Generalized EM (GEM). While this is a standard and practical approach, and the authors are transparent about it, it is a slight deviation from a true EM algorithm.

2.  **M-step Heuristic:** The paper notes that the gradient for `V` is only exact at the beginning of the M-step, and that the M-step's objective function is intractable to evaluate directly. The proposed solution—to perform a line search by checking for improvement in the true data likelihood `L` rather than the lower bound `F`—is a sensible heuristic that works well in practice. However, it does break the clean theoretical picture of coordinate ascent on the lower bound `F`. This is a minor point about implementation pragmatics rather than a fundamental flaw.

### **Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a highly novel and elegant EM-based approach to the difficult problem of learning DPP kernels. This is a significant conceptual contribution.
*   The method provides a principled way to handle the PSD and orthogonality constraints, cleverly sidestepping the known pitfalls of projected gradient methods.
*   The analysis of the baseline's failure modes is insightful and strongly motivates the work.
*   The algorithm is shown to be both faster and more robust than the baseline on a compelling real-world application.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The M-step for the eigenvectors is a single gradient update (GEM) rather than a full optimization, which could be seen as a minor methodological imperfection.
*   The line search for the eigenvector update relies on a practical heuristic (checking the true likelihood) rather than strictly following the optimization of the EM lower bound.

In my view, the arguments for acceptance are overwhelmingly stronger. The paper introduces a bold new direction for a well-known problem, is technically sound, and demonstrates clear practical benefits. The minor weaknesses are acceptable trade-offs for a tractable and effective algorithm and do not undermine the core contribution. This is an excellent paper that represents a clear advance in the field.
