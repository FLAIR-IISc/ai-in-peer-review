

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper proposes an EM algorithm for learning the marginal kernel K of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP), avoiding the problematic projection step required by gradient ascent methods. The authors argue that their approach is more robust to initialization and dataset changes, and asymptotically faster than gradient ascent. The paper is well-organized and makes a contribution to the growing literature on DPP learning.

## Strengths
The paper identifies a genuine problem with the naive gradient ascent approach (projection leading to diagonal matrices that fail to model diversity). The EM approach is theoretically sound and the experiments on the Amazon baby registry dataset provide convincing evidence of improved performance in challenging cases. The connection between the eigendecomposition of K and the EM algorithm is well-motivated.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Reference Omission**: The paper states "learning L from example subsets by maximizing likelihood is conjectured to be NP-hard [1, Conjecture 4.1]" but reference [1] is not listed in the references section. This is a serious omission that undermines the technical credibility of the paper.

2. **Formatting and Notational Errors**:
   - Equation (1): "λj 1− λj" should be "λj/(1-λj)"
   - Equation (3): "det(K − IY )" should be "det(K - I_Y)" where I_Y is the identity matrix with entries zeroed for items in Y
   - Algorithm 1: "η ← η2" should be "η ← η/2" (squaring the step size is incorrect)
   - Algorithm 2: "V ′ ← V exp[η ( V >G−G>V ) ]" contains a typo (missing space, incorrect operator)
   - "the Wishart's mean is NI" is technically incorrect (should be "N*I")

3. **Reproducibility Issues**:
   - The paper cites "Code and data for all experiments can be downloaded from https://code.google.com/p/em-for-dpps" but Google Code was shut down in 2016. This URL is now dead, making the work non-reproducible.

4. **Experimental Details**:
   - The paper states "EM gains in an average of 3.7%" but fails to specify whether this is percentage points or percentage (e.g., 3.7% improvement or 3.7 percentage points).
   - The data split (70% training, 30% testing) lacks specification of whether it was random or stratified.
   - The paper claims "the 4 categories for which EM has the largest gains all exhibit d > 0.025" but doesn't provide the actual d values for these categories.

5. **Logical Inconsistencies**:
   - The paper states "we could project V to ensure this constraint, but, as previously discussed for eigenvalues, projection steps often lead to poor local optima" - however, the previous discussion about projection was about the eigenvalue constraint, not the eigenvector constraint. This is a logical disconnect.

6. **Reference Issues**:
   - Multiple references have incomplete information (e.g., "Learning with Determinantal Point Processes, A. Kulesza, PhD thesis, University of Pennsylvania,, , ,").
   - Some references have inconsistent formatting (e.g., "In Conference on..." vs "In International Conference...").

7. **Minor Technical Errors**:
   - The paper states "k = maxi |Yi|" but fails to clarify that this is the maximum size of the example subsets.
   - "the 4 categories for which EM has the largest gains (safety, furniture, carseats, and strollers) all exhibit d > 0.025" - the paper should provide the actual d values for these categories to support this claim.

## Recommendation

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to DPP learning with reasonable experimental validation, the numerous technical errors, formatting inconsistencies, and critical omissions (especially the missing reference [1] and dead code URL) significantly undermine its quality. The paper fails to meet the basic standards for reproducibility and technical precision expected at NIPS.

The paper's contributions are interesting but the execution contains too many avoidable errors. The reference omission alone is a serious issue that suggests the authors may have been careless in preparing the manuscript. The formatting errors throughout the paper also indicate a lack of attention to detail.

The paper does not provide sufficient evidence of novelty compared to existing DPP learning approaches. While the EM approach is presented as novel, the connection between eigendecomposition and EM for DPPs is not sufficiently differentiated from prior work on DPPs.

Given the number of minor but significant technical flaws that affect the paper's credibility, I recommend rejection. The paper would benefit from a complete revision to address the technical errors, ensure reproducibility, and properly contextualize the contribution relative to prior work.
