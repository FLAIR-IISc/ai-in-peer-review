

# Peer Review: Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): efficiently estimating the marginal kernel matrix K from example subsets without restrictive assumptions. The authors propose an EM-based algorithm that exploits the eigendecomposition of K to avoid the problematic projection steps required by naive gradient ascent (K-Ascent). The paper builds on the DPP theory established in prior work (Kulesza & Taskar, 2012; Affandi et al., 2013) and demonstrates their approach on a product recommendation task using Amazon baby registry data. The key insight is that the hidden variable J (representing which eigenvectors are selected) enables a tractable EM optimization that avoids the degeneracy issues of projection-based methods.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by developing a principled EM algorithm for DPP learning that avoids the degenerate solutions caused by projection steps in gradient ascent. The derivation of the M-step updates (Equations 18-21) is elegant and well-justified, particularly the use of k-DPP marginalization to compute the eigenvalue updates efficiently (O(nNk²) complexity).

2. **Clear Methodological Differentiation**: The paper effectively differentiates their approach from prior work (Kulesza & Taskar, 2011; Affandi et al., 2013; Gillenwater et al., 2013) by showing that their method doesn't require restrictive parameterizations of L or lose DPP properties (unlike L-ensemble methods). The analysis of why projection-based methods fail (Section 3.1) is particularly compelling.

3. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The experiments on Amazon baby registry data (13 categories, N=71 on average) provide convincing evidence of the method's superiority. The analysis correlating the relative gain of EM over KA with the "d" metric (d = 1/N ||M||F/||diag(M)||²) is insightful and demonstrates a clear understanding of when the method provides the most value.

4. **Practical Impact**: The paper demonstrates a 2.1x runtime advantage of EM over KA in practice (Figure 2b), which is significant for real-world applications. The concrete example in Figure 2a showing how EM produces more natural product recommendations than KA further illustrates the practical value.

## Weaknesses and Limitations

1. **Limited Scope of Experiments**: The experiments are confined to a single dataset (Amazon baby registries) with only 13 categories. While the dataset is well-chosen for demonstrating diversity modeling, it's insufficient to establish the method's generalizability across different DPP application domains (e.g., document summarization, sensor placement). A broader empirical evaluation would strengthen the claims.

2. **Theoretical Limitations**: The paper claims the EM algorithm is "asymptotically faster" than gradient ascent (O(T₁nNk² + T₁T₂N³) vs. O(T₁nN³ + T₁T₂N³)), but this assumes k ≪ N, which may not hold in all applications. For example, in document summarization where k is comparable to N, the advantage would diminish. The paper should acknowledge this limitation.

3. **Initialization Sensitivity**: While the paper shows EM is more robust to initialization than KA, the experiments still rely on specific initialization strategies (Wishart and moment-matching). The authors don't explore how sensitive the EM algorithm is to different initializations, particularly for challenging cases where the true kernel has many small eigenvalues.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper doesn't compare with more recent DPP learning approaches, such as the Nyström approximation for large-scale DPPs (Affandi et al., 2013) or the recent work on MAP inference for DPPs (Gillenwater et al., 2013). This omission weakens the position of the paper within the current literature.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a valuable contribution to the DPP learning literature. The EM approach is theoretically well-motivated, avoids the degeneracy issues of previous methods, and demonstrates clear practical advantages. The paper is well-written and the experimental results are convincing within the scope of their evaluation.

The main limitations are the narrow scope of experiments and incomplete comparison with recent literature. However, these do not undermine the core technical contribution, which is novel and well-executed. The paper advances the state-of-the-art in DPP learning by providing a more robust and efficient optimization method that preserves the desirable properties of DPPs.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses an important problem in DPP learning with a novel and well-justified solution. The method has clear theoretical advantages and practical benefits, as demonstrated by the experiments. While the scope of experiments could be expanded, the core contribution is significant and would be valuable to the NIPS community.

The paper's contribution is sufficiently distinct from prior work (Kulesza & Taskar, 2012; Affandi et al., 2013) to warrant publication, and it advances the field by providing a more robust and efficient learning algorithm that avoids the pitfalls of projection-based methods. The method is likely to be widely adopted in applications requiring DPP modeling where diversity and quality balance is critical.
