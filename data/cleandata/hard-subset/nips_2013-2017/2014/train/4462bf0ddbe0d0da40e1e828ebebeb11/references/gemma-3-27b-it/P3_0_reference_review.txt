## Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix *L* for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) from data, a problem known to be NP-hard in general. The authors argue that existing approaches either partially learn *L* (e.g., learning scalar weights) or rely on restrictive parameterizations, losing the benefits of a fully-fledged DPP. They propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, leveraging the eigendecomposition of *L* to circumvent the problematic projection step inherent in naive gradient ascent methods, which often leads to degenerate (near-diagonal) solutions. The core idea is to introduce a hidden variable representing the eigenvectors used to construct a DPP, and then perform coordinate ascent on a lower-bound of the likelihood.  The authors demonstrate, through experiments on baby registry data, that their EM algorithm is both more robust and asymptotically faster than projected gradient ascent.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The EM formulation is a clever way to tackle the non-convex optimization problem of learning *L*. By operating in the eigenspace, it avoids the pitfalls of directly optimizing *L* subject to PSD constraints.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a clear theoretical motivation for the EM approach, grounded in the eigendecomposition properties of DPPs and leveraging Jensen's inequality for bounding the likelihood.
* **Practical Improvements:** The experimental results demonstrate that the EM algorithm outperforms projected gradient ascent, particularly in scenarios where diversity (captured by non-diagonal *L*) is crucial. The analysis showing the link between data characteristics (d, measuring interaction strength) and EM's advantage is insightful.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors correctly identify and analyze the computational complexity of both the EM algorithm and the baseline gradient ascent, highlighting the asymptotic advantage of their method.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the mathematical details. The connections between DPP properties, eigendecomposition, and the EM algorithm are articulated effectively.
* **Good experimental setup:** The use of real-world data (baby registries) is a strong point. The choice of comparing against a reasonable baseline (K-Ascent) and considering different initialization strategies is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Derivation:** While the theoretical exposition is clear overall, some of the derivations (particularly those related to the gradients in the M-step) are relegated to the supplement, making it harder to fully appreciate the technical nuances without diving deeper.  More detail in the main text would be beneficial.
* **Step-Size Heuristic:** The M-step relies on a line search for the optimal step size. The paper mentions using the E-step to evaluate different step sizes, but this feels somewhat heuristic and lacks a rigorous justification. It would be valuable to discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm to the step size choice and possibly explore more sophisticated line search strategies.
* **Scalability Concerns:** Although the authors claim asymptotic speedup, the runtime experiments are limited to relatively small datasets (N=71).  It would be informative to test the algorithm's scalability on larger datasets to more convincingly demonstrate its practical advantage. The complexity analysis suggests the EM algorithm has a favorable scaling with `k`, but this advantage is only truly visible with larger `N` and `n`.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the baby registry task is a good starting point, exploring other application domains would strengthen the paper.  It would be beneficial to demonstrate the algorithm's performance on datasets with different characteristics and potentially compare it to other DPP learning methods (e.g., Bayesian approaches).
* **Initialization Dependency:** The results show a clear dependence on the initialization strategy. The moments-matching initialization significantly improves performance, suggesting that the choice of initialization is crucial.  More discussion on the sensitivity to initialization and potentially developing a more robust initialization procedure would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in DPP learning.
* The EM algorithm demonstrably outperforms a reasonable baseline (K-Ascent) on real-world data.
* The paper provides a detailed computational analysis and identifies a clear asymptotic advantage of the proposed method.
* The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The runtime experiments are limited in scale, making it difficult to fully assess the practical scalability of the algorithm.
* The step-size heuristic in the M-step lacks rigorous justification.
* The scope of experiments is relatively narrow, and exploring other application domains would strengthen the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPPs. The EM algorithm is a clever and effective approach to learning the kernel matrix, and the experimental results are convincing. While there are some areas for improvement (scalability testing, step-size justification, broader experimental scope), the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS, potentially with a minor revision request to address the scalability concern and provide more details on the step size selection process.

**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions)
