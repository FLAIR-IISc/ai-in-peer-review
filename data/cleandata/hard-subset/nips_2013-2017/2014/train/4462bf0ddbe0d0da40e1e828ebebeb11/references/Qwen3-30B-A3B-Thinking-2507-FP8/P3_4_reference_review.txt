

# Peer Review: "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

## Summary
This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the marginal kernel matrix K of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without restrictive parameterizations. The authors address a key limitation of previous gradient-based approaches (K-Ascent) which require projection steps that often result in degenerate, nearly diagonal solutions that fail to model diversity. By leveraging the eigendecomposition of K, the EM algorithm avoids these problematic projections while maintaining the computational advantages of DPPs. The paper provides theoretical analysis of the EM approach and demonstrates empirical improvements over K-Ascent on a baby registry dataset for product recommendation.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper effectively identifies a significant limitation of existing DPP learning methods—projection-induced degeneracy—and provides a compelling theoretical argument for why the EM approach avoids this issue. The connection between the projection step in gradient ascent and the resulting near-diagonal kernels (which fail to capture diversity) is particularly well-explained.

2. **Theoretical Soundness**: The derivation of the EM algorithm is rigorous, with proper handling of the hidden variable J in the DPP mixture representation. The authors correctly identify the need for the Stiefel manifold update to maintain orthogonality constraints, and provide clear computational complexity analysis showing the asymptotic advantage over K-Ascent (O(nNk²) vs O(nN³)).

3. **Empirical Validation**: The experiments on the baby registry dataset are well-designed and demonstrate clear advantages of the EM approach over K-Ascent, particularly in domains with strong negative item interactions (e.g., safety, carseats). The analysis correlating the improvement with the value of d = ||M||_F/||diag(M)||_2 provides insightful validation of the theoretical claims.

4. **Practical Impact**: The paper demonstrates a 2.1x runtime improvement on real-world data, making the method more practical for applications where DPPs are used for subset selection with diversity requirements.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Scope**: While the baby registry dataset is well-chosen for demonstrating the method's advantages, the paper lacks experiments on other DPP application domains (e.g., document summarization, sensor placement) where diversity modeling is critical. This limits the generalizability of the claims about the method's broad utility.

2. **Incomplete Comparison with Prior Work**: The paper does not compare with other state-of-the-art DPP learning approaches, such as the L-ensemble method by [3] or Bayesian methods by [8]. While the authors justify focusing on K-Ascent as a baseline, a comparison with these alternatives would strengthen the argument for the EM approach's superiority.

3. **Convergence Analysis**: The paper lacks a theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the proposed EM algorithm. The authors mention that the M-step is not fully optimized (only a single gradient step is taken), but they do not establish whether this results in convergence to a local optimum or provide conditions for convergence.

4. **Initialization Sensitivity**: While the paper discusses two initialization methods, it doesn't thoroughly analyze how sensitive the EM algorithm is to different initializations. The empirical results show that the moments-matching initializer reduces the gap between EM and K-Ascent, but the authors don't explore whether EM is consistently better across all initialization strategies.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that addresses a meaningful limitation in DPP learning with a novel EM-based approach. The method is theoretically well-motivated, the derivation is rigorous, and the empirical results provide convincing evidence of its advantages over the baseline. While the experimental scope could be expanded, the current results are sufficient to demonstrate the method's value for the specific application domain studied.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the DPP literature by providing a learning method that avoids the degeneracy issues of previous approaches without restricting the kernel to a parametric form. The theoretical analysis of the computational complexity is particularly strong, showing a clear asymptotic advantage that is borne out in practice.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The method is novel, the theoretical and empirical analyses are sound, and the practical advantages are significant. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling for subset selection.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Including comparisons with other DPP learning methods (e.g., [3], [8])
2. Expanding the experimental validation to additional application domains
3. Providing more detailed convergence analysis of the EM algorithm

However, these are enhancements rather than necessary additions for acceptance, and the current paper makes a valuable contribution to the field.

## Final Verdict

This paper presents a significant improvement over existing methods for learning DPPs, with clear theoretical and empirical advantages. It addresses a well-known limitation in the field and provides a robust, efficient solution that avoids the degeneracy issues of previous approaches. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and has clear practical implications for applications requiring diverse subset selection.
