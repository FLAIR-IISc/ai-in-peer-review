

# Peer Review: "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

## Summary
This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning Determinantal Point Process (DPP) parameters from example subsets. DPPs are valuable for subset selection tasks requiring a balance between item quality and diversity, but maximum likelihood learning of the kernel matrix K is known to be NP-hard. The authors propose an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that leverages the eigendecomposition of K to avoid the problematic projection steps required by gradient ascent methods. Their key insight is viewing DPPs as mixtures of elementary DPPs, where eigenvalues serve as mixture weights. This approach enables direct optimization over eigenvalues and eigenvectors, eliminating the need for projection steps that often lead to degenerate (nearly diagonal) solutions. The paper demonstrates that their EM algorithm outperforms projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) in both likelihood and runtime on a baby registry recommendation task.

The work builds on foundational DPP literature (Kulesza & Taskar) and prior learning approaches (Affandi et al., Kulesza & Taskar), but distinguishes itself by providing a non-parametric method that avoids restrictive assumptions about the kernel structure. Unlike previous methods that either assume fixed parameters (Kulesza & Taskar) or learn ensembles of DPPs (Kulesza & Taskar), this approach directly learns the full kernel matrix.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with clear theoretical derivations of the EM algorithm. The authors provide thorough analysis of both the eigenvalue and eigenvector updates, carefully addressing the computational complexity (O(T₁nNk² + T₁T₂N³)) and demonstrating how it improves upon the O(T₁nN³) complexity of K-Ascent. The experimental section is well-designed with multiple initialization strategies (Wishart and moment matching), comprehensive evaluation metrics (test log-likelihood), and insightful analysis of when EM provides the most benefit (based on the diversity of the data). The authors acknowledge limitations, particularly regarding initialization requirements and the edge case when eigenvalues approach 1.

## Clarity
The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The background section concisely introduces DPPs and their relationship to the marginal kernel K. Algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are precise and well-explained. The experimental setup is thoroughly documented, with appropriate details about the baby registry dataset. The figures effectively illustrate key findings, particularly Figure 1 which shows the relative performance gains across different categories. The only minor weakness is that some technical derivations (e.g., eigenvector updates) are relegated to the supplement, which might hinder reproducibility for readers.

## Originality
The paper presents a novel approach to DPP learning by leveraging the eigendecomposition for EM optimization. While there has been prior work on DPP learning (Kulesza & Taskar, Affandi et al.), this is the first to develop an EM-based method that avoids projection steps. The key insight about viewing DPPs as mixtures of elementary DPPs for EM optimization is original and well-justified. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from previous work, particularly highlighting how it overcomes the degeneracy issues of gradient-based methods.

## Significance
The paper addresses an important problem with practical implications for recommendation systems, document summarization, and other subset selection tasks. The empirical results demonstrate meaningful improvements in both likelihood (up to 16.5% gain in some categories) and runtime (2.1x faster on average). The authors provide clear analysis of when the method provides the most benefit (when diversity is important), which is valuable for practitioners. This work advances the state of the art in DPP learning by providing a more robust and efficient approach that can be directly applied in real-world settings.

## Strengths
- Novel EM approach that avoids problematic projection steps in gradient ascent
- Strong theoretical justification with clear derivations
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation showing both performance and runtime advantages
- Insightful analysis of when the method provides the most benefit
- Well-written and well-organized paper with clear comparisons to prior work

## Weaknesses
- The method still requires a reasonable initialization (though they provide two sensible strategies)
- The paper doesn't fully explore the boundary case when eigenvalues approach 1
- Limited scalability analysis for very large N (though runtime analysis suggests good scaling)

## Recommendation
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling for subset selection. The EM-based learning approach is novel, well-justified, and empirically superior to existing methods. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with practical significance. While the contribution is not revolutionary, it represents a substantial improvement over previous approaches with clear theoretical and empirical justification.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is of high quality with strong theoretical and experimental support, clearly written, original in its approach to DPP learning, and significant for the field. I believe this work would be of interest to the NIPS community and would be valuable to both researchers and practitioners working with DPPs.

**Final Recommendation: Accept**
