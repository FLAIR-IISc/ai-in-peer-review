- Addresses NP-hardness of unconstrained DPP MLE by proposing an EM algorithm using eigendecomposition of marginal kernel K to avoid projection steps.  
- Builds on Kulesza & Taskar and prior DPP work, using eigendecomposition as a natural PSD-respecting parameterization.  
- Clear demonstration that gradient ascent (K-Ascent) suffers projection-induced degeneracy and fails to model diversity.  
- Theoretical development: technically sound EM derivation with clear E-step (Lemma 1) and M-step updates.  
- Insightful connection between hidden variable J and k-DPPs.  
- Strong experimental validation on Amazon baby registries showing EM models diversity better (notably in high-diversity categories) and up to 16.5% log-likelihood gains in data-poor settings.  
- Practical runtime advantage reported (EM ~2.1x faster on average) and avoids complex projection steps, improving robustness to initialization.  
- Limited experimental scope: evaluation only on a single dataset (13 categories), reducing claims of generalizability.  
- Missing comparisons to important baselines: Bayesian method of Affandi et al. and L-ensemble approach by Kulesza & Taskar.  
- Initialization sensitivity: both EM and K-Ascent struggle with poor initializations; moment-matching init appears ad-hoc and needs systematic analysis.  
- Theoretical gaps: no convergence guarantees for the EM algorithm despite claimed asymptotic runtime improvements.  
- Complexity claims (O(T₁nNk²) vs O(T₁nN³)) need empirical runtime validation across varying N.  
- Handling of boundary cases (eigenvalues exactly equal to 1) is underspecified and may cause numerical instability.  
- Contribution is novel and significant: principled unconstrained kernel estimation for DPPs via EM, distinct from prior NIPS work.  
- Recommendations: add comparisons to Bayesian and L-ensemble baselines, include at least one additional dataset (e.g., document summarization), analyze initialization sensitivity and boundary cases, and provide empirical runtime measurements.  
- Overall assessment: technically strong, practically effective, well-written; recommended for acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 7/10.
