

# Peer Review: "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

## Summary and Context

This paper proposes an EM-based algorithm for learning the marginal kernel K of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) without restrictive assumptions about its form. The authors address a key limitation of existing approaches: gradient ascent methods for DPP learning require projection steps to maintain positive semi-definiteness, which often results in degenerate (nearly diagonal) solutions that fail to model diversity. Previous work has either restricted learning to scalar weights [2], used L-ensembles [3] (losing DPP properties), or assumed parametric forms [8].

The paper's key insight is recognizing that a DPP can be viewed as a mixture of elementary DPPs (with different sets of selected eigenvectors), enabling an EM framework where the hidden variable is the set of selected eigenvectors J. The E-step computes the posterior distribution over J, and the M-step updates the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of K. The authors show this approach avoids the problematic projection steps of gradient ascent.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-justified theoretical derivations. The authors provide clear mathematical foundations for their EM algorithm, including the E-step (Lemma 1) and M-step updates (Equations 18-21). The complexity analysis showing O(nNk²) runtime for EM versus O(nN³) for gradient ascent is compelling, especially for small k (the maximum subset size).

The experimental evaluation on a baby registry dataset is well-designed. The authors compare their EM algorithm to K-Ascent (gradient ascent with projection) under two initialization strategies (Wishart and moment-matching). Their analysis using the d statistic (1/N ||M||F/||diag(M)||²) to correlate with EM's relative gains is insightful, demonstrating that EM excels when negative interactions between items are strong.

However, the evaluation has limitations:
1. The dataset size (N=71 items on average) is modest for a problem often applied to larger-scale scenarios
2. The paper lacks comparison with more recent DPP learning methods (e.g., [13, 19])
3. The runtime comparison is based on a single dataset without scalability analysis for larger N

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The connection between the DPP's eigendecomposition and the EM framework is clearly explained. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-2) are sufficiently detailed for reproduction. However, the eigenvector update (Equation 21) is somewhat opaque without more explanation of the Stiefel manifold optimization connection. A brief intuitive explanation would help readers understand why this multiplicative update is appropriate.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant contribution to DPP learning by introducing a novel EM approach that avoids projection steps while learning unconstrained kernels. The key insight—viewing DPPs as mixtures of elementary DPPs to enable EM—is not immediately obvious and represents a clean solution to a well-known problem in the literature. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from prior work, particularly the degeneracy issues of gradient-based methods.

## Significance

The paper addresses a practical limitation in DPP applications where modeling negative interactions (diversity) is crucial. The baby registry experiments demonstrate that EM produces more diverse and realistic recommendations than gradient ascent (Figure 2a vs. 2b). The asymptotic runtime advantage (O(nNk²) vs. O(nN³)) is particularly significant for large-scale applications where N is substantial.

The significance could be strengthened by:
1. Discussing applications beyond product recommendation
2. Providing more concrete examples of where the algorithm would be essential
3. Addressing potential limitations for extremely large N

## Strengths

1. Novel EM framework that avoids degeneracy issues of gradient ascent
2. Clear theoretical justification for the approach
3. Comprehensive experimental evaluation on a real-world dataset
4. Demonstrated runtime advantage with practical significance
5. Insightful analysis of when the method excels (strong negative interactions)

## Weaknesses

1. Limited dataset scale (N=71) for a problem often applied to larger domains
2. Missing comparison with more recent DPP learning methods
3. Insufficient theoretical analysis of EM convergence properties
4. Incomplete explanation of eigenvector update mechanism
5. Limited discussion of scalability to very large N

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a solid technical contribution to DPP learning, addressing a real limitation in existing methods with a novel approach. The experimental results are convincing, and the asymptotic runtime advantage is significant for practical applications.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a comparison with recent DPP learning methods (e.g., [13, 19]) to better position the work
2. Provide more detailed analysis of the EM algorithm's convergence properties
3. Include a more intuitive explanation of the eigenvector update (Equation 21)
4. Discuss potential limitations for extremely large-scale applications
5. Consider testing on a larger dataset to better demonstrate the asymptotic runtime advantage

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it presents a theoretically grounded method with clear practical significance for a growing area of machine learning applications. The contribution is both novel and practically relevant, advancing the state of the art in DPP learning.
