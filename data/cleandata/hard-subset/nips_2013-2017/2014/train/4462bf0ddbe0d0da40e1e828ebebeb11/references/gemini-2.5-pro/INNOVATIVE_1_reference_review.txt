**Summary**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full N x N kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. Maximum likelihood estimation for this problem is conjectured to be NP-hard, and prior work has either resorted to learning restricted parameterizations of the kernel or employed a straightforward projected gradient ascent method. The authors identify a critical flaw in the latter approach, arguing that the projection step required to maintain positive semi-definiteness often leads to degenerate, near-diagonal solutions that fail to model diversity. The core contribution of this paper is a novel expectation-maximization (EM) style algorithm for this learning problem. The key idea is to work with the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix and introduce a latent variable corresponding to the subset of eigenvectors chosen in the DPP's generative process. This formulation leads to an iterative algorithm that elegantly avoids the problematic projection step and is shown to be more robust and efficient than the gradient ascent baseline.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The primary strength of this paper is its bold and highly novel approach to a difficult problem. Instead of making incremental improvements to existing methods, the authors reframe the learning problem entirely. The insight to use the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs interpretation (Eq. 7) to define a latent variable and enable an EM algorithm is a significant conceptual leap. This is a creative and elegant piece of theoretical work that opens up a new direction for learning DPPs.

2.  **Solves a Critical Flaw in the Baseline:** The paper provides a very clear and compelling motivation for its new approach by diagnosing a fundamental weakness in the naive projected gradient ascent method (K-Ascent). The discussion of how the projection step can prematurely squash eigenvalues to zero, leading to near-diagonal kernels that cannot escape this poor local optimum, is insightful. The proposed EM algorithm, with its closed-form updates for eigenvalues and a principled Stiefel manifold update for eigenvectors, directly and effectively circumvents this major issue.

3.  **Strong Empirical Demonstration:** The experiments, while focused on a single domain, are well-designed and convincingly support the paper's claims. The comparison on the baby registry dataset clearly shows that the EM algorithm is more robust than K-Ascent, particularly when starting from a less-informed initialization or in a data-poor setting. The qualitative example—where K-Ascent recommends three different baby monitors while EM produces a more diverse and sensible set—is a powerful illustration of the practical importance of correctly learning the diversity-encoding off-diagonal terms. The reported speedup is also a significant practical advantage.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Approximations in the M-step:** The maximization step for the eigenvectors (V) is not fully optimized within each iteration. The authors resort to taking a single gradient step, using a gradient formula (Eq. 20) that is only exact at the beginning of the M-step. While this is a reasonable pragmatic choice that seems to work well, it deviates from a pure EM/GEM framework. A more thorough discussion of the implications of this approximation would be beneficial. However, for a paper introducing a fundamentally new direction, this level of methodological compromise is acceptable.

2.  **Limited Scope of Experimental Comparison:** The experiments focus exclusively on comparing the proposed EM algorithm to the projected gradient ascent baseline. While this is the most direct competitor for learning an unconstrained kernel, it would have been valuable to see a comparison against other established methods, such as those that learn restricted parameterizations (e.g., Kulesza & Taskar, 2011) or Bayesian approaches (Affandi et al., 2013). This would help to better quantify the practical benefit of learning a full, unconstrained kernel over these simpler but potentially effective alternatives.

**Recommendation**

Accept.

This paper presents a significant and exciting advance in the study of determinantal point processes. The problem of learning a full kernel is fundamental, and the proposed EM algorithm is a genuinely new and powerful tool for tackling it. The authors' approach is not merely an incremental tweak but a bold rethinking of the optimization landscape, which successfully addresses a critical failure mode of existing methods.

While the M-step contains a practical approximation and the experimental comparison could be broader, these are minor points that do not detract from the core contribution. The novelty of the EM formulation, its elegant connection to the generative view of DPPs, and its demonstrated robustness are outstanding. This work provides a new, superior method for a key machine learning problem and opens the door for future research. This is precisely the type of creative, high-impact paper that should be highlighted at NIPS.
