### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. Learning the full, unconstrained kernel via maximum likelihood is conjectured to be NP-hard, and prior work has typically focused on restricted learning settings, such as learning only item quality scores or learning mixtures of fixed DPPs. The main contribution of this paper is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full DPP kernel. The key idea is to leverage the eigendecomposition of the kernel and treat the selection of eigenvectors in the DPP's generative process as a latent variable. This formulation allows the authors to derive an EM procedure that iteratively updates the kernel's eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The authors compare their method to a natural baseline of projected gradient ascent on the kernel matrix. They provide a clear analysis showing that the projection step in the baseline can lead to poor, near-diagonal local optima that fail to capture the diversity-promoting aspect of DPPs. In contrast, their EM approach avoids this projection step, leading to a more robust algorithm. The paper demonstrates through experiments on a real-world product recommendation task (Amazon baby registries) that the proposed EM algorithm is not only more robust to initialization and data sparsity but is also asymptotically and empirically faster than projected gradient ascent.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:** The derivation of the EM algorithm is elegant and technically sound. The insight to use the eigenvector selection as a latent variable is novel and effective. A key technical contribution is Lemma 1, which shows that the posterior distribution over the latent variables required for the E-step is itself a tractable k-DPP. The M-step updates are also carefully derived: the eigenvalue update is a closed-form expression computed efficiently via k-DPP marginalization, and the eigenvector update cleverly uses optimization on the Stiefel manifold to avoid a problematic projection. The experimental work is thorough and convincing, providing strong empirical support for all of the paper's claims. The authors are careful to diagnose *why* the baseline fails, linking its poor performance to the projection step and a measurable property of the datasets.
*   **Weaknesses:** The M-step update for the eigenvectors is a slight weakness. It is not a full maximization but a single gradient step using a gradient formula (Eq. 20) that is only exact at the beginning of the M-step. While this is a pragmatic choice and the line search on the likelihood objective ensures monotonic improvement, a discussion of the theoretical implications of this approximation would strengthen the paper. For instance, does this impact the convergence guarantees of the algorithm?

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

*   **Strengths:** The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem and situating their work within the existing literature. The background on DPPs is concise and provides all the necessary information. The step-by-step development of the EM algorithm is easy to follow, and the explanation for why projected gradient ascent is flawed is particularly insightful and intuitive. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly. The paper is well-organized and provides enough detail for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.

*   **Strengths:** To my knowledge, this is the first application of an EM-style algorithm to learn the full, unconstrained kernel of a DPP. This is a significant departure from previous work which either learned restricted models or used different optimization techniques for parametric forms. The conceptual leap to frame the problem with a latent variable based on the eigendecomposition is a novel and powerful idea that elegantly sidesteps the difficulties of direct constrained optimization.

**Significance:** The results are significant and likely to have a strong impact.

*   **Strengths:** Learning is a fundamental bottleneck for the wider application of DPPs. This paper provides a principled, robust, and efficient algorithm for the most general version of the DPP learning problem. This is a substantial advance for the field. Researchers and practitioners who use DPPs for tasks like recommendation, summarization, or search will find this method highly valuable, as it allows them to learn the crucial diversity-modeling components of the kernel directly from data. The paper demonstrably advances the state of the art in DPP learning. The provided code is also a valuable contribution to the community.
*   **Weaknesses:** The computational advantage of the EM algorithm is most pronounced when the size of the observed subsets (`k`) is much smaller than the total number of items (`N`). While this is true for the application in the paper, the authors could be slightly more explicit about this limitation. In domains where `k` is close to `N`, the computational benefits over the baseline may be reduced.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Novelty:** Proposes a novel and elegant EM framework for the fundamental and difficult problem of learning a full DPP kernel.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The method is well-founded theoretically and the derivation is solid.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The algorithm is shown to be more robust and faster than a strong baseline on a compelling real-world task. The analysis of the baseline's failure modes is insightful.
4.  **High Impact:** This work significantly advances the state-of-the-art in learning DPPs and provides a practical tool that is likely to be widely used and built upon.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
1.  The M-step for the eigenvectors is based on an approximate, first-order gradient step rather than a full maximization, which is a minor deviation from the standard EM framework whose implications are not fully discussed.
2.  The computational advantage is dependent on the size of the observed subsets, a limitation that could be stated more prominently.

These weaknesses are minor compared to the paper's considerable strengths. This is an excellent paper that represents a clear and significant contribution to the machine learning community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
