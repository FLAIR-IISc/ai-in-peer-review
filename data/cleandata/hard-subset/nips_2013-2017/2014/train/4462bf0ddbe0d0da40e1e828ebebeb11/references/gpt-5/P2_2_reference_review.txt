Summary
The paper proposes an EM-style algorithm for maximum likelihood learning of a full, unconstrained DPP marginal kernel K (equivalently L) from observed subsets. The key idea is to exploit the known representation of a DPP as a mixture over elementary DPPs indexed by a subset J of eigenvectors. Treating J as a latent variable yields a tractable E-step: p(J|Y) is a k-DPP whose kernel can be constructed efficiently. The M-step decomposes into (i) closed-form updates of eigenvalues via expected inclusion probabilities under the k-DPP, computable in O(Nk^2) per example, and (ii) a gradient step for eigenvectors on the Stiefel manifold with a line search checked against the true likelihood. Compared to projected gradient ascent on K (KA), EM avoids the PSD/eigenvalue-capping projection that can induce degenerate near-diagonal solutions, is asymptotically faster when typical set size k ≪ N, and is empirically more robust to initialization. Experiments on a large-scale Amazon baby registry dataset show consistent test log-likelihood gains over KA (notably in categories with stronger negative interactions) and about 2x speedups; EM remains advantageous in data-poor regimes.

Relation to prior work
Prior DPP learning efforts either learned only per-item quality weights (convex but limited), learned mixtures of fixed kernels (losing DPP properties), or imposed parametric structures with Bayesian inference. This paper directly learns an unconstrained kernel by leveraging the eigendecomposition and the mixture view, which to my knowledge is novel. It also draws on k-DPP marginalization and optimization on the Stiefel manifold.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The EM lower bound is standard; the E-step (q = pK(J|Y)) is exact and its identification as a k-DPP is correct. The eigenvalue M-step is derived cleanly and uses k-DPP marginals to avoid exponential sums. The eigenvector update is a generalized EM step: the provided gradient (valid when q = p) enables a principled manifold step, and monotonicity is enforced by checking the true likelihood with refreshed E-steps. This is a reasonable and honest compromise given intractability of the full M-step. Complexity analysis is plausible: O(T1 n N k^2 + T1 T2 N^3) vs O(T1 n N^3 + T1 T2 N^3) for KA.
- Empirical support: The baby registry experiments are realistic and fairly large. Results substantiate the claims: EM improves test likelihood notably when off-diagonal structure matters, remains better in low-data settings, and is faster. The qualitative example highlights the diversity benefit. However, experiments are limited to one application domain, and comparisons to other learning approaches (e.g., Bayesian methods; quality-only baselines) are missing.
- Limitations and honesty: The paper acknowledges that V-updates are approximate, that λ must be kept below 1 for stability, and that both methods are sensitive to degenerate initializations. Proofs and some derivations are deferred to the supplement.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The background succinctly sets up L- and K-formulations and the mixture view. Algorithms 1 and 2 are helpful. Some derivations (Lemma 1, Eq. 20) are deferred; including brief intuition in the main text would aid readability. Notation is mostly consistent; a small glossary for U, R, BY would help.

Originality
The EM formulation for unconstrained DPP learning via the eigenstructure and latent J is, to my knowledge, new. The combination of k-DPP conditioning for the E-step and Stiefel manifold updates for V is also novel in this context. The analysis of KA’s projection pathology provides useful insight.

Significance
Learning full DPP kernels is important across summarization, recommendation, and sensor placement. The proposed method addresses a long-standing practical bottleneck (robustness and speed of training) and should be broadly useful. While demonstrated on one dataset, the approach is general and maintains DPP tractability.

Pros
- Conceptually elegant EM framework grounded in DPP mixture structure.
- Avoids projection-induced degeneracy; empirically more robust and faster than KA.
- Closed-form eigenvalue updates with efficient k-DPP marginals; principled manifold updates for eigenvectors.
- Realistic large-scale evaluation; code availability.

Cons
- Limited experimental breadth: one domain, no comparison to Bayesian/parametric DPP learning or to quality-only baselines.
- Generalized EM without formal convergence guarantees; reliance on recomputing E-steps for line search could be costly in some regimes.
- Performance gains depend on small k; discussion of behavior when k is large is brief.
- Some key proofs/derivations are deferred; more main-text guidance would help.

Questions and suggestions
- Please report the number of line-search trials (T2) and its variance across categories; how often does the likelihood decrease before accepting a step?
- Provide an ablation: eigenvalues-only updates vs full EM to quantify the contribution of V updates.
- Compare to at least one Bayesian/parametric DPP learner and to the convex quality-weights baseline to contextualize gains.
- Discuss/illustrate behavior when typical |Y| is large (k approaching N/2); does EM retain advantages?
- Clarify numerical stabilization near λ ≈ 1 and whether any regularization (e.g., trace or Frobenius penalties) improves generalization.
- Consider adding a second dataset (e.g., document summarization) to demonstrate generality.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a novel and practically relevant learning algorithm with solid technical underpinnings and convincing evidence on a real dataset. Expanding experimental comparisons and adding minor clarifications would strengthen it further.

Confidence
High on the DPP and optimization aspects; moderate on empirical generality pending broader evaluations.
