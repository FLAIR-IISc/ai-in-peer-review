This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), circumventing the difficulties associated with directly maximizing the likelihood via gradient ascent, which necessitates problematic projection steps. The core idea is to exploit the eigendecomposition of the DPP kernel, introducing a hidden variable related to the eigenvectors and formulating an EM algorithm that alternates between updating eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The authors provide theoretical justification for their approach and experimental validation on a baby registry dataset.

While the problem addressed – efficient DPP learning – is undeniably important and the proposed EM algorithm is a conceptually sound approach, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor but persistent flaws that collectively detract from its overall quality. The presentation, while generally clear, is often unnecessarily verbose and lacks precision. The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating some benefit of the EM algorithm, is not sufficiently rigorous to convincingly establish its superiority.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The EM approach to DPP learning, avoiding the problematic projection step of gradient ascent, is a valuable contribution. The connection to eigendecomposition and the introduction of a hidden variable for the eigenvectors are insightful.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the EM update equations appears correct, and the explanation of why gradient ascent struggles is reasonable.
*   **Practical Relevance:**  DPPs have applications in numerous areas, and efficient learning algorithms are crucial for their widespread adoption.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Excessive Verbosity & Lack of Precision:** The paper is significantly longer than necessary.  Many explanations are repetitive. For example, the motivation for using DPPs and the description of their properties are reiterated multiple times. Technical details are sometimes buried in lengthy prose. For instance, the explanation of the Stiefel manifold update (Section 3.5) is needlessly complex and lacks a clear justification for the simplifications made.
*   **Insufficient Experimental Rigor:** The experimental setup, while using a real-world dataset, is not entirely convincing. The comparison with K-Ascent (KA) relies on two initialization schemes, but the sensitivity of both methods to initialization is not thoroughly investigated.  The claim that EM outperforms KA "in the high-d setting" (where 'd' relates to the marginals) feels post-hoc and lacks a clear definition of what constitutes a "high-d setting."  The discussion of runtime improvements is weak, primarily stating an asymptotic advantage without sufficient empirical evidence to support the claim. Furthermore, error bars or statistical significance tests are missing from all results.
*   **Minor Technical Inconsistencies:** Equation (1) states K and L share the same eigenvectors ‘v’. While true, the subsequent discussion refers to eigenvalues λ of K corresponding to eigenvalues λ/(1-λ) of L, without explicitly mentioning the same eigenvectors are used, causing a slight loss of clarity. The explanation around Equation (17) lacks sufficient context; why is R used? What is its purpose?
*   **Formatting Issues:** The paper contains several minor formatting inconsistencies, such as inconsistent use of boldface and italics, and occasional awkward sentence constructions. The citation format appears non-standard (e.g. "[27, Page 326]").
*   **Overstated Claims:** The statement that EM "negates the need" for the projection step in gradient ascent is misleading. While it *avoids* it, this is a direct consequence of the EM formulation, not a fundamental property.
* **Missing Details:** The hyperparameter choices for both KA and EM (step sizes, number of iterations, convergence criteria) are not adequately documented, hindering reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The proposed EM algorithm is a novel and potentially valuable contribution to the field of DPP learning.
*   The theoretical justification is sound.
*   The experimental results, while not conclusive, suggest that the EM algorithm can outperform K-Ascent in certain scenarios.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper suffers from significant issues related to clarity, conciseness, and experimental rigor.
*   The experimental evaluation is not sufficiently convincing to establish the superiority of the proposed method.
*   Numerous minor technical and formatting flaws detract from the overall quality of the work.
*   The claims made are occasionally overstated.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the approach and the theoretical soundness of the derivation, the numerous flaws outlined above make it difficult to recommend acceptance. While the core idea is promising, the paper requires substantial revision to address the concerns raised. A more rigorous experimental evaluation, a clearer and more concise presentation, and careful attention to detail are essential for elevating this work to the standards expected of a NIPS publication.

**Recommendation: Reject.**  I would be open to reconsidering a substantially revised version of this paper in the future.
