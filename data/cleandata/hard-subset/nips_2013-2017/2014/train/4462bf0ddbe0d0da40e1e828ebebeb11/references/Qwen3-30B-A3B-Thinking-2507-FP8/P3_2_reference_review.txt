

# Peer Review: "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

## Summary and Context
This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in DPP learning: the NP-hardness of maximum likelihood estimation for unconstrained DPPs. The authors propose an EM-based algorithm that leverages the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K to avoid the problematic projection steps required by gradient ascent methods (K-Ascent). This work builds upon Kulesza and Taskar's foundational DPP literature [1, 5] and addresses limitations in prior approaches like L-ensembles [3] and Bayesian methods [8]. The key insight—using the eigendecomposition as a natural parameterization that respects the PSD constraint—represents a significant methodological contribution.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Identification of a Critical Problem**: The paper effectively demonstrates how gradient ascent methods (K-Ascent) often fail to model diversity due to projection-induced degeneracy (Figure 1a), a problem that has been noted but not adequately solved in prior work.

2. **Elegant Theoretical Development**: The derivation of the EM algorithm is technically sound, with clear E-step (Lemma 1) and M-step updates. The connection between the hidden variable J and k-DPPs is particularly insightful and well-justified.

3. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The baby registry experiments convincingly demonstrate EM's superiority in modeling diversity (high-d categories like "safety" and "carseats"), with statistically significant log-likelihood improvements (up to 16.5% in data-poor settings). The runtime analysis (2.1x faster on average) provides practical relevance.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The paper successfully avoids the degeneracy issue without relying on complex projection steps, making the method more robust to initialization than prior approaches.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Limited Experimental Scope**: The evaluation is restricted to a single dataset (Amazon baby registries) with only 13 categories. To establish broader significance, the authors should compare against additional datasets (e.g., document summarization [4] or sensor placement [6]) to demonstrate the method's generalizability.

2. **Incomplete Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The paper fails to compare against the Bayesian approach of Affandi et al. [8] or the L-ensemble method of Kulesza and Taskar [3]. These are relevant baselines that address similar learning challenges but with different trade-offs. Without these comparisons, the claim of "significant advantage" lacks full context.

3. **Initialization Sensitivity**: The paper acknowledges that both EM and K-Ascent struggle with poor initializations (e.g., diagonal kernels), but the moment-matching initialization (Equation 23) appears ad-hoc. The authors should provide more systematic analysis of how sensitive their method is to initialization choices.

4. **Theoretical Gaps**: While the paper claims asymptotic runtime improvements, it doesn't establish convergence guarantees for the EM algorithm. The practical impact of the O(T₁nNk²) vs. O(T₁nN³) complexity comparison could be strengthened with empirical runtime measurements for varying N.

5. **Handling Boundary Cases**: The paper mentions a "small caveat" about eigenvalues exactly equal to 1 but doesn't provide a robust solution. For real-world applications, this could lead to numerical instability that needs addressing.

## Significance and Originality

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to DPP learning by providing a principled method for unconstrained kernel estimation. The EM approach is a novel application of hidden variable techniques to DPPs, and the avoidance of projection steps represents a substantial practical improvement over gradient-based methods. The work is clearly distinct from prior NIPS publications like "Learning the Parameters of Determinantal Point Process Kernels" [8] and "k-DPPs: Fixed-Size Determinantal Point Processes" [3], as it addresses the unconstrained learning problem directly.

## Recommendations

This paper presents a solid methodological contribution with strong experimental validation on a relevant application. To strengthen the submission:

1. Expand the experimental section to include comparisons against Bayesian DPP learning [8] and L-ensemble methods [3].
2. Add at least one additional dataset (e.g., document summarization) to demonstrate broader applicability.
3. Provide more thorough analysis of initialization sensitivity and boundary cases.
4. Include empirical runtime measurements to complement the theoretical complexity analysis.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPP learning by addressing a persistent challenge in the literature. The EM-based approach is theoretically sound, practically effective, and demonstrates clear advantages over gradient ascent methods in modeling diversity. While the experimental scope could be broadened, the technical quality is high, and the method is sufficiently novel for NIPS. The paper is well-written, clear, and makes a strong case for the superiority of the proposed approach.

**Recommendation: Accept** (with minor revisions to address the experimental limitations and comparisons)

**Quality**: 8/10 (Strong technical content, but limited experimental scope)
**Clarity**: 9/10 (Well-organized and clearly written)
**Originality**: 8/10 (Novel application of EM to DPP learning)
**Significance**: 7/10 (Important problem, but could be strengthened with broader evaluation)
