This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing the difficulty of directly maximizing the likelihood function due to the positive semi-definiteness constraint on the kernel matrix L. The authors correctly identify existing approaches (scalar weighting, L-ensembles, Bayesian parameter estimation) as either incomplete or with limitations regarding computational properties and generality. Their core idea—leveraging the eigendecomposition of L and formulating an EM algorithm—is a reasonable approach. The paper is well-motivated, clearly explaining the benefits of DPPs and the challenges in learning their parameters.

However, while the approach is sound in principle, the paper presents it with a degree of optimism not fully justified by the experimental results. The claim of asymptotic runtime improvement over projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) is stated without sufficient detail, and the empirical demonstration, while favoring EM, isn't overwhelmingly convincing. The paper repeatedly emphasizes the problem of K-Ascent getting stuck in near-diagonal solutions, but the provided results (Figure 1a) show only marginal improvements in log-likelihood, often within the noise of a typical experimental setup. The reliance on the “baby registry” dataset, while practically relevant, feels somewhat narrow for a paper aiming for general NIPS acceptance.

The methodological soundness is generally good. The derivation of the EM update equations appears correct, and the justification for each step is provided. The use of the k-DPP in the E-step to simplify computations is clever. However, the practical implementation details—particularly the handling of the Stiefel manifold constraint and the step size selection in the M-step—feel glossed over. The claim of a “self-normalizing” eigenvalue update isn’t fully satisfying; capping the eigenvalues “slightly below 1” feels like a pragmatic workaround rather than a theoretically motivated solution. 

Clarity is a strength. The paper is well-written and logically organized. The background section provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with DPPs. The equations are clearly presented, and the algorithm descriptions are reasonably detailed. However, the connection between the theoretical advantages (asymptotic runtime, avoidance of projection pitfalls) and the observed experimental results is not fully elucidated.

The originality is moderate. The idea of using eigendecomposition to facilitate DPP learning isn’t entirely novel—it's a natural extension of existing DPP algorithms that rely on this decomposition for other purposes. The EM formulation itself, while clever, feels like a relatively straightforward application of a standard optimization technique. The real contribution lies in demonstrating that this approach *works* and potentially offers advantages in certain settings, but these advantages aren't dramatic. The paper does a reasonable job of citing related work, but there's a slight tendency to frame the contributions as more groundbreaking than they appear.

The significance is currently limited. While the results show EM can outperform K-Ascent, the gains are not consistently large. The specific application domain (product recommendation) is important, but the generalizability of the findings to other DPP applications is not thoroughly explored. The paper doesn't demonstrate a substantial advance in the state-of-the-art in DPP learning.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a technically sound and reasonably well-motivated approach to DPP learning.
*   Addresses a known challenge in DPP learning (likelihood maximization under PSD constraints).
*   Demonstrates empirical improvements over a baseline algorithm (K-Ascent).
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental results are not overwhelmingly convincing. The improvements over K-Ascent are often marginal.
*   The theoretical advantages (asymptotic runtime) are not fully substantiated by empirical evidence.
*   The application domain is somewhat narrow.
*   The originality is moderate; the core idea isn't a radical departure from existing work.
*   Implementation details are glossed over, particularly regarding the Stiefel manifold constraint.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards rejection. While the paper presents a valid and technically sound approach, the experimental results do not justify a strong endorsement. The claimed advantages are not sufficiently demonstrated, and the overall contribution feels incremental. The paper would benefit from more extensive experimentation on a wider range of datasets and a more thorough analysis of the computational trade-offs between EM and K-Ascent. The authors should address the concerns regarding the implementation details and provide a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of their approach.
