## Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix *L* in Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) from data. Existing methods either partially learn *L* (e.g., learning scalar weights) or rely on strong assumptions about its form (e.g., parametric forms or ensembles of kernels). The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning *L* directly from observed subsets, leveraging the eigendecomposition of *L* and avoiding the problematic projection steps inherent in naive gradient ascent approaches. They demonstrate through both theoretical analysis (asymptotic runtime) and empirical evaluation (product recommendation) that their EM algorithm outperforms a gradient ascent baseline, particularly when negative interactions between items are significant.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the existing landscape of DPP learning. They acknowledge prior efforts focusing on learning partial *L* (Kulesza & Taskar, UAI 2012), kernel ensembles (Affandi et al., ICML 2014), and Bayesian approaches (Shah & Ghahramani, UAI 2015), and clearly articulate how their method differs – namely, by avoiding restrictive assumptions on *L* and providing a more robust optimization procedure. They appropriately cite relevant works, demonstrating awareness of the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The EM formulation leveraging eigendecomposition is a genuinely novel approach to DPP learning. It sidesteps the difficulties of directly optimizing the likelihood of *L* due to its positive semi-definiteness constraint.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides theoretical analysis demonstrating the asymptotic runtime advantage of the EM algorithm over gradient ascent. This is a significant benefit for large-scale datasets.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on baby registry data convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the EM algorithm. The comparison to gradient ascent (KA) highlights the robustness of EM, particularly in scenarios with strong negative item interactions. The analysis of the runtime ratio further supports the theoretical claims.
* **Clarity & Thoroughness:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The derivations are clear and the explanations of the EM steps are sufficiently detailed. The inclusion of the appendix and availability of code is commendable.
* **Addressing a Core Challenge:** Learning DPPs is fundamentally difficult, and this work provides a substantial contribution toward solving this problem. The ability to learn a non-parametric *L* is a significant step forward.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While theoretically sound, the EM algorithm appears relatively complex to implement. The need for efficient marginalization using k-DPPs and the Stiefel manifold update for eigenvectors adds to the implementation burden. More detailed discussion on the practical challenges of implementation would be beneficial.
* **Sensitivity to Initialization:** While the EM algorithm is shown to be more robust than gradient ascent, the experiments highlight the importance of initialization. The Wishart initialization, while reasonable, might not always be ideal. Exploring and discussing the sensitivity to different initialization strategies in more detail could be valuable.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is focused on a single application (product recommendation). While the results are compelling, demonstrating the effectiveness of the algorithm on other tasks could strengthen the claims.
* **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art DPP learning:** The comparison is mostly to gradient ascent (KA). There are more recent or alternative methods that should be compared to, for example learning DPPs with neural networks, even if they are not directly comparable.
* **Discussion of k-DPPs is brief:** The paper mentions k-DPPs, but a more detailed explanation of their properties and their role in the EM algorithm would be helpful, particularly for readers unfamiliar with this concept.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamentally important problem in the field of DPPs.
* The proposed EM algorithm is a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness and robustness of the algorithm.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.
* The code availability fosters reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The implementation complexity might limit the widespread adoption of the algorithm.
* The empirical evaluation is currently limited to a single application.
* The sensitivity to initialization requires careful consideration.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of DPP learning. The proposed EM algorithm offers a promising solution to a challenging problem, and the theoretical and empirical results are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper with the following suggestions:

*  Expand the discussion on the practical challenges of implementing the EM algorithm.
*  Explore and analyze the sensitivity to different initialization strategies in more detail.
*  Consider including a brief discussion on the limitations of the current experiments and potential directions for future work.
*   Consider comparison to a wider range of DPP learning techniques.
*   Expand the explanation of k-DPPs and their relevance to the proposed algorithm.
