## Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix *L* for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) from data, avoiding the limitations of previous approaches that either partially learn *L* or rely on strong parametric assumptions. The authors propose an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that leverages the eigendecomposition of *L* to avoid the problematic projection steps inherent in naive gradient ascent methods, which can lead to degenerate solutions (near-diagonal *L* matrices hindering diversity modeling). The paper provides theoretical justification and experimental validation on a product recommendation task, demonstrating both improved performance and computational efficiency compared to gradient ascent.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Core Problem:** Learning DPPs is a fundamental challenge with significant practical applications, and the paper directly tackles a known difficulty (NP-hardness of full *L* learning).
* **Novel Approach:** The EM formulation, utilizing the eigendecomposition and avoiding the problematic projection step of gradient ascent, is a genuinely novel and clever idea.  The connection to hidden variables (the index set J) provides a sound theoretical basis for the algorithm.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the EM update equations appears rigorous, and the explanation of the computational complexity is well-defined. The discussion of the asymptotic runtime advantage over gradient ascent is compelling.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the baby registry dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed EM algorithm compared to the gradient ascent baseline. The analysis of the dataset characteristics (parameter *d*) relating to the performance gains is insightful.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the background, methodology, and experimental setup. The use of equations is appropriate and aids understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Baselines:** While the comparison to gradient ascent (K-Ascent) is valuable, comparing against other recent DPP learning methods (e.g., those focusing on learning low-rank approximations of *L*) would strengthen the evaluation.  The paper acknowledges limitations of prior work in the introduction but doesn’t directly benchmark against them.
* **Initialization Sensitivity:** The paper acknowledges the importance of initialization but doesn't delve deeply into a comparative analysis of different initialization strategies beyond the two explored. A more thorough investigation into initialization sensitivity, perhaps with a wider range of initializations, would be beneficial.
* **Complexity of Implementation:** Implementing the EM algorithm, especially the Stiefel manifold update, appears complex and could be a barrier to adoption. While the theoretical complexity analysis is provided, a practical discussion of the implementation challenges would be useful.
* **Dataset Specificity:**  The results are presented on a single dataset (baby registries). While insightful, demonstrating performance on other datasets with different characteristics would increase confidence in the generalizability of the approach.
* **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper doesn't include a detailed error analysis, explaining *why* the EM algorithm outperforms gradient ascent in certain scenarios. Specifically, detailing the impact of the projection step in the K-Ascent algorithm and how the EM algorithm avoids these pitfalls would be helpful.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis and experimental results support the claims made. The approach appears complete, and the authors acknowledge limitations and potential areas for future work.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are reasonably accessible.
* **Originality:** The EM formulation for DPP learning and the exploitation of the eigendecomposition are original contributions. The paper builds upon existing DPP theory but presents a novel approach to learning.
* **Significance:** The results are important, as they address a fundamental challenge in DPP modeling. The improved performance and computational efficiency compared to gradient ascent make this work potentially valuable to researchers and practitioners in areas like recommendation systems, document summarization, and feature selection.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to a significant problem.
* Demonstrable improvement over a baseline algorithm.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Potential for impact in various application areas.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Limited comparison against alternative DPP learning methods.
* Reliance on a single dataset.
* Implementation complexity might hinder adoption.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of DPP learning. The proposed EM algorithm offers a promising alternative to existing methods, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness. Addressing the weaknesses related to baseline comparisons and dataset diversity would further strengthen the paper. I believe this work is a good fit for NIPS and will be of interest to the broader machine learning community.
