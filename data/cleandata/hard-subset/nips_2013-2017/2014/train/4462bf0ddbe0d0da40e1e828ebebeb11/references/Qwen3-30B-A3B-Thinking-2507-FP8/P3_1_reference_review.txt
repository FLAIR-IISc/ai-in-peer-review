

# Peer Review: "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

## Summary and Context
This paper addresses a key challenge in learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the difficulty of maximizing likelihood without restrictive assumptions on the kernel matrix K. While prior work has explored partial learning (e.g., [2, 3]) or parametric assumptions (e.g., [8]), this paper proposes an EM algorithm that avoids the problematic projection steps required by gradient ascent methods, which often produce degenerate (nearly diagonal) kernels that fail to capture diversity. The authors leverage the eigendecomposition of K to develop a principled EM approach where the hidden variable J (representing which eigenvectors are used in the mixture representation of DPPs) enables efficient updates.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound method with clear theoretical foundations. The derivation of the EM algorithm is thorough, with appropriate use of Jensen's inequality to lower-bound the likelihood and careful handling of the non-convex eigenvector constraint via Stiefel manifold optimization. The runtime analysis correctly identifies the O(T₁nNk² + T₁T₂N³) complexity, which is indeed asymptotically faster than gradient ascent's O(T₁nN³ + T₁T₂N³) for k << N.

However, the experimental validation has limitations. While the baby registry dataset demonstrates clear advantages of the EM method (especially when off-diagonal components are important), the runtime comparison (2.1x faster on average) doesn't strongly support the "asymptotically faster" claim given the moderate N (~70) in their experiments. The paper would benefit from larger-scale experiments or a more rigorous asymptotic runtime analysis.

The theoretical analysis of the projection problem (Section 3.1) is insightful, correctly identifying how gradient ascent gets stuck in diagonal solutions. The connection between the projection step and the failure to model negative interactions is particularly compelling.

## Originality
The paper makes a novel contribution by developing an EM algorithm for unconstrained DPP learning that avoids problematic projection steps. While EM has been used for other probabilistic models, applying it to DPP learning in this specific way is original. The key insight—using the eigendecomposition to transform the learning problem into one with a tractable hidden variable—represents a significant conceptual advance over prior approaches.

The paper appropriately situates itself against related work (e.g., [2, 3, 8]), but could better engage with more recent DPP learning approaches like those in [14, 15, 20] that also address kernel learning. The claim that "the final distribution is no longer a DPP" in [3]'s ensemble approach is accurate, but the paper could more explicitly contrast this with their own approach that maintains the DPP structure.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial for applications requiring diversity modeling, such as recommendation systems, document summarization, and sensor placement. The baby registry experiment demonstrates practical impact, showing that EM can better capture negative interactions between items (e.g., avoiding three different video monitors in a registry). The method's robustness to initialization and dataset changes is particularly valuable in real-world applications.

However, the paper doesn't fully explore how the method scales to the large N settings where DPPs are most commonly applied (e.g., N=10,000+). While the runtime complexity suggests scalability, the paper lacks experiments on larger datasets that would better validate this claim.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to method to experiments. The mathematical derivations are presented with appropriate rigor, though some details (like the derivation of Equation 20) are relegated to the supplement. The experimental setup is well-described, though the paper could better explain why certain initialization methods (Wishart vs. moment matching) were chosen.

## Critical Weaknesses
1. **Limited scalability evidence:** The runtime advantage is demonstrated only on moderate-sized datasets (N≈70), not the large-scale settings where DPPs are typically deployed. A more convincing argument for asymptotic speedup would require larger experiments.

2. **Incomplete initialization analysis:** While the paper compares Wishart and moment-matching initializations, it doesn't thoroughly explore how these choices affect convergence behavior or final performance beyond the log-likelihood comparison.

3. **Overstated "asymptotic" advantage:** The paper claims "asymptotically faster" without providing evidence that the O(Nk²) term dominates in practice for relevant N and k values.

4. **Lack of comparison with recent methods:** The related work section misses opportunities to compare with more recent DPP learning approaches that might address similar challenges.

## Strengths
- Clear identification of the projection problem in gradient ascent methods
- Elegant EM formulation that avoids problematic projections
- Thorough theoretical derivation of the algorithm
- Strong experimental results demonstrating improved diversity modeling
- Practical application with real-world dataset

## Recommendation
This paper makes a solid contribution to DPP learning with a theoretically sound and practically effective algorithm. While the scalability claims could be better supported, the method is well-motivated, rigorously derived, and demonstrated to outperform existing approaches on a relevant application. The paper advances the state of the art in DPP learning by providing a principled method for unconstrained kernel learning that avoids the pitfalls of gradient-based approaches.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper is technically sound, makes a meaningful contribution to the field, and is well-suited for NIPS. The minor weaknesses (particularly regarding scalability evidence) don't detract from the paper's overall quality and significance. The method has clear potential for impact in diverse applications requiring diversity modeling.
