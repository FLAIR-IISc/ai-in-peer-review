Summary
The paper tackles maximum likelihood learning of general (nonparametric) determinantal point processes (DPPs) by exploiting the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K. The key idea is to introduce a latent subset J of eigencomponents, leveraging the well-known mixture view of a DPP as a mixture over elementary DPPs, and derive a Jensen lower bound that yields an EM-style algorithm. In the E-step, the posterior q(J|Y) becomes a k-DPP with a closed-form kernel; in the M-step, eigenvalues admit a simple update via k-DPP marginals, and eigenvectors are updated via a gradient step constrained to the Stiefel manifold. The approach avoids the PSD/eigenvalue projection that plagues naive projected gradient ascent on K. Experiments on a large-scale baby registry dataset show gains in test log-likelihood and runtime over the baseline, particularly when off-diagonal structure (negative interactions) is important.

Relation to prior work
Prior learning strategies either (i) adjust only per-item quality weights or mixtures of fixed kernels, or (ii) impose parametric forms and use Bayesian/posterior inference (e.g., Affandi et al.), thereby sacrificing full nonparametric flexibility. This paper differs by attempting to learn the full K without restrictive parameterization, with updates operating in the eigenspace. The use of the mixture/spectral representation and k-DPPs is aligned with classical DPP sampling and marginalization results, but using them to construct an EM algorithm for learning the eigenstructure appears novel. The paper also contrasts with works on MAP inference, Nyström approximations, and structured DPPs, which address different problems.

Technical quality
- Formulation: The decomposition p(Y) = sum_J p(Y|J)p(J) with p(J) factorized over eigenvalues and p(Y|J) an elementary DPP is correct and standard; using q(J|Y) and Jensen’s inequality is a sound variational EM construction.
- E-step: Showing q(J|Y) is a k-DPP with kernel Q_Y is both elegant and computationally enabling. The derivation appears consistent with known k-DPP identities; however, the proof is deferred to the supplement.
- M-step eigenvalues: The closed-form update λ_j = (1/n) sum_i q(j∈J|Y_i), together with efficient computation via k-DPP marginals, is a strong contribution. The numerical caveat near λ→1 and the practical clipping are reasonable.
- M-step eigenvectors: The gradient expression valid when q=p (Eq. 20) and the use of a single Stiefel-constrained step per EM iteration constitute a generalized EM. Line search against the true likelihood L (not the bound) is pragmatic; monotonicity is empirically ensured by accepting only improving steps, but a formal monotonicity guarantee is not established.
- Complexity: The stated O(T1 n N k^2 + T1 T2 N^3) runtime is plausible: computing k-DPP marginals and the V-gradient scale with n N k^2, while Stiefel manifold updates/eigendecompositions cost O(N^3) per line-search step. One point needing clarification is the cost to evaluate L during line search: using P(Y)=det(K_Y) det(I-K), likelihood evaluation can be O(n k^3) + O(N^3) to update det(I-K), but this is not explicit.

Empirical evaluation
- Dataset and setup are well described; using two initializations (Wishart and moment-matching) is appropriate and probes sensitivity to starting points.
- Results show consistent improvements in test log-likelihood and runtime, especially in settings with strong negative interactions (measured via d). The analysis connecting gains to off-diagonal signal is insightful.
- The comparison baseline (projected gradient on K) is reasonable, but additional baselines (e.g., parametric/Bayesian DPP learning, low-rank DPP learning) would further strengthen the case.
- Beyond likelihood, practical recommendation metrics (diversity/coverage, user-relevance proxies) would be useful to corroborate that learned negative interactions translate to better selections.
- Runtime comparisons are favorable to EM; a scaling study in N, n, and k, or experiments on larger N with approximation (Nyström) would add depth.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with helpful algorithm boxes and complexity discussions. A few points could be improved:
- Likelihood formulas: reconcile Eq. (3) with the standard P(Y)=det(K_Y) det(I−K) and explicitly derive computational shortcuts for evaluating L during line search. The definition of I_Y (zeros on Y or on Y^c) should be clarified to avoid confusion.
- State explicitly that the algorithm is a generalized EM (single constrained gradient step for V) and discuss monotonicity and convergence properties.
- Provide more implementation detail: how V is updated (matrix exponential approximation), step-size schedule, stopping criteria, and how λ clipping is handled numerically.

Originality
The EM construction over the spectral mixture and the k-DPP E-step leading to closed-form eigenvalue updates is, to my knowledge, novel and nontrivial. The algorithmic insight to avoid projections that induce degeneracy in K is valuable. While built on established DPP spectral theory, the learning algorithm itself is an original and practical contribution.

Significance
Learning full, nonparametric DPP kernels is important for applications where pairwise repulsion matters. The proposed method is likely to be adopted by practitioners given its favorable empirical behavior and computational profile. It also opens avenues to incorporate regularization, structure, or approximations within a principled EM framework.

Pros
- Elegant EM derivation leveraging k-DPPs; closed-form eigenvalue updates.
- Avoids projection pitfalls; more robust to initialization than projected gradient.
- Solid runtime profile; empirical gains in likelihood and speed.
- Clear connection between gains and off-diagonal structure.

Cons / Questions
- Limited baselines; no comparison to parametric/Bayesian DPP learning.
- Generalized EM lacks formal monotonicity/convergence guarantees; discuss conditions.
- Clarify likelihood computation cost and Eq. (3) vs standard formula.
- Sensitivity to λ near 1 and to k (size of subsets) deserves more analysis.
- No regularization to prevent overfitting (e.g., spectral shrinkage or sparsity in V).

Suggestions
- Add regularization (e.g., penalty on large λ, orthogonality-preserving sparsity for V), and report effects.
- Provide a brief proof sketch or intuitive derivation of Lemma 1 in the main text.
- Include additional datasets and baselines; report recommendation-oriented metrics.
- Explore low-rank variants or Nyström approximations to scale to large N.

Recommendation
A strong accept leaning. The paper presents a technically sound, original, and practically useful method for DPP learning, with clear advantages over a common baseline and well-grounded theory. Addressing the clarity points and adding a bit more empirical breadth would further strengthen the contribution.
