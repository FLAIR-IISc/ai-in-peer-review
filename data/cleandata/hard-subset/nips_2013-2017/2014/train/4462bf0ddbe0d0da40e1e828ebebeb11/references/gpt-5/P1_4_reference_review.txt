Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles maximum-likelihood learning of unconstrained determinantal point process (DPP) kernels without restrictive parameterizations. Prior work often (i) learns only per-item quality weights while fixing similarities (convex but limited), (ii) learns mixtures/weights over fixed kernels (loses DPP structure and can be memory-heavy), or (iii) assumes parametric forms with Bayesian inference (less flexible). The authors propose to learn the marginal kernel K directly by exploiting its eigendecomposition K=VΛV^T. They introduce a hidden variable J indexing subsets of eigenvectors, using the standard representation of a DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs. This yields a generalized EM approach:

- E-step: set q(J|Y)=pK(J|Y); the authors show q is a k-DPP with kernel QY=R ZY R where R=diag(sqrt(λ/(1−λ))) and ZY depends on V restricted to Y. This allows efficient marginals via k-DPP identities.

- M-step: closed-form eigenvalue updates λj = (1/n) Σi q(j∈J|Yi), computable in O(n N k^2) using k-DPP marginalization. For eigenvectors, they take a single Stiefel-manifold gradient step with a skew-symmetric update V ← V exp[η(V^T G − G^T V)] and choose η by line search, recomputing the E-step as needed.

They compare against naïve projected gradient ascent on K (KA), which requires projection to PSD and eigenvalues ≤ 1 and can collapse to near-diagonal solutions. On an Amazon baby registry dataset (13 categories, average N≈71, n≈8.6k), EM improves median test log-likelihoods by 3.7% on Wishart initializations (larger gains in categories with stronger negative interactions), is comparable with a moment-matching initializer, and is substantially better in low-data (n=2N) settings. EM is about 2.1× faster than KA empirically, consistent with asymptotic costs O(T1 n N k^2 + T1 T2 N^3) vs O(T1 n N^3 + T1 T2 N^3).

Strengths
- Clear exploitation of the DPP spectral mixture structure to derive a principled generalized EM with efficient E-step and closed-form eigenvalue updates.
- Good diagnosis of KA’s projection pathology and why it leads to near-diagonal degeneracy.
- Solid computational analysis; the per-iteration cost scales with average set size k rather than N for large parts of the M-step, which is important in many applications.
- Empirical evidence on real data with multiple categories; EM is both more robust to initialization and faster than KA. Nice qualitative example showing diversity benefits.
- Code and data availability is a plus.

Weaknesses and questions
- Limited baselines: comparison is only to KA. It would strengthen the case to include (i) per-item quality-only learning (convex approach), (ii) mixtures/ensembles of fixed kernels, and (iii) parametric/Bayesian DPP learning (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML 2014) to show advantages beyond KA.
- Scope of evaluation: only one domain (baby registry). Demonstrations on typical DPP tasks (document summarization, image search diversification, sensor placement) would broaden significance.
- Generalized EM details need tightening. Algorithm 2 mixes checks on the true likelihood L with computing δ from F, yet F is not directly evaluated in the line search; the acceptance rule uses L while δ is defined as F(V′,λ′)−F(V,λ). Please clarify consistency and whether you guarantee monotone increase in L (you appear to accept steps only if L increases, which is fine).
- Line search updates appear inconsistent: both Algorithms 1 and 2 show η ← η^2 inside the backtracking loop starting at η=1; this would not reduce step size. Likely a typo for η ← η/2 (or similar). Please correct.
- The Stiefel update uses a matrix exponential, incurring O(N^3), which may be heavy for large N. Modern manifold optimization often uses cheaper retractions (QR-based, Cayley transform) and first-order methods; consider these to reduce the T2 N^3 term.
- Numerical stability: when λj≈1, R=diag(sqrt(λ/(1−λ))) can blow up. You mention tightening the constraint; it would be helpful to spell out a principled strategy (e.g., barrier/penalty, soft clipping) and report its effect.
- Scalability limits: the approach still requires full N×N eigendecompositions and matrix exponentials. Discussion or experiments with low-rank approximations (e.g., Nyström) or structured kernels would be valuable.
- Theoretical guarantees: beyond monotonicity via line search on L, are there any convergence guarantees for the generalized EM with single V-step? Even a brief discussion would help.
- Minor clarity/typos: “negelecting” → “neglecting”; some references lack details; stray “Tuesday, August 5, 14” in the figure caption.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with correct use of DPP spectral identities and k-DPP marginalization to obtain efficient updates. The eigenvector gradient derivation is nontrivial; providing more detail (even in supplement) is appropriate. The generalized EM nature is honest; however, the algorithmic description has a few inconsistencies (η updates, F vs L) that should be fixed. Experiments support claims but could be broader.

Clarity
Generally well organized and clearly written, with good intuition (e.g., why KA degenerates). The EM construction is easy to follow. Algorithmic pseudocode needs the noted corrections. Adding a brief recipe for computing q(j∈J|Y) and an outline of how log-likelihood is evaluated efficiently would help reproducibility.

Originality
Using the hidden-variable mixture over eigenvectors to derive an EM-style algorithm for unconstrained DPP learning is, to my knowledge, novel and elegant. Prior work either restricted parameterization or used different learning paradigms; this work fills a gap.

Significance
Learning full, nonparametric DPP kernels robustly is important for many subset selection tasks. The method advances the state of the art beyond standard projected gradients by improving robustness and speed, and it may spur further work on scalable spectral learning for DPPs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix line-search update rules and harmonize the use of F vs L in Algorithm 2; state the monotonicity property clearly.
- Add baselines beyond KA and report results on an additional domain.
- Consider cheaper Stiefel manifold retractions to reduce N^3 overhead; discuss low-rank/structured variants for large N.
- Provide more details on numerical handling when λ→1 and on efficient computation of L(K).
- Expand the supplement with the full derivation of Eq. (20) and the complexity of evaluating L.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and principled EM formulation for unconstrained DPP learning.
- Solid algorithmic contributions with practical efficiency gains.
- Clear empirical improvements in robustness and runtime; code/data provided.
- Addresses an important problem with broad applicability.

Arguments against acceptance
- Narrow experimental scope and limited baselines.
- Some algorithmic description issues (typos/inconsistencies) need fixing.
- Scalability to large N remains a concern without low-rank/structured extensions.

Recommendation: Weak accept
Confidence: High
