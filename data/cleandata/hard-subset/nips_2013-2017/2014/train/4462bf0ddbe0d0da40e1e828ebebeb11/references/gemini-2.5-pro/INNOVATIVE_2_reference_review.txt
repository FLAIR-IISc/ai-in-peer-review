### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from observed subset data. The authors correctly point out that maximum likelihood estimation for an unconstrained DPP kernel is conjectured to be NP-hard, and that prior work has circumvented this by learning restricted or parametric forms of the kernel. The most direct approach, projected gradient ascent on the kernel matrix, is identified as a key baseline. However, the authors convincingly argue that the projection step required to maintain positive semi-definiteness and constrain eigenvalues can lead to degenerate, near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture the crucial diversity-modeling aspect of DPPs.

The core contribution of this work is a novel and elegant Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for this learning problem. The key insight is to re-frame the problem by working with the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix. Leveraging the known interpretation of a DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs, the authors treat the selection of eigenvectors as a latent variable. This enables the EM framework:
-   The E-step involves computing the posterior over the latent eigenvector selections given an observed subset. The authors provide a key theoretical result (Lemma 1) showing that this posterior is itself a tractable k-DPP.
-   The M-step involves updating the kernel's eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The eigenvalue update has a convenient closed-form solution. The eigenvector update is more complex and is handled by taking a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, a sophisticated approach that avoids the problematic projection of the baseline method.

Experiments on a real-world baby registry dataset demonstrate that the proposed EM algorithm is more robust and computationally faster than projected gradient ascent, particularly when starting from an uninformative initialization or in data-poor settings.

### Strengths
1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The central idea of formulating DPP learning as an EM algorithm by treating the eigenvector selection as a latent variable is highly novel and insightful. This represents a significant conceptual advance over previous work, which either learned restricted models or relied on standard gradient-based methods with known flaws. Instead of an incremental improvement, this paper introduces a fundamentally new, principled approach to a core problem in the area. This is precisely the kind of bold, creative work that can spur further research.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Elegance:** The technical development is impressive. The identification of the posterior in the E-step as a k-DPP is a non-trivial and elegant result that makes the algorithm tractable. Furthermore, the use of optimization on the Stiefel manifold to update the eigenvectors is the correct and modern way to handle the orthogonality constraint, cleverly sidestepping the projection issues that cripple the naive baseline. This demonstrates a strong command of both the probabilistic model and the relevant optimization machinery.

3.  **Clear Practical Value and Strong Empirical Support:** The paper provides a compelling demonstration of the proposed algorithm's practical advantages. The authors don't just show that their method works; they provide a clear diagnosis of *why* the baseline fails (the projection step collapsing the matrix to be near-diagonal) and show that their method rectifies this specific failure. The experiments are well-designed, testing different initializations and data regimes, and the results clearly show that the EM approach is more robust, especially in challenging settings where diversity is a key factor. The reported speedup is an additional, significant practical benefit.

### Weaknesses
While this is a strong paper, there are a few areas that could be improved:

1.  **Partial M-Step Optimization:** The M-step for the eigenvectors `V` is not a full maximization but rather a single gradient step. The paper notes that the derived gradient expression (Eq. 20) is only exact for the initial step. While this pragmatic approach appears to work well, the theoretical justification is incomplete. This is a minor methodological weakness, but it does not undermine the novelty or practical utility of the overall framework. A more detailed discussion of this approximation would be welcome.

2.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is convincing but is performed on a single type of dataset (product recommendation). While this is a well-suited application, demonstrating the algorithm's performance on other classic DPP tasks like document summarization or sensor placement would strengthen the claims of general applicability and provide a more comprehensive picture of its strengths and weaknesses.

### Recommendation
I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

The core contribution—a novel EM algorithm for learning unconstrained DPP kernels—is a significant and elegant conceptual leap. It addresses a fundamental problem with a new perspective that is both theoretically sound and practically effective. The paper successfully identifies a critical flaw in the most obvious baseline approach and presents a method that directly resolves it. While the M-step involves an approximation and the experiments could be broader, these are minor points in the context of the paper's outstanding novelty and importance. This work provides a valuable new tool for practitioners and is likely to inspire follow-up research on learning probabilistic models of diversity. It is an excellent example of the kind of innovative and impactful research that NIPS should showcase.
