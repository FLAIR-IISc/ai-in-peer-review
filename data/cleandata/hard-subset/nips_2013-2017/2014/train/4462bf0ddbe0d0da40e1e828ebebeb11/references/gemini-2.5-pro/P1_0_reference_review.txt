### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. While DPPs are powerful models for capturing the balance between item quality and diversity, learning the full kernel matrix via maximum likelihood is conjectured to be NP-hard. Previous approaches have either learned restricted versions of the kernel (e.g., only diagonal "quality" terms), relied on restrictive parametric forms, or combined fixed kernels, thereby losing the desirable properties of a single DPP.

The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to tackle this learning problem. The key insight is to reframe the problem by working with the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K. They leverage the generative interpretation of a DPP, where a sampled subset Y is generated via a latent intermediate subset J that selects a specific set of eigenvectors. This latent variable J is naturally incorporated into an EM framework.

The proposed algorithm alternates between an E-step, which computes the posterior distribution over the latent variable J given an observed subset Y, and an M-step, which updates the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of K. The M-step for the eigenvalues has a convenient closed-form solution. The M-step for the eigenvectors, which is a constrained optimization problem on a Stiefel manifold, is handled elegantly using a multiplicative update rule based on the matrix exponential, which avoids the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent. The authors compare their EM algorithm to a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) on a real-world product recommendation task using baby registry data, demonstrating that their method is more robust, less prone to finding poor local optima (especially near-diagonal solutions), and asymptotically faster.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a highly novel and elegant approach to a fundamental, difficult problem in the DPP literature. Learning an unconstrained DPP kernel is a long-standing challenge. The idea of using the generative interpretation of DPP sampling to introduce a latent variable and formulate an EM algorithm is a significant conceptual contribution. It moves beyond the limitations of prior work and provides a principled method for full kernel learning.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical development is solid. The derivation of the EM algorithm, from introducing the latent variable J and applying Jensen's inequality to the specific update steps, is well-executed.
    *   The closed-form update for the eigenvalues (Eq. 18) is a major advantage, as it sidesteps the projection that plagues the baseline method.
    *   The handling of the eigenvector update is particularly strong. Recognizing the `V^T V = I` constraint as optimization on a Stiefel manifold and applying a principled update rule (Eq. 21) is much more sophisticated than a simple projection and is crucial to the algorithm's success.
    *   The identification of the posterior `q(J | Yi)` as a k-DPP is a key insight that makes the E-step tractable.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors first clearly motivate the problem and lucidly explain the shortcomings of the straightforward projected gradient ascent approach. They then build their EM algorithm step-by-step, making the logic transparent. The distinction between the L-kernel and K-kernel, and the choice to work with K, is well-justified.

4.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the proposed method's superiority over the baseline.
    *   The choice of a real-world baby registry dataset is excellent, as product recommendation is a primary application for DPPs where diversity is critical.
    *   The analysis under different initializations (random Wishart vs. data-driven moments-matching) and data regimes (data-poor setting) is thorough and convincingly demonstrates the robustness of the EM algorithm. The failure of K-Ascent when starting from a poor initialization is a key result.
    *   The qualitative example in Figure 2a (three baby monitors vs. a more diverse set) is a powerful and intuitive illustration of why the EM algorithm's better modeling of diversity matters in practice.
    *   The runtime comparison confirms the theoretical asymptotic advantage of the EM method.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Approximation in the V Update:** The paper notes that the gradient for the eigenvectors in Eq. (20) is only exact at the beginning of the M-step (when `q = p`). They then state they take a single gradient step on V. This implies the M-step for V is not fully optimized, making the algorithm a form of Generalized EM (GEM). While this is a common and often effective practice, the paper could be slightly more explicit about this and offer a brief justification for why this single-step approximation is sufficient. Does it guarantee ascent on the true likelihood `L` or only the lower bound `F`? Clarifying this would add to the paper's technical completeness.

2.  **Scalability:** The algorithm's complexity includes an `O(N^3)` term for the eigenvector updates (matrix exponential) and eigendecompositions, which limits its direct applicability to problems with a very large ground set size N. While the method is faster than the baseline, this is an inherent limitation. A brief discussion in the conclusion or future work section about potential avenues for scaling up, such as combining this EM approach with low-rank or Nyström approximations for the kernel, would be a welcome addition.

3.  **Experimental Baseline:** While K-Ascent is a natural and important baseline, a comparison against the Bayesian learning method from Affandi et al. [8] could have provided additional context, even if the goals (MLE vs. posterior inference) are different. This is a minor point, however, as the paper's focus on MLE is clear and the chosen baseline is the most direct competitor.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling and machine learning. It tackles an important and difficult problem with a novel, theoretically sound, and practically effective algorithm. The paper is clearly written, and its claims are convincingly supported by strong empirical results. The weaknesses identified are minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the work. This paper is likely to be highly influential and will enable wider and more effective use of DPPs in various applications. It is a clear accept for NIPS.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and elegant EM algorithm for the difficult problem of learning a full DPP kernel.
*   The method is technically sound, employing principled optimization techniques (e.g., Stiefel manifold updates) that avoid the pitfalls of naive approaches.
*   Demonstrates superior robustness and speed over a strong baseline on a realistic, compelling application.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The justification for the single-step eigenvector update in the M-step could be more rigorous.
*   Scalability to very large `N` remains a challenge, a common issue for full-kernel methods.
