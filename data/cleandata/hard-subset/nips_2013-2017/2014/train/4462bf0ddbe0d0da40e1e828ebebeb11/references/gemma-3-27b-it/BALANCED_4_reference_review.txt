This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing the challenges of directly maximizing the likelihood function which is conjectured to be NP-hard. The core idea is to exploit the eigendecomposition of the DPP kernel matrix, circumventing the need for problematic projection steps required by naive gradient ascent methods. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on a baby registry dataset, showcasing improved performance and computational efficiency compared to a projected gradient ascent baseline.

The paper builds upon a well-established line of research on DPPs for subset selection, referencing key works like Kulesza & Taskar (UAI 2012, NIPS 2012, FTML 2014) and Affandi et al. (ICML 2016, NIPS 2017). It correctly identifies limitations in existing learning methods, such as the partial learning approaches of [2] and [3], and the parametric assumptions of [8]. The proposed EM algorithm is a significant step towards learning fully unconstrained DPP kernels, a long-standing challenge in the field. The connection to the eigendecomposition and its utilization for algorithm design is a particularly strong aspect of the work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the EM algorithm appears mathematically sound, leveraging established results on DPPs and matrix decompositions. The justification for avoiding the projection step in gradient ascent is clear and well-motivated.
*   **Novelty:** The EM approach presented is a novel contribution, offering a different perspective on learning DPPs compared to existing methods. The decomposition into eigenvalues and eigenvectors is cleverly exploited.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on the baby registry dataset provide convincing evidence that the proposed EM algorithm outperforms the K-Ascent baseline, particularly when dealing with datasets exhibiting strong item interactions or limited training data.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and algorithmic steps. The notation is consistent and the mathematical derivations are reasonably accessible.
*   **Significance:** The ability to efficiently learn DPPs without restrictive assumptions has the potential to impact various applications, including recommendation systems, document summarization, and feature selection.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Derivation:** While the overall idea is clear, the detailed mathematical derivations, particularly in Section 3.5, can be difficult to follow without significant background knowledge in optimization and matrix calculus. More intermediate steps or explanations could improve readability.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are currently limited to a single dataset (baby registries). While the results are promising, it would be beneficial to evaluate the algorithm's performance on other, diverse datasets to assess its generalizability.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of hyperparameters, such as the step size in the Stiefel manifold update, is not fully explored. A more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would strengthen the experimental results.
*   **Comparison to Other Baselines:** While K-Ascent is a reasonable baseline, comparing to other relevant approaches, such as Bayesian methods [8] or the L-ensemble method [3], could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed algorithm.
*   **Computational Cost Discussion:** The discussion of computational complexity is somewhat superficial. A more detailed analysis of the practical runtime bottlenecks and scalability limitations would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound algorithm for learning DPPs.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a clear improvement over a relevant baseline.
*   The work addresses a significant challenge in the field of DPPs and has the potential to impact various applications.
*   The authors clearly identify the limitations of existing approaches and provide a well-motivated solution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The mathematical derivations are complex and may be difficult for some readers to follow.
*   The experiments are limited to a single dataset.
*   The hyperparameter sensitivity analysis is incomplete.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of DPPs. While there are some areas for improvement, the novelty, technical soundness, and experimental validation justify acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper to NIPS, with a request for the authors to consider addressing the weaknesses identified above in a revised version. Specifically, I suggest adding more intermediate steps to the mathematical derivations, expanding the experimental evaluation to include other datasets, performing a more thorough hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, and providing a more detailed discussion of the algorithm's computational complexity.
