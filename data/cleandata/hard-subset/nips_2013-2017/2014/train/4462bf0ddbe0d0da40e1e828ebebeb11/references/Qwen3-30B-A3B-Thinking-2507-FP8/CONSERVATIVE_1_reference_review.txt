

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper proposes an EM algorithm for learning the marginal kernel K of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), arguing that it overcomes limitations of existing gradient ascent approaches that require problematic projection steps. The authors claim their method is more robust to initialization, asymptotically faster, and avoids the degenerate results common in gradient-based methods.

## Strengths
The paper is well-written with clear mathematical derivations, particularly regarding the EM steps and the relationship between the eigendecomposition and DPP properties. The theoretical development of the EM algorithm is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of how the eigendecomposition enables their approach to avoid projection steps. The clarity of the mathematical presentation is commendable, and the paper appropriately references foundational DPP work.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Experimental Validation:** The most significant weakness is the limited scope of experimental evaluation. The authors only test on a single dataset (baby registries from Amazon) across 13 categories, with only one baseline (projected gradient ascent). While the paper claims "asymptotic speedup," the empirical runtime comparison (2.1x faster) is modest and doesn't provide strong justification for adopting a more complex algorithm. The claimed 3.7% average test log-likelihood improvement (Figure 1a) is modest in practical terms, and the improvements vanish when using the moment-matching initialization (0.4% average gain). This suggests the method's advantages are highly dependent on initialization rather than being universally superior.

**Insufficient Comparison to Prior Work:** The paper cites existing work on DPP learning (e.g., [2], [3], [8]) but fails to adequately distinguish their approach from these methods. For instance, [8] proposes Bayesian methods for learning DPPs without restrictive parameterizations, which seems conceptually closer to the authors' approach than the gradient ascent baseline they compare to. The paper doesn't clarify how their EM method differs from or improves upon Bayesian approaches.

**Overstated Claims:** The paper claims their method "negates the need for the problematic projection step" and "is more robust to initialization and dataset changes," but Figure 1b shows that with the moment-matching initialization, KA and EM have nearly identical performance. This undermines the claim of robustness. The assertion that "EM is asymptotically faster" is based on theoretical complexity analysis (O(T1nNk²) vs. O(T1nN³)), but the paper doesn't demonstrate that this asymptotic advantage manifests in practice for realistic dataset sizes (N=71, n=8585).

**Lack of Practical Impact Demonstration:** The paper doesn't show how the improved log-likelihood translates to better performance on downstream tasks. For example, the "greedy approximation" in Figure 2a shows a qualitative difference between EM and KA recommendations, but the paper doesn't provide evidence that these differences lead to better user engagement or business metrics in a real recommendation system. The baby registry example is limited in scope and doesn't demonstrate general applicability.

**Theoretical Gaps:** While the EM algorithm is well-derived, the paper doesn't address convergence properties or provide guarantees about the quality of the solution found. The paper claims their method "is more robust to initialization" but doesn't provide any theoretical justification for this claim or analyze how the EM algorithm avoids local minima.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a technically sound but incremental contribution to DPP learning. While the EM approach is theoretically interesting, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate its practical advantages over existing methods. The modest improvements in log-likelihood and runtime, combined with the limited experimental validation, don't justify the adoption of this new method in practice.

The paper would benefit from:
1. More comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple datasets and applications
2. Comparison to Bayesian methods (e.g., [8]) that also learn unconstrained DPPs
3. Demonstration of how improved log-likelihood translates to better downstream performance
4. More thorough analysis of the conditions under which their method outperforms baselines

Given the modest gains demonstrated and the limited scope of validation, I cannot recommend acceptance. The contribution is too incremental to warrant publication at NIPS, especially given the skepticism toward new methods without extensive validation. The paper would be better suited for a more specialized venue focused on probabilistic modeling.

While the authors have made a solid technical contribution to DPP learning, the paper falls short of demonstrating significant advances over existing methods that would justify publication at NIPS. The field would benefit more from papers that demonstrate substantial improvements in practical performance or theoretical understanding rather than incremental algorithmic modifications with limited empirical support.
