

# Peer Review: "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

## Summary

This paper addresses a key challenge in learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) - the difficulty of optimizing the kernel matrix L (or K) without degenerate solutions. The authors identify that gradient ascent methods require problematic projection steps that often produce nearly diagonal kernels, failing to model the crucial negative interactions between items that enable DPPs' diversity properties. The authors propose an EM algorithm that leverages the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix, avoiding these projection steps and providing a theoretically grounded approach. The work builds upon prior DPP learning efforts including [2] (scalar weight learning), [3] (L-ensembles), and [8] (Bayesian methods), while avoiding their limitations of restrictive assumptions or loss of DPP properties.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness (Quality):** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for the proposed EM algorithm. The derivation of E-step and M-step updates is thorough, with clear connections to DPP properties and eigendecomposition. The authors carefully analyze the computational complexity and provide a detailed comparison with gradient ascent methods (K-Ascent). The experimental results on a baby registry dataset convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the EM approach in both log-likelihood (average 3.7% improvement over K-Ascent) and runtime (EM is 2.1x faster on average).

**Originality:** The paper's key insight - using the eigendecomposition to develop an EM algorithm for DPP learning - is novel. While EM has been used for DPP inference, the authors' application to learning the kernel matrix avoids the projection steps that plague gradient ascent methods. The paper clearly articulates how this differs from prior work, particularly addressing the limitation of gradient ascent methods identified in Section 3.1.

**Significance:** DPPs are widely used in applications requiring diversity (document summarization, product recommendation, sensor placement), so an efficient and robust learning algorithm has substantial practical value. The paper demonstrates this through a real-world application where EM better captures negative interactions between items (e.g., avoiding multiple similar products in recommendations), which is critical for the diversity-seeking nature of DPPs.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background on DPPs is concise yet complete, the mathematical derivations are clear, and the experimental setup is thoroughly described. The algorithms are presented in a readable pseudocode format.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments are conducted solely on baby registry data, which is a single application domain. While this is a reasonable starting point, the paper would be strengthened by additional experiments on other DPP applications (e.g., document summarization, sensor placement) to demonstrate broader applicability.

**Comparison with Other Methods:** The paper only compares against gradient ascent (K-Ascent), but there are other relevant learning methods for DPPs that could be considered. For instance, [8] proposed Bayesian methods for learning parametric forms of L, and [1] explored other optimization approaches. A comparison with these methods would better establish the relative advantages of the EM approach.

**Practical Considerations:** While the paper notes the asymptotic runtime advantage (O(nNk²) vs. O(nN³)), it doesn't fully address the practical considerations of eigendecomposition for very large N. The O(N³) cost of eigendecomposition could be prohibitive for extremely large problems, though the paper does acknowledge this in the runtime complexity analysis.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the DPP learning literature. The theoretical development is rigorous, the experimental results are convincing, and the proposed method addresses a genuine limitation in existing approaches. The main weaknesses are the limited scope of experiments and lack of comparison with other learning methods beyond gradient ascent.

The paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The EM algorithm is both theoretically sound and practically advantageous, and the clear presentation makes it accessible to the NIPS community. The work has direct implications for practitioners using DPPs in real-world applications requiring subset diversity.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. This is a high-quality paper that advances the state of the art in DPP learning with a novel, theoretically grounded approach that demonstrates clear empirical advantages. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling for subset selection tasks.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

*Pro:*
- Novel application of EM to DPP learning, avoiding problematic projection steps
- Rigorous theoretical foundation with clear derivation of E and M steps
- Demonstrated improvements in both log-likelihood and runtime on a real-world dataset
- Well-written and well-organized paper with clear mathematical exposition

*Con:*
- Limited to a single dataset and application domain
- Lacks comparison with other learning methods beyond gradient ascent
- Doesn't fully address numerical considerations for extremely large-scale problems

The paper's strengths substantially outweigh these limitations, and the work represents a meaningful contribution to the NIPS community.
