Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper tackles maximum-likelihood learning of determinantal point processes (DPPs) without restrictive parameterizations by exploiting the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K. Building on the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs view, the authors introduce an EM-style algorithm in which a latent set of eigenvector indices J plays the role of a hidden variable. The E-step sets q(J|Y) to the exact posterior, which the authors show is a k-DPP with a computable kernel; the M-step updates eigenvalues λ in closed form via posterior marginals and updates eigenvectors V with a single gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, with a line search that checks improvement in the true likelihood. This avoids the projection-to-PSD step required by naive projected gradient on K (KA), which the authors argue can degenerate to near-diagonal K. The algorithm has per-iteration complexity O(n N k^2) for E and λ-updates plus O(N^3) for the manifold update line search. Experiments on a large baby registry dataset show EM improves test log-likelihood over KA (notably when off-diagonal structure matters, and in data-poor settings) and is faster in practice.

The work situates itself against: (i) convex diagonal scaling/quality-only learning [2]; (ii) mixtures of fixed-L DPPs [3]; and (iii) Bayesian learning with parametric kernels [8]. Unlike these, it aims to learn a full, nonparametric K while retaining DPP tractability.

Strengths:
- Methodological contribution: A principled EM formulation for full DPP learning that uses the eigenstructure and a latent-variable reparameterization. The E-step as a k-DPP and the efficient λ-update via k-DPP marginals are neat and practically useful.
- Algorithmic insight: Careful discussion of why projection-based KA can get stuck near diagonal solutions; the Stiefel manifold update is appropriate for V.
- Empirical evidence: Real-world dataset with clear protocol; improvements in test likelihood (especially when diversity/negative interactions are important) and runtime; qualitative example illustrates the diversity benefit.
- Clarity of core ideas: The derivation of the EM lower bound and the computational complexity trade-offs are well presented. Code and data availability is a plus for reproducibility.

Weaknesses and questions:
Quality
- Convergence guarantees: The M-step for V performs only a single gradient step and the line search monitors L rather than the lower bound F. Algorithm 2 mixes checks on L and reports δ in F; it is unclear whether the procedure guarantees monotone improvement of L or F across iterations. Please clarify convergence properties, and whether monotonicity of L is ensured.
- Gradient for V: Equation (20) is said to be valid “for the first gradient step” when q=p. In EM, each E-step sets q=p for the current parameters; hence Eq. (20) should be valid at the start of each M-step, but not after moving V unless q is recomputed. This is what you do in practice; spelling this out explicitly would help.
- Numerical stability: KA’s derivative requires (K − I_Y)^−1; under what conditions is this invertible, and do you use a pseudoinverse or stabilization? Similarly, Q_Y involves R=diag(sqrt(λ/(1−λ))); how do you robustly handle λ near 1 beyond ad hoc clipping?
- Regularization/overfitting: No explicit regularization on λ or V is discussed. With limited data (e.g., n=2N) a full, unconstrained K could overfit. Consider adding and evaluating trace, nuclear norm, or eigenvalue shrinkage regularizers.

Clarity
- Notation: The switch between V and U=V^>, and definitions of V_Y, U_Y, and B_Y are a bit hard to follow; a compact notational table or clearer definition would help. Minor typos and formatting issues occur.
- Complexity constants: While asymptotics are given, a breakdown of dominant constants (e.g., number of k-DPP eigendecompositions per iteration, cost of matrix exponential in the manifold update) would aid practitioners.

Originality
- The latent-variable EM leveraging the elementary DPP mixture is a natural but, to my knowledge, novel application to learning a full K. Using k-DPP structure in the E-step is a nice idea. The eigenvector update on the Stiefel manifold is standard but apt.

Significance
- Practical impact: The approach can make fully learned DPPs more robust and faster than KA in moderate-sized problems (N up to ~100 in experiments). This is meaningful for applications where diversity modeling is crucial.
- Scope/limitations: The O(N^3) term from manifold updates remains a bottleneck for large N; discussion of scalability (e.g., low-rank K, Nyström approximations, sparsity, block structure, or stochastic/mini-batch EM) would broaden applicability.
- Empirical breadth: Comparisons are limited to KA. It would strengthen the case to include baselines representing (i) diagonal scaling/quality-only learning [2], (ii) mixtures/ensembles [3], and (iii) parametric/Bayesian DPP training [8], at least on subsets of the data. Additional public benchmarks (e.g., summarization or sensor placement) would also help.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled EM formulation with efficient updates leveraging k-DPP structure.
- Good practical insight and remedy for KA degeneracy; appropriate use of Stiefel manifold optimization.
- Real dataset, likelihood-based evaluation, and runtime benefits; qualitative evidence of improved diversity.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited baselines and domains; scalability to larger N not addressed experimentally.
- Convergence/monotonicity and numerical stability details need clarification.
- No explicit regularization or ablations (e.g., effect of manifold step size, number of V updates, initialization sensitivity) reported.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a precise convergence statement: what is guaranteed to increase (L or F), under what conditions, and how line search interacts with the lower bound.
- Add regularization and/or early stopping heuristics, and evaluate their effect, especially in low-data regimes.
- Include additional baselines (quality-only, ensemble/fixed-L mixtures, parametric/Bayesian) and at least one public benchmark.
- Discuss and, if possible, demonstrate scalability strategies (low-rank K, Nyström, sparsity) and approximate/stochastic E-steps.
- Clarify numerical handling of singularities in inverses and λ≈1, and clean up notational inconsistencies.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a solid and practical learning method for DPPs with a clear algorithmic contribution and evidence of robustness and speed. Addressing the above concerns—especially convergence clarity, broader baselines, and scalability—would strengthen the paper further.
