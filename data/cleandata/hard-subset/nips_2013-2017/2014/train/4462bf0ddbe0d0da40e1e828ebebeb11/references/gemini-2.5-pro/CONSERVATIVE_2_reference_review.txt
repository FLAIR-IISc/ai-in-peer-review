**Summary**
This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from observed subset data. The authors correctly note that existing methods are often restrictive (e.g., assuming a parametric form for the kernel) or can suffer from optimization issues. The main contribution is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for this learning task. The key idea is to work with the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix, K = VΛVᵀ, and introduce a latent variable representing the subset of eigenvectors chosen in the DPP's generative process. This formulation allows the authors to derive an EM algorithm that iteratively updates the eigenvalues (Λ) and eigenvectors (V). The authors claim this approach is more robust and computationally faster than a baseline of projected gradient ascent on the kernel matrix K. Experiments on a product recommendation task using baby registry data are presented to support these claims.

**Quality and Significance**
The paper's primary strength lies in its novel formulation. The idea of using the generative view of DPPs to introduce a latent variable and enable an EM-style algorithm is clever and elegant. However, the work's overall quality and significance are undermined by several methodological and experimental weaknesses.

The central claim is that the proposed EM algorithm is superior to projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent, KA). However, the baseline itself is presented as a "naive" implementation. Projected gradient methods are known to be sensitive to step size and the nature of the projection. The specific issue highlighted—that the projection can lead to a near-diagonal kernel—is a plausible failure mode, but it is not convincingly demonstrated that this is an inherent flaw of the entire class of constrained gradient-based methods rather than the specific implementation chosen. It would have been more compelling to compare against a more sophisticated constrained optimization method, for example, one using a log-determinant barrier function to enforce the PSD constraint, which would avoid the hard projection step altogether.

More critically, the experimental results do not provide unequivocal evidence for the superiority of the EM approach. When a sensible, data-driven "moments-matching" initialization is used, the performance difference between EM and the baseline KA becomes marginal (0.4% average gain). This suggests that the initialization strategy is a more critical factor for success than the choice of optimization algorithm. The EM algorithm's advantage is most pronounced when using a poor, random Wishart initialization or in a contrived data-poor setting. While robustness to initialization is a desirable property, the practical significance is limited if a simple, effective initialization heuristic exists for the established method.

Furthermore, there are concerns about the methodological completeness of the proposed M-step. The authors state that they only take a single gradient step for the eigenvectors V, which makes this a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm rather than a full EM. This is an acceptable simplification, but its implications for the speed and stability of convergence are not discussed. More importantly, the provided gradient formula (Eq. 20) is a simplification that holds when q=p. This is true at the beginning of the M-step, but the paper lacks a thorough discussion of this partial maximization and how the gradient would be computed for subsequent steps if a more complete M-step optimization were attempted. This lack of detail makes it difficult to fully assess the algorithm's properties and potential limitations. Finally, the paper offers no theoretical analysis of the algorithm's convergence, which would have been valuable given the non-standard update steps.

**Clarity and Originality**
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The background on DPPs is clear, and the derivation of the EM algorithm is easy to follow. The originality of the core idea—framing DPP learning within the EM framework via latent eigenvectors—is the paper's strongest point and represents a novel contribution to the literature.

**Pros and Cons**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and conceptually elegant EM-based algorithm for the difficult problem of learning a full DPP kernel.
*   The re-parameterization in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors and the introduction of a latent variable is an original and insightful approach.
*   The proposed method avoids the explicit projection step required by the baseline, which can be problematic under certain conditions.
*   The algorithm is shown to be computationally faster than the presented baseline implementation.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental comparison is made against a weak, "naive" baseline. The claimed superiority of the EM method is not established against stronger, standard constrained optimization techniques.
*   The performance gains are marginal when a reasonable, data-driven initialization is used, questioning the practical necessity of the more complex EM algorithm.
*   The methodological description of the M-step for the eigenvectors is incomplete. The algorithm is a GEM, and the potential consequences of the partial maximization step are not analyzed.
*   The paper lacks any theoretical convergence guarantees.
*   The claims of improved robustness and avoidance of poor local optima are not sufficiently substantiated, as they appear to be highly dependent on the choice of a poor random initialization.

**Conclusion**
While the paper introduces an interesting and original idea, the work in its current form is not sufficiently rigorous. The validation against a weak baseline and the marginal improvements under realistic settings diminish the significance of the contribution. The methodological description could also be more complete. For the work to be compelling, the authors would need to provide a much stronger empirical case, either by comparing against state-of-the-art constrained optimization baselines or by demonstrating a substantial advantage on a wider range of tasks where simple initialization heuristics fail.
