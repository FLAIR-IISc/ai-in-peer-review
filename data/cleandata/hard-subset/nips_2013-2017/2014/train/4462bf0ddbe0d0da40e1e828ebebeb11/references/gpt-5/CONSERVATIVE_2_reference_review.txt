Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles learning determinantal point processes (DPPs) without restrictive parameterizations by maximizing likelihood over an unconstrained, full kernel. The authors propose an EM-style algorithm that operates in the eigenspace of the marginal kernel K, introducing a latent variable J corresponding to a subset of eigenvectors. They derive an E-step where q(J|Y) is shown to be a k-DPP, and an M-step with a closed-form update for eigenvalues and a gradient-based update for eigenvectors on the Stiefel manifold. As a baseline they use projected gradient ascent on K with eigenvalue clipping.

This builds on a substantial body of DPP literature. Prior learning approaches either learn per-item quality weights (convex but limited) or mixtures/parametric kernels, often sacrificing DPP structure or generality. The paper’s main novelty is an eigendecomposition-driven generalized EM (GEM) method for full kernel learning. The authors compare to the projected gradient baseline; other learning approaches (e.g., diagonal reweighting, parametric feature-based DPPs, Bayesian variants) are discussed but not empirically compared.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The latent-variable formulation via mixtures of elementary DPPs is standard, and the E-step characterization as a k-DPP appears correct (though proof is deferred to the supplement). The eigenvalue update is simple and self-normalizing. However, the M-step for eigenvectors is only a single gradient step using a derivative that is stated to be valid for the “initial gradient” (q=p), and the paper then repeatedly redoes the E-step to pick a step size. This is not a full M-step, and the algorithm is therefore a generalized EM with no clear monotonic improvement guarantee on the variational bound; the authors check improvement on the true likelihood instead. While this may work in practice, the lack of convergence guarantees and reliance on an approximate gradient for V is a concern.
- Methodological completeness: Several derivations critical to understanding and reproducing the algorithm (Lemma 1, Eq. (19) marginals and their complexity, Eq. (20) derivative details) are deferred to the supplement. Regularization is not discussed; the practical fix to avoid λ=1 (tightening the constraint) is ad hoc. The projection pathology of the KA baseline is argued intuitively rather than analyzed rigorously.
- Complexity: The claimed runtime improvement O(nNk^2) vs O(nN^3) hinges on k≪N. The experiments use modest N (~71); the asymptotic advantage is not convincingly demonstrated at larger scales. Moreover, the manifold update requires matrix exponentials (O(N^3)) within the step-size search; this term can dominate.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clearly written and well organized, with helpful background and motivation. The algorithm descriptions are readable, and the connection to DPP eigendecompositions is clear. However, key proofs and algorithmic details are relegated to the supplement; inclusion of at least the crucial parts (e.g., a concise proof sketch of Lemma 1 and the validity and scope of Eq. (20)) in the main text would strengthen clarity and rigor. The use of “EM” should be clarified as generalized EM, with a precise statement of what is guaranteed to increase (likelihood vs bound) and under what conditions.

Originality:
Exploiting the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs view to derive a GEM procedure for full-kernel learning is a novel and reasonable idea. The use of k-DPP structure in the E-step and Stiefel updates for eigenvectors is a thoughtful combination of known tools. That said, the eigenvector update is not a principled closed-form M-step but a heuristic gradient step with manifold projection.

Significance:
Learning general DPP kernels is important. However, the empirical evaluation is limited:
- Baselines: Only KA is compared. Missing are stronger/established baselines such as convex quality-reweighting, feature-based parametric DPPs, and non-DPP diverse subset models (e.g., facility location/submodular functions) that are commonly used in practice.
- Datasets/tasks: All experiments are on one application (baby registries) with small-to-moderate N. No synthetic recovery experiments (where the ground-truth kernel is known) are provided to test whether the method actually learns meaningful off-diagonal structure. No evaluation beyond likelihood (e.g., MAP set quality, downstream recommendation performance) is given.
- Scale: Claims of asymptotic speed are not borne out on larger N; the reported 2.1× speedup is on small N and depends on step-size search overhead.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation for avoiding projection artifacts in KA and for working in eigenspace.
- Elegant E-step characterization as a k-DPP and efficient marginal computations.
- Closed-form eigenvalue updates without explicit projection.
- Code release and a reasonably sized real dataset; multiple initializations examined.

Weaknesses:
- The “EM” is in fact a GEM with only a partial, approximate M-step for V; no convergence guarantees or monotonicity of the bound.
- Limited baselines and domains; lack of comparisons to established DPP learning variants and submodular alternatives.
- Scalability claims not convincingly demonstrated; dependence on O(N^3) manifold updates may be significant.
- Minimal treatment of regularization and statistical robustness; several proofs relegated to supplement.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Recast as generalized EM and provide theoretical guarantees (e.g., conditions under which the likelihood increases; convergence to a stationary point).
- Include stronger baselines: (i) convex per-item scaling, (ii) a feature-based parametric DPP, (iii) a non-DPP submodular model, and (iv) a more sophisticated projected gradient variant (e.g., with proximal terms or Riemannian optimization directly on PSD cone).
- Add synthetic experiments with known kernels to assess recovery of off-diagonal structure and sensitivity to initialization.
- Evaluate on additional domains and larger N to substantiate scalability claims.
- Provide ablations (effect of step-size search, number of V-updates per M-step, impact of the λ<1 tightening).
- Move key derivations from the supplement into the main text or provide concise proof sketches.

Pros:
- Conceptually interesting latent-variable view leading to efficient E-step.
- Practical improvements over a naive baseline on a real dataset.
- Clear exposition and released code.

Cons:
- Heuristic M-step undermines EM guarantees.
- Narrow empirical validation and limited baselines.
- Scalability not convincingly demonstrated; reliance on N^3 steps remains.

Recommendation:
Borderline reject. The idea is promising and addresses an important problem, but the current evidence and methodological rigor are insufficient to support acceptance. Strengthening theoretical guarantees and expanding the empirical evaluation with stronger baselines and larger-scale experiments would substantially improve the paper.
