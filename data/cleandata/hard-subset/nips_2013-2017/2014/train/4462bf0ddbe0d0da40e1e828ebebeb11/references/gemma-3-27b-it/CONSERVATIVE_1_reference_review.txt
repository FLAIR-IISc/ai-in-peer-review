This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing the difficulty of directly maximizing the likelihood function due to the positive semi-definiteness constraint on the kernel matrix L. The authors highlight issues with gradient ascent – specifically, the tendency to learn near-diagonal L matrices that fail to capture item diversity – and argue that their EM approach overcomes this limitation by working with the eigendecomposition of L. They also claim asymptotic runtime improvements over gradient ascent.

The core idea of leveraging the eigendecomposition to sidestep the projection step required for gradient ascent is sensible, and the connection to existing DPP algorithms via the marginal kernel K is well-established. The mathematical derivations appear correct, and the decomposition into E and M steps follows the standard EM framework.  The identified weakness of gradient ascent collapsing to diagonal solutions is a valid concern, and the EM formulation’s avoidance of explicit projection is a clear strength. 

However, my overall assessment is skeptical. The paper feels incremental and doesn’t present sufficiently compelling evidence to justify a contribution to NIPS. While the authors acknowledge related work on partial learning of L and Bayesian approaches, they don’t adequately contextualize the EM method *within* that landscape. Is the advantage over existing methods (especially the Bayesian approach of [8]) substantial enough to warrant a new algorithm? The paper states EM is "asymptotically faster" but doesn't provide a sufficiently detailed complexity analysis to convincingly demonstrate this, especially considering the iterative nature of the M-step and the overhead of eigendecompositions. 

The experiments are a significant weakness. The “baby registry” dataset is a reasonable application, but the results are presented in a rather unconvincing manner.  Figures 1a and 1b show only *relative* gains, and the absolute improvements are not reported. A 3.7% improvement in log-likelihood (Figure 1a) isn’t a strong justification for the algorithmic complexity introduced. The comparison to K-Ascent (KA) is also somewhat unfair; the authors acknowledge KA's sensitivity to initialization and then present results where EM benefits from a better initialization strategy.  Furthermore, the claim that EM is 2.1x faster requires more granular reporting (e.g., wall-clock time for each step) to be fully believable.  The qualitative example in Figure 2a, while suggestive, is hardly a rigorous comparison. The reliance on a single dataset and limited baselines weakens the conclusions.

The paper lacks a thorough ablation study. How sensitive is the EM algorithm to the choice of step size in the M-step?  What is the impact of the approximate eigenvector update?  A more detailed analysis of the computational bottlenecks would also be beneficial. The discussion of the Stiefel manifold update feels hand-wavy; simply stating that a more sophisticated update exists doesn’t address the practical challenges of implementing it.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a legitimate challenge in DPP learning (likelihood maximization with PSD constraints).
*   Provides a mathematically sound EM algorithm that avoids the projection step of gradient ascent.
*   Demonstrates some improvement over gradient ascent on a specific dataset.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution – the core idea isn't radically new and builds heavily on existing DPP literature.
*   Weak experimental evaluation – limited datasets, insufficient baseline comparison, and unclear reporting of results.
*   Lack of thorough analysis – missing ablation studies and a detailed complexity analysis.
*   The practical advantages over existing methods (especially Bayesian approaches) are not convincingly demonstrated.



**Overall:**  I lean towards rejection. While the paper is technically sound and the authors demonstrate competence in the area, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty, significance, and experimental validation to meet the standards of NIPS.  The paper would be more suitable for a workshop or a more specialized conference. I encourage the authors to significantly strengthen the experimental evaluation, provide a more compelling comparison to existing methods, and conduct a more thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance and limitations.
