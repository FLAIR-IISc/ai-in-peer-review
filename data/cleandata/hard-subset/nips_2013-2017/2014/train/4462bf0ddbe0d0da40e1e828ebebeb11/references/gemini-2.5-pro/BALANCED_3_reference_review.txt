This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from observed subset data. Maximum likelihood estimation for an unconstrained DPP kernel is a non-convex problem, conjectured to be NP-hard. The authors first note that a naive projected gradient ascent approach on the marginal kernel `K` often fails. The projection step required to maintain positive semi-definiteness and cap eigenvalues can lead to poor local optima, particularly near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture the crucial diversity-promoting (repulsive) aspects of a DPP.

To overcome this, the paper proposes a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The key insight is to leverage the eigendecomposition of the kernel `K`. The generative process for a DPP sample can be viewed as first selecting a subset of eigenvectors (a latent variable `J`) and then sampling from the corresponding elementary DPP. By treating `J` as a hidden variable, the authors formulate the maximum likelihood problem within the EM framework. In the E-step, the posterior distribution over the latent variable `J` given an observed subset `Y` is computed. A key technical contribution is showing that this posterior is itself a `k`-DPP, allowing for efficient computation. In the M-step, the expected complete-data log-likelihood is maximized with respect to the kernel's eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The update for the eigenvalues has a closed form and naturally satisfies the required constraints, obviating the need for projection. The update for the eigenvectors is handled via a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, a principled approach to maintaining orthogonality without projection.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Elegance:** The formulation of DPP kernel learning as an EM problem is highly novel and insightful. Introducing the set of active eigenvectors `J` as a latent variable provides a new and powerful perspective on the problem. This is a significant conceptual contribution beyond a purely algorithmic improvement.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the EM algorithm, particularly the characterization of the posterior `p(J|Y)` as a `k`-DPP (Lemma 1), is elegant and crucial for the algorithm's tractability. The M-step updates are well-motivated: the closed-form update for eigenvalues and the use of Stiefel manifold optimization for eigenvectors are principled solutions that directly address the weaknesses of the projected gradient baseline.

3.  **Significance and Impact:** This work tackles a fundamental problem for the practical application of DPPs. Prior work often resorted to restrictive parameterizations of the kernel or methods that did not result in a true DPP. By providing a more robust and efficient method for learning a full, unconstrained kernel, this paper could significantly broaden the applicability of DPPs in domains where the underlying quality/similarity structure is unknown. The experimental results, showing improved robustness and speed, underscore this practical significance.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the intuition behind the failure of projected gradient ascent and how their EM approach specifically remedies this issue. The step-by-step development of the algorithm is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on the M-step for Eigenvectors (V):** There appears to be a slight ambiguity in the description of the eigenvector update. Section 3.5 states, "we simply take a single gradient step on V," but Algorithm 2 and the surrounding text describe a line search to find a step size `η` that improves the likelihood. This suggests a Generalized EM (GEM) approach, where the M-step only needs to improve the objective rather than fully maximize it. This is perfectly valid, but the description could be clarified. Does the line search re-evaluate the gradient (Eq. 20) for each trial `η`, or does it use the initial gradient and simply check for an increase in the true log-likelihood `L`? The latter seems more plausible given the runtime analysis, but making this explicit would improve the paper's reproducibility.

2.  **Limited Experimental Baseline:** The comparison to projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) is essential and well-executed. However, it is a relatively simple baseline. It would strengthen the paper to briefly discuss or compare against more sophisticated constrained optimization methods. More importantly, a comparison with the Bayesian parametric approach of Affandi et al. [8] would be valuable, even if the methods are different (non-parametric vs. parametric). This would help position the proposed method's performance within the broader landscape of DPP learning techniques.

3.  **Scalability Discussion:** The algorithm's complexity contains an `O(N^3)` term per iteration due to the eigendecomposition and matrix exponential for the `V` update. While the authors correctly point out the asymptotic advantage over the baseline for `k << N`, this `N^3` term remains a practical bottleneck for large ground set sizes `N`. A brief discussion of this limitation and potential avenues for future work, such as using low-rank approximations for `K` or approximate eigendecomposition methods, would make the paper more complete.

4.  **Qualitative Example Interpretation:** In the discussion of Figure 2a, the paper argues that the KA-selected items (three different baby monitors) are less plausible than the EM-selected set. While this example is intuitive, the argument would be stronger if supported by data. For example, what is the empirical co-occurrence frequency of multiple baby monitors in the training set? If it is indeed very low, this would provide quantitative evidence for the superiority of the EM-learned model beyond just the likelihood score.

**Decision:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, technically sound, and significant contribution to the literature on determinantal point processes. The proposed EM algorithm provides a more robust and principled approach to the difficult problem of learning an unconstrained DPP kernel. The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to points of clarification and potential extensions, which do not detract from the core contribution. I recommend **acceptance**.
