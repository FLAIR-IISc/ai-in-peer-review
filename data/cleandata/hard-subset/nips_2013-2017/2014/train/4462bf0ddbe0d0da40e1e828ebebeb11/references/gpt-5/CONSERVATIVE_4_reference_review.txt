Summary
The paper addresses maximum-likelihood learning for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without restrictive parameterizations, proposing an EM-style algorithm that operates in the eigenspace of the marginal kernel K. Using the mixture view of a DPP as a mixture over elementary DPPs (indexed by a subset of eigenvectors J), the authors introduce J as a latent variable. The E-step computes q(J|Y) (shown to be a k-DPP), and the M-step updates eigenvalues in closed form via expected inclusion probabilities and takes a gradient step for eigenvectors on the Stiefel manifold. Compared to projected gradient ascent on K (“K-Ascent”, KA), the method aims to avoid PSD projection degeneracy and reduce complexity per iteration from O(nN^3) to O(nNk^2) (plus an N^3 cost for the Stiefel update line search). Experiments on a baby registry dataset (13 categories, average N≈71) show modest test log-likelihood gains for EM over KA and 2.1× speedup on average.

Strengths
- Clear formulation leveraging the well-known mixture-of-elementary-DPPs view; the EM derivation is natural and highlights the role of k-DPPs for the E-step.
- Closed-form eigenvalue update (via k-DPP marginals) and principled constrained eigenvector update on the Stiefel manifold; avoids brittle PSD projections on K.
- Complexity analysis is thoughtful: replacing n many N×N inverses by k×k operations is attractive when k≪N.
- The empirical observation about KA’s projection leading to near-diagonal kernels is important and relevant in practice; the paper explains this failure mode convincingly.
- Writing is generally clear and well-organized; pseudocode and complexity statements are provided; code is promised.

Weaknesses
- Baselines are insufficient. Comparing only against a straightforward projected-gradient baseline on K is weak. Missing comparisons to established DPP learning approaches with demonstrated practice:
  - Convex quality-only learning (per-item weights) [Kulesza & Taskar, UAI 2011].
  - Mixtures/ensembles of fixed L kernels (e.g., Lin & Bilmes UAI) and Bayesian parametric approaches (Affandi et al., ICML; Snoek et al., NIPS), which are strong practical baselines despite constraints.
  - Low-rank factorized parameterizations of K or L with manifold optimization (Riemannian or proximal methods) that avoid hard projections.
  Without these, it is hard to assess the significance of the proposed method relative to established alternatives.
- Experimental scope is narrow. All results are on a single application (baby registries), modest N (~71), and no synthetic data where ground truth K is known. There is no evaluation beyond test log-likelihood (e.g., recommendation quality metrics, held-out prediction of co-selection, diversity/coverage). The gains reported are small on average (3.7% for Wishart init; 0.4% with moment matching) and depend on initialization and category. This weakens claims of robustness and general utility.
- V-update is only partially justified. The derivative used (Eq. 20) is valid at the start of the M-step when q=p, and the algorithm takes only a single gradient step per M-step (generalized EM). There is no monotonicity/convergence proof beyond checking L increases via a line search (which itself requires repeated E-steps). A more principled treatment or empirical convergence diagnostics would strengthen the method’s soundness claims.
- Numerical fragility: the method requires clipping eigenvalues away from 1 to avoid blow-up in R=diag(sqrt(λ/(1−λ))). This ad hoc fix may bias learning; sensitivity to the clipping threshold is not analyzed.
- Initialization sensitivity remains: both EM and KA stagnate from near-diagonal starts; the method still requires non-trivial off-diagonals initially. Robustness to initialization could be better quantified.
- Complexity and scalability: while the O(nNk^2) term is attractive, the algorithm also incurs repeated N^3 costs for the Stiefel manifold exponential during line search. No experiments with larger N (hundreds to thousands) are shown to substantiate the asymptotic advantage. Moreover, KA’s O(nN^3) baseline is arguably pessimistic; practical implementations can exploit structure/identities to avoid full inverses per example.
- Some key technical claims (e.g., Lemma 1 showing q(J|Y) is a k-DPP and the O(nNk^2) marginal computations) are deferred to the supplement; providing at least a proof sketch or more algorithmic details in the main text would aid reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you compare against at least one established practical baseline beyond KA (e.g., convex quality-only model; parametric/Bayesian learning; low-rank factorization with Riemannian optimization)? Even if not apples-to-apples, relative test likelihood and recommendation metrics would be informative.
2. How sensitive are results to the eigenvalue clipping threshold near 1? Does clipping impact the learned set size distribution or diversity?
3. Can you provide convergence plots (L over iterations), and quantify the number of E-steps per line search? Is monotonic improvement guaranteed in practice?
4. What is the actual computational cost breakdown (nNk^2 vs. N^3) on your datasets? How does the method scale to N≥500 and n large?
5. Can you include synthetic experiments with known K to assess recovery, and an ablation of the V-update (e.g., multiple manifold steps per M-step) to see impact on likelihood and runtime?

Minor comments
- The claim that KA gradients are diagonal when K is diagonal is correct, but practitioners could mitigate degeneracy with alternative parameterizations (e.g., low-rank factors) or Riemannian updates on the PSD cone; acknowledging this would temper the argument.
- Some references are incomplete; please standardize citations and add missing details.
- Ensure the code link is active and includes scripts to reproduce all figures.

Pros
- Conceptually sound EM formulation leveraging DPP structure.
- Avoids brittle PSD projection; principled handling of orthogonality via Stiefel updates.
- Potential computational benefits when k≪N.
- Clear exposition; useful to the DPP community.

Cons
- Limited and arguably weak baseline comparisons.
- Narrow empirical scope; modest gains; reliance on good initialization.
- Partial M-step and lack of convergence guarantees; numerical clipping.
- Scalability not convincingly demonstrated.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The methodological idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the empirical validation against established baselines and at larger scales is insufficient to justify acceptance at this stage. Strengthening the experimental section and addressing convergence/scalability concerns would significantly improve the paper. Confidence: high.
