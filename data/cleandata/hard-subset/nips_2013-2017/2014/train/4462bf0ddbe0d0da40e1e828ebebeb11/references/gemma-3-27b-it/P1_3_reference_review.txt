## Review of “Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes”

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix *L* (or its marginal form *K*) of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from observed subsets.  Existing approaches either partially learn *L* (e.g., learning item quality weights) or rely on restrictive parameterizations or ensembles that sacrifice the desirable properties of a true DPP. The authors propose an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that leverages the eigendecomposition of *L* to avoid the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent methods, which often leads to degenerate, nearly diagonal kernels and thus poor modeling of item diversity. The paper demonstrates, both theoretically (asymptotic runtime) and empirically (baby registry recommendation task), that the proposed EM algorithm is more robust and efficient than projected gradient ascent.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing DPP learning literature. It clearly articulates the limitations of previous work: the partial learning of *L* by Kulesza & Taskar (UAI 2012), the ensemble approach of Affandi et al. (ICML 2013), and the Bayesian methods of  Affandi et al. (NIPS 2014).  The contribution lies in providing a full *L* learning method without these drawbacks, by exploiting the eigendecomposition and developing a specifically tailored EM algorithm. The acknowledgement of the difficulty of gradient ascent (and the need for projection to maintain PSD) is also well-placed.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Approach:** The EM algorithm, leveraging eigendecomposition, provides a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to DPP learning.  Avoiding the projection step is a significant advantage, as highlighted by the authors' analysis and experimental results.
*   **Theoretical Justification:**  The paper provides a clear theoretical basis for the EM algorithm, connecting it to the hidden variable formulation of DPPs through the marginal kernel *K*.  The derivations of the M-step updates are well-presented.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the baby registry dataset provide compelling evidence of the EM algorithm’s superiority over projected gradient ascent. The analysis of the runtime improvements is valuable. The comparison against different initialization methods is insightful.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The explanations of the mathematical concepts are reasonably clear, even for readers not intimately familiar with DPPs.
*   **Addressing a Core Challenge:** Learning DPPs is a fundamental problem with broad applicability, and this work makes significant progress towards addressing it.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity Analysis:** While the authors state an asymptotic runtime advantage, the constant factors and practical implications of the *k* dependency (where *k* is the size of the largest subset) could be further discussed. Is *k* typically small enough in real-world applications for this runtime to be a genuine advantage?
*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** While the paper addresses initialization, the dependence on a "good" initial kernel is still apparent. The moment-matching initialization helps, but its robustness across diverse datasets isn’t fully explored. More robust initialization strategies (e.g., using random projections or sketching) could be considered.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments focus solely on a product recommendation task. While valuable, extending the evaluation to other domains (e.g., document summarization, image search) would strengthen the findings and demonstrate the broader applicability of the method.
*   **Step-Size Selection:** The paper mentions the need for step-size selection in the EM algorithm, but the details of the implemented strategy are somewhat glossed over. A more thorough discussion of this practical aspect would be beneficial.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:**  Equation (21) relating to the Stiefel manifold update could be clarified; the phrase "neglecting second-order information" is a bit informal for a NIPS paper.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in DPP learning with a novel and theoretically grounded approach.
*   The EM algorithm demonstrably outperforms existing methods on a relevant task.
*   The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method.
*   The authors acknowledge limitations and potential areas for future work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity analysis could be more detailed.
*   The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.
*   The dependence on initialization, while addressed, remains a potential concern.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPP learning. The proposed EM algorithm is a significant advance over existing methods, and the empirical results are compelling. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.**  Specifically, I recommend the authors:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity, including the practical implications of the *k* dependency.
2.  Explore additional initialization strategies and assess their robustness.
3.  Consider extending the experimental evaluation to other domains.
4.  Clarify the details of the step-size selection process.
5.  Provide more detail regarding the Stiefel manifold update.
