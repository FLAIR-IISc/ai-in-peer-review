

# Review: Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes

This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the marginal kernel K of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP), addressing a gap in the literature where previous methods either required restrictive parameterizations or relied on gradient ascent with problematic projection steps. The authors argue that their EM approach avoids degenerate solutions by working with the eigendecomposition of K, which they claim makes the method more robust to initialization and faster asymptotically than gradient-based approaches.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the DPP learning literature, addressing a recognized limitation in previous work (the degeneracy of projected gradient ascent). The EM framework is well-motivated and the experimental results on real-world product recommendation data demonstrate clear advantages of the proposed method in terms of both model quality and runtime. The connection between the EM approach and the eigendecomposition of K is well-explained and leverages key properties of DPPs.

**Critical Issues:**

1. **Technical Inconsistencies in the EM Derivation:** 
   The paper claims that Equation (19) "allows us to compute the eigenvalue updates in time O(nNk²)" but fails to justify why the marginal probability q(j ∈ J | Yi) equals Σᵣ v̂ᵣ(j)². This is a critical step that requires justification, particularly since it appears to be a direct consequence of the k-DPP structure, which isn't properly explained. The paper also states "the QYi matrix will be infinite if any λj is exactly equal to 1" but doesn't clarify why this is a practical issue rather than a theoretical concern (since they claim to simply tighten the constraint on λ).

2. **Inadequate Asymptotic Complexity Analysis:**
   The paper claims EM is "asymptotically faster" than K-Ascent, stating the complexity as O(T₁nNk² + T₁T₂N³) versus KA's O(T₁nN³ + T₁T₂N³). However, it doesn't clearly demonstrate why the Nk² term is substantially better than N³ for typical values of k (where k = max|Yᵢ|). For the baby registry experiments, k is at most 100 while N is 71, making the asymptotic advantage questionable in the practical regime the authors consider.

3. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:**
   - The paper contains a clear typo: "param- eterized" (split across two lines) on page 2.
   - There's an odd "Tuesday, August 5, 14" date on page 4 that appears to be a draft artifact.
   - The reference list has inconsistent formatting (e.g., some entries have commas after conference names, others don't).
   - The paper states "P(Y) = det(L_Y) det(L+ I) = |det(K - I_Y)|" (Equation 3) without justifying the equivalence between these expressions.

4. **Experimental Details:**
   The paper claims EM "gains an average of 4.5%, with a maximum gain of 16.5% for the safety category" (Figure 1b), but doesn't provide standard deviations or confidence intervals. The claim that "EM is 2.1 times faster than KA" lacks any statistical measure of variability across the 13 categories.

5. **Missing Context in Related Work:**
   The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from Affandi et al.'s "Learning the Parameters of Determinantal Point Process Kernels" (ICML 2013), which also addresses DPP parameter learning without restrictive assumptions. The authors cite this work but don't explain how their EM approach differs in a meaningful way from the Bayesian methods proposed in that paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to DPP learning with promising experimental results, the technical inconsistencies in the derivation, the lack of clear justification for key steps, and the inadequate complexity analysis undermine the paper's scientific rigor. The formatting issues and inconsistent presentation also suggest the paper wasn't thoroughly edited. These are not minor concerns—they directly impact the paper's technical credibility.

The novelty is somewhat limited given prior work on DPP learning, and the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how their method advances beyond existing approaches. The practical benefits shown in the experiments are meaningful, but the theoretical foundation isn't as strong as it should be for a NIPS paper.

**Recommendation:**

Reject. The paper's technical flaws are too substantial to overlook, even though the overall contribution has merit. The authors need to provide rigorous justification for their key equations, clarify their complexity claims, and better contextualize their work relative to existing literature before it could be considered for publication at NIPS. The current version contains too many unresolved technical gaps that would make it difficult for reviewers to assess the paper's true contribution.
