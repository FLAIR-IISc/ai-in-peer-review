- Problem: Learning the full DPP kernel L from subset examples is challenging; prior methods use restrictive parameterizations or partial learning.  
- Proposed method: An EM algorithm using L’s eigendecomposition and a hidden eigenvector variable to avoid projection issues in likelihood maximization.  
- Theoretical contribution: Derives M-step updates and provides computational complexity analysis, offering a solid theoretical foundation.  
- Empirical results: Experiments on baby registry data show EM outperforms projected gradient ascent in accuracy and runtime, especially with strong item interactions and limited data.  
- Novelty: The EM formulation for DPP learning is a genuinely new approach distinct from prior work.  
- Clarity: The paper is well-written, organized, and uses consistent notation, making complex ideas digestible.  
- Significance: A robust, efficient full-kernel DPP learner could impact applications like recommendations and summarization.  
- Implementation complexity: Practical implementation is intricate, requiring efficient k-DPP marginalization and handling Stiefel manifold constraints.  
- Data-dependent gains: EM’s advantage appears to depend strongly on dataset characteristics (interaction strength d), and the reason for this dependency needs deeper analysis.  
- Limited experimental scope: Evaluation is confined to a single domain (baby registries); broader experiments are needed to establish generality.  
- Initialization sensitivity: Performance varies with initialization (Wishart vs moment matching), indicating sensitivity that warrants further study.  
- Quality assessment: Technically sound and complete, with empirical support—rated strong.  
- Originality assessment: Novel contribution—rated strong.  
- Clarity assessment: Exceptionally clear—rated excellent.  
- Significance assessment: Important and impactful advancement—rated strong.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Tackles an important problem with a novel, theoretically sound algorithm and supportive empirical evidence; well-written.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Implementation barriers, dataset-dependent benefits, and limited experimental diversity.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong accept; the paper makes a significant contribution and meets conference standards.
