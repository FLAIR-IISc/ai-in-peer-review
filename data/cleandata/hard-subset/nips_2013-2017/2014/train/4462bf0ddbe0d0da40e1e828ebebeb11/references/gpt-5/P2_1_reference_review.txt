Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles maximum likelihood learning of determinantal point processes (DPPs) without constraining the kernel beyond PSD and spectral bounds. Prior work largely either: (a) learns only per-item quality weights while keeping similarities fixed, (b) learns mixtures/ensembles of fixed DPPs (sacrificing some DPP properties), or (c) imposes parametric forms and performs Bayesian inference. Direct projected gradient ascent (PGA) on K is simple but requires repeated PSD/spectral projection, which can push the solution toward near-diagonal kernels and stall diversity modeling.

The main contribution is an EM-style algorithm operating in the eigenspace of K. Using the mixture view of a DPP as a sum over elementary DPPs (indexed by a hidden set J of eigenvectors), the authors derive:
- E-step: q(J|Y) = pK(J|Y), shown to be a k-DPP with a computable kernel (proof relegated to supplement).
- M-step: closed-form eigenvalue update using k-DPP marginals; and a single gradient step on eigenvectors with a Stiefel-manifold multiplicative update, avoiding the problematic projection used by PGA.

They argue this improves robustness and yields better asymptotic complexity: O(T1 n N k^2 + T1 T2 N^3) vs O(T1 n N^3 + T1 T2 N^3) for PGA, where k is max set size and typically k << N.

Experiments on an Amazon baby registry dataset (13 categories, average N≈71, n≈8.6k) compare EM to PGA under two initializations (Wishart and moment-matching). EM yields higher test likelihood—particularly when off-diagonal structure is important—and is about 2x faster in practice. In data-poor regimes (n≈2N), EM’s gains are larger.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The EM derivation leverages a standard latent-variable view of DPPs; the E-step characterization as a k-DPP and the eigenvalue update via k-DPP marginals appear correct and computationally efficient. The eigenvector update is approximate (using the initial-gradient formula valid at q=p and taking one step), but the algorithm checks actual likelihood improvements and updates q accordingly, providing a pragmatic, stable procedure even if classical EM monotonicity on the lower bound is not guaranteed every substep.
- The complexity analysis is plausible and supported by experiments. Practical issues (e.g., capping λ<1 to avoid infinities) are acknowledged.
- One concern is limited theoretical guarantees for convergence of the V-update beyond empirical validation, and the reliance on a single-step update per M-step. Also, the necessity and cost of repeatedly evaluating L(K) and doing multiple E-steps in line search could be discussed more explicitly in complexity terms.

Clarity:
- The paper is well organized and clearly written. The motivation for avoiding projections is well articulated. Algorithmic steps are laid out, with attention to computational costs. Some derivations (E-step kernel, eigenvector gradient) are in the supplement; the main text gives clear statements and complexity, which is adequate but makes full verification dependent on the supplement.
- Minor clarity suggestions: briefly explain the cost of evaluating L(K) and numerical stabilization for det/inverses; clarify when (K−IY) is invertible and how near-singular cases are handled; and add a short note on computing det(K−IY) efficiently for small |Y|.

Originality:
- Using the mixture over elementary DPPs to introduce a hidden variable is known, but employing it to derive an EM algorithm that optimizes unconstrained K via eigenvalues/eigenvectors, with efficient k-DPP marginalization and Stiefel-manifold updates, is a novel and well-motivated combination.
- The identification and empirical demonstration of the projection pathology in PGA, and the proposed workaround via eigenspace EM, are original and practically meaningful.

Significance:
- Learning full, unconstrained DPP kernels is important for applications where negative interactions drive diversity. The proposed method is both more robust to initialization and faster than a standard baseline when k << N, a common regime. The availability of code increases impact.
- Experimental scope is limited to a single real dataset and one baseline (PGA). Stronger significance would come from additional baselines (e.g., Riemannian/spectrahedral optimization without projection, Burer–Monteiro-style factored PSD parameterization with spectral penalties) and scaling experiments to larger N.

Pros:
- Clear, principled EM framework with efficient steps via k-DPP machinery.
- Avoids projection-induced degeneracy; robust across initializations and data regimes.
- Better asymptotic and practical runtime when k << N.
- Solid empirical improvements in test likelihood; realistic application; code provided.

Cons:
- Eigenvector M-step is approximate; no convergence guarantees beyond empirical behavior.
- Experimental breadth is limited: one dataset and one main baseline; no synthetic recovery studies or large-scale tests.
- Some critical proofs/details deferred to the supplement; minor implementation details (numerics, cost of likelihood evaluation) could be clearer.

Questions for authors:
- Can you report the cost of evaluating L(K) per iteration and its contribution relative to the O(n N k^2) terms?
- How sensitive is performance to doing more than one V-update per M-step? Any monotonicity guarantees if you optimize the lower bound more faithfully?
- Could you compare against a Riemannian optimization baseline that optimizes K under PSD and spectral constraints without projection, or a factored K = B B^T with spectral regularization?

Minor comments:
- Typos: “negelecting” → “neglecting”; “justies” → “justifies”.
- Note that V is orthogonal (real case), not “unitary.”

Overall assessment:
Strong paper. Technically sound, clear, and practically relevant, with novel and effective methodology and convincing empirical evidence within its scope. With broader experimental comparisons, this could be a definitive reference for unconstrained DPP learning.
