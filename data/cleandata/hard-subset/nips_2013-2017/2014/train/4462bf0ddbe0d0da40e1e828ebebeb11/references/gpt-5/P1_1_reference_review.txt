Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses maximum likelihood learning of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without restrictive parameterizations. The key idea is to optimize the marginal kernel K via its eigendecomposition K = VΛV^T and to exploit a known mixture representation of a DPP as a mixture over elementary DPPs indexed by subsets of eigenvectors. Introducing the latent subset J yields an EM-style algorithm: the E-step sets q(J|Y)=pK(J|Y), which the authors show is a k-DPP with a computable kernel; the M-step (i) updates eigenvalues in closed form via k-DPP marginals, and (ii) updates eigenvectors by a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold using a skew-symmetric update and a line search that checks improvements in the true likelihood L. Compared to projected gradient ascent on K (which requires PSD projection and eigenvalue clipping), EM avoids projection-induced degeneracy and is asymptotically faster when typical set sizes k are much smaller than N.

The work is well positioned relative to prior DPP learning approaches: row-weight learning (convex but only learns item qualities), mixtures of fixed L-ensembles (losing DPP structure), and Bayesian parametric methods. Using the mixture-of-elementary-DPP representation in an EM framework and leveraging k-DPP marginal computations to obtain efficient updates appears novel and practically motivated.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The formulation of the likelihood in terms of K, the gradient for projected ascent, and the mixture representation are standard. The E-step result (q(J|Y) is a k-DPP with kernel QY) is plausible and consistent with prior k-DPP theory; the eigenvalue update λj = (1/n)∑i q(j∈J|Yi) is elegant and, with k-DPP marginals, computationally tractable. The eigenvector update uses a Stiefel manifold step with a line search on L; this is a reasonable Generalized EM step, though it optimizes a surrogate gradient valid when q=p and then re-evaluates.
- Claims vs. evidence: The paper claims robustness and speed improvements over projected gradient; experiments on a sizable baby registry dataset (13 categories, average N≈71) support this, with larger relative gains in regimes with stronger negative interactions and in data-poor settings. Runtime gains (~2.1×) are consistent with the asymptotic discussion. Code/data availability is a plus for reproducibility.
- Caveats: (i) The M-step V-gradient (Eq. 20) is derived for q=p; after moving V, q changes. The procedure is a GEM with a line search that checks L, but a brief discussion of convergence/monotonicity would help (e.g., showing the line search ensures non-decreasing L). (ii) The stated O(nNk^2) complexity for eigenvector derivatives seems to involve inversions of |Y|×|Y| matrices (HY)−1; please clarify how this avoids O(nk^3) or specify assumptions/precomputations. Similarly, computing k-DPP marginals via eigen-decomposition per QY should be detailed. (iii) Numerical stability when λ→1 is handled by capping; a brief discussion of how this affects bias/consistency would be useful.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with helpful algorithms and derivations. A few issues to improve:
- Define IY and the formula P(Y)=|det(K−IY)| more explicitly to avoid confusion with the standard det(KY)det(I−K) expression.
- The metric d used to correlate EM’s gains with off-diagonal strength has formatting ambiguity: d = (1/N)||M||F ||diag(M)||2—please clarify whether this is a product or ratio and the motivation.
- Minor typos: “negelecting”→“neglecting”. Ensure all symbols in Eq. (20) are defined in place.
- Since key proofs are deferred, a short sketch in the main text (e.g., why q(J|Y) is a k-DPP) would aid readability.

Originality:
Using the latent elementary-DPP mixture to derive an EM algorithm that directly updates eigenvalues in closed form and eigenvectors on the Stiefel manifold is, to my knowledge, new. Prior learning work either restricted parameterizations or used different objectives (e.g., Bayesian inference). The paper also identifies a concrete failure mode of projected gradient via PSD projection that is insightful.

Significance:
Learning full, nonparametric DPP kernels is important for applications where diversity matters (recommendation, summarization, sensor placement). The proposed method addresses a practical obstacle (projection degeneracy) and provides computational advantages when k≪N. The empirical results on a realistic dataset suggest impact for practitioners. However, broader empirical validation and comparisons would strengthen significance.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a short monotonicity/convergence discussion (GEM) showing that the line search on L ensures non-decreasing likelihood.
- Clarify the computational complexity of Eq. (19) and (20) with concrete algorithms (e.g., whether you avoid per-example eigendecompositions or use Cholesky/low-rank tricks) and the actual asymptotics in k.
- Include additional baselines: convex row-scaling [Kulesza & Taskar], a quality-only/diagonal model, and perhaps low-rank DPP learning where applicable, to contextualize gains.
- Add synthetic experiments with known ground truth to assess recovery of eigenvectors/eigenvalues and sensitivity to initialization.
- Discuss scalability beyond N≈100 (e.g., low-rank V, sparsity, Nyström approximations) and how your EM adapts.

Pros:
- Novel EM formulation leveraging the elementary-DPP mixture; elegant closed-form eigenvalue updates.
- Avoids projection-induced degeneracy, with practical robustness.
- Computationally appealing when sets are small; clear runtime advantage in experiments.
- Real-world dataset and qualitative examples; code/data released.

Cons:
- V-update is an approximate M-step; convergence properties not fully articulated.
- Complexity claims for the M-step need clearer justification.
- Experimental scope limited to one domain; missing comparisons to some established baselines.
- Scalability to large N not demonstrated.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The methodological contribution is solid and practically relevant, and the empirical results are convincing within scope. Addressing clarity on complexity/convergence and enriching the empirical comparisons would make this a strong accept.
