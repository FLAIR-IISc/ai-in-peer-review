This paper addresses the problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. The authors note that directly maximizing the log-likelihood of the data is a challenging non-convex problem, conjectured to be NP-hard. Previous work has circumvented this difficulty by learning restricted versions of the kernel, for example by learning only scalar quality weights for each item [2], learning a mixture of fixed kernels [3], or assuming a specific parametric form [8]. A straightforward approach for learning the full kernel is projected gradient ascent, but the authors argue that the projection step required to maintain the positive semi-definite (PSD) property can lead to poor local optima, particularly near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture diversity.

The main contribution of this paper is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full, unconstrained DPP kernel. The key idea is to reformulate the problem by introducing a latent variable based on the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel `K = VΛV^T`. Following the generative interpretation of DPP sampling [21], the authors treat the set of selected eigenvectors, `J`, as a latent variable. The resulting EM algorithm alternates between an E-step, which computes the posterior distribution over `J` for each observed subset, and an M-step, which updates the eigenvalues `Λ` and eigenvectors `V`. The authors show that the E-step is tractable, as the posterior `p(J | Y)` is itself a k-DPP whose kernel can be computed efficiently. The M-step involves a closed-form update for the eigenvalues that inherently satisfies the constraints, avoiding projection. The eigenvectors are updated via a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, which also avoids a simple projection. The proposed EM algorithm is shown to be asymptotically faster than projected gradient ascent.

### Strengths

**Quality (Technical Soundness):** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the learning problem within an EM framework is a clever and well-justified approach. The derivation hinges on introducing the eigenvector index set `J` as a hidden variable, which naturally fits the generative process of a DPP. The characterization of the posterior `p(J|Y_i)` as a k-DPP (Lemma 1) is a key insight that makes the E-step tractable. The M-step updates are derived correctly; the closed-form update for eigenvalues (Eq. 18) elegantly sidesteps the need for projection, and the use of an update on the Stiefel manifold for the eigenvectors (Eq. 21) is a principled way to handle the orthonormality constraint `V^T V = I`. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing the proposed method against a strong and natural baseline (projected gradient ascent) on a real-world dataset. The empirical results convincingly support the paper's claims about the robustness and efficiency of the EM algorithm. The analysis of the failure mode of the projection step in the baseline method is insightful and provides strong motivation for the proposed approach.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While EM is a standard technique, its application to learning an unconstrained DPP kernel is novel. To our knowledge, this is the first work to propose a method for learning the full kernel that is not based on direct projected gradient ascent or restrictive parametric assumptions. The central idea of leveraging the latent eigenvector structure is a significant conceptual contribution that reframes the learning problem in a productive way.

**Significance:** This paper addresses a central and challenging problem for the practical application of DPPs. The ability to learn a full kernel from data, without strong prior assumptions, greatly increases the utility of DPPs as a tool for modeling diversity in subset selection tasks. The proposed EM algorithm is shown to be more robust than projected gradient ascent, particularly with uninformative initialization or in data-poor settings, which are common practical challenges. Furthermore, the algorithm is computationally more efficient than the baseline for datasets where subset sizes are much smaller than the ground set size. Given the growing interest in DPPs across various machine learning domains (e.g., recommendation, summarization, search), this work is likely to have a substantial impact and will be widely used and built upon by researchers and practitioners.

### Weaknesses

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and clearly structured. However, a few points could be clarified.
1.  The M-step for the eigenvectors `V` is not a full maximization but a single gradient step. The algorithm is therefore a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm. While this is a common and valid practice, explicitly stating this would add precision and help manage expectations about convergence rates compared to a standard EM algorithm.
2.  The step-size selection for the `V` update involves checking for an increase in the true log-likelihood `L` rather than the lower bound `F`. This is a practical heuristic that breaks the monotonic improvement guarantee of the EM lower bound. A brief justification or acknowledgment of this choice would be helpful.
3.  Several key derivations (e.g., for Lemma 1, Eq. 19, Eq. 20) are deferred to the supplement. While this is understandable due to space constraints, it makes a complete verification of the technical details from the main paper difficult.

**Quality:** The superiority of EM over K-Ascent is less pronounced when a strong, data-driven moments-matching initializer is used with a sufficient amount of data. This suggests that while EM is more robust, a well-initialized simple baseline can be competitive in favorable conditions. This does not detract from the value of EM's robustness, but it is a nuance worth noting. The scalability of both methods is ultimately limited by operations with `O(N^3)` complexity, which may be a bottleneck for applications with very large ground sets (`N`).

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   **Pro:** The paper tackles a fundamental, difficult, and important problem in learning DPPs.
*   **Pro:** The proposed EM algorithm is novel, elegant, and technically sound.
*   **Pro:** The method demonstrates clear advantages in robustness and efficiency over a natural baseline on a real-world task.
*   **Pro:** The contribution is of high significance and is likely to be impactful for the growing community of researchers using DPPs.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
*   **Con:** The M-step is a single gradient step (GEM), a detail that could be made more explicit.
*   **Con:** The performance gain over the baseline is diminished under ideal initialization and data conditions.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, sound, and significant contribution to the literature on determinantal point processes.
