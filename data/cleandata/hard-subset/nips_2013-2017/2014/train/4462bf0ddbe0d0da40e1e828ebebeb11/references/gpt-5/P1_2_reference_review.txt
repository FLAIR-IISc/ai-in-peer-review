Summary
The paper addresses maximum-likelihood learning of determinantal point processes (DPPs) without imposing a parametric kernel form. The authors propose an EM-style algorithm that optimizes over the eigendecomposition K = VΛV^T of the DPP marginal kernel. They view DPPs as mixtures of “elementary DPPs” indexed by subsets of eigenvectors J, treat J as a latent variable, and derive:
- E-step: q(J|Y) = pK(J|Y), which they show is a k-DPP (k = |Y|) with an explicitly constructed kernel.
- M-step: closed-form updates for eigenvalues λj via posterior marginals q(j∈J|Y), computable efficiently using k-DPP marginalization; and a single gradient step on eigenvectors V on the Stiefel manifold using a multiplicative update based on a derived gradient valid at q=p.

They compare to projected gradient ascent on K with PSD and eigenvalue constraints (“K-Ascent”), highlighting that projection can collapse eigenvalues and trap the method near diagonal solutions, losing diversity modeling. On a large-scale baby registry recommendation dataset (N≈71 per category, 13 categories), their EM improves test log-likelihood over KA and is ~2x faster. They analyze sensitivity to initialization (Wishart vs moment-matching), data-poor regimes, and show stronger gains where off-diagonals matter more.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-situated within machine learning for DPPs. Prior methods either (i) learn only item quality scalings [2], (ii) combine fixed DPPs [3], or (iii) use Bayesian parametric assumptions [8]. The proposed method aims to learn a full, unconstrained kernel nonparametrically by exploiting the eigenstructure. The mixture-of-elementary-DPPs view and k-DPPs are standard [1,3,5,21], but their use to derive an EM procedure for MLE appears novel. The baseline (projected gradient on K) is natural and widely used in constrained matrix learning.

Strengths
- Technical novelty: Elegant use of the elementary DPP mixture and k-DPP conditionals to construct an EM framework with tractable E-step; closed-form λ updates; principled Stiefel-manifold update for V that avoids problematic PSD projection steps.
- Insight into KA failure modes: Clear diagnosis of projection-induced degeneracy and how working in eigen-space mitigates it.
- Computational analysis: Derives asymptotic runtime and shows regimes where EM is faster (k ≪ N), which is relevant in many subset selection problems.
- Empirical evaluation: Real-world dataset with a large number of users; comparisons under different initializations and data sizes; correlation analysis between task structure (off-diagonal strength) and EM’s gains; a qualitative MAP example illustrating diversity.
- Reproducibility: Code and data released.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope of experiments: Evaluation is limited to one domain. Comparisons to alternative DPP learning approaches (e.g., Bayesian parametric kernels [8], low-rank/structured DPPs, mixtures) or to strong submodular baselines (e.g., submodular shells [4]) would strengthen claims of generality.
- Partial M-step and objective monitoring: The eigenvector update uses only a single gradient step with a gradient valid at q=p and performs a line search that re-runs the E-step while checking improvement in the likelihood L rather than the lower bound F. This is a generalized EM, but a clearer discussion of convergence guarantees (monotonicity, stationarity conditions) would be helpful.
- Complexity details: The paper claims O(n N k^2) for computing ∂F/∂V (Eq. 20). Since H^Yi is |Yi|×|Yi|, inverting it naively is O(k^3) per example (leading to O(n k^3) plus the V/B mappings). Please clarify where the extra factor N replaces one factor k, or whether fast solves (e.g., Cholesky reused across terms) reduce this to O(k^2) per example.
- Notational clarity: In Lemma 1, ZYi = UYi(UYi)^T seems to be |Yi|×|Yi| if UYi selects rows, whereas QYi = R ZYi R is N×N; there appears to be a dimensional inconsistency. Similarly, the definitions around U, VYi, BYi, and HYi could be streamlined to avoid confusion.
- Numerical stability: The method requires clipping λ near 1 to avoid blow-up in R = diag(sqrt(λ/(1−λ))). Please provide practical settings for the cap (e.g., 1−ε) and discuss sensitivity and conditioning of HYi.
- Scalability: While asymptotically better than KA when k≪N, the method still requires repeated eigendecompositions/matrix exponentials with O(N^3) cost per line-search attempt. Discussion of low-rank EM variants (using truncated eigenspaces) would be valuable for N in the thousands.
- Statistical considerations: No regularization is used beyond the inherent λ∈[0,1] constraints; overfitting in small-n regimes is not analyzed beyond reporting higher held-out likelihood than KA. Adding simple priors/penalties or early-stopping criteria, and reporting variance/error bars, would strengthen the empirical story.

Quality
The paper is technically sound overall, with a solid derivation of the EM framework and a reasonable generalized EM implementation. Some derivations are deferred to the supplement; the main potential gap is the complexity claim for the V-gradient and notational clarity for the E-step kernel construction. Empirically, the setup is careful, and the analysis is insightful, though limited in breadth of domains/baselines.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized, with clear motivation and intuitive explanations (e.g., why KA can become near-diagonal). A few sections (Lemma 1, dimensions of matrices, the role of BYi, and the exact mechanics of the line search and convergence criteria) would benefit from additional detail and clearer notation.

Originality
The EM approach that leverages the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs and k-DPP conditionals to learn a full kernel appears novel and addresses a recognized pain point (projected gradient degeneracy). The Stiefel manifold update in this context is also a neat application.

Significance
Learning unconstrained DPP kernels is important for applications in recommendation, summarization, and sensor placement. Providing a practical, more robust alternative to projected gradient that is faster in common regimes is valuable. With additional validation across domains and comparisons to more baselines, this could be a method practitioners adopt.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled EM formulation exploiting DPP eigenstructure
- Addresses a real weakness of projected gradient and is more robust
- Solid computational analysis and faster in practice on a sizable real dataset
- Released code/data; good practical impact potential

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical scope limited to a single domain and one strong baseline (KA)
- Some derivational/notation clarity issues; complexity claim for V-update needs justification
- Convergence properties of generalized EM not fully discussed; reliance on line search with repeated E-steps
- Scalability to large N not demonstrated

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Clarify dimensions in Lemma 1 and the construction of QYi; consider adding a concise derivation in the main text.
- Justify the O(n N k^2) cost for Eq. (20) or adjust the statement; describe numerical linear algebra used (Cholesky, caching).
- Provide convergence discussion for the generalized EM (monotonicity of L, conditions under which the algorithm converges to a stationary point).
- Add comparisons to at least one alternative learning approach (e.g., parametric Bayesian [8] or submodular baselines) and a synthetic recovery study.
- Discuss/experiment with low-rank variants and regularization to improve scalability and generalization.
- Report variance/error bars for likelihood improvements and more details about line-search parameters and stopping criteria.

Overall recommendation
Leaning accept. The paper presents a technically interesting and practically useful advance in DPP learning with clear benefits over a common baseline, though broader empirical validation and a few clarifications would strengthen it further.
