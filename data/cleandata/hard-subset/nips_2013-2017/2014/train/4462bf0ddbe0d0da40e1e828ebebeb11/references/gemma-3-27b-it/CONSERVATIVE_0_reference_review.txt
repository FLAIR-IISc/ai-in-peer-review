This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing the challenge of optimizing the likelihood function when the DPP kernel matrix L is unknown. The authors correctly identify the difficulties of gradient ascent due to the positive semi-definiteness constraint and propose an eigendecomposition-based approach to circumvent this issue. The core idea of working with the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of L, and introducing a hidden variable to facilitate an EM formulation, is reasonable. However, despite the clear presentation and motivation, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating a significant advance over existing methods, particularly given the complexity of the proposed approach.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, presenting the background, proposed method, and experimental results in a logical order. The mathematical derivations appear correct, and the authors acknowledge potential limitations, such as the computational cost of certain steps. The comparison to a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) is appropriate, and the explanation of why K-Ascent can struggle with near-diagonal solutions is insightful. However, the experimental evaluation feels insufficient to justify the added complexity of the EM algorithm.

The primary weakness of this work lies in the limited and somewhat contrived experimental setting. While the “baby registry” dataset is a relevant application, the gains achieved by EM over K-Ascent are relatively modest (an average of 3.7% improvement in log-likelihood), and highly dependent on the initialization strategy. The authors themselves acknowledge that when using a moment-matching initialization, the performance difference diminishes significantly. The claim of asymptotic runtime advantage is also not strongly substantiated—the runtime analysis is presented, but the practical impact on the datasets tested isn’t overwhelming. The discussion of the dependence on the parameter ‘d’ (measuring off-diagonal dominance of the kernel) feels somewhat *post-hoc*; it doesn’t explain why the EM algorithm *should* perform better in such settings, just that it *does* in the experiments.

The originality of the work is incremental. While the EM formulation is novel, it builds heavily on existing work in DPPs and EM algorithms. The use of eigendecomposition and the introduction of a hidden variable are not groundbreaking ideas in themselves; the contribution lies in their specific application to the DPP learning problem. Related work is reasonably well-cited, though a more thorough discussion of recent advances in kernel learning could be beneficial. 

The significance of this work is questionable. While the EM algorithm may offer some advantages in specific scenarios (e.g., data-poor settings, or when strong negative interactions are present), the modest gains and the complexity of the implementation make it unlikely to be widely adopted. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method solves a critical bottleneck in DPP learning or opens up new avenues for research. The practical impact feels limited, and it’s unclear whether the benefits outweigh the computational overhead.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in DPP learning.
*   The proposed EM algorithm is theoretically sound and avoids the pitfalls of gradient ascent.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental results are modest and highly dependent on initialization.
*   The practical significance of the work is unclear.
*   The computational complexity of the EM algorithm is relatively high.
*   The originality of the approach is incremental.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. 

While the paper presents a technically sound and well-written contribution, it fails to demonstrate a compelling advantage over existing methods. The limited experimental results and the complexity of the proposed approach do not justify its inclusion in NIPS at this time. I encourage the authors to explore more challenging datasets, to further analyze the runtime performance of the EM algorithm, and to provide a more compelling argument for its practical significance. The sensitivity to initialization is also a concern, and needs further investigation.
