**Summary:**
This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from observed subsets. The authors correctly identify that the standard maximum likelihood approach via projected gradient ascent is problematic, as the projection step needed to maintain positive semi-definiteness can prematurely truncate eigenvalues, leading to a degenerate, near-diagonal kernel that fails to model diversity. To overcome this, they propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The key idea is to work with the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix and introduce a latent variable corresponding to the selection of eigenvectors in the DPP's generative process. This formulation allows for a closed-form, projection-free update for the eigenvalues and a gradient-based update for the eigenvectors on the Stiefel manifold. The authors demonstrate experimentally on a baby registry dataset that their EM approach is more robust to initialization and faster than the projected gradient ascent baseline.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The problem of learning a full, non-parametric DPP kernel is important, and existing solutions are limited. The proposed EM-style algorithm is a novel and elegant approach to this problem. Bypassing the problematic projection step is a significant advantage that could make learning DPPs more practical and reliable.
2.  **Methodological Insight:** The formulation of the learning problem in the eigenspace and the use of the latent eigenvector selection variable `J` is insightful. Showing that the E-step posterior `q(J | Yi)` is itself a k-DPP is a clean and theoretically pleasing result.
3.  **Experimental Results:** The experiments, while limited to one dataset, effectively demonstrate the primary claims. The proposed EM algorithm shows clear improvements over the projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent), particularly with a non-informative initialization or in a data-poor setting. The runtime comparison also validates the asymptotic complexity advantage of the EM method.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, despite its strong central idea, suffers from a notable number of minor methodological gaps, notational inconsistencies, and a lack of clarity in key areas. These issues, in aggregate, detract from the quality and reproducibility of the work.

1.  **Methodological Rigor in the M-step:** The treatment of the eigenvector update in the M-step is the most significant weakness. The gradient formula in Equation (20) is only valid when `q = p`, i.e., at the very beginning of the M-step, before `V` is updated. The paper states, "in practice we do not bother to fully optimize V; we simply take a single gradient step on V." This is a critical heuristic. The algorithm is not performing a true maximization in the M-step, but rather a single approximate gradient step. This makes it a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm, a detail that is not mentioned. More importantly, the paper provides no justification for why taking a step in a direction derived from a stale gradient should lead to convergence or a good solution. The entire M-step for `V` rests on this undocumented approximation.

2.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:**
    *   **Supplement Reliance:** Several crucial derivations are relegated to a supplement, including the proof of Lemma 1, the derivation of the efficient marginal computation (Eq. 19), and the derivation of the eigenvector gradient (Eq. 20). While common, the paper feels incomplete without these, especially given the complexity of the formulas.
    *   **Step Size Search:** The procedure for finding the step size `η` for the `V` update is convoluted and glossed over. The authors state they perform an E-step for *each trial step size*. This blurs the clean separation of E and M steps and seems computationally burdensome, potentially undermining the claimed speed advantages in practice, although the complexity analysis accounts for it.
    *   **Ad-hoc Initialization:** The moment-matching initialization in Equation (23) is not well-motivated. The origin of the `sqrt(max(Kii*Kjj - mij, 0))` term is unclear and seems ad-hoc. A citation or a brief derivation justifying this form would be necessary.
    *   **Practical Details:** The paper mentions that if `λj = 1`, the `QYi` matrix is infinite, and that "we simply tighten the constraint on λ to keep it slightly below 1." This is a crucial implementation detail that is left vague. How is this implemented? Does it affect the solution?

3.  **Formatting and Notational Issues:**
    *   **References:** The reference list is improperly formatted. It lacks author names, publication venues, and years for most entries, making it nearly useless for a reader trying to follow up on prior work. This is a significant oversight for a submission at this level.
    *   **Notation:** The use of `>` for matrix transpose (e.g., `v > j`) is highly unconventional and can be confusing. Standard `T` notation is preferred. The description of `IY` in Equation (3) is awkward; a clearer definition would be beneficial. The constraint `K ≻ 0` in Equation (4) denotes strict positive definiteness, while the text mentions PSD (`≽`), which includes the zero matrix. This is a minor but noticeable inconsistency. The date "Tuesday, August 5, 14" is inexplicably present at the bottom of page 5.

4.  **Baseline Comparison:** The K-Ascent baseline might be a strawman. Its poor performance is attributed to the projection step, exacerbated by a simple backtracking line search (`η ← η/2`). A more sophisticated line search (e.g., satisfying Armijo conditions) might significantly improve KA's performance, potentially reducing the reported gap with EM.

**Recommendation:** Reject

While the core idea of an EM algorithm for DPP learning is novel and promising, the paper is marred by a substantial number of small-to-medium flaws. The lack of rigor in the M-step's eigenvector update is a major methodological concern. This, combined with unclear explanations of key procedures, unconventional notation, and profoundly sloppy formatting (especially the reference list), brings the overall quality of the manuscript below the high standard expected for NIPS. The work has potential, but it requires a significant revision to address these numerous issues before it can be considered a complete and sound scientific contribution.
