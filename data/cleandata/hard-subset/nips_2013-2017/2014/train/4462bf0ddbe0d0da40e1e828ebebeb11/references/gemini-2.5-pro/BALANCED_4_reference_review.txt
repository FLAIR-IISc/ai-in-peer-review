This paper tackles the important and challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. While DPPs are powerful models for capturing diversity, their application has been hampered by the difficulty of learning the kernel; direct maximum likelihood estimation is conjectured to be NP-hard, and prior work has relied on restricted parameterizations. The main contribution of this work is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning an unconstrained, non-parametric DPP kernel.

The key idea is to re-parameterize the kernel `K` via its eigendecomposition `K = VΛV^T` and leverage the generative view of DPP sampling, which involves first selecting a latent subset of eigenvectors `J` before sampling an observation `Y`. By treating `J` as a hidden variable, the authors derive an EM algorithm. The E-step involves computing the posterior `p(J|Y)`, which is elegantly shown to be a conditional k-DPP itself. The M-step then updates the eigenvalues `Λ` and eigenvectors `V`. A major advantage of this formulation is that it leads to a closed-form, projection-free update for the eigenvalues. The eigenvectors are updated using a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, which cleverly avoids the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent—a step the authors show can lead to degenerate, near-diagonal solutions that fail to model diversity. The proposed EM algorithm is evaluated against a projected gradient ascent baseline on a real-world product recommendation task using Amazon baby registry data, demonstrating that it is more robust, particularly with uninformative initializations or in data-poor settings, and also computationally faster.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental bottleneck in the practical use of DPPs. Providing an effective algorithm for learning the full kernel from data is a significant contribution that will likely be widely used by researchers and practitioners applying DPPs to tasks in subset selection, recommendation, and summarization.
*   **Originality and Quality:** The proposed EM algorithm is novel, elegant, and technically sound. The formulation using the eigendecomposition and the latent eigenvector set `J` is insightful. The derivation is clear and the theoretical result that the posterior `p(J|Y)` is a k-DPP (Lemma 1) is a nice contribution in its own right. The method's primary advantage—avoiding the projection step that degrades the performance of standard gradient ascent—is well-motivated and convincingly demonstrated.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for a new learning algorithm is established clearly by first explaining the pitfalls of the simpler projected gradient ascent approach. The step-by-step derivation of the EM algorithm is logical and transparent. The experiments are well-described and the results are presented clearly.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are thorough and persuasive. The comparison to a natural baseline (projected gradient ascent) on a real-world dataset is compelling. The authors wisely test multiple initialization strategies and demonstrate the robustness of their method. The analysis in the data-poor regime and the correlation of performance gains with a metric of data diversity (`d`) provide valuable insights into when the proposed method offers the greatest advantage. The inclusion of runtime comparisons and a qualitative example further strengthens the experimental section.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **M-step for Eigenvectors:** The M-step update for the eigenvectors `V` consists of a single gradient step rather than a full maximization, which technically makes the algorithm a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm. This is a pragmatic and common choice, but the paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion of this. In particular, the gradient in Eq. (20) is derived under the condition `q=p`, but it is subsequently used in the M-step where `q` is fixed from the preceding E-step. A brief justification for why this remains a good ascent direction for the lower bound `F` would be beneficial.
*   **Line Search for V:** The paper states that the line search for the eigenvector update step size involves checking for improvement in the full likelihood `L`. This implies re-running parts of the E-step within the M-step's line search, which could be computationally expensive. It would be helpful to clarify the exact procedure. Was a line search on the lower bound `F` (which is cheaper to evaluate, though intractable itself) also considered? A more detailed description in the supplement would be welcome.
*   **Scalability:** The algorithm's complexity contains an `O(N^3)` term due to the eigendecomposition and matrix exponential, which will be a bottleneck for applications with very large ground sets `N` (e.g., > 1000s of items). While the method is shown to be faster than the baseline for the tested `N`, it is worth briefly acknowledging this limitation and perhaps pointing towards future work that could incorporate kernel approximation techniques (e.g., Nyström, as in Affandi et al. [15]) to scale the eigenvector-related computations.
*   **Baseline Strength:** The K-Ascent baseline uses a simple line search. While its failure mode is well-explained, one might wonder if a more sophisticated constrained optimization method (e.g., acting on the Cholesky decomposition of K to enforce the PSD constraint) could serve as a stronger baseline. This is a minor point, as the elegance and robustness of the proposed EM method are compelling regardless, but acknowledging alternative optimization strategies could provide additional context.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance. It presents a high-quality, novel, and significant contribution to the machine learning literature on probabilistic modeling. The paper solves a key problem for an important model class (DPPs) with an elegant and effective algorithm. The technical details are sound, the presentation is crystal clear, and the experimental results are convincing. The identified weaknesses are minor and mostly pertain to requests for additional discussion and clarification, which do not detract from the overall excellence of the work. This paper will be of great interest to the NIPS community.
