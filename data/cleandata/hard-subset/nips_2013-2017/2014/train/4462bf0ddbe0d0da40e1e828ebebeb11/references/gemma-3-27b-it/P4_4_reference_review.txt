This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing the challenge of learning the kernel matrix L from example subsets, a problem conjectured to be NP-hard. Previous work often resorted to partial learning or restrictive parameterizations of L. The key idea is to leverage the eigendecomposition of L and formulate an EM algorithm that avoids the problematic projection step inherent in naive gradient ascent, which can lead to degenerate solutions (near-diagonal L, failing to capture diversity). The authors demonstrate asymptotic runtime improvements over gradient ascent.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear and mathematically well-founded derivation of the EM algorithm. The connection to eigendecomposition and the use of Jensen's inequality are justified and standard techniques. The derivation of the update rules for both eigenvalues and eigenvectors (albeit with simplifications for the eigenvector update) appears solid.
*   **Novelty:**  The EM approach for full, unconstrained learning of L in DPPs is a novel contribution. Existing methods either learn only partial parameters (e.g., scalar weights for rows of L) or rely on restrictive parameterizations.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The background section provides necessary context for understanding DPPs.  The motivation for avoiding the projection step in gradient ascent is clearly explained.
*   **Significance:** The ability to learn a non-parametric L without restrictive assumptions is significant. DPPs have applications in diverse fields, and a robust learning method could broaden their applicability. The empirical results showing improved performance and runtime over gradient ascent further support this.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Implementation:** While the algorithm is well-defined, the practical implementation appears non-trivial, particularly the handling of the eigenvector update and the Stiefel manifold constraint. The paper mentions simplifications, and a more detailed discussion of practical considerations (e.g., numerical stability) would be valuable.
*   **Empirical Evaluation Depth:** The experiments primarily focus on a single application (baby registry data). While the results are encouraging, the breadth of evaluation could be expanded to demonstrate the algorithm's generalizability to other domains. Further baselines beyond the K-Ascent method would also be helpful.
*   **Limited discussion of the role of the initial kernel:** The impact of initialization on EM convergence and final performance is extensively discussed. However, the justification for the two proposed initialization methods feels somewhat ad-hoc. A deeper analysis of the initialization’s effect on the learned kernel would strengthen the work.
*   **Asymptotic Runtime Discussion:** The claim of faster asymptotic runtime compared to gradient ascent relies on k << N, where k is the average size of the example subsets. This assumption is not always valid in real-world applications. A discussion on the practical implications of this assumption is warranted.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in DPP learning.
*   The EM algorithm offers significant advantages over existing methods, particularly in terms of robustness and asymptotic runtime.
*   The empirical results, while focused on a single application, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The practical implementation of the algorithm might be complex.
*   The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, covering a wider range of datasets and baselines.
*   The assumption underlying the asymptotic runtime analysis (k << N) might not hold in all scenarios.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of DPP learning. The proposed EM algorithm is a well-motivated and technically sound solution to a challenging problem. The empirical results are promising, and the potential impact of this work is significant. While some aspects of the implementation and evaluation could be further refined, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
