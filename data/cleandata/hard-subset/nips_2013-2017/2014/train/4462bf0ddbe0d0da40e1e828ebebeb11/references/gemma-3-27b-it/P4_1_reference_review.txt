The paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing the difficulty of directly maximizing the likelihood function due to its non-convexity and the need to maintain positive semi-definiteness of the kernel matrix L. The authors highlight issues with gradient ascent, specifically the tendency to learn nearly diagonal L matrices that fail to capture item diversity. Their approach leverages the eigendecomposition of L, introduces a hidden variable based on DPP sampling, and develops an iterative procedure to optimize a lower bound on the likelihood.

This work builds upon a substantial body of recent research on DPPs for various machine learning tasks (Kulesza & Taskar, 2012; Kulesza et al., 2012; Affandi et al., 2014). Previous approaches have included learning scalar weights for each row of L (Kulesza & Taskar, 2012), learning weights for a linear combination of DPPs (Affandi et al., 2014), or assuming a specific parametric form for L (e.g., Bayesian methods). This paper distinguishes itself by learning an unconstrained, non-parametric L without relying on projections that can lead to suboptimal solutions. The connection to eigendecomposition and the use of an EM algorithm are novel within this context.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the EM algorithm appears well-motivated and logically sound. The use of Jensen’s inequality and the exploitation of the eigendecomposition are clever. The explicit formulas for the M-step updates, particularly the eigenvalue update (Equation 19), are significant achievements, enabling efficient computation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, background, and proposed method. The notation is consistent, and the algorithmic descriptions (Algorithms 1 & 2) are easy to follow.
*   **Novelty:** The proposed EM algorithm represents a novel approach to learning DPPs without restrictive assumptions on L or requiring problematic projection steps. The utilization of a hidden variable based on DPP sampling to facilitate the EM framework is a key contribution.
*   **Significance:** The problem of efficiently learning DPPs is important, and the authors demonstrate empirical improvements over a gradient ascent baseline. The focus on maintaining diversity in subset selection makes this work relevant to a wide range of applications. The analysis of runtime complexity (O(T1nNk2 + T1T2N3)) suggests potential scalability advantages.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments on baby registry data are informative, the scope is relatively limited. Evaluating the method on more diverse datasets and comparing it to other recent DPP learning approaches (beyond K-Ascent) would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** Although the EM algorithm is claimed to be more robust to initialization, the paper dedicates significant attention to initialization strategies (Wishart vs. moment matching). This suggests that initialization remains a crucial factor, and a more thorough exploration of its impact would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Complexity Discussion:** The asymptotic runtime analysis is valuable, but it lacks a detailed discussion of the practical implications of the k parameter (representing the maximum subset size) and its potential impact on performance.
*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on algorithmic development and empirical evaluation. A more in-depth theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the EM algorithm and the conditions under which it achieves optimal solutions would be desirable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in DPP learning with a novel and technically sound approach.
*   The proposed EM algorithm demonstrates empirical improvements over a baseline method.
*   The derivations and algorithmic descriptions are clear and well-presented.
*   The work has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in DPP modeling and subset selection.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.
*   The reliance on specific initialization strategies indicates potential sensitivity to initial conditions.
*   The lack of more comprehensive theoretical analysis limits the understanding of the algorithm's behavior.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of DPP learning. The proposed EM algorithm is a novel and well-motivated approach that addresses a significant challenge in the area. While the experimental evaluation could be broadened and a more thorough theoretical analysis would be beneficial, the strengths of the work outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, including addressing the points raised regarding experimental scope and initialization sensitivity.
