### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. While DPPs are powerful models for capturing diversity in subset selection tasks, learning their parameters via maximum likelihood is a non-convex problem, conjectured to be NP-hard. Previous approaches have circumvented this difficulty by learning restricted versions of the kernel (e.g., only diagonal or quality terms), learning ensembles of fixed kernels, or assuming specific parametric forms.

This paper presents a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full, unconstrained marginal kernel K. The key insight is to leverage the eigendecomposition of K and view the DPP's generative process as a mixture of "elementary DPPs," where each component corresponds to a subset of eigenvectors. This introduces a latent variable—the chosen subset of eigenvectors—which naturally lends itself to an EM formulation. The authors derive the E-step, showing that the posterior over the latent variable is itself a k-DPP, and the M-step, which yields a closed-form update for the eigenvalues and a gradient-based update for the eigenvectors on the Stiefel manifold. The proposed EM method is compared against a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent), demonstrating superior performance in terms of robustness, final log-likelihood, and computational speed on a real-world product recommendation task.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The main contribution—framing DPP learning as an EM problem by introducing a latent variable over the eigenspace—is highly original and theoretically elegant. It provides a new and powerful perspective on the DPP likelihood function and connects it to the well-understood mixture model framework. This is a significant conceptual advance over prior learning approaches.

2.  **Addresses a Critical Flaw in a Naive Baseline:** The paper clearly identifies a major weakness of the straightforward projected gradient ascent approach: the projection step can prematurely collapse the kernel's eigenvalues to zero, resulting in a near-diagonal matrix that fails to model diversity. The proposed EM algorithm elegantly sidesteps this issue by providing a closed-form, projection-free update for the eigenvalues, making it inherently more robust.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments on the baby registry dataset are well-designed and compelling.
    *   The choice of a real-world dataset highlights the practical relevance of the problem.
    *   Comparing two distinct initializations (uninformative Wishart vs. data-informed moments-matching) effectively showcases the superior robustness of the EM algorithm, especially when starting from a poor initial guess.
    *   The data-poor setting experiment further reinforces EM's advantages.
    *   The qualitative example of the three baby monitors provides a clear, intuitive illustration of *why* learning a good diversity model matters and how the baseline fails.
    *   The runtime comparison confirms the theoretical asymptotic advantage of the EM approach.

4.  **Technical Soundness:** The derivations appear solid. The identification of the posterior in the E-step as a k-DPP (Lemma 1) is a key result that makes the algorithm tractable. The M-step updates are well-motivated, with a closed-form solution for the eigenvalues and a principled approach using Stiefel manifold optimization for the eigenvectors.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Approximation in the M-step for Eigenvectors:** The paper notes that the gradient for the eigenvectors V (Eq. 20) is only valid at the beginning of the M-step, and thus a single gradient step is taken. This is a reasonable heuristic (essentially a Generalized EM step), but its theoretical implications are not fully discussed. It is unclear how this approximation affects convergence guarantees or the quality of the final solution compared to a full maximization of the M-step objective. A more detailed justification would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Scalability Remains a Challenge:** While the proposed EM algorithm is asymptotically faster than the K-Ascent baseline for `k << N`, it still contains an `O(N^3)` complexity in the eigenvector update step (due to the matrix exponential for the Stiefel manifold update). This remains a significant bottleneck for applications with very large ground sets (large N), which is a common scenario for DPPs. The paper could be strengthened by acknowledging this limitation and briefly discussing potential avenues for scaling, such as using approximations for the matrix exponential or low-rank assumptions on K.

3.  **Dependence on Set Sizes:** The complexity of the algorithm, `O(T1*n*N*k^2 + T1*T2*N^3)`, depends on `k = max_i |Y_i|`. This could be problematic for datasets where the observed subsets have a very large range of sizes, or are large themselves. While not an issue for the chosen dataset, this dependency is a practical limitation worth noting.

### Detailed Comments

*   The intuition behind Lemma 1 is very important. While the proof is rightly left to the supplement, a single sentence in the main text providing some intuition as to *why* the posterior over J is a k-DPP would be helpful for the reader.
*   In the M-step, the authors state they update q's distribution for each trial step size to check for improvement in the true likelihood L. This is a good practical choice but means the "M-step" involves re-evaluating parts of the E-step. This blurs the lines of a classic EM algorithm but is a sensible approach. Clarifying this as a variant (e.g., an EM-like procedure with a line search on the true objective) might improve precision.
*   The connection made between the performance gain of EM and the off-diagonal "strength" of the data (measured by `d`) is an excellent piece of analysis that provides insight into *when* the proposed method will be most impactful.

### Assessment

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound, the claims are well-supported by both theory and strong experimental results, and the authors are careful to explain the limitations of the baseline they are comparing against. This is a complete and well-executed piece of research.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is very well-written, organized logically, and easy to follow. The background is concise but sufficient, and the progression from the baseline method to the development of the EM algorithm is clear. The notation is consistent.
*   **Originality:** High. The core idea of using an EM algorithm on the eigendecomposition of the DPP kernel is, to my knowledge, completely novel. It provides a new and effective tool for a fundamental problem in the area, clearly distinguishing itself from prior work.
*   **Significance:** High. Learning the DPP kernel is a central and difficult problem. This work provides a practical, robust, and often faster algorithm than the most obvious alternative. Given the increasing popularity of DPPs in machine learning for modeling diversity, this contribution is likely to be widely used and built upon by other researchers. It demonstrably advances the state-of-the-art in learning DPPs from data.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and elegant core idea, backed by solid theory and convincing experiments. It solves an important problem in a satisfying way. I recommend acceptance.
