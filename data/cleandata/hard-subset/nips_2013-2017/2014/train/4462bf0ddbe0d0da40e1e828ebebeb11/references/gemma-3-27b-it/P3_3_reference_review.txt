## Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix *L* for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) from observed subsets.  Directly maximizing the likelihood is conjectured to be NP-hard, and existing approaches rely on partial learning or restrictive parameterizations of *L*. The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that leverages the eigendecomposition of *L* to circumvent the difficulties encountered with naive gradient ascent (specifically, the need for potentially destabilizing projections to maintain positive semi-definiteness). The core idea is to introduce a hidden variable representing the eigenvectors used to form a subset, allowing for a tractable lower bound on the likelihood and a coordinate ascent optimization procedure.  The paper provides a detailed algorithmic description, theoretical justification, and empirical validation on a product recommendation task using baby registry data.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing DPP learning literature.  It acknowledges the limitations of scalar weighting approaches [2], L-ensemble methods [3], and Bayesian methods [8].  The strength of this work lies in avoiding those limitations by tackling the full *L* matrix learning problem without strong parametric assumptions.  The connection to the eigendecomposition and its use in defining a generative process, reminiscent of work on k-DPPs [3], is well-articulated. The authors acknowledge and build upon previous work in DPP sampling and marginalization [21] to make their algorithm efficient.  

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The EM approach is a genuinely novel contribution to DPP learning.  Formulating the problem in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors, and then cleverly introducing a hidden variable to enable EM, is a significant insight.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the EM algorithm, including the lower bound on the likelihood and the updates for both eigenvalues and eigenvectors, appears mathematically sound and well-motivated.  The connection to Jensen’s inequality is clearly explained.
* **Practical Advantages:**  The authors convincingly demonstrate that the EM algorithm avoids the pitfalls of gradient ascent (particularly the problematic projections). The asymptotic runtime analysis suggests a potential advantage over gradient-based methods for larger datasets.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the baby registry data provide compelling evidence of the EM algorithm’s effectiveness, demonstrating both improved log-likelihood and the ability to generate more sensible recommendations compared to the K-Ascent baseline.  The analysis of the dataset characteristics (parameter *d*) and their correlation with the EM’s gains is insightful.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations are clearly presented, and the algorithmic steps are easy to follow.  The inclusion of algorithm pseudocode is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the theoretical description is clear, implementing the EM algorithm, particularly the step involving the Stiefel manifold update (even with simplifications), is likely to be non-trivial.  A publicly available, well-documented codebase would significantly strengthen the impact of this work.
* **Scalability Concerns (Beyond Asymptotic Analysis):**  The experiments are performed on datasets of moderate size (N = 71). While the asymptotic analysis is favorable, the practical scalability of the algorithm to truly large datasets (N > 1000) remains an open question.  The O(nNk²) term in the runtime, while potentially better than gradient ascent, could still be a bottleneck.
* **Sensitivity to Initialization:**  While the EM algorithm is more robust than K-Ascent, the choice of initialization still seems important.  The comparison of Wishart and moments-matching initialization suggests that the latter is preferable, but the paper doesn't delve into the robustness of the algorithm to *different* moments-matching initializations, or the impact of initialization on convergence.
* **Limited Baselines:** The comparison is primarily to K-Ascent. Comparing against more sophisticated DPP learning methods (if any exist, given the challenges of the problem) would further strengthen the evaluation.  
* **Discussion of Hyperparameters:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameters used in the experiments (e.g., step size η, number of EM iterations) and their impact on performance.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound solution to a challenging problem in DPP learning.
* The EM algorithm avoids the limitations of existing approaches and demonstrates empirical improvements.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a compelling justification for its contributions.
* The insights into the relationship between dataset characteristics and algorithm performance are valuable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The implementation complexity could be a barrier to adoption.
* Scalability to very large datasets is not fully demonstrated.
* The reliance on initialization and the limited comparison to other baselines warrant further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of DPP learning. The proposed EM algorithm is a clever and effective solution to a challenging problem. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them.  I believe that this work is well-suited for NIPS and has the potential to be highly impactful.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend that the authors address the following points in a revised version:

*   Provide more details on the implementation of the Stiefel manifold update and its computational cost.
*   Discuss the scalability of the algorithm to larger datasets and provide some preliminary results or analysis on this topic.
*   Explore the sensitivity of the algorithm to different initialization schemes and provide guidelines for choosing a suitable initialization.
*   Consider adding a comparison to other relevant DPP learning methods, if available.
*   Include a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameters used in the experiments.
