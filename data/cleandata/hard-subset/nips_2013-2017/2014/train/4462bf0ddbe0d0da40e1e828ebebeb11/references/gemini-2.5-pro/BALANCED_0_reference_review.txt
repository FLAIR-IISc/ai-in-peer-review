### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. Maximum likelihood estimation for a general, unconstrained DPP kernel is conjectured to be NP-hard. Prior work has circumvented this by learning restricted or parametric forms of the kernel. A naive approach using projected gradient ascent on the kernel matrix can suffer from poor local optima, particularly by collapsing to near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture the diversity-promoting nature of DPPs.

The main contribution of this paper is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for this learning problem. The key idea is to reparameterize the DPP kernel `K` via its eigendecomposition (`K = VΛV^T`) and to leverage the view of a DPP as a mixture of "elementary" DPPs. This introduces a latent variable `J` that selects a subset of eigenvectors. The authors then derive an EM algorithm to optimize the eigenvalues (`Λ`) and eigenvectors (`V`). The E-step involves computing the posterior over the latent variable `J` given an observed subset `Y`, which is shown to be a conditional DPP itself. The M-step then updates the parameters: the eigenvalue update has a closed-form solution, while the eigenvector update is handled by taking a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, which elegantly enforces the orthogonality constraint without a problematic projection step.

Experiments on a real-world baby registry dataset demonstrate that the proposed EM algorithm is more robust to initialization and more effective in data-poor settings compared to a projected gradient ascent baseline. Furthermore, the EM algorithm is shown to be asymptotically and empirically faster.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel and elegant EM-based approach to the important and difficult problem of learning an unconstrained DPP kernel. This is a significant step forward from prior work, which was limited to restricted parameterizations or ensembles. By providing a more robust method for learning the full kernel, this work can enable wider and more effective application of DPPs in areas like recommendation and summarization.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical derivation is sound and clever. The formulation using the latent variable `J` (the set of chosen eigenvectors) is insightful and provides a natural path to an EM algorithm. The characterization of the posterior `p(J|Y)` as a *k*-DPP (Lemma 1) is a key result that makes the E-step tractable. The M-step updates are well-motivated, particularly the use of optimization on the Stiefel manifold for the eigenvectors, which cleverly sidesteps the projection issues that plague the baseline method.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It provides a clear motivation, a concise background on DPPs, and a step-by-step derivation of the algorithm. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the intuition behind the technical details, such as the failure mode of projected gradient ascent and the generative view of DPPs that underpins their EM approach.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. The comparison to a projected gradient ascent baseline is appropriate. The use of both a naive (Wishart) and a data-driven (moments-matching) initialization effectively highlights the proposed method's superior robustness. The analysis in a data-scarce setting further strengthens this claim. The qualitative example from the baby registry data provides a compelling illustration of *why* learning a better diversity model matters in practice. The inclusion of runtime comparisons, which confirm the theoretical asymptotic advantage, is also a welcome addition.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **M-step for Eigenvectors:** The treatment of the eigenvector (`V`) update could be clarified. The authors state they take a single gradient step, which suggests this is a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm. The line search for the step size `η` is described as checking for improvement in the true log-likelihood `L`, which requires re-running the E-step for each trial `η`. This seems computationally expensive and might slow down the M-step considerably. Could the authors clarify the exact procedure used in the experiments? For instance, is a simpler heuristic like a fixed or decaying step size used, or is the full line search performed? A more detailed description in the supplement would be beneficial.

2.  **Scalability:** The algorithm's complexity contains an `O(N^3)` term per iteration due to the eigendecomposition and the matrix exponential for the `V` update. This makes the approach challenging for problems with a very large ground set size `N`. While this is a common limitation for kernel-based methods and DPPs, the paper would be stronger if it briefly acknowledged this limitation and perhaps pointed towards potential future work for scaling, such as using low-rank approximations for the kernel `K`, which has been explored in other DPP contexts (e.g., [15]).

3.  **Local Optima:** As with any EM algorithm, the proposed method finds a local optimum of the likelihood function. The experiments convincingly show that it finds *better* local optima than the projected gradient ascent baseline. However, a brief discussion of the non-convex nature of the problem and the potential for getting stuck in poor local optima (even if it is less likely than with the baseline) would make the paper more complete.

4.  **Minor Clarification on `k`:** The computational complexity is given in terms of `k = max_i |Y_i|`. For the baby registry dataset, the subsets were filtered to be between 5 and 100 items. This suggests `k` could be up to 100, which is larger than `N` (average `N=71`). This seems counterintuitive. I assume the experiments were run on the category-specific sub-registries *after* filtering the ground set, so `k` would be smaller than `N` for each category. Clarifying the range of `k` relative to `N` in the experimental setup would be helpful.

### Decision

This is a strong paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound algorithm for a significant problem in the DPP literature. The writing is clear, and the experimental results are convincing. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to requests for clarification or discussion of limitations, rather than fundamental flaws. The work is a solid contribution that is likely to be influential for researchers and practitioners working with DPPs. I recommend acceptance.
