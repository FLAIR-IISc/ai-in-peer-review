### Summary of the paper

This paper addresses the problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. Learning the full, unconstrained kernel is a notoriously difficult problem, conjectured to be NP-hard. Previous work has circumvented this by learning restricted versions, such as learning scalar quality weights for items [2], learning a mixture of fixed DPPs [3], or assuming a specific parametric form for the kernel [8].

The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for this learning task. The key idea is to re-parameterize the marginal kernel `K` via its eigendecomposition `K = VΛV^T` and to leverage the view of a DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs. In this formulation, an observed set `Y` is generated by first selecting a subset of eigenvectors, indexed by a latent set `J`, and then sampling `Y` from the elementary DPP defined by `J`.

The proposed EM algorithm treats `J` as the hidden variable.
- In the E-step, the algorithm computes the posterior distribution `p(J|Y)` over the latent eigenvector sets for each observed subset `Y`. The authors show that this posterior is itself a conditional `k-DPP` and provide an explicit construction for its kernel.
- In the M-step, the algorithm updates the eigenvalues `Λ` and eigenvectors `V` to maximize the expected log-likelihood under the posterior from the E-step. The paper derives a closed-form, projection-free update for the eigenvalues. For the eigenvectors, a gradient step is taken on the Stiefel manifold to preserve the orthogonality constraint, again avoiding a potentially problematic projection.

The authors compare their EM algorithm to a projected gradient ascent baseline on the kernel entries (`K-Ascent`). Experiments on a product recommendation dataset derived from baby registries show that the proposed EM method is more robust, particularly with non-informative initializations or in data-poor settings, and is also computationally faster.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the problem within the EM framework is elegant and well-justified.
- **Strength:** A core technical contribution is the characterization of the posterior `p(J|Y)` as a tractable `k-DPP` (Lemma 1), which makes the E-step feasible.
- **Strength:** The derivation of the M-step updates is a significant strength. The closed-form update for eigenvalues (Eq. 18) and the use of a Stiefel manifold update for eigenvectors (Eq. 21) are principled ways to handle the parameter constraints (`0 <= λ <= 1` and `V^T V = I`). This contrasts favorably with the naive projection step in the baseline, which the authors convincingly argue can lead to poor local optima (e.g., near-diagonal kernels that fail to model diversity).
- **Weakness:** The experimental validation, while compelling, is limited. The comparison is made only against the projected gradient ascent baseline. Including comparisons to other existing methods, such as the parametric Bayesian approach of Affandi et al. [8], would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's performance and situate it more broadly within the literature. The experiments are also restricted to a single application domain.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized.
- **Strength:** The motivation for a new learning algorithm is established clearly in the introduction by highlighting the shortcomings of prior work and the baseline gradient ascent method. The background section provides the necessary context on DPPs and their eigendecomposition. The step-by-step derivation of the EM algorithm is easy to follow.
- **Weakness:** A few technical details could be clarified in the main text. For instance, the paper states that the eigenvector gradient formula (Eq. 20) is only valid for the first gradient step of the M-step. The reason for this (that `q=p` only at the beginning of the M-step) and the implications for the algorithm (i.e., taking only a single gradient step per M-step) are important practical details that could be stated more explicitly. The description of the line search for the eigenvector update, which relies on the true likelihood `L` rather than the auxiliary function `F`, is also a subtle but important point that deviates from a pure coordinate ascent on `F`.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.
- **Strength:** To my knowledge, this is the first application of an EM algorithm to learn the full, unconstrained kernel of a DPP. The choice of the latent variable `J` (the set of active eigenvectors) is novel and insightful, directly connecting the learning process to the generative structure of DPPs. This provides a new and powerful perspective on the learning problem.

**Significance:** The paper makes a significant contribution to the field.
- **Strength:** It addresses a fundamental, open problem in the application of DPPs: learning the kernel from data. By providing a more robust and faster algorithm, this work has the potential to broaden the applicability of DPPs in machine learning, particularly in areas like recommendation systems where modeling diversity is crucial.
- **Strength:** The empirical results demonstrate a clear advantage over a natural baseline, especially in challenging scenarios (poor initialization, limited data). The qualitative example in Figure 2a, showing the baseline's tendency to select three similar baby monitors while EM selects a more diverse set, is an effective illustration of the practical impact of the proposed method.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Pro:**
1.  **Novelty:** The paper introduces a novel and elegant EM formulation for learning an unconstrained DPP kernel, a significant and difficult problem.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The method is well-founded, avoiding the problematic projection steps required by naive gradient ascent by using closed-form and manifold-based updates.
3.  **Empirical Performance:** The algorithm is shown to be more robust and computationally faster than a strong and natural baseline on a real-world dataset.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Con:**
1.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The experiments compare against only one baseline method. A broader comparison would strengthen the claims.
2.  **Scalability:** Like the baseline, the method involves `O(N^3)` computations, limiting its application to problems with a moderately sized ground set `N`. A discussion of this limitation would be beneficial.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novelty and technical quality in addressing a fundamental problem, significantly outweigh its weaknesses. It represents a clear and important advance in the study of determinantal point processes.
