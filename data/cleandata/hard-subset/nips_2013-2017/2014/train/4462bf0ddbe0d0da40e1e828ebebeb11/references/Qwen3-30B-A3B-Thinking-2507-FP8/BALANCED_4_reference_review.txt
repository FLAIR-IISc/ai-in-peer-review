

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper addresses the important problem of learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) from example subsets. DPPs are widely used for subset selection tasks requiring both quality and diversity (e.g., product recommendation, document summarization). The authors note that learning the kernel matrix L (or marginal kernel K) by maximum likelihood is challenging, as gradient ascent methods require problematic projection steps that often produce degenerate results (near-diagonal L). Previous work has either made restrictive assumptions about L (e.g., [2, 3, 8]) or used Bayesian methods that don't learn unconstrained L. The authors propose an EM-based algorithm that exploits the eigendecomposition of K to avoid projection steps, demonstrating improved robustness and asymptotic runtime.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness and Novelty:** The paper presents a theoretically grounded approach that effectively avoids the projection step required by gradient ascent. The derivation of the EM algorithm (Sections 3.2-3.5) is thorough, with clear mathematical justification for each step. The authors successfully connect the DPP's eigendecomposition to the EM framework, which is a novel contribution compared to previous work on DPP learning.

2. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on a real-world baby registry dataset (29,632 registries across 13 categories) provide strong empirical evidence of the method's advantages. The results consistently show EM outperforming K-Ascent (the baseline gradient approach) in log-likelihood (average 3.7% gain with Wishart initialization, up to 16.5% in data-poor settings) and runtime (EM is 2.1x faster on average). The analysis correlating performance gains with the degree of negative interactions (d metric) is insightful.

3. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear mathematical derivations, precise algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-2), and well-structured sections. The relationship between K and L, the eigendecomposition, and the EM framework is explained with remarkable clarity, making the paper accessible to DPP researchers.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper only compares to K-Ascent (gradient ascent with projection), but doesn't benchmark against other DPP learning approaches like Bayesian methods [8] or the L-ensemble approach [3]. It would strengthen the contribution to show how EM compares to these alternatives, particularly since [8] uses a different learning paradigm.

2. **Initialization Sensitivity:** While the paper demonstrates that EM is more robust than K-Ascent to initialization, it doesn't thoroughly explore how sensitive EM itself is to initialization. The two initialization methods (Wishart and moment matching) are presented as "naive" and "better," but the paper doesn't quantify the impact of different initializations on EM's performance or convergence.

3. **Practical Complexity Considerations:** The asymptotic runtime analysis (O(T1nNk² + T1T2N³)) is sound, but the paper doesn't discuss the practical implications of the O(N³) term (from the Stiefel manifold update). For large N, this could still be a bottleneck, and the paper doesn't provide guidance on how to handle very large-scale problems.

4. **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper demonstrates the algorithm's practical advantages but lacks theoretical convergence guarantees for the EM algorithm in this specific context. While the M-step updates for eigenvalues are closed-form and the E-step is well-defined, the paper doesn't prove that the EM algorithm converges to a local optimum or discuss potential issues with local optima.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically strong paper that makes a clear contribution to DPP learning. The EM approach is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated on a relevant dataset. The paper addresses a genuine limitation of previous work (degenerate solutions from projection steps) and provides a theoretically sound alternative. The quality of writing and mathematical presentation is excellent.

The main limitations are the narrow comparison to existing methods and the lack of thorough discussion on EM's sensitivity to initialization. These are important concerns for the broader community, as they affect how widely the method can be applied.

**Recommendation:**

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the learning of DPPs that addresses a real limitation in prior work. The method is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and empirically validated. While the comparison to other DPP learning approaches could be expanded, the paper's core contribution is substantial and clearly articulated. The work is clearly within NIPS scope (DPPs have been featured in multiple NIPS papers [8, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 19, 2, 3, 20]) and makes a demonstrable advance over the current state of the art.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Add a comparison to Bayesian DPP learning approaches (e.g., [8]) to better position the contribution.
2. Include a brief analysis of EM's sensitivity to different initialization methods.
3. Discuss potential limitations for very large-scale applications (N > 10,000) and how the O(N³) term might be addressed.
4. Add a short discussion of theoretical convergence properties of the EM algorithm in this context.
