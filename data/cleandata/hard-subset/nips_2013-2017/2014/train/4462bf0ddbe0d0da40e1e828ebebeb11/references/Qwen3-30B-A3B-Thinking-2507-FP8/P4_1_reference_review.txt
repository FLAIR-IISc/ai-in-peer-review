

# Review: Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes

This paper addresses the problem of learning the kernel matrix for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) from example subsets, a task that has been recognized as computationally challenging. The authors propose an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that leverages the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K to avoid the problematic projection step required in previous gradient-based approaches. The work builds upon prior NIPS contributions on DPPs, including Kulesza and Taskar's foundational work on DPPs for machine learning [5, 15] and recent advances in DPP learning [8, 11, 12, 13, 14, 16, 18, 19, 20].

The main contribution is a novel EM algorithm for learning unconstrained DPPs. The authors show that by recognizing the hidden variable J (the set of selected eigenvectors) in the DPP generative process, they can develop an EM scheme that avoids the projection issues of gradient ascent methods. The key insight is that the E-step results in a k-DPP, which can be efficiently computed, and the M-step provides closed-form updates for eigenvalues and efficient gradient steps for eigenvectors using Stiefel manifold optimization.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous derivations for the EM algorithm. The authors carefully analyze the E-step and M-step updates, providing closed-form solutions for eigenvalues and efficient gradient computations for eigenvectors. The theoretical analysis is supported by empirical results on a real-world baby registry dataset (13 categories with 71 products on average), comparing the EM approach to projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent). The paper thoroughly addresses the limitations of gradient ascent, particularly how projection leads to degenerate solutions that fail to model diversity. The experimental design is sound, with proper training/test splits and multiple initialization methods (Wishart and moment matching) to evaluate robustness. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the initialization sensitivity of both methods, and provides a meaningful analysis of when EM provides the greatest advantage (when off-diagonal components are important).

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem and relates it to prior work. The background section concisely covers necessary DPP concepts, including the L-K relationship and eigendecomposition properties. The algorithm development is methodical, with each step building logically on the previous one. The experimental section provides sufficient detail about the dataset, initialization methods, and evaluation metrics. The algorithms are clearly presented with appropriate mathematical notation. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational complexity analysis for the M-step updates, but the existing treatment is sufficient for understanding the main contributions.

**Originality**: The proposed EM approach for learning unconstrained DPPs represents a significant departure from prior work. Previous methods either restricted the form of L (e.g., [2, 8]) or used ensemble methods that lost DPP properties (e.g., [3]). The paper clearly articulates how their approach differs from these prior methods and provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for their algorithm. The use of the eigendecomposition to develop an EM algorithm is novel, as is the analysis of how projection steps in gradient ascent lead to degenerate solutions. While DPPs have been studied extensively at NIPS (e.g., [5, 15, 18, 19, 20]), this specific learning approach appears to be new to the literature.

**Significance**: The problem of learning DPPs has broad applicability in subset selection tasks (recommendation systems, document summarization, sensor placement). The authors demonstrate significant practical improvements on a real-world product recommendation task, with EM achieving 3.7% higher test log-likelihood than gradient ascent on average. The paper also shows that EM is more robust to initialization and provides a 2.1x speedup in runtime. The analysis of when EM provides the greatest benefit (when negative interactions between items are important) provides valuable insight for practitioners. This work advances the state of the art in DPP learning by providing a more robust and efficient method that does not require restrictive assumptions about the kernel.

**Strengths**:
- Novel EM algorithm for unconstrained DPP learning
- Rigorous theoretical derivation with clear closed-form updates
- Thorough experimental validation on a real-world dataset
- Clear analysis of when the method provides the most benefit
- Demonstrated runtime improvements over gradient ascent
- Careful treatment of initialization and degeneracy issues

**Weaknesses**:
- Limited discussion of scalability to extremely large datasets (N > 1000)
- The moment-matching initialization is not perfect and still has limitations
- The paper could provide more intuition about why the EM approach avoids the projection issues
- The theoretical analysis of convergence properties is limited to empirical observations

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments**:
*Pro*: The paper presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically effective method for learning DPPs without restrictive assumptions. The results on a real-world dataset demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods, with improved likelihood, robustness, and efficiency. The work addresses a well-motivated problem with broad applicability in machine learning.

*Con*: The paper does not thoroughly address scalability to very large datasets, which is a common concern in machine learning applications. The experimental evaluation, while thorough for the given dataset size, does not explore the performance on much larger scale problems.

**Conclusion**: This paper makes a significant contribution to the learning of Determinantal Point Processes by developing a novel EM algorithm that avoids the limitations of gradient-based approaches. The theoretical development is sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the work has clear practical significance. The paper addresses a well-known challenge in DPP learning and provides a robust, efficient solution. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.
