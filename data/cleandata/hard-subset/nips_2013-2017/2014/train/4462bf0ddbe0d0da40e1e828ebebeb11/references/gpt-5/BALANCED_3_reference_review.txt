Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles maximum-likelihood learning of determinantal point processes (DPPs) without a restrictive parametric form. The key contribution is an EM-style algorithm that operates in the eigenspace of the DPP marginal kernel K, introducing a hidden variable J indexing the subset of eigenvectors used in an “elementary DPP” mixture representation. The E-step sets q(J|Y) to the true posterior pK(J|Y) and shows it is a k-DPP (fixed-size DPP) with a derived kernel. The M-step splits into updates for eigenvalues and eigenvectors: (i) eigenvalues admit a simple, self-normalizing update requiring the computation of k-DPP inclusion marginals; and (ii) eigenvectors are updated by a single (generalized EM) gradient step on the Stiefel manifold using a multiplicative update with matrix exponential. The method avoids the projection-induced degeneracy that plagues naive projected gradient ascent on K. Complexity is O(T1 n N k^2 + T1 T2 N^3), with k = max|Yi|, potentially better than O(T1 n N^3 + T1 T2 N^3) for projected gradient (KA), especially when k ≪ N.

This work builds on the standard DPP foundations (Macchi; Hough et al.; Kulesza & Taskar’s tutorials). It relates to prior learning approaches: convex item-quality learning (Kulesza & Taskar, UAI), mixtures of fixed L-ensembles (Lin & Bilmes, UAI), and Bayesian parametric learning (Affandi et al., ICML), but differs by directly optimizing an unconstrained nonparametric kernel via EM in the eigenspace. The use of k-DPPs in the E-step leverages prior work on fixed-size DPPs (Kulesza & Taskar, ICML). From the NeurIPS perspective, the paper is well within scope (probabilistic models, discrete subset selection, structured ML) and connects to earlier NeurIPS results on DPP inference and MAP (e.g., Gillenwater et al.).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: An elegant EM formulation using the elementary DPP mixture view and a hidden eigen-index set J. The E-step as a k-DPP is insightful and leads to efficient marginals.
- Algorithmic practicality: Avoids hard PSD/eigenvalue projections that can cause degeneracy; handles orthogonality constraints with a principled Stiefel update. Clear complexity analysis showing advantage when k ≪ N.
- Solid derivations: The gradient w.r.t. K for the KA baseline and the initial eigenvector gradient (Eq. 20) are clearly presented; the eigenvalue update (Eq. 18–19) is simple and self-normalizing.
- Empirical evidence: On a sizable, real recommendation dataset (13 categories, median N ≈ 71, n ≈ 8.6k), EM improves test log-likelihood over KA with naive Wishart init (median ~3.7%), remains more robust in data-poor regimes (n ≈ 2N), and is faster (~2.1×), with a qualitative example illustrating diversity.
- Relevance: DPP learning is important for diverse subset selection (summarization, search, sensor placement, recommendation); improvements to robustness and speed are practically meaningful.

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines: KA is a fairly weak comparator. Stronger alternatives (e.g., optimizing eigenvalues via unconstrained parameters λ = σ(θ) to avoid projections, manifold optimization over V with better line-search, proximal/mirror descent, or low-rank factorizations) could better test the claim that projection is the main culprit in degeneracy.
- Incomplete EM monotonicity discussion: The M-step does not fully maximize F; instead, a single generalized EM step is taken and step sizes are chosen by checking L after re-running the E-step. While GEM can still guarantee non-decreasing likelihood, this distinction and conditions for monotonicity are not made explicit.
- Clarity/notation issues: The construction of q’s k-DPP kernel (Eqs. 16–17) has dimensional ambiguities (e.g., ZYi = UYi(UYi)ᵀ appears |Yi|×|Yi| while R is N×N; how is QYi formed as N×N?). The derivation and conditions under which Eq. (19) reduces to a simple sum of squared eigenvector entries for k-DPP marginals should be clarified (k-DPP marginals generally depend on eigenvalues via elementary symmetric polynomials; the projection case makes this form plausible but should be stated).
- Scope of experiments: Only one domain. No synthetic results to test recovery when ground-truth K is known, quantify sensitivity to initialization, or measure how close KA gets to diagonal in controlled settings. No downstream utility metrics (e.g., diversity/coverage or user-centered measures) beyond likelihood.
- Scalability limits: The N^3 term due to manifold steps (matrix exponential/eigendecompositions) could be a bottleneck for large N; the paper would benefit from discussion of low-rank or partial-eigendecomposition variants.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The main ideas are sound and well-motivated. The E-step result and efficient eigenvalue update are particularly compelling. Some details (k-DPP marginal formula; dimensional consistency of QYi) should be tightened. The GEM nature and convergence properties need explicit discussion.
- Empirics: Credible and supportive but somewhat narrow; stronger baselines and broader datasets would increase confidence.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with useful algorithms and complexity comparisons. However, fix notation/dimension issues in Sec. 3.4–3.5, and better explain the implementation details of the manifold update and line search.

Originality
- The EM formulation in eigenspace with a k-DPP posterior is original and non-trivial relative to prior DPP learning approaches.

Significance
- Likely to influence how practitioners train general DPPs, improving robustness and speed. The approach may spur further work on scalable GEM/variational methods for DPPs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen baselines: include (i) eigenvalue parameterization λ = σ(θ) with unconstrained optimization; (ii) manifold optimization on V with more steps per M-step; (iii) low-rank parameterizations.
- Clarify the E-step kernel construction and provide a concise derivation of Eq. (19), specifying assumptions (e.g., projection kernel) under which the sum-of-squares form holds.
- State the algorithm as a generalized EM, and provide a monotonicity argument (conditions under which each outer iteration increases L), or report empirical monotonicity plots.
- Add synthetic experiments with known ground-truth K to evaluate recovery, and ablations on initialization sensitivity and k/N scaling.
- Discuss computational tricks for large N (low-rank approximations, partial eigen-updates, approximate manifold steps).

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and principled EM approach for nonparametric DPP learning.
- Clear computational benefits and improved robustness over a standard baseline.
- Techniques (k-DPP posterior, Stiefel updates) are of broad methodological interest.

Arguments against acceptance
- Baseline comparison may be too weak to fully substantiate claims.
- Some derivations and notational details need clarification.
- Limited experimental scope and scalability discussion.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper presents a neat and useful advance in DPP learning with promising empirical gains and a principled algorithmic framework. Addressing the clarity points and strengthening baselines would further solidify the contribution.
