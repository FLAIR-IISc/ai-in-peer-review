### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. The standard maximum likelihood estimation problem is challenging due to its non-convexity and the positive semi-definite (PSD) constraint on the kernel matrix. The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to solve this problem. The key idea is to leverage the eigendecomposition of the kernel and view the DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs, which introduces a natural latent variable corresponding to a selection of eigenvectors. The authors derive the E-step, which involves computing the posterior over this latent variable, and the M-step, which updates the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the kernel. For comparison, they introduce a baseline method based on projected gradient ascent. Experiments on a baby registry dataset show that the proposed EM algorithm is more robust and computationally faster than this baseline, particularly when initialization is poor or data is scarce.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its derivation of the EM algorithm. The formulation, which introduces a latent variable based on the mixture representation of a DPP, is clever and leads to a principled optimization algorithm. The M-step updates are derived carefully, with a closed-form solution for the eigenvalues and a gradient-based update on the Stiefel manifold for the eigenvectors. This is a more sophisticated approach than a simple projection.

However, the overall quality of the work as a scientific contribution is severely hampered by its experimental evaluation. The central weakness is the choice of baseline. The paper's primary comparison is against "K-Ascent" (KA), a projected gradient ascent method that the authors introduce themselves. While serving as a useful ablation, this method is not an established state-of-the-art competitor. The paper's claims of superiority are therefore made in a vacuum. It is critical to compare against existing, published methods for learning DPPs, such as the parametric Bayesian approach of Affandi et al. (2013) or the convex method for learning structured DPPs from Kulesza & Taskar (2011). Without these comparisons, it is impossible to gauge whether the proposed method offers a genuine advance over the current state of the art. The paper argues that prior methods are restrictive, yet it provides no empirical evidence that its unconstrained approach actually yields better performance on a real-world task.

Furthermore, the motivation for the EM algorithm rests heavily on the purported failure of the projection step in the KA baseline. The authors argue that this projection leads to poor, near-diagonal local optima. This is an interesting hypothesis, but it is not explored with sufficient rigor. It is possible that a more carefully implemented gradient-based method—for instance, one using a more sophisticated line search or a manifold optimization framework for the PSD cone—would not suffer from this issue as severely. The paper presents the EM approach as a solution to a problem that may be an artifact of a simplistic baseline implementation.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The background on DPPs is concise and accurate, and the derivation of the EM algorithm is presented in a logical, step-by-step manner. The algorithms are clearly stated. The authors do a good job of explaining the intuition behind the failure of the projection step, even if the evidence is not entirely convincing. One point of ambiguity is the practical implementation and cost of the line search for the eigenvector update (Algorithm 2). The paper states that for each trial step size, an E-step is performed to check for likelihood improvement. If the E-step involves re-computing posteriors for all `n` data points, this could make the M-step extremely expensive, potentially undermining the reported runtime advantages. This aspect requires clarification.

**Originality:** The application of an EM algorithm to learn the full, unconstrained DPP kernel is novel. While the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs representation is known, using it to define a latent variable for EM appears to be a new contribution. The specific derivations for the M-step, particularly the use of Stiefel manifold optimization for the eigenvectors, are also non-trivial. The work is clearly distinguished from prior work which either learns restricted versions of the kernel or uses different learning paradigms.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is conditional on its performance relative to established methods. Learning a full DPP kernel is an important problem, and a robust and efficient algorithm would be a valuable contribution. The proposed EM method is elegant and shows promise. However, because its advantages are only demonstrated against a weak, author-devised baseline on a single dataset, its practical significance remains unproven. Researchers and practitioners are unlikely to adopt this more complex method without compelling evidence that it outperforms existing, and potentially simpler, alternatives on standard benchmarks. The current experiments are insufficient to provide such evidence.

### Conclusion and Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and challenging problem in the literature on probabilistic modeling: learning the full kernel of a DPP.
*   The proposed EM algorithm is a novel and principled approach that avoids the projection step common in constrained optimization.
*   The paper is clearly written, and the technical derivations appear to be correct.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   **Lack of adequate baselines:** The paper fails to compare against any previously published method for learning DPPs. The claims of superiority are based solely on comparison to a simple projected gradient method, which is not a sufficient benchmark to establish a state-of-the-art contribution.
*   **Insufficient experimental validation:** The empirical results are limited to a single dataset. The generalizability and robustness of the method are not adequately tested.
*   **Weak justification:** The primary motivation—the failure of projection-based methods—is not rigorously established and may be an artifact of the specific baseline implementation.
*   The potential significance of the work is undermined by the weak empirical evaluation, making it difficult to assess its practical value to the community.

Overall, while the proposed method is methodologically interesting, the paper does not meet the standards of empirical rigor required for publication at NeurIPS. The lack of comparison to established baselines is a critical flaw. I recommend rejection, but I would encourage the authors to re-evaluate their work against stronger competitors on a wider range of datasets, as the core idea has merit.
