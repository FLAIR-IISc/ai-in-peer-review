### Paper Summary

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. The standard maximum likelihood estimation approach is conjectured to be NP-hard, and a naive projected gradient ascent method is shown to be problematic; specifically, the projection step required to maintain positive semi-definiteness can lead to degenerate, near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture the diversity-promoting properties of DPPs.

To overcome this, the authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The key idea is to reformulate the learning problem by working with the eigendecomposition of the DPP's marginal kernel, K. This allows the introduction of a latent variable for each observed subset, corresponding to a selection of eigenvectors from the kernel. The paper leverages the view of a DPP as a mixture of "elementary DPPs" to formalize this. The resulting EM algorithm alternates between an E-step, which calculates the posterior distribution over the latent eigenvector selections, and an M-step, which updates the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of K. The eigenvalue update is shown to have a closed-form solution, while the eigenvector update is handled by taking a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, thus avoiding the problematic projection of the baseline method.

The proposed EM algorithm is compared against the projected gradient ascent baseline (termed K-Ascent) on a real-world product recommendation dataset derived from Amazon baby registries. Experiments show that the EM method is more robust, especially with a non-informative initialization or in a data-poor setting, leading to significantly better test log-likelihoods. Furthermore, the EM algorithm is shown to be asymptotically and empirically faster than the baseline.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The problem of learning a full, unconstrained DPP kernel is fundamental and important for the practical application of these models. Prior work has often resorted to learning restricted parameterizations or ensembles, losing some of the elegance and computational benefits of a single DPP. This paper presents a principled and novel approach to the full learning problem. The EM formulation based on the eigendecomposition is a clever and non-obvious way to structure the optimization, successfully circumventing the key issue (the projection step) that plagues a more direct approach.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the EM algorithm is well-motivated and appears correct. The identification of the posterior over the latent variables as a conditional k-DPP (Lemma 1) is an elegant result that allows for efficient computation in the E-step. The M-step updates are handled carefully, with a clean closed-form update for the eigenvalues and a principled approach for updating the eigenvectors via optimization on the Stiefel manifold. The theoretical analysis of the runtime complexity, which demonstrates an asymptotic advantage for the EM method, is also a valuable contribution.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It provides a concise but sufficient background on DPPs, clearly motivates the problem with the shortcomings of projected gradient ascent, and then systematically develops the EM algorithm. The logic flows naturally from one section to the next. The inclusion of algorithm boxes for both the baseline and the proposed method aids clarity and reproducibility.

4.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The choice of the baby registry dataset is very appropriate, as product recommendation is a canonical application where modeling diversity (i.e., negative correlations) is crucial. The experiments are well-designed, testing across different initializations (random Wishart vs. data-driven moments-matching) and data regimes (full vs. data-poor). This design effectively highlights the primary strength of the EM algorithm: its robustness. The qualitative example of the selected "safety" items provides a compelling, intuitive illustration of how K-Ascent's failure to model diversity results in a less useful recommendation. The inclusion of runtime comparisons further strengthens the paper's claims.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Details Deferred to Supplement:** Several key derivations (proof of Lemma 1, derivations for equations 19 and 20) are left to the supplement. While this is standard practice due to space constraints, the main paper would benefit from a sentence or two providing more intuition for these results. For example, a brief intuitive explanation for why the posterior `q(J|Yi)` takes the form of a k-DPP would be helpful for the reader.

2.  **Generalized EM Nature:** The M-step for the eigenvectors `V` does not fully maximize the objective; instead, a single gradient step is taken. This makes the algorithm a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm. While this is a practical and well-justified choice, it would be clearer to explicitly state this and briefly mention that convergence is still guaranteed to a stationary point of the likelihood objective under standard conditions.

3.  **Scalability:** The runtime of the proposed EM algorithm contains an O(N^3) term due to the eigendecomposition and the Stiefel manifold update for the eigenvectors. While the paper correctly shows this can be faster than the baseline, this complexity still limits the method's applicability to problems with very large ground sets (large N). It would be valuable for the authors to acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion and perhaps speculate on potential avenues for future work, such as incorporating kernel approximation techniques (e.g., Nyström method) within the EM framework to improve scalability.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on learning probabilistic models for subset selection. It tackles a difficult and important problem with a novel, elegant, and technically sound solution. The proposed EM algorithm is well-motivated and convincingly demonstrated to be superior to a natural baseline method in terms of robustness, solution quality, and computational speed. The paper is clearly written and the experiments are thorough. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the work. This paper is a strong candidate for acceptance and is likely to be influential for researchers and practitioners working with DPPs.
