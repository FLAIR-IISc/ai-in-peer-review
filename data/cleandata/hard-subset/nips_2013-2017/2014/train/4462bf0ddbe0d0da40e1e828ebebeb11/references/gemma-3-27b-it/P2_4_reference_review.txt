## Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix *L* for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) from observed subsets. Existing methods either perform partial learning (e.g., learning scalar weights) or rely on restrictive parameterizations or approximations that sacrifice DPP properties. The authors propose an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that leverages the eigendecomposition of *L*, avoiding the problematic projection steps inherent in naive gradient ascent approaches. They demonstrate both theoretical advantages (asymptotic runtime) and empirical improvements over gradient ascent on a product recommendation task.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant problem:** Learning DPPs is crucial for diverse subset selection, but computationally hard. This paper directly tackles this core challenge.
* **Novel approach:** The EM algorithm, utilizing eigendecomposition and a hidden variable representing eigenvector selection, is a clever and well-motivated approach. It's a substantial departure from prior work.
* **Theoretical justification:** The authors provide arguments for the algorithm's robustness (avoiding degenerate solutions) and asymptotic speedup compared to gradient ascent.
* **Empirical validation:** The experiments on baby registry data demonstrate the algorithm’s superiority to K-Ascent, particularly in scenarios where negative interactions between items are strong or data is limited.
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The background section provides sufficient context, and the algorithmic details are clearly described.
* **Good connection to related work:** The authors position their work well within the existing literature on DPP learning, highlighting the limitations of previous approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the M-step:** While the authors provide closed-form updates for the eigenvalues, the eigenvector update relies on approximations and a step-size search, increasing practical complexity.  The reliance on a Stiefel manifold update (even simplified) adds some implementation burden.
* **Initialization sensitivity:** Although EM is presented as more robust, the experiments show performance still depends on initialization. The effect of different initialization strategies could be explored further. The moments-matching initialization helps, but isn’t a panacea.
* **Limited scope of experiments:** The experiments focus exclusively on a product recommendation task with a specific dataset.  Exploring other application domains or dataset characteristics (e.g., varying sparsity) would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Runtime analysis nuance:** The asymptotic runtime comparison is valuable, but the practical runtime gains observed in the experiments (2.1x speedup) are relatively modest.  A more detailed runtime breakdown (e.g., profiling the EM vs. KA steps) would be insightful.
* **Discussion of assumptions:** The assumption of a DPP structure itself is a strong one. The paper doesn't delve into how well the data inherently fits this model or methods for detecting model misspecification.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the EM algorithm appears correct, and the experimental results provide evidence of its effectiveness. The analysis of the gradient ascent issues is convincing. However, the practical considerations of the M-step complexity and the reliance on approximations should be acknowledged more prominently. **Score: 8/10**
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow, though some of the mathematical details (particularly around the M-step updates) could benefit from further elaboration. The notation is consistent. **Score: 8.5/10**
* **Originality:** The proposed EM algorithm represents a novel approach to DPP learning. It offers a significant improvement over existing methods by avoiding the projection issue and providing a more flexible learning framework. **Score: 9/10**
* **Significance:** The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of DPP learning and subset selection. A robust and efficient learning algorithm for DPPs could be applied to a wide range of applications.  The demonstrated improvements on the product recommendation task are encouraging. **Score: 8/10**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a core challenge in DPP modeling.
* The EM algorithm is a novel and well-motivated solution.
* The empirical results demonstrate improvements over existing methods.
* The theoretical analysis provides insights into the algorithm’s properties.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The M-step complexity and approximation may limit practical scalability.
* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.
* The strong assumptions regarding the underlying DPP model are not thoroughly discussed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPP learning. While there are some minor weaknesses, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and empirical results outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically:

* **Elaborate on the M-step complexity:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the eigenvector update and the step-size search.
* **Expand experimental evaluation:** Consider adding experiments on other datasets or application domains.
* **Discuss model assumptions:** Briefly address the limitations of assuming a DPP structure and potential methods for model validation.
