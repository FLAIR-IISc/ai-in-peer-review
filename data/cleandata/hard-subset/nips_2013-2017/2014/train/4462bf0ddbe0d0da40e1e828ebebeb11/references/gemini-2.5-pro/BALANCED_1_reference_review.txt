This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. While DPPs are powerful models for capturing diversity in subset selection tasks, learning the full kernel via maximum likelihood is conjectured to be NP-hard. Previous approaches have circumvented this by learning restricted or parametric forms of the kernel. This work proposes a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to learn a full, non-parametric kernel.

The key idea is to leverage the eigendecomposition of the DPP's marginal kernel, K. This allows the authors to re-cast the DPP distribution as a mixture of "elementary DPPs," where each elementary DPP corresponds to a specific subset of eigenvectors. This formulation naturally introduces a latent variable—the index set of the chosen eigenvectors—enabling the use of an EM algorithm. The E-step involves computing the posterior over this latent variable for each observed data point, which is shown to be a conditional DPP itself. The M-step then updates the kernel's eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The eigenvalue update has an elegant closed-form solution, while the eigenvector update is handled via a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, cleverly avoiding the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent. The authors compare their EM algorithm to a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) on a real-world product recommendation task, demonstrating that their method is more robust, particularly with uninformative initializations or sparse data, and computationally faster.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental problem in the application of DPPs. The proposed EM algorithm is a novel and elegant approach to learning the full kernel matrix. Moving beyond restricted parameterizations is a significant step forward that could broaden the applicability of DPPs in various machine learning domains. The method's demonstrated robustness makes it a valuable contribution.

*   **Technical Quality:** The technical development is sound and well-motivated. The formulation of the DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs to introduce a latent variable is insightful. The derivation of the E- and M-steps is clear. In particular, the use of optimization techniques on the Stiefel manifold to update the eigenvectors is a sophisticated and appropriate choice that directly addresses the shortcomings of the projection-based baseline. This shows a deep understanding of both the probabilistic model and the associated optimization challenges.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It provides clear motivation, a concise background on DPPs, and a step-by-step derivation of the algorithm. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the intuition behind why the baseline method (K-Ascent) can fail—specifically, how the projection step can collapse the kernel to a near-diagonal matrix, destroying its ability to model diversity. This clear exposition makes the paper accessible and the contribution easy to appreciate.

*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The use of a real-world baby registry dataset is compelling. The authors wisely test their method under different conditions (two types of initializations, data-rich vs. data-poor settings), which clearly highlights the robustness of the proposed EM algorithm. The quantitative results (log-likelihood, runtime) are supported by a strong qualitative example that provides an intuitive understanding of the improved performance. The analysis linking EM's advantage to the importance of the kernel's off-diagonal entries is a nice piece of diagnostic work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **M-step for Eigenvectors:** The M-step update for the eigenvectors `V` is not a full maximization but rather a single gradient step. This makes the overall procedure a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm. While this is a valid and often practical approach, the paper could be strengthened by explicitly framing it as such and briefly discussing the convergence properties of GEM algorithms. Furthermore, the line search for the step size `η` for `V` appears to check for improvement in the true log-likelihood `L` rather than the auxiliary function `F`. This is a practical choice but deviates slightly from a standard EM/GEM framework; a short justification would be beneficial.

*   **Scalability:** The runtime complexity includes an `O(N^3)` term due to the eigenvector updates (specifically, the matrix exponential in the Stiefel manifold update). While the algorithm is shown to be faster than the baseline for the "moderate" `N` in the experiments, this term will be a bottleneck for applications with very large ground sets (large `N`). The paper would be more complete with a brief discussion of this limitation and potential avenues for future work to scale the method, such as using low-rank approximations or randomized numerical algebra techniques.

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  In Section 3.5, I suggest adding a sentence to clarify that taking a single gradient step for `V` makes the algorithm a Generalized EM algorithm, which is still guaranteed to increase the likelihood at each iteration. This would add formal precision without requiring significant changes.

2.  A brief discussion on scalability in the conclusion would be valuable. Mentioning the `O(N^3)` bottleneck and pointing towards potential solutions like low-rank approximations (perhaps citing work like Affandi et al. [15] in this context) would provide a good roadmap for future research building on this excellent work.

3.  The computational complexity of the eigenvalue update in Equation (19) is stated as `O(nNk^2)`. It might be helpful for the reader to add a brief parenthetical note explaining that this comes from performing an eigendecomposition on a `k x k` matrix for each of the `n` data points, as derived from k-DPP marginalization properties.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel, elegant, and technically sound solution to the important problem of learning full DPP kernels.
*   It convincingly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed EM algorithm over a natural and strong baseline, both in terms of model quality and computational efficiency.
*   The work is well-written, clearly motivated, and supported by strong empirical evidence.
*   It represents a significant contribution that is likely to be impactful and useful for researchers and practitioners working with DPPs.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The scalability of the method to very large `N` is a limitation, though this is also true for the baseline and many kernel-based methods.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is an excellent paper. It identifies a clear weakness in a naive approach to a difficult problem and proposes a principled and effective solution. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which can be easily addressed. I strongly recommend acceptance.
