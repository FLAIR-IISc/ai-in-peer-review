This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing the difficulty of directly maximizing the likelihood function due to the positive semi-definiteness constraint on the kernel matrix L. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing methods – scalar weighting, L-ensembles, and Bayesian approaches – and propose a solution based on eigendecomposition that avoids problematic projection steps encountered in naive gradient ascent. The paper is well-motivated, framing the problem within the context of subset selection and highlighting the importance of diversity. 

However, despite the novelty of the approach and the potential benefits, a multitude of minor, yet persistent, issues detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. The presentation, while generally clear, suffers from inconsistencies, unnecessary complexity in certain derivations, and a lack of rigorous justification for some design choices. The experimental evaluation, though performed, feels incomplete and lacks sufficient detail to fully assess the claimed advantages.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The EM algorithm based on eigendecomposition offers a genuinely new approach to learning DPPs, circumventing the projection issues of gradient ascent.
*   **Theoretical Motivation:** The connection to eigendecomposition and the use of Jensen’s inequality provide a sound theoretical basis for the proposed method.
*   **Potential for Improvement:** The algorithm has the potential to outperform existing methods, particularly when dealing with datasets exhibiting strong negative interactions between items.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistency:** Throughout the paper, there’s a fluctuating use of notation. For example, K is sometimes referred to as the "marginal kernel" and other times simply “the kernel.” Similarly, LY is used inconsistently, and the definition of IY is initially unclear. These small inconsistencies, while not individually significant, accumulate and hinder readability.
*   **Derivation Complexity:** Several derivations, particularly in Section 3.5 concerning the eigenvector update, are presented without sufficient intermediate steps. While the final equations are stated, the reader is left to fill in significant gaps, diminishing understanding and potentially introducing errors. The justification for neglecting second-order information in the Stiefel manifold update (Equation 21) feels particularly hand-wavy.
*   **Experimental Detail:** The experimental section, while providing some results, lacks crucial details. The specific hyperparameters used for both EM and K-Ascent are not consistently reported, making it difficult to reproduce the results. The description of the Wishart initialization is vague, and the justification for rescaling by 1/N is not thoroughly explained. The comparison using the moment-matching initializer is beneficial, but further exploration with different initializations would be valuable.
*   **Runtime Analysis:** The runtime complexity analysis in Section 3.1 is somewhat convoluted and could be presented more clearly. The assertion that EM is asymptotically faster than gradient ascent is correct in principle, but the practicality of this advantage in real-world scenarios (with moderate N and n) needs more nuance.
*   **Minor Technical Issues:** The statement regarding the QYi matrix becoming infinite when λj = 1 is true, but the proposed solution ("tighten the constraint on λ") feels ad-hoc. A more robust approach might involve a regularization term. Also, the lack of a discussion on the computational cost of marginalization in the E-step is a notable omission.
*   **Figure 2a lacks clarity:** The comparison of the recommended items is weak. The rationale for why the KA selection is "unlikely" is subjective and not sufficiently justified.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a challenging problem in DPP learning with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a potential advantage over existing methods, particularly in specific scenarios.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The numerous minor flaws, particularly in presentation and experimental detail, detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.
*   The lack of rigorous justification for some design choices and the ad-hoc nature of certain solutions raise concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the proposed method.
*   The experimental evaluation, while promising, is incomplete and lacks the detail needed to fully assess the claimed advantages.
*   The paper, as it stands, does not meet the NIPS standard for technical polish and rigor.



**Overall Assessment:**

Reject. While the core idea is promising and the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the underlying theory, the paper suffers from too many minor flaws to warrant acceptance. The lack of polish and the insufficient experimental detail undermine confidence in the results and limit the potential impact of the work. A major revision addressing the points outlined above would be necessary for reconsideration. The authors should focus on improving the clarity of the presentation, providing more rigorous justifications for their design choices, and conducting a more thorough experimental evaluation.
