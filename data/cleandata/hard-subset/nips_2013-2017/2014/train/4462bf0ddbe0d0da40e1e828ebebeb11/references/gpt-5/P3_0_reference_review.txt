Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses maximum-likelihood learning of DPP kernels without restrictive parameterizations. The key idea is to optimize in the eigenspace of the marginal kernel K using an EM-style algorithm. Leveraging the mixture representation of a DPP as a mixture over elementary DPPs (indexed by subsets J of eigenvectors), the authors introduce J as a latent variable: the E-step computes q(J|Y), which they show is a k-DPP; the M-step updates eigenvalues in closed form via k-DPP marginals and takes a manifold-constrained gradient step for eigenvectors. This avoids the PSD/eigenvalue clipping projection that plagues naive projected gradient ascent (KA), which can create degenerate near-diagonal solutions. Complexity improves to O(n N k^2) per iteration for the data-dependent terms when typical set sizes k are small relative to N.

This fills a gap between prior “partial” learning strategies—learning only item qualities (diagonal scalings) or mixture weights of fixed DPPs—and Bayesian parametric treatments with fixed functional forms. The use of the mixture/eigendecomposition view to drive an EM algorithm and the identification of the E-step distribution as a k-DPP are, to my knowledge, novel and technically insightful. The paper situates itself well relative to Kulesza & Taskar’s foundational work and the Bayesian approach of Affandi et al.

Quality
- Theory: The latent-variable formulation and Jensen lower bound are standard and appropriate. The characterization of q(J|Y) as a k-DPP with kernel QY is plausible and matches known results about conditional DPPs; however, the main text defers all proofs to the supplement. The eigenvalue update (Eq. 18–19) via k-DPP marginals is elegant and computationally efficient (using low-rank structure to achieve O(N k^2) per example). The eigenvector update is less clean: Eq. (20) is derived only for the initial gradient when q = p, and the algorithm then performs a single gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, re-running the E-step and accepting steps that improve likelihood. This amounts to a generalized EM (GEM) with inexact M-steps.
- Convergence: Because the V-update does not maximize (or even consistently increase) the lower bound F and the line search evaluates the true likelihood L after recomputing q, monotonic increase of the bound is not guaranteed. The method should be clearly presented as GEM, and a short convergence discussion (e.g., to stationary points under mild conditions) would strengthen the technical claims.
- Numerical details: The λ≈1 issue is noted and handled by clamping; a brief discussion of how sensitive performance is to this clamp would help. The Stiefel update via matrix exponential is reasonable, but computationally dominated by O(N^3) operations in the step-size search; discussing alternative retractions (e.g., Cayley, QR) could reduce constants.
- Baseline: The projected gradient baseline is fair, and the diagonal-trap analysis is convincing, but the baseline could be stronger by including quasi-Newton methods with PSD-preserving parameterizations (e.g., K = U diag(σ(s)) U^T, or low-rank plus diagonal) that avoid hard projections.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The algorithms are clearly presented. A few suggestions:
- Make explicit that the algorithm is a generalized EM and clarify which objective (bound vs. likelihood) is monitored for acceptance.
- Provide a brief proof sketch or intuition for Lemma 1 in the main text (the conditional distribution being a k-DPP is a key insight).
- Clarify the computational steps to obtain Eq. (19) efficiently (skinny eigendecomposition via the rank-k factorization of QY).
- Define all matrices when they first appear (e.g., BY in Eq. 20) and keep notation consistent between U, V, VY, UY.
- Report stopping criterion c and typical iteration counts, and specify line-search parameters.

Originality
The EM formulation for unconstrained DPP learning that operates in eigenspace, with a k-DPP E-step and closed-form eigenvalue updates, is novel to the best of my knowledge. While EM and manifold optimization are standard tools, their combination here for DPP MLE is a meaningful methodological contribution. The critique of projection-based gradient ascent in this context is also useful.

Significance
Learning nonparametric DPP kernels is important for applications where diversity/repulsion matters. The proposed method has the potential to become a default optimizer for DPP MLE, especially when subsets are small (k ≪ N). That said, the empirical section is somewhat narrow: one real dataset (albeit large) with modest average gains in test log-likelihood (3.7% vs Wishart init; parity with moment-matching init; larger gains in data-poor settings), and no comparisons to stronger optimizers or to parametric/low-rank approaches. Demonstrating performance on larger N, synthetic recovery with known ground truth, and downstream task metrics (e.g., next-item prediction or MAP quality) would bolster significance.

Experiments
- Positives: Solid engineering, sensible initializations, speed measurements, insightful correlation analysis relating gains to the strength of off-diagonals (negative interactions).
- Limitations: Only one domain; no ablations on k, sensitivity to initialization, or robustness across random seeds beyond the Wishart trials. No comparison to low-rank or Bayesian learners, nor to alternative optimizers (e.g., Riemannian methods on V with different step rules). The MAP qualitative example is illustrative but anecdotal. It would be useful to include a simple independent Bernoulli baseline and an Ising/LogDet baseline (if feasible) for calibration.

Pros
- Conceptually clean EM/GEM formulation via eigendecomposition; elegant eigenvalue updates.
- Avoids degeneracies of projection-based KA; more robust across initializations and data regimes.
- Computationally attractive when k is small; practical manifold update for V.
- Code and data provided; reproducibility emphasized.

Cons
- Inexact M-step and reliance on line search over the true likelihood weaken theoretical guarantees; convergence analysis is minimal.
- Experimental scope limited; modest average gains over a relatively weak baseline.
- No comparison to stronger optimizers or low-rank/parametric DPP learning.
- Some proofs deferred; small gaps in clarity and implementation details.

Suggestions for improvement
- Recast the method explicitly as generalized EM and add a short convergence result (or cite standard GEM convergence) under the proposed acceptance rule.
- Strengthen baselines (quasi-Newton with PSD-preserving parameterization; low-rank DPP learning), and add synthetic recovery experiments.
- Evaluate downstream recommendation metrics (next-item prediction, MAP set quality) and scalability to larger N.
- Consider alternative Stiefel retractions with cheaper updates; report sensitivity to λ clamping.
- Add more implementation details (step sizes, stopping conditions) and minor notation clarifications.

Overall
A technically interesting and relevant contribution with a novel EM view that meaningfully advances DPP learning. Some theoretical and empirical gaps remain, but the core idea is strong and likely to influence practice. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying GEM status and broadening the empirical evaluation.
