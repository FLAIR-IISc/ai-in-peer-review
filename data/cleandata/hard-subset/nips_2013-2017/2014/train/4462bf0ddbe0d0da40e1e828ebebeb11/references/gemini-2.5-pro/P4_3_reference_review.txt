### Paper Summary

This paper addresses the problem of learning the kernel matrix `L` (or equivalently, the marginal kernel `K`) of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a set of observed subsets. Maximum likelihood estimation for a general DPP kernel is conjectured to be NP-hard. The authors first observe that a naive approach using projected gradient ascent on the entries of `K` can perform poorly, as the projection step required to maintain positive semi-definiteness and constrain eigenvalues can lead to degenerate, near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture the diversity-modeling aspect of DPPs.

The main contribution of the paper is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for this learning problem. The key idea is to reformulate the learning problem by working with the eigendecomposition of the kernel, `K = VΛV^T`, and introducing a latent variable `J`, which represents a subset of eigenvector indices. This leverages the known generative interpretation of a DPP as a mixture of "elementary DPPs," where each elementary DPP corresponds to a specific choice of `J`.

The resulting algorithm alternates between two steps:
1.  **E-step:** Given the current parameters (`V`, `Λ`), compute the posterior distribution over the latent variables, `p(J | Y_i)`, for each observed subset `Y_i`. The authors show that this posterior is itself a k-DPP and provide an explicit formula for its kernel.
2.  **M-step:** Update the parameters (`V`, `Λ`) to maximize the expected log-likelihood under the posterior distribution computed in the E-step. The update for the eigenvalues `Λ` has a closed-form solution that circumvents the need for projection. The update for the eigenvectors `V` is performed with a single gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, which also avoids a projection step.

The authors compare their proposed EM algorithm to the projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) on a real-world product recommendation task using Amazon baby registry data. The experiments demonstrate that the EM algorithm is more robust, particularly with a random initialization or in data-poor settings, and is empirically faster than the baseline.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality: Technical Soundness**

The paper presents a technically sound and well-reasoned approach. The formulation of DPP learning as an EM problem is elegant and novel. The derivation relies on established properties of DPPs, and the use of an auxiliary distribution and Jensen's inequality to construct the lower bound is a standard and appropriate technique. The insight that the posterior `p(J|Y_i)` is a k-DPP is a key technical result that makes the E-step tractable. The M-step updates are also well-motivated; the closed-form update for eigenvalues is a significant advantage over projection, and handling the eigenvector update via optimization on the Stiefel manifold is more principled than projection.

A minor weakness is that the M-step does not fully maximize the objective with respect to the eigenvectors `V` in each iteration. Instead, it takes a single gradient step. The line search for this step is not performed on the M-step objective (which is intractable) but by checking for improvement in the true likelihood. While this is a practical heuristic, the theoretical properties of this mixed optimization scheme (partially optimizing `F` for `Λ`, taking a single step on `V` guided by `L`) are not fully discussed.

**Clarity**

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The background section provides the necessary definitions for DPPs. The derivation of the EM algorithm is presented in a logical, step-by-step manner that is easy to follow. The algorithms are presented in clear pseudocode. The experimental setup and results are also clearly described. The deferral of several key proofs and derivations to a supplement is standard but makes a complete verification of the technical details from the main paper alone challenging.

**Originality**

The paper is highly original. While DPPs have been used in machine learning for some time, learning the full, unconstrained kernel from data remains a difficult and relatively underexplored problem. Previous works have focused on more restricted learning settings (e.g., learning weights for a fixed kernel, using parametric forms, or L-ensembles). This paper's main contribution—framing the problem in an EM framework by introducing latent variables corresponding to the eigenbasis—is a novel and insightful approach. It provides a new optimization tool for a fundamental problem in the application of DPPs.

**Significance**

The work is significant as it provides a practical and more robust algorithm for a core problem in machine learning with DPPs. The failure mode of projected gradient ascent highlighted by the authors is an important practical consideration for anyone attempting to learn DPP kernels. By providing an alternative that avoids this pitfall and is also computationally faster, the paper has the potential to enable more effective use of DPPs in applications where diversity is a key component that must be learned directly from data, such as product recommendation, document summarization, and diverse search results. The demonstrated advantages on a real-world dataset underscore its practical utility.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**

1.  **Novelty:** The EM formulation for learning an unconstrained DPP kernel is a highly novel and clever contribution to the literature.
2.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental and difficult problem. The proposed algorithm is more robust and efficient than the natural baseline, which could have a significant impact on the practical application of DPPs.
3.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical development of the algorithm is solid, and the experimental evaluation is well-executed on a real-world dataset.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

1.  **Approximation in M-step:** The M-step update for the eigenvectors is a single gradient step rather than a full maximization. While pragmatic, a deeper discussion of the implications of this approximation would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The experimental comparison is limited to a single projected gradient ascent baseline. While this is the most direct competitor, a discussion of how the method compares to other approaches like the Bayesian parametric learning of Affandi et al. (2013) would provide more context, even if an empirical comparison is not directly applicable due to differing assumptions.
3.  **Reliance on Supplement:** Many of the key mathematical derivations are in the supplement, which makes the paper less self-contained.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. The paper introduces a novel, technically sound, and practically useful algorithm for an important problem.
