

# Review

This paper presents a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) approach for learning Determinantal Point Process (DPP) parameters, specifically addressing a key limitation in prior work: the problematic projection steps required in gradient ascent methods for maximum likelihood estimation. The authors recognize that previous approaches either impose restrictive parametric forms on the kernel matrix L (e.g., [3, 8]) or suffer from degenerate results due to projection steps (as shown in Section 3.1). Their EM method cleverly exploits the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K, transforming the learning problem into one where the hidden variable corresponds to the set of eigenvectors used in the DPP mixture representation.

The paper's main strength lies in its conceptual novelty. While DPP learning has been studied before (e.g., [8, 2, 3]), the authors' EM approach using eigendecomposition represents a fundamentally different direction from previous work. This is not merely an incremental improvement but a bold conceptual shift that directly addresses a core limitation of gradient-based methods. The paper convincingly demonstrates that gradient ascent with projection often leads to nearly diagonal matrices that fail to model the negative interactions between items (which are crucial for DPPs' diversity property), while the EM approach avoids this pitfall entirely.

The theoretical development is sound, with clear derivations of the E-step (Lemma 1) and M-step updates (Equations 18-20). The authors provide a careful analysis of the computational complexity, showing that EM is asymptotically faster than gradient ascent (O(T₁nNk² + T₁T₂N³) vs. O(T₁nN³ + T₁T₂N³)). This theoretical advantage is validated in experiments on a baby registry dataset from Amazon, where EM consistently outperforms gradient ascent (K-Ascent) in log-likelihood, particularly in categories where negative interactions between items are strong (d > 0.025). The runtime analysis (Figure 2b) confirms that EM is 2.1x faster on average.

The paper's contribution is significant for the DPP literature, as it provides a principled method for learning unconstrained DPPs without requiring restrictive parametric forms. Unlike [3]'s L-ensemble approach, which loses the attractive computational properties of DPPs, and unlike [8]'s Bayesian method, which requires parametric assumptions, this approach maintains the full power of DPPs while avoiding the degeneracy issues of gradient ascent.

The only notable weaknesses are that the paper doesn't compare to other non-gradient-based methods (though it does cite prior work that explores similar alternatives), and the theoretical convergence guarantees for the EM algorithm are not established. However, these are minor concerns given the paper's strong novelty and empirical validation.

This paper excels in originality, presenting a bold new approach to a well-known problem in DPP learning. The conceptual contribution is substantial, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate its advantages over the natural baseline. The paper is well-written, clearly positioned in the literature, and makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling with DPPs.

I strongly recommend acceptance. While the paper could benefit from additional comparison to other learning approaches (e.g., variational inference methods), the novelty and practical significance of the proposed EM method outweigh this limitation. This is not an incremental paper but a genuinely new direction in DPP learning that addresses a fundamental problem in the field. The authors have made a compelling case for the superiority of their approach in both theory and practice, making this a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
