Summary
The paper addresses learning determinantal point processes (DPPs) from example subsets by maximizing likelihood without imposing restrictive parameterizations on the kernel. The key idea is to work in the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K and introduce a hidden variable J corresponding to a subset of eigenvectors, leveraging the known mixture representation of a DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs. This yields an EM-style algorithm: the E-step computes the posterior over J given each training set, which the authors show is a k-DPP with a closed-form kernel; the M-step updates eigenvalues in closed form via k-DPP marginals and takes a Stiefel-manifold gradient step for eigenvectors. Compared to projected gradient ascent on K (KA), the EM approach avoids projection-induced degeneracy (near-diagonal kernels), is more robust to initialization and data regime, and is asymptotically faster when typical set sizes k are much smaller than N. Experiments on a large Amazon baby registry dataset show modest to substantial test likelihood gains (especially under weak initialization or limited data) and 2× speedups.

Relation to prior work
Prior learning methods for DPPs typically (i) learn only item-quality scalings (convex, but no similarity learning), (ii) learn mixtures/ensembles of fixed DPPs (losing core DPP properties and increasing storage), or (iii) assume parametric kernels and use Bayesian sampling. The proposed method departs by directly optimizing an unconstrained, nonparametric K through its spectrum and eigenvectors via a latent J. The connection between DPP sampling/eigendecomposition and mixture-of-elementary DPPs is known, but using it to derive an EM algorithm with efficient k-DPP-based E-step and spectral M-step appears novel and practically meaningful.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: elegant exploitation of the elementary DPP mixture and the latent J to build an EM algorithm that operates in the spectral domain, with closed-form eigenvalue updates and a principled Stiefel-manifold step for eigenvectors. This is a clear new direction relative to restricted or heuristic learning approaches.
- Practical robustness: convincingly argues and shows that projection in KA can collapse to near-diagonal K, destroying diversity modeling. The EM method avoids this failure mode.
- Efficiency: for k ≪ N, per-iteration complexity O(n N k^2) for the main work plus O(N^3) for line search updates compares favorably to KA’s repeated O(N^3) inverses and projections; measured ≈2× speedups on real data.
- Empirical evidence: on 13 categories with tens of thousands of registries, EM improves test log-likelihood, particularly under Wishart initialization and in data-poor regimes. The qualitative example (avoid recommending three video monitors) nicely illustrates modeling negative interactions.
- Clarity of the main derivations: E-step characterization as a k-DPP and the efficient eigenvalue update via k-DPP marginals are well-motivated and clearly tied to DPP properties.

Weaknesses and questions
- Limited baselines: Comparing only to projected gradient on K is a weak baseline. Stronger alternatives include (i) optimizing in the spectral parameterization directly with Riemannian methods (Stiefel + box constraints) without EM, (ii) quasi-Newton or momentum-based methods with spectral projection, (iii) parameterizations via Cholesky/factor models with implicit PSD, or (iv) previously proposed Bayesian/parametric DPP learning. This would better isolate the value of the latent-variable EM idea versus “just” optimizing in the spectral domain.
- EM fidelity: The eigenvector update uses only a single gradient step with the gradient valid for q = p, making this a generalized EM with partial M-step and repeated E-steps for line search. While the authors check L for improvement, some discussion of monotonicity guarantees and convergence properties (e.g., to stationary points) would strengthen the method’s footing.
- Scope of experiments: All results are on baby registry categories with moderate N (~70) and relatively short sets. Demonstrating behavior on larger N (e.g., 500–5,000) or on other canonical DPP tasks (document summarization, sensor placement) would increase confidence in generality and scaling, especially since the purported complexity advantages hinge on k ≪ N.
- Regularization and overfitting: No regularization is used. Some analysis of overfitting, sensitivity to eigenvalues approaching 1 (the noted numerical caveat), and the effect of priors/penalties (e.g., trace/nuclear norm, spectral shrinkage) would be valuable.
- Clarity/notation: The switch U = V^T and the interplay of VYi, UYi, and BYi could be streamlined; it’s easy to get lost in the index gymnastics. It would help to summarize the computational steps and costs for Eqns. (19) and (20) in the main text (not just the supplement).

Quality
Technically sound overall; the derivations for the E-step and eigenvalue updates are solid, and the computational claims are plausible. The eigenvector update is approximate, but the approach is reasonable and practical. Empirical evidence backs the main claims, though broader baselines and datasets would improve rigor.

Clarity
Generally well-organized and readable. Some notation overload around V/U and submatrices could be simplified. Minor editorial issues aside, an expert reader should be able to implement the method given the supplement.

Originality
High. Leveraging the elementary DPP mixture/latent J to derive an EM algorithm with k-DPP posteriors for learning unconstrained K is a notable conceptual contribution beyond prior restricted or ensemble-based learning.

Significance
High for researchers and practitioners using DPPs. The method opens a new avenue for learning full DPP kernels, avoids a known failure mode of projected gradient, and offers computational benefits. I expect this to be adopted and extended.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add stronger baselines (Riemannian spectral optimization without EM; quasi-Newton with projection; parametric/Bayesian methods).
- Report results on at least one additional domain and a larger-N regime.
- Provide a brief convergence discussion for the generalized EM.
- Include ablations on initialization, k/N, and the line-search/E-step coupling.
- Consider modest regularization and report its effects.

Recommendation
Accept (8/10). Novel, bold, and practically impactful approach to a core DPP problem with clear benefits over naïve methods. Some experimental breadth and baseline comparisons are missing, but the conceptual advance justifies acceptance.

Confidence
High (4/5).
