

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the marginal kernel K of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), as an alternative to the projected gradient ascent method (K-Ascent) previously used for DPP parameter estimation. The authors argue that their approach avoids the problematic projection steps required by gradient ascent, which often lead to degenerate solutions where the kernel becomes nearly diagonal and fails to model diversity. They claim their method is asymptotically faster and achieves better performance on a product recommendation task using Amazon baby registry data.

The paper provides a clear background on DPPs, their applications, and the challenges of learning their parameters. The EM approach is well-motivated, with the authors correctly identifying the limitations of gradient ascent with projection. The mathematical derivations appear sound, and the authors provide detailed algorithms for both the E-step and M-step. The experimental section demonstrates that EM outperforms K-Ascent on a baby registry dataset, with the authors attributing this to EM's ability to avoid degenerate solutions.

The paper's strengths include its clear exposition of DPPs, the well-structured derivation of the EM algorithm, and the empirical demonstration of the method's advantages on a real-world dataset. The authors also provide code and data, enhancing reproducibility.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's novelty, experimental validation, and theoretical claims. The core idea of using EM for DPP learning is not sufficiently novel. While the authors cite prior work on DPP learning (e.g., [2], [3], [8]), they fail to compare against these methods, which would have strengthened their novelty claim. The use of eigendecomposition in the EM framework for DPP learning appears to be a straightforward extension of standard EM techniques to this specific model, rather than a truly novel method.

The experimental validation is severely limited. The authors test only on 13 categories from Amazon baby registries (with N=71 on average), which is insufficient to demonstrate the method's general utility. They don't compare against other established DPP learning approaches (like the L-ensemble method in [3] or the Bayesian approach in [8]), which would have been essential to justify their claims. The paper also doesn't explore how their method scales to larger datasets or different applications beyond product recommendation.

The theoretical claim of "asymptotic speedup" is unsubstantiated. The authors provide a runtime comparison (O(T1nNk^2 + T1T2N^3) vs O(T1nN^3 + T1T2N^3)), but this comparison is not rigorous. The paper doesn't provide a proof of convergence rate or demonstrate that the k in the EM runtime (which depends on the maximum set size) is consistently smaller than N in practice. The empirical runtime results (Figure 2b) show a modest speedup (2.1x), but this doesn't justify the claim of "asymptotic" advantage without proper theoretical justification.

Most critically, the paper fails to adequately address the limitations of their approach. They don't discuss how sensitive the method is to initialization, nor do they provide any analysis of when the method might fail. The paper's conclusion that "EM will similarly have a significant advantage" in other applications is unsupported by the limited experimental evidence.

The paper also doesn't engage sufficiently with the broader literature on DPP learning. While they cite relevant papers, they don't adequately distinguish their approach from existing methods. For example, the Bayesian approach in [8] also avoids the projection problem, but the authors don't compare against it.

While the paper presents a technically sound algorithm that works better than the specific baseline they chose, it doesn't demonstrate sufficient novelty or general utility to warrant publication at NIPS. The method appears to be a straightforward application of standard EM techniques to DPP learning, rather than a truly novel contribution. The limited experimental validation and lack of comparison against other established DPP learning methods make it difficult to assess the method's true value.

I recommend rejection. The paper would need substantially more experimental validation across diverse datasets, comparison against other established DPP learning approaches, and stronger theoretical justification for the claimed advantages to meet NIPS standards. The current work is more of a technical note than a significant contribution to the field.
