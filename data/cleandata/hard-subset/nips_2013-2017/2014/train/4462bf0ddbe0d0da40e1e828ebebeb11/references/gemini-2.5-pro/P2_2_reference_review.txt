### **Summary**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. While maximum likelihood estimation for a full, unconstrained DPP kernel is conjectured to be NP-hard, existing methods either learn restrictive, simplified models (e.g., only item qualities) or rely on ensembles of fixed kernels, sacrificing the desirable properties of a single DPP.

The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to tackle this problem. The key idea is to re-parameterize the DPP kernel `K` via its eigendecomposition (eigenvalues `Λ` and eigenvectors `V`). They leverage the known interpretation of a DPP as a mixture of "elementary DPPs," where each component in the mixture corresponds to a subset of eigenvectors `J`. This subset `J` is treated as a latent variable.

The resulting algorithm alternates between two steps:
1.  **E-step:** Computes the posterior distribution over the latent eigenvector subset `J` for each observed data point `Yi`. The authors show that this posterior is itself a tractable *k*-DPP.
2.  **M-step:** Updates the parameters (`Λ`, `V`) to maximize the expected log-likelihood. The update for the eigenvalues is shown to be a simple, closed-form expression that requires no projection. The update for the eigenvectors is performed via a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, which elegantly enforces the orthogonality constraint without a potentially harmful projection step.

Experiments on a real-world baby registry dataset demonstrate that the proposed EM algorithm is more robust and faster than a standard projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent). It particularly excels when the underlying data exhibits strong diversity (negative correlations), a setting where projected gradient ascent is shown to often converge to poor, near-diagonal solutions.

### **Evaluation**

#### **Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed EM formulation is sound, and the derivation is well-motivated.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The core idea of using the eigenvector subset `J` as a latent variable is elegant and provides a principled way to derive an EM algorithm.
    *   The derivation of the eigenvalue update (Eq. 18-19) is a significant result, as it yields a closed-form solution that automatically satisfies the `0 ≤ λ ≤ 1` constraints, thus avoiding the main pitfall of the projected gradient method.
    *   The handling of the non-convex eigenvector optimization using a principled update on the Stiefel manifold (Eq. 21) is sophisticated and appropriate for the problem, again avoiding a simple projection that the authors convincingly argue can be detrimental.
    *   The experimental claims are well-supported. The comparison with projected gradient ascent is fair and insightful. The analysis linking EM's superior performance to datasets with high diversity (the `d` metric) provides strong evidence for the authors' central hypothesis about the failure modes of the baseline. The qualitative example of the three baby monitors is a compelling illustration of the practical difference.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The paper relies heavily on the supplement for several key derivations (e.g., Lemma 1, Eq. 19, Eq. 20). While this is standard practice, the main text would benefit from slightly more intuition on *why* these results hold, particularly for the crucial Lemma 1, which underpins the tractability of the E-step.
    *   The M-step for the eigenvectors `V` is not a full maximization but rather a single gradient step. This makes the algorithm a form of Generalized EM (GEM). While perfectly valid, framing it as such would be more precise.

#### **Clarity**

The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of first motivating the problem, then introducing the baseline approach and its weaknesses, and finally presenting their own method as a direct solution to these weaknesses. The flow is logical and easy to follow. The notation is consistent and the formalisms are clear. The background section provides sufficient context for readers familiar with machine learning but perhaps not with DPPs. The algorithms are presented clearly.

#### **Originality**

The work is highly original. To my knowledge, this is the first application of an EM-style algorithm for learning a full, unconstrained DPP kernel. Previous works, as correctly cited by the authors, have focused on much more constrained learning problems. The main conceptual contribution—framing DPP learning as a latent variable problem over eigenvector subsets—is novel and powerful. This is not simply an incremental improvement but a new and different approach to a fundamental problem in the DPP literature.

#### **Significance**

The significance of this work is high. Learning the DPP kernel from data is a critical bottleneck that has limited the application of DPPs. By providing a practical, robust, and efficient algorithm, this paper could enable wider adoption of DPPs in applications like product recommendation, document summarization, and search, where diversity is a learned property rather than a hand-engineered one.

The paper also makes a significant secondary contribution by clearly diagnosing a failure mode of the most obvious baseline, projected gradient ascent. The insight that repeated projections can destroy off-diagonal information and lead to degenerate solutions is valuable for any researcher working on constrained optimization for kernel learning. The proposed EM method will likely become the standard algorithm for this task.

### **Arguments for Acceptance**
1.  **Novel and Elegant Method:** Proposes a new EM algorithm for the difficult problem of learning an unconstrained DPP kernel, based on a clever latent variable formulation.
2.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The algorithm avoids the problematic projection steps of naive gradient methods by using a closed-form eigenvalue update and a principled Stiefel manifold update for eigenvectors.
3.  **Superior Performance:** The method is shown to be more robust, especially on problems where diversity is important, and computationally faster than a strong baseline.
4.  **High Impact:** Addresses a fundamental bottleneck in the practical use of DPPs, potentially enabling many new applications. The work is likely to be highly cited and built upon.
5.  **Clear and Insightful Experiments:** The experiments are well-designed, use real-world data, and provide clear insights into the relative strengths of the proposed method.

### **Arguments Against Acceptance**
1.  **Limited Scope of Comparison:** The comparison is only against a projected gradient ascent baseline. While this is the most natural baseline, a discussion of other potential constrained optimization techniques could have strengthened the paper.
2.  **Reliance on Supplement:** Some of the key technical derivations are deferred to the supplement, making the paper slightly less self-contained.

### **Conclusion**

The strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, technically sophisticated, and practically significant solution to an important and difficult problem. The proposed EM algorithm is a clear advance over the state-of-the-art and is likely to become a foundational technique for anyone working with DPPs. I strongly recommend acceptance.
