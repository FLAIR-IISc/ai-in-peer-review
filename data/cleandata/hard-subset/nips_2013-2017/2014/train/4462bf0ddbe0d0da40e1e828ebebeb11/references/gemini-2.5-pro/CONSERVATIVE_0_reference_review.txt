**Summary**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. The authors note that direct maximum likelihood estimation via gradient ascent is hampered by a projection step required to maintain the positive semi-definite (PSD) nature of the kernel matrix. This projection can lead to degenerate, near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture the diversity-promoting properties of DPPs. To circumvent this issue, the authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The key idea is to work with the eigendecomposition of the kernel and introduce a latent variable representing the selection of a subset of eigenvectors. The E-step involves computing the posterior distribution over this latent variable, which is shown to be a k-DPP. The M-step then updates the eigenvalues in closed form and the eigenvectors via a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, which elegantly enforces the orthogonality constraint without a problematic projection. Experiments on a product recommendation task using baby registry data show that the proposed EM method is more robust and computationally faster than a projected gradient ascent baseline.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles a central and difficult problem in the practical application of DPPs. Learning a full, unconstrained kernel is crucial for leveraging the full modeling power of DPPs, and existing methods are often limited by restrictive parameterizations or optimization difficulties.
2.  **Methodological Novelty:** The formulation of DPP learning as an EM algorithm is novel and well-motivated. The insight to use the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs representation to define a latent variable is clever and provides a principled way to avoid the projection step that plagues the baseline approach.
3.  **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the algorithm appears sound. The characterization of the posterior in the E-step as a k-DPP is an elegant result, and the use of a Stiefel manifold optimization step for the eigenvectors is an appropriate and sophisticated choice for handling the orthogonality constraint.
4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are performed on a real-world dataset that is well-suited to the DPP framework (product recommendation). The authors convincingly demonstrate the failure mode of the projected gradient ascent baseline, particularly with a naive initialization, and show their method's superior robustness. The inclusion of a runtime comparison is also a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Baseline Comparison:** The primary contribution is framed as overcoming the limitations of projected gradient ascent (KA). While KA is a natural baseline, it is also a relatively simple one. The core issue with KA is the naive projection onto the PSD cone after an unconstrained gradient step. More sophisticated methods for constrained optimization exist that might handle the PSD constraint more gracefully, such as interior-point methods or Frank-Wolfe algorithms adapted for this domain. The paper's claims of superiority would be substantially stronger if the proposed EM algorithm were benchmarked against a more state-of-the-art constrained optimization technique, not just the simplest one. Is the main advantage of EM over *any* gradient-based method, or just this specific, arguably flawed, implementation?

2.  **Nature of the M-Step:** The M-step for the eigenvectors (V) is not a full maximization but rather a single gradient step. This makes the proposed algorithm a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm, not a classic EM algorithm. While this is a common and practical approach, the paper should be more explicit about this distinction. The convergence guarantees for GEM are weaker than for EM, and it would be beneficial to include a brief discussion of the convergence properties of the proposed algorithm in this light.

3.  **Sensitivity to Initialization:** The experiments reveal that the performance gap between EM and KA is highly dependent on initialization. With the moments-matching initializer, which provides a strong starting point, the advantage of EM in terms of likelihood is marginal (0.4% average gain). The advantage only re-emerges in a data-poor setting. This suggests that the primary benefit of the EM approach is its robustness to poor initializations, rather than an inherent ability to find a superior optimum from a reasonable starting point. While robustness is valuable, this context is important for assessing the overall significance of the contribution.

4.  **Computational Complexity of the M-Step:** The paper states the overall runtime includes a O(T1 * T2 * N^3) term, stemming from the line search for the eigenvector update step, which involves repeated matrix exponentials. This N^3 complexity can still be a bottleneck for moderately large N. Could the authors provide more detail on the typical number of iterations required for the line search (T2) in their experiments? Is this step a significant practical cost, and how does it scale as N increases?

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is strong, and the technical development of the EM algorithm is explained logically. The algorithms are presented clearly, and the experimental setup and results are easy to understand. The connection between the projection issue in the baseline and the proposed solution is a compelling narrative throughout the paper.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a solid and technically interesting contribution to the literature on learning DPPs. The proposed EM algorithm is a novel and principled approach that successfully addresses a known pathology of a standard baseline method (projected gradient ascent). The work is methodologically sound and supported by reasonable experiments on a relevant task.

However, my enthusiasm is tempered by the reliance on a somewhat weak baseline to demonstrate the method's superiority. The claims would be more convincing with a comparison to more advanced constrained optimization techniques. Furthermore, the fact that the performance gap largely disappears with a good initialization suggests the contribution is more about robustness than finding a globally superior solution in all cases.

Despite these reservations, the paper introduces a valuable new algorithm for a significant problem. The methodological novelty and the clear demonstration of the failure modes of simpler approaches make this a worthwhile contribution to the community. I lean towards acceptance, but would strongly encourage the authors to address the points above, particularly regarding the choice of baseline and the framing of their method's primary advantages.
