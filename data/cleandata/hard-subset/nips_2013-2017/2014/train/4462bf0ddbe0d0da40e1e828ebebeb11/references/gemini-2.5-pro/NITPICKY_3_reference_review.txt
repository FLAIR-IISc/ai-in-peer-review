### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. Learning the full DPP kernel via maximum likelihood is a challenging, likely NP-hard problem. Previous approaches have resorted to learning restricted parameterizations, mixtures of DPPs, or Bayesian methods on parametric kernels. The authors identify a key weakness in the naive approach of projected gradient ascent on the marginal kernel `K`: the projection step required to maintain positive semi-definiteness can lead to degenerate, near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture the diversity-promoting properties of DPPs.

The proposed solution recasts the problem by working with the eigendecomposition of the kernel `K`. Leveraging the generative view of DPP sampling, which involves a latent variable `J` that selects a subset of eigenvectors, the authors formulate an EM algorithm. The E-step involves computing the posterior distribution over this latent variable `J` given an observed set `Y_i`, which is shown to be a k-DPP. The M-step then optimizes the expected complete-data log-likelihood with respect to the eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The update for the eigenvalues has a closed-form solution, while the eigenvectors are updated via a single gradient step on the Stiefel manifold. The authors claim their EM algorithm is more robust to initialization, less prone to degenerate solutions, and asymptotically faster than the projected gradient ascent baseline. Experiments on a baby registry dataset support these claims, showing that EM produces better models (especially with poor initialization or sparse data) and runs faster.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Novelty:** The formulation of an EM algorithm for learning an unconstrained DPP kernel is a novel and clever contribution. The insight to use the latent eigenvector-selection variable `J` to structure the optimization is elegant and well-suited to the problem.
2.  **Methodological Improvement:** The algorithm directly addresses a significant flaw in the projected gradient ascent baseline—the tendency of the projection step to destroy off-diagonal information and produce near-diagonal kernels. By avoiding projection on the eigenvalues and using a manifold optimization approach for the eigenvectors, the proposed method appears more principled.
3.  **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a convincing empirical comparison against a relevant baseline (K-Ascent) on a real-world product recommendation task. The experiments are well-designed, testing different initializations and data regimes (data-poor setting), and clearly demonstrate the practical advantages of the EM approach in terms of both model quality and computational speed.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection):**

The paper, while containing a promising core idea, is marred by a substantial number of technical ambiguities, methodological shortcuts, and presentational flaws.

1.  **Lack of Technical Rigor in M-step:** The treatment of the eigenvector (V) update in the M-step is concerning.
    *   Equation (20) for the gradient `∂F/∂V` is stated to be valid only "for the initial gradient, where we have q = p". It is unclear how the gradient is computed in subsequent iterations of the EM algorithm. The paper seems to imply this same formula is used throughout Algorithm 2, which would be an approximation. The validity and impact of this approximation on convergence are not discussed.
    *   The M-step for `V` is not fully optimized; instead, a single gradient step is taken (making this a Generalized EM algorithm). The line search for this step is unconventional: it checks for improvement in the true log-likelihood `L(K)` rather than the auxiliary function `F`. This requires re-running the E-step for each trial step size, which seems computationally burdensome and blurs the clean separation of E and M steps. A more thorough analysis of this procedure and its convergence properties is required.

2.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is not self-contained, making it difficult to fully assess.
    *   Key derivations for Lemma 1, Equation (19), and Equation (20) are relegated to the supplement. While common, the lack of even a sketch of these derivations in the main text hinders understanding of the core algorithm mechanics.
    *   The "moment matching" initialization in Equation (23) is heuristic and not well-motivated. More critically, it concludes with a projection step ("clipping its eigenvalues at 0 and 1"), the very procedure that the paper argues is problematic for the baseline K-Ascent algorithm.

3.  **Numerous Formatting and Notational Errors:** The manuscript lacks the level of polish expected for a NIPS publication.
    *   **Notation:** The use of `>` for transpose instead of the standard `T` (e.g., `V >` instead of `V^T`) is non-standard and confusing. The notation for positive semi-definiteness `K ≻ 0` in Eq. (4) is incorrect; it should be `K ≽ 0`. In Eq. (21), the derivative is written `∂L/∂V`, which is inconsistent with the derivation in Eq. (20) that uses the auxiliary function `F`.
    *   **Citations:** The reference list is poorly formatted. Reference [1] has empty fields (`PhD thesis, University of Pennsylvania,, , ,`). Other references have inconsistent formatting for conference names and are missing information.
    *   **Artifacts:** The manuscript contains artifacts like the date "Tuesday, August 5, 14" at the bottom of page 6, and a footnote with a broken link to `code.google.com`, which was shut down years ago. The caption for Figure 1a is entirely missing.

### Decision

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the central idea of an EM algorithm for DPP learning is valuable and potentially significant, the current manuscript does not meet the high standards of NIPS. The execution of this idea is undermined by a crucial lack of clarity and justification in the M-step for the eigenvectors, which is a core component of the proposed algorithm. Furthermore, the paper is plagued by a distracting number of minor but cumulative errors in notation, formatting, and referencing. These issues, taken together, suggest a lack of care in the preparation of the work and prevent a confident assessment of its technical soundness. The paper would need a major revision to address the methodological ambiguities and to correct the numerous presentational flaws before it could be considered for publication.
