

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper addresses the important problem of learning the kernel matrix for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without restrictive assumptions or parametric forms. The authors propose an EM algorithm that leverages the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K, avoiding the problematic projection steps required by gradient ascent methods. This is a significant departure from prior work, including K-Ascent (projected gradient ascent), learning scalar weights for rows of L, and L-ensembles.

The paper's core insight is recognizing that DPPs can be viewed as mixtures of elementary DPPs (with specific eigenvectors), making them amenable to an EM framework. By working with the eigendecomposition rather than directly optimizing K, the authors eliminate the need for projection steps that often lead to degenerate, near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture diversity. The theoretical development is sound, with careful derivation of the E-step (using k-DPPs) and M-step (with closed-form eigenvalue updates and a Stiefel manifold update for eigenvectors). The asymptotic runtime analysis shows clear advantages over gradient ascent (O(T1nNk² + T1T2N³) vs O(T1nN³ + T1T2N³)).

The experiments on a real-world baby registry dataset (13 categories with 71 products each, 8,585 sub-registries) demonstrate the method's superiority. EM outperforms K-Ascent by 3.7% on average with Wishart initialization, and by 4.5% (up to 16.5%) in data-poor settings (n=2N). Crucially, the improvement is most pronounced when negative interactions between items are strong (d > 0.025), confirming the authors' hypothesis that K-Ascent's projection step causes degeneracy in these cases. The runtime analysis shows EM is 2.1x faster on average, validating the asymptotic analysis.

This work stands out for its novel approach to a well-known problem. Previous methods either made restrictive assumptions (Kulesza & Taskar, 2012; Affandi et al., 2013) or suffered from degenerate results (K-Ascent). The EM approach is fundamentally different and addresses a key limitation of prior work. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work in the introduction and discussion.

Strengths:
- Highly novel approach (EM via eigendecomposition) that solves a known problem in DPP learning
- Strong theoretical justification with careful derivations
- Clear experimental validation showing consistent improvements over baselines
- Practical runtime advantage with real-world data
- Well-written with clear organization

Weaknesses:
- Requires non-trivial initialization (avoiding near-diagonal K), though the paper provides two initialization methods
- The runtime advantage is asymptotic; the 2.1x speedup on moderate-sized data may not be dramatic for all applications
- Limited to the specific application of product recommendation (though the method is general)

The paper's novelty and significance far outweigh its minor weaknesses. The authors have introduced a fundamentally different approach to DPP learning that avoids a key limitation of prior methods. This is not just an incremental improvement but a novel direction that should be valuable to the DPP research community. The method's robustness to initialization (with the moment-matching initializer) and clear runtime advantage further strengthen its contribution.

I am particularly impressed by the elegance of the solution: using the eigendecomposition to transform the learning problem into one that avoids the problematic projection step. This is the kind of bold, novel approach that NIPS values. The paper makes a clear case for why the EM approach is superior to gradient ascent, with both theoretical and empirical evidence.

While the paper could have included more discussion of potential limitations (e.g., scalability to very large N), these are minor concerns given the paper's strong contribution. The authors acknowledge the initialization sensitivity and provide practical solutions, which is appropriate for a methodological contribution.

This paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on DPPs, offering a novel learning method that addresses a key limitation of previous approaches. It is not merely an incremental improvement but represents a new direction in DPP learning. The theoretical development is sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the method has clear practical advantages.

I strongly recommend acceptance. The novelty, significance, and technical soundness of this work make it an excellent fit for NIPS. The paper introduces a new concept that should be widely adopted and built upon by the DPP community, which aligns perfectly with NIPS's emphasis on novel, impactful contributions.
