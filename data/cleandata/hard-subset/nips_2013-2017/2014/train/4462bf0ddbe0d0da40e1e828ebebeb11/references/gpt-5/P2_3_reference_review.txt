Summary
The paper proposes an EM-style algorithm to learn a full, unconstrained DPP kernel from observed subsets by exploiting the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K. It introduces a hidden variable J corresponding to the subset of eigenvectors selected in the elementary DPP mixture representation. The E-step sets q(J|Y) = pK(J|Y) and shows this is a k-DPP with a computable kernel; the M-step has (i) closed-form eigenvalue updates via expected inclusion probabilities q(j ∈ J | Y) and (ii) a gradient step for eigenvectors on the Stiefel manifold using a matrix-exponential update. Compared to projected gradient ascent on K (KA), EM avoids degenerate projections that can drive K towards near-diagonality, better captures negative interactions, and has asymptotic runtime O(T1 n N k^2 + T1 T2 N^3) for k ≪ N. Experiments on an Amazon baby-registry dataset show improvements in test log-likelihood (notably when off-diagonal structure matters or data are scarce) and 2.1× speedup over KA.

Relation to prior work
- Distinguishes from prior partial learning approaches: item-quality scaling (diagonal weighting) and mixtures of fixed DPPs, which either ignore similarity structure or lose DPP properties. Also contrasts with Bayesian parametric learning that assumes kernel parameterizations. This paper aims at nonparametric learning of the full kernel without restrictive parameter forms.
- Uses the known mixture view of DPPs over elementary kernels, but leverages it for an EM algorithm with eigenvalue/eigenvector updates; to my knowledge, this specific EM formulation and the efficient k-DPP E-step/marginalization for eigenvalue updates are new.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The EM formulation is well-motivated. The E-step derivation (q is a k-DPP) and the eigenvalue update (λj = average inclusion probability) are natural and computationally efficient using k-DPP marginalization. The eigenvector update acknowledges the nonconvex Stiefel constraint and uses a principled manifold update.
- Caveats: (1) The eigenvector gradient formula (Eq. 20) is only valid at the initial q = p and the method takes a single gradient step per M-step; the bound is not fully optimized w.r.t. V. (2) Monotonicity/convergence guarantees of EM are weakened because the M-step is approximate and relies on line search with likelihood checks; while the algorithm enforces non-decreasing L by acceptance tests, theoretical convergence to a stationary point is not established. (3) Numerical issues when λ → 1 are mitigated by clipping but not deeply analyzed.
- Empirical evaluation: The comparison to KA is fair and highlights a specific failure mode of projection (near-diagonal degeneracy) that EM avoids. Results include both standard and data-scarce regimes, two initializations (Wishart, moment-matching), correlation analysis with a proxy for off-diagonal strength, wall-clock time, and an illustrative qualitative example. However, evaluation is limited to one domain, and the metric is primarily log-likelihood.

Clarity
- Generally clearly written, with helpful algorithms and a coherent narrative linking eigendecomposition, the hidden variable, and computational benefits. Key derivations are deferred to the supplement; this is acceptable but leaves the main text relying on references for some details. Minor typos exist. The complexity discussion is explicit.

Originality
- The EM formulation over the elementary-DPP mixture with efficient k-DPP-based E-step and closed-form eigenvalue updates is novel. Prior work did not provide a practical nonparametric MLE-like procedure for full K without restrictive parameterizations. Using Stiefel updates for eigenvectors in this setting is appropriate and, to my knowledge, novel for DPP learning.

Significance
- Learning full DPP kernels is important across applications where diversity matters. The method addresses a recognized bottleneck (projected gradient instabilities and NP-hardness conjecture) with an algorithm that is practically faster and more robust in tested regimes. While gains in log-likelihood are modest on average, they are substantial in regimes where diversity structure is pronounced or data are scarce. This work is likely to be used and extended (e.g., to low-rank or structured parameterizations) and provides a foundation for scalable DPP learning research.

Strengths
- Conceptually elegant EM leveraging the elementary-DPP mixture.
- Efficient and closed-form eigenvalue updates using k-DPP marginals.
- Principled handling of orthogonality via Stiefel manifold updates.
- Clear diagnosis of KA’s projection pathology; EM avoids near-diagonal traps.
- Demonstrated runtime benefits and improved robustness across inits and data regimes.
- Public code and concrete dataset.

Weaknesses
- Approximate M-step for V with no monotonic bound improvement guarantee; convergence theory is limited.
- Evaluation confined to a single domain; no tests on canonical DPP benchmarks (e.g., summarization, image search) or larger N.
- Baseline limited to simple projected gradient; missing comparisons to Bayesian DPP learning or low-rank/structured approaches.
- Scalability concerns for very large N: storing V ∈ R^{N×N} and repeated N^3 operations may be prohibitive; no exploration of low-rank variants.
- Empirical metrics focus on likelihood; task-level improvements (MAP set quality) are only qualitatively illustrated.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide convergence diagnostics (e.g., stationarity conditions, likelihood traces) and discuss when the approximate M-step may stall.
- Compare against a Bayesian parametric learner and a low-rank factorization baseline to contextualize performance and scalability.
- Evaluate on additional datasets (document summarization, image search) and report MAP set quality, not only likelihood.
- Explore low-rank EM variants where V is N×r with r ≪ N to reduce memory/time and potentially improve generalization.
- Clarify computational costs of computing Q_Yi eigen-decompositions and constants in the O(n N k^2) term.

Overall recommendation: Lean accept
Reasoning: The paper addresses an important and challenging problem with a novel, well-motivated algorithm that is demonstrably more robust and faster than a widely used baseline. While theoretical guarantees are limited and experiments could be broader, the contribution is concrete, technically sound, and practically useful, and should be of interest to the NIPS community working on probabilistic models and subset selection.
