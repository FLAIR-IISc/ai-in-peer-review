

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the kernel matrix of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a key limitation of previous gradient-based approaches. The authors identify that projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) for learning DPP parameters requires projection steps to maintain positive semi-definiteness, which often results in degenerate solutions (near-diagonal matrices that fail to capture diversity). The paper builds on prior work by Kulesza and Taskar [5, 12, 19] who established DPPs' utility in modeling diversity, and by Affandi et al. [8] who proposed Bayesian methods for learning parametric forms of DPPs. The authors' key contribution is an EM algorithm that exploits the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K, avoiding the problematic projection step while maintaining the computational advantages of DPPs.

The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The derivation of the EM algorithm is rigorous, with careful development of the E-step (using the hidden variable J representing the eigenvector set) and M-step (with closed-form eigenvalue updates and efficient eigenvector updates using Stiefel manifold optimization). The authors provide mathematical proofs for key steps (e.g., Lemma 1) and carefully analyze the computational complexity (O(T₁nNk² + T₁T₂N³) versus K-Ascent's O(T₁nN³ + T₁T₂N³)). The experimental evaluation on Amazon baby registry data (13 categories with 71 products on average) demonstrates consistent improvements over K-Ascent in terms of test log-likelihood (up to 16.5% gain) and runtime (2.1x faster on average). The analysis of how the relative gains correlate with the importance of off-diagonal kernel entries (d = 1/N ||M||F / ||diag(M)||₂) provides valuable theoretical insight into when the EM approach is most beneficial.

The paper excels in clarity. The background section concisely establishes DPP fundamentals and their relationship to the marginal kernel K. The algorithmic development is well-structured, with clear transitions between the motivation, derivation, and implementation details. The experimental section thoroughly evaluates the method with multiple initialization strategies (Wishart and moment-matching) and provides concrete examples (Figure 2) showing how EM produces more diverse recommendations than K-Ascent. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, noting that both methods require non-trivial initial off-diagonals and that the EM approach has a similar runtime complexity to K-Ascent when N is small.

The novelty of the approach is significant. Previous work either restricted the form of the kernel (e.g., [2] learned scalar weights for rows, [3] used L-ensembles, [8] used Bayesian methods with parametric forms) or relied on gradient methods with problematic projections. The EM approach presented here is the first to learn unconstrained, non-parametric DPP kernels without requiring projection steps, directly addressing a known limitation in the field. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, particularly in Section 1 where it systematically reviews and contrasts previous learning methods.

The significance of this work is well-established. DPPs are increasingly important in applications requiring diversity (e.g., recommendation systems, document summarization), and this method enables more effective learning of DPP parameters in these settings. The runtime advantage and robustness improvements make the approach more practical for real-world applications. The paper's empirical results demonstrate clear improvements in both likelihood and diversity modeling, with the strongest gains in categories where negative item interactions are most important (e.g., safety, carseats).

Minor weaknesses include:
1. The paper doesn't fully explore the scalability of the method for very large N (e.g., N > 10,000), which would be important for real-world applications.
2. The theoretical convergence properties of the EM algorithm aren't analyzed in depth, though this is common in EM papers.
3. The comparison is limited to K-Ascent, without benchmarking against other recent DPP learning methods.

Strengths outweigh weaknesses. The paper makes a clear technical contribution that addresses a known limitation in DPP learning, with strong theoretical foundations and empirical validation. The method is both novel and practically significant for the field.

Pro: The paper presents a technically sound, novel approach that directly addresses a well-known limitation in DPP learning. The EM algorithm avoids problematic projection steps, leading to more robust and diverse models. The theoretical development is rigorous, and the empirical results consistently demonstrate improvements over the baseline.

Con: The scalability for very large datasets isn't thoroughly explored, and the comparison to other recent learning methods is limited. The theoretical convergence properties could be more thoroughly analyzed.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in DPP learning. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling for subset selection tasks. It meets all NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, novelty, and significance. I recommend acceptance.
