This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), specifically tackling the intractability of maximum likelihood estimation. The authors propose an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm leveraging the eigendecomposition of the DPP kernel, aiming to avoid the pitfalls of gradient-based approaches, particularly the projection step that can lead to degenerate solutions. The motivation is sound – DPPs are useful for diverse subset selection – and the technical approach, building on existing DPP theory, is presented with reasonable detail.

However, while the paper identifies a real problem and proposes a technically plausible solution, I remain unconvinced of its substantial contribution, primarily due to the limited novelty and the presentation of experimental results. The core idea of using the eigendecomposition isn’t fundamentally new; it’s a well-established technique in DPP literature (see, e.g., [21] cited in the paper), and the EM framework, while applied cleverly, isn't a radical departure. The authors correctly identify limitations of existing approaches, but the EM algorithm feels more like a carefully engineered implementation detail than a breakthrough. The claimed asymptotic runtime improvement, while interesting, isn't fully substantiated in a way that clearly demonstrates practical benefit, especially considering the overhead of the EM procedure itself.

The experimental evaluation is also a weakness. The “baby registry” dataset is a reasonable application, but the comparison to the projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) method feels somewhat unfair. The authors themselves acknowledge that K-Ascent struggles with initialization and can get stuck in poor local optima *because* of the projection step. The moments-matching initialization improves K-Ascent’s performance, diminishing the EM algorithm’s advantage. The conclusion that EM is significantly better rests heavily on the Wishart initialization scenario, which seems artificially chosen to highlight EM’s strengths. The runtime comparison, while presented, lacks sufficient detail (e.g., hardware specifications, precise timings) to be fully convincing. A comparison to more sophisticated existing DPP learning methods, even those with limitations (like the Bayesian approach [8]), would be valuable. Furthermore, the observed gains are relatively modest (a few percentage points in log-likelihood), questioning the practical significance. The discussion of the parameter *d* is insightful, connecting the gains to the importance of off-diagonal elements, but it feels post-hoc.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the mathematical foundations. However, there are opportunities for improved clarity, particularly in the presentation of the M-step update for the eigenvectors (Equation 20), which relies on several intermediate definitions. More thorough cross-referencing within the supplementary material would also enhance readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a challenging problem in DPP learning.
*   Technically sound and builds on existing DPP theory.
*   Demonstrates a practical algorithm that avoids the pitfalls of gradient ascent.
*   Clear and reasonably well-written.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty – the core idea isn’t a radical departure.
*   Experimental results are not entirely convincing, relying on a specific initialization that favors the proposed EM algorithm.
*   Modest performance gains compared to existing methods.
*   Insufficient runtime analysis to convincingly demonstrate practical improvements.
*   Comparison to a somewhat straw-man implementation of gradient ascent.

**Overall assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the work is technically competent and addresses a relevant problem, the limited novelty and the unconvincing experimental results do not warrant acceptance to NIPS. The paper feels more like a well-engineered implementation of existing ideas than a significant advancement in the field. The authors would significantly strengthen the paper by including a more comprehensive comparison to established DPP learning methods, providing more robust experimental validation, and more clearly demonstrating the practical benefits of the proposed algorithm.
