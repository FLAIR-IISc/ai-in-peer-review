

# Review: Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes

This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the marginal kernel K of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a significant limitation in existing approaches. The authors demonstrate that gradient ascent methods for DPP learning require problematic projection steps that often produce degenerate, nearly diagonal solutions, failing to model the diversity-seeking nature of DPPs. Their EM-based approach avoids these projections by working with the eigendecomposition of K, resulting in more robust learning and asymptotic runtime improvements.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical grounding. The authors carefully develop the EM algorithm by:
1) Recognizing the eigendecomposition of K as key to DPP properties
2) Deriving the lower bound using Jensen's inequality
3) Providing closed-form updates for both eigenvalues and eigenvectors
4) Justifying the approach through theoretical analysis of the optimization landscape

The experimental validation on a real-world baby registry dataset is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare their EM method against the gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) across multiple initialization strategies and dataset conditions. The results clearly demonstrate EM's advantages: 3.7% average log-likelihood improvement over K-Ascent with Wishart initialization, and 4.5% average gain with limited data (n=2N training examples). The runtime analysis showing EM's asymptotic advantage (O(nNk²) vs. KA's O(nN³)) is particularly compelling.

The paper acknowledges limitations honestly, noting the sensitivity to initialization and the need for non-diagonal starting kernels. The authors also properly contextualize their work against prior methods, distinguishing their approach from row-weight learning [2], L-ensembles [3], and parametric Bayesian methods [8].

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section provides a concise yet complete overview of DPPs, their properties, and the learning problem. The algorithm development follows a logical progression from problem statement to theoretical justification to implementation details. Key concepts like the hidden variable J and the k-DPP formulation are clearly explained with appropriate mathematical notation.

The experimental section effectively communicates results through well-chosen visualizations (Figures 1-2) and meaningful metrics (log-likelihood, runtime). The supplemental material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The paper would be accessible to NIPS readers with background in machine learning, though some familiarity with DPPs would be helpful.

## Originality

This work represents a significant original contribution to the DPP learning literature. While prior work has explored DPP learning in restricted settings [2,3,8], the authors successfully develop a general method that doesn't require fixed values or restrictive parametric assumptions for the kernel. The key insight—using the eigendecomposition to avoid projection steps—addresses a fundamental limitation in existing approaches that has been noted but not solved previously.

The paper's distinction from prior work is clear and well-supported. The authors properly cite and contrast with relevant NIPS/ICML papers on DPPs [1, 2, 3, 8, 12, 15, 16, 17, 19, 20]. The EM approach represents a novel combination of standard optimization techniques with the specific properties of DPPs.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. DPPs are increasingly important for subset selection tasks where diversity and quality must be balanced (recommendation systems, document summarization, sensor placement). The proposed method directly addresses a practical limitation in DPP adoption—the degenerate solutions produced by existing learning approaches.

The baby registry experiments provide concrete evidence of the method's advantages in a real-world setting. The finding that EM performs particularly well when the true kernel has strong off-diagonal components (d > 0.025) aligns with the theoretical motivation and suggests the method will be most valuable in applications where negative item interactions are meaningful.

The asymptotic runtime advantage (2.1x faster on average) is particularly relevant for practical applications, as it enables learning on larger datasets without sacrificing model quality. The paper's conclusion that "EM will similarly have a significant advantage" in other applications where modeling negative interactions is important is well-supported.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong contribution to the DPP learning literature. It addresses a clear limitation in existing methods with a theoretically sound, practically effective solution. The quality of the theoretical analysis and experimental validation is high, the writing is clear, the approach is novel, and the significance is substantial.

The main limitation is that the experimental validation is limited to a single dataset (baby registries), though the authors do test across multiple categories within this dataset. Additional validation on other DPP application domains would strengthen the case for broader impact, but the current results are sufficiently compelling.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in DPP learning. The technical quality, clarity, originality, and significance all meet NIPS standards, with no major weaknesses that would prevent acceptance. The work has clear potential for impact in both theoretical and applied machine learning communities.

**Strengths:**
- Novel EM approach avoids problematic projection steps in DPP learning
- Strong theoretical justification with clear algorithmic development
- Comprehensive experimental validation showing clear advantages
- Asymptotic runtime analysis demonstrating practical benefits

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited to a single dataset (though the dataset is large and diverse)
- Could potentially include more comparisons to recent DPP learning methods
