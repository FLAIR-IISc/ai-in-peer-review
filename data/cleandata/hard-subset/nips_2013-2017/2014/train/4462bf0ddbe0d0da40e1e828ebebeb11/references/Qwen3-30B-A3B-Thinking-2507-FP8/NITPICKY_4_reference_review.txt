

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper proposes an EM algorithm for learning the marginal kernel K of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), as opposed to the naive projected gradient ascent approach (K-Ascent) that requires projection to maintain positive semi-definiteness. The authors claim their method is more robust to initialization and asymptotically faster than gradient ascent.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a clear contribution to the DPP learning literature by developing an EM-based approach that avoids the problematic projection steps required in gradient ascent. The theoretical development of the EM algorithm, including the E-step and M-step derivations, is generally well-motivated and carefully presented. The experimental results on the baby registry dataset demonstrate that the EM approach outperforms K-Ascent in terms of log-likelihood, particularly when the true kernel has significant off-diagonal elements.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Notational Confusion:** The paper consistently confuses L and K throughout. In Section 1, the authors state "learning L from example subsets" but then claim the EM algorithm "exploits the eigendecomposition of L" while later stating "we use the K-based formula for P(Y) and learn the marginal kernel K." The algorithm and theoretical derivations are all for K, not L. This confusion is problematic as the paper's main contribution is about learning K, yet it refers to "learning L" multiple times. The authors should consistently use K throughout the paper.

2. **Technical Inconsistencies:** 
   - In Equation (3), the expression "det(K − IY )" should be "det(K − I_Y)" where I_Y is the identity matrix with entries for Y set to zero.
   - In Section 3.1, the authors state "the computational complexity of KA is dominated by the matrix inverses of the L derivative" but the derivative is with respect to K, not L.
   - In Equation (1), the summation notation is incorrect: "K = N∑ j=1 λjvjv > j" should be "K = ∑_{j=1}^N λ_j v_j v_j^T".
   - The statement "L and K share the same eigenvectors v" is technically correct but should be explicitly justified since it's a key assumption.

3. **Algorithmic Details:**
   - Algorithm 1 has a minor but critical error: the projection step uses "min(max(λ, 0), 1)" but doesn't specify how to handle the case where λ > 1 (the min(λ,1) should come before max(λ,0)).
   - In Equation (17), "R = diag (√ λ/(1− λ) )" is incorrectly formatted; it should be "R = diag(√(λ/(1-λ)))".
   - The authors claim "the EM algorithm negates the need for the problematic projection step" but this is misleading since they're optimizing K, not L, and the projection issue applies to K, not L.

4. **Experimental Weaknesses:**
   - The paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests for the performance differences between EM and KA. The reported "average gains" are based on medians from 25 trials but without error bars or significance testing.
   - The dataset description is vague: "We discarded categories with N < 25 or fewer than 2N remaining (non-empty) sub-registries for training." It's unclear how this affects the results or whether the categories were selected based on this criterion.
   - The paper doesn't compare against other relevant methods in the DPP learning literature, such as the approach in Affandi et al. (ICML 2013).

5. **Minor Formatting Issues:**
   - In Section 4.1, "Wishart's mean is NI" should be "Wishart's mean is N*I" to clarify it's N times the identity matrix.
   - The reference to "Learning with Determinantal Point Processes" has an incomplete URL.
   - The paper states "the corresponding DPP will place a non-trivial amount of probability mass on small sets" but doesn't define what "small sets" means in this context.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper presents a theoretically sound EM algorithm for learning DPPs, the numerous notational inconsistencies, minor technical errors, and lack of statistical significance testing in the experiments are significant enough to warrant rejection. The confusion between L and K throughout the paper is particularly problematic as it undermines the clarity of the main contribution. The paper's technical content is sound but the presentation contains enough minor errors that would likely cause confusion for readers attempting to implement the method.

The paper's contribution is incremental rather than groundbreaking, and the claimed advantages over existing methods (particularly the asymptotic speedup) are not sufficiently demonstrated. The experiments are limited to a single dataset, and the authors do not provide adequate comparisons to other learning methods.

Given the perfectionist criteria for NIPS, where even minor technical inconsistencies can lead to rejection, I recommend rejecting this paper. The authors should address the notational inconsistencies and provide more rigorous experimental validation before resubmitting.
