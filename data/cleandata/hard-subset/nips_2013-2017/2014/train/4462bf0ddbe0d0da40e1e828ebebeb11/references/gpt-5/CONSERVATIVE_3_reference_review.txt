Summary
The paper proposes an EM-style algorithm to learn unconstrained determinantal point process (DPP) kernels from observed subsets. The key idea is to move from entry-wise optimization of the marginal kernel K to its eigendecomposition (eigenvalues/eigenvectors), introducing a latent variable J that indexes which eigenvectors are “active.” Using the known mixture-of-elementary-DPP view, the authors derive a Jensen lower bound and obtain: (i) a closed-form, self-normalizing update for eigenvalues via k-DPP marginals; and (ii) a gradient step for eigenvectors under a Stiefel-manifold update, where the tractable gradient expression holds when q = p (the first step) and they then perform generalized EM with line search by checking the true likelihood. They argue this avoids the degeneracy caused by projection in naive projected gradient ascent on K, improves robustness to initialization, and is asymptotically faster when subsets are small. Experiments on Amazon baby registries compare EM to a projected gradient baseline (KA) under two initializations (Wishart and moment-matching), showing modest test log-likelihood gains (3.7% median with Wishart, ~0.4% with moment-matching) and ~2× speedup, with larger gains in data-poor settings and categories with stronger negative interactions.

Relation to prior work
The paper positions itself against prior partial learning approaches: (1) convex per-item quality weights; (2) mixtures of fixed DPPs; (3) parametric/Bayesian models of L. The eigen-decomposition view, k-DPP machinery, and mixture interpretation are standard in the DPP literature, but using them to construct a generalized EM for full, unconstrained K is novel to my knowledge. That said, the empirical comparison is limited to a single baseline (their KA) and does not engage with alternative, established optimization paradigms for PSD-constrained learning (e.g., low-rank/factorized parameterizations, mirror descent on the spectahedron, proximal methods, or Bayesian/parametric DPP learners).

Strengths
- Clear motivation: projection in KA can zero eigenvalues and trap the optimizer near-diagonal, harming diversity modeling; the EM formulation neatly sidesteps this.
- Sensible use of DPP structure: exploiting the mixture-of-elementary DPPs leads to a clean E-step (a k-DPP) and closed-form eigenvalue updates via marginals; this is elegant and well aligned with known DPP algorithms.
- Complexity analysis suggests advantages when subset sizes are small (k ≪ N), which is a common regime.
- The baby registry study is realistic, shows robustness to poor initializations, and includes both likelihood and runtime comparisons. Linking EM’s gains to a simple statistic (off-diagonal mass in empirical marginals) is insightful.

Weaknesses
Quality
- The “EM” is effectively generalized EM with one eigenvector gradient step and a line search based on recomputing the E-step. There is no convergence analysis or monotonic lower-bound guarantee; the method checks true likelihood improvement, but this is not a standard EM monotonicity guarantee and the M-step objective is acknowledged intractable. A clearer theoretical treatment (global convergence to stationary points, or at least monotonicity of the lower bound under the proposed inexact M-step) is missing.
- The eigenvector gradient in Eq. (20) is derived only at q = p and “valid for the first gradient step,” after which the paper does not provide a principled approximation for subsequent steps beyond repeating E-steps and checking likelihood. This undermines the methodological soundness claim.
- The baseline is narrow. KA is a strawman that suffers from an avoidable projection pathology. Established alternatives (low-rank factorization L = BB^T with manifold optimization; parameterizing eigenvalues via logistic transforms; spectahedral mirror descent; proximal/Frank–Wolfe on PSD cones; structured parametric/Bayesian DPP learners) are not compared. Without these, it is hard to assess the claimed advantages.
- Overfitting and regularization are not discussed. Full N×N K is highly parameterized; outside of eigenvalue capping, there is no penalty (e.g., nuclear norm, Frobenius, sparsity) or cross-validation of regularization strength. The data-poor setting (n = 2N) especially calls for regularization; yet EM still appears superior without addressing potential overfitting.
- Empirical evaluation relies almost entirely on test log-likelihood on a single domain. There is no predictive task (e.g., next-item prediction), MAP set quality evaluation, or comparison to independent models/submodular alternatives to demonstrate practical significance.

Clarity
- Generally well written and well organized. However, the limitations around Eq. (20), the generalized EM nature, and the lack of a monotonic lower-bound guarantee should be made prominent and precise.
- Several key derivations and complexity details are deferred to the supplement; some of these (e.g., the stability of q(J|Y) when λ → 1, numerical safeguards) are important for reproducibility.

Originality
- Casting DPP learning as generalized EM over eigenstructures is novel and interesting. The k-DPP E-step and closed-form eigenvalue updates are nice contributions.

Significance
- Potentially useful if it indeed scales better and avoids projection degeneracy; however, significance is limited by the narrow baseline and the lack of broader validation across domains and against stronger competitors. Reported likelihood gains are modest under stronger initialization.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide convergence guarantees (e.g., that your generalized EM increases the Jensen bound F, or at least converges to a stationary point under mild assumptions)?
- How sensitive is performance to near-unity eigenvalues (R exploding in Eq. (17))? What thresholds and their impact?
- How does EM compare to low-rank factorized learning (e.g., optimizing V ∈ R^{N×r}, λ ∈ [0,1]^r with r ≪ N) both in accuracy and runtime?
- Can you report results on additional datasets (e.g., summarization, image search) and practical tasks (prediction, MAP selection quality), and include independent and parametric DPP baselines?
- Do you employ any regularization? If not, can you quantify overfitting (train vs test likelihood) especially in the low-data regime?

Pros
- Elegant use of DPP structure; closed-form eigenvalue updates.
- Avoids known projection pitfalls of naive KA; empirically more robust to initialization.
- Potential runtime advantages; decent clarity.

Cons
- Generalized EM without clear convergence guarantees; eigenvector update only partially justified.
- Limited baselines; no comparison to established PSD/factorized optimization methods or parametric/Bayesian DPP learning.
- Single-domain experiments; modest gains under stronger initialization; limited task-level validation.
- No regularization/overfitting analysis.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The approach is promising, but the current evidence and methodological rigor are insufficient relative to the claims. Strengthening the theoretical guarantees, widening baselines, adding regularization, and validating on multiple domains/tasks would make this substantially more compelling.
