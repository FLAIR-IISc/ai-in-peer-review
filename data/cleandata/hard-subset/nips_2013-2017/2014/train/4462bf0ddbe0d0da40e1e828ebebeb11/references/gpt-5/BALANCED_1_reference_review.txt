Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes an EM-style algorithm to learn unconstrained determinantal point process (DPP) kernels from observed subsets. Rather than optimizing the marginal kernel K entrywise under PSD and spectral constraints via projected gradient ascent (KA), the authors reparameterize K by its eigendecomposition (eigenvalues λ and eigenvectors V) and introduce a latent variable J indicating which eigenvectors are active. Using the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs view, they derive a Jensen lower bound and perform coordinate ascent: an E-step sets q(J|Y)=pK(J|Y), which is shown to be a k-DPP, and M-steps update λ in closed form using k-DPP marginals and take a Stiefel-manifold-constrained gradient step on V. The resulting algorithm avoids the potentially harmful spectral projection in KA, is more robust to initialization, and is asymptotically faster when observed sets are small (k ≪ N). Experiments on a large Amazon baby-registry dataset show consistent test log-likelihood gains and 2× speedups over KA, especially when negative interactions (off-diagonals) matter.

The work builds on and contrasts with: (i) partial learning schemes such as learning per-item quality weights (convex) [2] and mixtures of fixed DPPs [3], which either restrict kernel structure or lose DPP properties; (ii) Bayesian/parametric approaches to DPP kernel learning [8]; and (iii) recent DPP applications and algorithms [5, 16, 21]. The spectral/lattice formulation and use of k-DPP conditionals connects to standard DPP sampling/marginalization techniques, while the Stiefel-manifold update follows matrix optimization literature [26].

Strengths
- Technical idea: Clever exploitation of the DPP’s spectral mixture representation with a latent J leads to a principled generalized-EM framework. The E-step characterization of q(J|Y) as a k-DPP is elegant and useful.
- Algorithmic practicality: Closed-form, self-normalizing λ updates via k-DPP marginals; efficient gradient for V at O(n N k^2); appropriate Stiefel-manifold update avoiding ad hoc projections.
- Clear identification of KA failure mode: Spectral projection can collapse to near-diagonal K, impeding modeling of repulsion; the EM method mitigates this.
- Empirical evidence: On a real, sizable dataset (13 categories, ~8.6k sub-registries per category), EM improves test log-likelihood and wall-clock time; analysis correlates EM gains with off-diagonal “importance.”
- Reproducibility: Code and dataset details are provided; two sensible initialization strategies are discussed.

Weaknesses and areas for improvement
Quality
- Convergence theory: The method is a generalized EM with an inexact M-step for V (single gradient step using only the “initial” derivative in Eq. (20)). The paper monitors the true likelihood L to pick step sizes, but does not establish monotonicity of the lower bound F or of L across full E/M cycles. A formal statement clarifying that this is generalized EM and conditions under which L (or F) is guaranteed non-decreasing would strengthen the contribution.
- V-update approximation: Eq. (20) is valid only for the first gradient step (q=p). The algorithm alternates with fresh E-steps during line search, but the derivation and potential bias of using an approximate gradient thereafter are not fully analyzed. A discussion of the approximation error and its empirical impact would be valuable.
- Numerical stability: In both KA and EM, matrices of the form K−IY and HYi can be ill-conditioned (e.g., λ near 1; diagonal dominance). The paper briefly suggests capping λ below 1; more implementation details (regularization/damping, conditioning) would help reproducibility.
- Complexity claims: The stated O(n N k^2) cost of Eq. (19) relies on structure in QYi; a brief sketch in the main text of how eigen-quantities are computed without O(N^3) per example would clarify the claim.

Clarity
- The interplay between L and the lower bound F in the line search is somewhat confusing. A tighter narrative explaining which objective is monitored when (and why) would improve readability.
- Some key derivations (Lemma 1, Eqs. (19)–(20)) are deferred to the supplement. Given their centrality, a concise derivation or intuitive argument in the main text would help.
- Minor typos (“negelecting”), and a few notation jumps (switching between U,V, VYi, BYi) slow reading.

Originality
- The spectral EM framework for unconstrained DPP learning, with a latent J and k-DPP E-step, is, to my knowledge, novel and nontrivial. Prior works either constrain K structurally or adopt Bayesian parametrics. The combination of k-DPP conditionals, closed-form λ updates, and Stiefel updates of V appears original.

Significance
- Learning full, nonparametric DPP kernels reliably is important for practical subset selection with diversity. The method addresses a known bottleneck (projection-induced degeneracy) and shows speed/robustness gains. However, significance would be stronger with additional domains (e.g., document summarization, image search) and more diverse baselines (e.g., Bayesian parametric kernels [8], mixtures [3], or recent scalable DPP learning approximations).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a generalized-EM convergence statement (monotonic increase in L under the proposed line search) or empirical monotonicity plots; clarify whether F or L is used to guarantee progress.
- Expand on the efficient computation behind Eq. (19) and Eq. (20), including numerical stabilization for λ≈1 and for inverting HYi.
- Add baselines: a parametric/Bayesian kernel-learning method [8], and possibly a mixture-of-DPPs approach [3], to gauge gains beyond KA.
- Evaluate on at least one additional public benchmark (e.g., summarization, image search) and include task-level metrics (MAP selection quality), not just likelihood.
- Sensitivity analyses: initialization, k, step-size strategy, and λ-capping threshold. Inspect learned spectra and off-diagonal structure to support claims about modeling repulsion.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled EM framework for unconstrained DPP learning.
- Practical, efficient updates; avoids projection pitfalls.
- Clear empirical improvements in both likelihood and runtime on a realistic dataset.
- Good alignment with established DPP theory and optimization on manifolds.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical scope and baselines.
- Inexact M-step for V with unclear convergence guarantees; reliance on heuristic line search.
- Some key derivations deferred; complexity and numerical-stability details could be clearer.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The methodological contribution is solid and relevant, the empirical gains are meaningful, and the approach is likely to be useful to practitioners working with DPPs. Strengthening the convergence discussion and expanding the experimental evaluation would make this a clear accept.
