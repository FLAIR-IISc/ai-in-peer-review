

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the marginal kernel K of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), contrasting with the existing projected gradient ascent method (K-Ascent) that requires projection steps to maintain positive semi-definiteness. The authors claim their approach avoids the degenerate solutions produced by K-Ascent and is asymptotically faster.

## Strengths

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The mathematical derivations are thorough, particularly the development of the EM algorithm using the eigendecomposition of K. The connection between the DPP theory and the EM algorithm is well-explained, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with DPPs. The experimental section provides a reasonable validation on a real-world dataset (baby registries), showing the proposed method outperforms K-Ascent in terms of test log-likelihood and runtime.

## Weaknesses

Despite the technical soundness of the EM derivation, the paper has significant shortcomings in terms of validation and comparison to established methods. The most concerning issue is the limited comparison to relevant baselines. The authors only compare to K-Ascent (Algorithm 1), but the introduction mentions several other established approaches to learning DPPs that should be compared against:

1. Affandi et al. (2013) developed a method for learning DPP kernels using maximum likelihood that avoids the projection issues through a different parameterization. This work is directly relevant but not properly compared against in the experiments.
2. Kulesza and Taskar (2011) presented a method for learning a scalar weight for each row of L, which the authors acknowledge as "partial learning" but don't compare against.
3. The paper also mentions "L-ensemble" methods (from [3]) and Bayesian approaches (from [8]) but fails to demonstrate how their EM method compares to these established techniques.

The experimental validation is also limited to a single dataset (baby registries), and the paper doesn't demonstrate the method's robustness across multiple domains or datasets. The comparison to K-Ascent is somewhat unbalanced: when using a moments-matching initialization, the performance gap between EM and K-Ascent disappears (0.4% average gain), suggesting the advantage of EM might be limited to poor initialization scenarios rather than being a fundamental improvement.

The claim about asymptotic runtime advantages is not convincingly demonstrated. The paper states that EM has runtime O(T1nNk² + T1T2N³) versus KA's O(T1nN³ + T1T2N³), but the experiments don't clearly show this advantage in practice. The runtime comparison in Figure 2b shows EM is "2.1 times faster" on average, but this is for moderate N and n (N=71, n=8,585), not demonstrating the asymptotic advantage they claim.

The theoretical justification for why K-Ascent fails (Section 3.1) is somewhat hand-wavy. The authors state that "the KA algorithm cannot easily move to any highly non-diagonal matrix" but don't provide rigorous evidence of this behavior or demonstrate how the EM algorithm specifically overcomes this limitation. The claim that "projection can sometimes lead to learning a nearly diagonal L" isn't supported by sufficient empirical evidence in the paper.

## Significance and Originality

The paper's main contribution—applying EM to DPP learning—is not particularly novel. The EM framework has been successfully applied to many other probabilistic models, and the paper doesn't make a compelling case for why this specific application is novel or substantially different from existing approaches. The paper's claim that "this is the first EM approach for DPP learning" isn't supported by a thorough literature review.

The paper also fails to adequately reference related work. Affandi et al. (2013) developed a method for learning DPP kernels that avoids the projection step by using a different parameterization, which is directly relevant to the paper's claims. The authors should have compared their approach against this work.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a theoretically sound EM algorithm for DPP learning, the lack of comprehensive comparison to established baselines, limited experimental validation, and insufficient demonstration of the claimed advantages make it difficult to assess the true contribution of this work. The paper claims to overcome a fundamental limitation of gradient ascent methods, but the evidence provided doesn't convincingly demonstrate this.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Comparing against Affandi et al. (2013) and other established DPP learning methods
2. Demonstrating the method's performance across multiple datasets and applications
3. Providing more rigorous evidence for the claimed asymptotic runtime advantages
4. Better explaining why the EM approach avoids the degeneracy issues that plague K-Ascent

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of new approaches without thorough validation, I find this paper's claims insufficiently supported. The paper presents a technically sound algorithm, but the evidence for its superiority over existing methods is incomplete and unconvincing. Without more comprehensive validation against relevant baselines, the paper's contribution appears marginal.

I recommend rejection, as the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to justify its claims of superiority over existing methods. The authors should address these validation gaps before the work can be considered a significant contribution to the field.
