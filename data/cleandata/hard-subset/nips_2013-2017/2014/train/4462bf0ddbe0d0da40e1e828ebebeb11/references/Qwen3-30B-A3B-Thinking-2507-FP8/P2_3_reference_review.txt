

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the NP-hardness of maximum likelihood estimation for unconstrained kernel matrices. The authors propose an EM-based algorithm that leverages the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel matrix K, avoiding the problematic projection steps required by gradient ascent methods. Their approach works with the eigenvalues and eigenvectors as parameters, developing a novel EM procedure that alternates between an E-step (computing posterior probabilities over hidden eigenvector indices) and an M-step (updating eigenvalues and eigenvectors). The paper demonstrates theoretically and empirically that this method is more robust to initialization, produces better likelihoods, and has better asymptotic runtime than gradient ascent approaches.

The work builds on Kulesza & Taskar's foundational DPP literature [5, 15] and addresses limitations in prior learning approaches: [2] only learns item quality (not similarity), [3] loses DPP properties by learning ensembles, and [8] assumes parametric forms for L. The authors position their work as the first to learn unconstrained, non-parametric L without projection steps.

## Strengths

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous mathematical derivations. The theoretical analysis of the EM algorithm, including the E-step (Lemma 1) and M-step (Equations 18-20), is well-developed. The authors clearly demonstrate the limitations of gradient ascent (Section 3.1), showing how projection steps lead to nearly diagonal matrices that fail to model diversity. The experimental results on the Amazon baby registry dataset provide strong empirical support for their claims, with clear metrics showing EM's advantages in likelihood (up to 16.5% gain) and runtime (2.1× faster on average).

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations are presented with clear explanations, and the algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are precise. The connection between the eigendecomposition and the DPP properties is elegantly presented. The experimental section clearly describes the dataset, initialization methods, and evaluation metrics, making it straightforward for readers to understand and potentially reproduce the results.

**Originality**: The core contribution—applying EM to DPP learning via eigendecomposition—is novel. While EM is a standard technique, its application to DPP learning in this specific way (without projection steps) represents a significant methodological advance. The paper clearly differentiates from prior work and provides a thorough literature review. The authors also extend k-DPP theory to develop the E-step, which is a valuable theoretical contribution in itself.

**Significance**: The paper addresses a substantial limitation in DPP applications—learning the kernel without restrictive assumptions. This has direct practical implications for diverse applications like document summarization, sensor placement, and product recommendation where modeling both quality and diversity is crucial. The demonstrated improvements in likelihood and runtime make this a valuable contribution to the DPP literature, with clear potential for broader adoption across multiple domains.

## Weaknesses

**Quality**: While the theoretical analysis is strong, the paper lacks convergence guarantees for the EM algorithm. The authors note the non-convexity of the eigenvector update (Section 3.5) but do not provide any analysis of convergence behavior or empirical evidence of convergence rates. This is a minor but notable gap in the theoretical foundation.

**Clarity**: The paper's focus on a single application domain (baby registry recommendations) limits its generalizability claims. While the application is well-motivated, additional experiments on other DPP applications (e.g., document summarization or sensor placement) would strengthen the significance argument. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of how the method scales to larger datasets (N > 10,000) beyond the moderate N ≈ 71 in their experiments.

**Originality**: The paper could better differentiate from recent work on DPP learning. For instance, [13] (Approximate Inference in Continuous Determinantal Point Processes) and [18] (Markov Determinantal Point Processes) present alternative learning approaches that should be compared against. The related work section could be expanded to include these more recent NIPS papers.

**Significance**: The practical significance is slightly limited by the single-application focus. While the baby registry example is compelling, a broader discussion of how this method could impact other DPP applications would strengthen the significance argument. The paper could also better articulate the practical barriers to adoption (e.g., computational requirements for very large N).

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the DPP learning literature. The EM approach addresses a well-known limitation of prior methods (projection steps leading to degenerate solutions) with a theoretically sound and practically effective solution. The paper is well-written, technically rigorous, and demonstrates clear advantages over the baseline gradient ascent method.

The weaknesses identified are relatively minor in the context of the paper's overall quality and contribution. The lack of convergence guarantees is a theoretical shortcoming but doesn't undermine the practical results. The single-application focus is understandable given space constraints but could be briefly addressed in future work.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), presents a novel method (Originality), and addresses an important problem with practical impact (Significance). The results advance the state of the art in DPP learning in a demonstrable way.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the DPP learning literature with a novel, theoretically sound, and empirically effective algorithm. The method is well-motivated, rigorously derived, and demonstrated to be superior to existing approaches in both likelihood and runtime. While there are minor weaknesses in the theoretical analysis and application scope, these do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution to the field. The work will be of interest to researchers working on probabilistic modeling, subset selection, and applications of DPPs.
