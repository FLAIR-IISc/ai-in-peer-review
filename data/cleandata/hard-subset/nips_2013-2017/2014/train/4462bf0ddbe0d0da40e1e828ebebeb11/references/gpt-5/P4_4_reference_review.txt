Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses maximum likelihood learning of determinantal point processes (DPPs) over a ground set of size N. A DPP is parameterized by a positive semidefinite kernel L (or equivalently, the marginal kernel K = L(L+I)−1), and induces probabilities over subsets that balance item quality and diversity. Prior work often avoids learning a full, unconstrained kernel due to nonconvexity and hardness: learning scalar quality weights is convex but ignores similarity structure; mixtures of fixed DPPs break the DPP structure; and Bayesian approaches impose parametric forms. The paper proposes an expectation–maximization (EM) algorithm that learns an unconstrained K by exploiting the eigendecomposition K = VΛV^T. It builds on the mixture-of-elementary-DPP view: a DPP is a mixture over subsets J of eigenvectors, with mixture weights determined by eigenvalues. Introducing J as a latent variable, the E-step sets q(J|Y) = pK(J|Y), which the authors show is a k-DPP (k = |Y|) with a computable kernel. The M-step updates eigenvalues in closed form via k-DPP marginals and takes a gradient step for eigenvectors on the Stiefel manifold using a multiplicative update. The method avoids the projection step required by naive projected gradient ascent on K, which can yield degenerate near-diagonal solutions, and achieves better asymptotic complexity when observed set sizes k are small. Experiments on a large Amazon baby registry dataset compare EM to projected gradient ascent (KA), showing improved test likelihood, robustness to initialization, and faster runtime. The work relates to DPP learning and inference literature (e.g., Kulesza–Taskar, Affandi et al.) but differs by learning a nonparametric kernel via an EM scheme grounded in the eigendecomposition.

Strengths
- Methodological contribution: A principled EM/GEM formulation leveraging the mixture-of-elementary-DPP representation. The E-step identifies q(J|Y) as a k-DPP; the eigenvalue M-step has a simple closed-form update that is self-normalizing and efficiently computable using k-DPP marginalization (Eq. 19).
- Practical optimization details: Eigenvector updates respect the orthogonality constraint via a Stiefel manifold update (Eq. 21), avoiding naive projection. The algorithm enforces likelihood improvement through step-size search.
- Complexity: The per-iteration cost O(nN k^2 + T2 N^3), with k = max |Yi|, is favorable relative to KA’s O(nN^3 + T2 N^3) when subsets are small, a common DPP regime.
- Empirical evidence: On 13 product categories, EM improves median test log-likelihood (notably with Wishart initialization), is more robust in data-poor settings, and is ~2× faster than KA. The analysis connects EM’s advantage to stronger off-diagonal structure in the true kernel and illustrates qualitative differences in MAP selections.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Theoretical guarantees: The algorithm is effectively a generalized EM (single gradient step for V, and step acceptance by checking L rather than the lower bound F). Monotonic improvement of the true likelihood is empirically enforced via backtracking, but the paper does not provide a formal proof of monotonicity for the stated updates or clarify the relationship between the F and L checks in Algorithm 2 (note that δ uses F while the acceptance check uses L). Clarifying this would strengthen the technical soundness.
- Clarity and notation: Lemma 1’s construction of QYi (QYi = R ZYi R with R ∈ RN×N and ZYi ∈ R|Yi|×|Yi|) is dimensionally ambiguous in the main text; an explicit embedding of ZYi into an N×N matrix (or explicit use of selection matrices) would improve readability. Similarly, defining BYi earlier and specifying the shapes of U, UYi, ZYi, HYi in Eq. (20) would aid reproducibility without relying on the supplement.
- Scope of experiments: Comparisons are limited to a single baseline (KA). While the paper’s goal is nonparametric K learning, comparing against representative parametric/Bayesian DPP learning (e.g., Affandi et al., ICML) or L-ensemble mixtures (Lin & Bilmes; Kulesza & Taskar) on likelihood or predictive tasks would contextualize gains. Synthetic recovery experiments could assess convergence to ground truth and sensitivity to initialization and subset size k.
- Numerical stability: The method requires clamping λ away from 1 to avoid infinities in R; a short discussion of the practical threshold and its effect on performance would be useful. Similarly, the cost of repeatedly recomputing E-steps during line search deserves a brief empirical profile.

Quality
- The derivations for the KA gradient (Eq. 5), EM lower bound (Eq. 10–13), eigenvalue update (Eq. 18–19), and first-step eigenvector gradient (Eq. 20) are well-motivated, and the optimization respects constraints via manifold updates. Some steps are deferred to the supplement; bringing key dimensional details into the main text would improve completeness. The algorithm is sound as a GEM; formal monotonicity conditions could be more explicit.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a coherent narrative from background to algorithm to experiments. Notational precision around the E-step/k-DPP construction and gradient shapes could be improved. Algorithm pseudocode is helpful, though the F vs L acceptance criterion should be made consistent and justified.

Originality
- Using the eigendecomposition latent-variable view to derive an EM procedure for unconstrained DPP kernel learning appears novel. The combination of closed-form eigenvalue updates via k-DPP marginals and Stiefel-manifold updates for eigenvectors in this context is an original and useful synthesis. Connections to prior DPP work are appropriately cited.

Significance
- Learning full DPP kernels is important for applications where diversity modeling matters. The proposed method addresses practical issues of previous approaches (projection degeneracy, cost) and shows empirical benefits on a sizable real dataset. The asymptotic speedup when k is small is relevant in many applications. Broader validation would further establish impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean EM formulation leveraging DPP structure.
- Addresses a practical and recognized pain point (projection degeneracy in KA).
- Solid complexity analysis and observed speedups.
- Empirical gains in likelihood and robustness on a real-world dataset.
- Method likely to be adopted by practitioners working with DPPs.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited breadth of baselines and domains; no synthetic recovery studies.
- Minor inconsistencies in the algorithmic presentation (F vs L) and reliance on supplement for key derivations.
- No formal convergence/monotonicity proof for the generalized M-step on V.
- Numerical stability near λ ≈ 1 is handled heuristically without analysis.

Overall recommendation
This is a technically sound and practically useful contribution to DPP learning with moderate novelty and clear empirical benefits. With clarifications on the E-step construction, acceptance criterion, and expanded comparisons, it would be a strong addition.
