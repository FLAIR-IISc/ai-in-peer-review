## Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix *L* (or its marginal form *K*) for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) from observed subsets. Existing methods either partially learn *L* (e.g., learning quality weights) or resort to approximations that sacrifice DPP properties (e.g., ensembles of DPPs, Bayesian parameterizations). The authors propose an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that leverages the eigendecomposition of *L* to avoid the problematic projection step inherent in naive gradient ascent, which often leads to degenerate solutions (near-diagonal *L*). The core idea is to introduce a hidden variable representing the eigenvectors defining the DPP, allowing for a more stable and efficient optimization process. The authors demonstrate empirically that their EM algorithm outperforms gradient ascent (K-Ascent) in terms of log-likelihood and runtime, particularly in scenarios where negative interactions between items are important.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a fundamental challenge:** Learning DPPs is critical for leveraging their benefits in diverse applications, and the NP-hardness conjecture makes this a significant open problem.
* **Novel and well-motivated approach:** The use of EM, coupled with eigendecomposition, is a clever way to bypass the difficulties associated with directly optimizing the likelihood function subject to PSD constraints.  The motivation stemming from the DPP's generative process through eigenvectors is strong.
* **Theoretical justification:** The authors provide a clear derivation of the EM algorithm, including updates for both eigenvalues and eigenvectors, and analyze their computational complexity.
* **Empirical validation:** The experiments on baby registry data demonstrate the effectiveness of the EM algorithm compared to K-Ascent, especially in data-poor settings or when diversity is crucial. The analysis of the "d" parameter linking to EM’s gains is insightful.
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is well-structured, and the technical details are presented clearly. The connections to related work are well-defined.
* **Practical considerations:** The authors acknowledge and address practical challenges like initialization strategies and step-size selection, and provide a robust implementation (code available).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the M-step:** While the eigenvalue update is closed-form, the eigenvector update requires approximations and relies on a single gradient step with a step size search.  The authors acknowledge this limitation but a more detailed discussion of the impact of this simplification would be helpful. The dependence on the Stiefel manifold update, while acknowledged, feels a bit hand-waved; more insight into its practical performance impact would be useful.
* **Scalability limitations:** Although asymptotically faster than gradient ascent, the runtime complexity of O(T1nNk2 + T1T2N3) still depends on *N* and *k*, where *k* is the maximum subset size.  This may become prohibitive for very large *N* and/or large typical subset sizes. The paper doesn't explicitly discuss these limitations.
* **Dataset specificity:** The experiments are conducted on a single dataset (baby registries). While the results are compelling, it would be beneficial to see performance on diverse datasets with different characteristics to demonstrate the generalizability of the approach.
* **Limited comparison to other DPP learning methods:** The comparison is primarily to K-Ascent. While a reasonable baseline, a comparison to more recent or specialized DPP learning techniques (beyond the brief mentions in the introduction) would strengthen the evaluation.



**Originality:**

The approach of applying EM to DPP learning via eigendecomposition is novel. While EM is a standard technique, its application to this specific problem, circumventing the difficulties of direct optimization, is a significant contribution. The use of the hidden variable representing the eigenvectors is a key insight.

**Significance:**

This work has the potential to significantly impact the field of DPP modeling. The proposed EM algorithm provides a more robust and efficient way to learn DPPs, enabling their application to a wider range of problems.  The ability to capture negative interactions between items, as demonstrated in the experiments, is particularly valuable for applications like recommendation systems and document summarization.  The insights into the limitations of gradient ascent and the importance of negative interactions are valuable contributions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach. The theoretical justification is solid, and the empirical results are compelling. While there are some limitations in terms of scalability and dataset diversity, the overall contribution is significant and warrants acceptance. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to DPP learning.
* Empirically outperforms a reasonable baseline (K-Ascent).
* Addresses a fundamental challenge in the field.
* Clear writing and well-organized presentation.
* Code publicly available.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Scalability may be a concern for very large datasets.
* Limited evaluation on diverse datasets.
* Simplifications in the eigenvector update step.
* Limited comparison to a wider range of DPP learning methods.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** (with minor revisions). I recommend acceptance, suggesting the authors address the scalability concerns in the discussion, consider expanding the dataset evaluation, and provide more details on the impact of the eigenvector update simplification.
