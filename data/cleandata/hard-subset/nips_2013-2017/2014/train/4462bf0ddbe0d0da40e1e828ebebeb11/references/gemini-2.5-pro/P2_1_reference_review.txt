**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. The authors correctly identify that while DPPs are powerful models for capturing quality-diversity tradeoffs, learning the full, unconstrained kernel via maximum likelihood is a challenging non-convex problem. The standard approach of projected gradient ascent can be problematic, as the projection step required to maintain positive semi-definiteness can lead to poor, near-diagonal solutions that fail to model diversity.

The paper's main contribution is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) style algorithm for this learning problem. The key insight is to re-frame the DPP generative process as a mixture of elementary DPPs, which introduces a latent variable corresponding to a selection of the kernel's eigenvectors. By treating this eigenvector selection as the hidden variable, the authors derive an elegant EM algorithm that operates on the eigendecomposition of the kernel. This formulation avoids the problematic projection step of the baseline method. The authors demonstrate through experiments on a real-world product recommendation dataset that their proposed EM algorithm is not only more robust and accurate than projected gradient ascent, but also asymptotically and practically faster.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The core idea of using the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs formulation to introduce a latent variable and enable an EM algorithm is highly original and insightful. It provides a principled way to avoid the projection step that plagues naive gradient-based methods.
2.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The derivation of the algorithm is sound. The E-step is shown to correspond to computing the kernel of a conditional k-DPP, and the M-step yields a closed-form update for the eigenvalues and a gradient-based update for the eigenvectors on the Stiefel manifold. This is a complete and non-trivial technical development.
3.  **Demonstrable Practical Advantage:** The paper provides a compelling empirical evaluation. The comparison against projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) on a real-world baby registry dataset is thorough. The authors astutely show that EM's advantage is most pronounced when the baseline's weakness (the projection step) is most likely to be an issue—specifically, with a less-informed initialization or in a data-poor setting.
4.  **Excellent Analysis and Intuition:** The paper clearly explains *why* the baseline method fails (Section 3.1), providing intuition about how projection can lead to degenerate diagonal solutions. The qualitative example of selecting three different baby monitors (Figure 2a) is a very effective illustration of the practical difference between the models learned by the two methods. The runtime analysis and corresponding empirical results (Figure 2b) further strengthen the paper's claims.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Clarity of the M-step for Eigenvectors:** The derivation for the eigenvector gradient (Eq. 20) is presented for the specific case where `q=p`, which is only true at the beginning of an M-step. The paper then states that in practice they "simply take a single gradient step on V". It is not entirely clear if Eq. 20 is used as an approximation for the gradient throughout the optimization, or if another (unspecified) gradient is used. If it is an approximation, this should be stated more explicitly, positioning the algorithm as a Generalized EM algorithm. This is a minor point of clarity rather than a fundamental flaw, but it could be improved.
2.  **Cost of Step-Size Search:** The line search for the eigenvector update requires re-running the E-step for each trial step size to evaluate the true likelihood `L`. While the overall runtime is shown to be superior, this step could be computationally intensive. A brief discussion of the practical cost of this line search (e.g., the typical number of trial steps needed) would make the complexity analysis more complete.

**Detailed Comments on Review Criteria**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical derivations appear sound and the claims are well-supported by a strong set of experiments. The work is complete, presenting a fully-realized algorithm and evaluating it thoroughly. The authors are honest about the limitations of both their method and the baseline, for example by noting that both methods struggle when initialized with a diagonal kernel.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the background section is concise and informative, and the algorithmic development is logical. The use of equations, algorithms, and figures is effective. The only minor point of unclarity is the precise gradient used for the eigenvector update in the M-step, as noted above. Overall, the paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While EM is a standard technique, its application to learning DPPs by introducing a latent variable over the eigenspace is a novel and clever contribution. This approach is significantly different from prior work on DPP learning, which has focused on more restricted parametric forms, ensembles of fixed kernels, or Bayesian methods. The paper correctly situates its contribution with respect to these prior works.

*   **Significance:** The results are significant. Learning a full, unconstrained DPP kernel is a fundamental and difficult problem that has been a major barrier to the broader application of DPPs. This paper provides a practical, robust, and efficient algorithm that advances the state of the art. Researchers and practitioners who use DPPs for tasks like recommendation, summarization, or search will likely find this method very useful. The work opens the door to applying DPPs in settings where learning the diversity/similarity structure from data is paramount, rather than relying on pre-specified kernels.

**Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, technically sound, and practically significant contribution to the literature on learning probabilistic models for subset selection. I strongly recommend acceptance.
