

# Review: Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes

This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the marginal kernel matrix K of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a key limitation in existing gradient-based approaches. The authors demonstrate that naive gradient ascent methods require projection steps that often lead to degenerate solutions (nearly diagonal K matrices), failing to model the diversity-seeking nature of DPPs. Their EM approach leverages the eigendecomposition of K to develop an algorithm that avoids these problematic projections.

**Quality (4/5):** The paper is technically sound with rigorous mathematical derivations and theoretical justification. The EM algorithm is derived carefully, with clear exposition of the E-step and M-step updates. The computational complexity analysis (showing asymptotic runtime advantages over gradient ascent) is thorough. The experimental validation on a baby registry dataset is well-designed, comparing the proposed EM algorithm against K-Ascent under multiple initialization schemes. Results consistently show EM outperforming K-Ascent in log-likelihood (up to 16.5% gain in some categories) and runtime (2.1x faster on average). The paper acknowledges limitations of their method but could have more explicitly discussed potential constraints on dataset size or sensitivity to initialization. The theoretical analysis of the degeneracy issue in gradient ascent (Section 3.1) is particularly strong.

**Clarity (4/5):** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from background to method to experiments. Mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained, and the algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are precise. The authors effectively explain the connection between DPPs and eigendecomposition, which is crucial for understanding their approach. A minor improvement would be providing more intuitive explanation for why projection steps in gradient ascent lead to degenerate solutions (beyond the brief mention in Section 3.1). The supplement contains sufficient details for reproducibility, though some key computational complexity arguments (like the O(nNk²) term) could be more explicitly explained in the main text.

**Originality (4/5):** The core contribution—applying EM to DPP learning via eigendecomposition—is novel and represents a significant departure from previous work. Prior methods either used gradient ascent with problematic projections (Affandi et al., 2013) or made restrictive assumptions about the kernel structure (Kulesza & Taskar, 2011; Kulesza & Taskar, 2012). The paper clearly distinguishes its approach from these prior works, showing that the EM method avoids the degeneracy issues of gradient ascent. While the use of EM for DPPs might seem conceptually straightforward once presented, the specific application to DPP learning with eigendecomposition is novel and well-justified. The authors properly cite relevant prior work, including the recent NIPS papers by Affandi et al. (2013) and Kulesza & Taskar (2012).

**Significance (4/5):** DPPs are widely used in machine learning for subset selection tasks (e.g., recommendation, summarization), making this work practically significant. The proposed EM algorithm offers both theoretical and practical advantages over existing methods, with demonstrable improvements in likelihood and runtime. The baby registry experiments provide a compelling real-world application, though the paper could have better articulated how these improvements translate to actual user experience (e.g., better product recommendations). The method is general enough to be applied to other DPP applications, suggesting broader impact in the field. The paper advances the state of the art in DPP learning by providing a theoretically sound and computationally efficient alternative to existing methods.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a strong contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling and subset selection. The paper addresses a clear limitation in existing DPP learning methods, proposes a theoretically sound and computationally efficient solution, and provides empirical validation. The quality of the work is high, the writing is clear, and the contribution is sufficiently novel to warrant publication.

**Pro arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel application of EM to DPP learning via eigendecomposition
- Theoretical and empirical demonstration of advantages over existing methods
- Clear and well-structured presentation of the method
- Practical significance in subset selection applications
- Demonstrated runtime improvements (2.1x faster on average)

**Con arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited experimental validation (only one dataset)
- Could have provided more intuition for why projection steps cause degeneracy
- Slightly limited discussion of potential limitations in extreme cases

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPP learning. The authors have addressed a meaningful problem with a thoughtful solution that advances the state of the art. I believe this work would be of significant interest to the NIPS community, particularly to researchers working on probabilistic models for subset selection.

**Recommendation: Accept**
