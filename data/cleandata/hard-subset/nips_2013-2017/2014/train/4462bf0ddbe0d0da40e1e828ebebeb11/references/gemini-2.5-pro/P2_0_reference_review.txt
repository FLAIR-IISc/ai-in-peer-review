### **Summary**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. While DPPs are powerful models for capturing diversity, learning their parameters via maximum likelihood is conjectured to be NP-hard, and existing methods either learn restricted forms of the kernel or are computationally burdensome. The authors' main contribution is a novel expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for learning a full, unconstrained kernel matrix. The key idea is to re-parameterize the DPP kernel via its eigendecomposition and treat the selection of eigenvectors as a latent variable. This formulation allows for the derivation of an EM algorithm where the M-step updates for the eigenvalues have a closed form and the updates for the eigenvectors are handled elegantly on the Stiefel manifold, critically avoiding the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent. The authors compare their method to a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) on a real-world product recommendation dataset (Amazon baby registries), demonstrating that their EM approach is not only faster but also more robust, finding better solutions, particularly when diversity is a strong signal in the data.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance of Contribution:** The paper tackles a fundamental and important problem in the DPP literature. Learning a full, non-parametric kernel has been a major obstacle to the wider application of DPPs. The proposed EM framework is a highly original and elegant approach to this problem. It cleverly recasts the problem by introducing a latent variable based on the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs representation, which is a significant conceptual leap over previous attempts. This work substantially advances the state of the art in DPP learning.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical derivation of the EM algorithm is sound and well-explained. The formulation of the E-step and M-step is rigorous. A key theoretical contribution is Lemma 1, which shows that the posterior over the latent eigenvector indices is itself a k-DPP, allowing for efficient computation in the E-step. The derivations for the M-step updates are also solid; the closed-form update for eigenvalues is a major advantage, and the use of established methods for optimization on Stiefel manifolds for the eigenvectors is principled and avoids the pitfalls of naive projection.

3.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
    *   **Realistic Dataset:** The use of the Amazon baby registry dataset is excellent, as it provides a natural and challenging real-world application where the diversity-promoting aspect of DPPs is clearly valuable.
    *   **Insightful Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting higher log-likelihoods. The analysis correlating EM's performance gain with the `d` metric (measuring the importance of off-diagonal entries) provides a clear intuition for *why* and *when* the EM algorithm is superior to the baseline. The demonstration of EM's robustness in a data-poor setting further strengthens its case.
    *   **Convincing Qualitative Results:** The example of K-Ascent recommending three different baby monitors versus EM's more diverse and sensible selection is a powerful and intuitive illustration of the practical impact of the improved learning algorithm.
    *   **Computational Efficiency:** The paper not only claims asymptotic speed-ups but also demonstrates them empirically, showing that EM is about twice as fast as the baseline on their dataset.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The background section is concise but sufficient. The step-by-step development of the EM algorithm in Section 3 is logical and easy to follow. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Approximation in the M-step:** The M-step for the eigenvectors `V` is not fully optimized in each iteration. The paper notes that the gradient formula (Eq. 20) is only exact at the beginning of the M-step, and so a single gradient step is taken. This is a form of Generalized EM (GEM), which is a valid approach but could be made more explicit. It would be helpful to clarify that this approach still guarantees an increase in the likelihood objective `L` due to the line search, even if it doesn't fully maximize the auxiliary function `F` with respect to `V`.

2.  **Scalability Concerns:** The algorithm's runtime complexity contains an `O(N^3)` term due to the eigendecomposition and the matrix exponential for the `V` update. While this is an improvement over the baseline in some respects, it still limits the applicability of the method to problems with a very large ground set size `N` (e.g., `N > 1000`). A brief discussion of this limitation and potential avenues for future work (e.g., using low-rank approximations for `K`, or leveraging methods like the Nyström approximation as in related DPP work) would make the paper more complete.

3.  **Local Optima:** Like all EM algorithms, the proposed method is susceptible to local optima. The experiments convincingly show that it finds *better* local optima than the projected gradient baseline, especially from a naive initialization. However, it would be fair to explicitly mention this limitation in the main text.

### **Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical derivations and strong, insightful experimental results. The work is complete and presents a finished, usable algorithm.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The logical flow is excellent, making the complex material accessible. The authors have done a great job of explaining both the "how" and the "why" of their method.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. The application of an EM algorithm to learn a full DPP kernel, by using the eigendecomposition to define latent variables, is a novel and powerful idea that is a clear departure from previous work.
*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant. This work solves a long-standing and critical problem for a widely used class of models. By providing a robust, efficient, and principled way to learn DPPs from data, this paper is likely to significantly broaden their practical impact in areas like recommendation systems, summarization, and search. Other researchers and practitioners will almost certainly use and build upon these ideas.

**Recommendation: Strong Accept**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, elegant, and practical solution to a significant problem. The technical contribution is strong, the writing is clear, and the experiments are thorough and convincing. It represents a clear advance in the field of probabilistic modeling and is a perfect fit for NIPS.
