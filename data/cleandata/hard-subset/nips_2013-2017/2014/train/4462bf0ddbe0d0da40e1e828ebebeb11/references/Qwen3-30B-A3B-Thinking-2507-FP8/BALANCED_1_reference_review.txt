

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the marginal kernel K of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without assuming fixed values or restrictive parametric forms. The authors address a well-known challenge in DPP learning: maximizing likelihood is difficult due to the positive semi-definite constraint on the kernel matrix, which often requires problematic projection steps in gradient ascent approaches. They leverage the eigendecomposition of K to develop an EM-based method that avoids these projection issues, demonstrating both theoretical and empirical advantages over gradient ascent methods. The work builds upon foundational DPP literature (Kulesza & Taskar, 2012; Affandi et al., 2013) and previous attempts to learn DPPs (e.g., scalar weights for rows [2], L-ensembles [3], parametric forms [8]).

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness and Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for their EM approach. The derivation of the E-step and M-step updates is rigorous, and they clearly explain how the eigendecomposition enables avoiding the projection steps that plague gradient ascent methods. The analysis of why gradient ascent fails (e.g., leading to nearly diagonal matrices) is particularly insightful and well-justified.

2. **Clear Experimental Validation:** The experiments on baby registry data demonstrate clear advantages of the EM approach over gradient ascent (K-Ascent), both in terms of likelihood and runtime. The analysis of why EM performs better in high-diversity settings (d > 0.025) provides valuable theoretical grounding for the empirical results. The runtime comparison (EM being 2.1x faster on average) is a compelling practical advantage.

3. **Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-organized, with clear sections explaining the background, problem, proposed method, and experiments. The pseudocode for both algorithms (K-Ascent and EM) is clear and well-explained. The supplementary material appears to provide additional technical details that would be valuable for replication.

4. **Honest Discussion of Limitations:** The authors acknowledge that both methods require non-trivial initialization (avoiding diagonal matrices) and that the EM method has a computational bottleneck related to the Stiefel manifold optimization. They also note that the moments-matching initializer helps but doesn't fully solve KA's issues.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments are confined to a single dataset (baby registry recommendations), which is a strength for demonstrating the method on a concrete application but limits the generalizability of the claims. The authors should compare with more recent DPP learning methods (e.g., Approximate Inference in Continuous Determinantal Point Processes by Affandi et al., 2013) to better establish the novelty and relative advantages of their approach.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** While the paper shows empirical advantages, it lacks theoretical guarantees about convergence or optimality properties of the EM algorithm. It would strengthen the paper to provide a brief discussion of whether the method converges to a local optimum or global optimum, and under what conditions.

3. **Comparison with Alternative Learning Methods:** The paper focuses on comparing with gradient ascent (K-Ascent) but doesn't address other learning approaches that might be relevant, such as Bayesian methods (e.g., Affandi et al., 2013) or variational inference approaches. A more comprehensive comparison would better establish the paper's contribution.

4. **Practical Implementation Details:** The paper mentions that "in practice, we simplify this algorithm by neglecting second-order information," but doesn't discuss how this affects performance or convergence in practice. More details on implementation choices would be helpful for reproducibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a solid contribution to the DPP learning literature with a technically sound EM approach that addresses a well-known limitation of gradient-based methods. The paper is well-written, the theoretical development is rigorous, and the experiments provide convincing evidence of the method's advantages. However, the limited experimental scope and lack of comparison with more recent learning methods reduce the paper's significance and novelty claims.

The paper is of good quality and makes a meaningful contribution to the field, but the significance could be strengthened with more comprehensive experiments and comparisons. The work is clearly relevant to NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with important applications in subset selection tasks.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper is technically sound and addresses a meaningful problem, but the authors should address the following points in their revision:

1. Expand the experimental section to include comparisons with at least one recent DPP learning method (e.g., Affandi et al., 2013).
2. Add a brief discussion of theoretical convergence properties of the proposed EM algorithm.
3. Provide more details on practical implementation choices, particularly regarding the Stiefel manifold optimization.
4. Clarify how the method differs from and improves upon recent work in DPP learning.

The paper is a strong candidate for NeurIPS, and with these minor revisions, it will make a valuable contribution to the field.
