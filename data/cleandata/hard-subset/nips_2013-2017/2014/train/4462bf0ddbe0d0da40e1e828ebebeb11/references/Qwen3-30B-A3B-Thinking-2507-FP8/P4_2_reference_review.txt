

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper addresses the problem of learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) from example subsets, a task that is known to be computationally challenging. The authors propose an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that leverages the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel matrix K, avoiding the problematic projection step required by gradient ascent methods. This work builds upon prior NIPS and ML literature on DPPs (Kulesza & Taskar, 2011; Affandi et al., 2013; Kulesza & Taskar, 2012), which have primarily focused on restricted learning scenarios: learning scalar weights for rows of L (Kulesza & Taskar, 2011), learning weights for linear combinations of fixed DPPs (Kulesza & Taskar, 2011), or assuming parametric forms for L (Affandi et al., 2013). The authors correctly identify that these approaches either fail to model diversity or lose attractive computational properties of DPPs.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-justified EM algorithm that avoids the degenerate solutions caused by projection steps in gradient ascent methods. The authors provide rigorous theoretical analysis of the EM algorithm, including its asymptotic runtime complexity (O(T1nNk^2 + T1T2N^3)), which is superior to the gradient ascent baseline (O(T1nN^3 + T1T2N^3)). The experimental results on a real-world baby registry dataset (with 13 categories, N=71 products on average, n=8,585 sub-registries) consistently demonstrate the superiority of the EM approach over gradient ascent in terms of test log-likelihood (3.7% median gain) and runtime (2.1x faster on average).

2. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The background section effectively introduces DPPs and their properties, and the algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are precise and well-documented. The connection between the EM algorithm and the eigendecomposition of K is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is thoroughly described. The supplementary materials are referenced appropriately.

3. **Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by introducing an EM approach that works with the eigendecomposition of K, eliminating the need for projection steps. This is a novel approach compared to previous work, and the authors correctly differentiate their method from prior contributions. The key insight—using the eigendecomposition to avoid problematic projections—represents a meaningful advancement in DPP learning.

4. **Significance:** The problem of learning DPPs is important for many applications where subset diversity is crucial (recommendation systems, document summarization, etc.). The proposed method provides a more robust and efficient way to learn DPPs without restrictive assumptions, making it directly applicable to real-world problems. The experimental results demonstrate clear practical improvements over the baseline method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper does not thoroughly explore the sensitivity of the EM algorithm to different initialization strategies beyond the two tested approaches (Wishart and moment matching). While the paper acknowledges that poor initialization leads to poor results, a more systematic analysis of initialization effects would strengthen the claims.

2. The runtime analysis assumes k << N, which holds for the baby registry dataset (k = |Yi| is small), but the paper does not discuss how the algorithm behaves when k is comparable to N (e.g., in applications where large subsets are common).

3. The paper does not compare against other recent DPP learning methods, such as the MAP inference approach in Gillenwater et al. (NIPS 2012) or the Bayesian methods in Affandi et al. (ICML 2013), which might provide additional context for the proposed method's advantages.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The paper presents a technically sound and novel solution to a well-known problem in DPP learning.
- The EM approach avoids degenerate solutions that plague gradient ascent methods.
- The theoretical analysis and experimental results consistently demonstrate superiority over the baseline.
- The work has clear practical significance for real-world applications requiring subset diversity.

*Con:*
- The comparison to other recent learning methods is limited, potentially underestimating the contribution.
- The analysis of initialization sensitivity is not comprehensive.
- The runtime analysis assumes small k, which may not hold in all applications.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the DPP learning literature by introducing an EM algorithm that avoids the problematic projection steps in gradient ascent methods. The approach is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrated to be superior on a real-world dataset. The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and makes a strong case for the advantages of the proposed method. While there are minor limitations in the scope of comparisons and initialization analysis, these do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance. The work advances the state of the art in DPP learning and provides a robust, efficient method for applications requiring subset diversity.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in machine learning with practical applications. The contribution represents a meaningful advance over prior work, and the results are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence. I recommend acceptance.
