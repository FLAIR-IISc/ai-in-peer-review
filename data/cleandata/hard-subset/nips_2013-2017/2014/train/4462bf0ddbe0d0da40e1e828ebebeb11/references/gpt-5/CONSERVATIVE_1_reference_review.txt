Summary
The paper proposes an EM-style algorithm to learn a full (nonparametric) DPP kernel K from observed subsets. The key idea is to exploit the eigendecomposition K = VΛV^T and the known mixture representation of a DPP as a mixture over elementary DPPs (indexed by subsets of eigenvectors J). Treating J as a latent variable yields a lower bound via Jensen’s inequality. The E-step sets q(J|Y) = pK(J|Y), which the authors show is a k-DPP with an explicit kernel, enabling tractable marginal computations. The M-step splits into: (i) a closed-form update for eigenvalues λj using k-DPP marginals; and (ii) a single gradient step for eigenvectors V on the Stiefel manifold using a first-gradient formula (Eq. 20) that is exact only at the start (q = p), with step size chosen by checking improvement in the observed log-likelihood. The method aims to avoid problematic PSD projections in naive projected gradient ascent (“KA”) and to be asymptotically faster when max set size k ≪ N. Experiments on an Amazon baby-registry dataset compare EM to KA under two initializations (Wishart and moment-matching), reporting median test log-likelihood gains (≈3.7% with Wishart; ≈0.4% with moment-matching; larger gains in data-poor settings) and ≈2.1× runtime speedups.

Strengths
- Sound exploitation of the DPP eigenstructure: the derivation of the E-step as a k-DPP and the closed-form eigenvalue updates via k-DPP marginals are elegant and potentially useful.
- Practical attention to optimization constraints: optimization on the Stiefel manifold for V avoids naive projections; the discussion of why KA can get stuck near-diagonal is insightful.
- Complexity analysis suggests plausible gains when k ≪ N; the experiments show some speedup and improved robustness to poor initialization.
- Generally clear exposition, with algorithms summarized and connections to prior DPP work articulated.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Incomplete M-step: Eq. (20) is only valid for the initial gradient (q = p), and the method then resorts to a single gradient step per M-step with step-size chosen by checking observed likelihood. This departs from standard EM guarantees; no monotonicity or convergence analysis is provided for this generalized EM. The repeated E-steps during line search also undermine the clean complexity claim.
- Numerical fragility and ad hoc fixes: the method clips eigenvalues near 1 to avoid blow-ups in QY (via R = diag(sqrt(λ/(1−λ)))), but there is no analysis of bias or stability introduced by this clipping.
- Limited baselines and validation: comparison is only to KA. There are established alternatives for learning DPPs or related models, e.g., convex quality-weight learning, parametric/Bayesian DPP learning, mixtures/ensembles, or more principled constrained optimizers on the spectrahedron (e.g., Frank–Wolfe/Riemannian methods). The experimental evidence is narrow (one application domain), with modest average gains and no statistical tests.
- Scalability and overfitting: learning a full N×N kernel without structural constraints or regularization risks overfitting and does not scale to large N. The paper does not investigate regularization (trace/diagonal penalties, low-rank), nor demonstrate performance or runtime for larger N.
- The claimed asymptotic advantage hinges on k ≪ N; however, the method still incurs O(N^3) operations in the manifold update per line search iteration, and the empirical speedup (~2×) is modest for the moderate N tested.
- Several key derivations are deferred; some notational choices (e.g., U = V^T, VY, UY) reduce readability. The paper would benefit from a more explicit, self-contained derivation of the E-step kernel and the eigenvector gradient, and a clear statement of which parts are approximate.

Quality
Technically plausible but incomplete. The E-step and eigenvalue update are well-justified. The eigenvector update is approximate, with no convergence proof and line search based on the observed likelihood rather than the EM bound. Numerical clipping near λ = 1 is heuristic. Experimental support is limited: one dataset, modest gains overall, narrow baselines.

Clarity
Overall well-written and organized. However, the scope and implications of using only a single gradient step per M-step, the lack of convergence guarantees, and the reliance on repeated E-steps during line search should be made explicit. Some derivations are deferred to the supplement; bringing critical parts into the main text would improve reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. Using the mixture-of-elementary-DPP view to derive an EM procedure for nonparametric K appears novel, as does the specific k-DPP E-step and eigenvalue update. However, optimization over K with spectral constraints is well-trodden, and alternatives to KA exist that are not compared against.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners who want to learn a full DPP kernel without parametric assumptions on small to medium N, particularly when negative interactions are important. However, absent stronger empirical validation across diverse tasks and stronger theoretical guarantees, the impact is uncertain.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a convergence analysis (or at least monotonicity guarantees) for the generalized EM with a single manifold step for V. If not possible, quantify how often likelihood decreases are observed and how line search affects complexity in practice.
- Add stronger baselines: convex quality-weight learning, parametric/Bayesian DPP learning, mixtures/ensembles, and optimization methods tailored to the spectrahedron (e.g., Frank–Wolfe, Riemannian trust-region).
- Include regularization (e.g., trace/diagonal penalties, low-rank constraints) and study generalization, with statistical significance tests.
- Evaluate on additional standard DPP applications (e.g., summarization, sensor placement, image search) and report MAP/diversity metrics alongside likelihood.
- Analyze sensitivity to initialization and to eigenvalues approaching 1; quantify the effect of clipping.
- Clarify derivations (especially Lemma 1 and Eq. 20) in the main text and tidy notation.

Pros
- Clever use of DPP eigenstructure; closed-form eigenvalue updates.
- Avoids naive projections; practical manifold optimization.
- Some empirical robustness and speed improvements.

Cons
- Approximate M-step with no convergence guarantees; dependence on heuristic clipping.
- Limited baselines and domains; modest gains; no statistical tests.
- Scalability and overfitting concerns for full K learning without structure.
- Complexity claims weakened by line search requiring repeated E-steps.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the current paper lacks rigorous convergence analysis, broader baselines, and stronger empirical validation to justify recommending a new learning algorithm over established alternatives.
