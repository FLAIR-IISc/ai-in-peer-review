### Paper Summary

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. Learning the full, unconstrained kernel is conjectured to be NP-hard, and prior work has resorted to learning restricted or parametric forms of the kernel, or using methods that lose the attractive computational properties of DPPs. The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to maximize the data likelihood. The key idea is to work with the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel `K = VΛV^T` and to reframe the DPP generative process as a mixture model over elementary DPPs. This introduces a latent variable `J`, representing a subset of eigenvectors, which is marginalized out.

The authors derive an EM algorithm based on this formulation. The E-step involves computing the posterior distribution over the latent variable `J` given an observed set `Yi`. The authors show that this posterior is itself a `k-DPP`, for which an explicit kernel can be computed. The M-step then optimizes the eigenvalues (`Λ`) and eigenvectors (`V`) to maximize a lower bound on the log-likelihood. The update for the eigenvalues is shown to be a closed-form expression, while the update for the eigenvectors is performed with a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold to preserve orthogonality without a problematic projection. The proposed EM algorithm is compared against a baseline of projected gradient ascent on the kernel entries (K-Ascent), demonstrating that EM is more robust, less prone to finding degenerate (near-diagonal) solutions, and asymptotically faster. These claims are supported by experiments on a real-world product recommendation task using a dataset of Amazon baby registries.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a significant and difficult open problem in the DPP literature: full kernel learning. The proposed EM-style algorithm is highly novel. The insight to use the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs formulation to introduce a latent variable is elegant and effective. This work provides a principled and practical algorithm for a fundamental task, which is likely to be widely used and built upon by researchers working with DPPs.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical derivation of the EM algorithm is solid and well-presented. The characterization of the posterior `p(J | Yi)` as a `k-DPP` (Lemma 1) is a crucial and non-trivial result that makes the E-step tractable. The M-step is also handled carefully, with a closed-form solution for the eigenvalues and a principled manifold optimization approach for the eigenvectors, cleverly avoiding the projection step that cripples the baseline method.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It begins by clearly motivating the problem and situating the work within the existing literature. The background is concise and sufficient. The derivation of the baseline and the proposed EM algorithm is presented in a logical, step-by-step manner. The connection between the theoretical failure mode of the baseline (projection leading to diagonal matrices) and the experimental results is clearly articulated.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. The choice of the Amazon baby registry dataset is well-suited to demonstrate the importance of modeling diversity (negative interactions).
    *   The comparison to projected gradient ascent is a strong and necessary baseline.
    *   The use of two different initializations (uninformed Wishart vs. informed moments-matching) effectively demonstrates the robustness of EM, a key claim of the paper.
    *   The analysis in a data-poor setting further strengthens the case for EM.
    *   The qualitative example of the "safety" category recommendations provides a powerful and intuitive illustration of EM's advantage.
    *   The inclusion of runtime comparisons validates the theoretical complexity analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity on the V-update Gradient:** The M-step for the eigenvectors `V` is a single gradient step, making the algorithm a Generalized EM (GEM) procedure. The paper states that the gradient formula in Equation (20) is simplified "for the particular case of the initial gradient, where we have q = p". This is slightly ambiguous. It is unclear if this formula is only valid for the very first M-step of the entire algorithm, or if it is used in every M-step (i.e., assuming `q` from the E-step is the "new `p`" for the gradient calculation). The latter is standard for GEM, but this could be clarified. If the formula is an approximation in later steps, its justification should be briefly discussed.

2.  **Scalability Discussion:** While the proposed algorithm is shown to be faster than K-Ascent for the tested problem sizes, the overall complexity still contains an `O(N^3)` term (from the matrix exponential in the `V` update) and an `O(nNk^2)` term. The paper would benefit from a short discussion on the limitations of the method for very large `N` (e.g., millions of items) and potential avenues for future work to scale it further, perhaps by leveraging approximation techniques like Nyström, which the authors have cited in other contexts.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** The paper presents a novel, elegant, and technically sound solution to the important and difficult problem of learning a full DPP kernel.
*   **Pro:** The proposed EM algorithm is well-motivated and demonstrates clear advantages over a natural baseline method (projected gradient ascent) in terms of robustness, solution quality, and speed.
*   **Pro:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using a real-world dataset, and provides strong quantitative and qualitative evidence to support the paper's claims.
*   **Pro:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, making a sophisticated method accessible. This work represents a significant advance for the practical application of DPPs.

### Arguments against Acceptance

*   **Con:** There is a minor ambiguity in the derivation of the gradient for the eigenvector update (Eq. 20) that could be clarified.
*   **Con:** A discussion of the algorithm's scalability limitations to very large-scale problems is absent.

These are minor points that can be easily addressed in a final version and do not detract from the overall strength of the contribution.

### Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on determinantal point processes. The proposed EM algorithm is novel, principled, and shown to be effective. I strongly recommend **acceptance**. The authors should be encouraged to address the minor points on clarity and scalability in the camera-ready version.
