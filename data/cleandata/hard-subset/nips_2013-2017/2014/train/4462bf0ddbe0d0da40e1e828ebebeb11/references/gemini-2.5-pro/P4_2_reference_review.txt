### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. The authors note that directly maximizing the likelihood of the DPP kernel `L` (or the equivalent marginal kernel `K`) is conjectured to be NP-hard, and that previous approaches have either been restricted to learning limited parameterizations (e.g., [2, 8]) or have sacrificed the desirable computational properties of DPPs by learning ensembles [3].

As a primary contribution, the paper proposes a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to learn the full, unconstrained kernel matrix. The key idea is to leverage the eigendecomposition of the kernel `K = VΛV^T` and reframe the problem by introducing a latent variable `J`, which corresponds to a subset of eigenvector indices in the DPP generative process. This formulation leads to an iterative algorithm where:
1.  The E-step computes the posterior distribution over the latent variable `J` for each observed subset `Y_i`. The authors show that this posterior is itself a k-DPP and provide a closed-form expression for its kernel.
2.  The M-step updates the parameters (eigenvalues `Λ` and eigenvectors `V`). The update for the eigenvalues is a closed-form expression that avoids any projection step. The update for the eigenvectors is performed via a single gradient step on the Stiefel manifold to maintain the orthogonality constraint `V^T V = I`, again avoiding a potentially problematic projection.

The authors compare their EM algorithm to a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) on a product recommendation task using a real-world baby registry dataset. The experiments demonstrate that the proposed EM method is more robust to initialization, more effective in data-scarce settings, and computationally faster than the baseline.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper presents a complete and technically sound piece of work. The derivation of the EM algorithm, from the introduction of the latent variable to the specific E- and M-step updates, appears correct and is well-motivated. The use of Stiefel manifold optimization for the eigenvector update is appropriate for the given constraints. Claims are well-supported by experimental results on a real-world dataset. The experiments are designed to directly test the central hypotheses of the paper: that avoiding the projection step makes the algorithm more robust and that the EM formulation is more efficient. One minor weakness is that the M-step for the eigenvectors is an approximation—a single gradient step is taken rather than a full optimization. While the line search procedure ensures ascent on the true likelihood, a brief discussion of the potential implications of this approximation would be beneficial.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. It starts with a strong motivation, provides sufficient background on DPPs, and then logically develops the proposed algorithm. The distinction between the proposed EM method and the K-Ascent baseline is made very clear, particularly regarding the problematic nature of the projection step in the latter. The experimental setup and results are presented lucidly. However, several key derivations (proof of Lemma 1, derivation of the eigenvalue update in Eq. 19, and the eigenvector gradient in Eq. 20) are deferred to the supplement. While this is understandable for space reasons, including brief sketches of these derivations in the main text would improve the paper's self-containedness and ease of verification for the reader.

**Originality:** The primary contribution—formulating the DPP kernel learning problem within an EM framework by introducing a latent variable based on the eigendecomposition—is highly original. This approach provides a novel way to tackle the non-convex optimization problem while naturally satisfying the positive semi-definite and eigenvalue constraints without resorting to projection. This stands in contrast to prior work that used restrictive parameterizations or the more straightforward but potentially flawed projected gradient ascent approach explored as a baseline.

**Significance:** This work addresses a fundamental and challenging problem for practitioners wishing to use DPPs. Learning a full, unconstrained kernel is crucial for capturing complex diversity patterns, but has been hampered by optimization difficulties. By providing a more robust and efficient algorithm, this paper has the potential to significantly advance the practical application of DPPs in areas like recommendation systems, document summarization, and search. The empirical results, showing improved performance and speed over a strong and natural baseline, demonstrate a clear advance in the state of the art for learning DPPs.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Formulation:** The EM algorithm based on the eigendecomposition and a latent eigenvector index set is a novel and elegant solution to a difficult learning problem.
2.  **Solves a Real Problem:** The method successfully avoids the projection step required by gradient ascent, which the authors convincingly argue can lead to poor local optima (e.g., near-diagonal kernels that fail to model diversity).
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed method is shown to be more robust, more accurate in data-poor settings, and faster than a well-motivated projected gradient ascent baseline on a real-world task.
4.  **High Potential Impact:** A better algorithm for learning full DPP kernels is a significant contribution that could unlock wider use of DPPs in various machine learning applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The evaluation is primarily against a single, author-devised baseline (K-Ascent). While this comparison effectively highlights the benefits of the EM approach, the paper would be stronger if it included a comparison or at least a thorough discussion with respect to other published learning methods, such as the Bayesian parametric approach of Affandi et al. (ICML 2013).
2.  **Approximation in M-step:** The M-step does not fully optimize the eigenvectors, instead taking a single gradient step. While pragmatic and seemingly effective, this is a deviation from a standard EM implementation whose theoretical properties could be discussed in more detail.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its originality and significance, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. The proposed algorithm is a valuable contribution to the machine learning literature on probabilistic modeling and specifically for users of DPPs.
