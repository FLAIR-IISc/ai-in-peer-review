

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

## Summary
This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the marginal kernel K of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without assuming restrictive parameterizations. The authors claim their method avoids the problematic projection steps required by gradient ascent approaches (K-Ascent), which often produce degenerate solutions that fail to model negative item interactions. The method leverages the eigendecomposition of K, with the EM algorithm alternating between an E-step (computing conditional distributions) and an M-step (updating eigenvalues and eigenvectors). The authors compare their method against K-Ascent on a baby registry dataset with 13 categories, claiming better log-likelihood performance and faster runtime.

## Strengths
The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the DPP learning literature by formalizing an EM approach. The derivation of the EM algorithm using Jensen's inequality and the characterization of the E-step as a k-DPP is technically correct. The authors correctly identify a key limitation of gradient-based methods (the projection step leading to near-diagonal kernels) and provide a plausible solution. The runtime complexity analysis showing asymptotic speedup over gradient ascent is mathematically rigorous.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The paper's main claim - that EM is more robust than gradient ascent - is supported only by experiments on a single, narrow dataset (baby registry categories). This is insufficient to demonstrate general applicability to DPP learning, which has been successfully applied to document summarization, sensor placement, and image search. The authors should compare against established DPP learning baselines (e.g., Bayesian methods in [8] or L-ensembles in [3]) on multiple standard benchmarks.

2. **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The paper reports log-likelihood improvements as medians across 25 trials but provides no statistical tests to confirm these differences are significant. The "average 3.7% improvement" claim is particularly concerning given the small dataset sizes (N=71 on average).

3. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to K-Ascent (KA) is limited and misleading. KA is presented as a "naive optimization procedure" without proper context. The authors should have compared against more sophisticated DPP learning methods that have been previously proposed and validated in the literature. The "moments-matching" initialization is presented as a fix for KA, but this appears to be an ad hoc solution rather than a principled comparison.

4. **Overstated Advantages:** The claim that "EM has a much greater advantage for some categories than for others" is not convincingly supported. The correlation with their "d" metric (d = 1/N ||M||F ||diag(M)||2) is presented as evidence, but this appears to be a post-hoc explanation rather than a principled analysis. The paper fails to demonstrate that the observed differences are due to the algorithm rather than dataset characteristics.

5. **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The paper claims EM "overcomes the need for the problematic projection step," but fails to provide evidence that their method avoids the local minima that plague gradient ascent. The paper does not show convergence properties, sensitivity to initialization, or how the EM algorithm compares to other optimization methods (e.g., BFGS, L-BFGS) on the same problem.

6. **Insufficient Theoretical Justification:** While the paper provides a derivation of the EM algorithm, it does not establish convergence guarantees for the algorithm, which is a critical aspect of any optimization method. The paper also does not address whether the EM approach could get stuck in poor local optima due to the non-convexity of the eigenvector update.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to DPP learning, but the experimental validation is insufficient to support the claims of robustness and advantage over existing methods. The narrow scope of the experiments and lack of comparison to established baselines in the DPP literature significantly undermine the paper's contribution. The authors have not provided enough evidence to convince a skeptical reviewer that this method represents a meaningful advance over existing techniques.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Extending experiments to standard DPP benchmarks (document summarization, image search)
2. Comparing against established DPP learning methods beyond K-Ascent
3. Providing statistical significance tests for all reported improvements
4. Adding convergence analysis and more thorough exploration of the algorithm's behavior

As it stands, the paper lacks sufficient empirical evidence to support its claims of superiority over established methods. The proposed method appears to be a reasonable extension of existing techniques but does not demonstrate sufficient novelty or advantage to warrant acceptance at NIPS.

**Decision: Reject**
