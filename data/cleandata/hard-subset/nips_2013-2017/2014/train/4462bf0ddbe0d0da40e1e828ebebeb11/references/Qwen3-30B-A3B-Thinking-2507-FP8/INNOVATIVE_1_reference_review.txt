

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper introduces a novel expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the kernel matrix of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) without assuming fixed values or restrictive parametric forms. The key insight is to exploit the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K to develop an optimization method that naturally respects the constraints (PSD with eigenvalues ≤ 1), avoiding the problematic projection steps required by gradient ascent approaches. The authors demonstrate that their method is asymptotically faster than gradient ascent and more robust to initialization, particularly in cases where the true kernel exhibits significant off-diagonal structure (representing negative interactions between items).

Previous work on DPP learning has either restricted the kernel to specific forms (e.g., learning scalar weights for rows of L [2]), used ensembles of DPPs with fixed kernels [3], or employed Bayesian methods with parametric assumptions [8]. The authors correctly identify that gradient ascent, while straightforward, requires projection that often leads to degenerate solutions (near-diagonal kernels that fail to model diversity). The EM approach presented here elegantly circumvents this issue by reparameterizing the problem in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors, making it a significant conceptual advance over prior methods.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a theoretically grounded EM algorithm that leverages the eigendecomposition of K. The authors provide a clear derivation of the E-step (identifying a k-DPP as the hidden variable distribution) and M-step (with closed-form updates for eigenvalues and a Stiefel manifold update for eigenvectors). The theoretical justification using Jensen's inequality is sound, and the asymptotic runtime analysis demonstrates a clear advantage over gradient ascent (O(nNk²) vs O(nN³)).

The experiments on a baby registry dataset are well-motivated and relevant. The results consistently show EM outperforming gradient ascent in log-likelihood (with gains of 3.7% on average with Wishart initialization and 4.5% in data-poor settings), particularly in categories where negative item interactions are strong (e.g., safety, carseats). The runtime analysis confirms EM's asymptotic advantage, showing it's 2.1x faster than gradient ascent on the dataset. The concrete example in Figure 2a compellingly illustrates how EM produces more natural product recommendations by capturing item diversity.

The paper's novelty is significant—it presents a fundamentally different approach to DPP learning that addresses a well-known limitation in the field. The authors successfully avoid the common pitfall of gradient-based methods (degenerate solutions) by rethinking the optimization problem through the lens of eigendecomposition. This represents a bold conceptual shift rather than an incremental improvement.

Minor weaknesses include the limited experimental comparison (only against gradient ascent) and the narrow application domain (baby registry recommendations). However, the paper's focus is on a core methodological contribution to DPP learning, and the theoretical foundation is robust enough to generalize to other applications. The caveat about eigenvalues exactly equal to 1 is noted but not deeply explored, though this appears to be a minor implementation detail.

The paper's strengths far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It introduces a genuinely novel approach to a well-studied problem, provides strong theoretical justification, and demonstrates clear practical advantages. The method is elegant, conceptually clean, and avoids the degenerate solutions that plague previous work. In the context of NIPS, where novel algorithmic directions are highly valued, this paper makes a significant contribution that should influence future work on DPP learning.

I recommend acceptance. The paper's novelty and bold approach to a persistent problem in DPP learning outweigh the minor limitations in experimental scope. The method is a clear step forward in the field, with potential applications beyond product recommendation to any subset selection task requiring diversity modeling. The authors have successfully addressed a key limitation in prior work with a theoretically sound and practically effective solution.
