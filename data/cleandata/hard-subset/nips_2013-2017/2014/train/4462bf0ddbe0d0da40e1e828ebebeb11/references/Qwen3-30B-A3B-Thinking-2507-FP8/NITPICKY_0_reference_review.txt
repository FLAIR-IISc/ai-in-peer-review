

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper proposes an EM algorithm for learning the marginal kernel K of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) without restrictive assumptions about K's structure. The authors argue that their approach overcomes limitations of projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent), particularly the degenerate solutions caused by projection steps. While the paper addresses an important problem in DPP learning, I identify several technical inconsistencies, formatting issues, and insufficient justifications that collectively undermine the paper's contribution.

## Technical Issues

The most significant issue is in Section 3.5, where the authors claim the eigenvalue update is "self-normalizing" without explicit enforcement of the 0 ≤ λj ≤ 1 constraint. This is mathematically incorrect. Equation (18) shows λj = (1/n)Σ_i Σ_{J:j∈J} q(J|Yi), but the authors don't prove that this expression automatically satisfies the constraint. In fact, if q(J|Yi) concentrates on sets containing j for many examples, λj could exceed 1. The authors mention "in practice, we simply tighten the constraint on λ to keep it slightly below 1," but this contradicts their claim of self-normalization. This oversight is critical for the algorithm's theoretical soundness.

The complexity analysis in Section 3.1 contains errors. The authors claim the EM algorithm has runtime O(T1nNk² + T1T2N³), but the first term (nNk²) is inconsistent with the complexity of computing the eigenvalue updates (Equation 19). The complexity of Equation (19) is actually O(nNk²) only if k is the maximum set size, but k is defined as max_i |Yi|, not the rank of the DPP. The authors fail to clarify this distinction, making the complexity claim misleading.

Section 3.2 contains a critical typo: "VYi = (U Yi)>" should be "VYi = (U_{Yi})^T" where U_{Yi} is the submatrix of U corresponding to items in Yi. This notation error appears in the main text without correction, potentially confusing readers.

## Notational Inconsistencies

The paper inconsistently switches between L and K for the kernel matrix, causing confusion. In the introduction, L is the kernel matrix, but in the background section, K is introduced as the marginal kernel. The paper then claims "we use the K-based formula for P(Y) and learn the marginal kernel K" but doesn't consistently maintain this notation. For example, in Section 3.1, Equation (4) uses K, but in Equation (5), the derivative is with respect to K, while the constraint "K ≽ 0" is used, which should be K ≽ 0 and K ≼ I for marginal kernel. This inconsistency undermines the technical clarity.

## Missing Mathematical Justifications

The paper claims in Section 3.5 that "the QYi matrix will be infinite if any λj is exactly equal to 1 (due to R in Equation (17))" but provides no mathematical justification for this claim. The matrix R = diag(√(λ/(1-λ))) would indeed have infinite entries if λj = 1, but the authors don't explain why this is a problem for the algorithm or how they handle it in practice. This is a significant omission.

The paper fails to prove that their EM algorithm converges to a better solution than gradient ascent. While they provide empirical evidence, they don't establish a theoretical guarantee that EM avoids the degeneracy problem of gradient ascent. The claim that "the EM algorithm overcomes this failing" is unsupported by rigorous analysis.

## Formatting and Presentation Issues

The paper contains several typographical errors that reduce readability. For instance, "g in an average of 3.7%" (Section 4.1) should be "gains in an average of 3.7%." The phrase "the Wishart's mean is NI" is incorrect; the mean of a Wishart distribution W_N(N, I) is N*I, not NI (which would be a scalar times identity).

The paper references "Lemma 1" without providing the proof in the main text, stating it's in the supplement. While this is acceptable, the authors should have at least included the statement of Lemma 1 in the main text for clarity, as it's central to the E-step.

The experimental section lacks sufficient detail. The paper states "we used 70% of the data for training and 30% for testing" but doesn't specify if this split was random or stratified. The claim "EM gains an average of 4.5%, with a maximum gain of 16.5% for the safety category" lacks standard deviation or confidence intervals, making it difficult to assess the statistical significance of these results.

## Conclusion

While the paper addresses a relevant problem in DPP learning and presents a novel algorithm, the technical inaccuracies, notational inconsistencies, and lack of mathematical justification for key claims are substantial flaws. The complexity analysis is misleading, the self-normalization claim is incorrect, and the paper fails to provide sufficient theoretical justification for its claims. These issues collectively undermine the paper's contribution and make it difficult to assess the algorithm's true merits.

The paper's strengths lie in its practical application to a real-world recommendation task and the empirical comparison with existing methods. However, the technical flaws are too significant to overlook. For a NIPS paper, the theoretical foundation must be sound, and the paper falls short in this regard.

I recommend rejection due to the combination of technical inaccuracies and insufficient theoretical justification, which are critical for a machine learning paper at this level. The authors should address these issues before considering a future submission.
