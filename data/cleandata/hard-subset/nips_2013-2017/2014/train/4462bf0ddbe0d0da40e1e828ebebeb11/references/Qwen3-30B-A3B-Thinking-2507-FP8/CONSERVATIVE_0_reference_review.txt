

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper proposes an expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the marginal kernel K of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP), contrasting with the projected gradient ascent method (K-Ascent) commonly used in prior work. The authors argue that K-Ascent requires problematic projection steps that often result in degenerate solutions (near-diagonal K matrices) that fail to model the diversity-seeking nature of DPPs. Their EM approach leverages the eigendecomposition of K to avoid these projections, claiming improved robustness to initialization and dataset variations, along with asymptotic computational advantages.

The paper's main contributions are the theoretical derivation of an EM algorithm for DPP learning and empirical validation on a baby registry dataset. The authors show that their EM method outperforms K-Ascent on this dataset, with average log-likelihood improvements of 3.7% when using Wishart initialization and 4.5% in data-poor settings. The runtime analysis claims EM is asymptotically faster than K-Ascent (O(T1nNk² + T1T2N³) vs. O(T1nN³ + T1T2N³)).

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical derivation of the EM algorithm is well-structured and clearly presented, with careful attention to the DPP's properties and eigendecomposition.
2. The paper provides a thorough runtime complexity analysis that justifies the claimed computational advantages.
3. The experimental setup on a real-world baby registry dataset is well-motivated and relevant to DPP applications in recommendation systems.
4. The paper adequately acknowledges prior work on DPP learning (K-Ascent, moment matching, Bayesian approaches) and positions its contribution within this context.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Insufficient empirical validation:** The paper's claims about the superiority of the EM approach are based on a single dataset (baby registry) with limited comparison to other established DPP learning methods. The authors only compare against K-Ascent, not against other state-of-the-art approaches like Bayesian methods [8] or structured kernel learning [3]. Without these comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether the observed improvements are truly significant or just specific to this dataset.

2. **Modest performance gains:** The average improvements (3-4%) are relatively modest for a method claiming to "overcome" a fundamental limitation of prior approaches. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that these improvements are meaningful for real-world applications where small likelihood gains might not translate to tangible benefits.

3. **Lack of convergence analysis:** The paper provides no theoretical guarantees about the convergence of the proposed EM algorithm. For an optimization method, this is a significant omission that undermines claims about the method's robustness.

4. **Superficial comparison to prior work:** The paper states that "the EM algorithm overcomes this failing, making it more robust to initialization and dataset changes" but provides minimal evidence for this claim. The comparison to K-Ascent is limited to two initialization methods on one dataset, with no analysis of how the EM approach handles different data distributions or problem complexities.

5. **Unsubstantiated claims about novelty:** While the paper correctly identifies that their approach differs from prior work by not assuming fixed values or restrictive parameterizations for K, it doesn't sufficiently demonstrate that their EM approach is fundamentally different from existing methods. The derivation appears to be a standard application of EM to a specific problem, not a novel optimization technique.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a technically sound derivation of an EM algorithm for DPP learning, but the empirical validation is insufficient to support the claims of significant advantages over established methods. The modest performance gains observed on a single dataset do not convincingly demonstrate that this approach represents a meaningful advance for the field. As a reviewer who prioritizes rigorous validation against established baselines, I cannot endorse this paper for acceptance at NIPS. The paper would require substantially more thorough experimental validation, including comparisons to other state-of-the-art DPP learning methods, broader empirical evaluation across multiple datasets, and theoretical analysis of the EM algorithm's convergence properties.

The paper is well-written and theoretically sound, but it falls short of the rigorous empirical validation expected for a NIPS contribution. The authors have made a reasonable effort to apply a standard technique (EM) to a specific problem (DPP learning), but the lack of comprehensive comparison to established methods and the modest performance gains undermine the significance of the contribution. I recommend rejection.
