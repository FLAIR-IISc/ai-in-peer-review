This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), motivated by the difficulty of directly maximizing the likelihood of a DPP due to the NP-hardness of learning the kernel matrix L. The authors argue that existing approaches, such as gradient ascent and partial learning of L, suffer from limitations – gradient ascent requiring problematic projections and other methods either being restrictive or losing the attractive properties of DPPs. Their proposed solution leverages the eigendecomposition of L to formulate an EM algorithm, avoiding the pitfalls of gradient-based methods.

The core idea – utilizing the eigendecomposition and framing the learning problem as an EM – is conceptually reasonable and builds upon established DPP theory. The authors correctly identify the challenges with direct optimization of L and provide a clear motivation for their approach. The connection to hidden variables (the index set J in the DPP sampling process) is well-articulated, and the derivation of the EM updates, while lengthy, appears mathematically sound, referencing relevant matrix calculus results. The discussion of computational complexity and asymptotic runtime is also a strength, positioning EM as potentially more efficient than gradient ascent.

However, despite the theoretical appeal, I remain skeptical of the practical significance and novelty of this work. The core contribution – an EM algorithm – feels like a natural extension of existing DPP techniques rather than a fundamentally new breakthrough. While the authors highlight the problems with gradient ascent, a careful implementation of gradient ascent with appropriate regularization and step-size control can often yield competitive results, especially with readily available optimization libraries. The claim that the projection step *always* leads to degenerate results (near-diagonal L) seems overstated and requires more convincing empirical evidence.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation, while performed on a reasonable dataset (baby registries), doesn't overwhelmingly support the authors’ claims. The improvement in log-likelihood over K-Ascent is modest, particularly when using the moment-matching initialization. The observation that EM performs better when the data exhibits strong negative interactions (high ‘d’ value) is interesting, but a deeper analysis of *why* EM excels in those scenarios is lacking. The examples provided (Figure 2a) are suggestive but not particularly compelling. The runtime comparison, while showing EM being faster, doesn't quantify the trade-off between speed and accuracy. A more thorough comparison against state-of-the-art DPP learning methods, including those leveraging structured kernels or Bayesian approaches, is needed.

The paper suffers from several clarity issues. The exposition is dense and relies heavily on mathematical notation without sufficient intuitive explanation. The connection between the various equations and the overall algorithm flow is not always immediately apparent. The discussion of the Stiefel manifold update (Equation 21) feels rushed and lacks sufficient detail for someone unfamiliar with the technique.

**Strengths:**

*   Mathematically sound derivation of the EM algorithm.
*   Clear motivation based on the limitations of existing DPP learning methods.
*   Potential for improved efficiency compared to gradient ascent.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in subset selection and recommendation systems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Modest experimental improvement over baseline K-Ascent.
*   Overstated claims about the failures of gradient ascent.
*   Lack of comparison to other state-of-the-art DPP learning methods.
*   Dense and mathematically heavy exposition, hindering clarity.
*   Limited insights into *why* the EM algorithm performs better in certain scenarios.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a theoretically grounded algorithm for DPP learning.
*   The EM approach offers a potential alternative to gradient-based optimization.
*   The experiments demonstrate some improvement over a simple baseline.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   The empirical results are not compelling enough to justify the complexity of the proposed method.
*   The paper lacks a thorough comparison to other relevant DPP learning techniques.
*   The clarity of the exposition could be significantly improved.
*   The novelty of the contribution is incremental rather than groundbreaking.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the technical work is solid, the limited empirical evidence and lack of a strong comparative analysis do not justify acceptance to NIPS. The paper feels like an incremental contribution that doesn’t significantly advance the field. The authors need to provide more convincing evidence of the practical benefits of their EM algorithm, demonstrate its superiority over existing methods, and improve the clarity of the presentation. A more detailed analysis of the experimental results, including ablation studies and error analysis, would also be beneficial.
