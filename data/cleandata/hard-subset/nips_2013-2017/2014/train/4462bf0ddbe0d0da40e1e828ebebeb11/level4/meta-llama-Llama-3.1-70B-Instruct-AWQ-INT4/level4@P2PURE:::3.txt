This paper proposes a novel learning method for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), a distribution over a fixed ground set that favors diverse sets. DPPs are typically parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix (L), but learning L is NP-hard, prompting previous work to focus on partial learning or restricting the parametric form of L. In contrast, this paper presents a method that does not restrict the parameterization of L, thereby preserving the diversity dimension of DPPs.

The authors' approach avoids the need for a projection step, which can compromise the similarity property and diversity of the DPP. Instead, they employ an Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure on eigenvalues and eigenvectors, leveraging Jensen's inequality to lower bound the objective function. This allows them to explore optimization algorithms that do not require projecting values. Notably, they exploit the full-rank property of the matrix V to avoid projecting eigenvectors.

The proposed learning method is valuable, as it preserves the diversity property of DPPs, whereas previous work has primarily focused on the quality property. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is insightful, and the authors' use of the Stiefel manifold to eliminate eigenvector projection is a solid contribution. They also demonstrate that the inverse distribution is a DPP, further supporting their approach.

The authors evaluate their learning algorithm on synthetic datasets and a product recommendation task, comparing its performance to the KA algorithm. The experiments show that the proposed method retains both the quality and diversity of the DPP, with only two of the top 10 products being replaced by less likely products. While the conclusion could be more comprehensive in highlighting the paper's contributions, the overall writing is cohesive, and the proofs are well-written. The reduction of optimization to EM is particularly well-explained.

To further illustrate the importance of the "diversity" produced by this algorithm, additional experiments on real-world datasets would be beneficial. Nevertheless, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field, and its findings have the potential to impact future research on DPPs.
