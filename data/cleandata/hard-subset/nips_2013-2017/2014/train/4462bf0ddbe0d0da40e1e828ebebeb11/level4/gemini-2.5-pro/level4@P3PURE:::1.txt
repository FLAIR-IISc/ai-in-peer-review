This paper presents a novel method for learning the kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP). The primary contribution is an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework that reformulates the learning objective. This approach is contrasted with the standard baseline of performing projected gradient ascent on the log-likelihood of the kernel parameters, which the authors term K-Ascent (KA).

The core idea is to represent any DPP as a mixture of "elementary DPPs," where each elementary DPP corresponds to a single subset of items. A variational distribution over these subsets is then introduced. The algorithm proceeds by alternating between an E-step, which updates this variational distribution (itself a k-DPP), and an M-step, which involves taking single gradient steps on the kernel parameters. The results indicate that the proposed EM method significantly outperforms a naively initialized KA baseline. When KA is initialized more carefully, the EM method still demonstrates a modest improvement in some settings.

**Strengths**

The proposed EM-based framework is a creative and interesting approach to the kernel learning problem for DPPs. The reformulation provides a new perspective on the optimization landscape. The empirical results demonstrate a clear improvement over the baseline K-Ascent method, particularly when the baseline is poorly initialized.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The M-step of the algorithm is quite involved and requires several approximations and caveats to be computationally tractable. While the authors succeed in making the optimization work, these complexities detract somewhat from the elegance of the conceptual EM framework.

2.  The experimental analysis could be strengthened with a more thorough investigation of initialization. A crucial experiment would be to initialize the EM algorithm with the solution found by the K-Ascent baseline. This would clarify whether the EM framework is simply finding a better local optimum from a standard starting point, or if it can serve as a refinement technique to further improve upon existing solutions.

3.  The choice of "relative log-likelihood" as the primary evaluation metric is unclear. The difference in log-likelihoods has a natural interpretation as the log-ratio of probabilities. However, reporting the ratio of log-likelihoods is non-standard and makes the quantitative improvements difficult to interpret. I would strongly recommend the authors instead report the direct difference in log-likelihoods or provide a clear justification for their choice of metric.

**Minor Comments**

I would advise the authors to revise the writing style in a few places. Describing one's own method as "elegant" can come across as subjective, especially when the M-step involves notable approximations. Additionally, the use of bold text in the introduction for emphasis is generally unnecessary and can be distracting.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper introduces a novel and interesting EM-based approach for learning DPP kernels. While the performance gains over a well-initialized gradient-based method are not always substantial, and the experimental analysis could be expanded, the proposed method is a valuable contribution to the literature. Overall, this is a solid paper with clear merit.
