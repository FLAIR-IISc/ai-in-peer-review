This paper addresses learning full kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The key contribution is an EM-style optimization that reparameterizes the DPP in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors and introduces a latent variable over subsets of eigenvectors. Using the known representation of a DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs, the authors define an auxiliary distribution q(J|Y) over eigen-index sets J. The E-step sets q to the true posterior pK(J|Y), which is itself a k-DPP that can be handled efficiently. The M-step updates eigenvalues in closed form via k-DPP marginals and updates eigenvectors with a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold. This approach avoids the problematic PSD projection in the standard projected gradient method on matrix entries (KA), which can drive the solution toward near-diagonal kernels. On a product recommendation dataset (Amazon baby registries), the EM method improves test log-likelihood over KA, substantially so under naive initialization, modestly under a moment-matching initialization, and it is typically faster in wall-clock time.

Relation to prior work: Prior learning strategies for DPPs largely focused on convex but restricted parameterizations (e.g., per-row weights or mixtures over fixed kernels), or Bayesian learning under parametric assumptions. This paper targets learning an unconstrained kernel by optimizing the full eigendecomposition, while retaining DPP structure and associated computational benefits.

Quality:
- The derivation of the EM lower bound and the E-step (q(J|Y) as a k-DPP) is sound and leverages known DPP identities. The eigenvalue update is principled and efficiently computable via k-DPP marginals.
- The eigenvector update is implemented as a single gradient step per M-step with a line search on the Stiefel manifold, making the method a generalized EM. This is reasonable in practice, but the paper would benefit from a brief discussion of convergence guarantees (e.g., monotonic improvement of the data likelihood under the proposed line search) and practical safeguards near λ ≈ 1.
- Runtime analysis is clear; the algorithm reduces expensive matrix inversions and benefits when observed sets are small (k ≪ N). Empirical runtimes corroborate theory on the presented dataset.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized and readable. The description of the E-step and the construction of QYi are helpful; still, a concise intuition for why the KA projection induces near-diagonality and how the eigenspace updates avoid this would aid readers.
- The use of “relative log-likelihood” is unclear. Because differences of log-likelihoods already correspond to log probability ratios, reporting “relative” log-likelihood can be hard to interpret. Please report absolute test log-likelihood differences and, if desired, normalized per-element or per-set metrics with error bars.
- Stylistic suggestions: avoid self-assessments such as calling the method “elegant,” and consider removing bold face in the introduction to keep the tone neutral.

Originality:
- Casting full-kernel DPP learning as an EM problem over the eigenspace is a novel and natural idea. While the components (mixture view of DPPs, k-DPP marginals, Stiefel manifold updates) are known, their combination for learning an unconstrained K is new and addresses a long-standing challenge.

Significance:
- Empirically, the method improves over projected gradient, especially with naive initialization and in data-poor regimes; with stronger initialization, gains are smaller. The runtime benefits are notable.
- The evaluation is limited to a single (though substantial) application. Demonstrations on additional domains (e.g., summarization, sensor placement) or synthetic data with known ground truth would strengthen claims of robustness and generality.

Suggestions and questions:
- Initialization: Please analyze how EM behaves with different initializations. In particular, does EM further improve when initialized from KA’s solution? How sensitive is EM to the Wishart scaling and to the moment-matching procedure?
- Convergence: Provide a brief statement (and, if possible, a plot) showing monotonic increases of the data likelihood across EM iterations under your line search, and clarify stopping criteria.
- Metrics: Report absolute test log-likelihoods (and optionally per-set averages), with standard deviations across runs. Consider including MAP subset quality or diversity-aware downstream metrics to complement likelihood.
- Scaling: Discuss memory/runtime for larger N and whether approximations (e.g., Nyström) could be incorporated.
- Practical details: Clarify how λ is kept below 1 in practice, and whether this ever limits performance.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Addresses an important and difficult problem: learning full DPP kernels without restrictive parameterizations.
- Technically sound EM formulation leveraging DPP structure; avoids fragile PSD projections.
- Empirical improvements in both likelihood and runtime; robustness in harder settings.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- M-step uses single-step updates without strong convergence guarantees; reliance on line search and repeated E-steps may be costly in some regimes.
- Gains over a well-initialized KA are modest; broader empirical validation would increase confidence.
- Some presentation choices (metric definition, stylistic claims) hinder interpretability.

Overall assessment: A solid and interesting contribution that advances practical learning for DPPs by exploiting their eigendecomposition within an EM framework. While the improvements over a strong baseline can be modest and the evaluation could be broader, the approach is technically well-motivated, shows clear benefits in challenging regimes, and is likely to be useful to practitioners.
