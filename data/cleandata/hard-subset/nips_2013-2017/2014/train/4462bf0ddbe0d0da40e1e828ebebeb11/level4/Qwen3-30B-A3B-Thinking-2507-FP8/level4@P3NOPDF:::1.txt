

This paper proposes an EM-based approach for learning kernel matrices in determinantal point processes (DPPs), offering a novel alternative to the standard K-Ascent (KA) method that optimizes the log-probability via projected gradient ascent. The key insight is recognizing that any DPP can be decomposed as a mixture over elementary DPPs (one per subset), enabling an EM framework where the E-step computes a variational distribution over subsets (represented as a k-DPP) and the M-step performs a single gradient update on the kernel parameters. The authors demonstrate that this EM approach significantly outperforms naive KA initialization (by 5–10% in log-likelihood) and marginally improves upon KA initialized with a better starting point (0.5–2% gain).  

The core contribution—reformulating DPP kernel learning within an EM framework—is conceptually interesting and well-motivated. The technical execution, however, relies on non-trivial approximations (e.g., the k-DPP representation for the E-step) and specific caveats to ensure convergence, which somewhat undermine the elegance of the approach. While the authors successfully implement this framework, the M-step’s complexity and dependency on these approximations warrant stronger justification. The paper does not sufficiently clarify why these approximations are necessary or how they impact the solution’s quality.  

Experimentally, the results are promising but could be strengthened. The authors report improvements over KA but omit a critical analysis: how does EM perform when initialized with KA’s output? If EM consistently refines KA’s solution (even slightly), this would better establish its practical utility over a simple post-hoc improvement. The current experiments only compare EM to KA *with different initializations*, leaving ambiguity about whether EM is a distinct optimization strategy or merely a better initialization method for KA.  

A more significant issue concerns the evaluation metric. The paper reports *relative log likelihood* (i.e., log-probability differences) as the primary performance measure. This is problematic because log-likelihood differences do not directly reflect meaningful probability ratios; they represent *additive* differences in log-probabilities, not multiplicative ratios. For example, a 0.1 difference in log likelihood corresponds to a ratio of exp(0.1) ≈ 1.105, but reporting "0.1 relative log likelihood" is misleading—it conflates absolute differences with relative scaling. The authors should instead report *absolute log-likelihood* or *log-likelihood differences* (with clear interpretation), as the current metric obfuscates the practical significance of the gains.  

The writing style contains minor but distracting issues. The authors’ claim that their method is "elegant" is premature given the caveats in the M-step; elegance implies simplicity and robustness, neither of which is fully demonstrated. Additionally, bolding text in the introduction (e.g., "Our approach is elegant") is unprofessional and undermines the paper’s credibility. Such stylistic choices should be removed.  

Overall, the paper presents a promising new direction for DPP kernel learning. The EM reformulation is a valuable contribution that addresses a practical limitation of gradient-based methods. While the improvements over KA are not revolutionary (particularly when KA is well-initialized), the approach is robust and the experimental results are convincing for the cases studied. The method’s ability to handle naive initializations effectively is a tangible advantage.  

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:  
1. Clarify the M-step’s approximations and their impact on solution quality.  
2. Report absolute log-likelihoods (not relative differences) and explicitly interpret the magnitude of gains.  
3. Add experiments initializing EM with KA’s output to demonstrate incremental improvement.  
4. Remove subjective claims like "elegant" and stylistic flourishes (e.g., bold text).  

These revisions would significantly enhance the paper’s clarity, rigor, and impact. The core idea is sound and merits publication, but the current presentation requires refinement to fully convey its value. The work is a solid contribution to DPP learning, and with the suggested improvements, it would make a strong addition to the literature.
