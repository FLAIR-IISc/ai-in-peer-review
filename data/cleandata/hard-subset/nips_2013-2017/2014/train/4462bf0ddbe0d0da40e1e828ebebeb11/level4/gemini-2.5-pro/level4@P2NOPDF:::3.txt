This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix (L) for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP). DPPs are probabilistic models over sets that favor diversity, and their behavior is governed by the positive semidefinite matrix L. Because learning an unconstrained L is NP-hard, prior work has typically resorted to restrictive parameterizations or partial learning schemes. These simplifications, however, often compromise the model's ability to capture complex diversity patterns, focusing more on item quality.

The central contribution of this work is a novel algorithm for learning the full, unconstrained L matrix. Instead of using projected gradient ascent, which can distort the learned structure and compromise the desired diversity properties, the authors propose an elegant Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The method operates on the eigendecomposition of L (L = VΛVᵀ), optimizing over the eigenvalues (Λ) and eigenvectors (V) separately. A key insight is to re-frame the constrained optimization over the orthonormal eigenvectors as an unconstrained problem on the Stiefel manifold, thereby cleverly avoiding a difficult projection step for V. The EM procedure is derived by applying Jensen's inequality to a lower bound of the log-likelihood objective.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The proposed method is a significant step forward in learning DPPs. By enabling the learning of a full, unconstrained kernel matrix, it allows the model to capture both quality (via eigenvalues) and diversity (via eigenvectors) without the limitations imposed by prior approaches. This directly addresses a major bottleneck in the practical application of DPPs.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper's technical contributions are solid. The derivation of the EM algorithm is clear, insightful, and well-justified. The formulation of the eigenvector update as an optimization on the Stiefel manifold is particularly elegant and effectively sidesteps the problematic projection step required by standard gradient methods. Furthermore, the proof that the inverse distribution is also a DPP is a valuable theoretical contribution.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is written cohesively, and the proofs are clear and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem and presenting their step-by-step derivation of the EM algorithm, making a complex topic accessible.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real-world product recommendation data effectively support the paper's main claim. The comparison against the KA baseline demonstrates that the proposed method successfully retains both quality and diversity. While the performance improvement on the recommendation task was modest (replacing two items in the top-10), the nature of the change—substituting less likely items with more diverse ones—perfectly illustrates the practical value of the proposed learning algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Real-World Experiments:** The primary weakness is the limited evaluation on real-world datasets. While the product recommendation task is illustrative, the paper would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating the algorithm's effectiveness on additional, larger-scale applications where diversity is critical (e.g., document summarization, search result diversification). This would provide more compelling evidence of the practical importance of learning the full DPP kernel.
*   **Minor Weakness in Conclusion:** The conclusion feels somewhat brief and does not fully recapitulate the paper's important technical contributions. A more comprehensive summary would better highlight the work's impact.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant technical contributions to the field of probabilistic modeling. The proposed EM algorithm provides a principled and effective way to learn an unconstrained DPP kernel, overcoming key limitations of previous methods. The work is technically sound, well-presented, and opens up new possibilities for applying DPPs in practice.
