Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes an expectation–maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full kernel of a determinantal point process (DPP) from subsets. The key idea is to reparameterize the DPP in terms of the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the marginal kernel K, introduce a latent variable J indexing an “elementary” DPP (subset of eigenvectors), and derive a Jensen lower bound on the log-likelihood. The E-step computes a k-DPP over J conditioned on each observed set, while the M-step updates eigenvalues in closed form via k-DPP marginals and takes a Stiefel-manifold gradient step for the eigenvectors. The method avoids the problematic PSD projection required by naive projected gradient ascent on K. Experiments on a real-world baby registry recommendation task compare the proposed EM method to a projected gradient baseline (KA), demonstrating consistent improvements in test log-likelihood and lower runtime.

The work builds on a well-developed DPP literature, including learning restricted parameterizations (e.g., per-item quality scalings [2]) and mixtures of fixed kernels [3], as well as Bayesian parametrizations [8]. It relates to past NIPS work on DPPs for MAP inference and applications [15, 16], k-DPPs [3], and efficient sampling/marginalization [21]. The contribution here is learning an unconstrained kernel via an EM view rooted in the eigendecomposition, which to my knowledge has not previously appeared in NIPS.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The derivation is principled. The use of the mixture representation over elementary DPPs to introduce a latent J is standard and appropriate; the E-step’s identification of q(J|Y) as a k-DPP is elegant and computationally useful. The eigenvalue update is simple and self-normalizing; the eigenvector update via a Stiefel-manifold step is reasonable.
- Claims and support: The paper provides complexity comparisons (EM O(nNk^2) per iteration for k = max set size, plus O(N^3) for manifold updates during line search) versus KA’s O(nN^3) inverses and projections. Empirical results show up to 16.5% gains in test log-likelihood over KA and roughly 2× speedups on a moderately sized dataset. However, experimental validation is limited to one application and one family of baselines.
- Caveats: The M-step optimizes V only via a single gradient step with line search, not a full maximization of the lower bound; while the authors check improvement in the likelihood L and re-run E-steps during line search, it would help to clarify whether the procedure guarantees monotonic lower-bound improvement and whether convergence properties of EM still hold under this inexact M-step.

Clarity:
The paper contains many good ideas but is harder to follow than necessary because of organization and missing definitions. Examples:
- p.2, line 81: The marginal kernel K is used before it is defined formally; define K = L(L + I)^{-1} when first mentioned, and state its eigenvalue constraints (PSD, eigenvalues ≤ 1).
- p.2, line 100: V is used without definition; define K = VΛV^T explicitly when first invoking the eigendecomposition.
- p.3, line 159: “minimizes the change to Frobenius norm” should read “chooses the closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix to Q.”
- Section 3.5: The discussion of the Stiefel manifold update would benefit from a concise, self-contained description of the simplified update you actually use (the matrix exponential update), its computational cost, and a brief intuition, rather than primarily referring the reader to [26].
- p.4, line 189: Clarify what “weight” refers to (mixture weight over J? eigenvalue weight?).
- p.7, line 324 and Eq. (30): Please justify the initialization K̃ij = sqrt(max(K̃ii K̃jj − mij, 0)). Why not use empirical pairwise marginals directly for off-diagonals, or the empirical covariance as a starting kernel? A brief derivation or rationale (e.g., ensuring PSD and consistency with DPP marginals) would help.

I also recommend restructuring Section 3 to first present the final EM algorithm cleanly (definitions, E/M steps, updates, complexities), then discuss alternatives (e.g., KA’s projection pitfalls) and implementation choices. This would improve readability.

Originality:
The EM formulation for learning an unconstrained DPP kernel via eigenvalue/eigenvector updates and a k-DPP E-step appears novel. Prior learning methods for DPPs typically restrict the parameterization (per-item weights [2], mixtures [3]) or adopt Bayesian models [8]. The blend of the mixture view, k-DPP conditioning, and Stiefel updates is a fresh and technically interesting contribution.

Significance:
Learning full DPP kernels is important for modeling diversity and negative interactions in subset selection. The proposed algorithm addresses a long-standing practical difficulty (non-convexity and PSD constraints) and shows compelling improvements over a strong naive baseline. That said, the experimental scope is narrow: one application domain, modest N, and no comparisons to non-DPP subset selection methods (e.g., submodular maximization models or alternative diversity-encouraging probabilistic models). Demonstrating downstream gains (e.g., MAP set quality, precision/recall in recommendation) and broader benchmarks would strengthen the case.

Pros:
- Principled EM formulation leveraging the eigendecomposition and k-DPP structure.
- Avoids PSD eigenvalue clipping/projection that can lead to degeneracy in KA.
- Clear eigenvalue update; practical Stiefel-manifold update for eigenvectors.
- Theoretical and empirical runtime advantages over projected gradient.
- Demonstrated improvements in test log-likelihood and qualitative examples.

Cons:
- Limited experimental evaluation: single dataset, no comparisons to other subset selection approaches beyond KA.
- Clarity/organization issues and several undefined or delayed definitions.
- Inexact M-step raises questions about monotonicity and convergence guarantees.
- Scalability to larger N not demonstrated; costs dominated by repeated eigendecompositions during line search.
- Initialization strategy and its rationale (especially Eq. (30)) need clearer justification.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add comparisons to alternative subset selection baselines included in the introduction (e.g., mixture-of-DPPs [3], Bayesian parametric DPPs [8], and non-DPP submodular models), reporting both likelihood and task metrics (e.g., MAP set quality, recommendation diversity/coverage).
- Clarify the convergence properties: does each EM iteration increase the lower bound F(q,V,Λ)? If not, state practical criteria used and observed behavior.
- Provide ablations: effect of the number of eigenvector gradient steps per M-step; sensitivity to initialization; behavior when k is large.
- Report wall-clock breakdowns (E-step vs V-update vs eigendecomposition), and experiments at larger N to validate scalability claims.
- Improve notation and structure per the clarity comments above, and include a brief, self-contained derivation of the matrix-exponential Stiefel update you employ.

Recommendation:
Weak accept. The paper presents a technically novel and useful method for learning full DPP kernels with clear advantages over projected gradient ascent. With improved exposition and a broader empirical evaluation, it would make a strong contribution to the NIPS literature on DPPs and subset selection.
