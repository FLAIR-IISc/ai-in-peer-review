Determinantal Point Processes offer a principled approach to modeling set diversity, parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix (L). Learning L directly is NP-hard, motivating prior work to focus on restricted learning settings – scalar weighting of rows [2] or learning combinations of fixed DPPs [3]. This paper presents a novel algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix L without restrictive parameterizations. The key idea is to reparameterize L in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors, then leverage an expectation-maximization (EM) approach to bypass the need for problematic projection steps inherent in naive gradient ascent, which can lead to degenerate, near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture item diversity. The authors demonstrate that their EM algorithm is asymptotically faster than gradient ascent and achieves improved performance on a real-world product recommendation task.

The paper addresses an important problem in a well-motivated manner. The proposed method offers a significant advantage over existing approaches by avoiding restrictive assumptions on L and maintaining the crucial diversity property of DPPs, a point effectively highlighted through comparison to previous work. The detailed derivation of the EM procedure is a strength, providing valuable insight into the optimization process. Transforming the optimization problem to leverage the Stiefel manifold to avoid eigenvector projection, and demonstrating that the resulting distribution remains a DPP, represent solid theoretical contributions. The experimental evaluation, using both synthetic and real-world data, supports the paper's claims: the proposed method demonstrably retains both the quality and diversity aspects of DPPs when compared to projected gradient ascent (KA). The inclusion of a concrete example illustrating the differences in recommendations generated by the two methods further clarifies the benefit of the proposed approach.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The proofs are presented rigorously, and the step-by-step reduction to the EM formulation is particularly insightful. One potential area for improvement would be the inclusion of more extensive experiments on diverse real-world datasets to further illustrate the benefits of the learned diversity, and to benchmark against a wider range of baseline methods. While the discussion of asymptotic runtime complexity is valuable, providing empirical runtime comparisons would also strengthen the paper. The conclusion could be slightly expanded to more fully emphasize the contributions of the work, particularly the theoretical advancements and the demonstrated practical benefits.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in DPP learning.
*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound EM algorithm.
*   Avoids restrictive parameterizations and maintains DPP diversity.
*   Demonstrates improved performance on a real-world task.
*   Clear and well-written presentation with rigorous proofs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation could be broadened with more diverse datasets and baseline comparisons.
*   Empirical runtime comparisons would complement the theoretical analysis of complexity.
*   The conclusion could more thoroughly highlight the key contributions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of determinantal point processes. The proposed EM algorithm offers a significant improvement over existing methods for learning DPPs, and the paper is well-written and thoroughly researched. Despite minor suggestions for improvement, I believe this paper is of high quality and is suitable for acceptance to NIPS.
