This paper tackles learning determinantal point processes (DPPs) by directly estimating an unrestricted kernel, addressing a key limitation in prior work that focused on heavily constrained parameterizations. A DPP over a fixed ground set promotes diverse subsets, typically parameterized by a PSD kernel L (or equivalently the marginal kernel K = L(L + I)−1). Maximizing the likelihood of observed subsets with respect to the full kernel is nonconvex and conjectured NP-hard, so earlier approaches learned only per-item weights (convex but ignores pairwise similarity), learned mixtures of fixed kernels (losing DPP structure), or imposed parametric forms and used Bayesian inference. The authors’ main contribution is an EM-style algorithm that reparameterizes in the eigenbasis (eigenvalues and eigenvectors of K) and introduces a natural latent variable: the subset of eigenvectors selected in the elementary-DPP mixture representation. A Jensen-based lower bound yields an EM objective. The E-step sets q(J | Y) to the conditional DPP (a k-DPP) whose kernel is given in closed form. The M-step updates eigenvalues in closed form using k-DPP marginals and updates eigenvectors via a gradient step constrained to the Stiefel manifold using a multiplicative update. Crucially, this avoids the PSD projection step required by naive projected gradient on K, which can truncate eigenvalues to zero and drive the kernel toward diagonal structure, thereby destroying the negative correlations that drive diversity.

Runtime analysis shows that the EM updates for eigenvalues and the initial eigenvector gradient can be computed in O(n N k^2) per EM iteration (where k = max |Yi|), plus O(N^3) costs associated with manifold updates and step-size search, which can be substantially faster than the O(n N^3) scaling of projected gradient ascent (KA). Empirically, on a real-world baby registry recommendation dataset with multiple product categories, the EM method outperforms KA in test log-likelihood (with median gains and up to 16.5% in a low-data regime), is more robust to initialization, and is about 2× faster in wall-clock time. The authors also analyze when KA fails—projection can induce near-diagonal kernels—and show that EM better preserves off-diagonal structure that encodes negative interactions. Qualitative examples illustrate that EM avoids redundant recommendations (e.g., multiple video monitors).

Quality
- The technical development is solid. The latent-variable construction via the elementary-DPP mixture and the use of k-DPP marginalization to obtain closed-form eigenvalue updates are correct and well motivated.
- The eigenvector update on the Stiefel manifold is appropriate and avoids problematic projection; the multiplicative update is clearly specified.
- The complexity analysis is careful. Practical issues (e.g., λj < 1 to avoid numerical blow-ups) are acknowledged.
- Limitations: the objective remains nonconvex; the M-step for eigenvectors uses only a single gradient step with step-size chosen by repeated E-steps (costly); and exact evaluation of the lower bound is intractable, so progress is monitored via likelihood. Empirical evaluation focuses primarily on KA as the baseline.

Clarity
- The paper is well organized, with clear derivations and algorithm summaries. The EM construction, the role of the hidden variable, and the connection to k-DPPs are explained cleanly.
- The discussion of why projection harms KA (eigenvalue truncation leading to near-diagonal K and thus diagonal gradients) is insightful.
- Minor issues: the conclusion could better synthesize the contributions and limitations; a brief practical guide on step-size tuning and handling λ near 1 would help.

Originality
- Learning a full DPP kernel via EM in the eigenbasis with a k-DPP-based E-step, and optimizing eigenvectors on the Stiefel manifold, appears novel relative to prior restricted learning setups (per-item weights, mixtures of fixed kernels, or parametric Bayesian models).
- The insight that avoiding PSD projection mitigates degeneracy in learning is a useful contribution.

Significance
- Removing restrictive parameterizations and providing a practical learning method for full DPP kernels is important, given the widespread use of DPPs for diversity.
- The reported gains in likelihood and robustness, along with better runtime scaling in realistic regimes (k ≪ N), suggest practical impact.
- Significance would be strengthened by broader empirical comparisons (e.g., to per-item weight learning, mixtures of DPPs, and Bayesian parametric approaches), additional datasets, and diversity/quality metrics beyond likelihood.

Pros
- Conceptually elegant and well-justified EM formulation using the elementary-DPP mixture.
- Avoids PSD projection pitfalls; more robust to initialization.
- Efficient eigenvalue updates via k-DPP marginals; sound manifold update for eigenvectors.
- Empirical improvements in likelihood and runtime on a real recommendation dataset; clear qualitative evidence of improved diversity.

Cons
- Nonconvex optimization with only partial M-step for eigenvectors; convergence guarantees are limited.
- Empirical evaluation is narrow: primarily compared against KA; limited datasets and metrics.
- Practical details (e.g., handling λ → 1, sensitivity to initialization, step-size protocol) could be expanded.
- Scalability to very large N is not fully demonstrated; O(N^3) manifold updates may be a bottleneck.

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare against additional baselines: per-item weight learning [convex], mixtures of fixed kernels, and parametric Bayesian learning, to contextualize gains.
- Report sensitivity to initialization (Wishart vs. moment-matching) and to training set size across categories.
- Include diversity-oriented evaluation (e.g., pairwise similarity of recommendations, coverage) in addition to likelihood.
- Provide more details on step-size selection, number of E-steps per line search, and wall-clock/iteration breakdown.
- Discuss scalability and potential approximations (e.g., low-rank structure, Nyström) for larger N.
- Strengthen the conclusion by summarizing contributions and limitations, and outlining future work.

Overall, this is a clear, technically sound, and original contribution that addresses an important gap in learning DPPs. With broader empirical validation and comparisons, it would make a strong NIPS paper.
