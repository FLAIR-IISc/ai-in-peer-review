This paper addresses the problem of learning kernel matrices for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). The core contribution is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that offers an alternative to standard approaches like projected gradient ascent on the kernel entries (termed K-Ascent or KA by the authors). The authors cleverly reformulate the problem by leveraging the eigendecomposition of the kernel and exploiting the representation of a DPP as a mixture of “elementary DPPs,” introducing a hidden variable representing subsets of items. This allows them to develop an iterative EM procedure with an E-step involving a variational distribution (a k-DPP) and an M-step updating the kernel parameters. Experimental results demonstrate that the EM method consistently outperforms KA, especially when KA is initialized less effectively, though the gains aren't always substantial.

The approach is interesting and represents a worthwhile exploration of alternative optimization strategies for DPP learning. While the M-step is complex and relies on approximations to maintain tractability, the fact that the algorithm converges and improves upon existing methods is a significant achievement. The paper clearly connects to prior work in DPP learning, specifically addressing the limitations of previously proposed methods like learning single row weights [2] or ensembles of fixed kernels [3]. The authors also appropriately cite relevant work on related optimization techniques like projected gradient ascent [23] and the Stiefel manifold [26].

Several aspects could be strengthened. A more comprehensive analysis of initialization strategies would be valuable. Investigating whether initializing the EM algorithm with the output of KA leads to further improvements would reveal whether the EM algorithm effectively refines solutions found by gradient ascent. The choice of reporting results as *relative* log-likelihood gains is somewhat unclear; presenting absolute differences in log probabilities would likely be more intuitive and readily interpretable. 

Additionally, while not critical, the writing style could be refined. Removing subjective statements about the algorithm’s “elegance” would enhance the objectivity of the presentation. Similarly, the use of bold text in the introduction seems unnecessary.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Approach:** The EM formulation provides a new perspective on learning DPP kernels.
*   **Improved Performance:** The EM algorithm consistently outperforms K-Ascent in the experiments.
*   **Theoretical Grounding:** The method is well-motivated by the properties of DPPs and leverages eigendecomposition effectively.
*   **Clear Connection to Prior Work:** The paper appropriately positions itself within the existing literature on DPP learning.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** The M-step involves approximations and caveats, reducing the theoretical cleanliness of the solution.
*   **Limited Gains:** The performance improvements over K-Ascent, while consistent, are not always large.
*   **Reporting Metric:** Relative log-likelihood gains are difficult to interpret.
*   **Writing Style:** Subjective language and unnecessary formatting choices detract slightly from the clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in DPP learning.
*   The EM algorithm demonstrably improves upon existing methods.
*   The theoretical justification and connection to prior work are strong.
*   The empirical evaluation provides convincing evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The performance gains, while consistent, are not dramatic.
*   The complexity of the M-step and reliance on approximations might limit the applicability of the method.
*   Minor stylistic issues detract from the overall clarity of the presentation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper that makes a solid contribution to the field of DPP learning. While the improvements over existing methods are not groundbreaking, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical justification, and the empirical validation warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above regarding the reporting metric and stylistic concerns.
