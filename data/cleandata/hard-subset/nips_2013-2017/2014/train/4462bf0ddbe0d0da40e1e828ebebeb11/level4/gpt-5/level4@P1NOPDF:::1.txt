This paper addresses learning kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The main contribution is a reformulation of the learning objective that outperforms the standard baseline of differentiating the log probability with respect to kernel parameters and performing projected gradient ascent (the K-Ascent, KA, method). The authors cast the problem into an EM framework by observing that any DPP can be expressed as a mixture over “elementary DPPs,” one for each subset of items. They introduce a variational distribution over these subsets, represent it as a k-DPP, and alternate between updating this distribution (E-step) and taking single gradient steps (M-step). Empirically, the EM approach substantially outperforms KA when KA is naively initialized, and offers modest improvements in some settings when KA uses a stronger initialization.

Overall, the approach is interesting and the EM method appears to deliver consistent gains. However, the M-step is quite involved and relies on caveats and approximations that make the method feel less principled than the presentation suggests. Still, it is valuable to see an EM-based formulation that works in practice.

I would like more analysis of initialization effects on EM. Specifically:
- What happens if EM is initialized from a KA solution? Does it further improve the objective or generalization?
- How sensitive is EM to different random or heuristic initializations relative to KA?

I am also confused by the reporting of “relative log likelihood.” Differences in log likelihoods correspond to log ratios of probabilities, which are standard and interpretable. In contrast, taking ratios of log probabilities is hard to interpret and obscures the magnitude of improvements. Why not report raw log-likelihood values or their differences, along with standard deviations across runs?

A few minor style notes:
- Please avoid calling your own algorithm “elegant,” especially given the caveats and approximations required in the M-step.
- The extensive use of bold text in the introduction seems unnecessary and distracts from the content.

In summary: this is an interesting new approach to learning DPP kernels. The experimental section could be strengthened—particularly with a careful study of initialization and clearer, more interpretable metrics. The gains over a basic projected gradient method are not dramatic in all settings, but the EM formulation appears to provide a meaningful, if sometimes modest, improvement. Overall, a solid paper.
