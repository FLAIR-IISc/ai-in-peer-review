

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), which are probabilistic models that favor diverse subsets. While previous work has focused on restricted learning settings (e.g., learning a scalar weight per row [2] or weights for a linear combination of DPPs with fixed kernels [3]), this paper proposes a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) approach that avoids these restrictions by working with the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix. The authors demonstrate that their method eliminates the problematic projection steps required in gradient ascent approaches, which often result in degenerate, near-diagonal kernel matrices that fail to model the crucial negative interactions between items.

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, it provides a clear and technically sound derivation of the EM algorithm for DPP learning by transforming the optimization problem using eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The authors cleverly apply Jensen's inequality to lower-bound the log-likelihood, then develop a coordinate ascent procedure on this lower bound. A particularly elegant contribution is their handling of the eigenvector optimization constraint (V^TV = I), where they recognize the problem as optimization over a Stiefel manifold and employ an efficient multiplicative update that avoids projection. The paper also includes a detailed theoretical analysis showing that the EM approach is asymptotically faster than gradient ascent (O(T1nNk² + T1T2N³) vs. O(T1nN³ + T1T2N³)).

The experimental evaluation on a real-world product recommendation task (Amazon baby registries) provides strong empirical support for the authors' claims. The results demonstrate consistent improvements over the naive gradient ascent approach (K-Ascent), with average gains of 3.7% in test log-likelihood across 13 categories, and up to 16.5% in the safety category. The authors effectively analyze why the EM approach outperforms K-Ascent: the projection step in K-Ascent often leads to near-diagonal kernel matrices that fail to capture negative item interactions, which are essential for modeling diversity. The paper's theoretical analysis of the relationship between the off-diagonal components of the kernel matrix and performance gains is particularly insightful.

The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear organization and precise mathematical derivations. The proofs are rigorous and well-motivated, making the technical content accessible to the NIPS audience. The authors effectively connect their method to prior work while clearly articulating their contributions relative to previous approaches. The supplemental material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the code and data are publicly available.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with strong theoretical foundations and compelling experimental results. The method is complete, not just a position paper, and the authors carefully evaluate both strengths and weaknesses of their approach. The clarity is excellent, with the paper providing enough detail for experts to reproduce the results. The originality is significant, as the paper addresses a fundamental limitation in DPP learning that previous work has not resolved. The significance is demonstrated through the practical application to product recommendation, where the method effectively preserves both quality and diversity properties of DPPs.

The main weaknesses are somewhat limited to the scope of the experiments. While the product recommendation task is compelling, additional experiments on other domains (e.g., document summarization or sensor placement) would further strengthen the case for the method's broader applicability. The paper also briefly mentions but does not fully explore the potential limitations of the EM approach, such as sensitivity to initialization beyond the two methods tested.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel EM-based approach for learning full DPP kernel matrices without restrictive parameterizations
- Theoretical and empirical demonstration of improved performance over existing methods
- Clear derivation and implementation details that facilitate reproducibility
- Strong experimental results on a relevant real-world application
- Theoretical analysis of computational complexity showing asymptotic advantages

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited experimental scope to only one application domain
- Insufficient exploration of potential limitations and failure cases
- The practical impact of the 16.5% improvement might be questioned in some application contexts

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on DPPs by solving a fundamental learning problem that has remained open since the initial DPP papers. The method is both theoretically sound and practically useful, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. The authors successfully demonstrate that their approach preserves the diversity property of DPPs, which is critical for applications where subset diversity is essential. The paper addresses a clear limitation in prior work and provides a robust solution that outperforms existing methods on real-world data. While additional experiments across different domains would strengthen the claims, the current results are compelling and sufficiently demonstrate the value of the proposed method.

Given the paper's strong technical contributions, clear presentation, and significant practical impact on a meaningful application, I recommend acceptance for NIPS. The work represents a substantial advance in DPP learning that will be valuable to both theoretical and applied researchers in machine learning.
