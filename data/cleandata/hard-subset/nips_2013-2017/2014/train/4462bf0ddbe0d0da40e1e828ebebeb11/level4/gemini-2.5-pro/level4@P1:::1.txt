This paper addresses the problem of learning the kernel matrix for a determinantal point process (DPP) from data. The main contribution is a novel algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) that reformulates the non-convex maximum likelihood objective. This approach is presented as an alternative to the standard baseline of projected gradient ascent on the kernel matrix entries, which the authors term K-Ascent (KA). The key idea is to view a DPP as a mixture of "elementary DPPs," one for each subset of the ground set. The algorithm introduces a latent variable representing the choice of elementary DPP and then alternates between an E-step, which updates a variational distribution over this latent variable (itself a k-DPP), and an M-step, which takes a gradient step to update the kernel's eigenvalues and eigenvectors. Experimental results on a product recommendation task demonstrate that the proposed EM method significantly outperforms KA when initialized naively and provides a modest improvement in certain settings when KA uses a more sophisticated initialization.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a novel reformulation of the DPP learning problem within an EM framework, which is a creative approach to a difficult non-convex optimization problem.
*   **Robustness:** The proposed EM algorithm is shown to be more robust to naive initialization compared to the projected gradient ascent baseline, avoiding the issue of converging to near-diagonal solutions.
*   **Efficiency:** The EM algorithm is asymptotically faster than the baseline and demonstrates significant practical speedups in the experiments.

**Arguments for Rejection (Weaknesses):**
*   **Algorithmic Complexity:** The M-step is quite involved and relies on several approximations and heuristics (e.g., single gradient steps, using the true likelihood for line search), which makes the algorithm less straightforward than the EM framework might suggest.
*   **Limited Empirical Gains:** When the baseline is well-initialized using moment matching, the performance gains of the EM method are modest. The improvements are not consistently large across all settings.
*   **Unclear Evaluation Metric:** The use of "relative log-likelihood" is non-standard and makes it difficult to interpret the magnitude of the improvements.

**Detailed Comments:**

Overall, I find the approach interesting, and the EM method appears to yield some tangible improvements over the baseline. The main strength of the paper is the novelty of the EM formulation for this challenging learning problem.

However, the M-step is fairly involved, and the caveats required to make it work are a bit unsatisfying. For instance, the M-step only takes a single gradient step on the eigenvectors and relies on an expensive line search that repeatedly performs an E-step to check for improvement in the true likelihood. These practical compromises detract somewhat from the elegance of the EM framework, but the authors should be commended for developing a workable algorithm despite these complexities.

Experimentally, the analysis could be strengthened by a more thorough investigation of the effect of initialization on the EM algorithm. For example, if the EM algorithm were initialized with the converged result of KA, would it improve upon the solution? This would help clarify whether EM is finding better local optima or is simply a more robust optimization method from the start.

I am also confused by the use of "relative log-likelihood" as a metric. A difference in log-probabilities has a clear interpretation as the log of the ratio of probabilities. Taking the ratio of log-probabilities is not a standard practice and makes it difficult to interpret the magnitude of the reported gains. Why not just report the absolute difference in log-likelihoods? This would be more informative and align with common practice.

Finally, on a minor note about presentation, the use of boldface for emphasis in the introduction is unconventional for a formal research paper and should be removed.

**Conclusion:**

This paper introduces an interesting new method for learning kernels in DPPs. The experiments could be improved, and the performance gains over a well-initialized baseline are not always large, but the proposed algorithm is more robust and efficient. Overall, this is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field.
