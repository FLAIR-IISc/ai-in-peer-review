This paper addresses the challenging non-convex problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from data. Previous work has circumvented the conjectured NP-hardness of this problem by focusing on more restricted, convex learning settings, such as learning weights for kernel rows or for a linear combination of fixed kernels. The main contribution of this work is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the unconstrained kernel matrix.

The core idea is to reparameterize the kernel matrix via its eigendecomposition. This reformulation allows the authors to leverage the mixture representation of a DPP, where any DPP can be seen as a mixture of "elementary DPPs," each defined by a subset of the kernel's eigenvectors. This subset of eigenvectors is treated as a latent variable, enabling the development of an EM algorithm. The E-step calculates the posterior distribution over these latent subsets for each data point, which is shown to be a k-DPP. The M-step then updates the kernel's eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The eigenvalue update has a closed-form solution, while the eigenvector update is performed via a single gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, avoiding the problematic projection step required by naive gradient methods. The authors compare their EM algorithm against a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) on a product recommendation task, demonstrating that their method is more robust, asymptotically faster, and achieves significant gains in test log-likelihood, particularly with a naive initialization or in data-scarce settings.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a central and difficult problem in the practical application of DPPs. Moving beyond the restricted learning settings of prior work to learn a full, non-parametric kernel is a significant contribution. The proposed method makes DPPs more readily applicable to new domains where defining a fixed kernel or a basis of kernels is difficult.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The formulation of the problem within an EM framework is both novel and insightful. The key step of using the eigendecomposition to introduce a latent variable is clever, as it circumvents the projection step that, as the authors rightly point out, can cripple standard gradient ascent by collapsing the kernel to a near-diagonal matrix. The resulting algorithm is not only more robust but also has a better asymptotic runtime than the baseline, which is a strong practical advantage.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, and the motivation for the new algorithm is clearly articulated. The technical development of the EM algorithm, from the introduction of the latent variable to the derivations of the E and M steps, is presented logically and is generally easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **M-Step Approximations:** The M-step is somewhat complex and relies on certain approximations that slightly diminish its elegance. Specifically, instead of fully maximizing the objective with respect to the eigenvectors `V`, the authors take only a single gradient step. Furthermore, because the M-step objective is intractable to evaluate, the line search for the step size requires performing an E-step for each trial, effectively checking for improvement on the true likelihood `L` rather than the auxiliary function `F`. While this pragmatic approach appears to be effective, these details make the algorithm feel less like a conventional EM procedure. A more thorough justification for these choices would be beneficial.

2.  **Experimental Analysis:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the advantage of EM over K-Ascent, especially concerning robustness to initialization. However, the analysis could be deepened. A valuable experiment would be to initialize the EM algorithm with the converged solution from the K-Ascent method. This would help clarify whether EM is primarily a superior optimization procedure that can escape the poor local optima found by KA, or if its main benefit comes from a more stable optimization path from the start.

3.  **Clarity of Evaluation Metric:** The paper reports "relative test log-likelihood differences." This metric appears to be the ratio of log-likelihoods, which is difficult to interpret. A standard difference in log-likelihoods, which corresponds to the log of the ratio of probabilities, is a much more conventional and interpretable measure of performance improvement. The authors should either clarify the motivation for their chosen metric or report the more standard difference.

4.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** The use of bolded text in the introduction is stylistically unconventional for a scientific paper and slightly distracting. Additionally, toning down subjective descriptors for the proposed method would contribute to a more objective tone.

**Summary:**

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and practical solution to the important problem of learning DPP kernels. The proposed EM algorithm is well-motivated and demonstrates clear advantages over a standard projected gradient baseline in terms of both robustness and computational efficiency. While the M-step involves some approximations and the experimental analysis could be expanded, the core contribution is significant and likely to be influential for practitioners and researchers using DPPs. The work clearly advances the state of the art in learning these models.
