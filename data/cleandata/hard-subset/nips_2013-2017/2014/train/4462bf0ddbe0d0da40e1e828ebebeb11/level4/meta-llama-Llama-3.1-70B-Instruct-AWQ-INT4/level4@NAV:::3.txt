This paper presents a novel learning method for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), a distribution that favors diverse sets by assigning higher probabilities. DPPs are typically parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix L, but learning L is NP-hard. Existing approaches have either resorted to partial learning or restrictive parameterizations of L, often compromising on the diversity aspect. In contrast, the proposed method allows for unrestricted parameterization of L and avoids the need for a projection step, which can compromise the similarity property and diversity.

The authors employ Expectation-Maximization (EM) on eigen-values and eigen-vectors to overcome the limitations of previous methods. They explore various optimization algorithms that eliminate the need for projection, including projected gradient ascent, which requires the projection of both eigen-values and eigen-vectors. By exploiting the full-rank property of the matrix V, they successfully avoid the projection of eigen-vectors.

The learning method is noteworthy for preserving the diversity property of DPPs, a key aspect that has been overlooked in previous work. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is insightful, and the mapping of the constraint to an optimization over the Stiefel manifold is a solid contribution. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their learning algorithm on synthetic datasets and a product recommendation task, showcasing its ability to retain both quality and diversity.

The experiments support the paper's claims, particularly in comparison to the Kullback-Leibler (KA) algorithm. Although the conclusion could be more comprehensive in highlighting the paper's contributions, the overall writing is cohesive, and the proofs are well-written. The reduction of optimization to EM is an interesting and well-explained aspect of the paper.

To further illustrate the importance of the "diversity" produced by this algorithm, additional experiments on real-world datasets would be beneficial. Nonetheless, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of DPPs, offering a novel learning method that preserves the diversity property, a crucial aspect of these distributions.
