- Addresses learning the full marginal kernel K for DPPs, overcoming prior methods limited to scalar row weights or fixed-kernel combinations.  
- Notes learning K via log-likelihood is conjectured NP-hard and prior projected gradient (KA) methods cause degeneracy by forcing near-diagonal K.  
- Proposes an EM method using K’s eigendecomposition to avoid projections and preserve diversity modeling.  
- Uses Jensen’s inequality to lower-bound the likelihood and derives a k-DPP E-step (Lemma 1).  
- Provides closed-form updates for eigenvalues and optimizes eigenvectors on the Stiefel manifold (Section 3.5).  
- Preserves negative item interactions crucial for diversity, unlike projection-based approaches.  
- Empirically achieves up to 16.5% higher test log-likelihood than KA on a large product recommendation dataset.  
- Shows asymptotic runtime advantage O(nNk^2) vs. O(nN^3) and is ~2.1× faster on average (Figure 2b).  
- Strength: technically sound, novel EM derivation and manifold optimization advancing full-kernel DPP learning.  
- Strength: clear, well-structured algorithmic presentation and elegant connection between DPP mixtures and k-DPP E-step.  
- Weakness: experiments are limited to product recommendation; broader tasks (e.g., summarization, sensor placement) not evaluated.  
- Weakness: performance advantage is sensitive to initialization—moment-matching initialization reduces gain to 0.4%.  
- Weakness: conclusion overstates generality by claiming similar advantages in all applications without supporting analysis.  
- Pro/con summary: first full-kernel method without projections with strong theory and results (pro); limited experimental scope and initialization dependence weaken generalizability (con).
