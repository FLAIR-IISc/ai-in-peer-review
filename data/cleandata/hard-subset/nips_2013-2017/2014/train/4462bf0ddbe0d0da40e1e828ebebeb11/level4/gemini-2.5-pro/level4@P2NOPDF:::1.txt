This paper addresses the problem of learning kernel matrices for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). The central contribution is a novel reformulation of the learning objective within an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework. This approach is presented as an alternative to the standard method of projected gradient ascent on the log-probability of the kernel parameters, which the authors term K-Ascent (KA).

The proposed method is based on the observation that a DPP can be expressed as a mixture of "elementary DPPs," where each component corresponds to a specific subset of items. The algorithm introduces a variational distribution over these subsets and then alternates between two steps: an E-step that updates this distribution (itself a k-DPP) and an M-step that performs a single gradient update on the kernel parameters.

The results show that the EM method yields a significant improvement over the KA baseline when KA is initialized naively. When KA is initialized more effectively, the EM method still demonstrates modest improvements in some settings.

**Strengths**

*   The formulation of DPP kernel learning as an EM algorithm is novel and provides an interesting alternative to direct gradient-based optimization. It is a creative approach to a challenging problem.
*   The paper demonstrates empirically that the proposed method can find better solutions than the standard K-Ascent baseline, especially when the baseline is susceptible to poor initialization.
*   The technical derivation, particularly for the M-step, is non-trivial, and the authors have successfully developed a workable, albeit complex, algorithm.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Complexity of the M-Step and Practical Gains:** While the EM framework is appealing in principle, the practical implementation of the M-step is quite involved, requiring several approximations and caveats to make it tractable. This complexity somewhat detracts from the method's elegance. Furthermore, the performance gains over a well-initialized K-Ascent baseline appear to be marginal in some experiments. This raises questions about the practical trade-off between the proposed method's increased complexity and its benefits.

2.  **Experimental Analysis of Initialization:** The experimental analysis could be strengthened with a more thorough investigation of initialization. A key question is how the EM algorithm is affected by its own starting point. For instance, it would be highly informative to see an experiment where the EM algorithm is initialized with the solution found by K-Ascent. This would help clarify whether EM's primary advantage is in avoiding poor local optima early on, or if it can consistently refine an already strong solution.

3.  **Choice of Evaluation Metric:** The use of "relative log likelihood" as the primary evaluation metric is confusing and its motivation is unclear. Differences in log-likelihood have a direct and standard probabilistic interpretation (as the log of a ratio of probabilities). However, a ratio of log-likelihoods is difficult to interpret and does not correspond to a standard measure of model comparison. The authors should either provide a clear justification for this choice of metric or, preferably, report direct differences in log-likelihoods to facilitate a more straightforward interpretation of the results.

4.  **Presentation and Writing Style:** On a minor note, the writing style could be improved. The authors should avoid using subjective and self-congratulatory descriptors like "elegant" to describe their own work, especially when the claim is debatable given the approximations required. Additionally, the excessive use of bold text in the introduction is distracting and stylistically unconventional for a scientific paper. A more objective tone and standard formatting would improve the paper's professionalism and readability.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this paper introduces an interesting and novel EM-based approach for learning DPP kernels. While the improvements over a strong baseline are not consistently large and the method introduces significant technical complexity, it represents a valuable contribution to the literature. The paper is promising, but it could be significantly strengthened by expanding the experimental analysis and addressing the clarity of the evaluation metric and presentation.
