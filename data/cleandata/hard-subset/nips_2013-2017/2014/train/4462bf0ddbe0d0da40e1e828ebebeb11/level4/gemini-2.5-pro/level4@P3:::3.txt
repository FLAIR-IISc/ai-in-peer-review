### **Summary**

This paper addresses the challenging non-convex problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from data. A DPP is a probabilistic model over subsets of a ground set, capable of elegantly capturing notions of both item quality and diversity. The model is parameterized by a positive semi-definite kernel matrix, `L`. While DPPs have attractive inference properties, learning the full kernel `L` via maximum likelihood is conjectured to be NP-hard. Consequently, prior work has focused on more constrained, convex learning settings, such as learning scalar weights for each row of `L` (learning item quality but not similarity) or learning weights for a linear combination of fixed DPPs (losing the desirable computational properties of a single DPP).

The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) style algorithm to tackle the full learning problem. The key insight is to re-parameterize the kernel matrix by its eigendecomposition (eigenvalues `λ` and eigenvectors `V`). By viewing the DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs, they introduce a latent variable `J` corresponding to the selection of a subset of eigenvectors. This allows for the application of Jensen's inequality to derive a tractable lower bound on the log-likelihood. The algorithm then performs coordinate ascent on this lower bound, alternating between an E-step (updating the posterior over the latent variable `J`) and an M-step (updating the parameters `λ` and `V`). A significant technical contribution is the handling of the orthogonality constraint on the eigenvectors `V` by performing gradient updates on the Stiefel manifold, which elegantly avoids the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent. This projection step is shown to be a major weakness of the baseline approach, as it can cause the learned kernel to become near-diagonal, thereby failing to model the crucial diversity-inducing negative interactions between items.

The proposed EM algorithm is compared against a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) on a real-world product recommendation task using Amazon baby registry data. The experiments demonstrate that the EM approach is more robust, achieving significant relative gains in test log-likelihood (up to 16.5%), particularly when initialization is less informed or when training data is limited. Furthermore, the EM algorithm is shown to be asymptotically and empirically faster than the baseline.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel and principled approach to a well-known and difficult problem in learning DPPs. Moving beyond the restricted learning settings of prior work to learn the full kernel is a significant contribution. This work enables DPPs to be applied in settings where the underlying similarity structure is unknown and must be learned from data, greatly expanding their practical utility.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The derivation of the EM algorithm is technically sound and insightful. The re-parameterization via eigendecomposition and the introduction of a latent variable to facilitate an EM framework are clever and effective. The identification of the posterior `q(J|Y_i)` as a k-DPP is an elegant result. Crucially, recognizing that the eigenvector update is an optimization problem on a Stiefel manifold and applying the appropriate machinery is a sophisticated solution that directly addresses the core weakness of the naive projection-based method. The theoretical analysis of the algorithm's runtime complexity is also a valuable contribution.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, reviewing the necessary background on DPPs, and then systematically building up their proposed algorithm from the re-parameterization to the final EM steps. The justification for why projected gradient ascent is problematic is clear and convincing.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively support the paper's central claims. The comparison with the K-Ascent baseline is fair and well-designed. The use of different initialization schemes (Wishart vs. moments-matching) and data regimes (full vs. data-poor) clearly demonstrates the superior robustness of the proposed EM method. The qualitative example of the recommended "safety" items, where the baseline suggests three redundant baby monitors while the EM model produces a more diverse set, provides a compelling and intuitive illustration of the algorithm's practical advantages.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is strong, and the weaknesses are minor.

1.  **Scope of Experiments:** The empirical validation is convincing but is confined to a single type of real-world task (product recommendation). Given that DPPs are widely used in other areas like document summarization, sensor placement, and image search, demonstrating the algorithm's effectiveness on at least one other domain would further strengthen the claims of its general applicability and impact.

2.  **Conclusion:** As the draft review correctly notes, the conclusion is somewhat brief. While it effectively summarizes the main result, it could be slightly expanded to reiterate the key technical insights (e.g., the Stiefel manifold update) and to more broadly speculate on the future impact this work might have on applications that rely on learning models of diversity from data.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

-   The connection between the performance gain of EM and the off-diagonal "heaviness" of the true data marginals (the `d` value) is a nice piece of analysis that reinforces the paper's core motivation.
-   The proof in the supplement for Lemma 1, showing that the posterior `q(J|Y_i)` is a k-DPP, is an important and elegant theoretical result that underpins the efficiency of the E-step.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and technically sound contribution to the literature on learning probabilistic models of diversity. It addresses a key limitation of previous work with a novel and well-motivated algorithm, supported by strong empirical evidence.
