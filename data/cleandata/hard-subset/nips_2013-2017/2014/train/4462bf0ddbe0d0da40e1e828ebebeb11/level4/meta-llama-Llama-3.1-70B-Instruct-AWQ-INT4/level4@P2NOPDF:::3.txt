This paper presents a novel learning method for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), a distribution over a fixed ground set that assigns higher probability to diverse sets. DPPs can be parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix L. However, learning L is NP-hard, and previous work has only discussed partial learning of L or resorted to restricting its parametric form. In contrast, the authors propose a learning method that does not restrict the parameterization of L, thereby preserving the diversity property of the DPP.

The proposed method avoids the need for a projection step, which can compromise the similarity property and diversity of the DPP. Instead, the authors use Expectation-Maximization (EM) on eigen-values and eigen-vectors to overcome this disadvantage. They explore various optimization algorithms to solve this problem without requiring projection. Notably, they exploit the fact that the matrix V is full-rank to avoid projecting eigen-vectors. Jensen's inequality is used to lower bound the objective function and construct an EM procedure.

The learning method is useful as it preserves the diversity property of the DPP, which is often compromised in previous work that focuses solely on the quality property. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is insightful, and the mapping of the constraint to an optimization over the Stiefel manifold to eliminate eigen-vector projection is a solid contribution. The authors also demonstrate that the inverse distribution is a DPP.

The paper is well-supported by experiments on both synthetic datasets and a product recommendation task. The results show that the learning method retains the quality and diversity of the DPP, outperforming the Kulesza and Taskar (KA) algorithm. While the KA algorithm replaces only two of the top 10 products with less likely ones, this still highlights the contribution of the paper. However, the conclusion could be improved by emphasizing all the contributions of the paper.

The paper is written cohesively, and the proofs are well-written. The step-by-step reduction of optimization to EM is interesting and well-explained. One potential improvement is to include more experiments on real-world datasets to illustrate the importance of the "diversity" produced by this algorithm when compared to others in this area. Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of DPPs and is well-suited for publication.
