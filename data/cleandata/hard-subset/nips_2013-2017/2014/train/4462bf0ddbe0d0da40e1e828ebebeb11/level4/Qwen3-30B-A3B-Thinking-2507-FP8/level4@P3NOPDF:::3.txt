

This paper addresses a significant limitation in learning Determinantal Point Process (DPP) parameters: the NP-hardness of estimating the full positive semidefinite matrix \(L\), which previous work circumvented by imposing restrictive parametric forms. These restrictions often sacrifice diversity—critical to DPPs' core appeal—by focusing solely on quality (e.g., item relevance). The authors propose a novel learning method that avoids such restrictions by parameterizing \(L\) freely, without requiring projection steps that compromise diversity (as in gradient ascent approaches). Their key insight leverages the full-rank property of eigenvectors to eliminate the need for eigenvector projection, while using Jensen’s inequality to derive a tractable EM procedure over eigenvalues and eigenvectors. This approach preserves both quality and diversity, a gap in prior work.

The technical contributions are well-motivated and rigorous. The derivation of the EM algorithm is particularly compelling: by mapping the constrained optimization to the Stiefel manifold (where eigenvectors reside), the authors avoid projections entirely, ensuring the similarity constraint is maintained without diversity loss. They further prove that the inverse distribution remains a valid DPP—a non-trivial result that solidifies their theoretical foundation. The step-by-step reduction from the original optimization problem to the EM procedure is clear and logically sound, enhancing reproducibility.

Experiments validate the method’s effectiveness. On synthetic data, the algorithm successfully maintains diversity while matching quality benchmarks. In a real-world product recommendation task, it outperforms the baseline (KA) by retaining high-quality, diverse top-10 recommendations: KA replaced only two top-10 items with less probable alternatives, demonstrating that the method preserves diversity without sacrificing relevance. This aligns with the paper’s core claim. However, the experimental scope is limited to one real-world dataset, which somewhat weakens the argument for broader applicability. Additional experiments on diverse real-world datasets would better illustrate how the diversity metric impacts downstream tasks compared to existing methods.

The paper is cohesively written, with proofs presented concisely and accessibly. The EM derivation is the strongest aspect, offering a clear pathway to avoid common pitfalls in DPP learning. That said, the conclusion is underdeveloped—it fails to explicitly highlight the method’s key innovation (free parameterization without projection) and the theoretical proof about the inverse DPP. This omission diminishes the impact of the work. A more thorough discussion of these contributions would strengthen the narrative.

Strengths outweigh weaknesses. The method directly addresses a well-known challenge in DPP learning, and the technical execution is robust. The avoidance of projection steps is a practical advance, as prior methods (e.g., projected gradient ascent) risk distorting the diversity property through iterative corrections. The authors’ focus on preserving both quality and diversity—rather than optimizing for one at the expense of the other—is a meaningful contribution to the field. The experiments, while limited, provide sufficient evidence for the core claim.

Minor concerns: The phrase "compromises on the diversity dimension" is imprecise; "sacrifices diversity" would be clearer. The term "scalar vector" for \(L\)’s parameterization is misleading (it should be "scalar diagonal" or similar). The reference to "KA" as a baseline requires prior definition in the paper, which the review assumes is present. 

Overall, this is a strong contribution to DPP learning. It offers a theoretically grounded, practical solution that maintains the fundamental diversity property without restrictive assumptions. The work advances the state of the art by resolving a key limitation in prior approaches. While the experimental section could be expanded, the method’s novelty and soundness make it a valuable addition to the literature. I recommend acceptance. The paper should be strengthened by explicitly emphasizing the avoidance of eigenvector projection and the inverse DPP proof in the conclusion, but this does not detract from its technical merit.
