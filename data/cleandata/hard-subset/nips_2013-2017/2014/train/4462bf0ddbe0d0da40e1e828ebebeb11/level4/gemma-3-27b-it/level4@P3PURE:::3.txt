Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) define a distribution over a fixed ground set, assigning higher probability to diverse subsets. DPPs are parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix L; however, learning L is NP-hard, leading prior work to focus on partial learning or restrictive parameterizations. Learning a low-rank approximation of L, for example, can compromise the DPP’s ability to model diversity. This paper presents a learning method for L without restricting its parameterization. Crucially, the proposed method avoids the projection steps inherent in gradient ascent, which can degrade the similarity properties and, consequently, the diversity of the resulting DPP. Instead, the authors leverage an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm operating on the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of L to circumvent this issue. They explore optimization techniques that enable this without requiring projection, noting that projected gradient ascent necessitates projections on both eigenvalues *and* eigenvectors. By exploiting the full-rank property of the eigenvector matrix, they avoid eigenvector projection. Jensen’s inequality is employed to lower bound the objective function, facilitating the construction of the EM procedure.

The strength of this learning method lies in its preservation of the DPP’s diversity property, a characteristic often overlooked in prior work that prioritizes only the quality of selections. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is particularly insightful. Mapping the constraint to an optimization problem over the Stiefel manifold to eliminate eigenvector projection and demonstrating that the inverse distribution is itself a DPP are significant contributions. The authors validate their learning algorithm on both synthetic and real-world datasets, specifically a product recommendation task. The experimental results support the paper’s claims; the learned DPP retains both quality and diversity, as demonstrated through comparisons with the Kernel Alignment (KA) method. Even though KA replaces only two of the top ten recommended products with less relevant items, this result effectively highlights the contribution of the paper and substantiates its initial premise. The conclusion is somewhat abrupt, failing to fully reiterate all the paper’s contributions, but overall, the paper is written cohesively. The proofs are well-structured, and the systematic reduction of the optimization problem to an EM algorithm is a noteworthy achievement.

The paper is coherent and well-written, with clear and well-supported proofs. The step-by-step reduction to the EM algorithm is particularly interesting and well explained. Further experiments on additional real-world datasets could more fully illustrate the importance of the “diversity” produced by this algorithm compared to other approaches in this field.
