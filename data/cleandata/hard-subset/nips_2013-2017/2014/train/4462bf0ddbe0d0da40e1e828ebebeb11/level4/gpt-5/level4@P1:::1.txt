This paper addresses learning the kernel matrix for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The main contribution is an EM-based reformulation that aims to overcome the shortcomings of optimizing the log-likelihood directly with projected gradient ascent on the kernel entries (the KA baseline). The key idea is to view a DPP as a mixture over “elementary DPPs” indexed by subsets, introduce a variational distribution over these subsets, and alternate between: (i) an E-step that updates this distribution (represented as a k-DPP), and (ii) an M-step that takes single gradient steps. Empirically, the EM method substantially outperforms KA under naive initialization and provides modest improvements in some settings under more informed initialization.

Quality:
- The approach is technically sound overall and clearly motivated by the failure modes of projected gradient methods for PSD kernels with eigenvalue constraints. Framing learning through the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs view is natural and well-justified.
- The M-step is fairly involved, and the caveats and approximations required to make it practical are somewhat unsatisfying, although the authors do succeed in making the method work in practice.
- The experimental comparison is informative: EM provides clear gains over KA with naive initialization, and smaller gains when KA is initialized more intelligently. I would appreciate additional analysis of initialization effects on EM itself. For example, if EM were initialized from the final KA solution, would it improve upon it?
- I am also confused by the use of “relative log-likelihood” as the reported metric. Log-likelihood differences correspond to log probability ratios; taking ratios of log-likelihoods is nonstandard and hard to interpret. Why not report absolute log-likelihoods or differences in log-likelihood? Please clarify the rationale and, if possible, include more interpretable measures.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized, and the high-level story is clear. The description of the mixture view and the k-DPP E-step is helpful.
- That said, parts of the M-step discussion feel dense, with important caveats (e.g., taking only single gradient steps) buried among derivations. It would help to isolate assumptions and approximations and summarize their practical implications.
- A style suggestion: avoid subjective claims about elegance (e.g., calling the algorithm “elegant”) when the method relies on nontrivial approximations. Also, the boldface text in the introduction is unnecessary and distracts from the content.

Originality:
- Recasting DPP learning via an EM framework grounded in the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs perspective, and representing the E-step distribution as a k-DPP, is a novel and interesting direction relative to the standard projected gradient approach on kernel entries. The use of this structure to sidestep problematic projections is appealing.

Significance:
- The method yields consistent improvements over KA when KA is poorly initialized, which is a practically relevant regime. With stronger initialization, the improvements are smaller but still present in some settings. Overall, this is a useful contribution for practitioners who want to learn expressive DPP kernels beyond heavily constrained parameterizations.
- The gains are not universally large, and the reliance on a careful M-step (with single-step updates) tempers enthusiasm somewhat. Still, the method is a meaningful advance over the commonly used projected gradient baseline.

Questions and suggestions:
- Initialization: Please evaluate EM initialized from the final KA solution. Does EM consistently improve upon KA’s endpoint? More generally, how sensitive is EM to different initializations?
- Reporting: Please justify the use of relative log-likelihood and consider supplementing with absolute log-likelihoods or differences, which are more standard and interpretable.

Pros:
- Well-motivated EM formulation using the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs viewpoint and k-DPP structure in the E-step.
- Robust empirical gains over KA under naive initialization; some gains with stronger initialization.
- Addresses a known weakness of projected gradient with PSD/eigenvalue constraints.

Cons:
- M-step requires approximations and single-step updates; the presentation could better separate assumptions from guarantees.
- Limited analysis of EM’s sensitivity to initialization beyond the comparison to KA.
- Use of relative log-likelihood is nonstandard and difficult to interpret.

Overall assessment:
Interesting and generally solid contribution to learning expressive DPP kernels. While the improvements over a well-initialized projected gradient method are not huge in all cases, the approach is promising and practically useful. With clearer reporting and additional analysis of initialization effects, this would be a strong paper.
