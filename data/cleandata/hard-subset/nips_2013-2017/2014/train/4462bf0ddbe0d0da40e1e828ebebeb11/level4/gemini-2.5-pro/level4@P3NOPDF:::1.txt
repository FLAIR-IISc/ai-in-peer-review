This paper addresses the problem of learning kernel matrices for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). The primary contribution is a novel reformulation of the learning objective within an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework. This approach is proposed as an alternative to the standard method of performing projected gradient ascent on the log-likelihood with respect to the kernel parameters, which the authors term K-Ascent (KA).

The core idea is to represent any DPP as a mixture of "elementary DPPs," where each elementary DPP corresponds to a specific subset of items. The EM algorithm then proceeds by introducing a variational distribution over these subsets in the E-step, which is updated to be the posterior distribution (itself a k-DPP). The M-step involves taking a single gradient step to update the kernel parameters.

**Strengths:**

The reformulation of the DPP learning problem as an EM algorithm is a novel and interesting contribution. The conceptualization of a DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs provides a fresh perspective and a principled motivation for the proposed algorithm. The experimental results show that the EM method can significantly outperform the baseline KA approach when the latter is initialized naively. Even when KA is initialized more intelligently, the EM method demonstrates modest improvements in some settings.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**

1.  **Experimental Analysis of Initialization:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the sensitivity of the baseline KA method to initialization. However, a similar analysis for the proposed EM algorithm is missing. A crucial experiment would be to initialize the EM algorithm with the converged solution from the KA method. Would the EM algorithm be able to further refine and improve upon this solution? Such a result would provide stronger evidence that the EM formulation is not just a better initialization scheme but a more effective optimization procedure for this problem.

2.  **Clarity of Evaluation Metric:** I am confused by the use of "relative log-likelihood" as the primary performance metric. The difference between two log-likelihoods has a clear probabilistic interpretation, as it corresponds to the log of the ratio of probabilities. However, reporting the *ratio* of two log-likelihoods is non-standard, and its interpretation is not immediately obvious. I would have a difficult time understanding the magnitude of the reported improvements based on this metric. Could the authors please clarify the motivation for this choice, or preferably, report the standard difference in log-likelihoods to facilitate a more direct and interpretable comparison?

3.  **Complexity of the M-Step:** While the EM framework is conceptually appealing, the practical implementation of the M-step is noted to be quite involved. The approximations and caveats required to make it tractable somewhat diminish the practical elegance of the approach.

**Minor Comments on Presentation:**

*   I would suggest the authors refrain from using subjective descriptors like "elegant" to describe their own algorithm, particularly when the practical implementation involves non-trivial approximations.
*   The use of bolded text in the introduction to emphasize contributions felt unnecessary and was visually distracting. The quality of the ideas should speak for itself without such formatting.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this paper presents an interesting new approach for learning kernels in DPPs. The improvements over the basic projected gradient algorithm are promising, though not always substantial. The paper makes a solid contribution, but its impact could be strengthened by a more thorough experimental analysis and the use of more standard evaluation metrics for clarity. It is a good paper that warrants acceptance.
