This paper addresses the problem of learning kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The primary contribution is a novel reformulation of the learning objective, which is shown to outperform the standard approach of projected gradient ascent on the log-likelihood with respect to the kernel parameters (referred to as K-Ascent or KA). The proposed method frames the problem using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework. This is achieved by leveraging the insight that a DPP can be viewed as a mixture of "elementary DPPs," each corresponding to a specific subset of items. The algorithm introduces a variational distribution over these subsets and then alternates between an E-step, which updates this distribution (represented as a k-DPP), and an M-step, which involves performing gradient-based updates to the kernel parameters.

The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed EM method significantly outperforms a naively initialized KA baseline. When KA is initialized more effectively, the proposed method still shows a modest improvement in some settings. Overall, the proposed approach is conceptually interesting and appears to provide tangible improvements. The derivation of the M-step is non-trivial, and the approximations and constraints required for its implementation slightly detract from the method's elegance. Nevertheless, the successful implementation of this complex step is a notable achievement.

Regarding the experiments, a more thorough analysis of the EM algorithm's sensitivity to initialization would be beneficial. A particularly insightful experiment would be to initialize the EM algorithm with the converged solution from the KA baseline to determine if it can further refine the learned kernel.

The choice of "relative log-likelihood" as an evaluation metric is unclear. Differences in log-likelihoods have a natural interpretation as the log-ratio of probabilities. Reporting the ratio of log-likelihoods, however, is non-standard, and its statistical meaning is difficult to interpret. The authors should clarify the motivation for this choice or consider reporting the standard difference in log-likelihoods instead.

On a minor note regarding presentation, the manuscript could be strengthened by adopting a more objective tone. For instance, self-characterizing the proposed algorithm as "elegant" is best avoided, particularly given the approximations required in the M-step. Additionally, the use of bold text in the introduction for emphasis is generally unnecessary in formal scientific writing.

In summary, this paper introduces a novel and interesting EM-based approach for learning DPP kernels. While the empirical gains over a well-initialized baseline are not always substantial and the experimental analysis could be expanded, the contribution is sound and the paper is of good quality.
