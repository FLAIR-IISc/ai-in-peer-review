This paper addresses the problem of learning kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The core contribution lies in a novel reformulation of the kernel learning objective, offering an alternative to the standard approach of differentiating the log-likelihood with respect to kernel parameters and employing projected gradient ascent (referred to as K-Ascent, or KA, in the paper). The authors propose an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework, leveraging the decomposition of any DPP into a mixture of “elementary DPPs” – one for each possible subset of elements. Their algorithm iteratively updates a variational distribution over these subsets, modeled as a k-DPP, and then performs a single gradient step in the M-step. The experimental results demonstrate that the EM method consistently outperforms KA when the latter is initialized with naive parameters, and shows modest improvements even with more informed KA initializations.

Overall, I find the proposed approach to be interesting and the empirical evidence suggests that the EM formulation yields a worthwhile improvement. While the M-step is mathematically complex and relies on caveats and approximations to achieve practicality, the fact that it functions at all is a significant achievement. 

However, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened. I would be interested to see an analysis of the impact of initialization on the EM algorithm's performance. Specifically, exploring whether initializing the EM algorithm with the solution obtained from KA could lead to further improvements would be insightful. 

I also have concerns regarding the reported evaluation metric: relative log-likelihood. Log-likelihood differences directly correspond to probability ratios, and thus, taking the ratio of log-likelihoods obscures the interpretability of the results. Reporting differences in log-probabilities would be clearer and more readily understandable. I found it difficult to assess the practical significance of the reported relative log-likelihood values.

Finally, I offer a minor comment regarding the presentation. While the approach is novel, the authors’ use of subjective descriptors like “elegant” feels unnecessary, particularly given the approximations and caveats required in the M-step. Furthermore, the bolding of text within the introduction detracts from readability. 

In summary, this paper presents an interesting new approach to learning kernels within the context of DPPs. While the experimental improvements over the baseline projected gradient algorithm are not substantial, the methodology is sound and the results are promising. With some refinement of the experimental analysis and a more conservative writing style, this paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field.
