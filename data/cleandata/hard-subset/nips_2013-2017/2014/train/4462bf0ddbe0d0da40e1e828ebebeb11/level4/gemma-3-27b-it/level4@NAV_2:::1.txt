This paper addresses the problem of learning kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The core contribution is a novel expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm that offers an alternative to standard gradient-based methods with projection (K-Ascent or KA, as termed in the paper) for maximizing the log-likelihood. The approach leverages the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix and a mixture representation involving “elementary DPPs” to formulate a tractable EM procedure. The algorithm alternates between updating a variational distribution over subsets (represented as a k-DPP) and performing gradient steps on the kernel parameters. Experimental results demonstrate that the EM method generally outperforms KA, particularly when KA is initialized naively, with more modest gains observed with improved KA initialization.

The paper presents an interesting and technically sound approach to a challenging problem. The reformulation within an EM framework is a valuable contribution, and the resulting algorithm appears to offer genuine improvements over existing methods. While the M-step involves some approximations and caveats, the authors effectively address these complexities and demonstrate practical performance. The work is well-motivated, clearly connected to previous research in DPPs and related areas [1, 2, 3], and appropriately cites relevant literature. 

I would have appreciated a more in-depth analysis of the impact of initialization on the EM algorithm’s performance. Specifically, investigating whether initializing EM with the solution obtained from KA could further enhance the results would be insightful. Additionally, the use of relative log-likelihood as the primary evaluation metric is somewhat unclear. The interpretation of ratios of log probabilities is not immediately intuitive, and reporting differences in log probabilities might provide a more direct and interpretable assessment. A minor stylistic point: the authors’ self-referential descriptions of the algorithm’s elegance could be toned down, as the complexity of the method and necessary approximations somewhat qualify such claims. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to DPP kernel learning.
*   Empirically demonstrates improved performance compared to a standard baseline (KA).
*   Well-motivated and clearly presented, with appropriate connections to related work.
*   Addresses a challenging optimization problem with a creative solution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The performance gains over KA, while consistent, are not always substantial.
*   The M-step involves approximations and requires careful consideration.
*   The evaluation metric (relative log-likelihood) could be more interpretable.
