

This paper addresses learning kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The primary contribution is a reformulation of the learning objective within an EM framework, which outperforms the standard approach of differentiating the log-probability with respect to kernel parameters and performing projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent, or KA). The key insight leverages the representation of any DPP as a mixture over "elementary DPPs" (one per subset of elements), introducing a variational distribution over subsets. The algorithm alternates between updating this subset distribution (as a k-DPP) and taking single gradient steps in the M-step. Results demonstrate that the EM method significantly outperforms KA with naive initialization, and marginally improves upon KA with intelligent initialization.  

Overall, the approach is interesting and yields tangible improvements. While the M-step is complex and requires caveats that feel unsatisfying, the method successfully achieves its goal.  

Experimentally, a deeper analysis of initialization effects would strengthen the work. For instance, initializing EM with KA’s solution and evaluating its performance relative to KA would clarify the benefits.  

I am also puzzled by the use of relative log likelihood as a reporting metric. Since log-probability differences reflect probability ratios, reporting ratios of log probabilities obscures interpretation—differences in log probabilities would be more intuitive and meaningful.  

Minor stylistic notes: Avoiding self-referential claims of elegance is advisable, especially given the M-step’s caveats and approximations. Additionally, bolding text in the introduction is unnecessary.  

This presents an interesting new approach for DPP kernel learning. While experimental improvements over the baseline are modest, and the paper could better address initialization and metrics, it remains a solid contribution.
