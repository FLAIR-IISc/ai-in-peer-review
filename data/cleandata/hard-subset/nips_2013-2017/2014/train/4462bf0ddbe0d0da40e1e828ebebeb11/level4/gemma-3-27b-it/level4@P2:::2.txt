This paper introduces an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). The method cleverly combines several techniques: eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix, optimization on the Stiefel manifold, representing DPPs as mixtures of elementary DPPs, and lower-bounding the log-likelihood using Jensen’s inequality. Experiments demonstrate that the proposed approach outperforms a simpler projected gradient ascent method ("K-Ascent").

**Quality:** The method appears technically sound and the claimed improvements are plausible. However, the evaluation could be strengthened. The experiments are limited in scope and do not provide comparisons to alternative subset selection techniques (e.g., those referenced in the introduction). This makes it difficult to assess the true benefit of adopting a DPP approach over existing methods. A more thorough experimental evaluation, including comparisons to established baselines, would significantly bolster the paper’s claims.

**Clarity:** The paper’s clarity could be substantially improved. The writing style is somewhat convoluted and occasionally difficult to follow. Notation is not always clearly defined, and definitions sometimes appear *after* their initial use. The narrative structure, which interweaves the method's development with discussions of alternative approaches and potential issues, disrupts the flow. A more direct presentation of the developed method, followed by a dedicated discussion section, would enhance readability.

**Originality:** The paper appears to present a novel contribution. I am not aware of other work that tackles DPP learning with this specific combination of techniques. The approach of leveraging eigendecomposition and an EM framework within the context of DPP learning seems original.

**Significance:** The proposed method represents a valuable advancement in subset selection. A robust and efficient algorithm for learning DPPs has the potential to impact various applications where modeling diversity and quality tradeoffs is crucial, as outlined in the introduction (recommendation systems, document summarization, etc.).

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **p.2, line 81:** The “marginal kernel K” is used without prior definition. Clarify what qualifies a kernel as a “marginal kernel.”
*   **p.2, line 100:** Variable ‘V’ is not defined before its use.
*   **p.3, line 159:** The phrase "minimises the change to Frobenius norm" is imprecise. Suggest rephrasing as "chooses the closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix to Q."
*   **p.4:** The discussion of the Stiefel manifold and the reference to [26] make this section difficult to follow.  It would be more accessible to briefly outline the simplified approach directly within the paper, rather than requiring the reader to consult another source.
*   **p.4, line 189:** Clarify what is meant by "weight" in this context.
*   **p.7, line 324:** The rationale behind equation (30) is unclear. Explain why `m_{ij}` is not used and why initializing with the empirical covariance matrix directly was not successful.

**Summary of Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to DPP learning.
*   Demonstrated improvement over a baseline method (K-Ascent).
*   Potential for significant impact in applications requiring diverse subset selection.
*   Addresses a challenging problem (learning full kernel matrices for DPPs).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation, lacking comparison to standard subset selection methods.
*   Clarity of writing could be significantly improved.
*   The benefits of the DPP approach, relative to other techniques, are not definitively established.



In conclusion, the paper presents a worthwhile contribution, but improvements in clarity and experimental validation are needed before strong acceptance can be recommended. I believe the paper has the potential to be a strong contribution with revisions addressing these points.
