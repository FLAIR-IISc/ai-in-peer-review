This paper presents a novel approach for learning kernel matrices in determinantal point processes (DPPs). The main contribution is a reformulation of the objective within an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework, which outperforms the standard approach of differentiating the log-probability with respect to kernel parameters and doing projected gradient ascent (referred to as K-Ascent or KA in the paper). 

The EM method works by representing any DPP as a mixture over "elementary DPPs", where there is one elementary DPP for each subset of elements. A variational distribution is instantiated over these subsets, and the algorithm alternates between updating the distribution over these subsets, represented as a k-DPP, and taking single gradient steps in the M-step. 

The paper is well-motivated, and the EM approach seems interesting. The authors provide a clear explanation of the EM algorithm and its components, including the E-step and M-step. The M-step is fairly involved, and the caveats required to make it work are a bit unsatisfying, but the authors do a good job of explaining the challenges and their solutions.

The experimental results show that the EM method outperforms the KA method by a significant margin when KA is initialized naively. However, when KA is initialized more intelligently, the EM method only provides a small improvement. This suggests that the EM method is more robust to initialization, but its advantages may be limited in certain settings.

One potential area for improvement is in the analysis of the effect of initialization on the EM algorithm. For example, it would be interesting to see how the EM algorithm performs when initialized with the result of KA. This could provide insight into whether the EM algorithm can improve upon the solution obtained by KA.

Additionally, the use of relative log-likelihood as a measure to report results is somewhat unclear. Log-probability differences reflect ratios of probabilities, so it is not immediately apparent why the authors take ratios of log-probabilities. A more conventional approach would be to report differences in log-probabilities.

The writing style is generally clear, but there are a few minor issues. The authors' tendency to describe their own algorithm as "elegant" is somewhat debatable, given the caveats and approximations required in the M-step. Additionally, the bolding of text in the introduction is unnecessary and can be distracting.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting new approach for learning kernels in DPPs. While the improvements over the basic projected gradient algorithm are not huge, the EM method is more robust to initialization and provides a useful alternative. With some minor revisions to address the issues mentioned above, the paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field.

Strengths:

* The EM approach is novel and interesting.
* The paper provides a clear explanation of the EM algorithm and its components.
* The experimental results show that the EM method can outperform the KA method in certain settings.
