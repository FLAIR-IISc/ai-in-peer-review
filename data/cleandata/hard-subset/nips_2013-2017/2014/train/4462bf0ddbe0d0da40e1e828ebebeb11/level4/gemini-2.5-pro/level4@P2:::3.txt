This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from data. DPPs are probabilistic models that capture both item quality and set diversity, parameterized by a positive semi-definite kernel matrix L. Maximizing the log-likelihood to learn L is a non-convex problem, conjectured to be NP-hard. Consequently, prior work has focused on restricted, convex learning settings, such as learning a single weight per item or learning weights for a mixture of fixed DPPs. While tractable, these methods limit the model's ability to learn the crucial similarity and diversity relationships between items directly from data.

The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to learn the full kernel. The key insight is to reparameterize the kernel in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors. By introducing a hidden variable related to the selection of eigenvectors and applying Jensen's inequality, they derive an iterative procedure that effectively optimizes the log-likelihood. This EM approach leads to a closed-form update for the eigenvalues and, notably, avoids the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent, which can lead to poor local optima. The update for the eigenvectors is handled elegantly through an optimization over a Stiefel manifold, which naturally enforces the orthogonality constraint without projection.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The primary strength of this work is its novel and effective approach to a fundamental, hard problem in the DPP literature: learning the full kernel matrix without restrictive assumptions. This is a significant advance over prior methods that could only learn partial or highly constrained parameterizations, often sacrificing the ability to model diversity. This work provides a practical algorithm for a problem previously considered intractable, which is likely to be impactful for researchers and practitioners using DPPs.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical contribution is strong. The derivation of the EM algorithm is elegant and well-motivated. The formulation involves several clever steps, including identifying the conditional distribution in the E-step as a k-DPP (allowing for efficient computation of marginals) and employing Stiefel manifold optimization for the M-step. This approach successfully avoids the pitfalls of standard projected gradient ascent, which the authors show can collapse to near-diagonal solutions that fail to model diversity.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The step-by-step derivation of the algorithm from the eigendecomposition perspective is easy to follow and insightful. The motivation for moving away from projected gradient ascent is well-argued and supported by the experiments.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The empirical evaluation on a real-world product recommendation task provides convincing support for the proposed method. The experiments demonstrate that the EM algorithm is not only more robust than the K-Ascent baseline (especially with less informative initializations or in data-poor settings) but also asymptotically and practically faster. The qualitative example in Figure 2, where the baseline method recommends three different baby monitors while the EM-trained model produces a more diverse and plausible set, is a compelling illustration of the method's practical benefit.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Scope of Experiments:** While the baby registry dataset is a good fit for the problem, the paper's claims of general applicability could be further strengthened by testing the algorithm on tasks from other domains where DPPs are used, such as document summarization or image search. This would provide a broader demonstration of the algorithm's robustness and utility.

2.  **Conclusion:** The conclusion section is somewhat brief and could do more to summarize the key technical innovations (e.g., the reparameterization and the Stiefel manifold update) and their broader implications for learning DPP models.

**Evaluation Summary**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by both the theoretical derivation of the EM algorithm and the experimental results. The work is a complete and practical contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is very clear and well-organized. The authors effectively explain the limitations of prior work and motivate their proposed solution. The derivations are presented logically and are easy to follow for a reader familiar with the area.
*   **Originality:** The proposed EM algorithm for learning a full DPP kernel is highly original. It recasts a difficult non-convex problem into a tractable iterative procedure by cleverly changing the parameterization, which is a novel and important contribution to the field.
*   **Significance:** The work is highly significant. It provides the first practical algorithm for learning an unconstrained DPP kernel from data, overcoming a major limitation of previous work. This allows the crucial diversity component of the model to be learned directly from data, rather than being pre-specified. The method's improved robustness and superior speed are also significant practical advantages that will likely lead to its adoption.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and empirically-validated solution to an important and difficult problem. It represents a clear advance for the field of machine learning with determinantal point processes.
