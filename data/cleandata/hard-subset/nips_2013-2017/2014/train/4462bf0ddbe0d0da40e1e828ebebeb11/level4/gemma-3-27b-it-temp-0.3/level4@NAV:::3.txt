Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) define a distribution over a fixed ground set, favoring diverse subsets. These are parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix, L; however, full learning of L is computationally intractable, leading prior work to focus on partial learning or restrictive parameterizations. Existing approaches that learn a scalar vector for L sacrifice diversity, while those restricting L’s form limit flexibility. This paper introduces a novel learning method for L without such constraints.

A key advantage of this method is the avoidance of projection steps – common in gradient-based approaches – which can degrade the DPP’s diversity properties. Instead, the authors leverage an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm operating on the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of L. This approach circumvents the need to project both eigenvalues *and* eigenvectors, a significant computational burden. By exploiting the full rank of the eigenvector matrix, they avoid eigenvector projection specifically. Jensen’s inequality is effectively employed to lower-bound the objective function, facilitating the EM procedure’s construction.

This learning method is valuable because it explicitly preserves the diversity inherent in DPPs, a focus often lacking in previous work that prioritizes only the quality of sampled sets. The detailed, step-by-step derivation of the EM algorithm is particularly insightful. Furthermore, mapping the constraints to an optimization problem on the Stiefel manifold to avoid eigenvector projection, and demonstrating that the resulting inverse distribution remains a DPP, represent substantial contributions. The method is evaluated on both synthetic data and a product recommendation task, with experimental results validating the claim that it maintains both quality and diversity – demonstrated by the limited replacement of relevant products by less likely candidates when compared to a Kernel Alignment (KA) baseline. While the conclusion could more fully highlight the paper’s contributions, the overall presentation is cohesive, the proofs are rigorous, and the reduction of the optimization problem to an EM framework is compelling. 

The paper is well-written and logically structured, with clear and thorough proofs. The detailed explanation of the EM reduction is particularly noteworthy. Expanding the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of real-world datasets would further demonstrate the practical benefits of the algorithm’s diversity-preserving properties compared to existing methods.
