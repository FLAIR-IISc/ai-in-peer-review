This paper addresses the problem of learning kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The primary contribution is a reformulation of the learning objective that outperforms the standard approach of differentiating the log-probability with respect to kernel parameters and performing projected gradient ascent—an approach the authors term K-Ascent (KA). Their key idea is to recast the problem within an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework. This is motivated by the observation that any DPP can be represented as a mixture of “elementary DPPs,” with one elementary DPP corresponding to each possible subset of elements. The algorithm instantiates a variational distribution over these subsets, represented as a k-DPP, and alternates between updating this distribution (the E-step) and taking a gradient step in the M-step. The results demonstrate that the EM method consistently outperforms KA when initialized naively, and provides a modest improvement even with more informed KA initialization.

Overall, I find this approach interesting, and the EM method appears to offer a tangible improvement in performance. While the M-step is computationally complex and requires several caveats to function correctly, achieving any result under these conditions is noteworthy.

Experimentally, I would appreciate a more detailed analysis of the impact of initialization on the EM algorithm’s performance. Specifically, exploring whether initializing EM with the solution obtained from KA leads to further improvements would be valuable.

I am also puzzled by the use of relative log likelihood as the reported evaluation metric. Differences in log probabilities directly correspond to ratios of probabilities. Why report ratios of log probabilities instead? Differences in log probabilities would be more interpretable. I struggle to understand the meaning of the presented numbers. 

Finally, a minor comment (and apologies if this comes across as overly critical): I found some aspects of the writing style distracting. I suggest the authors refrain from describing their own algorithm as “elegant,” particularly given the caveats and approximations necessary within the M-step. Additionally, the bolding in the introduction seems unnecessary. This is an interesting new approach to learning kernels for DPPs. While the experiments could be refined and the performance gains over the basic projected gradient algorithm aren’t substantial, it is a solid contribution overall.
