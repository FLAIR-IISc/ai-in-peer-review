### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from data. Learning the kernel via maximum likelihood is a non-convex problem, conjectured to be NP-hard. Previous work has circumvented this difficulty by focusing on more restricted, convex learning settings, such as learning weights for kernel rows or for a linear combination of fixed kernels.

The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to learn the full kernel matrix. The key idea is to reparameterize the kernel by its eigendecomposition (eigenvalues and eigenvectors). By viewing the selection of a subset of eigenvectors as a latent variable, the authors derive a lower bound on the log-likelihood using Jensen's inequality. The algorithm then alternates between an E-step, which computes the posterior distribution over this latent variable, and an M-step, which updates the eigenvalues and eigenvectors to maximize the lower bound. The eigenvector update is handled elegantly via optimization on the Stiefel manifold.

Experiments on a real-world product recommendation dataset show that the proposed EM algorithm achieves significant gains in test log-likelihood (up to 16.5%) compared to a baseline of projected gradient ascent on the kernel matrix entries. The EM method is also shown to be more robust, especially when initialization is poor or data is scarce, and is asymptotically faster.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a central and difficult problem in the practical application of DPPs: learning the full kernel. The proposed EM-based approach is novel, combining several non-trivial components (eigendecomposition, latent variable formulation, Stiefel manifold optimization) into a coherent and effective algorithm. This represents a solid advance and a valuable contribution to the literature, potentially making DPPs more accessible and powerful for a wider range of applications.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the EM algorithm appears to be well-founded. The use of the eigendecomposition to introduce a hidden variable that facilitates a lower-bound optimization is clever and theoretically grounded. The method avoids the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent, providing a clear advantage that is well-motivated in the paper and supported by the experiments.

3.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, though focused on a single task, clearly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method. The comparison against projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) effectively highlights the robustness and superior performance of the EM algorithm, particularly in challenging settings (e.g., with a naive initialization or limited training data). The analysis of runtime also confirms the claimed asymptotic advantages.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is not always easy to follow. The write-up is structured as a narrative of the derivation process, with discussions of potential issues and alternative approaches interwoven with the main thread. This makes it challenging for the reader to get a clear, high-level picture of the final algorithm. A more direct presentation—stating the final method first and then detailing its derivation—could significantly improve readability. Several notational issues also hinder clarity (see details below).

2.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** The empirical evaluation is focused on comparing the proposed EM method to only one baseline (K-Ascent). While this is a reasonable and important comparison, the paper would be much stronger if it included comparisons to other, non-DPP methods for diverse subset selection (e.g., methods mentioned in the introduction). This would provide a clearer picture of how much is gained by using a DPP with this learning approach versus simpler alternatives for the product recommendation task.

### Detailed Comments for Improvement

The paper is promising, but its presentation could be substantially improved. Here are some specific suggestions:

*   **Notation and Definitions:** Several terms are used before being formally defined. For instance, the marginal kernel `K` is introduced on p. 2, line 81, but its properties and relationship to `L` are not fully explained until later. Similarly, the eigenvector matrix `V` is used in Equation (1) without a prior definition. Ensuring all notation is defined upon first use would help the reader.

*   **Clarity of Explanations:**
    *   On p. 3, the description of the projection step for K-Ascent could be more precise. Instead of the current phrasing, consider stating that the projection "chooses the closest PSD matrix to Q in terms of Frobenius norm."
    *   The M-step update for the eigenvectors (Section 3.5) relies on optimization over a Stiefel manifold. While citing [26] is appropriate, the paper would be more self-contained and accessible if it briefly outlined the simplified update rule used (Eq. 21) and the rationale behind it, rather than requiring the reader to consult the original, more complex method.
    *   In the discussion of Equation (7) on p. 4, it would be helpful to explicitly state that the "mixture weight" for a given set of eigenvectors `J` corresponds to the product term `∏_{j∈J} λ_j ∏_{j∉J} (1− λ_j)`.

*   **Rationale for Initialization:** The moment-matching initialization in Equation (23) is an interesting heuristic. The paper would benefit from a short discussion on the rationale behind this specific form. For example, why is the empirical covariance matrix not used directly as an initializer? A clearer motivation for the chosen formula would be valuable.
