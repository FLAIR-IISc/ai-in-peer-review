

Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) are distributions over a fixed ground set that assign higher probability to diverse subsets. DPPs are parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix \(L\). Since learning \(L\) is NP-hard, prior work has focused on partial learning of \(L\). Learning a scalar vector for \(L\) compromises the diversity property of the DPP. Previous approaches have resorted to restricting the parametric form of \(L\). In this paper, the authors propose a learning method that does not restrict the parameterization of \(L\). Unlike gradient ascent, which requires projection steps that can compromise both the similarity property and diversity, their method avoids such projections. They employ Expectation-Maximization (EM) over eigenvalues and eigenvectors to overcome this limitation, exploring optimization algorithms that eliminate the need for projecting eigenvalues or eigenvectors. By exploiting the full-rank property of \(V\), they avoid projecting eigenvectors. Jensen’s inequality is employed to derive a lower bound on the objective function, enabling the construction of the EM procedure.  

The method is valuable because it preserves the diversity property of the DPP, contrasting with prior work that focused solely on quality. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is insightful. Key contributions include mapping the constraint to optimization over the Stiefel manifold (eliminating eigenvector projection) and demonstrating that the inverse distribution remains a DPP. The authors validate their algorithm on synthetic datasets and a product recommendation task. Experimental results support the paper’s claims: the method retains both quality and diversity of the DPP, as evidenced by comparison with KA. While KA replaces only two of the top 10 products with less likely alternatives, this sufficiently demonstrates the paper’s contribution. The conclusion is somewhat premature, as it does not fully articulate all contributions, though the paper is otherwise well-written and cohesive. The proofs are clear, and the step-by-step reduction from optimization to EM is interesting and well-explained.  

The paper is coherently written with well-structured proofs. The step-by-step reduction to EM is particularly engaging. Additional experiments on real-world datasets would better illustrate the practical importance of the diversity produced by this algorithm compared to existing methods in the field.
