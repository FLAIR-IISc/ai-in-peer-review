The paper "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes" presents a novel algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP), a probabilistic model that assigns higher probability to diverse sets. Unlike previous work, the proposed method does not restrict the parameterization of the kernel matrix, allowing for the preservation of both the quality and diversity properties of the DPP.

The authors begin by summarizing the challenges of learning DPPs, which involve maximizing the log-likelihood of the available data. They note that the log-likelihood is non-convex in the entries of the kernel matrix, making the learning problem NP-hard. To overcome this, the authors propose an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that exploits the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix.

The EM algorithm is derived by introducing a hidden variable and applying Jensen's inequality to lower-bound the likelihood objective. The authors then develop a coordinate ascent procedure to optimize the lower-bound, which alternates between an "expectation" step and a "maximization" step. The expectation step is solved by setting the auxiliary distribution to the conditional DPP, while the maximization step is solved by updating the eigenvalues and eigenvectors.

The authors also propose a novel initialization method that uses moment matching to provide a good starting point for the EM algorithm. They evaluate their method on a product recommendation task, comparing it to a baseline method called K-Ascent (KA). The results show that the EM algorithm outperforms KA in terms of test log-likelihood, with an average gain of 3.7% and a maximum gain of 16.5% in one category.

The paper makes several solid contributions, including the derivation of the EM procedure, the mapping of the constraint to an optimization over the Stiefel manifold, and the demonstration that the inverse distribution is a DPP. The experiments are well-designed and support the claim that the learning method retains the quality and diversity of the DPP.

However, the conclusion could be more comprehensive, highlighting all the contributions of the paper. Additionally, more experiments on real-world datasets would be beneficial in illustrating the importance of the "diversity" produced by this algorithm when compared to others in this area.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proofs are clear and concise. The step-by-step reduction to EM is interesting and well-explained. The paper demonstrates a good understanding of the challenges and limitations of learning DPPs and provides a novel solution that addresses these challenges.
