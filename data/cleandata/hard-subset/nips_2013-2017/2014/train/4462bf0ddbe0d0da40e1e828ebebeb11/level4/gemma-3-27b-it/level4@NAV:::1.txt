This paper investigates learning kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The primary contribution is a novel objective reformulation that surpasses the conventional method of differentiating the log-likelihood with respect to kernel parameters and performing projected gradient ascent – an approach the authors term K-Ascent (KA). The proposed method casts the problem within an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework, leveraging the representation of any DPP as a mixture of “elementary DPPs,” each corresponding to a unique subset of elements. The algorithm alternates between updating a variational distribution over these subsets (approximated as a k-DPP) and performing a gradient update in the M-step. The results demonstrate that the EM method consistently outperforms KA when initialized simply, and achieves modest improvements even with more informed KA initializations.

I consider this approach to be a worthwhile contribution, as the EM formulation appears to provide a demonstrable performance gain. While the M-step is complex and requires certain conditions for practical implementation, achieving a functional solution is a significant result. 

Regarding the experimental evaluation, I would be interested in seeing a more thorough analysis of the impact of initialization on the EM algorithm. Specifically, investigating whether initializing EM with the solution obtained from KA could yield further improvements would be valuable. 

I also have a question regarding the reported evaluation metric: the use of relative log-likelihood is unclear. Given that log-likelihood differences directly correspond to probability ratios, reporting ratios *of* log-likelihoods is not intuitive. Reporting differences in log-probabilities would likely be more interpretable. 

Finally, while minor, I found certain stylistic choices distracting. Self-referential descriptions of the algorithm as “elegant” seem unwarranted, particularly considering the approximations and caveats inherent in the M-step. Additionally, the extensive use of bold text in the introduction appears unnecessary. In summary, this is an interesting and novel approach to kernel learning for DPPs. The experiments could be strengthened, and the performance gains over the baseline algorithm are not substantial, but overall this is a solid paper.
