This paper addresses the problem of learning kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The core contribution is a novel reformulation of the learning objective using an expectation-maximization (EM) framework, which outperforms the standard approach of maximizing likelihood via projected gradient ascent (referred to as K-Ascent or KA). The key idea leverages the representation of a DPP as a mixture of “elementary DPPs,” introducing a hidden variable representing subsets of elements. The algorithm alternates between updating a variational distribution over these subsets (represented as a k-DPP) and performing gradient steps in the M-step to update the kernel parameters. The experimental results demonstrate that the EM method consistently outperforms KA, particularly when KA is initialized naively, with a modest improvement even with more informed initialization.

Overall, I find this approach to be interesting and the EM method yields a demonstrable improvement. The M-step is complex, and the necessary approximations and caveats are somewhat limiting, but achieving a working solution is a significant accomplishment. 

Experimentally, I would appreciate a more thorough analysis of the impact of initialization on the EM algorithm. Specifically, evaluating the performance of EM when initialized with the result of KA would be insightful. 

I also have a question regarding the evaluation metric. The use of *relative* log-likelihood is unclear. Log-likelihood differences directly reflect probability ratios; reporting ratios of log-likelihoods is less intuitive. Differences in log-probabilities would be more readily interpretable. 

Finally, a minor stylistic note: I suggest the authors avoid self-aggrandizing descriptions such as calling their algorithm “elegant,” especially given the approximations and caveats required in the M-step. The bolding of text in the introduction also appears unnecessary.

In conclusion, this is a promising new approach for learning kernels in DPPs. While the experimental improvements over the basic projected gradient algorithm aren't substantial, and some aspects of the method require approximation, it is a solid paper that makes a worthwhile contribution to the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper proposes a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem (learning DPP kernels).
*   The EM framework provides a viable alternative to existing methods, demonstrating consistent performance improvements.
*   The theoretical justification for the approach is clear and well-explained.
*   The experiments are well-designed and provide evidence of the method's effectiveness.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The performance gains, while consistent, are not overwhelmingly large.
*   The M-step involves approximations and caveats that slightly diminish the elegance of the solution.
*   The experimental analysis could be strengthened by exploring the impact of different initialization strategies, specifically using KA’s output to initialize EM.
*   The evaluation metric (relative log-likelihood) is somewhat unconventional and difficult to interpret.
