### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from data. Learning a DPP kernel by direct maximization of the log-likelihood is a non-convex optimization problem, conjectured to be NP-hard. Consequently, prior work has focused on more restricted, convex learning settings, such as learning weights for rows of a fixed kernel or for a linear combination of fixed kernels.

The main contribution of this work is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) style algorithm for this learning problem. The key insight is to reparameterize the kernel matrix `K` in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The authors leverage the known property that any DPP can be viewed as a mixture of "elementary DPPs," where each elementary DPP corresponds to a subset of the eigenvectors. This formulation naturally introduces a latent variable—the chosen subset of eigenvectors `J`—for each observed data point.

The proposed algorithm then proceeds by lower-bounding the log-likelihood using Jensen's inequality, introducing an auxiliary distribution `q(J | Y)` over the latent variables. The algorithm alternates between an E-step, which updates this distribution (shown to be a conditional k-DPP), and an M-step, which updates the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the kernel `K`. The eigenvalue update has a closed-form solution, while the eigenvector update is performed via a single gradient step on the Stiefel manifold.

Experiments on a real-world product recommendation task show that the proposed EM algorithm outperforms a baseline of projected gradient ascent on the kernel entries (termed K-Ascent). The improvement is particularly significant when the baseline is poorly initialized or when training data is limited, demonstrating the robustness of the EM approach. Furthermore, the EM algorithm is shown to be asymptotically and empirically faster than the baseline.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel and principled method for a difficult and important problem. Unconstrained learning of the full DPP kernel is a significant step beyond the more limited learning settings explored in prior work. This contribution substantially advances the state of the art in learning DPPs and is likely to be influential for researchers and practitioners using these models.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The reformulation of the learning problem into an EM framework is highly creative. The derivation is technically sound, and the connection between the latent variable `J` and the mixture of elementary DPPs is elegant. A key technical strength is that the algorithm avoids the explicit projection step required by the gradient ascent baseline. The paper correctly identifies that this projection is a major weakness of the baseline, as it can prematurely truncate eigenvalues and lead to poor, near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture diversity. The proposed M-step updates cleverly sidestep this issue.

3.  **Clarity and Empirical Validation:** The paper is well-written and the algorithm is clearly explained. The experiments effectively demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed EM method. The results convincingly show that EM is more robust to initialization and more efficient than the K-Ascent baseline. The qualitative example of the baby registry recommendation provides a clear illustration of how the baseline's failure to model diversity results in a less sensible output.

**Weaknesses (Areas for improvement):**

1.  **Approximations in the M-step:** The M-step for the eigenvectors is not a full maximization, but rather a single gradient step. Furthermore, because the M-step objective is intractable, the line search for the step size is performed by evaluating the true log-likelihood `L` rather than the lower bound `F`. While these are reasonable practical choices, they represent a deviation from a standard EM procedure. A brief discussion acknowledging these approximations and their potential impact would improve the paper's transparency.

2.  **Clarity of Evaluation Metric:** The choice to report "relative test log-likelihood differences" is unconventional. A difference in log-likelihoods already corresponds to a log-ratio of probabilities, which is a standard and interpretable metric. Taking a ratio of log-likelihoods is less common and its interpretation is not immediately obvious. The authors should either justify this choice or, preferably, report absolute differences in test log-likelihood, which would be clearer.

3.  **Potential for Stronger Experimental Analysis:** The experimental comparison is good, but could be made even more compelling. For instance, to further demonstrate that the EM algorithm finds better local optima, an experiment could be run where the EM algorithm is initialized with the converged solution from K-Ascent. If EM is able to further improve upon this solution, it would provide very strong evidence for its superiority in navigating the non-convex landscape.

### **Detailed Comments**

*   The authors make a strong case for why the projection step in the K-Ascent baseline is problematic. The proposed EM algorithm's ability to avoid this is a very compelling advantage.
*   The runtime analysis and empirical timing results are a valuable addition, highlighting a practical advantage of the EM algorithm that makes it more appealing for real-world applications.
*   A minor point on presentation: while the work is strong, the authors might consider adopting a more neutral scientific tone to allow the excellent technical contributions and results to speak for themselves.

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and effective algorithm for a significant problem in the field. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and do not detract from the core contribution.
