

The paper introduces an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a significant limitation in prior work that has focused on restricted convex learning settings. The authors make a novel contribution by parameterizing the kernel in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors, leveraging the eigendecomposition to develop an effective optimization procedure that avoids the problematic projection steps required in previous gradient-based approaches. This work builds on the foundational NIPS contributions by Kulesza and Taskar (2012) who established DPPs as powerful models for subset selection, and extends the line of work on DPP learning initiated by Affandi et al. (2013) and Gillenwater et al. (2013). While prior work has explored learning scalar weights for DPP rows (Kulesza and Taskar, 2011) or linear combinations of DPPs with fixed kernels (Affandi et al., 2013), this paper provides the first method for learning the full kernel matrix without restrictive assumptions.

Quality: The proposed EM algorithm is technically sound and well-motivated. The authors provide a rigorous derivation of the algorithm by reformulating the likelihood optimization problem using the eigendecomposition of the kernel and applying Jensen's inequality to create a tractable lower bound. The asymptotic runtime analysis demonstrates that the EM approach is theoretically superior to projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent), and the experimental results on product recommendation tasks confirm this advantage, showing up to 16.5% relative gains in test log-likelihood. However, the evaluation could be strengthened by comparing against alternative subset selection methods beyond DPP-based approaches (e.g., submodular maximization techniques mentioned in the introduction), which would better establish the practical significance of the DPP framework for this application.

Clarity: The paper would benefit from improved organization and notation. The presentation of the method is somewhat convoluted, with the authors interleaving discussion of alternative approaches and potential problems with the main development of their algorithm. Key definitions appear after their first use (e.g., marginal kernel K on p.2, line 81; V on p.2, line 100), and the description of the Stiefel manifold update in Section 3.5 would be clearer if the authors directly outlined their simplified approach rather than referring readers to [26]. The phrase "minimises the change to Frobenius norm" (p.3, line 159) is imprecise; it would be better to state "chooses the closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix to Q" as suggested in the guidelines. The writing is occasionally unnecessarily complex, and a more streamlined presentation of the core algorithm would significantly improve readability.

Originality: The paper presents a novel approach to DPP learning that appears to be original to the best of my knowledge. The combination of eigendecomposition, EM optimization, and the avoidance of projection steps represents a significant departure from prior work that has either restricted the learning problem to convex settings or required fixed kernel matrices. While Kulesza and Taskar (2012) established DPPs as a modeling tool, and Affandi et al. (2013) explored Bayesian approaches to DPP learning, this work provides the first method for learning the full kernel matrix without restrictive parameterizations, making it a meaningful contribution to the NIPS literature on DPPs.

Significance: The method presented is a solid advance in DPP learning and offers practical benefits for applications requiring subset selection with diversity constraints, such as product recommendation. The demonstrated improvements in both log-likelihood and runtime over the baseline K-Ascent method (2.1x faster on average) are meaningful, and the approach is robust to initialization changes. The results suggest that the EM algorithm can effectively model the negative interactions between items that are crucial for the diversity-seeking behavior of DPPs, which the K-Ascent method often fails to capture due to its projection steps. While the experimental evaluation is limited to a single application domain, the method's general applicability to other DPP applications (e.g., document summarization, sensor placement) makes it a valuable contribution to the subset selection literature.
