This paper addresses the problem of learning kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The main contribution is a novel reformulation of the learning objective, which is shown to outperform the standard baseline of performing projected gradient ascent on the log-likelihood with respect to the kernel parameters (referred to as K-Ascent or KA).

The proposed method frames the problem using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. This is enabled by the observation that a DPP can be viewed as a mixture of 'elementary DPPs,' each corresponding to a specific subset of items. The algorithm introduces a variational distribution over these subsets and then alternates between an E-step, which updates this distribution (represented as a k-DPP), and an M-step, which performs a gradient update on the kernel parameters. The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed EM method significantly outperforms KA with a naive initialization. When KA is initialized more carefully, the proposed method still shows a modest improvement in some settings.

The proposed EM-based approach is interesting and appears to yield tangible improvements over the baseline. The formulation is novel, and the authors should be commended for developing a workable algorithm, especially given the complexity of the M-step. However, the M-step's reliance on specific approximations and caveats is a notable drawback that somewhat detracts from the method's elegance.

The experimental analysis could be strengthened by a more thorough investigation of the EM algorithm's sensitivity to initialization. For instance, it would be highly informative to see if the EM algorithm can further refine a solution that has been pre-trained using the standard KA method. This would help clarify whether the EM framework is finding a better local optimum or simply has a more robust optimization path.

I found the choice of "relative log-likelihood" as an evaluation metric confusing. The difference in log-likelihoods has a clear probabilistic interpretation (the log of the ratio of probabilities), but the ratio of log-likelihoods is difficult to interpret. The authors should clarify the motivation for this metric or, preferably, report the standard difference in log-likelihoods to facilitate easier comparison and interpretation of the results.

On a minor note concerning presentation, the manuscript could be improved by adopting a more objective tone. For example, characterizing the proposed algorithm as "elegant" seems subjective, particularly given the approximations required in the M-step. Additionally, the use of boldface text in the introduction for emphasis is unconventional and could be removed for a more professional presentation.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and interesting EM-based approach for learning DPP kernels. While the empirical improvements over a well-initialized baseline are not always substantial and the experimental analysis could be expanded, the proposed method is a valuable contribution to the literature. Overall, this is a solid paper.
