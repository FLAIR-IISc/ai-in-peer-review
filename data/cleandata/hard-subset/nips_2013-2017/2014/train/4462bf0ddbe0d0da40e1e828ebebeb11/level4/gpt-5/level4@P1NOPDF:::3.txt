Determinantal point processes (DPPs) are distributions over a fixed ground set that assign higher probability to diverse subsets. A DPP can be parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix L. Since learning L is NP-hard, prior work has focused on partial learning or on restricting the parameterization of L. In particular, learning only a scalar vector for L compromises the diversity dimension of the DPP. Previous approaches therefore often restrict the form of L.

This paper proposes a learning method that does not restrict the parameterization of L. Unlike projected gradient ascent, the method avoids projection steps that can distort the similarity structure and thereby harm diversity. By using an EM procedure over the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of L, the authors overcome this disadvantage and explore optimization algorithms that do not require projecting values. Whereas projected gradient ascent would require projection of both eigenvalues and eigenvectors, the authors exploit the fact that V is full-rank to avoid projecting the eigenvectors. They apply Jensen’s inequality to lower bound the objective and derive the EM updates.

The method is valuable because it explicitly preserves the diversity property of DPPs, in contrast to much of the prior work that primarily targets the “quality” aspect. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is clear and insightful. Mapping the constraint to an optimization over the Stiefel manifold to eliminate eigenvector projection, and proving that the inverse distribution is a DPP, are solid technical contributions.

The authors evaluate their algorithm on synthetic data and a product recommendation task. The experiments support the central claim: the method retains both quality and diversity, as demonstrated in comparison with KA. In the top-10 product recommendations, KA replaces only two items with less likely products; this difference still highlights the contribution and supports the paper’s initial claims. The experimental section could be strengthened with additional real-world datasets to more fully illustrate the diversity advantages over competing methods.

Overall, the paper is coherent, and the proofs are well written. The reduction from the constrained optimization problem to an EM procedure is particularly well explained. The conclusion feels somewhat hasty and does not fully summarize all contributions, but the work is technically sound and makes a meaningful advance in learning unrestricted DPP parameters while preserving diversity.
