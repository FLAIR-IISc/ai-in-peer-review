

This paper addresses kernel matrix learning for determinantal point processes (DPPs), proposing a reformulation of the objective within an EM framework. The key contribution is a method that outperforms the standard K-Ascent (KA) baseline—where kernel parameters are optimized via gradient ascent on the log-probability with projection—particularly when KA initialization is random. The authors observe that any DPP can be expressed as a mixture over "elementary DPPs" (one per subset of elements), then introduce a variational distribution over subsets represented as a k-DPP. The algorithm alternates between updating this variational distribution and taking single gradient steps in the M-step. Results demonstrate significant improvements over naive KA initialization, with modest gains when KA uses better initialization.  

The approach is conceptually interesting and the EM-based method does yield consistent improvements. However, the M-step involves nontrivial approximations and caveats, which somewhat undermine the method's elegance. It is noteworthy that the authors claim their algorithm is "elegant," yet the required approximations and caveats make this characterization debatable.  

Experimentally, the paper would benefit from deeper analysis of initialization effects. Specifically, it would be valuable to test whether initializing EM with KA's solution further improves performance. The current experiments focus on KA with random vs. intelligent initialization but do not explore EM's behavior when seeded by KA.  

The use of relative log-likelihood as a performance metric is problematic. Log-probability differences directly reflect relative likelihoods (i.e., ratios of probabilities), so reporting ratios of log-probabilities is counterintuitive and difficult to interpret. For instance, a relative log-likelihood of 0.1 does not convey the same meaning as a difference of 0.1 in log-probabilities. The authors should clarify why this metric was chosen over standard log-probability differences.  

Stylistically, the paper contains minor issues that detract from readability. The authors' self-description of the method as "elegant" is unnecessary and misleading given the M-step's complexities. Additionally, bolding key terms in the introduction is distracting and unprofessional.  

Overall, the paper presents a novel and useful approach to kernel learning for DPPs. While the improvements over KA are not transformative, they are consistent and meaningful, particularly under practical initialization scenarios. The method's implementation is sound, and the core idea is well-motivated. The experimental section could be strengthened by addressing the initialization and metric concerns raised above. The paper is a solid contribution to the field, though the claims of elegance and the metric choice warrant revision.
