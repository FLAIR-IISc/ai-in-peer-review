

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), which has been largely overlooked due to the NP-hardness of the maximum likelihood estimation problem. While previous work has focused on restricted learning settings—such as learning scalar weights for each row of the kernel matrix [2] or learning weights for linear combinations of DPPs with fixed kernels [3]—this paper proposes a novel EM-based approach that directly optimizes the full kernel matrix without restrictive parameterizations.

The authors make a significant contribution by recognizing that the kernel matrix's eigendecomposition provides a natural way to formulate an EM optimization procedure. By reparameterizing from kernel entries to eigenvalues and eigenvectors, and using Jensen's inequality to construct a lower bound on the likelihood, they develop an algorithm that avoids the problematic projection steps required by gradient ascent methods. The paper demonstrates that gradient ascent (K-Ascent) often results in near-diagonal kernel matrices that fail to capture the negative interactions between items, which are crucial for DPPs' diversity properties. The authors show that their EM approach overcomes this limitation, achieving up to 16.5% relative gains in test log-likelihood on a real-world product recommendation task.

Regarding quality, the paper provides a technically sound approach with rigorous theoretical derivations. The authors clearly show how their method avoids the projection issues inherent in gradient ascent by exploiting the eigendecomposition and Stiefel manifold optimization. The experimental results are comprehensive, comparing their method against a strong baseline (K-Ascent) on 13 product categories from Amazon baby registries. The analysis of the relationship between the off-diagonal components of the kernel matrix and the relative gains of their method provides strong empirical support for their theoretical claims.

The paper excels in clarity. The step-by-step derivation of the EM algorithm is exceptionally well-organized, with clear explanations of each component (E-step, M-step, and the necessary approximations). The connection between the eigendecomposition and the DPP's generative process is particularly well-explained. The authors provide sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce their results, including specific implementation details about the Stiefel manifold optimization and the use of k-DPPs in the E-step.

The originality of this work is substantial. While DPPs have been widely used in various applications [8, 11-20], the learning problem for the full kernel matrix has remained largely unexplored due to its non-convexity. The authors' key insight—using the eigendecomposition to formulate an EM procedure—represents a novel approach that significantly advances the state of the art. This work clearly differentiates itself from prior NIPS papers on DPP learning [1, 2, 3, 8, 11-20] by addressing the full kernel learning problem without restrictive parameterizations.

The significance of this work is demonstrated through the experiments on product recommendation, where preserving diversity is crucial. The paper convincingly shows that their method maintains the diversity property of DPPs that is lost in previous approaches. The 16.5% relative gain in test log-likelihood represents a meaningful improvement that would be valuable to practitioners. The authors' analysis of how the method performs better when negative item interactions are strong (as measured by the d metric) provides additional insight into the conditions where their approach is most beneficial.

Strengths:
- Novel EM-based approach to full kernel learning that avoids projection issues
- Theoretical insight into why gradient ascent fails (near-diagonal kernels)
- Comprehensive experimental validation on a real-world dataset
- Clear and well-structured derivation of the algorithm
- Demonstrated improvements in both test log-likelihood and diversity preservation

Weaknesses:
- The conclusion could more explicitly highlight the key contributions
- The analysis of runtime complexity could be more detailed
- While the product recommendation experiment is compelling, additional experiments on other applications would further strengthen the significance claim

The paper is well-written, with proofs that are clear and well-motivated. The step-by-step reduction of the optimization problem to the EM procedure is particularly insightful. The authors effectively demonstrate how their method preserves the diversity property of DPPs, which previous approaches had neglected.

Pro acceptance arguments: 
- Addresses a significant open problem in DPP learning
- Provides a theoretically sound and practically effective solution
- Demonstrates meaningful improvements over existing methods
- Well-motivated, clearly written, and technically sound
- Strong experimental validation on a real-world task

Con acceptance arguments:
- Conclusion could better summarize the paper's contributions
- Additional experiments on other applications would strengthen significance claims

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of DPP learning by providing a novel EM-based method that effectively learns the full kernel matrix without restrictive parameterizations. The authors demonstrate that their approach preserves the diversity property of DPPs that is compromised in previous gradient-based methods, achieving significant improvements in real-world product recommendation tasks. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a meaningful problem in the machine learning community. I recommend acceptance.
