

This paper presents a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a significant limitation in previous work. Prior approaches have focused on restricted learning settings: [2] learned scalar weights for each row of the kernel matrix, while [3] learned weights for linear combinations of DPPs with fixed kernels. The authors correctly note that learning the full kernel matrix is conjectured to be NP-hard [1], and previous attempts to maximize likelihood via projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) require problematic projection steps that often produce degenerate, near-diagonal kernels that fail to model diversity. By leveraging the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K, the authors develop an EM-style optimization procedure that avoids these projection issues and provides a more robust learning method.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. The authors successfully transform the learning problem by reparameterizing from kernel entries to eigenvalues and eigenvectors, then lower-bound the log-likelihood using Jensen's inequality. The derivation of the EM algorithm is mathematically rigorous, and the theoretical analysis of computational complexity (showing the EM algorithm is asymptotically faster than gradient ascent) is compelling. However, the experimental evaluation is limited. The paper only compares against K-Ascent (the naive projected gradient approach), not against alternative subset selection methods mentioned in the introduction (e.g., those in [4-9]). This omission makes it difficult to assess the practical value of DPPs versus other approaches for the product recommendation task. The paper would benefit from additional experiments comparing DPPs to non-DPP methods for subset selection.

**Clarity:** The paper suffers from significant clarity issues that impede understanding. Key notations (e.g., the marginal kernel K, eigenvector matrix V) are introduced after first use, and the writing is unnecessarily convoluted by interleaving discussions of alternative approaches with the main development. For example, Section 3.2's explanation of DPPs as mixtures of elementary DPPs is important but obscured by the presentation. The authors should present the developed method clearly first, then discuss alternatives and limitations in a dedicated section. The Stiefel manifold update (Equation 21) is referenced without sufficient explanation, requiring readers to consult [26] to understand the non-trivial optimization technique. A brief explanation of the simplified update would greatly improve accessibility.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original. The authors' approach of using eigendecomposition to develop an EM algorithm for full kernel learning represents a novel contribution to the DPP literature. While Kulesza and Taskar's work [2, 14] has extensively studied DPPs, this paper addresses a fundamental gap in the learning framework that has not been previously solved. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by not assuming fixed kernel values or restrictive parameterizations, which is a significant advancement over [2, 3, 8].

**Significance:** The method represents a meaningful technical advance for DPP applications requiring full kernel learning. The empirical results on product recommendation demonstrate tangible improvements (up to 16.5% relative gains in test log-likelihood) over the baseline K-Ascent method. The paper's claim that the EM approach is more robust to initialization and dataset changes is well-supported by the experiments. For applications where modeling negative interactions between items is critical (e.g., product recommendation, document summarization), this method provides a practical solution to a previously intractable learning problem. However, the significance would be stronger with comparisons to non-DPP subset selection methods.

**Pro and Con Acceptance:**
- *Pro:* Novel approach to a fundamental problem in DPP learning; technically sound with clear theoretical advantages; demonstrated improvements in empirical results; addresses a significant limitation in the literature.
- *Con:* Limited experimental evaluation (only compared to K-Ascent, not other subset selection methods); unclear writing and notation issues that hinder comprehension; lacks comparison to non-DPP approaches for the same task.

**Specific Comments:**
- On page 2, line 81, the marginal kernel K is used before definition. The paper should define K when first introduced.
- On page 2, line 100, the matrix V is referenced without prior definition.
- On page 3, line 159, the phrase "minimises the change to Frobenius norm" is unclear. The authors should state "chooses the closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix to Q" as suggested in the draft.
- The explanation of the eigenvector update (Equation 21) would be clearer if the authors briefly outlined their simplified approach rather than simply citing [26].
- On page 7, line 324, the rationale for using the moment-matching initialization (Equation 23) is not fully explained. Why not simply use the empirical covariance matrix as the kernel?

**Overall Recommendation:** This paper presents an important technical contribution to DPP learning that addresses a fundamental limitation in the literature. The method is well-founded and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over the naive gradient approach. While the writing requires significant improvement to enhance clarity, and the experimental evaluation would benefit from broader comparisons, the core technical contribution is strong and advances the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and expand the experimental evaluation to include comparisons with non-DPP subset selection methods.
