Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) are distributions over a fixed ground set that assign higher probability to diverse subsets. A DPP can be parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix L. Because learning L is NP-hard, prior work typically performs only partial learning, often by restricting L to a scalar-weighted form, which compromises diversity, or by imposing strong parametric constraints on L.

This paper proposes a learning method that does not restrict the parameterization of L. Unlike projected gradient ascent—which requires projection steps that can disrupt the similarity structure and thereby harm diversity—the authors employ an EM approach on the eigenvalues and eigenvectors, avoiding these drawbacks. They explore optimization algorithms that eliminate the need for projection: whereas projected gradient ascent would require projecting both eigenvalues and eigenvectors, they exploit the fact that V is full rank to avoid projecting the eigenvectors. They use Jensen’s inequality to derive a lower bound on the objective and construct the EM procedure.

The method is valuable because it preserves the diversity property of DPPs, in contrast to previous approaches that focus primarily on quality. The step-by-step EM derivation is clear and insightful. Mapping the constraints to an optimization over the Stiefel manifold to eliminate eigenvector projection, and showing that the inverse distribution is also a DPP, are solid contributions. The authors evaluate on synthetic data and a product recommendation task. The experiments support their claims: the method retains both quality and diversity, as shown by comparisons with KA. Although in the top-10 recommendations KA replaces only two items with less likely products, this still highlights the paper’s contribution and is consistent with the initial claim. The conclusion is somewhat hasty and does not fully emphasize all contributions, but overall the paper is cohesive. The proofs are well written, and the step-by-step reduction from the original optimization to EM is interesting and well explained.

More experiments on real-world datasets would further demonstrate the practical importance of the diversity achieved by this algorithm relative to competing methods.
