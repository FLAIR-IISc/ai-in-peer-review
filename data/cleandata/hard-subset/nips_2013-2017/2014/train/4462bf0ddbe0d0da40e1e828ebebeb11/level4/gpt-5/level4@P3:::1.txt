Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses learning determinantal point process (DPP) kernels from observed subsets by maximizing likelihood. Prior work largely avoids learning a full kernel due to nonconvexity and an NP-hardness conjecture, instead opting for convex surrogates such as per-item reweighting of a fixed kernel [Kulesza & Taskar, UAI’11], mixtures/linear combinations of fixed DPPs [e.g., Lin & Bilmes, UAI’12], or Bayesian learning with parametric forms [Affandi et al., ICML’14]. The authors propose to learn the full marginal kernel K by reparameterizing via its eigendecomposition and formulating an EM procedure that leverages the representation of a DPP as a mixture of “elementary” DPPs (sets of eigenvectors). Introducing the latent index set J of selected eigenvectors, they derive a variational lower bound. The E-step yields a conditional k-DPP over J with a computable kernel; the M-step updates eigenvalues in closed form via k-DPP marginals and takes a Stiefel-manifold gradient step for eigenvectors. This approach avoids the projection step required by projected gradient ascent on K’s entries (KA), which can collapse eigenvalues to zero and stall off-diagonals. Empirically, on a large Amazon baby registry recommendation dataset, the EM method improves test log-likelihood relative to KA, notably under naive initialization, and is typically about 2x faster in wall-clock time.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The EM lower bound is standard and the latent-variable construction is well justified by the DPP mixture view. The E-step result (q as a conditional k-DPP) and the closed-form eigenvalue update are appealing and computationally efficient, relying on k-DPP marginalization. The eigenvector update is more heuristic: only a single Riemannian gradient step is taken per M-step, with step size chosen by checking improvement in the true likelihood after recomputing the E-step. This breaks the usual EM monotonic improvement guarantee, though in practice it seems to work and avoids degenerate projections.
- Empirical support: The experiments are careful about initialization, comparing a Wishart-based scheme and a moment-matching scheme, and show where EM helps most (categories with strong off-diagonals). That said, evaluation is on a single real-world domain with multiple categories; stronger baselines and additional datasets would help substantiate generality.
- Complexity claims: The runtime analysis is clear: O(T1 n N k^2 + T1 T2 N^3) for EM versus O(T1 n N^3 + T1 T2 N^3) for KA, with k = max set size. The empirical speedups are consistent with the analysis.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized and accessible, with helpful algorithm summaries. The presentation of the mixture view, the k-DPP E-step, and the complexity advantages is clear. Some stylistic choices could be toned down (self-assessments of elegance; boldface in the introduction). The reporting of “relative test log-likelihood” gains is hard to interpret; differences in average log-likelihood (e.g., per-example, per-item) would be clearer.

Originality:
Using the eigendecomposition and the DPP mixture representation to derive an EM procedure that learns the full kernel appears novel in the DPP learning literature. The combination of k-DPP marginals for eigenvalue updates and Stiefel-manifold steps for eigenvectors is a creative and practically motivated design.

Significance:
While average gains over a tuned KA baseline are modest, improvements are meaningful in regimes where modeling negative interactions (off-diagonals) matters, and the method appears more robust to initialization. The speed improvements and the avoidance of degeneracy make this a promising direction for practical DPP learning in recommendation, summarization, and sensor placement.

Pros:
- Conceptually neat EM formulation exploiting the DPP mixture over elementary DPPs.
- Avoids problematic PSD/eigenvalue projections that can collapse structure.
- Efficient eigenvalue updates via k-DPP marginals; sound complexity analysis.
- Demonstrated robustness and speed advantages; code release is valuable.

Cons:
- No monotone EM guarantee due to partial M-step; reliance on line search and repeated E-steps.
- Empirical evaluation limited to one domain; comparisons lack other learning baselines (e.g., per-item scaling, mixtures of fixed kernels, Bayesian approaches).
- Metric reporting (relative log-likelihood) is nonstandard and difficult to interpret.
- Sensitivity to initialization not fully characterized; unclear whether EM can improve a strong KA solution.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Initialization: If EM is initialized from a converged KA solution (both Wishart- and moment-matching-initialized KA), does it further improve test log-likelihood? Conversely, how often does EM escape poor local optima that trap KA?
- Monotonicity and stopping: Can you provide empirical evidence that the lower bound or the true likelihood increases in most M-steps? Would a few Riemannian gradient steps (instead of a single step) materially help without losing efficiency?
- Parameterization: To avoid λ→1 singularities, consider a logistic reparameterization λ = σ(θ) to ensure 0<λ<1 without ad hoc clipping. Did you try this, and if so, how did it affect stability?
- Baselines: Please compare against (a) per-item scaling of a similarity kernel (convex) and (b) mixtures/linear combinations of fixed DPPs, to situate improvements against the most common alternatives in practice. A Bayesian parametric baseline would also be informative if feasible.
- Metrics: Report absolute test log-likelihood differences (per example or per item), with standard errors across random initializations. If possible, include task-relevant metrics (e.g., MAP set quality via greedy MAP or recommendation coverage/diversity) to complement likelihood.
- Ablations: Analyze the effect of k (max set size) on runtime and accuracy; show an ablation on the number of eigenvector update steps; test more advanced PGD baselines (momentum, better line search) to ensure KA is competitive.
- Clarity: Minimize subjective language; avoid boldface. Briefly summarize the conditional k-DPP derivation in the main text (currently deferred), as it is central to practicality.

Overall recommendation:
A solid contribution with a novel and practically effective EM approach to full DPP kernel learning. The method is well motivated and technically sound, with demonstrated benefits in robustness and speed. I recommend acceptance, contingent on improved evaluation (baselines and metrics) and clarifications around initialization sensitivity and the partial M-step.
