A Determinantal Point Process (DPP) is a distribution over a fixed ground set that assigns higher probability to diverse subsets. DPPs can be parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix L. Since learning L exactly is NP-hard, prior work has primarily considered partial learning of L. Learning only a scalar weighting vector for L undermines the diversity dimension of DPPs, and previous approaches often restrict the parametric form of L. This paper proposes a learning method that does not constrain the parameterization of L. Unlike gradient-ascent methods that require projection steps—which can compromise similarity and thereby diversity—the proposed approach employs EM over eigenvalues and eigenvectors to avoid this drawback. The authors explore optimization algorithms that obviate the need for projections. While projected gradient ascent requires projecting both eigenvalues and eigenvectors, they leverage the full-rank property of V to avoid projecting eigenvectors. Jensen’s inequality is used to lower-bound the objective and to construct the EM procedure.

The learning method is valuable because it preserves the diversity property of DPPs, in contrast to prior work that has largely emphasized only quality. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is insightful. Mapping the constraint to an optimization over the Stiefel manifold to eliminate eigenvector projection, and showing that the inverse distribution is itself a DPP, are strong contributions. The authors evaluate the learning algorithm on synthetic datasets and a product recommendation task. The experiments support the paper’s claim—that the method retains both quality and diversity—via comparisons with KA. Although KA replaces only two of the top-10 products with less likely items, this still underscores the paper’s contribution and backs the initial claim. The conclusion is somewhat rushed and does not fully highlight all contributions, but overall the paper is cohesive. The proofs are clearly presented, and the step-by-step reduction of the optimization to EM is compelling.

The paper is coherent and the proofs are well written. The staged reduction to EM is clear and well explained. Additional experiments on real-world datasets would better illustrate the importance of the diversity produced by this algorithm relative to others in the area.
