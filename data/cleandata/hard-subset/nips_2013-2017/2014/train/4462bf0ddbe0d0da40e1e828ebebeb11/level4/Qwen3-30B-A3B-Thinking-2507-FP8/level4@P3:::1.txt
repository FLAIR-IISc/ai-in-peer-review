

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for determinantal point processes (DPPs) without restrictive assumptions, which has been a significant limitation in prior work. The authors propose an EM-based learning algorithm that reformulates the likelihood optimization problem by leveraging the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix. Previous approaches have largely focused on restricted learning settings: [2] learned a scalar weight per row of the kernel matrix, [3] learned weights for linear combinations of DPPs with fixed kernels, and [8] assumed a parametric form for the kernel. The authors correctly note that these approaches either fail to model item diversity (in the case of row-wise weights) or lose the attractive computational properties of DPPs (in the case of kernel ensembles).

The main contribution is a novel EM algorithm that avoids the problematic projection step required in naive gradient ascent approaches (K-Ascent). The key insight is to represent the DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs, where each elementary DPP corresponds to a subset of eigenvectors. This allows the authors to derive a lower bound on the log-likelihood using Jensen's inequality, which leads to an alternating optimization procedure between the E-step (computing a k-DPP distribution over subsets) and M-step (updating eigenvalues and eigenvectors). The authors provide detailed derivations of the M-step updates, including the efficient computation of eigenvalue updates using k-DPP marginalization algorithms.

The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed EM method on a product recommendation task using a baby registry dataset from Amazon. The results show that EM outperforms K-Ascent by up to 16.5% in test log-likelihood when using a naive Wishart initialization, but the gap narrows to about 0.4% when using a moments-matching initialization. The authors provide a thoughtful analysis of why the gap exists, linking it to the strength of negative item interactions (measured by d = 1/N ||M||F/||diag(M)||2), which aligns with their theoretical discussion of K-Ascent's projection issues.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the DPP learning problem by addressing the full kernel learning challenge without restrictive assumptions. This fills an important gap in the literature.
2. The theoretical development is thorough and well-motivated, with careful attention to the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm (O(T1nNk2 + T1T2N3) vs. K-Ascent's O(T1nN3 + T1T2N3)).
3. The experimental design is appropriate for the problem, with a real-world application that clearly demonstrates the practical value of the method.
4. The authors thoughtfully address the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding initialization sensitivity.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper does not sufficiently address how the EM algorithm might be initialized with the results of K-Ascent (as suggested in the draft review). This would provide a more complete comparison between the methods, as it would show whether EM can refine solutions from K-Ascent.
2. The use of relative log-likelihood differences as the primary metric is confusing. As the draft review notes, reporting ratios of log probabilities (e.g., "16.5% gain") is not standard practice. The authors should clarify why they choose this metric over absolute differences in log-likelihood, which would be more interpretable.
3. The writing contains some stylistic issues that detract from the paper's professionalism. For instance, the authors claim their algorithm is "elegant" despite the need for careful handling of the eigenvector updates and the requirement to keep eigenvalues slightly below 1. The use of bold text in the introduction is unnecessary and unprofessional.
4. The paper could better contextualize the significance of the 16.5% improvement. While this is substantial in the contexts where the initialization is poor, the average gain across categories is only 3.7%, which is modest for a method that requires significant algorithmic complexity.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with well-supported claims through both theoretical analysis and empirical results. The authors are careful to evaluate both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, particularly regarding initialization sensitivity. The algorithm is complete and well-motivated, though the M-step requires several caveats that slightly undermine the elegance of the approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, though the writing style issues noted above could be improved. The mathematical derivations are thorough, but the paper would benefit from clearer explanations of the practical implications of the theoretical results, particularly regarding the interpretation of the relative log-likelihood metric.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution to the DPP literature by addressing the full kernel learning problem, which has been conjectured to be NP-hard. While the EM approach is standard in optimization, its application to DPP kernel learning is novel and well-justified by the problem's characteristics.

**Significance:** The results demonstrate that the proposed method can significantly outperform existing approaches in settings where the initialization is poor, which is a common practical scenario. The method could be valuable in applications where modeling negative item interactions is critical, such as product recommendation, document summarization, and sensor placement. However, the modest average improvement (3.7%) when using better initialization suggests that the method may not be transformative for all settings.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a solid contribution to the DPP learning literature by addressing a meaningful problem that has been largely overlooked in previous work. While the improvements over the baseline are not revolutionary in all settings, the method is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear practical advantages in challenging scenarios. The paper's main weakness is the confusing metric reporting, but this can be addressed in the camera-ready version. The authors have provided a valuable algorithm for a problem that has been a limitation in the DPP literature, and the method has the potential to be widely adopted in applications requiring diversity-aware subset selection.
