Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) define a distribution over a ground set, favoring diverse subsets, and are parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix, *L*. Due to the NP-hardness of fully learning *L*, prior work has focused on partial learning or restricting *L*'s parametric form, often at the cost of diminishing diversity. This paper introduces a novel learning method for *L* without such restrictions. Unlike gradient ascent approaches which can degrade the DPP’s diversity through necessary projection steps, this method leverages an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework operating directly on eigenvalues and eigenvectors. This avoids the problematic projections—which would require projection of both eigenvalues *and* eigenvectors—by exploiting the full-rank property of the eigenvector matrix, *V*. A lower bound on the objective function, derived using Jensen's inequality, facilitates the construction of this EM procedure.

A key strength of this approach lies in its preservation of DPP diversity, a feature often overlooked in previous work that prioritized only quality. The detailed derivation of the EM algorithm is particularly valuable, as are the strong theoretical contributions of mapping the constraints to an optimization problem on the Stiefel manifold and demonstrating the resulting inverse distribution is also a DPP.  The proposed algorithm is evaluated on synthetic data and a product recommendation task. Results demonstrate the method successfully balances quality and diversity, as shown by a comparison with the Kernel Alignment (KA) method; even modest improvements in recommendation diversity, such as replacing only a small fraction of top recommendations with less probable items, highlight the paper's contribution and validate its initial claim. While the conclusion could more comprehensively summarize the work’s contributions, the paper is generally well-written and cohesive, with clear and rigorous proofs. The detailed reduction of the optimization problem to an EM formulation is notably insightful.

The manuscript is well-structured and the proofs are presented clearly. This reduction to EM is interesting and effectively explained. Further experimentation with broader, real-world datasets could strengthen the argument for the importance of the diversity achieved by this algorithm relative to competing approaches in the field.
