Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) define a distribution over a fixed ground set, assigning higher probability to more diverse subsets. DPPs are parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix, L. Learning the full matrix L is NP-hard, so prior work has focused on partial learning or restricting the parameterization of L. Learning a low-rank approximation for L, for example, can compromise the diversity captured by the DPP. This paper presents a learning method that avoids such restrictions on the parameterization of L. 

A key advantage of the proposed method is that it avoids the projection steps inherent in gradient ascent, which can degrade the similarity properties of L and consequently reduce the diversity of the sampled sets. Instead, the authors leverage an Expectation-Maximization (EM) approach operating directly on the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of L. This allows them to explore optimization algorithms that circumvent the need for projection. While projected gradient ascent necessitates projecting both eigenvalues and eigenvectors, the authors exploit the full-rank nature of the eigenvector matrix to avoid eigenvector projection. They utilize Jensen’s inequality to lower bound the objective function and formulate the EM procedure.

This learning method is valuable because it preserves the diversity property of DPPs, a feature often overlooked in previous work that primarily focused on maximizing “quality.” The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is particularly insightful. Mapping the constraint to an optimization problem on the Stiefel manifold—effectively eliminating eigenvector projection—and demonstrating that the resulting inverse distribution is also a DPP are significant contributions. 

The authors evaluate their learning algorithm on both synthetic datasets and a product recommendation task. The experiments validate the paper's claims, showing that the learned DPP retains both quality and diversity. The comparison with the Kernel Alignment (KA) method, where KA replaces only two of the top ten recommended products with less relevant items, effectively demonstrates the benefits of the proposed approach and supports the initial claim regarding diversity. The conclusion feels somewhat rushed, failing to fully reiterate the paper’s contributions, but overall, the paper is well-written and cohesive. The proofs are clear and the reduction of the optimization problem to an EM formulation is compelling. 

The paper is coherent and the proofs are well-structured. The step-by-step reduction to EM is both interesting and well-explained. Further experiments on more real-world datasets could more clearly illustrate the importance of the “diversity” produced by this algorithm compared to alternative approaches in the field.
