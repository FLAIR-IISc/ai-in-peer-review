

Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) define distributions over subsets of a fixed ground set that favor diverse selections. Parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix \(L\), learning \(L\) is NP-hard, limiting prior work to partial learning or restricted parameterizations. Previous approaches often used scalar parameterizations of \(L\), which compromise diversity, or imposed structural constraints on \(L\). This paper introduces a novel learning method that avoids such restrictions. Crucially, it eliminates the need for projection steps common in gradient ascent, which can degrade both the similarity property and diversity. The authors achieve this by applying Expectation-Maximization (EM) over eigenvalues and eigenvectors, leveraging the full-rank property of the eigenvector matrix \(V\) to avoid projecting eigenvectors. Jensen’s inequality is employed to derive a lower bound on the objective, enabling a clean EM procedure.  

The method’s key strength is preserving DPP diversity—unlike prior work that prioritized quality alone. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is particularly insightful, as is the elegant mapping of the constrained optimization to the Stiefel manifold, which eliminates eigenvector projection. The proof that the inverse mapping retains DPP structure is a solid theoretical contribution. Experiments on synthetic data and a real-world product recommendation task validate the approach: the learned DPP maintains both quality and diversity, outperforming the baseline KA. Specifically, while KA replaces only two top-10 recommendations with less probable items, it still demonstrates the method’s practical value in preserving diversity.  

The paper is well-written, with clear proofs and a compelling reduction of the optimization problem to the EM framework. However, the conclusion is somewhat premature, omitting key contributions like the Stiefel manifold formulation and the theoretical guarantee on diversity preservation. A stronger conclusion would better highlight these advances. While the current experiments support the claims, additional testing on broader real-world datasets would further underscore the practical significance of the diversity maintained by the proposed algorithm, especially compared to competing methods in the field. Overall, the paper makes a valuable theoretical and methodological contribution to DPP learning, with clear empirical validation.
