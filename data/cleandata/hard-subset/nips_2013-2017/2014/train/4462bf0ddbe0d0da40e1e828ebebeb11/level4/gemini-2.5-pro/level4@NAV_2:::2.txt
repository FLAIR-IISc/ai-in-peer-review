This paper presents a novel algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP), a task conjectured to be NP-hard due to the non-convex nature of the maximum likelihood objective. Whereas prior work focused on convex but restricted learning settings, this work tackles the full problem by reparameterizing the kernel via its eigendecomposition. The authors skillfully derive an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm by treating the selection of eigenvectors as a hidden variable, which allows them to optimize a lower bound on the log-likelihood. The resulting algorithm avoids the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent and involves a closed-form update for eigenvalues and an update on the Stiefel manifold for eigenvectors. Experiments on a real-world product recommendation task show that the proposed EM method is more robust and computationally faster than a projected gradient ascent baseline.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Technical Depth:** The proposed method is highly novel and technically sophisticated. The application of an EM framework to the eigendecomposition of the DPP kernel, combined with optimization on a Stiefel manifold, is a creative and powerful approach to a known difficult problem.
*   **Significance:** This work represents a solid advance in the practical application of DPPs. By providing an effective method for learning an unconstrained kernel, the paper potentially enables broader use of DPPs in applications that require modeling complex diversity patterns directly from data.
*   **Soundness:** The derivation of the EM algorithm appears to be technically sound and well-founded, logically combining several advanced mathematical concepts into a coherent learning procedure.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is not always easy to follow. The writing sometimes feels convoluted, as the main algorithmic description is interleaved with discussions of alternatives and potential issues. A more direct structure—presenting the final algorithm and objective first, then discussing the rationale and alternatives—would likely improve readability. Some notation is also used without a clear, upfront definition.
*   **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The empirical evaluation is focused on comparing the proposed EM algorithm to a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent). While this demonstrates the superiority of the proposed optimization technique, the experiments do not include comparisons to other non-DPP methods for diverse subset selection. This makes it difficult to gauge the performance of the learned DPPs against the broader state-of-the-art in this area.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The description of the projected gradient ascent baseline in Section 3.1 could be stated more formally. For instance, rather than "chooses the closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix," it would be clearer to state that the projection finds the unique matrix in the positive semi-definite cone that minimizes the Frobenius distance to the matrix Q.
*   Section 3.5 describes the eigenvector update, which relies on optimization over a Stiefel manifold. The paper refers to an algorithm from [26] and states that a simplified version is used. For the paper to be more self-contained, it would be helpful to briefly outline the general update rule and then explicitly state the simplifications (e.g., neglecting second-order information) that lead to the final update in Equation (21).
*   The moments-matching initialization in Section 4 is an interesting heuristic. Could the authors provide more intuition for the specific form of the off-diagonal entries in Equation (23)? A brief discussion on why this form was chosen over a more direct approach, such as initializing with the empirical covariance of item occurrences, would be insightful.
*   Minor clarification points:
    *   In Section 2, the marginal kernel `K` is introduced. While the formula is given, a clearer motivation for why this is called a "marginal" kernel and its properties could be provided before it is used.
    *   In Section 3.2, the term "mixture weight" refers to the probability `p_K(J)`. It would be helpful to explicitly label this term as such in Equation (9) to improve clarity.
