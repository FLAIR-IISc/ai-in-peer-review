This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from data. A DPP is a probabilistic model over subsets that naturally captures the notion of diversity. It is parameterized by a positive semi-definite kernel matrix, L, where diagonal entries typically encode item "quality" and off-diagonal entries encode item "similarity." Learning the full matrix L via maximum likelihood is a non-convex problem, conjectured to be NP-hard. Consequently, prior work has focused on restricted, convex learning settings, such as learning a scalar weight for each row of L (which models quality but not similarity) or learning a mixture of DPPs with fixed kernels (which sacrifices the computational benefits of a single DPP).

The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to learn the full kernel matrix without such restrictions. The key innovation is to reparameterize the kernel via its eigendecomposition (eigenvalues and eigenvectors). By viewing the selection of eigenvectors as a latent variable, they derive a lower bound on the log-likelihood using Jensen's inequality. The algorithm then proceeds by alternating between an E-step, which computes the posterior over the latent variables, and an M-step, which updates the eigenvalues and eigenvectors. A significant technical contribution is the handling of the eigenvector update: instead of using a problematic projection step to enforce the orthogonality constraint, the authors frame it as an optimization on a Stiefel manifold, leading to a more robust multiplicative update. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this EM approach on a real-world product recommendation task, showing significant improvements in test log-likelihood and qualitative results over a baseline of projected gradient ascent.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and difficult problem in the DPP literature: learning the full, unconstrained kernel matrix. Moving beyond the restricted learning scenarios of prior work is a significant step forward, enabling DPPs to be applied more broadly and effectively. The proposed EM algorithm is a novel and elegant solution to this non-convex optimization problem.
*   **Technical Quality:** The derivation of the EM algorithm is technically sound and insightful. The reparameterization via eigendecomposition is well-motivated, and the formulation with a latent variable is clever. Two key technical highlights are: (1) showing that the posterior distribution required for the E-step is itself a k-DPP, allowing for efficient computation, and (2) avoiding the pitfalls of projection for the eigenvector update by employing techniques for optimization on Stiefel manifolds. These contributions are solid and well-executed.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the step-by-step derivation of the EM algorithm is easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of explaining why the naive projected gradient ascent approach can fail (i.e., by collapsing to near-diagonal solutions that do not model diversity), which effectively motivates their more sophisticated approach.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide strong support for the paper's claims. The comparison against projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) on a real-world baby registry dataset is convincing. The proposed EM method not only achieves higher test log-likelihood, particularly in data-poor settings or with a less-informed initialization, but it is also shown to be significantly faster. The qualitative example comparing the recommended "safety" items is illustrative and effectively highlights the practical value of the improved diversity modeling.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The primary weakness is that the proposed method is only compared against projected gradient ascent on the full kernel. While this is the most direct baseline, it would have been more comprehensive to also compare against the restricted learning methods from prior work (e.g., Kulesza & Taskar, UAI 2011; ICML 2012). Such a comparison would more clearly quantify the practical benefit of learning the full kernel matrix over these simpler, convex alternatives.
*   **Qualitative Evaluation:** The qualitative example of the top-ten recommended baby registry items is helpful. However, the difference between the EM and KA models was a swap of two items. While the authors make a convincing argument that the EM result is more sensible, the benefit might appear subtle. Expanding this analysis, perhaps with more examples or even a small user study, could further strengthen the claim that the model produces more practically useful diverse sets.
*   **Conclusion:** The conclusion is a bit brief and could do more to summarize the paper's specific technical contributions. Highlighting the key insights—such as the connection to k-DPPs in the E-step and the use of Stiefel manifold optimization for the M-step—would provide a more complete takeaway for the reader.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on learning probabilistic models of diversity. The proposed EM algorithm for learning a full DPP kernel is novel, well-motivated, and technically sound. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate its superiority over a natural baseline. The work is well-presented and should be of high interest to researchers and practitioners working with DPPs and related models for subset selection.
