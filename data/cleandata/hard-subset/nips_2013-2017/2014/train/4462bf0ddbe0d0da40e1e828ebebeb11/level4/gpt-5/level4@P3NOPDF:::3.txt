Summary
This paper addresses the problem of learning Determinantal Point Process (DPP) kernels without imposing restrictive parameterizations. DPPs, parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix L, favor diverse subsets, but directly learning L is NP-hard. Prior work often learns only simple scalar or diagonal parameters, which can diminish the diversity benefits of DPPs, or relies on projected gradient methods that require repeated projections on eigenvalues and eigenvectors, potentially distorting the kernel. The authors propose an EM-based learning procedure that operates on the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of L. By using Jensen’s inequality to lower-bound the log-likelihood, they derive an EM algorithm that avoids costly and potentially harmful projection steps. They further map the constrained optimization over eigenvectors to the Stiefel manifold, and they show that an “inverse” distribution (as defined in the paper) also forms a DPP. Experiments on synthetic data and a product recommendation task suggest that the method preserves both quality and diversity compared to a KA baseline.

Strengths
- Technical novelty and clarity: The EM derivation based on an eigen-decomposition is well motivated and carefully developed. The use of Jensen’s inequality to obtain a tractable lower bound and the step-by-step EM construction are clear and insightful.
- Avoiding projections: The paper convincingly argues that projected gradient ascent can undermine the learned kernel’s structure by requiring separate projections of eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The proposed approach avoids projecting eigenvectors by formulating their update as an optimization over the Stiefel manifold, which is elegant and principled.
- Preservation of diversity: Unlike approaches that only tune item “quality” parameters (e.g., diagonal/weight-only models), the method targets the full kernel, thereby better preserving the diversity-inducing properties of DPPs.
- Additional theoretical contributions: The mapping to the Stiefel manifold and the result that the inverse (as defined by the authors) also induces a DPP are interesting and useful observations.
- Presentation quality: The paper is coherent, the proofs are well written, and the derivations are accessible.

Weaknesses and questions
- Experimental scope: The empirical evaluation is limited. Beyond synthetic data, there is only one product recommendation task. This makes it difficult to assess generality, robustness, and practical impact across domains.
- Baselines and metrics: It is not fully clear what “KA” refers to or how strong it is as a baseline. Moreover, explicit diversity metrics (e.g., intra-list similarity, coverage) and ranking-quality metrics (e.g., NDCG/precision) would provide a more quantitative and interpretable assessment of the quality–diversity trade-off.
- Practicality and scalability: The computational complexity per EM iteration, memory footprint, and scalability to large ground sets (common in recommendation) are not sufficiently discussed. How does the method compare in runtime and convergence behavior to projected gradient methods or diagonal-only learning?
- Sensitivity and initialization: EM can be sensitive to initialization and prone to local optima. The paper would benefit from a discussion or empirical study of initialization strategies, convergence diagnostics, and regularization of eigenvalues.
- Clarity on assumptions: The text mentions exploiting that V is full rank; in practice, the eigenvector matrix is orthonormal for nonzero eigenvalues, but L may be rank-deficient. Clarifying the exact assumptions and how rank deficiency is handled would improve rigor.
- Interpretation of the recommendation results: The narrative around “only two of the top-10 items are replaced” is suggestive but somewhat ambiguous. Are the replacements demonstrably improving a diversity metric while maintaining comparable quality? Clear quantitative evidence would strengthen the claim.

Suggestions for improvement
- Expand the empirical evaluation:
  - Include more real-world datasets (e.g., image summarization, document/video selection, or other recommendation domains).
  - Report standard ranking and diversity metrics, and include coverage/novelty measures to substantiate the diversity gains.
  - Compare against stronger and more varied baselines, such as diagonal-only/quality-only learning, low-rank factorizations of L, k-DPP variants, and projected gradient approaches.
  - Provide ablations (e.g., updating only eigenvalues vs. both eigenvalues and eigenvectors) to isolate where gains come from.
- Provide computational analysis:
  - Detail the per-iteration complexity and memory costs, and how these scale with the ground set size and effective rank.
  - Include runtime/convergence plots and sensitivity to initialization.
  - Discuss any regularization or constraints on eigenvalues to ensure numerical stability.
- Clarify technical points:
  - Precisely define “KA” in the experiments and justify its selection as a baseline.
  - Clarify the “inverse distribution is a DPP” statement and its implications.
  - Specify assumptions regarding rank and how zero or near-zero eigenvalues are handled in the EM updates.
- Strengthen the conclusion: Summarize all key contributions (EM derivation, Stiefel-manifold optimization, theoretical results) and the empirical takeaways more explicitly.

Overall assessment
This is a well-written and technically solid paper that proposes a principled EM procedure for learning unrestricted DPP kernels without reliance on potentially distorting projection steps. The theoretical treatment is careful, and the optimization on the Stiefel manifold is an elegant way to respect eigenvector constraints. The main limitation is the relatively narrow experimental validation and unclear baseline framing, which currently understate the practical impact. With an expanded empirical section, clearer baselines and metrics, and more discussion of scalability and initialization, this work would make a strong contribution.
