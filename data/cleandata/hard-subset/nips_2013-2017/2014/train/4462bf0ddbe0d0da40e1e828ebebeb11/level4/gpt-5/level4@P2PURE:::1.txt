This paper studies the problem of learning kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs). Its main contribution is a reformulation of the learning objective that outperforms the standard baseline of differentiating the log-probability with respect to kernel parameters and performing projected gradient ascent (referred to as K-Ascent, KA). The key idea is to cast learning in an EM framework. The authors observe that any DPP can be represented as a mixture over “elementary DPPs,” one for each subset of elements. They introduce a variational distribution over these subsets, update this distribution (represented as a k-DPP) in the E-step, and take single gradient steps in the M-step. Empirically, the EM method substantially outperforms KA when KA is naively initialized, and offers modest improvements in some settings when KA uses a stronger initialization.

Overall, the approach is interesting, and the EM method appears to provide some gains. The M-step is fairly complex, and the caveats needed to make it work are somewhat unsatisfying, but it is still notable that the method can be made to work.

Experimentally, I would like more analysis of how initialization affects EM. For example, if EM were initialized with the KA solution, would it further improve the objective?

I am also confused by the use of relative log-likelihood as the reported measure. Differences in log probabilities correspond to ratios of probabilities; why report ratios of log probabilities instead? Why not simply report differences in log probabilities? As presented, the numbers are hard to interpret.

A minor stylistic note: I would prefer the authors not call their own method elegant, especially given the caveats and approximations required in the M-step, and I find the boldface in the introduction unnecessary.

In summary, this is an interesting new approach to learning kernels for DPPs. The experiments could be strengthened, and the improvements over basic projected gradient ascent are not large, but overall this is a solid paper.
