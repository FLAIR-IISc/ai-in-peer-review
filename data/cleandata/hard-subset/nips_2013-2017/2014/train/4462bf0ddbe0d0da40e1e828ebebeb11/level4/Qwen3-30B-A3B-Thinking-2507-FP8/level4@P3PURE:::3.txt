

The paper addresses the challenge of learning the kernel matrix \(L\) for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), which are distributions over subsets that favor diversity. Prior work has faced two key limitations: (1) learning \(L\) is NP-hard, leading to partial learning approaches; (2) methods that learn a scalar vector for \(L\) sacrifice diversity, while parametric restrictions on \(L\) are commonly adopted. This paper proposes a novel learning method that avoids such restrictions, preserving both diversity and quality properties.  

The core contribution lies in avoiding projection steps (unlike gradient ascent, which risks compromising diversity via similarity constraints). By leveraging the full-rank property of the eigenvector matrix \(V\), the authors eliminate the need to project eigenvectors during optimization. Instead, they employ an EM procedure derived via Jensen’s inequality to lower-bound the objective, reducing the problem to optimization over the Stiefel manifold. This avoids eigenvector projection while ensuring the inverse distribution remains a valid DPP. The step-by-step derivation of the EM algorithm is well-motivated and insightful.  

Experiments on synthetic data and a product recommendation task validate the approach. Compared to the baseline KA, the method retains both quality and diversity: only two of the top-10 recommended products are replaced by less probable items under KA, demonstrating the preservation of DPP properties. The paper’s proofs are rigorous and well-structured, and the writing is coherent. However, the conclusion prematurely summarizes contributions without fully elaborating on the novelty of the eigenvector-free optimization or the Stiefel manifold mapping. While the experimental results support the claims, additional real-world experiments would better quantify the *impact* of the preserved diversity relative to competing methods.  

Overall, the paper makes a solid technical contribution by resolving a key limitation in DPP learning, with strong theoretical grounding and empirical validation. The method’s avoidance of projection steps and explicit preservation of diversity represent significant advances over prior work.
