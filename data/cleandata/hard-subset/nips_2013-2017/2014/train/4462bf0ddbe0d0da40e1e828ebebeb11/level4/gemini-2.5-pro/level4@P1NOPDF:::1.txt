This paper addresses the problem of learning kernel matrices for use in determinantal point processes (DPPs). The main contribution is a reformulation of the learning objective, which is optimized within an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework. This approach is contrasted with the standard method of maximizing the log-likelihood via projected gradient ascent on the kernel parameters, which the authors term K-Ascent (KA).

The proposed EM algorithm is based on the observation that any DPP can be represented as a mixture of "elementary DPPs," one for each possible subset of items. The algorithm introduces a variational distribution over these subsets and then alternates between an E-step, which updates this distribution (represented as a k-DPP), and an M-step, which performs a single gradient update on the kernel parameters. The results demonstrate that the EM method significantly outperforms a naively initialized KA baseline. When KA is initialized more intelligently, the proposed method offers modest improvements in some settings.

Overall, the approach is interesting, and the EM formulation appears to yield tangible improvements. The M-step is computationally intricate, and the approximations and heuristics required to make it tractable detract somewhat from the method's elegance. Nevertheless, the authors deserve credit for developing a functional algorithm based on this novel perspective.

On the experimental side, the analysis could be strengthened by a more thorough investigation of the EM algorithm's sensitivity to initialization. For example, it would be insightful to see if the EM algorithm can further refine a solution found by the K-Ascent method. If EM were initialized with the final parameters from a converged KA run, would it find a better local optimum? This would help clarify the relationship between the two optimization landscapes.

I was also unclear on the motivation for using relative log-likelihood as the primary evaluation metric. Differences in log-likelihoods have a clear interpretation as the logarithm of the ratio of probabilities. However, the ratio of log-likelihoods is more difficult to interpret. Reporting the direct difference in log-likelihoods would be more standard and provide a more intuitive measure of performance improvement.

Finally, a minor note on presentation: the authors' description of their own algorithm as "elegant" feels premature, particularly given the approximations required in the M-step. Similarly, the use of bold text in the introduction is unnecessary and slightly distracting.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and interesting approach for learning DPP kernels. While the empirical gains over a well-initialized baseline are not always substantial and the experimental section could be strengthened, the work represents a solid contribution to the literature.
