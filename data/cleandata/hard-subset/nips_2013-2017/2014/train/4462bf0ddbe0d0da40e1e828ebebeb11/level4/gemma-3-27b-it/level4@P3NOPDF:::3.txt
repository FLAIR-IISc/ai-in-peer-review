## Review of NIPS 2014 Submission

This paper addresses the problem of learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), a class of distributions over a fixed ground set prized for their ability to model diversity. DPPs are parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix *L*, but learning *L* directly is NP-hard. Prior work has typically resorted to either partial learning of *L* (compromising diversity) or restricting its parametric form. This paper presents a compelling learning method that avoids these limitations by learning *L* without imposing restrictive parameterizations.

The core contribution lies in a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning *L*. Unlike gradient-based approaches that require projections to maintain the positive semidefinite property, potentially sacrificing diversity, this method directly optimizes the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of *L*. The authors cleverly exploit the full-rank property of the eigenvector matrix to avoid projecting eigenvectors, a significant computational and theoretical simplification. They utilize Jensen's inequality to derive a lower bound on the objective function, enabling the construction of the EM procedure. This approach elegantly sidesteps the pitfalls of previous methods and directly addresses the challenge of preserving both the quality and diversity inherent in DPPs.

The advantages of this learning method are clearly articulated: it preserves the diversity property often overlooked in prior work, which tends to focus predominantly on quality. The detailed, step-by-step derivation of the EM algorithm is particularly insightful and strengthens the paper’s technical contribution. Further solid contributions include mapping the constraints to an optimization problem on the Stiefel manifold, thereby eliminating eigenvector projections, and demonstrating that the resulting inverse distribution is indeed a DPP. 

The experimental validation is thorough, encompassing both synthetic datasets and a practical product recommendation task. The results convincingly support the paper's claims, demonstrating that the proposed learning method retains both the quality and diversity of DPPs. The comparison with the Kernel Alignment (KA) method is particularly effective, illustrating that while KA may replace a small number of top-ranked products with less relevant alternatives, the proposed method consistently prioritizes diverse and high-quality recommendations. 

The paper is well-written and cohesive, with proofs presented in a clear and accessible manner. The reduction of the optimization problem to an EM procedure is a particularly noteworthy aspect of the technical presentation. While the conclusion could be more comprehensive in reiterating the key contributions, it remains a minor point. 

However, while the experiments are encouraging, expanding the evaluation to include more real-world datasets would further underscore the practical importance of the “diversity” produced by this algorithm compared to alternative approaches. Demonstrating the benefits in scenarios where diversity is explicitly valued – such as news recommendation or portfolio optimization – could significantly strengthen the paper’s impact.

**Overall:** This is a strong paper that presents a significant advance in the learning of DPPs. The proposed EM algorithm offers a principled and effective solution to a challenging problem, successfully balancing the need for quality and diversity. The theoretical contributions are well-supported by experimental results, and the paper is written with clarity and precision. I recommend acceptance.
