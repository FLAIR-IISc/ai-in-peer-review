This paper addresses learning kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The primary contribution is a reformulation of the learning objective that surpasses the conventional strategy of differentiating the log-likelihood with respect to kernel entries followed by projected gradient ascent (the baseline termed K-Ascent, KA). The approach casts learning into an EM framework. It exploits the fact that any DPP can be decomposed as a mixture over “elementary DPPs,” one per subset of items. A variational distribution is defined over these subsets; the algorithm alternates between updating this distribution—represented as a k-DPP—and performing single gradient steps in the M-step. Empirically, the EM method outperforms KA substantially when KA is initialized in a naive manner, and modestly in some scenarios when KA is initialized more carefully.

Overall, the idea is appealing, and the EM procedure does appear to deliver improvements. The M-step is somewhat intricate, and the practical adjustments needed to make it function are a bit unsatisfying, though there is value in demonstrating that the method can be made to work.

Experimentally, I would have appreciated more analysis of EM’s sensitivity to initialization. For instance, if the EM algorithm were started from the solution produced by KA, would it further improve upon that result?

I am also unclear about the use of “relative log-likelihood” as the reported metric. Differences in log probabilities correspond to ratios of probabilities. Why report ratios of log probabilities? Why not simply report log-likelihood differences? As presented, the numbers are difficult to interpret.

A minor stylistic note: it would be preferable for the authors not to characterize their own method as “elegant,” especially given the approximations and caveats in the M-step, and the boldface in the introduction seems unnecessary.

In sum, this is an interesting new approach to learning DPP kernels. The experiments could be strengthened, and the gains over basic projected gradient are not uniformly large, but overall this is a solid paper.
