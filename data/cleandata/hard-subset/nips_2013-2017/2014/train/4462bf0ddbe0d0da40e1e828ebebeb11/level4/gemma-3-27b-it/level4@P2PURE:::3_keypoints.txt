- DPPs favor diverse subsets but learning the kernel L is NP-hard, so prior work uses partial learning or restrictive parameterizations.  
- Low-rank approximations to L can reduce the diversity captured by the DPP.  
- The paper proposes a method to learn L without restrictive parametric assumptions.  
- Key advantage: the method avoids projection steps in gradient ascent that can degrade similarity and diversity.  
- The authors employ an EM algorithm that operates directly on L’s eigenvalues and eigenvectors.  
- Jensen’s inequality is used to lower-bound the objective, enabling the EM formulation.  
- By leveraging the full-rank property of the eigenvector matrix, the method avoids eigenvector projections required in projected gradient approaches.  
- The constraints are mapped to an optimization problem over the Stiefel manifold to eliminate eigenvector projection.  
- They show the inverse distribution is itself a DPP, which is a notable theoretical contribution.  
- The step-by-step derivation and proofs are clear and insightful.  
- Experiments on synthetic data and a product recommendation task show the learned DPP preserves both quality and diversity, outperforming KA in preserving top recommendations.  
- Suggested improvements: add larger real-world experiments and a more thorough conclusion summarizing contributions.
