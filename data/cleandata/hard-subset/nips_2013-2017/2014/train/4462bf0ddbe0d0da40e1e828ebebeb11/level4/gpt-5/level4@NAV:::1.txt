This paper addresses learning kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The principal contribution is a reparameterization of the objective that surpasses the standard baseline of differentiating the log-probability with respect to kernel parameters followed by projected gradient ascent (referred to as K-Ascent (KA) in the paper). The approach recasts the problem within an EM framework by noting that any DPP can be expressed as a mixture over “elementary DPPs,” one for each subset of items. A variational distribution is placed over these subsets, and the algorithm alternates between updating this subset distribution—modeled as a k-DPP—in the E-step and taking single gradient updates in the M-step. Empirically, the EM method substantially outperforms KA when KA uses a naive initialization, and provides modest gains in some cases when KA is initialized more carefully.

Overall, I find the idea compelling, and the EM method appears to yield improvements. The M-step is rather intricate, and the caveats needed to make it function are somewhat unsatisfying, but it is nonetheless notable that the method works in practice.

From an experimental perspective, I would have liked more investigation into the role of initialization for EM. For instance, if EM were started from the KA solution, would it further improve the objective?

I am also puzzled by the use of relative log likelihood as a reported metric. Differences in log probabilities correspond to ratios of probabilities. Why consider ratios of log probabilities? Why not simply report differences in log probabilities? As presented, the numbers are hard to interpret.

A minor stylistic comment (forgive the crankiness): I find parts of the writing distracting. I would prefer the authors not label their own method “elegant,” especially since its elegance is debatable given the caveats and approximations in the M-step. The boldface in the introduction also feels unnecessary. In sum: an interesting new approach for learning DPP kernels. The experiments could be strengthened, and the gains over basic projected gradient are not dramatic, but overall this is a solid paper.
