

This paper addresses a significant challenge in learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the NP-hardness of fully learning the kernel matrix L from data. Prior work has circumvented this by restricting learning to scalar weights per row [2] or linear combinations of fixed-kernel DPPs [3], both of which sacrifice the diversity modeling capability inherent to DPPs. The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) approach that learns the full kernel matrix without restrictive parametric assumptions, leveraging the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K. By reparameterizing in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors and applying Jensen’s inequality to lower-bound the likelihood, they develop an algorithm that avoids the problematic projection steps required by gradient ascent (e.g., K-Ascent), which often yields degenerate, near-diagonal kernels that fail to capture negative item interactions critical for diversity. The method is rigorously derived, with careful handling of the non-convex eigenvector constraint via Stiefel manifold optimization.

**Strengths**  
*Quality & Technical Soundness*: The paper is technically robust. The EM derivation is thorough, with clear exposition of the E-step (showing the conditional distribution is a k-DPP) and M-step (closed-form eigenvalue updates and efficient eigenvector updates). The runtime analysis (O(T₁nNk² + T₁T₂N³) vs. K-Ascent’s O(T₁nN³ + T₁T₂N³)) is compelling, and the experiments demonstrate a substantial 16.5% relative gain in test log-likelihood for the "safety" category, with consistent average gains of 3.7% across 13 product categories. Crucially, the method preserves diversity: K-Ascent’s projection step causes it to learn nearly diagonal kernels (e.g., replacing diversity-focused items like "lens filters" with redundant "baby monitors" in Figure 2a), while EM maintains meaningful negative interactions between items. The proofs and derivations (e.g., Equation 19 for marginal computation) are well-justified and accessible.

*Originality*: This work directly addresses a gap in NIPS literature. While Kulesza & Taskar’s foundational NIPS papers [1,19] established DPPs’ computational properties, prior learning methods [2,3,8] all imposed constraints (scalar weights, fixed-kernel ensembles, or parametric forms) that sacrifice the core diversity advantage. The EM approach using eigendecomposition and avoiding projections is novel, as confirmed by a review of recent NIPS proceedings (e.g., [15,18,20] focus on applications or restricted learning, not full kernel learning).

*Significance*: The application to product recommendation (baby registry data with 29k examples) demonstrates clear practical value. DPPs excel when both item quality *and* diversity matter (e.g., avoiding redundant car seats in a registry), and this method enables effective learning for such tasks. The 16.5% log-likelihood gain is significant for a real-world task where diversity directly impacts user satisfaction. The approach could generalize to document summarization [4,5], sensor placement [6,7], or image search [3,8], where modeling negative interactions is crucial.

*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-organized. The background (Section 2) concisely establishes DPP properties, the learning problem (Section 3) logically motivates the EM approach, and the experiments (Section 4) clearly link results to the core challenge (e.g., Figure 1 correlating gains with the diversity metric d). Algorithm 2 is cleanly presented, and the supplement provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**  
The conclusion briefly summarizes results but understates the contribution: it should explicitly highlight how the EM method resolves the fundamental limitation of prior work (full kernel learning without degeneracy) and emphasize the practical impact (e.g., "enabling DPPs to model diversity in real-world applications where it was previously infeasible"). While the baby registry experiments are strong, additional comparisons to [8]’s Bayesian approach or [3]’s L-ensemble would further contextualize the method’s advantages. The runtime analysis assumes k ≪ N; while valid for the experiments (k ≤ 10), this could be briefly acknowledged as a potential limitation for very large sets.

**Overall Assessment**  
This is a high-quality contribution that advances DPP learning by solving a long-standing NP-hardness barrier without compromising the model’s diversity properties. The method is theoretically sound, experimentally validated on a meaningful real-world task, and avoids the degeneracy pitfalls of prior approaches. It directly addresses NIPS’ criteria: the work is technically rigorous (Quality), clearly presented (Clarity), solves a novel learning problem (Originality), and enables new applications where diversity matters (Significance). The minor weakness in the conclusion’s synthesis does not diminish the paper’s strong merits.

**Recommendation**: Accept. The paper makes a clear, impactful contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling, with strong theoretical and empirical support. It is a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
