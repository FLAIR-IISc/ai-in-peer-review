The paper presents an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs). The method combines several nontrivial ingredients: eigendecomposition of the kernel, optimization on the Stiefel manifold, representation of DPPs as mixtures of elementary DPPs, and lower-bounding the log-likelihood via Jensen’s Inequality. The experiments demonstrate favorable performance of the proposed approach compared to a simpler “K-Ascent” scheme.

**Quality:** The method appears well-founded, and it is plausible that the proposed techniques achieve the stated results. However, the evaluation could be improved. The experiments are somewhat limited and do not compare against alternative approaches for subset selection (e.g., those mentioned in the introduction), making it unclear how much benefit is derived specifically from the DPP approach. While the theoretical underpinnings seem solid, more comprehensive empirical validation would strengthen the contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is not particularly clearly written. The write-up is somewhat disorganized and can be difficult to follow. Not all notation is consistently defined, and sometimes definitions appear after their initial use. The writing is also occasionally convoluted, likely due to structuring the text to describe the development process with side-remarks and alternative considerations interleaved with the main narrative. A clearer presentation of the developed method, followed by a separate discussion of alternatives, would improve readability.

**Originality:** The paper appears quite original; I am not aware of similar work in this area. The combination of techniques – particularly leveraging the eigendecomposition and EM framework – seems novel for DPP learning.

**Significance:** The method presented in the paper represents a solid advance and a potentially useful contribution to the subset selection literature. Addressing the NP-hardness of full kernel learning in DPPs is a significant step, and the proposed EM algorithm offers a practical approach to this problem.

**Details:**

- p.2, line 81: The marginal kernel K is used before being defined. Clarification of what constitutes a marginal kernel is needed.
- p.2, line 100: V is not defined before its use.
- p.3, line 159: “minimises the change to Frobenius norm” is unclearly stated. It would be more precise to state “chooses the closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix to Q”.
- p.4: This section is difficult to understand. Instead of referencing [26], it would be beneficial to outline the simplified approach directly to avoid requiring the reader to consult the original, more complex method.
- p.4, line 189: Clarify what “weight” refers to in this context.
- p.7, line 324: I do not fully understand the rationale behind equation (30). Why is m_{ij} not used here? Why would simply using the empirical covariance matrix as an initial kernel not have worked?

In summary, the paper puts forward a new method for learning Determinantal Point Processes for subset selection. The method is highly non-trivial and appears original. The write-up could benefit from improved clarity, and the experiments would be strengthened by comparisons to a broader range of alternative methods.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a challenging problem (NP-hardness of DPP kernel learning).
*   Solid theoretical foundation and justification of the proposed method.
*   Demonstrated improvement over a baseline approach (K-Ascent) in the experimental setting.
*   Potential for significant impact in applications where DPPs are relevant (recommendation systems, diversity-promoting subset selection).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation and lack of comparison to non-DPP methods.
*   Clarity of writing could be improved.
*   Some notation is poorly defined or used before definition.
*   The motivation for certain design choices (e.g. initialization) is not fully explained.
