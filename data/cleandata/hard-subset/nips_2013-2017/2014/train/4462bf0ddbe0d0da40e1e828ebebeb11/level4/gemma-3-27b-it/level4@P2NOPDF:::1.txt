This paper investigates learning kernel matrices for use with determinantal point processes (DPPs). The primary contribution is a novel reformulation of the kernel learning objective, demonstrating performance gains over the standard approach of differentiating the log-likelihood with respect to kernel parameters and employing projected gradient ascent—a method the authors term K-Ascent (KA). Their approach leverages an expectation-maximization (EM) framework, motivated by the observation that any DPP can be decomposed into a mixture of “elementary DPPs,” one for each possible subset of elements. The algorithm introduces a variational distribution over these subsets, modeled as a k-DPP, and iteratively alternates between updating this distribution (the E-step) and performing a single gradient step in the M-step.  Experimental results indicate that the EM method consistently outperforms KA when initialized with naive parameters and shows modest improvements even with more informed KA initializations.

I find the proposed approach conceptually interesting, and the empirical evidence suggests that the EM formulation does indeed offer performance benefits. While the M-step is computationally complex and relies on caveats and approximations to achieve practicality, successfully navigating these challenges represents a worthwhile achievement. 

However, the experimental analysis could be strengthened.  Specifically, it would be valuable to investigate the effect of initializing the EM algorithm with the solution obtained from KA.  Determining whether EM can refine a solution found by gradient ascent would provide further insight into its capabilities.

I also have concerns regarding the reported evaluation metric, relative log-likelihood.  Given that log-likelihood differences naturally represent ratios of probabilities, reporting ratios *of log-probabilities* is unclear and difficult to interpret. Presenting the results as simple differences in log-probabilities would likely provide more intuitive and understandable values. 

Finally, I offer a minor stylistic note: while the work itself is interesting, I recommend the authors avoid self-congratulatory language, such as describing their algorithm as “elegant,” particularly given the approximations inherent in the M-step. Furthermore, the bolding of text within the introduction feels unnecessary.

Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a new approach to learning kernels for DPPs. While the experimental improvements over the basic projected gradient method are not dramatic, the work is promising and well-executed. The suggested clarifications and additional experiments would further enhance the paper’s impact.
