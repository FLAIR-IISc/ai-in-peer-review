This paper tackles maximum-likelihood learning for determinantal point processes (DPPs), which model the trade-off between item quality and diversity via a positive semidefinite kernel L (or marginal kernel K). Directly optimizing the likelihood over L’s entries is non-convex and conjectured NP-hard, and naive projected gradient ascent (KA) can produce degenerate, nearly diagonal kernels that fail to model negative interactions. Prior work has therefore learned only restricted parameterizations: per-item weights (convex but ignores similarity structure), mixtures of fixed kernels, or Bayesian learning under strong parametric assumptions. In contrast, this paper proposes an EM-style algorithm that learns the full kernel by reparameterizing in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors and introducing a hidden variable over eigenvector subsets. Using Jensen’s inequality yields a tractable lower bound: the E-step identifies a conditional k-DPP over eigenvector indices; the M-step has a closed-form eigenvalue update and takes a gradient step on the eigenvectors using a Stiefel manifold update, avoiding ad hoc projection. Empirically, the method outperforms KA in both accuracy and speed, especially when off-diagonal structure is important.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: preserving diversity requires learning off-diagonal structure; projection-based KA can destroy that structure by pushing eigenvalues to zero and freezing the kernel near-diagonal.
- Solid technical contribution: a careful EM derivation built around the eigendecomposition of K; the E-step’s conditional distribution is shown to be a k-DPP; the M-step includes an efficient, self-normalizing eigenvalue update and a principled eigenvector update over the Stiefel manifold.
- Practical algorithmic advantages: avoids problematic projections; asymptotically faster than KA (O(T1 n N k^2 + T1 T2 N^3) vs. O(T1 n N^3 + T1 T2 N^3)).
- Empirical support: on product recommendation (baby registries) and synthetic data, EM improves test log-likelihoods (up to 16.5% in sparse data regimes), is more robust to initialization, and is about 2.1× faster on average. The analysis linking gains to the magnitude of off-diagonal structure (via a simple statistic on data marginals) is informative. The qualitative example illustrates how KA can propose redundant items (e.g., multiple monitors), whereas EM preserves diversity.

Weaknesses
- Experimental scope: real-world evaluation is limited to a single domain. Additional datasets would better demonstrate generality and the impact of diversity on downstream tasks.
- Evaluation metrics: emphasis is on log-likelihood; while appropriate, task-specific metrics (e.g., recommendation diversity/coverage or user-oriented proxies) would strengthen the case.
- Presentation: the conclusion is brief relative to the contributions; highlighting the k-DPP E-step, Stiefel optimization, and robustness findings would improve emphasis.

Quality
- The method is technically sound, with careful derivations and appropriate use of EM machinery. The argument about KA’s projection leading to near-diagonal degeneracy is compelling and supported empirically. Complexity analyses are provided, and implementation details (e.g., step-size search with re-run E-steps) are thoughtfully discussed.

Clarity
- The paper is coherent and well organized. The step-by-step EM derivation is insightful, and the proofs are clearly written. The mapping of the eigenvector constraint to optimization on the Stiefel manifold is well explained. Minor suggestion: clarify terminology (e.g., emphasize that the E-step’s conditional distribution is a k-DPP).

Originality
- Recasting DPP learning in the eigenbasis with a latent subset over eigenvectors and constructing an EM procedure around k-DPP structure is novel relative to prior convex partial-learning and mixture approaches. The Stiefel manifold update to avoid eigenvector projection is also a notable design choice.

Significance
- Learning the full DPP kernel without restrictive parameterizations is important for applications where negative interactions drive performance. The proposed method advances the practical state of the art by improving both robustness and runtime over KA, and it should be useful to researchers and practitioners modeling diversity.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled EM framework for full DPP learning.
- Addresses a known failure mode of projected gradient methods.
- Strong empirical improvements when off-diagonal structure matters; faster in practice.
- Clear derivations; useful connections to k-DPPs and Stiefel optimization.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited diversity of real-world datasets; reliance on log-likelihood as the primary metric.
- The conclusion underplays key contributions; broader empirical validation would help.

Suggestions
- Include additional real-world domains and task-oriented evaluation of diversity benefits.
- Expand the conclusion to reiterate the main methodological and empirical contributions.
- Provide sensitivity analyses (e.g., to initialization, k, and step-size schedules) and ablations of EM components.

Overall, this is a well-executed and timely contribution. The EM approach offers a practical and theoretically grounded path to learning full DPP kernels, preserving diversity where it matters and outperforming KA in both robustness and speed.
