Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses learning determinantal point processes (DPPs) for modeling diversity in subset selection. A DPP is parameterized by a positive semidefinite kernel L (or equivalently the marginal kernel K), and learning L by maximum likelihood is non-convex and conjectured to be NP-hard. Prior work therefore focused on restricted, convex formulations: learning per-item scalings (quality terms) on fixed similarities, or learning mixtures of fixed DPPs, or Bayesian learning under parametric assumptions. In contrast, this paper proposes to learn the full kernel without restrictive parameterization by reparameterizing K via its eigendecomposition (eigenvalues Λ and eigenvectors V) and optimizing a Jensen lower bound in an expectation–maximization (EM) framework.

The key idea is to exploit the standard DPP sampling view: first sample a hidden index set J of eigenvectors, then sample Y from the corresponding elementary DPP. Introducing J as a latent variable enables a tractable lower bound. The E-step sets q(J|Y) to the exact posterior pK(J|Y), which the authors show is a k-DPP with a computable kernel. In the M-step, eigenvalues admit a closed-form update via expected inclusion probabilities p(j ∈ J | Y), computed efficiently using k-DPP marginalization; eigenvectors are updated by a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, avoiding the projection step that plagues naive projected gradient ascent (KA) on K. The authors argue this avoids the near-diagonal degeneracy often induced by clipping eigenvalues to [0,1] during projection. They analyze complexity and show the EM approach is asymptotically faster than KA when observed set sizes are small (k ≪ N).

Experiments on a real-world product recommendation task (Amazon baby registry categories) show that EM improves held-out log-likelihood relative to KA, especially when off-diagonal structure (negative interactions) is strong; it is also faster in practice. The paper connects these gains to a simple statistic measuring the strength of pairwise interactions in the data and provides a qualitative example where KA recommends redundant items.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The derivation of the EM lower bound is standard and well executed. The E-step identification of q(J|Y) as a k-DPP is elegant and practically useful. The M-step eigenvalue update is clean, and the eigenvector update via a Stiefel manifold step is appropriate. The authors are careful to note that they take only one gradient step for V per M-step and use a line search on the true likelihood with repeated E-steps, which is a reasonable pragmatic choice.
- Claims vs. support: The paper supports its main claims with both quantitative improvements in test log-likelihood and runtime and a qualitative example highlighting diversity. The empirical link between off-diagonal importance and EM’s gains is insightful.
- Limitations: The M-step for V is not optimized to convergence, and monotone improvement of the lower bound is not guaranteed; the authors instead verify improvement on the true likelihood, which requires additional E-steps. Convergence properties and sensitivity to step size could be discussed further. The complexity still includes an O(N^3) term for the manifold update’s matrix exponential during line search; considering alternative retractions or Cayley transforms could improve scalability. Finally, the empirical evaluation primarily compares against KA; comparisons to other learning strategies (restricted convex models, mixtures, or Bayesian approaches) would strengthen the case.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The step-by-step development from the DPP mixture view to the EM algorithm is pedagogical. Algorithmic pseudocode is helpful. Some notational density around the E-step (definitions of R, U, ZYi) could be lightened by a brief intuition box. It would also help to explicitly state the monotonicity behavior (likelihood vs. bound), and to summarize the computational cost of each step in a small table.

Originality:
Reparameterizing DPP learning via eigendecomposition and casting it as an EM problem with a k-DPP posterior is, to my knowledge, new relative to prior work at NIPS and elsewhere, which either imposed parametric forms or learned only item-wise quality weights. The use of Stiefel-manifold optimization to avoid projection artifacts is also a novel and fitting choice in this setting.

Significance:
Being able to learn full DPP kernels is important for applications where negative correlations drive performance (recommendation, summarization, sensor placement). The proposed approach is likely to be adopted by practitioners who have avoided full-kernel learning due to instability and projection issues. The empirical gains, while modest on average, are substantial in regimes where diversity matters most and in data-poor settings, and the runtime improvements make the method practical.

Pros:
- Conceptually clean EM formulation leveraging the DPP mixture interpretation.
- Closed-form eigenvalue updates and principled eigenvector updates on the Stiefel manifold; avoids problematic PSD projections.
- Clear complexity analysis; faster than KA in theory and practice.
- Empirical evidence on a real-world dataset; improvements correlate with strength of negative interactions.
- Solid, well-explained derivations and useful connections to k-DPPs.

Cons:
- Limited baselines (primarily KA); missing comparisons to restricted convex learning, mixtures of DPPs, and Bayesian parametric methods.
- M-step for V is approximate; convergence guarantees (for either the bound or the likelihood) are not provided.
- Scalability could be further discussed, especially the dependence on k and the cost of manifold updates; alternatives to matrix exponential could be explored.
- Evaluation focuses on likelihood; additional task metrics (e.g., diversity-aware MAP selection quality) would be informative.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include baselines beyond KA (e.g., per-item scaling, L-ensemble mixtures, or Bayesian learning under parametric kernels) to situate gains more broadly.
- Provide a brief convergence discussion: under what conditions does the procedure increase the likelihood monotonically? Report typical numbers of line-search/E-steps per M-step.
- Explore alternative Stiefel retractions to reduce the O(N^3) cost of the matrix exponential, and report wall-clock breakdowns for E vs. M steps.
- Add an analysis of sensitivity to initialization and to the λ cap near 1; consider regularization or priors to mitigate overfitting.
- Report additional application-level metrics (e.g., MAP set diversity/coverage) alongside likelihood, and, if possible, a small-scale synthetic experiment demonstrating recovery of known structure.

Overall assessment:
This is a strong and timely paper that advances the state of the art in learning DPPs by enabling full-kernel training with a principled and practical EM approach. The method is technically sound, novel, and addresses a real pain point in prior work. With broader empirical comparisons and a bit more discussion of convergence and scalability, it would make a valuable contribution to NIPS. I lean toward acceptance.
