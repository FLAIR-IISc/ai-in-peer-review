Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) are distributions over a fixed ground set that assign higher probabilities to diverse sets. DPPs can be parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix (L). However, learning L is NP-hard, and prior work has only discussed partial learning of L. Previous approaches have either learned a scalar vector for L, compromising on the diversity dimension of DPP, or resorted to restricting the parametric form of L. In contrast, the authors of this paper propose a learning method that does not restrict the parameterization of L. 

This learning method avoids the need for a projection step, which can compromise the similarity property and, in turn, the diversity property. By using Expectation-Maximization (EM) on eigenvalues and eigenvectors, the authors overcome this disadvantage. They explore various optimization algorithms to solve this problem without requiring projection. Notably, they exploit the fact that the matrix V is full-rank, thereby avoiding the projection of eigenvectors. The authors use Jensen's inequality to lower bound the objective function and construct an EM procedure.

The proposed learning method is useful because it preserves the diversity property of the DPP, unlike previous work that has primarily focused on the quality property. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is insightful, and the authors make solid contributions by mapping the constraint to an optimization over the Stiefel manifold to eliminate the projection of eigenvectors. They also show that the inverse distribution is a DPP. The authors test their learning algorithm on both synthetic datasets and a product recommendation task. The experiments support the paper's claims, demonstrating that the learning method retains the quality and diversity of the DPP. This is evident when comparing the results to those obtained using Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence approximation (KA), where only two of the top 10 products are replaced by less likely products.

Although the conclusion is somewhat hasty and does not fully highlight the contributions of the paper, the overall writing is cohesive. The proofs are well-written, and the step-by-step reduction of the optimization problem to EM is interesting and well-explained. To further illustrate the importance of the "diversity" produced by this algorithm, additional experiments on real-world datasets would be beneficial.
