This paper proposes a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP). The problem of learning a DPP kernel by maximizing the data log-likelihood is known to be a difficult, non-convex optimization problem. Previous work has circumvented this by focusing on more constrained, convex settings, such as learning weights for a fixed kernel or for a linear combination of kernels. This paper tackles the full learning problem directly. The key idea is to re-parameterize the kernel via its eigendecomposition and treat the selection of eigenvectors as a latent variable. This allows the authors to formulate an EM-style algorithm that lower-bounds the log-likelihood using Jensen's inequality. The resulting method avoids the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent and is shown to be asymptotically faster. The proposed algorithm is evaluated on a real-world product recommendation task, where it demonstrates improved performance over a projected gradient ascent baseline, particularly in data-poor settings or with less-informed initializations.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a central and challenging problem in the application of DPPs: learning the full kernel from data. The proposed EM-based approach, which leverages the eigendecomposition and a latent variable formulation, is a novel and elegant solution to this non-convex problem. This represents a significant step beyond prior work that relied on more restrictive or convex formulations and could be a valuable contribution to the literature on DPPs and diverse subset selection.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the algorithm appears solid, cleverly combining several advanced concepts: the representation of a DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs, the use of Jensen's inequality to construct a tractable lower bound, and optimization on the Stiefel manifold to handle the orthonormality constraint on eigenvectors. The resulting algorithm is well-motivated and avoids the pitfalls of the simpler baseline, such as collapsing to near-diagonal solutions.

3.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while limited in scope, effectively demonstrate the primary advantages of the proposed EM algorithm over the projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent). The results show that the EM method is more robust, especially when the initialization is poor or data is scarce, and also computationally faster in practice. The qualitative example of the baby registry recommendation provides a compelling case for the improved diversity modeling.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance):**

1.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper's presentation is difficult to follow at times. The narrative is structured as a journey of discovery, describing the process of arriving at the final method, with side-remarks and potential issues interleaved. This makes it challenging for the reader to grasp the final algorithm and its objective function cleanly. The clarity could be significantly improved by first presenting the final model and optimization problem, and then detailing the derivation steps. Several notational issues also hinder readability (see detailed comments).

2.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** The empirical evaluation is confined to a single baseline: a projected gradient ascent method (K-Ascent) that the paper itself argues is flawed. While this comparison is useful for demonstrating the superiority of the EM optimization strategy, it does not situate the work in the broader context of subset selection. It remains unclear how the learned DPP model compares against other, non-DPP based methods for diverse recommendation or subset selection (e.g., submodular maximization approaches). This makes it difficult to assess the practical benefit of using this complex DPP learning method over simpler alternatives.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity of Presentation:** I strongly recommend restructuring the paper to improve clarity. A better structure might be: (1) Define the DPP and the log-likelihood objective. (2) Present the final EM algorithm and the lower-bound objective function (F(q, V, Λ)). (3) Then, provide the detailed derivation of the E-step and M-step, including the justification for the lower bound. This would give the reader a clear picture of the destination before taking them through the derivational journey.

*   **Notation:** Some notation is used before it is formally defined, which can be confusing.
    *   In Section 2, the marginal kernel K is mentioned before being defined in relation to L. It would be clearer to introduce L first, then define K based on it.
    *   The eigenvectors `v_j` are used in Equation (1), but the full eigenvector matrix `V` is not formally defined until Section 3.2.

*   **Self-Containment:** The M-step update for the eigenvectors relies on an algorithm for optimization on a Stiefel manifold from [26]. Rather than just citing the reference, it would greatly benefit the reader if the paper briefly outlined the simplified update rule used (Equation 21) and provided a short intuition for why this form maintains the orthonormality constraint. This would make the paper more self-contained.

*   **Initialization Rationale:** In Section 4, the moments-matching initialization in Equation (23) is interesting. Could the authors provide more rationale for this specific form? For example, why is this formulation chosen over simply using the empirical covariance of the data as the initial kernel matrix?

*   **Projection Step Explanation:** The description of the projection step in Section 3.1, "chooses the closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix," is a helpful clarification and should be retained.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and technically impressive algorithm for a difficult and important problem. The main weaknesses are the clarity of the presentation and the limited scope of the experimental comparison. I believe the paper is a strong candidate for acceptance if the authors can substantially improve the clarity and organization in a revised version.
