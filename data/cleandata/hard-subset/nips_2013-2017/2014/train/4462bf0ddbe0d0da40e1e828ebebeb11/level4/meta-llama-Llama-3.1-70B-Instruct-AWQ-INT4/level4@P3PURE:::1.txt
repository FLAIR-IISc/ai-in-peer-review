This paper presents a novel approach to learning kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The primary contribution of the paper lies in reformulating the objective function, which outperforms the standard method of differentiating the log probability with respect to kernel parameters and applying projected gradient ascent (referred to as K-Ascent, or KA, in the paper). By leveraging an EM framework, the authors represent any DPP as a mixture over "elementary DPPs," where each subset of elements corresponds to a single elementary DPP. A variational distribution is introduced over these subsets, and the algorithm alternates between updating the distribution over the subsets (represented as a k-DPP) and taking single gradient steps in the M-step. 

The experimental results demonstrate that the EM method significantly outperforms the KA method when the latter is initialized naively, and yields moderate improvements in certain settings when KA is initialized more intelligently.

Overall, I find the proposed approach interesting, and it appears that the EM method offers some improvement over the standard method. Although the M-step is relatively complex, and the necessary caveats to make it work are somewhat unsatisfying, the authors' effort to make it functional is commendable.

However, I would have liked to see more analysis on the effect of initialization on the EM algorithm. For instance, would initializing the EM algorithm with the result of KA lead to improved solutions? This additional analysis would provide further insights into the strengths and limitations of the proposed method.

Furthermore, I am perplexed by the use of relative log likelihood as a performance metric. Log probability differences reflect ratios of probabilities, so it is unclear why the authors take ratios of log probabilities instead of simply reporting differences in log probabilities. This makes it challenging to interpret the reported results.

On a minor note, I find certain aspects of the writing style to be distracting. I suggest that the authors avoid describing their own algorithm as "elegant," particularly given the caveats and approximations required in the M-step. Additionally, I believe that bolding text in the introduction is unnecessary.

In conclusion, the paper presents an interesting new approach to learning kernels in DPPs. While the experiments could be improved, and the improvements over the basic projected gradient algorithm are not substantial, the paper is generally well-written and contributes to the field.
