Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) define distributions over subsets of a fixed ground set that favor diversity. They are parameterized by a positive semidefinite kernel L. Because maximizing the likelihood to learn the full L is NP-hard, prior work has focused on restricted settings, such as learning a single scalar weight per row or imposing parametric forms on L. This paper proposes a learning approach that does not constrain L’s parameterization. The method avoids the projection step required by naive gradient ascent—whose projections can degrade similarity structure and thereby harm diversity—by reparameterizing in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors and applying an EM scheme. The authors develop optimization updates that bypass explicit projections: projected gradient ascent would otherwise project both eigenvalues and eigenvectors, whereas here, by exploiting that V is full-rank and optimizing on a Stiefel manifold, eigenvector projection is avoided. Jensen’s inequality is used to lower-bound the log-likelihood and derive the EM procedure.

The approach is valuable because it maintains the diversity-seeking behavior of DPPs, unlike prior work that emphasizes only item quality. The step-by-step EM derivation is clear and instructive. Mapping the orthogonality constraint to an optimization over the Stiefel manifold to eliminate eigenvector projection, and showing that the relevant conditional distribution over latent indices is itself a DPP, are solid contributions. The method is evaluated on both synthetic data and a product recommendation application, and the experiments substantiate the claim that the approach better preserves both quality and diversity than KA (projected gradient on K). For example, even when only two of the top-10 products differ under KA—being replaced by less plausible alternatives—the example effectively illustrates the paper’s central point. The conclusion feels somewhat brief and does not fully highlight all contributions, but overall the paper is cohesive. The proofs are well presented, and the reduction to an EM algorithm is engaging.

The paper is clearly written and the proofs are well organized. The EM reduction is well explained. Additional experiments on real-world datasets would further demonstrate the practical importance of the method’s diversity benefits relative to competing approaches.
