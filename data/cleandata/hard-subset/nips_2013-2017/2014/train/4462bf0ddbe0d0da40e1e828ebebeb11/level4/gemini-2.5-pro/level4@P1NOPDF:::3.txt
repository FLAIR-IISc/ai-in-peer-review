This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix L for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP). DPPs are probabilistic models over subsets of a ground set that naturally favor diversity, a property encoded in the positive semidefinite matrix L. As learning the full matrix L is NP-hard, prior work has typically resorted to simplified approaches, such as learning a restricted parametric form of L or learning only a scalar quality vector, which compromises the model's ability to capture diversity.

The authors propose a novel learning method that does not impose restrictions on the parameterization of L. A key insight is to avoid the projection step required by standard gradient ascent methods. Such projection steps can be problematic, as they risk compromising the diversity properties of the learned model. To circumvent this, the authors develop an elegant Expectation-Maximization (EM) procedure based on the eigendecomposition of L. The algorithm optimizes the eigenvalues and eigenvectors separately. The projection of eigenvectors is cleverly avoided by reformulating the constraint as an optimization problem on the Stiefel manifold. The EM framework is constructed by using Jensen's inequality to derive a tractable lower bound on the objective function.

The paper's primary strength lies in its principled approach to learning a full, unconstrained DPP kernel, successfully preserving both the quality and diversity aspects of the model. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is insightful and clearly presented. The theoretical contributions are solid, particularly the formulation of the eigenvector update as an optimization on the Stiefel manifold and the proof showing that the inverse distribution is also a DPP. The paper is cohesive and the proofs are well-written.

The authors validate their method on both synthetic data and a real-world product recommendation task. The experiments successfully support the central claim that the proposed algorithm maintains both quality and diversity. While the improvements in the product recommendation task are modest (replacing two of the top ten products compared to the baseline), the results nonetheless validate the authors' central claim.

A few minor points could further strengthen the paper. The conclusion feels somewhat brief and could be enhanced by more explicitly summarizing the paper's novel contributions. To better illustrate the practical impact of the proposed algorithm, the authors could consider including experiments on a broader range of real-world datasets. This would provide a more comprehensive demonstration of the benefits of modeling diversity compared to competing approaches. Overall, this is a well-executed paper with interesting and valuable contributions.
