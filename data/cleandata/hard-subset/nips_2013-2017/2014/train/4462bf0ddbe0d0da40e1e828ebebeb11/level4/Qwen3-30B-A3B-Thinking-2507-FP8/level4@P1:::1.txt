

This paper addresses the problem of learning the kernel matrix for determinantal point processes (DPPs), which are probabilistic models for subset selection tasks that balance item quality and diversity. The authors propose an expectation-maximization (EM) approach to learn the full kernel matrix, contrasting with previous work that either restricts learning to single row weights [2] or learns weights for linear combinations of DPPs with fixed kernels [3]. The key insight is recognizing that any DPP can be represented as a mixture of "elementary DPPs," where each elementary DPP corresponds to a subset of eigenvectors. The authors develop an EM algorithm that alternates between updating a variational distribution over these subsets (represented as a k-DPP) and performing gradient steps in the M-step. The approach avoids the problematic projection steps required by the baseline K-Ascent (KA) method, which uses projected gradient ascent on kernel entries.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution by providing a novel optimization framework that leverages the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix. The theoretical analysis is clear, with appropriate connections to prior work on DPPs [1, 8, 11-15, 20-21]. The authors demonstrate that their EM algorithm consistently outperforms KA when initialized naively (Wishart initialization), achieving up to 16.5% relative gains in test log-likelihood. When KA is initialized with moment-matching (a more sophisticated initialization), the EM method still shows modest but consistent improvements, particularly in data-poor settings. The runtime analysis shows that EM has better asymptotic complexity than KA, and the empirical results confirm that EM is approximately 2.1 times faster on average.

The paper is well-written with good organization, though some sections could be clearer. The introduction effectively motivates DPPs for subset selection tasks, and the background section provides sufficient mathematical foundations. The experimental setup is appropriate for the problem, using real-world baby registry data to test the approach on a practical recommendation task. The authors provide a thorough analysis of how the EM method performs relative to KA under different initialization schemes, and they thoughtfully investigate why KA struggles with projection steps.

However, the paper has a few notable weaknesses. The most significant is the use of relative log-likelihood differences as the primary performance metric, which is confusing and difficult to interpret. The authors report "relative gains" as ratios of log-likelihood differences, but it's unclear why this metric is preferred over absolute differences in log-likelihood. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how these relative gains translate to practical improvements.

The M-step of the EM algorithm involves several non-trivial approximations and caveats that slightly undermine the elegance of the approach. While the authors acknowledge these limitations, the paper could better address why these approximations are necessary and whether they could be avoided with alternative approaches. The paper's claim that the algorithm is "elegant" seems premature given these caveats.

The experimental section could be strengthened by exploring additional initialization strategies. For instance, it would be valuable to see how the EM algorithm performs when initialized with the result of KA, which would provide insight into whether EM can refine KA solutions. The paper's analysis of initialization effects is somewhat limited, particularly regarding how the quality of the initial kernel affects the final solution.

The paper demonstrates significant technical merit and addresses an important problem in DPP learning. The proposed method is theoretically grounded, empirically validated, and has clear practical implications for applications requiring subset diversity. While the improvements over the baseline KA method are not dramatic in all settings, they are consistent and meaningful, particularly in challenging scenarios where the initial kernel is poor.

In terms of significance, this work advances the state of the art in DPP learning by enabling unconstrained kernel learning without sacrificing computational efficiency. The authors' approach could be applied to other domains requiring diversity-aware subset selection, such as document summarization, sensor placement, or image search. The paper's focus on learning the full kernel matrix represents a meaningful step beyond previous restricted learning approaches.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses an important gap in DPP learning. The technical content is sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the approach has clear potential for broader application. While the improvements over the baseline are modest in some settings, the paper provides a principled approach to a challenging optimization problem with clear theoretical and practical benefits. The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it makes a scientifically rigorous contribution to an important area of machine learning research.
