Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) define a distribution over a fixed ground set, assigning higher probability to diverse subsets. DPPs are parameterized by a positive semi-definite matrix (L). Learning L is conjectured to be NP-hard, leading prior work to focus on partial learning (e.g., learning a scalar weight for each row of L) or restricting the parameterization of L. This paper proposes a novel learning method that avoids these restrictions. The authors argue that naive gradient ascent requires a projection step that can compromise the diversity-promoting properties of DPPs, and demonstrate that an Expectation-Maximization (EM) approach, operating on the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of L, overcomes this disadvantage. This EM formulation avoids the need for problematic projections and exploits the eigendecomposition to achieve a more effective optimization procedure. They detail optimization algorithms for this approach, and leverage the fact that the eigenvector matrix has full rank to further simplify computations. Jensen’s inequality is used to lower-bound the objective function and construct the EM procedure.

The proposed learning method is valuable because it preserves the diversity property of DPPs, a characteristic often overlooked in previous work that primarily focused on maximizing the quality of selected items. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is insightful, and the mapping of the constraint to an optimization over the Stiefel manifold (to avoid eigenvector projection) is a strong contribution. Demonstrating that the resulting inverse distribution is itself a DPP is also a significant result. The authors test their learning algorithm on a product recommendation task, comparing it to projected gradient ascent (KA). The experimental results support their claims; the method effectively retains both the quality and diversity properties of the DPP. While the differences in recommendations between the EM approach and KA are sometimes subtle (e.g., replacing only two of the top ten recommended products), the results highlight the contribution of the paper and substantiate the initial claim regarding the importance of diversity. The conclusion could be strengthened by more fully summarizing the paper's contributions, but overall the work is presented cohesively. The proofs are well-written, and the step-by-step reduction to the EM formulation is well-explained. 

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical derivations are presented logically and are easy to follow. While the experiments are compelling, exploring the method on a wider range of real-world datasets could further illustrate the importance of the diversity captured by this algorithm compared to alternative approaches.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed EM algorithm offers a new approach to learning DPPs without the restrictive assumptions or projection issues of previous methods.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivations are rigorous, the use of the Stiefel manifold is justified, and the algorithm builds on established principles (EM, eigendecomposition).
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the mathematical details are clearly presented.
*   **Empirical Support:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms a reasonable baseline (KA) in a relevant application.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Modest Gains:** While the results are positive, the gains over the baseline are not always dramatic.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** Additional experiments on diverse datasets would strengthen the claims about the general applicability of the method.
