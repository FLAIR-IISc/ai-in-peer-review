This paper proposes an EM algorithm for learning full determinantal point process (DPP) kernels. The key idea is to reparameterize the kernel via its eigendecomposition and introduce a latent index set over eigenvectors, exploiting the representation of a DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs. The log-likelihood is lower-bounded using Jensen’s inequality, yielding an EM procedure in which (i) the E-step computes a conditional k-DPP over latent eigenvector subsets, and (ii) the M-step updates eigenvalues in closed form and takes a gradient step for eigenvectors with orthogonality preserved via a Stiefel-manifold update. This approach avoids the PSD projection step required by projected gradient ascent on K (“K-Ascent,” KA), which the authors argue can trap learning in near-diagonal solutions. The method is asymptotically faster than KA and, on a baby registry recommendation dataset, improves test log-likelihood by up to 16.5% and is about 2× faster, with larger gains when off-diagonal structure (negative interactions) is important.

Relation to prior work: Prior DPP learning often restricts the model to convex subproblems, such as learning per-item quality weights [Kulesza & Taskar] or mixtures of fixed kernels [Affandi et al.], which sacrifice expressivity or DPP structure. This paper tackles full-kernel learning without restrictive parameterizations, combining known DPP properties (elementary mixtures, k-DPPs, eigendecomposition) with an EM lower bound and Stiefel updates.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound and the derivations are plausible. The E-step as a conditional k-DPP and the eigenvalue update are well motivated, and the Stiefel update is a reasonable choice to maintain orthogonality. The critique of KA’s projection-induced degeneracy is convincing and supported empirically. However, the experimental evaluation is narrow: it compares only to KA on a single application/dataset and primarily on test log-likelihood. Important baselines are missing—e.g., convex per-item weight learning, mixtures of fixed kernels, or non-DPP subset selection methods (facility location/submodular objectives). Additional analyses (ablation on EM components, sensitivity to initialization, robustness across domains) would strengthen the claims.

Clarity: The exposition could be significantly improved. Definitions (e.g., marginal kernel K, the matrix V) are sometimes used before being introduced; notation is occasionally undefined or introduced late. The narrative interleaves derivation, side-remarks, and alternatives in a way that obscures the main thread. A clearer structure would present: (1) DPP basics and the mixture view; (2) the EM lower bound; (3) E-step as a k-DPP; (4) M-step updates, with a self-contained description of the Stiefel-manifold update used in practice; (5) complexity. Where possible, include brief derivations or algorithmic steps inline so the reader need not consult external references to follow the main method.

Originality: The combination of an EM lower bound over latent eigenvector subsets with eigenvalue/eigenvector updates for full DPP kernel learning appears novel. I am not aware of prior work that learns the full kernel via an EM scheme leveraging k-DPP conditionals and Stiefel optimization.

Significance: Enabling full-kernel learning for DPPs without restrictive parameterizations is valuable, as it better captures negative interactions central to diversity modeling. The empirical results suggest improved robustness and speed over KA. The broader impact would be clearer with comparisons to stronger baselines and additional domains.

Pros:
- Conceptually elegant and technically solid EM formulation leveraging DPP structure.
- Avoids PSD projections that can cause degeneracy in KA; maintains orthogonality via principled updates.
- Asymptotically and empirically faster; more robust to initialization in challenging regimes.
- Clear practical motivation (recommendation) and insightful analysis of when gains are largest (strong off-diagonal structure).

Cons:
- Limited experimental scope and baselines; no comparison to other DPP learning schemes or non-DPP subset selection methods.
- Presentation issues (missing definitions, meandering exposition) hinder readability.
- Evaluation focuses on test log-likelihood; additional metrics (e.g., MAP set quality, diversity/coverage) and qualitative analyses across datasets would help.
- Some implementation details (e.g., step-size selection, handling λ near 1) deserve clearer discussion.

Detailed comments:
- p.2, l.81: Define the marginal kernel K before first use, and clearly state the conditions (PSD with eigenvalues ≤ 1) and its relationship to L via K = L(L+I)−1.
- p.2, l.100: Define V explicitly as the matrix of eigenvectors of K (K = VΛVᵀ).
- p.3, l.159: “minimizes the change to Frobenius norm” is unclear. Prefer “chooses the closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix to Q.”
- p.4: The Stiefel-manifold update is central. Provide a concise, self-contained outline of the specific multiplicative update used (and its rationale) rather than relying solely on [26].
- p.4, l.189: Clarify what “weight” refers to in the mixture representation (the Bernoulli product over eigenvalue-dependent inclusion probabilities).
- p.7, l.324: Please justify the moment-matching initialization in Eq. (30). Why is m_ij not used directly? Would initializing K with the empirical covariance (projected to [0,1] eigenvalues) fail, and if so, why? A brief comparison or explanation would help.

Overall: A solid and likely original contribution with promising technical ideas; acceptance would be stronger with clearer writing and broader empirical comparisons.
