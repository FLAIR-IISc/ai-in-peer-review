This paper presents an innovative approach to learning kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs) by reformulating the objective within an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework. The main contribution of the paper is an improvement over the standard method of differentiating the log probability with respect to kernel parameters and performing projected gradient ascent, referred to as K-Ascent (KA) in the paper. The EM approach works by representing any DPP as a mixture over "elementary DPPs," where each subset of elements corresponds to one elementary DPP. A variational distribution is introduced over these subsets, and the algorithm alternates between updating the distribution over the subsets, represented as a k-DPP, and taking single gradient steps in the M-step.

The experimental results demonstrate that the EM method outperforms the KA method, particularly when the latter is initialized naively. Even with more intelligent initialization, the EM method shows slight improvements in certain settings. Overall, the approach is intriguing, and the EM method appears to yield some improvement. However, the M-step is quite involved, and the necessary caveats to make it work are somewhat unsatisfying.

One area for improvement is in the experimental analysis, specifically regarding the effect of initialization on the EM algorithm. For instance, it would be interesting to investigate whether initializing the EM algorithm with the result of KA would lead to further improvement over the solution. Additionally, the use of relative log likelihood as a performance metric is unclear. Log probability differences reflect ratios of probabilities, making it unclear why the authors choose to take ratios of log probabilities. A more straightforward approach would be to report differences in log probabilities, which would be easier to interpret.

Furthermore, some minor adjustments to the writing style would enhance the overall quality of the paper. It would be preferable for the authors to avoid describing their own algorithm as "elegant," particularly given the caveats and approximations required in the M-step. Moreover, bolding text in the introduction is unnecessary. Overall, the paper presents an interesting new approach for learning kernels in DPPs. While the experiments could be improved, and the improvements over the basic projected gradient algorithm are not substantial, the paper is well-written and contributes to the field.
