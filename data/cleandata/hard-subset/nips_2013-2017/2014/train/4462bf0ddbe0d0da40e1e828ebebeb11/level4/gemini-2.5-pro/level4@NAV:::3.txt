Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) are probabilistic models defined over a ground set, which inherently favor diverse subsets. These processes are parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix, denoted as L. The NP-hard nature of learning L has led prior work to focus on partial learning, often by imposing restrictive parametric forms on L, such as a scalar vector, which compromises the model's ability to capture diversity. This paper introduces a novel learning method that does not constrain the parameterization of L. A key advantage of the proposed approach is that it circumvents the projection step required by methods like projected gradient ascent, which can degrade both the similarity and diversity properties of the learned model. The authors adeptly overcome this limitation by formulating an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm over the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of L. They cleverly avoid the projection of eigenvectors by leveraging the full-rank property of the eigenvector matrix V. The EM procedure is formally derived by establishing a lower bound on the objective function using Jensen's inequality.

The proposed learning method is significant because it successfully preserves the crucial diversity property of DPPs, in contrast to previous approaches that have primarily concentrated on the quality aspect. The step-by-step derivation of the EM algorithm is particularly insightful and easy to follow. Notable technical contributions include mapping the eigenvector orthonormality constraint to an optimization problem on the Stiefel manifold, which elegantly eliminates the need for projection, and the proof that the inverse distribution is also a DPP. The authors validate their algorithm on both synthetic data and a real-world product recommendation task. The experimental results substantiate the paper's central claim that the method maintains both quality and diversity, as demonstrated by the comparison against the KA baseline. While the observed improvement in the recommendation task (replacing two of the top 10 products with more diverse, albeit less likely, items) may seem modest, it effectively highlights the paper's core contribution. The conclusion feels somewhat rushed and could better summarize the paper's contributions; however, the overall manuscript is cohesive, with well-written proofs and an engaging explanation of the reduction from the optimization problem to the EM framework.

The paper is coherently written, and the proofs are presented with clarity. The detailed derivation of the EM algorithm is a highlight and is explained effectively. To further solidify the claims and demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method, the work could be strengthened by including more extensive experiments on a wider range of real-world datasets. Such experiments would better illustrate the tangible benefits of the enhanced diversity captured by this algorithm when benchmarked against other state-of-the-art methods in the field.
