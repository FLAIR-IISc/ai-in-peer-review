Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) define a distribution over a fixed ground set, favoring diverse subsets. DPPs are parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix, L; however, learning L is NP-hard, leading prior work to focus on partial learning or restrictive parameterizations. Learning a low-rank approximation for L, for instance, can diminish the diversity captured by the DPP. This paper presents a learning method for L without such parametric restrictions. A key advantage of this approach is that it avoids the projection steps inherent in gradient ascent methods, which can degrade the similarity properties and, consequently, the diversity of the DPP. The authors overcome this limitation by employing an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm operating directly on the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of L. They explore optimization algorithms that obviate the need for these projections. Notably, projected gradient ascent would require projection of both eigenvalues *and* eigenvectors, while leveraging the full-rank property of the eigenvector matrix allows them to avoid eigenvector projection. Jensen’s inequality is utilized to lower bound the objective function, enabling the construction of the EM procedure.

This learning method is valuable because it preserves the diversity property of DPPs, a characteristic often sacrificed in previous work that prioritizes only the “quality” of the selected subsets. The detailed, step-by-step derivation of the EM algorithm is particularly insightful. Furthermore, mapping the constraints to an optimization problem over the Stiefel manifold to eliminate eigenvector projection and demonstrating that the inverse distribution is itself a DPP represent significant contributions. The algorithm is evaluated on both synthetic datasets and a product recommendation task. The experimental results support the paper’s claims, demonstrating that the learned DPP retains both quality and diversity. For example, comparison with the KA algorithm shows it replaces only a small number of top products with less likely recommendations – a result that effectively highlights the contribution of the paper and substantiates the initial claims. While the conclusion could more thoroughly summarize the paper’s contributions, the overall presentation is cohesive. The proofs are well-written, and the reduction of the optimization problem to an EM formulation is notably interesting.

The paper is well-written and the proofs are clear. The step-by-step reduction to EM is presented in a compelling and accessible manner. Additional experiments on larger, real-world datasets could further illustrate the importance of the diversity promoted by this algorithm compared to alternative approaches in this area.
