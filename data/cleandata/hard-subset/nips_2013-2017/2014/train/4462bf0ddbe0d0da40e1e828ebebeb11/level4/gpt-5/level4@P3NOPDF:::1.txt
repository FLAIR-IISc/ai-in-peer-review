Summary
The paper studies learning kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The central idea is to recast maximum likelihood learning in an EM framework, leveraging the fact that a DPP can be written as a mixture over “elementary DPPs” indexed by subsets. The method introduces a variational distribution over these subsets, represented as a k-DPP, and alternates between updating this distribution (E-step) and performing a constrained update to the kernel parameters (M-step). Empirically, the EM-based approach outperforms a baseline that directly ascends the log-likelihood with projected gradients (K-Ascent, KA), especially under naive initialization, and shows modest gains under stronger initializations.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: Reformulating DPP learning via an EM procedure is a neat perspective that connects mixture representations with tractable variational approximations (k-DPPs).
- Empirical signal: Consistent performance improvements over KA, with particularly large gains when KA is naively initialized.
- Practicality: The approach appears implementable with standard DPP primitives and could be useful for practitioners seeking more stable learning than vanilla projected gradient ascent.

Weaknesses and concerns
- M-step complexity and approximations: The M-step is fairly involved and seems to rely on caveats/approximations that make the overall “elegance” and theoretical guarantees of EM less clear. It is not obvious under what conditions monotonic improvement of the (marginal) log-likelihood is preserved.
- Sensitivity to initialization: While results compare KA under different initializations, the sensitivity of EM itself to initialization is not fully explored.
- Metric choice: The use of “relative log-likelihood” is not clearly motivated or defined, and is difficult to interpret. Ratios of log-likelihoods are unconventional; typically one compares log-likelihood differences or per-datapoint averages.
- Scope of empirical analysis: It is unclear how robust the gains are across datasets, hyperparameters (e.g., k-DPP parameters), and computational budgets. Runtime comparisons and convergence diagnostics are limited.

Requests for clarification
- EM objective and guarantees: Please state explicitly the surrogate optimized in the E-step and the M-step, and whether each step provably increases a lower bound on the log-likelihood. If not, characterize when monotonic improvement is expected or observed.
- Definition of relative log-likelihood: Provide a precise formula. If it is log p_model − log p_baseline, call it “log-likelihood difference.” If it is a ratio such as log p_model / log p_baseline, please justify why this normalization is meaningful and how to interpret its magnitude.
- Computational cost: Compare per-iteration and total wall-clock time of EM vs KA, including eigendecomposition and sampling costs. Are the improvements due to better updates or simply more computation per iteration?

Suggestions for improvement
- Initialization study:
  - Initialize EM from the final KA solution and report whether EM further improves the objective and test performance.
  - Conversely, initialize KA from the EM solution to gauge complementary strengths.
  - Report multiple random restarts and variance to assess robustness.
- Ablations:
  - Study the effect of the k-DPP variational family and its parameters on performance and convergence.
  - Evaluate the impact of the M-step approximations and step sizes; show sensitivity curves.
- Convergence diagnostics:
  - Plot objective values (true log-likelihood if available, or the EM lower bound) across iterations for both methods, with confidence intervals.
  - Report stopping criteria and the number of iterations to convergence.
- Evaluation metrics:
  - Prefer reporting log-likelihood differences (and/or per-instance averages) rather than ratios of log-likelihoods. Consider additional downstream metrics relevant to DPP applications (e.g., diversity/coverage).
- Practical guidance:
  - Provide recommendations on choosing initialization, step sizes, and regularization to help practitioners reproduce gains.

Writing and presentation
- Tone: Avoid self-descriptors like “elegant,” especially when the method relies on approximations that complicate guarantees. Let the evidence speak for itself.
- Formatting: The extensive bolding in the introduction distracts from the content; reserve emphasis for key definitions or results.
- Clarity: Briefly recap the mixture representation of DPPs and how the k-DPP variational family approximates the posterior over subsets. A compact schematic of the EM loop (E-step update rule, M-step update rule, constraints) would help.
- Limitations: Be more explicit about the limitations introduced by the approximations in the M-step and any known failure modes (e.g., sensitivity to hyperparameters or datasets).

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and practically relevant contribution to DPP learning. The EM reformulation is well-motivated and yields tangible improvements over projected gradient ascent, particularly from weaker initializations. However, the theoretical underpinnings of the approximate EM steps and the interpretability of the reported metric need clarification, and the empirical section would benefit from a deeper analysis of initialization, sensitivity, and runtime. With these improvements, the paper would provide both stronger evidence and clearer guidance for using EM to learn DPP kernels. I lean toward acceptance.
