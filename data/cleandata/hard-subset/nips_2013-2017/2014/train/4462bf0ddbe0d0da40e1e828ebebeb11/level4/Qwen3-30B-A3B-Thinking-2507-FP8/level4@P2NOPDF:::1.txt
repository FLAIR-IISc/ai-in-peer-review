

This paper addresses the problem of learning kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs) via a novel reformulation within an expectation-maximization (EM) framework. The core contribution is a method that significantly outperforms the standard K-Ascent (KA) baseline—where kernel parameters are optimized via projected gradient ascent on the log-probability—particularly when KA initialization is suboptimal. The authors observe that any DPP can be expressed as a mixture over "elementary DPPs" (one per subset of elements), and propose a variational approach where the mixture weights are represented as a k-DPP. The algorithm alternates between updating this variational distribution (E-step) and performing single gradient steps on the kernel (M-step). Empirical results demonstrate clear gains for the EM method over KA under naive initialization, with modest but consistent improvements when KA is initialized more effectively.  

The approach is theoretically sound and offers a compelling alternative to gradient-based optimization for DPP kernel learning. However, the M-step involves non-trivial approximations to handle the intractable mixture over subsets, which somewhat undermines the claimed elegance of the method. The authors acknowledge these approximations as caveats, but the reliance on them feels like a pragmatic concession rather than a clean solution.  

Experimentally, the paper would benefit from deeper analysis of initialization effects. Crucially, it does not investigate whether initializing the EM algorithm with the solution from KA (rather than random initialization) yields further improvements—a gap that weakens the comparison to the baseline. Additionally, the use of "relative log likelihood" as a metric is problematic. Log-probability differences inherently reflect ratios of probabilities, so reporting ratios of log probabilities (e.g., log(P₁/P₂)) is counterintuitive; absolute differences (log P₁ - log P₂) would be more interpretable and standard. The current metric obscures the practical significance of the reported gains.  

The writing contains minor stylistic issues that distract from the technical contribution. The authors’ self-description of the method as "elegant" is premature given the M-step’s approximations, and the bolded text in the introduction is unnecessary and unprofessional. These choices detract from the paper’s credibility.  

Overall, the paper presents a novel and useful approach to DPP kernel learning with solid empirical validation. The EM reformulation provides a meaningful improvement over the standard KA method in practice, particularly in initialization-sensitive scenarios. While the gains over KA are not revolutionary (they are modest when KA is well-initialized), the method is robust and well-motivated. The core idea—leveraging the mixture representation of DPPs within EM—is interesting and could inspire further work in probabilistic modeling.  

The paper would be strengthened by:  
1. Replacing "relative log likelihood" with standard log-probability differences for clearer interpretation.  
2. Including experiments that initialize EM with KA’s solution to assess synergy between the methods.  
3. Softening the claim of "elegance" to reflect the M-step’s approximations.  
4. Removing stylistic distractions like bolded text.  

The technical contribution is valid, the experiments are well-executed, and the method is practically useful. The weaknesses are minor and fixable, but they prevent the paper from being outstanding. It is a solid contribution to the field, suitable for acceptance with minor revisions.
