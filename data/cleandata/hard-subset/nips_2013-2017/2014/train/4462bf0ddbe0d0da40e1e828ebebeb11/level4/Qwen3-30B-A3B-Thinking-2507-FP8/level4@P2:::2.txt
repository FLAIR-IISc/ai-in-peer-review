

This paper proposes an EM algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a key limitation in prior work. Previous approaches had focused on restricted learning settings: learning scalar weights for each row of the kernel matrix [2] or learning weights for linear combinations of DPPs with fixed kernel matrices [3]. The authors recognize that learning the full kernel matrix is conjectured to be NP-hard [1], and they present a novel approach that avoids the problematic projection steps required in naive gradient ascent methods. By reparameterizing the kernel using its eigendecomposition and applying Jensen's inequality to lower-bound the likelihood, they develop an effective optimization procedure. The method is tested on a real-world product recommendation task, showing up to 16.5% relative gains in test log-likelihood compared to projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent).

Quality: The technical approach is well-founded and appears sound, with clear theoretical motivation. The EM framework elegantly avoids the projection issues that plague gradient ascent methods, as demonstrated by the experiments. However, the experimental evaluation is limited in scope. While the comparison against K-Ascent is useful, the paper fails to compare against other subset selection methods mentioned in the introduction (e.g., submodular optimization approaches [4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9]). This omission makes it difficult to assess whether the DPP approach itself provides significant advantages over alternative methods for subset selection. Additionally, the analysis of why EM outperforms K-Ascent (particularly regarding the projection step) would be strengthened by more detailed experiments on how the learned kernels differ between the methods.

Clarity: The paper's presentation could be significantly improved. The writing is often convoluted, with the main method presented alongside discussions of alternative approaches and potential pitfalls. This makes it challenging to follow the core contribution. Several notational issues further reduce clarity: the marginal kernel K is used before its definition on page 2, line 81; V is not defined when first used on page 2, line 100; and the phrase "minimises the change to Frobenius norm" (page 3, line 159) is unclear and should be rephrased as "chooses the closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix to Q." The section on the Stiefel manifold update (page 4) references [26] without providing a clear explanation of the simplified approach used, which would be helpful for readers. A more straightforward presentation of the algorithm and its key innovations would greatly enhance the paper's readability.

Originality: The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to DPP learning. The combination of eigendecomposition, EM optimization, and Stiefel manifold updates for learning the full kernel matrix appears to be original, as the authors correctly identify and distinguish their work from prior restricted learning settings. The method represents a significant technical advance over previous approaches that could not learn unconstrained kernel matrices.

Significance: The method offers practical advantages for DPP learning, with demonstrated improvements in both performance (up to 16.5% relative gain in test log-likelihood) and efficiency (asymptotically faster runtime). The application to product recommendation is well-motivated, as DPPs are well-suited for modeling the diversity-quality tradeoff in subset selection. However, the significance would be stronger if the paper more clearly articulated how this approach advances the broader field of subset selection beyond just DPPs. The experiments are limited to DPP-based methods, making it difficult to assess the method's value relative to non-DPP approaches.

Strengths: The paper makes a technically sound contribution to DPP learning with a well-motivated EM algorithm that overcomes limitations of prior methods. The experiments demonstrate clear advantages over the naive K-Ascent baseline. The theoretical development is solid, and the method has clear practical benefits.

Weaknesses: The experimental evaluation is limited to comparisons within DPP methods, omitting comparisons to other subset selection approaches. The writing is often unclear, with poor notation and convoluted presentation. The paper would benefit from a more focused presentation of the core method before discussing alternatives.

Overall, this paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to learning DPPs that addresses a significant limitation in prior work. While the method appears to be a solid contribution to the DPP literature, the paper would benefit from a clearer presentation, more comprehensive experimental comparisons, and a stronger articulation of its significance within the broader context of subset selection. The technical contribution is valuable, but the current presentation and evaluation limit its impact.
