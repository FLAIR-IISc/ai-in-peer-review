Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) are distributions over a fixed ground set that assign higher probability to diverse sets. DPPs can be parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix (L). However, learning L is NP-hard, which has limited previous work to partial learning of L. Learning a scalar vector for L compromises on the diversity dimension of DPP. Previous work has resorted to restricting the parametric form of L. In this paper, the authors propose a learning method that does not restrict the parameterization of L. The learning method does not require a projection step, as in gradient ascent, which can lead to the similarity property being compromised and the diversity property being lost. Using Expectation-Maximization (EM) on eigenvalues and eigenvectors overcomes this disadvantage.

The authors explore optimization algorithms to solve this problem without needing to project values. Using projected gradient ascent requires projection of both eigenvalues and eigenvectors. However, exploiting the fact that V is full-rank, they avoid the projection of eigenvectors. Jensen's inequality is used to lower bound the objective function and construct an EM procedure.

The learning method is useful as it preserves the diversity property of the DPP, as opposed to other previous work where only the quality property has been focused upon. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is insightful. Mapping the constraint to an optimization over the Stiefel manifold to eliminate the projection of eigenvectors and showing that the inverse distribution is a DPP are solid contributions.

The authors test the learning algorithm on a product recommendation task and compare it to the K-Ascent (KA) algorithm. The experiments support the claim in the paper – the learning method retains the quality and diversity of the DPP. While the results show that the EM algorithm outperforms KA in some cases, it would be beneficial to see more experiments on real-world datasets to further illustrate the importance of the "diversity" produced by this algorithm when compared to others in this area.

The paper is written cohesively, and the proofs are well-written. The step-by-step reduction to EM is interesting and well-explained. However, the conclusion could be improved by highlighting all the contributions of this paper. Overall, the paper presents a novel learning method for DPPs that preserves the diversity property, making it a valuable contribution to the field.
