

The paper introduces a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a key limitation in prior work. Previous approaches either restricted learning to row weights [2] or linear combinations of fixed kernel matrices [3], while naive gradient ascent (K-Ascent) requires problematic projections that often yield degenerate, near-diagonal kernels. The authors instead leverage the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K, reformulating the likelihood as a mixture of elementary DPPs and applying Jensen’s inequality to derive a tractable lower bound. This avoids projection steps, enables robust optimization via Stiefel manifold updates, and achieves better test log-likelihoods (up to 16.5% gain) on a real-world product recommendation task.  

**Quality.** The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. The EM derivation correctly handles the hidden variable J (via DPP mixture representation), and the eigenvalue update (Equation 18) is self-normalizing, eliminating the need for projection constraints. The runtime analysis (O(T₁nNk² + T₁T₂N³)) is thorough and aligns with empirical observations. However, the evaluation lacks critical context: the experiments compare only to K-Ascent, not to standard subset selection methods (e.g., greedy maximization of submodular functions [28] or non-DPP approaches like [4,5]). Without such comparisons, it remains unclear whether DPPs *themselves* provide a meaningful advantage over simpler techniques for the recommendation task.  

**Clarity.** The writing suffers from inconsistent notation and structural disorganization. Key definitions are introduced after usage:  
- The marginal kernel K (p.2, l.81) is used before its definition.  
- V (p.2, l.100) is referenced without prior introduction.  
- The "weight" in p.4, l.189 is ambiguous (does it refer to eigenvalues? marginal probabilities?).  
The exposition interleaves method development with side discussions (e.g., comparing to [3] during the EM derivation), which impedes readability. For instance, the Stiefel manifold update (p.4) cites [26] without simplifying the approach—directly outlining the multiplicative update (Equation 21) would be more helpful. The phrase "minimises the change to Frobenius norm" (p.3, l.159) is imprecise; it should state "chooses the closest (in Frobenius norm) PSD matrix to Q."  

**Originality.** The work is highly original. To the best of my knowledge, no prior NIPS/ICML paper has proposed an EM-based method for unconstrained DPP kernel learning. While [8, 11] use Bayesian approaches for parametric kernels, and [2, 3] restrict to convex settings, this paper’s use of eigendecomposition to avoid projections and its EM framework represent a significant departure from existing methods. The mixture-of-elementary-DPPs perspective (Equation 7) is also novel for learning.  

**Significance.** The method advances DPP learning by enabling full kernel optimization without restrictive assumptions, which is critical for capturing negative item interactions (e.g., avoiding duplicate product recommendations). The empirical gains—particularly in high-diversity categories (safety, carseats) where K-Ascent fails due to projection artifacts—demonstrate practical value. However, the significance is partially undermined by the limited experimental scope. The paper claims DPPs "offer a tradeoff of quality and diversity" (p.1), but without comparing against non-DPP subset selection baselines, it fails to prove DPPs are *necessary* for this task. A stronger case would show DPPs outperform non-probabilistic methods (e.g., greedy submodular maximization) in the same recommendation setting.  

**Recommendation.** The paper presents a technically sound, original contribution to DPP learning. While the method is compelling, the evaluation would benefit from broader baselines to establish DPPs’ unique value. The writing requires substantial revision for clarity—specifically, defining notation upfront and streamlining the exposition. Given the technical depth and potential impact on subset selection applications, I recommend **acceptance** with the following revisions:  
1. Add a comparison to non-DPP subset selection methods (e.g., [28]) in experiments.  
2. Clarify all notation (e.g., define K, V, and "weight" explicitly at first use).  
3. Simplify the Stiefel manifold description by emphasizing Equation 21 over [26].  
4. Justify why the moments-matching initialization (Equation 23) is preferable to a naive covariance matrix (p.7, l.324).  

The paper’s core contribution—overcoming the projection barrier in DPP learning—is significant and well-executed. Addressing the clarity and evaluation gaps would strengthen its impact for the NIPS community.
