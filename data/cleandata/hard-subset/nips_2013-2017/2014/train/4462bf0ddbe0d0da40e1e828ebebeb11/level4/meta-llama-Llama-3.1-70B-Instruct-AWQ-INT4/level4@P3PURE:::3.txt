Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) are distributions over a fixed ground set that assign higher probabilities to diverse sets. DPPs can be parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix (L). However, learning L is NP-hard, and previous work has only discussed partial learning of L. One common approach is to learn a scalar vector for L, which compromises on the diversity dimension of DPP. Other work has resorted to restricting the parametric form of L. In contrast, this paper proposes a learning method that does not restrict the parameterization of L. 

The learning method does not require a projection step, as in gradient ascent, which can compromise the similarity property and, consequently, the diversity property. The authors overcome this disadvantage by using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm on eigen-values and eigen-vectors. They explore optimization algorithms that solve this problem without needing to project values. In particular, they note that using projected gradient ascent requires the projection of both eigen-values and eigen-vectors. However, by exploiting the fact that V is full-rank, they avoid the projection of eigen-vectors. The authors use Jensen's inequality to lower bound the objective function and construct an EM procedure.

The proposed learning method is useful because it preserves the diversity property of the DPP, which is not the case for previous work that has primarily focused on the quality property. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is insightful. The authors make solid contributions by mapping the constraint to an optimization over the Stiefel manifold to eliminate the projection of eigen-vectors and showing that the inverse distribution is a DPP. They test the learning algorithm on both synthetic datasets and a product recommendation task. The experiments support the claim made in the paper, demonstrating that the learning method retains the quality and diversity of the DPP by comparing it with KA. While only two of the top 10 products are replaced by KA with products that are less likely, this result still illustrates the contribution of the paper and supports the claim made in the introduction.

The conclusion could be improved by highlighting all the contributions of this paper. Nevertheless, the paper is written cohesively, and the proofs are well-written. The step-by-step reduction of optimization to EM is interesting and well-explained. To further illustrate the importance of the "diversity" produced by this algorithm, additional experiments on real-world datasets would be beneficial when compared to other methods in this area.
