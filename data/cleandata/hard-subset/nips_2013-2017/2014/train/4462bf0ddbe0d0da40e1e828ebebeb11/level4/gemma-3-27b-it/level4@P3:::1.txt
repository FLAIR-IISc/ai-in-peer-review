This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning kernel matrices for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), a task hindered by the non-convexity of the likelihood function. The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that reformulates the learning problem by exploiting the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix and introducing a hidden variable representing a mixture over “elementary DPPs.” This approach avoids the problematic projection step required by standard gradient ascent methods (termed K-Ascent or KA) to maintain positive semi-definiteness. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed EM method consistently outperforms KA, particularly when KA is initialized naively, achieving relative test log-likelihood gains of up to 16.5% in a product recommendation task. 

This work builds on a substantial body of research utilizing DPPs for diverse applications, including subset selection, recommendation systems [3, 8, 11, 15], and document summarization [4, 5]. Previous attempts to learn DPP kernels have focused on constrained settings – learning scalar weights [2] or combinations of fixed kernels [3] – due to the NP-hardness of full kernel learning [1]. The authors effectively circumvent this difficulty through a clever algorithmic reformulation. The connection to existing work on k-DPPs [3] is appropriately noted and leveraged within the EM framework.

The strengths of this paper lie in its technical innovation and practical improvement over existing methods. The EM formulation is a significant contribution, offering a more robust and efficient alternative to projected gradient ascent. The theoretical justification, drawing upon the eigendecomposition and mixture representation of DPPs, is sound. The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate a clear advantage for the EM algorithm, especially in scenarios with limited data. The inclusion of both a naive and a more informed initialization strategy for KA provides valuable insight into the algorithm's sensitivity to starting conditions.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The M-step, while theoretically grounded, involves approximations and caveats that somewhat diminish the elegance of the approach, as acknowledged by the authors. The runtime analysis, while mentioning asymptotic advantages, could benefit from more detailed empirical comparisons of computational costs beyond the overall runtime ratio presented. The explanation for why the EM algorithm outperforms KA is largely attributed to KA's projection step leading to near-diagonal kernels. While plausible, a more detailed analysis of the learned kernel structures (e.g., visualizing the learned kernels or examining their rank) would strengthen this claim. 

Regarding the experimental evaluation, the use of "relative log-likelihood" as a metric is unconventional and requires further justification. Standard log-likelihood differences are more interpretable. Furthermore, investigating the performance of EM when initialized with the output of KA could reveal if it serves as an effective refinement step and enhance the algorithm's overall robustness. Finally, while the authors acknowledge the limitations of their writing style, reducing self-promotional language (e.g., describing the algorithm as "elegant") would enhance the paper's objectivity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging learning problem.
*   Demonstrates consistent performance improvements over existing methods.
*   Offers a practical algorithm with potential for real-world applications.
*   Builds upon and extends a relevant body of research in the DPP literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The M-step involves approximations and caveats that reduce the algorithm’s elegance.
*   The experimental evaluation could be strengthened with more detailed analysis and the use of standard metrics.
*   The writing style could be more objective and less self-promotional.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of DPP learning. The proposed EM algorithm offers a significant improvement over existing methods, and the theoretical justification is well-supported. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly concerning the experimental evaluation and writing style.
