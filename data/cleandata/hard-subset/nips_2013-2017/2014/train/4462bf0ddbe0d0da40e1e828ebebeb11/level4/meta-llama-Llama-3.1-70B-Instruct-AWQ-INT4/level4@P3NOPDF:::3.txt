This paper proposes a novel learning method for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), a distribution over a fixed ground set that assigns higher probability to diverse sets. DPPs are typically parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix (L), but learning L is NP-hard. As a result, previous work has focused on partial learning of L or restricting its parametric form. In contrast, the authors of this paper develop a learning method that does not restrict the parameterization of L, thereby preserving the diversity property of the DPP.

The proposed learning method avoids the need for a projection step, which can compromise the similarity property and diversity dimension of the DPP. Instead, the authors employ an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm on eigenvalues and eigenvectors to overcome this disadvantage. By exploiting the fact that the matrix V is full-rank, they avoid the projection of eigenvectors, which is a significant contribution. The use of Jensen's inequality to lower bound the objective function and construct an EM procedure is also noteworthy.

The learning method is useful because it preserves the diversity property of the DPP, which is often neglected in favor of the quality property in previous work. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is insightful, and the mapping of the constraint to an optimization over the Stiefel manifold to eliminate the projection of eigenvectors is a solid contribution. Additionally, the authors demonstrate that the inverse distribution is a DPP, which is a valuable result.

The authors evaluate their learning algorithm on both synthetic datasets and a product recommendation task. The experiments support the claim that the learning method retains the quality and diversity of the DPP, as evidenced by its comparison to the Kullback-Leibler (KL) algorithm (KA). While the KA algorithm replaces only two of the top 10 products with less likely products, this result still highlights the contribution of the paper and supports the claim made in the introduction.

The paper is well-written, and the proofs are clear and concise. The step-by-step reduction of the optimization problem to an EM procedure is interesting and well-explained. However, the conclusion could be improved by highlighting all the contributions of the paper. Nevertheless, the paper is cohesive, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of their method.

One potential area for improvement is the inclusion of more experiments on real-world datasets to illustrate the importance of the "diversity" produced by this algorithm when compared to others in this area. This would help to further demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed learning method. Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPPs and is a worthwhile read for researchers interested in this area.
