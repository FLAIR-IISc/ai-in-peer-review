This paper proposes a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the parameters of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP). The method is technically sophisticated, combining several non-trivial ingredients: the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix, optimization on the Stiefel manifold, the representation of DPPs as mixtures of elementary DPPs, and the use of Jensen's Inequality to lower-bound the log-likelihood. The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method offers improved performance over a simpler "K-Ascent" baseline scheme.

**Quality and Significance**

The method presented appears to be technically sound and well-founded. The derivation is plausible, and the resulting algorithm seems likely to work as described. The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of learning DPPs from data, which is a critical step for making them more broadly applicable in subset selection tasks. As such, the work represents a solid advance and a potentially useful contribution to the field.

However, the paper's quality is diminished by a limited experimental evaluation. The experiments are narrow in scope and, most importantly, do not include comparisons against alternative, non-DPP approaches for subset selection (such as those mentioned in the introduction). This omission makes it difficult to gauge the practical advantage of using a learned DPP over other established techniques. To convincingly demonstrate the significance of this work, the authors should benchmark their method against a wider range of relevant baselines.

**Clarity**

The primary weakness of this paper is its lack of clarity. The writing is often convoluted and difficult to follow, which obscures the paper's technical contributions. The main issue is the overall structure of the text, which seems to follow the authors' discovery process rather than presenting the final method in a direct and understandable manner. The core description of the algorithm is frequently interrupted by side-remarks about alternative approaches and intermediate problems. A much more effective structure would be to present the final, developed method as clearly as possible in the main body and postpone discussions of alternatives and design choices to a later section.

Furthermore, the write-up suffers from imprecision. Notation is not always defined before use, and some definitions are provided only after the corresponding terms have appeared in the text. This forces the reader to re-read sections to piece together the authors' intent.

**Originality**

To my knowledge, the proposed EM framework for learning DPPs is highly original. I am not aware of similar work that combines these specific techniques for this purpose. The paper's novelty is a significant strength.

**Detailed Comments**

*   **p. 2, l. 81:** The term "marginal kernel K" is used without a prior definition. Please clarify what kind of kernel qualifies as a marginal kernel.
*   **p. 2, l. 100:** The matrix V is used without being defined.
*   **p. 3, l. 159:** The phrase "minimises the change to Frobenius norm" is unclearly stated. It would be much clearer to state that the algorithm "chooses the closest positive semi-definite (PSD) matrix to Q, as measured by the Frobenius norm."
*   **p. 4:** This section was particularly difficult to understand. Rather than just referring to the more complex method in [26], it would greatly benefit the reader if the authors could outline their own (presumably simpler) approach directly. This would make the paper more self-contained and accessible.
*   **p. 4, l. 189:** It is unclear what the term "weight" refers to in this context. Please clarify.
*   **p. 7, l. 324:** The rationale behind the initialization procedure in Eq. (30) is not sufficiently explained. Why are the co-occurrence counts `m_ij` not used? Furthermore, why would a simpler approach, such as initializing the kernel with the empirical covariance matrix, not be a suitable starting point? A clearer justification for this specific choice is needed.

In summary, this paper introduces a novel and non-trivial method for learning DPPs. However, its impact is currently limited by a weak experimental evaluation and a lack of clarity in the presentation. I believe the paper has the potential to be a strong contribution if the authors can substantially revise the manuscript to improve its clarity and provide a more comprehensive experimental validation against relevant baselines.
