Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) are probability distributions over subsets of a fixed ground set that favor diversity. They can be parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix L. Because learning L is NP-hard in general, prior work has typically restricted the parameterization of L (e.g., using limited scalar weights), which often sacrifices diversity.

This paper proposes an EM-based learning method that does not restrict the parameterization of L and avoids the projection steps commonly required in gradient-based methods to enforce constraints. Such projections can degrade the similarity structure and, in turn, harm diversity. By operating directly on the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of L, the authors derive an EM procedure using Jensen’s inequality to lower bound the objective. They further exploit the structure of the problem—specifically, that the eigenvector matrix V is full-rank—and recast the constrained optimization over eigenvectors as an optimization on the Stiefel manifold, thereby eliminating the need to project eigenvectors. This approach also reveals that the inverse distribution is itself a DPP, which is a neat theoretical insight.

The main contribution is a learning algorithm that preserves the diversity property of DPPs rather than emphasizing only “quality,” as much of the earlier work does. The step-by-step derivation of the EM updates is clear, technically sound, and instructive. The manifold-based treatment of the eigenvector constraints is elegant and practically useful, since it avoids potentially costly and distorting projections.

The experimental evaluation includes both synthetic data and a product recommendation task. The results support the claim that the proposed method maintains both quality and diversity better than the KA baseline. In the recommendation task, KA replaces two of the top-10 products with lower-likelihood items, whereas the proposed method retains higher-quality, more diverse selections, aligning with the paper’s goals.

The paper is cohesive and clearly written, and the proofs are well organized. The reduction of the learning problem to an EM procedure is particularly well explained. That said, the conclusion feels a bit rushed and does not fully highlight the paper’s key contributions. In addition, more experiments on real-world datasets would help demonstrate the practical importance of the diversity achieved by this method and better quantify its advantages over competing approaches.
