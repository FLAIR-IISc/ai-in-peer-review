Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) define a probability distribution over subsets of a ground set, assigning higher probability to more diverse sets. A DPP is parameterized by a positive semidefinite kernel matrix, L. As learning the full L matrix is NP-hard, prior work has typically focused on partial learning, for instance by restricting the parametric form of L or learning a simple scalar weighting, which compromises the model's ability to capture diversity.

This paper proposes a method for learning the full, unrestricted L matrix. The approach is based on an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that operates on the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of L. This avoids the projection step required by standard gradient ascent methods, which can degrade the learned kernel and impair its diversity-modeling properties. While projected gradient ascent would necessitate projecting both eigenvalues (to maintain non-negativity) and eigenvectors (to maintain orthonormality), the authors cleverly avoid the latter by reformulating the orthonormality constraint as an optimization problem on the Stiefel manifold. The EM procedure is then constructed by using Jensen's inequality to derive a tractable lower bound on the objective function.

The primary strength of this work is its focus on preserving the diversity property of DPPs, in contrast to previous methods that have concentrated primarily on item quality. The paper's key contributions are solid and well-presented: the step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is insightful, the formulation for handling eigenvector constraints via the Stiefel manifold is elegant, and the proof that the inverse distribution is also a DPP is a valuable theoretical result.

The proposed learning algorithm is validated on both synthetic data and a real-world product recommendation task. The experiments support the paper's central claim that the method effectively learns a DPP that balances both quality and diversity, showing improvements when compared against the KA method. Although the improvement in the recommendation task is modest (e.g., replacing only two of the top ten products), the result still effectively demonstrates the value of the proposed contribution.

The paper is cohesive and the proofs are well-written. The main text clearly explains the reduction of the optimization problem into an EM framework. A minor weakness is that the conclusion is somewhat brief and does not fully recapitulate all of the paper's contributions. Furthermore, including experiments on a wider variety of real-world datasets could more powerfully illustrate the practical benefits of the diversity captured by this algorithm compared to other state-of-the-art methods.
