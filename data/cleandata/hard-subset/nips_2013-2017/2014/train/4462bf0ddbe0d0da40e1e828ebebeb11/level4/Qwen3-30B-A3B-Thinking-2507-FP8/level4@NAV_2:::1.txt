

This paper addresses the problem of learning the kernel matrix for determinantal point processes (DPPs), which is known to be challenging due to the non-convexity of the log-likelihood objective. The authors propose an expectation-maximization (EM) framework that reformulates the learning problem by leveraging the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel K, rather than directly optimizing over kernel entries as in previous approaches. This work builds on prior NIPS contributions such as Kulesza and Taskar's foundational work on DPPs [15, 20] and Affandi et al.'s exploration of DPP parameter learning [8], but differs significantly by avoiding restrictive assumptions about the kernel structure. Unlike [2] which learns scalar weights per row or [3] which uses linear combinations of fixed DPPs, the proposed method learns the full kernel matrix without parametric constraints, while maintaining computational efficiency.

Quality: The paper presents a technically sound approach that successfully addresses the non-convexity issue in DPP learning through an EM framework. The authors provide a detailed derivation of the EM algorithm, including the E-step (which identifies a k-DPP as the variational distribution) and M-step (with closed-form eigenvalue updates and a gradient-based eigenvector update). The experimental validation on a real-world product recommendation task demonstrates consistent improvements over the K-Ascent baseline. However, the M-step involves several approximations and caveats (notably the need to avoid λj = 1 and the use of a single gradient step instead of full optimization), which slightly diminish the theoretical elegance of the approach.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with thorough explanations of the DPP background, the EM derivation, and experimental setup. The algorithmic descriptions are precise, and the supplementary materials provide necessary technical details. The manuscript would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the initialization effects, particularly regarding how EM performs when initialized with K-Ascent results.

Originality: The paper makes a meaningful contribution by introducing a novel EM-based learning approach for full kernel matrix estimation in DPPs. This differs substantially from prior work that either restricts the kernel structure ([2, 3]) or uses Bayesian methods ([8]). The authors thoroughly compare with previous approaches and demonstrate that their method avoids the projection issues that plague the K-Ascent baseline, particularly in scenarios where strong negative interactions between items are present.

Significance: The results show that the proposed method achieves meaningful improvements over the baseline (up to 16.5% relative gain in test log-likelihood), which is significant for applications like product recommendation where modeling item diversity is crucial. The method's computational efficiency (asymptotically faster than K-Ascent) and robustness to initialization make it practically valuable. The approach could be extended to other domains requiring diversity modeling, such as document summarization or sensor placement.

Strengths:
- Novel EM framework that avoids problematic projection steps in gradient-based approaches
- Comprehensive theoretical derivation with careful attention to computational complexity
- Demonstrated empirical improvements on a real-world dataset
- Clear explanation of how the method relates to and improves upon prior work
- Detailed analysis of the relationship between kernel structure and performance gains

Weaknesses:
- The M-step involves several approximations and caveats that slightly reduce the theoretical appeal
- Insufficient analysis of initialization effects (e.g., what happens if EM is initialized with K-Ascent results)
- The use of relative log-likelihood differences as the primary metric is confusing; absolute log-probability differences would be more interpretable
- Some writing choices (e.g., calling the approach "elegant" despite the caveats, bolding text in the introduction) detract from the professionalism

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling with DPPs. The method provides a practical and theoretically sound approach to a challenging learning problem, with clear advantages in both performance and robustness. While the improvements over the baseline aren't massive in all settings, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on DPP learning. The approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates practical significance for applications requiring diverse subset selection. The paper is sufficiently original to warrant publication at NIPS, particularly given the lack of prior work that learns the full kernel matrix without restrictive assumptions.
