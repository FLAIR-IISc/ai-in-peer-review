This paper addresses the problem of learning kernel matrices for determinantal point processes (DPPs). The central contribution is a reformulation of the learning objective that demonstrably outperforms the standard approach of differentiating the log-likelihood with respect to kernel parameters and performing projected gradient ascent – a baseline method the authors term K-Ascent (KA). The key idea is to recast the problem within an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework. This is motivated by the observation that any DPP can be represented as a mixture of “elementary DPPs,” one for each possible subset of elements. The authors instantiate a variational distribution over these subsets, represented as a k-DPP, and alternate between updating this distribution (the E-step) and taking gradient steps on the kernel parameters (the M-step). Results indicate that the EM method yields a significant improvement over KA when initialized naively, and a more modest improvement even with informed KA initialization. 

Overall, I find this approach interesting, and the EM method appears to provide a tangible benefit. The M-step is computationally complex, and the caveats necessary for its implementation are somewhat unsatisfying; nevertheless, the fact that it works is noteworthy. 

Experimentally, I would appreciate further analysis regarding the effect of initialization on the EM algorithm’s performance. Specifically, would initializing EM with the solution obtained from KA lead to further improvement?

I am also puzzled by the authors’ choice of relative log likelihood as the primary evaluation metric. Log-likelihood differences represent ratios of probabilities, so taking ratios of log probabilities seems counterintuitive. Why not simply report differences in log probabilities, which are more directly interpretable? 

Finally, a minor stylistic point: I found some of the writing to be unnecessarily assertive. The authors should refrain from describing their own algorithm as “elegant,” particularly given the approximations and caveats inherent in the M-step. Furthermore, the bolding in the introduction appears superfluous. Despite these minor concerns, this is an interesting and novel approach for learning kernels in DPPs. While the improvements over the basic projected gradient algorithm are not substantial, the paper as a whole is of good quality and represents a valuable contribution.
