This paper studies learning in Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), distributions over a fixed ground set that assign higher probability to diverse subsets. DPPs are parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix L. Because learning L is NP-hard, prior work has largely focused on partial learning, often restricting the parameterization of L (e.g., to a scalar vector), which compromises DPPs’ diversity dimension and emphasizes only the quality property.

The authors propose a learning method that does not restrict the parameterization of L. Unlike projected gradient ascent, their approach avoids projection steps that can distort similarity and thereby compromise diversity. By formulating an EM procedure over eigenvalues and eigenvectors, they overcome this disadvantage. They explore optimization algorithms that do not require projecting values: whereas projected gradient ascent would require projecting both eigenvalues and eigenvectors, they exploit the fact that V is full-rank to avoid projecting the eigenvectors. Using Jensen’s inequality, they obtain a lower bound on the objective and construct the EM updates. Mapping the constraint to an optimization over the Stiefel manifold to eliminate eigenvector projection, and showing that the inverse distribution is itself a DPP, are solid contributions.

The proposed learning method is compelling because it preserves the diversity property of the DPP, in contrast to earlier approaches that focus primarily on quality. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is insightful, and the proofs are well written. The reduction of the constrained optimization problem to EM is interesting and well explained.

The experimental evaluation includes both synthetic datasets and a product recommendation task. The results support the claim that the method retains both quality and diversity, particularly in comparison to KA. In the top-10 product recommendations, KA replaces two items with less likely products; although the change is modest, it still highlights the paper’s contribution and is consistent with the claim made at the outset.

The paper is coherent and clearly written. However, the conclusion feels somewhat hasty and does not fully foreground all contributions. Additionally, more experiments on real-world datasets would help illustrate and quantify the practical importance of the diversity achieved by this method relative to competing approaches.
