

The paper addresses a significant gap in learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), which model diverse subsets of a ground set via a positive semidefinite matrix parameter \(L\). Prior work has largely focused on partial learning of \(L\) or restricted parameterizations (e.g., scalar vectors), which sacrifice diversity for tractability. The authors propose a novel learning method that avoids restricting \(L\)'s parameterization and eliminates projection steps required in gradient ascent approaches, which often compromise both diversity and similarity properties.  

The key innovation lies in leveraging the full-rank property of the eigenvector matrix \(V\) to avoid projecting eigenvectors during optimization. Instead of projecting both eigenvalues and eigenvectors (as in projected gradient ascent), the authors derive an EM procedure by:  
1. Using Jensen’s inequality to lower-bound the objective function,  
2. Mapping the constrained optimization to the Stiefel manifold to eliminate eigenvector projection,  
3. Solving the resulting problem via eigenvalue/eigenvector updates.  
This approach preserves the DPP’s diversity property—unlike prior methods that prioritize "quality" (e.g., likelihood) at the expense of diversity. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is clear and insightful, with rigorous justification for each step. Crucially, the authors prove that the inverse distribution remains a valid DPP, a non-trivial contribution.  

Experiments validate the method on synthetic data and a product recommendation task. Compared to the baseline KA (a common DPP learning approach), the proposed method maintains both quality and diversity: KA replaced only two of the top-10 recommended products with less probable items, confirming that the algorithm’s diversity preservation is meaningful in practice. The results support the paper’s core claim that their method avoids the diversity trade-offs inherent in prior work.  

The paper is well-structured and technically sound. Proofs are concise and well-motivated, and the reduction of the optimization problem to EM is elegantly explained. However, the conclusion somewhat prematurely summarizes contributions without fully highlighting the novelty of the Stiefel-manifold mapping and the diversity-preserving guarantees. A more comprehensive discussion of these aspects would strengthen the impact.  

While the experiments are convincing, additional testing on larger real-world datasets would better demonstrate the practical significance of the diversity metric. For instance, showing how the algorithm’s diversity leads to more user-satisfying recommendations in large-scale e-commerce settings would reinforce its value beyond synthetic benchmarks.  

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances DPP learning by resolving a key limitation in prior work. The method’s theoretical grounding (via EM and Stiefel manifold optimization) and empirical validation make it a notable step toward scalable, diversity-aware DPP applications. The writing is clear, and the technical details are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. Minor improvements to the conclusion and expanded experimental scope would elevate the paper further, but the core contributions are compelling and well-executed. The work merits publication at NIPS.
