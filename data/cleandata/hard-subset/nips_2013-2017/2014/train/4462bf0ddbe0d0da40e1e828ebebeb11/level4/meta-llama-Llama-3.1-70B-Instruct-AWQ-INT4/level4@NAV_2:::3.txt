Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs) are probabilistic models that assign higher probability to diverse sets. They can be parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix (L). However, learning L is conjectured to be NP-hard, leading previous work to focus on partial learning of L or restricting its parametric form. This paper proposes a novel learning method that does not restrict the parameterization of L, preserving both the quality and diversity properties of the DPP. The authors exploit the eigendecomposition of the marginal kernel matrix to develop an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, which avoids the need for a projection step that can compromise the similarity property and diversity of the DPP.

The paper provides a clear and insightful step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure, including the use of Jensen's inequality to lower-bound the objective function and the mapping of the constraint to an optimization over the Stiefel manifold to eliminate the projection of eigenvectors. The authors also show that the inverse distribution is a DPP, which is a solid contribution. The learning method is evaluated on a product recommendation task, where it is compared to a naive approach using projected gradient ascent (KA). The experiments demonstrate that the proposed EM algorithm retains the quality and diversity of the DPP, outperforming KA in terms of test log-likelihood.

The paper is well-written, and the proofs are well-explained. The step-by-step reduction to EM is interesting and well-explained. However, the conclusion could be more comprehensive, highlighting all the contributions of the paper. Additionally, more experiments on real-world datasets would be beneficial to illustrate the importance of the "diversity" produced by this algorithm when compared to others in this area.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel learning method for DPPs that preserves both the quality and diversity properties.
* The EM algorithm is well-derived, and the use of Jensen's inequality and the Stiefel manifold optimization are solid contributions.
* The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in a product recommendation task.

Weaknesses:

* The conclusion could be more comprehensive, highlighting all the contributions of the paper.
* More experiments on real-world datasets would be beneficial to illustrate the importance of the "diversity" produced by this algorithm.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and effective learning method for DPPs.
* The EM algorithm is well-derived, and the experiments demonstrate its effectiveness.
* The paper is well-written, and the proofs are well-explained.

Arguments for rejection:

Overall, the paper is a solid contribution to the field of DPPs, and its acceptance would be justified. However, the authors could improve the conclusion and provide more experiments to strengthen their claims.
