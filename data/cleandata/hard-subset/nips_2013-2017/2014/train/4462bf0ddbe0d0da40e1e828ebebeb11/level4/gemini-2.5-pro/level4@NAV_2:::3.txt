This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from data. The authors correctly point out that this maximum likelihood problem is non-convex and conjectured to be NP-hard. They situate their work in the context of prior approaches that have circumvented this difficulty by focusing on more restricted, convex learning settings, such as learning weights for each row of the kernel [2] or for a linear combination of fixed kernels [3]. The core contribution of this work is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the unconstrained kernel. The key insight is to reparameterize the kernel matrix via its eigendecomposition and then treat the selection of eigenvectors as a latent variable. By applying Jensen's inequality, the authors derive a lower bound on the log-likelihood, leading to an iterative optimization procedure. This approach cleverly avoids the projection step required by naive projected gradient ascent, which the authors argue can lead to poor local optima (e.g., near-diagonal kernels) that fail to capture the crucial diversity aspect of DPPs.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel and principled approach to a long-standing and difficult problem in learning DPPs. Moving beyond restricted parameterizations to learn the full kernel is a significant step forward for the practical application of these models. The proposed EM algorithm provides an effective tool for a task where previous methods were either limited in scope or relied on heuristics.
*   **Quality:** The technical derivation of the EM algorithm is sound and insightful. The formulation using the eigendecomposition and the introduction of the eigenvector index set as a hidden variable is elegant. Key technical contributions include the closed-form M-step update for the eigenvalues and the use of optimization on the Stiefel manifold for the eigenvectors, which neatly avoids a problematic projection step. The demonstration that the posterior distribution required for the E-step is itself a k-DPP is another strong technical result. The empirical evaluation supports the paper's claims, showing that the EM method is not only more robust but also asymptotically faster than the projected gradient ascent baseline.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, coherent, and logically structured. The motivation is clearly established, and the step-by-step derivation of the algorithm is easy to follow. The connection between the failure mode of the baseline method (collapsing to a near-diagonal solution) and the necessity of the proposed approach is well-argued.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Experimental Comparison:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the advantage of the proposed EM algorithm over projected gradient ascent (KA). However, the evaluation would be strengthened by including comparisons to other learning methods discussed in the introduction, such as the L-ensemble method [3]. This would provide a more complete picture of where the proposed method stands in relation to the state of the art.
*   **Scope of Experiments:** The algorithm is tested on a single, albeit interesting, real-world application (product recommendation). Demonstrating its effectiveness across a broader range of applications where DPPs are used, such as document summarization or image search, would further solidify the paper's claims of general utility.
*   **Minor Point on Presentation:** The conclusion feels slightly brief and does not fully recapitulate all of the paper's strong contributions. Expanding it slightly could enhance the paper's impact.

Overall, this is a strong paper with solid technical contributions to an important problem. The proposed EM algorithm is elegant, well-justified, and empirically shown to be more effective and efficient than a natural baseline.
