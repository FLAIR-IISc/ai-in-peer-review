This paper addresses the problem of learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), distributions over a ground set that favor diverse subsets. DPPs are typically parameterized by a positive semidefinite matrix *L*, but learning *L* directly is computationally intractable. Prior work has largely focused on partial learning of *L* or restricting its parametric form, often at the cost of diversity. This paper presents a novel learning method for *L* that avoids such restrictions. 

The core contribution lies in an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm designed to learn *L* without the projection steps inherent in gradient-based methods like projected gradient ascent. These projections, often necessary to maintain the positive semidefinite property of *L*, can inadvertently compromise the diversity property of the DPP. By operating directly on the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of *L*, and leveraging the fact that the eigenvector matrix is full rank, the authors circumvent the need for eigenvector projections. This is achieved through a clever application of Jensen's inequality to derive a tractable EM procedure.

The proposed method’s ability to preserve DPP diversity, as opposed to solely focusing on subset quality as in prior approaches, is a significant strength. The paper provides a clear, step-by-step derivation of the EM algorithm, which is particularly insightful. Further solid contributions include mapping the constraint optimization problem to one over the Stiefel manifold – thus eliminating eigenvector projections – and demonstrating that the resulting inverse distribution remains a DPP.

The experimental evaluation, conducted on both synthetic and real-world datasets (specifically, a product recommendation task), supports the paper’s central claim. Results show that the learned DPPs maintain both quality and diversity. In the product recommendation setting, the comparison to a Kernel Alignment (KA) baseline demonstrates that while KA may occasionally replace highly-ranked items with less probable ones, the proposed method consistently preserves both desirable characteristics. While the conclusion feels somewhat abbreviated and could more thoroughly highlight the paper’s contributions, the overall writing is cohesive and the proofs are well-structured. The reduction of the optimization problem to the EM framework is presented in a clear and compelling manner.

The paper is well-written and technically sound. The detailed derivation of the EM algorithm is a strong point. To further strengthen the work, expanding the experimental evaluation with additional real-world datasets would be beneficial. Such experiments could more convincingly demonstrate the practical importance of the diversity achieved by this algorithm, particularly in comparison to alternative approaches commonly used in the field.
