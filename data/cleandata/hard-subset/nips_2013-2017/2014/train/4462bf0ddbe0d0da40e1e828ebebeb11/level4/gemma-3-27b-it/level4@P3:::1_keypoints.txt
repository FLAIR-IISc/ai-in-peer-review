- Addresses learning kernel matrices for DPPs, a hard problem due to non-convex likelihood.
- Proposes a novel EM algorithm using eigendecomposition and a hidden mixture over “elementary DPPs.”
- EM avoids the positive semi-definiteness projection step required by standard gradient ascent (KA).
- Experimental results show EM consistently outperforms KA, with up to 16.5% relative test log-likelihood gain in a product recommendation task.
- Builds on substantial prior DPP work and appropriately leverages connections to k-DPPs.
- Major strength: technical innovation providing a more robust and efficient alternative to projected gradient ascent.
- Theoretical justification based on eigendecomposition and mixture representation is sound.
- Empirical strength: well-designed experiments showing EM advantage, especially with limited data.
- Inclusion of both naive and informed KA initializations highlights sensitivity to starting conditions.
- Weakness: M-step requires approximations and caveats that reduce methodological elegance.
- Weakness: runtime analysis mentions asymptotic benefits but lacks detailed empirical computational-cost breakdowns.
- Claim that KA’s projection produces near-diagonal kernels explains EM’s superiority but needs stronger analysis (e.g., visualizing learned kernels or examining rank).
- Metric concern: use of “relative log-likelihood” is unconventional and needs justification; standard log-likelihood differences are more interpretable.
- Suggested experiment: evaluate EM initialized from KA’s output to test EM as a refinement and improve robustness.
- Writing critique: reduce self-promotional language to improve objectivity.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to strengthen experimental analysis, clarify M-step approximations, justify metrics, and tone down promotional wording.
