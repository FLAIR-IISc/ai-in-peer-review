This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP), a task conjectured to be NP-hard. The main contribution is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that provides an alternative to the standard projected gradient ascent approach (termed K-Ascent or KA). The key idea is to reformulate the DPP distribution as a mixture over "elementary DPPs," which are defined by subsets of the kernel's eigenvectors. This introduces a latent variable—the choice of eigenvector subset—and allows for an EM-style optimization. The algorithm alternates between an E-step, which computes the posterior over these subsets (represented as a k-DPP), and an M-step, which updates the kernel's eigenvalues and eigenvectors.

**Strengths:**
*   The reformulation of the non-convex DPP learning problem into an EM framework is a creative and interesting contribution. It provides a new way to approach this optimization challenge, avoiding the potentially problematic projection step required by gradient-based methods.
*   The proposed EM algorithm is shown to outperform the KA baseline, particularly when the baseline is initialized naively or when data is limited. This suggests the EM method is more robust and less sensitive to initialization, which is a valuable practical property.
*   The paper provides compelling evidence that the EM method can be significantly faster than the KA baseline, which is supported by both empirical runtime comparisons and an asymptotic complexity analysis.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The M-step optimization is quite involved and relies on approximations. For example, a single gradient step is taken for the eigenvectors in each M-step because the objective is intractable to evaluate, and the associated line search requires re-evaluating the E-step for each trial step size. It would be helpful if the authors could comment on the sensitivity of the final solution to these approximations.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive. A valuable experiment would be to initialize the EM algorithm with the converged solution from the KA algorithm. This would help determine if EM can serve as a fine-tuning method capable of improving upon an already good solution, or if its main advantage lies in escaping the poor local optima that KA is prone to finding.
*   The choice of "relative test log-likelihood differences" as the evaluation metric is unconventional and makes the results difficult to interpret. A simple difference in log-likelihoods has a clear probabilistic interpretation (log of the ratio of probabilities). The ratio of log-likelihoods, however, does not. The authors should clarify the rationale for this metric or, preferably, report absolute differences in log-likelihood to make the magnitude of the improvement clearer.
*   A minor point on presentation: the use of bolded text for emphasis in the introduction is somewhat distracting.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and interesting approach for learning DPP kernels. The proposed EM algorithm shows clear benefits in terms of robustness and computational efficiency compared to a projected gradient ascent baseline. While the performance gains over a well-initialized baseline are not always large, the work represents a solid contribution to the field. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough experimental analysis and clarification of the evaluation metric.
