This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix `L` for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP). The authors correctly note that learning a general positive semidefinite matrix `L` is NP-hard, and that prior work has often resorted to restricting the parametric form of `L` (e.g., to a diagonal matrix) or employed projected gradient ascent. Such approaches can compromise the crucial diversity property of DPPs, which is a key motivation for their use.

The central contribution of this work is a novel learning algorithm that does not restrict the parameterization of `L`. The proposed method cleverly avoids the projection steps common in gradient-based methods, which can degrade the model's ability to capture similarity and diversity. The key idea is to perform optimization directly on the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of `L` within an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework. The authors use Jensen's inequality to derive a lower bound on the objective function, constructing a well-defined EM procedure. A significant technical insight is to handle the orthonormality constraint on the eigenvector matrix `V` by framing it as an optimization problem on the Stiefel manifold, thereby circumventing the need for a projection step on the eigenvectors.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Preservation of DPP Properties:** The primary strength of the proposed learning method is its ability to preserve both the quality and diversity properties inherent to DPPs. This stands in contrast to previous methods that often focus heavily on the quality aspect at the expense of diversity.

2.  **Technical Contributions:** The paper contains several solid technical contributions. The step-by-step derivation of the EM procedure is clear and insightful. Mapping the eigenvector constraint to an optimization over the Stiefel manifold is an elegant solution to a difficult problem. The proof that the inverse distribution is also a DPP is another valuable contribution.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is written coherently, and the proofs are well-executed. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the reduction of the complex optimization problem to a tractable EM algorithm.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The authors validate their algorithm on both synthetic data and a real-world product recommendation task. The experiments effectively support the paper's central claim. The comparison with the Kulesza and Taskar (KA) baseline is particularly illustrative; it highlights how the proposed method retains a high-quality and diverse set of recommendations while the baseline is more prone to substituting top items with less likely ones.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**

1.  **Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are convincing, the paper's impact could be further strengthened by evaluation on a broader range of real-world datasets. Additional experiments would more comprehensively demonstrate the practical importance of the diversity captured by this algorithm when compared to other state-of-the-art methods.

2.  **Conclusion:** The conclusion feels a bit hasty and does not fully recapitulate the breadth of the paper's contributions, such as the novel optimization approach and the theoretical insights.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant technical novelty. It makes a valuable contribution to the literature on learning DPPs by proposing a method that can learn a full-rank kernel without compromising the model's expressive power.
