Summary
The paper tackles maximum likelihood learning of determinantal point processes (DPPs) by optimizing the full kernel, rather than restricted parameterizations explored in prior work. The key idea is to reparameterize the marginal kernel K via its eigendecomposition (eigenvalues λ and eigenvectors V), introduce a latent variable J indexing mixtures of elementary DPPs, and derive an EM-style procedure. The E-step sets q(J|Y) to the exact posterior pK(J|Y), which is a k-DPP with a closed-form kernel; the M-step updates λ in closed form via k-DPP marginals and makes a single Stiefel-manifold gradient step for V using a tractable gradient valid at q = p. The method avoids the projection onto the PSD cone with eigenvalue clipping required by naive projected gradient ascent on K entries (KA), which the authors argue can induce near-diagonal degeneracy. Experiments on an Amazon baby registry recommendation dataset show that EM improves held-out log-likelihood over KA, especially with weak/random initialization or limited data, and is faster asymptotically and in practice.

Relation to prior work: The work builds on DPP fundamentals and sampling via eigendecomposition (Kulesza & Taskar), the NP-hardness conjecture of full MLE for L, and previous convex learning settings that either scale row weights or learn mixtures of fixed kernels. It differs by learning a full, unconstrained kernel while retaining the DPP structure, leveraging the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs view to instantiate EM.

Strengths
- Conceptually neat use of the known mixture representation of DPPs to derive an EM procedure in the eigenbasis; the E-step yields a k-DPP posterior with an explicit kernel.
- Avoids the problematic projection steps of KA; this is well-motivated both theoretically (projection-induced degeneracy) and empirically (KA becomes near-diagonal in high-interaction settings).
- Efficient updates: eigenvalue update reduces to computing k-DPP marginals with complexity O(n N k^2); eigenvector update uses a Stiefel-manifold step with clear computational accounting.
- Empirical evidence on a realistic task, demonstrating both accuracy gains (up to 16.5% in test log-likelihood with limited data) and practical speedups (~2.1×).
- Clear exposition of algorithms, including pseudocode, complexity breakdown, and thoughtful discussion of initialization.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Convergence/monotonicity: Although a lower bound F is introduced, the method does not fully maximize the M-step (single gradient step for V, gradient only valid at q = p), and the line search accepts steps based on the likelihood L rather than the bound. A discussion or guarantee of monotonic improvement (even local) is missing.
- Evaluation breadth: Comparisons are only against KA. While prior convex methods ([row weights], [mixtures]) are not directly comparable in expressive power, side-by-side comparisons would contextualize trade-offs (e.g., robustness, runtime, accuracy) and help practitioners.
- Scalability: While asymptotics are favorable when max set size k ≪ N, updates still require O(N^3) operations per step-size search due to eigendecompositions/matrix exponentials. Practical limits for larger N and strategies (low-rank, sparsity, approximate manifold steps) could be discussed or evaluated.
- Sensitivity and regularization: No structured regularization on λ or V is considered. Overfitting and numerical issues when λ approaches 1 are acknowledged but not systematically handled. Empirical analysis of sensitivity to initialization and to λ clipping thresholds would be helpful.
- The eigenvector gradient (Eq. 20) is only valid for the initial gradient with q = p. The paper’s heuristic (one step per M-step) is reasonable but would benefit from empirical ablations (e.g., more steps, approximate retractions) and a clearer justification.

Quality
- Technically sound derivations for the E-step and λ update; complexity analyses are careful. The eigenvector gradient derivation at q = p is plausible and useful, with a principled Stiefel-manifold update.
- The use of repeated E-steps inside the line search is a practical workaround but increases per-iteration cost; this trade-off is transparent.
- Experiments are solid for the presented application, with multiple initializations and analysis of when EM helps (correlation with off-diagonal strength). Additional datasets or synthetic stress tests would strengthen claims.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized and readable. The notation is consistent, and the supplement is referenced for proofs and algorithmic details. The role of k in complexity and the k-DPP marginal computations are clearly stated.
- Minor suggestions: summarize numerical stabilization for λ near 1, and clearly state whether line-search acceptance guarantees non-decreasing likelihood across EM iterations.

Originality
- Learning full DPP kernels via EM in the eigenbasis, exploiting the mixture representation with a tractable k-DPP E-step, appears novel and well-motivated relative to prior convex or Bayesian parameterizations.

Significance
- Full-kernel learning for DPPs is important for applications where negative interactions matter. The method is likely to be adopted by researchers and practitioners working with DPPs, given its conceptual simplicity and empirical advantages over KA.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you provide any monotonicity guarantees (on L or F) for the proposed updates, or empirical plots of L across iterations to demonstrate stability?
- How does performance change with multiple manifold steps per M-step, or with approximate retractions avoiding full matrix exponentials?
- Could you compare to at least one prior learning strategy (e.g., row-weight learning or mixture-of-L) on a task where they are applicable, to contextualize gains vs. model capacity?
- Do low-rank constraints or spectral regularization (e.g., nuclear/trace penalties, shrinkage on λ) improve generalization or runtime?
- For larger N, can you exploit sparsity/structure in V or block-diagonalization suggested by item features?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled EM approach for full-kernel DPP learning.
- Avoids known pitfalls of projected gradient and improves both likelihood and runtime on a real task.
- Clear algorithmic contributions with practical complexity.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of convergence guarantees and reliance on heuristic M-step for V.
- Limited experimental scope and baselines.
- Scalability to substantially larger N not fully demonstrated.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a meaningful advance in DPP learning with a well-motivated, implementable algorithm and promising empirical results; addressing convergence discussion and expanding evaluation would further strengthen it. Confidence: medium.
