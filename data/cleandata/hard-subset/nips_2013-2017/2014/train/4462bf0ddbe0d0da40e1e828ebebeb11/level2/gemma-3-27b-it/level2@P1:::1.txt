This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP). Learning DPPs is challenging because maximizing the log-likelihood is NP-hard, and prior work has focused on restricted learning settings. The authors cleverly re-parameterize the kernel in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors and then leverage Jensen’s inequality to derive a tractable lower bound for the likelihood, leading to their EM algorithm.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles an important problem. DPPs are a powerful tool for modeling diversity and subset selection, and being able to learn the full kernel matrix would unlock their full potential. The authors do a good job of contextualizing their work within the existing literature, clearly outlining the limitations of previous approaches (scalar weights, fixed kernel combinations, Bayesian methods) and explaining how their method overcomes them.  The introduction effectively highlights the practical relevance of DPPs across a range of applications. The derivation of the EM algorithm seems sound, and the explanations are relatively easy to follow, even for someone not intimately familiar with DPPs. The inclusion of computational complexity analysis is also appreciated.

A significant strength of the paper is the experimental evaluation. Testing on a real-world product recommendation task demonstrates tangible improvements (up to 16.5% relative gain in log-likelihood) compared to projected gradient ascent. The analysis of the impact of initialization (Wishart vs. moment-matching) and the discussion of how dataset characteristics (the 'd' parameter) influence performance add further depth to the evaluation. The runtime comparison provides valuable practical insight.

However, there are a few areas for improvement. While the paper clearly states the limitations of prior work, a more detailed comparison to existing methods beyond just the log-likelihood metric would be beneficial. For instance, how does the diversity of the selected subsets compare across different methods? Are there qualitative differences in the recommendations generated? A deeper analysis of the learned kernels could also shed light on the types of diversity the EM algorithm is capturing.

The discussion of the Stiefel manifold update (Equation 21) feels slightly hand-wavy. While referencing the work of Edelman et al., a bit more explanation of *why* neglecting the Hessian is acceptable in this context would strengthen the argument.

Furthermore, while the paper presents a clear algorithmic description (Algorithms 1 and 2), reproducing the results could be challenging without access to well-documented code and potentially significant computational resources. The authors mention providing code, which is excellent.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The EM algorithm for learning the full DPP kernel matrix is a novel contribution.
*   **Significance:** Overcoming the NP-hardness of full kernel learning for DPPs has the potential to significantly impact applications relying on diversity and subset selection.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical derivations appear to be correct, and the algorithm is well-motivated.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in a real-world setting.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited comparison metrics:**  The evaluation primarily focuses on log-likelihood. More comprehensive comparison metrics would be valuable.
*   **Implementation complexity:** Reproducing the results may require significant effort.
*   **Minor clarity issues:** The explanation of the Stiefel manifold update could be more detailed.



**Overall assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. The proposed EM algorithm represents a valuable contribution to the field of DPPs, and the experimental results are promising.  I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the minor points raised above, particularly providing clear and well-documented code for reproducibility.  The additional analysis of diversity in generated subsets could elevate the paper further.
