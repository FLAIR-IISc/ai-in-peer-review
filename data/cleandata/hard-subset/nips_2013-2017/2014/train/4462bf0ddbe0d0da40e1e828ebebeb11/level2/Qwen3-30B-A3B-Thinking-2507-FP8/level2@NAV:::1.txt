

This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) without restrictive parameterizations, addressing a key limitation in existing approaches. The authors' main claim is that their method overcomes the degenerate solutions produced by projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent) due to the problematic projection step, achieving better log-likelihood and faster runtime.

The paper is technically sound, with a clear theoretical derivation of the EM algorithm using the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix. The authors provide strong experimental validation on a real-world product recommendation task using Amazon baby registry data, demonstrating up to 16.5% relative improvement in test log-likelihood over K-Ascent, particularly in categories where items exhibit strong negative interactions (d > 0.025). The runtime analysis shows EM is asymptotically faster than K-Ascent, with empirical results confirming an average 2.1x speedup.

The work is highly original, as it's the first to propose an EM-based approach for full kernel learning without restrictive parameterizations. Previous work either learned row weights (Kulesza & Taskar, UAI 2010) or used ensembles of fixed kernels (Kulesza & Taskar, ICML 2011), which lose DPP's computational properties. The paper properly cites these prior works and clearly articulates its contribution.

The significance of this work is high, as DPPs are widely used in applications requiring subset selection with diversity (e.g., document summarization, product recommendation). The authors successfully demonstrate the practical advantages of their method, with the EM algorithm producing more natural recommendations (e.g., avoiding multiple similar baby monitors).

One limitation is that both methods require careful initialization (Wishart vs. moment matching), though the paper acknowledges this. The experiments are well-designed but could benefit from additional baselines (e.g., Bayesian methods from Affandi et al., ICML 2013).

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state-of-the-art in DPP learning, with clear theoretical and empirical support. The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the EM steps and thorough experiments. I recommend acceptance.
