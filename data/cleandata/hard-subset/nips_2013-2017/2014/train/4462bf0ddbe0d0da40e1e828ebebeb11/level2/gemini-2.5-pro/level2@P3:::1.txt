This paper tackles the important and challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. The maximum likelihood objective for this problem is non-convex, and previous work has largely circumvented this difficulty by focusing on more restricted, convex learning settings which do not capture the full pairwise item-similarity structure. The central contribution of this work is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning an unconstrained kernel matrix. The key insight is to re-parameterize the kernel via its eigendecomposition and to introduce the selection of eigenvectors in the DPP generative process as a latent variable. This formulation leads to an effective optimization procedure that avoids the problematic projection steps inherent in naive gradient-based methods. The proposed EM algorithm is shown to be more robust and computationally faster than a projected gradient ascent baseline on a real-world product recommendation task.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental open problem in the application of DPPs. The ability to learn the full kernel from data, without strong parametric assumptions, significantly broadens the applicability of DPPs as models of diversity. This work represents a substantial advance over prior methods that could only learn restricted versions of the kernel, and it provides a practical tool for a wide range of applications.
*   **Originality and Quality:** The proposed EM algorithm is a novel and elegant solution. The core idea of treating the eigenvector index set `J` as a latent variable to facilitate an EM-style optimization is creative and well-executed. The technical derivations appear sound, including the key result that the posterior distribution in the E-step is a k-DPP, which allows for efficient computation. The use of optimization techniques on the Stiefel manifold to update the eigenvectors is appropriate and successfully avoids the pitfalls of simple projection, a major weakness of the baseline method that is clearly demonstrated in the experiments.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the problem is clearly established, the limitations of prior work are well-summarized, and the development of the EM algorithm is presented in a logical, step-by-step manner. The use of a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) serves as an effective didactic tool to highlight the specific challenges (e.g., the projection step leading to degenerate solutions) that the proposed method overcomes.
*   **Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The choice of a real-world product recommendation dataset is compelling. The experiments thoughtfully explore different initialization strategies and a data-poor scenario, demonstrating the robustness of the EM approach, particularly in challenging settings where the baseline fails. The qualitative example of the selected baby registry items provides a concrete illustration of the improved diversity modeling. Furthermore, the runtime analysis confirms the theoretical asymptotic advantages of the proposed algorithm.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The optimization for the eigenvectors `V` in the M-step involves a single gradient step rather than a full maximization. While this is a reasonable practical approach given the intractability of the full M-step objective, this technically makes the procedure a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm. Acknowledging this distinction could add precision and may warrant a brief comment on the convergence properties of GEM algorithms.
*   The complexity of the proposed algorithm, while an improvement over the baseline, is still cubic in the number of ground set items `N`. This may limit its direct application to problems with very large ground sets. A brief discussion in the conclusion regarding potential avenues for future work to scale the method, perhaps through low-rank approximations of the kernel `K`, would be a valuable addition and would help position the work in a broader context.

**Summary of Review:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on learning determinantal point processes. It presents a novel, technically sound, and well-motivated EM algorithm for the previously unsolved problem of learning the full DPP kernel. The paper is clearly written, and its claims are well-supported by strong experimental evidence. The identified weaknesses are minor and are offered as suggestions for further improvement. This work is likely to be influential and to be built upon by other researchers working with DPPs.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, difficult, and important problem in the field.
*   Presents a novel and elegant EM-based solution with sound theoretical justification.
*   Demonstrates superior performance (robustness, speed, and quality of solution) over a strong baseline in well-designed experiments.
*   The paper is very clear and well-written.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   None that would warrant rejection. The minor points raised above are addressable in a final version.
