This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. The standard maximum likelihood objective for this problem is non-convex and conjectured to be NP-hard. Previous work has circumvented this difficulty by learning restricted parameterizations of the kernel, such as learning a single weight per item or learning a weighted combination of fixed kernels.

The key contribution of this paper is a novel expectation-maximization (EM) style algorithm for learning the full, unconstrained kernel matrix. The authors re-parameterize the marginal kernel `K` via its eigendecomposition into eigenvalues `Λ` and eigenvectors `V`. They then leverage the view of a DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs, which allows for the introduction of a latent variable `J` corresponding to a subset of chosen eigenvectors. This framing naturally leads to an EM algorithm. In the E-step, the posterior over the latent variable `J` given an observed set `Y_i` is computed. The authors show this posterior is itself a k-DPP, for which an explicit kernel can be constructed. In the M-step, the expected complete data log-likelihood is maximized. The update for the eigenvalues `Λ` has an elegant closed-form solution, while the update for the eigenvectors `V` is performed via a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, which cleverly avoids the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent.

The proposed EM method is compared against a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) on a real-world product recommendation task using Amazon baby registry data. The experiments show that the EM algorithm is more robust, achieving significant gains in test log-likelihood, particularly when starting from a random initialization or when learning from sparse data. The paper provides a convincing analysis that K-Ascent's projection step can lead to poor, near-diagonal solutions that fail to capture diversity, a problem that the proposed EM method overcomes. The EM algorithm is also shown to be computationally faster in practice.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the EM algorithm, from the re-parameterization and introduction of a latent variable to the specific E- and M-step updates, is well-founded and clearly presented. The use of Stiefel manifold optimization for the eigenvector update is an appropriate and effective way to handle the orthogonality constraint without resorting to a naive projection. The experimental evaluation is strong; it uses a real-world dataset, compares against a sensible baseline, and thoughtfully investigates different initializations and data regimes to highlight the specific advantages of the proposed method. The claims are well-supported by the empirical results and the qualitative analysis (e.g., the failure mode of K-Ascent).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the problem is clear, the background on DPPs is concise and helpful, and the proposed algorithm is developed in a logical, step-by-step manner. The inclusion of pseudocode for both the proposed and baseline algorithms is very useful. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. To my knowledge, this is the first practical and principled algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of a DPP via maximum likelihood. While EM is a standard framework, its application here is novel and non-trivial, requiring a clever re-parameterization of the problem and insights into the structure of DPPs. This work makes a significant departure from previous, more constrained approaches to DPP learning.

**Significance:** The contribution is very significant. The difficulty of learning the kernel has been a major barrier to the wider application of DPPs. By providing an effective learning algorithm, this paper has the potential to make DPPs a much more accessible and useful tool for practitioners and researchers in numerous fields where diverse subset selection is important, such as recommendation systems, document summarization, and computational biology. The demonstrated superiority over a natural gradient-based approach underscores the practical value of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**
*   Provides the first effective algorithm for the important problem of learning a full, unconstrained DPP kernel.
*   The EM formulation based on the eigendecomposition is elegant and novel.
*   The method is more robust and computationally faster than a projected gradient ascent baseline.
*   The paper provides a clear analysis of the failure modes of the baseline method and how the proposed algorithm avoids them.
*   The paper is very well-written and the experiments are convincing.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The M-step for the eigenvectors is not a full maximization but a single gradient step. This is a common practice in generalized EM algorithms and works well here, but it is a slight deviation from the standard EM procedure.
*   The algorithm's runtime complexity includes a term cubic in `N` (the number of items), which may limit scalability to settings with very large ground sets. This is a common challenge for kernel-based methods and does not detract significantly from the contribution for moderately-sized problems, as demonstrated in the paper.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong and novel contribution to an important problem. It is technically sound, clearly presented, and the results are significant. I strongly recommend acceptance.
