This paper presents a novel algorithm for learning determinantal point processes (DPPs) by exploiting their eigendecomposition. The proposed Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm is shown to be more robust and efficient than the naive approach of maximizing likelihood by projected gradient ascent. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on a real-world product recommendation task, achieving relative gains of up to 16.5% in test log-likelihood compared to the baseline method.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and methodology. The background section provides a thorough introduction to DPPs, their properties, and their applications, which is helpful for readers unfamiliar with the topic. The authors also provide a detailed explanation of their EM algorithm, including the E-step and M-step updates, which is essential for understanding the method.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, with the authors comparing their EM algorithm to the baseline method (K-Ascent) on a real-world dataset. The results show that the EM algorithm outperforms K-Ascent in terms of test log-likelihood, especially when the off-diagonal components of the kernel matrix are important. The authors also provide an analysis of the runtime of the two methods, showing that the EM algorithm is asymptotically faster than K-Ascent.

The paper's strengths include:

1. Novelty: The proposed EM algorithm is a new contribution to the field of DPP learning.
2. Effectiveness: The experimental results demonstrate the superiority of the EM algorithm over the baseline method.
3. Clarity: The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to readers with a basic understanding of machine learning and linear algebra.

However, there are some minor weaknesses and suggestions for improvement:

1. Related work: While the authors provide a thorough background on DPPs, they could have discussed more recent work on DPP learning, such as [8] and [3], which also address the issue of learning DPPs from data.
2. Theoretical analysis: The paper could benefit from a more in-depth theoretical analysis of the EM algorithm, including convergence guarantees and bounds on the approximation error.
3. Experimental evaluation: While the authors provide a thorough experimental evaluation, they could have included more datasets and scenarios to demonstrate the robustness of their method.

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel and effective algorithm for learning DPPs, which is a valuable contribution to the field of machine learning. The authors demonstrate the superiority of their method over the baseline approach, and the paper is well-written and easy to follow. With some minor improvements, this paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the NIPS conference.
