This paper tackles the long-standing challenge of learning full determinantal point process (DPP) kernels by maximizing likelihood. The main claim is that reparameterizing the kernel in terms of eigenvalues/eigenvectors and introducing the latent subset of active eigenvectors enables an EM-style optimization that (i) avoids problematic PSD projections, (ii) is more robust to initialization than projected gradient on K, and (iii) can be asymptotically faster when observed subsets are small (k ≪ N). The method leverages the mixture view of DPPs: the E-step computes a conditional k-DPP over eigen-index sets; the M-step yields a closed-form eigenvalue update via k-DPP marginals and performs a Stiefel-manifold step for eigenvectors. On an Amazon baby registry dataset, the approach improves test log-likelihood over projected gradient ascent by up to 16.5% (notably in data-poor or strongly interacting categories) and runs about 2.1× faster.

Quality: The derivations are careful and technically sound, with appropriate use of k-DPP conditioning and Stiefel updates. The complexity analysis is plausible and explains when EM should win. However, empirical support is limited to one domain with moderate N, and only a single baseline (projected gradient on K). Statistical significance of gains is not reported; ablations (e.g., number of eigenvector steps per M-step, sensitivity to λ near 1) are missing.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well organized. The EM lower bound and k-DPP E-step are well motivated; some proofs are deferred, which is acceptable for a conference. The presentation of the manifold update could benefit from a brief, self-contained explanation of the matrix exponential step and its cost.

Originality: Framing full-kernel DPP learning via an EM lower bound in eigen-space and operationalizing the E-step as a k-DPP is novel relative to prior convex partial-learning and fixed-mixture approaches. The work is well positioned with respect to prior DPP learning and k-DPP literature.

Significance: If robust and scalable, the method could materially impact applications where diversity modeling matters. Demonstrated gains and speedups are encouraging, but broader comparisons and larger-scale tests are needed.

Pros:
- Clear, principled EM formulation exploiting DPP structure.
- Avoids degeneracies from PSD projection; robust to initialization.
- Solid runtime analysis; observed speedups and likelihood gains.
- Code and practical initializations provided.

Cons:
- Narrow empirical evaluation; no comparison to alternative optimizers (e.g., Riemannian or mirror descent on PSD cones, quasi-Newton).
- No synthetic recovery or larger-N benchmarks; limited statistical analysis.
- Convergence properties of the partial M-step are not characterized.

Suggestions: Add synthetic experiments with known ground truth; compare against stronger baselines; report statistical significance; analyze sensitivity to k, initialization, and eigenvalue clipping; discuss convergence monotonicity guarantees or diagnostics. Overall, a promising and potentially impactful contribution; I encourage acceptance contingent on strengthening the empirical evaluation.
