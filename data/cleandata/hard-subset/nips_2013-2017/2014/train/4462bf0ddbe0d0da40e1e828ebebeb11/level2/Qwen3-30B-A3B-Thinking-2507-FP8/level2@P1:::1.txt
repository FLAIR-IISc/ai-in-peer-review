

This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) approach for learning the full kernel matrix of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a significant gap in the literature where previous work has restricted the learning to either single weights per row or linear combinations of fixed kernel matrices. The authors' key insight is to parameterize the marginal kernel K in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors, which allows them to develop an EM algorithm that avoids the problematic projection steps required in naive gradient ascent methods.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through careful theoretical development. The authors provide a rigorous derivation of the EM algorithm by leveraging the eigendecomposition of K and the hidden variable interpretation of DPPs as mixtures of elementary DPPs. The theoretical foundation is well-supported, with clear derivations of the E-step and M-step updates, including the important observation that the eigenvalue updates are self-normalizing (no need for explicit projection). The paper also provides a thorough complexity analysis showing the EM algorithm's asymptotic advantage over gradient ascent methods.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with well-organized exposition and clear algorithm descriptions. The authors effectively connect the theoretical development to practical implementation considerations, particularly regarding the computational complexity. However, some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations of why the eigendecomposition approach is necessary and how it overcomes the limitations of previous methods. The connection between the hidden variable interpretation and the practical advantages of the EM algorithm would be clearer with a more concrete example.

The paper demonstrates significant originality by tackling the full kernel learning problem that has been previously conjectured to be NP-hard. While prior work has explored restricted learning settings, this paper develops a novel EM framework that handles the full kernel without restrictive assumptions. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from related work (e.g., [2], [3], [8]), and their experimental results on a real-world product recommendation task provide compelling evidence of the method's advantages.

The significance of this work is substantial. DPPs are increasingly used in applications requiring subset diversity (e.g., document summarization, product recommendation), and the ability to learn the full kernel matrix is crucial for capturing complex item interactions. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves up to 16.5% relative gains in test log-likelihood compared to baseline methods, with particularly strong improvements in domains where negative item interactions are important. The runtime analysis shows that the EM method is asymptotically faster than gradient ascent methods, which is practically significant for large-scale applications.

Strengths:
- Addresses a meaningful gap in DPP learning literature
- Theoretical development is sound and well-justified
- Clear experimental validation on a real-world task
- Demonstrates practical advantages over existing methods
- Provides a thorough complexity analysis

Weaknesses:
- Some theoretical connections could be more intuitively explained (e.g., why eigendecomposition is necessary)
- The paper could better clarify why the EM approach avoids the degeneracy issues seen in gradient ascent
- The initialization strategies could be more thoroughly compared
- The analysis of when the method performs best (e.g., categories with high d values) is somewhat preliminary

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling, particularly for applications requiring subset diversity. The EM approach for full kernel learning represents a significant step beyond previous restricted learning methods. While the paper's main contribution is algorithmic, it's well-motivated by practical limitations of existing approaches and validated through meaningful experiments.

The authors have carefully addressed the problem of learning the full DPP kernel matrix, which has been a known challenge in the literature. Their method avoids the degenerate solutions that plague gradient ascent approaches by leveraging the eigendecomposition and EM framework. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the method's advantages in both log-likelihood and runtime.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a clear technical contribution that advances the state of the art in DPP learning, with well-supported theoretical foundations and practical validation. The work is particularly relevant to NIPS audiences interested in probabilistic modeling and its applications to subset selection problems. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a meaningful problem in the field. The proposed method has clear potential for broader application in diverse domains requiring diversity-aware subset selection.
