Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses maximum-likelihood learning for determinantal point processes (DPPs) by optimizing the full kernel, rather than restricted parameterizations considered previously. The key idea is to reparameterize the marginal kernel K in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors, introduce the standard DPP mixture latent variable (the subset of active eigenvectors J), and derive an EM-style lower bound. The E-step yields a k-DPP posterior over J conditional on each observed subset, enabling efficient computation of expected sufficient statistics. The M-step has a closed-form update for eigenvalues and a single manifold-constrained gradient step for eigenvectors using a Stiefel update via a matrix exponential. The proposed EM procedure avoids the degeneracies observed with naive projected gradient ascent on K’s entries. Experiments on an Amazon baby registry recommendation task show improvements in test log-likelihood (up to 16.5% in a data-poor setting) and reduced runtime relative to the projected gradient baseline.

This work connects to and extends a line of research on DPPs in machine learning, including foundational results and applications [Kulesza & Taskar Foundations and Trends; Hough et al. Prob. Surveys], MAP inference [Gillenwater et al., NIPS], k-DPPs [Kulesza & Taskar, ICML], and scalable sampling/approximations [Affandi et al., AISTATS; Kang, NIPS]. Prior learning efforts largely constrained the kernel (e.g., per-item weights or mixtures of fixed kernels). By directly tackling full-kernel learning with an EM scheme grounded in DPP spectral structure and k-DPP marginals, the paper offers a novel and practically effective alternative.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The latent-variable formulation based on the DPP mixture over elementary DPPs is standard, and the lower-bounding EM derivation is appropriate. The E-step characterization of q(J|Y) as a k-DPP with kernel QY (via R diag(√λ/(1−λ)) and ZY = UY UY^T) is plausible and consistent with known k-DPP identities; details are deferred to the supplement. The eigenvalue update λj = (1/n)∑i q(j∈J|Yi) is standard EM for Bernoulli latent selectors and is efficiently computable using k-DPP marginals (Eq. 19), with complexity O(n N k^2). The eigenvector update uses a Stiefel-manifold multiplicative step and checks true likelihood improvement, which mitigates the inexactness of using only the first gradient expression (Eq. 20).
- Theoretical guarantees: Monotonic increase of the true likelihood is not formally guaranteed because only a single V-step is taken and the M-step objective is not optimized exactly; however, the algorithm accepts steps only when the data likelihood improves, providing a practical safeguard. Convergence and stationarity guarantees could be discussed in more depth.
- Baselines and experiments: The projected gradient baseline is natural and carefully implemented with eigenvalue clipping. The failure mode due to projection-induced near-diagonality is convincingly analyzed. Empirical results on a real recommendation dataset indicate robustness to initialization and improved speed. That said, the empirical evaluation is limited to a single domain and one primary baseline.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The spectral perspective, EM lower bound, and computational tricks for k-DPP marginals are explained with sufficient detail to re-implement. Pseudocode for KA and EM is helpful. A few minor issues merit clarification: the mapping of citations [2]/[3] to the described learning settings appears inconsistent with the reference list; the handling of eigenvalues at or near 1 (stability and practical clipping) could be formalized; and the use of L versus F in the line search/acceptance criteria in Algorithm 2 should be made fully explicit.

Originality:
Using an EM lower bound with the DPP mixture latent variables to learn the full kernel is, to the best of current knowledge, novel. The combination of k-DPP posteriors for E-steps, closed-form eigenvalue updates, and Stiefel manifold updates for eigenvectors provides a previously unexplored and principled route to full-kernel DPP learning.

Significance:
Full-kernel learning is an important open problem for DPPs, long hindered by nonconvexity and conjectured hardness. The proposed method offers a practical, faster, and more stable alternative to naive projected gradient, with clear gains on a real task. Given the breadth of DPP applications (summarization, recommendation, sensor placement, vision), the approach is likely to be adopted and extended.

Strengths:
- Principled EM formulation leveraging the spectral mixture structure of DPPs.
- Efficient E-step via k-DPP marginals; closed-form eigenvalue updates.
- Appropriate manifold optimization for eigenvectors; avoids problematic PSD projections.
- Solid runtime analysis and empirical evidence of speed and robustness.
- Real-world dataset and public code.

Weaknesses:
- Evaluation limited to one application and primarily one baseline; lacks comparisons to alternative parameterizations/optimizers (e.g., spectral parameterization with retractions and second-order methods, Cholesky or softplus-PSD parameterizations, or Bayesian approaches with flexible priors).
- No synthetic studies demonstrating recovery under controlled conditions or sensitivity to noise, sample size, and rank.
- Convergence properties are empirical; no analysis of stationary points or monotonicity of the EM surrogate given the inexact V-step.
- Some citation mapping and minor notation inconsistencies.

Suggestions for improvement:
Essential:
- Add at least one additional baseline beyond KA, such as: (a) direct optimization in spectral space without EM (eigenvalues via logistic transform, eigenvectors via Stiefel retractions) using L-BFGS; or (b) a Cholesky-type parameterization with eigenvalue bounding via a smooth link to avoid hard projection. This would strengthen the claim that EM is superior to general-purpose alternatives.
- Include a small synthetic experiment with known ground-truth K to assess parameter recovery, sensitivity to initialization, and effect of k (subset size) on runtime and accuracy.
- Clarify and correct citation references for prior learning approaches, and explicitly discuss how the proposed method differs from and improves upon them.

Optional but beneficial:
- Provide ablations on the number of V-updates per M-step and the effect on likelihood and runtime; report the frequency of eigenvalues approaching 1 and the clipping strategy.
- Add a second domain (e.g., document summarization or image search) to demonstrate broader applicability.
- Discuss or experiment with simple regularization (e.g., trace/nuclear norm or eigenvalue priors) to mitigate overfitting and improve conditioning.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and well-motivated method addressing a central challenge in DPP learning.
- Technically sound with efficient computational machinery.
- Demonstrated empirical gains and speedups; practical relevance.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited breadth of empirical validation and baselines.
- Lack of theoretical convergence guarantees beyond acceptance tests.
- Minor clarity and citation issues.

Overall, this is a strong, impactful contribution to DPP learning that would be of interest to the NIPS community. With modest extensions to the evaluation and clarifications, the paper would be even more compelling.
