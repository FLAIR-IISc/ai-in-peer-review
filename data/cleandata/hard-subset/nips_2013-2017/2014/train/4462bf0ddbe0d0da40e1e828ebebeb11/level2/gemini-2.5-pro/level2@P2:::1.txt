This paper addresses the important but challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from data. The standard maximum log-likelihood objective is non-convex and conjectured to be NP-hard. Previous work has circumvented this difficulty by learning restricted versions of the kernel, for example by learning only item quality scores or by learning a mixture of DPPs with fixed kernels. This work proposes the first, to my knowledge, effective algorithm for learning the full, unconstrained kernel matrix.

The key idea is to change the parameterization from the kernel matrix entries to its eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The authors then cleverly formulate the problem in an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework by introducing a latent variable corresponding to the set of eigenvectors chosen in the DPP's generative process. This allows them to derive a coordinate ascent algorithm on a lower bound of the log-likelihood. The M-step updates are particularly elegant: the eigenvalue update is in closed form, and the eigenvector update is performed via a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, which neatly avoids the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent. The proposed EM algorithm is compared against a baseline of projected gradient ascent on the kernel matrix entries (K-Ascent), demonstrating significant improvements in both solution quality and runtime.

**Quality**
The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation of the EM algorithm, based on the generative view of DPPs, is sound and well-motivated. The identification of the posterior over the latent variables as a k-DPP (Lemma 1) is a key insight that makes the E-step tractable. Claims are well-supported by thorough experiments on a real-world product recommendation task. The authors are careful to evaluate their method under different initializations and in a data-poor setting, which provides a nuanced and honest view of the algorithm's strengths. The claim that projected gradient ascent can get stuck in poor local optima (near-diagonal kernels) is plausible and well-supported by the experimental results. The work is a complete contribution, presenting theory, algorithm, and convincing empirical validation.

**Clarity**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the logical flow from the problem definition to the baseline method and finally to the proposed EM algorithm is easy to follow. The technical details of the EM derivation are presented with sufficient clarity for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work. The inclusion of algorithm boxes for both the baseline and the proposed method is helpful.

**Originality**
The work is highly original. While DPPs have seen increasing use in machine learning, learning the full kernel has remained a major open problem. The proposed EM approach, which leverages the kernel's eigendecomposition and a latent variable formulation, is a novel and non-obvious way to tackle this non-convex optimization problem. It is clearly and substantially different from prior work, which focused on convex but more restrictive learning settings. The paper does an excellent job of citing and distinguishing itself from relevant prior literature.

**Significance**
The significance of this paper is high. DPPs are powerful models for capturing diversity, but their practical application has been limited by the difficulty of learning their parameters from data. This paper provides a practical, robust, and relatively efficient algorithm for this task. This contribution is likely to be widely used by researchers and practitioners who wish to apply DPPs to new domains. It demonstrably advances the state-of-the-art by providing a superior solution to the naive baseline and enabling a much more flexible learning paradigm than previous methods.

**Strengths:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant EM-based algorithm for the difficult problem of learning a full DPP kernel.
*   The reparameterization via eigendecomposition and the use of updates on the Stiefel manifold are clever and effectively avoid the pitfalls of naive projected gradient methods.
*   The empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating that the proposed method is more robust to initialization, more effective in data-poor settings, and faster than the baseline.
*   The paper is extremely clear and well-written, making a complex topic accessible.
*   The contribution has a high potential impact, as it makes learning expressive DPP models much more practical.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The performance advantage over the baseline is significantly reduced when a strong, data-dependent ("moments-matching") initializer is used in a data-rich setting. While the authors correctly show their method still excels in other settings (uninformed initialization, less data), this suggests that with good initialization, the simpler baseline can be competitive.
*   The M-step for the eigenvectors is not a true maximization but a single gradient ascent step, making the algorithm a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm. This is a common and valid approach, but the distinction could be made more explicit for technical precision.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**
1.  The step-size search for the eigenvector update seems to require re-evaluating the E-step for each trial step size to check for improvement in the true likelihood. This could be computationally intensive. It would be helpful to report the typical number of line search iterations (`T2`) needed in the experiments to give a better sense of the practical cost.
2.  In Section 3.5, the gradient for V (Eq. 20) is only exact at the beginning of the M-step (when `q=p`). The paper notes this and explains a single gradient step is taken. It might be worthwhile to explicitly call the algorithm a Generalized EM (GEM) algorithm to reflect that the M-step is not fully optimized.
3.  The moment-matching initialization itself requires a projection step to ensure the resulting kernel is valid. It would be good to briefly comment on whether this one-time projection can also cause issues, or why it is less problematic than the iterative projections in the K-Ascent algorithm.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to an important problem. The proposed method is elegant, well-justified, and empirically validated. It is a clear advance for the field of probabilistic modeling with DPPs.
