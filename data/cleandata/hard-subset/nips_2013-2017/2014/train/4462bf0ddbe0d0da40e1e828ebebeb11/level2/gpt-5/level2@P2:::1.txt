Summary
The paper proposes an expectation–maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full kernel of a determinantal point process (DPP) from observed subsets by maximizing likelihood. The key idea is to reparameterize the DPP in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the marginal kernel K, introduce a latent set J indicating which eigenvectors are “active,” and derive a Jensen bound that leads to an EM-style lower-bounded objective. The E-step sets q(J|Y) = pK(J|Y), which is shown to be a k-DPP with a closed-form kernel. The M-step yields closed-form updates for the eigenvalues via k-DPP marginals and a single gradient step on eigenvectors constrained on the Stiefel manifold, with a step-size line search monitored by the true likelihood. The algorithm avoids the problematic PSD/eigenvalue projection of naive projected gradient ascent (KA). Experiments on an Amazon baby registry recommendation dataset show consistent improvements in test log-likelihood (up to 16.5% relative) and a roughly 2× runtime speedup, particularly in categories where negative interactions are strong. The method is contrasted with prior convex or parametric learning approaches that restrict L (e.g., per-row weights [2] or mixtures [3]) and with KA.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself well within the DPP learning literature, which has focused on restrictive convex parameterizations [2,3] or Bayesian learning under parametric forms [8]. It leverages well-known spectral characterizations of DPPs and the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs interpretation [21], and correctly contrasts its approach with L-ensemble mixtures that break DPP structure and lose attractive properties. The eigen-based EM appears to be new in the DPP learning context and is distinct from prior work that learns only quality terms or structured/low-rank forms.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivations are generally sound and well motivated. The E-step result that q(J|Y) is a k-DPP with kernel QY is plausible and, if correct as per the supplement, underpins efficient marginal computations used in the eigenvalue update (Eq. 19). The eigenvector update uses a first-order gradient valid when q = p and updates on the Stiefel manifold via a matrix exponential (Eq. 21), with a line search checked against the true likelihood. While this deviates from a textbook EM with guaranteed monotonicity of a single lower bound, the authors explicitly verify likelihood improvement at each step, which is reasonable in practice.
- Complexity analysis is clear. The O(T1 n N k^2 + T1 T2 N^3) cost can be better than KA’s O(T1 n N^3 + T1 T2 N^3) when k ≪ N, which aligns with typical subset sizes.
- Experiments: The study is careful: two initializations (Wishart and moment-matching), multiple random trials, correlation analysis between off-diagonal signal and gains, data-poor regime tests, and runtime comparisons. The qualitative MAP example is illustrative.

Clarity
The paper is well written and organized. Background is sufficient, the EM derivation is clear, and algorithms are summarized. Some steps depend on supplemental proofs (Lemma 1, Eq. 19–20). Minor notation points could be clarified (see suggestions).

Originality
The EM framework for unconstrained full-kernel DPP learning via spectral parameterization and a latent J is novel relative to prior works that either restrict the parameterization or use Bayesian inference over parametric forms. Using k-DPP structure in the E-step and Stiefel manifold updates in the M-step is an elegant combination.

Significance
Learning full DPP kernels directly is an important problem. The proposed method advances the state of the art by enabling practical, scalable learning without restrictive assumptions and by outperforming a natural baseline. The approach should be relevant to applications where negative interactions matter (recommendation, summarization, sensor placement).

Pros
- Conceptually clean EM formulation exploiting the spectral mixture view of DPPs.
- Avoids degeneracy from PSD projection in KA; more robust to initialization and data regimes.
- Efficient eigenvalue updates via k-DPP marginals; principled eigenvector updates on Stiefel manifold.
- Empirical gains in likelihood and runtime; correlation analysis connecting gains to interaction strength.
- Reproducibility: code/data provided.

Cons
- Monotonicity of EM is not guaranteed due to the approximate M-step (single gradient step using a derivative valid at q = p) and re-evaluation of q during line search; theory could be strengthened.
- Evaluation limited to one real dataset and KA baseline; no comparisons to other learning strategies (e.g., per-row convex learning [2], mixture learning [3], parametric/Bayesian methods) on the same task.
- No regularization or structural constraints; learning a full N×N kernel may overfit in low-data regimes (beyond the data-poor experiment). Effects on generalization beyond likelihood are not explored.
- Scalability to larger N is unclear due to repeated N×N matrix exponentials; low-rank or sparse extensions are not discussed.
- Some numerical issues are deferred (λ near 1); stability safeguards could be elaborated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a brief theoretical discussion of convergence/monotonicity: under what conditions does the procedure guarantee non-decreasing likelihood? Can a generalized EM perspective justify the approximate M-step?
- Include an additional baseline beyond KA, e.g., the convex per-item quality learning [2], or the mixture approach [3], to contextualize gains (even if those methods have limitations).
- Investigate simple regularizers (e.g., nuclear norm, Frobenius norm, graph Laplacian priors) or low-rank constraints to mitigate overfitting and improve scalability, and report their impact.
- Report sensitivity to initialization, step-size schedule, and the λ cap (e.g., λ ≤ 1 − ε), with numeric stability notes.
- Clarify Eq. (3): the appearance of |det(K − IY)| is unusual; a short derivation or reference would help avoid confusion.
- Expand on runtime details: cost of repeated matrix exponentials in Eq. (21), and whether approximations (e.g., Cayley transforms, QR-based retractions) perform similarly.
- Consider synthetic experiments to assess recovery under controlled conditions (varying k, N, noise, and similarity strength).

Minor comments
- Typo: “negelecting” → “neglecting”.
- Notation: In Eq. (20), define HYi and BYi more explicitly upfront; ensure consistent use of U = V>.
- Clarify whether V is full N×N or whether rank truncation is allowed; discuss implications.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a novel and well-executed approach to a challenging and important problem in DPP learning, with clear empirical benefits and solid technical ideas likely to influence subsequent work.
