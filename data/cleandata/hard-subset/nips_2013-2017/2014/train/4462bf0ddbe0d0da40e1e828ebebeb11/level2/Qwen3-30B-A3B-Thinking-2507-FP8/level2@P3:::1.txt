

The paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), addressing a key limitation in prior work that had to restrict the learning problem to partial kernel parameterizations. Previous approaches either learned only a single weight per row of the kernel matrix [2] or learned weights for linear combinations of DPPs with fixed kernels [3], both of which fail to capture the full diversity modeling capabilities of DPPs. The authors' key insight is to change the parameterization from kernel entries to eigenvalues and eigenvectors, enabling the development of an EM-style optimization that avoids the problematic projection step required in naive gradient ascent methods.

The technical contribution is strong, with a clear mathematical derivation of the EM algorithm. The paper demonstrates that the naive gradient ascent approach (K-Ascent) requires projection to maintain positive semi-definiteness, which often results in degenerate solutions that fail to model negative interactions between items. The EM approach circumvents this issue by working with the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix, leading to a more robust learning procedure. The authors provide a detailed derivation of the E-step and M-step updates, including efficient computational methods for handling the exponential-size sums. The runtime analysis shows the proposed method has asymptotic complexity O(T1nNk² + T1T2N³), which is better than the O(T1nN³ + T1T2N³) complexity of the gradient ascent approach.

The experimental evaluation on a real-world product recommendation task using Amazon baby registry data demonstrates clear advantages of the proposed method. In the Wishart initialization setting, the EM approach achieves an average 3.7% improvement in test log-likelihood over K-Ascent, with gains up to 16.5% in the safety category. The analysis of the relative gains correlates well with the degree of negative interactions in the data, as measured by the metric d = (1/N)||M||F/||diag(M)||². The authors also show that the EM method is consistently faster than K-Ascent, with an average speedup of 2.1 times across the tested categories.

The paper's strengths include its technical rigor, clear presentation of the mathematical framework, and meaningful empirical validation. The derivation of the EM updates is carefully worked out, and the connection to the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix provides important theoretical insight into why the method works better than gradient ascent. The experiments are well-designed, with appropriate baseline comparisons and analysis of when the method provides the most significant gains.

However, the paper could strengthen its contribution by providing more thorough comparisons to related work. While the paper references [8] as using Bayesian methods for DPP parameter learning, it does not sufficiently explain how the proposed EM approach differs from or improves upon that work. The paper also does not discuss how the proposed method compares to the recent work on learning DPPs with kernel parameterizations [11, 12], which may have similar goals. A more comprehensive comparison would better establish the novelty of the contribution.

The paper could also benefit from more detailed discussion of the method's limitations. While the authors note that both methods perform poorly when initialized from a diagonal kernel, they do not explore how the EM approach might be affected by other initialization challenges. A more thorough analysis of when the method might fail or require additional tuning would strengthen the practical applicability of the work.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with well-organized sections that build logically from background to proposed method to experiments. The mathematical derivations are presented with sufficient detail for experts to follow, and the experimental results are clearly presented. The only minor issue is that the paper could provide more concrete guidance on how to choose the step size η in the EM algorithm, which appears to be a critical hyperparameter.

In terms of significance, the work addresses an important problem in DPP learning that has been recognized as challenging [1]. The ability to learn the full kernel matrix without restrictive parameterizations opens up new possibilities for using DPPs in applications where modeling negative interactions between items is crucial, such as product recommendation, document summarization, and sensor placement. The demonstrated improvements in log-likelihood and runtime make a compelling case for the method's value.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by providing a technically sound and practically useful method for learning DPPs. The approach is novel in its application of EM to the DPP learning problem, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its advantages over previous methods. The work advances the state of the art in DPP learning by addressing a long-standing limitation in the field.

The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission: it is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written, original in its approach to the problem, and significant in its practical implications for DPP applications. The minor weaknesses identified are largely in the presentation and comparison to related work, not in the core technical contribution. The proposed method represents a meaningful advancement that should be of interest to the machine learning community.
