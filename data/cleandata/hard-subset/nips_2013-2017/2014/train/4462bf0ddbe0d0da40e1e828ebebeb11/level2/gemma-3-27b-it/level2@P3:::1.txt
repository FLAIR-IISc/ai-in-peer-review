This paper addresses the problem of learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), a task conjectured to be NP-hard. The work proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of a DPP, circumventing the limitations of prior approaches that either learn only partial information about the kernel or rely on restrictive parameterizations. The core idea is to re-parameterize the kernel in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors and then leverage a lower bound on the likelihood to formulate an effective optimization procedure. The approach is evaluated on a product recommendation task, demonstrating improvements over a projected gradient ascent baseline.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing methods and the challenges inherent in learning DPPs. The proposed EM algorithm appears to be a significant contribution, offering a potentially more robust and efficient alternative to gradient-based methods. The re-parameterization strategy, changing from kernel entries to eigenvalues/eigenvectors, is a clever move that avoids the need for projection steps to maintain positive semi-definiteness, which the authors convincingly argue can lead to suboptimal solutions. The experimental results, while limited to a single application, are encouraging and demonstrate a clear advantage over the baseline. The connection drawn between the diversity gains and the data characteristics (the 'd' metric) provides additional insight.  The inclusion of runtime comparisons is also valuable.

However, there are areas where the paper could be strengthened. While the paper references related work extensively, the discussion of the computational complexity of the EM algorithm feels somewhat condensed. The claim of asymptotic speedup compared to gradient ascent is significant, and a more detailed breakdown of the complexities and a clearer explanation of the assumptions leading to this result would be helpful.  The discussion regarding the Stiefel manifold update is also brief. Expanding on the practical implementation details – how the step size is selected, what regularization (if any) is applied – would increase reproducibility. 

A potential weakness lies in the sensitivity of the algorithm to initialization.  While the authors test two initialization schemes, it is unclear how robust the algorithm is to other initialization strategies. A more thorough investigation of the impact of different initializations would be beneficial. The paper states that the moment-matching initializer alleviates issues with the projection step in KA, but doesn't fully explain *why* it is more effective. 

Regarding clarity, while the overall writing is good, some sections (particularly those detailing the mathematical derivations) could benefit from more exposition. For example, the jump between equations (13) and (20) feels abrupt. More intermediate steps and explanations would make the derivation more accessible.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a challenging problem.
*   Strong theoretical justification for the proposed algorithm.
*   Demonstrated improvements over a relevant baseline in a real-world application.
*   Potential for wider impact in areas where DPPs are used.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation (single application).
*   Complexity analysis could be more detailed.
*   Robustness to initialization needs further investigation.
*   Some mathematical derivations could be clarified.

**Overall assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of DPP learning. The proposed EM algorithm is a promising approach with the potential to address the limitations of existing methods. While some areas could be strengthened through further experimentation and clarification, the originality, technical soundness, and potential significance of the work make it a strong candidate for acceptance. The revisions suggested above – a more detailed complexity analysis, exploration of initialization strategies, and increased clarity in the mathematical derivations – would substantially enhance the paper.
