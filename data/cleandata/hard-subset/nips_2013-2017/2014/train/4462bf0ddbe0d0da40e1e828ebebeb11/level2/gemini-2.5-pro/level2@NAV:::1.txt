This paper addresses the challenging non-convex problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from observed subset data. Previous work, such as Kulesza & Taskar (UAI 2011, ICML 2012), has focused on more restricted, convex learning settings. The main contribution of this paper is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that operates on the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix. By treating the set of active eigenvectors as a latent variable, the authors derive an iterative procedure that avoids the problematic projection step required by standard gradient-based methods.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The approach is novel and addresses a fundamental limitation in the practical application of DPPs. Learning a full, unconstrained kernel is crucial for capturing complex diversity patterns, and this work provides a principled and effective method to do so. The formulation of the problem within the EM framework is elegant and insightful.
*   **Quality:** The technical work is sound. The paper clearly identifies a critical flaw in the naive projected gradient ascent baseline—its tendency to collapse to a near-diagonal kernel, thereby failing to model diversity—and provides a well-motivated solution. The claims are convincingly supported by experiments on a real-world baby registry dataset. The results demonstrate that the proposed EM algorithm is not only more robust, leading to significant gains in test log-likelihood (up to 16.5%), but is also computationally faster than the baseline.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the technical derivation is laid out logically, and the experimental setup and results are presented transparently.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The M-step for the eigenvectors involves a single gradient step on the Stiefel manifold rather than a full optimization of the objective. While this is a reasonable and practical choice given the intractability of the full M-step objective, a brief comment on the potential impact of this approximation would be beneficial.
*   A short discussion on scalability would strengthen the paper. The algorithm's complexity includes an O(N^3) term, which could be a bottleneck for applications with a very large ground set `N`. Acknowledging this limitation and perhaps suggesting potential avenues for future work (e.g., using low-rank approximations) would be valuable.

**Recommendation:**
Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a clever and practical solution to an important problem in machine learning. It is technically solid, empirically validated, and clearly communicated. I strongly recommend acceptance.
