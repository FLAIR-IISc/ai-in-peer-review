## Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), which is conjectured to be NP-hard. The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm by re-parameterizing the kernel in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors and deriving a lower bound on the likelihood. This is a compelling approach, addressing a significant limitation of previous DPP learning methods that focused on restricted convex settings (learning single weights or ensembles of fixed kernels). The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the shortcomings of existing techniques and positioning their contribution as a more general and flexible learning algorithm.  It builds on the well-established DPP literature (e.g., Kulesza & Taskar, UAI 2012; Kulesza, ICML 2013) and the benefits of eigendecompositions for DPP operations.

The strengths of the paper are considerable. The proposed EM algorithm is theoretically sound, with a clear derivation of the lower bound and update rules. The authors demonstrate a practical advantage over a projected gradient ascent baseline on a product recommendation task, achieving up to 16.5% gains in test log-likelihood. The analysis of the runtime complexity, showing potential asymptotic speedup over gradient ascent, is also a valuable contribution. The inclusion of comparisons against a naive approach (K-Ascent) provides a concrete benchmark. 

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the experiments demonstrate improvement, the dataset and experimental setup seem somewhat limited.  The choice of the “baby registry” task is practical, but lacks broader applicability insights.  Further exploration of different datasets and potentially more complex scenarios would strengthen the findings. The discussion regarding the sensitivity of the approach to initialization is good, but a more systematic analysis of initialization strategies could be included. Additionally, while the authors acknowledge the non-convexity of the eigenvector update, the simplification of using a single gradient step feels somewhat ad-hoc; exploring more sophisticated methods from Stiefel manifold optimization as suggested, would enhance the technical depth. The connection to the dataset characteristic ‘d’ is interesting but could be explored more thoroughly, perhaps with ablation studies. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a long-standing open problem in DPP learning.
*   Proposes a theoretically well-motivated and practically effective algorithm.
*   Demonstrates significant improvements over a strong baseline.
*   Presents a clear and well-written exposition of the method.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of experiments.
*   Simplification in the eigenvector update may not be optimal.
*   Dataset selection may not generalize well to other application areas.



**Overall Assessment:** I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While there's room for expansion in the experimental validation and technical refinement, the novelty and potential impact of the proposed EM algorithm are significant. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically encouraging the authors to explore more diverse datasets and to elaborate on the eigenvector update strategy.
