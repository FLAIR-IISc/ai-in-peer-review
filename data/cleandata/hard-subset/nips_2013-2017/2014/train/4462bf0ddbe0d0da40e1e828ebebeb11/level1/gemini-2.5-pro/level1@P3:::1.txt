**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. The standard maximum log-likelihood objective for this problem is non-convex and conjectured to be NP-hard. Consequently, prior work has focused on restricted, convex learning settings, such as learning weights for each item (quality) but not their interactions (diversity) [2], or learning weights for a mixture of fixed DPPs [3].

The main contribution of this paper is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for this non-convex learning problem. The key idea is to re-parameterize the DPP's marginal kernel K in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors. This allows the authors to view the DPP generative process as a mixture of elementary DPPs, each corresponding to a subset of eigenvectors. This subset of eigenvectors, denoted by J, is treated as a hidden variable. By applying Jensen's inequality, the authors derive a lower bound on the log-likelihood, leading to an EM procedure.

The E-step involves computing the posterior distribution over the hidden variable J given an observed subset Yi. The authors show that this posterior is itself a k-DPP, for which efficient inference is possible. The M-step maximizes the lower bound with respect to the eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The update for the eigenvalues has a convenient closed form. The update for the eigenvectors is more complex due to the orthogonality constraint (VᵀV = I). The authors address this by performing a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold, avoiding the problematic projection steps used by naive gradient methods.

The proposed EM algorithm is compared against a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) on a real-world product recommendation task using Amazon baby registry data. The experiments show that the EM method is more robust, particularly with uninformative initializations or in data-poor settings, achieving relative gains of up to 16.5% in test log-likelihood. Furthermore, the EM algorithm is shown to be asymptotically and empirically faster than the baseline.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a fundamental and important open problem in the DPP literature. Learning a full, unconstrained kernel matrix directly from data has been a major barrier to the wider application of DPPs. The proposed EM framework is a novel and elegant approach to this non-convex problem. It moves beyond the restrictive assumptions of prior work and represents a significant step forward for making DPPs a more practical and powerful modeling tool.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The technical development of the algorithm is excellent. The re-parameterization via eigendecomposition and the introduction of the latent variable J is a very clever way to frame the problem for an EM approach. The derivations for the E-step (characterizing the posterior as a k-DPP) and M-step (closed-form eigenvalue update and a principled Stiefel manifold update for eigenvectors) are sound and well-explained. The use of a manifold optimization technique for the eigenvectors is particularly well-motivated, as it neatly sidesteps the pitfalls of simple projection that the paper effectively demonstrates.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It begins with a clear motivation and background, carefully situating the work within the existing literature. The derivation of the algorithm is presented in a logical, step-by-step manner. The connection between the eigendecomposition and the generative view of DPPs as mixtures is explained with great clarity, making the intuition behind the method accessible.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide convincing evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The comparison to projected gradient ascent is a strong and appropriate baseline. The use of a real-world dataset is commendable. The analysis is thorough, exploring the impact of different initializations and the amount of training data, which clearly highlights the superior robustness of the EM algorithm. The qualitative example of the selected baby registry items provides a compelling, intuitive illustration of why learning a good diversity model matters and how the EM method succeeds where the baseline fails. The runtime comparison further strengthens the case for the proposed algorithm.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Local Optima:** As the authors correctly state, the log-likelihood objective is non-convex. While the proposed EM algorithm is shown to be empirically superior to projected gradient ascent, it is still a local optimization method and offers no guarantees of convergence to a global optimum. The quality of the solution will still depend on initialization, even if the method is more robust than the baseline. A brief discussion of this inherent limitation would be appropriate.

2.  **Approximation in the M-step:** The M-step for the eigenvectors V is not fully optimized in each iteration; instead, a single gradient step is taken. This is a practical choice, justified by the intractability of the full M-step objective for V. However, it is a deviation from the standard EM procedure. It would be helpful to briefly comment on the theoretical implications of this approximation, for instance, whether monotonic improvement of the auxiliary function F is still guaranteed (the line search on the true likelihood L ensures L improves, which is what matters, but the connection to the standard EM theory is looser).

3.  **Scalability:** The algorithm's complexity contains an O(N³) term due to the eigenvector update (matrix exponential) and a separate O(nNk²) term. While this is an improvement over the baseline, the O(N³) complexity could be a bottleneck for applications with very large ground set sizes (N). A short discussion on potential avenues for scaling up the method, perhaps by leveraging low-rank approximations like the Nyström method [15], would enhance the paper's impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a central, difficult, and long-standing problem in the field of DPPs.
*   The proposed EM algorithm is a highly novel, technically sound, and elegant solution.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.
*   Empirical results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the method over a sensible baseline in terms of both accuracy and speed.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The method is a local optimization algorithm for a non-convex problem, with no global optimality guarantees.
*   The M-step for the eigenvectors is an approximation, not a full maximization.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the machine learning literature on probabilistic models of diversity. It presents a novel and effective algorithm for a hard and important problem. The technical quality is high, the writing is clear, and the experimental validation is convincing. The weaknesses identified are minor and do not detract from the overall strength of the contribution. This paper is a clear candidate for acceptance and is likely to be highly influential in future work on learning and applying DPPs.
