This paper tackles the important and challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. The core difficulty lies in the non-convex nature of the maximum log-likelihood objective with respect to the kernel matrix entries, a problem conjectured to be NP-hard. The authors situate their work effectively in the context of previous literature, which has largely circumvented this difficulty by focusing on more restricted, convex learning settings. For instance, Kulesza & Taskar (2011) learned scalar weights for rows/columns (learning item "quality" but not "similarity"), while their 2012 work on k-DPPs learned weights for a fixed ensemble of kernels, sacrificing the compact DPP representation. This paper proposes a novel approach to learn the full, unconstrained kernel.

The key idea is to reparameterize the kernel matrix `K` via its eigendecomposition (`V`, `Λ`) and to formulate the learning problem within an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework. This is achieved by introducing a latent variable `J`, which represents the subset of selected eigenvectors in the generative view of a DPP as a mixture of elementary DPPs. The log-likelihood is then lower-bounded using Jensen's inequality. The algorithm proceeds by coordinate ascent on this lower bound: the E-step involves computing the posterior distribution over the latent variable `J` given an observed subset `Yi`, which the authors cleverly show is itself a tractable k-DPP. The M-step updates the parameters `V` and `Λ`. The update for the eigenvalues `Λ` is shown to have a closed-form solution, while the update for the eigenvectors `V` is handled by taking a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold to preserve orthogonality, thus avoiding the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation of the EM algorithm is sound, and the connection between the generative process of a DPP and the choice of the latent variable is elegant. The insight that the posterior `q(J | Yi)` is a k-DPP is crucial and enables an efficient E-step. The use of manifold optimization methods for the eigenvector update is appropriate for the `V'V = I` constraint and cleverly sidesteps the main weakness of the baseline method. Claims are well-supported by both the theoretical development and the experimental results. The experiments on the baby registry dataset convincingly demonstrate the proposed EM algorithm's superiority over the projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent), particularly in terms of robustness to initialization and computational efficiency. The analysis of K-Ascent's failure mode—where the projection step can prematurely zero out eigenvalues and lead to a near-diagonal kernel that cannot model diversity—is insightful and clearly motivates the need for the proposed method.

A minor weakness is that the baseline, while natural, is somewhat simplistic. It would have been interesting to see a comparison against more sophisticated general-purpose constrained non-convex optimization methods. However, the chosen baseline serves its purpose well by highlighting the specific pitfalls of projection that the EM approach is designed to avoid.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, and the background section provides the necessary context on DPPs. The development of the algorithm is logical, proceeding step-by-step from the reparameterization and introduction of the latent variable to the details of the E- and M-steps. The inclusion of algorithm blocks for both K-Ascent and the proposed EM method is very helpful for the reader. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the intuition behind their technical choices, such as why the projection step in K-Ascent is problematic and how the EM framework avoids it.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. To my knowledge, this is the first practical and effective algorithm for learning the full, unconstrained kernel of a DPP. The core idea of using an EM algorithm on the eigendecomposition of the kernel is novel and non-obvious. While the constituent parts (EM, DPPs, manifold optimization) are known, their synthesis to solve this specific, important problem is a significant conceptual contribution. It represents a clear departure from previous attempts that relied on convex relaxations or restrictive parameterizations.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is very significant. The difficulty of learning the kernel has been a major bottleneck for the widespread application of DPPs. By providing a robust and efficient learning algorithm, this work makes DPPs a much more accessible and powerful tool for practitioners and researchers. The ability to learn both item quality (related to eigenvalues) and pairwise similarity/diversity (encoded in the eigenvectors) from data is a substantial step forward. The problem of modeling diversity is fundamental in many areas, including recommender systems, document summarization, and search. This algorithm is likely to be widely used and built upon by others working in these fields. The experimental results, showing relative gains of up to 16.5% in test log-likelihood, demonstrate a clear and meaningful advance over the naive approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, long-standing, and difficult problem in learning DPPs.
*   Proposes a novel and elegant EM-based solution that is technically sound.
*   The method is shown to be more robust and efficient than a natural baseline (projected gradient ascent).
*   The work has high potential impact, significantly broadening the practical applicability of DPPs.
*   The paper is very clearly written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation is limited to a single task (product recommendation). Demonstrating its effectiveness in another domain would have further strengthened the paper.
*   The baseline method is a straightforward implementation of projected gradient ascent, and a comparison to more sophisticated non-convex solvers could have provided additional context.

In conclusion, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, high-quality, and significant contribution that solves a key problem in the field. It is a clear and strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
