

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper addresses a significant challenge in Determinantal Point Process (DPP) learning: the difficulty of learning the full kernel matrix by maximizing log-likelihood. Previous work has focused on restricted learning settings due to the conjectured NP-hardness of the problem, such as learning scalar weights for each row of the kernel matrix [2] or learning weights for linear combinations of DPPs with fixed kernels [3]. The authors propose a novel EM-based approach that changes the parameterization from kernel entries to eigenvalues and eigenvectors, avoiding the problematic projection step required in gradient ascent methods.

The paper builds upon the foundational work of Kulesza and Taskar [1, 2, 3, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 18, 20] on DPPs, which have been extensively studied at NIPS for applications including document summarization, sensor placement, and recommendation systems. The authors demonstrate that their approach differs from prior work by not assuming fixed values or restrictive parameterizations for the kernel matrix, while leveraging the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix to develop an effective optimization procedure.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical justification. The authors derive the EM algorithm through careful application of Jensen's inequality to lower-bound the likelihood, and provide a clear explanation of why the naive gradient ascent method (with projection) fails to model negative interactions between items. The experimental evaluation on a real-world product recommendation task (Amazon baby registry data) is thorough, with appropriate data preprocessing, multiple initialization strategies, and clear metrics (test log-likelihood). The paper acknowledges limitations, including the need for a good initialization and the asymptotic runtime advantage over gradient ascent. The authors are careful to evaluate both strengths and weaknesses of their approach, including the observation that the moments-matching initializer can partially mitigate the baseline's projection problem but is not a complete solution.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section on DPPs provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with the topic, and the mathematical derivations are clear and well-explained. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are precise and would be reproducible by an expert reader. The experimental section provides detailed dataset characteristics, evaluation metrics, and results with appropriate statistical analysis (medians from 25 trials). The paper effectively explains why their method outperforms the baseline, using both theoretical analysis and concrete examples (Figure 2a).

**Originality:** This paper presents a novel approach to DPP learning that significantly departs from previous work. The key insight of changing parameterization to eigenvalues and eigenvectors to develop an EM algorithm is original and well-justified. The authors clearly articulate how their method differs from prior work, including the limitations of the K-Ascent baseline. They provide a comprehensive comparison with relevant prior work, including NIPS papers on DPPs (e.g., [1, 2, 3, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 18, 20]) and distinguish their contribution from these prior efforts. The paper does not merely combine familiar techniques but presents a fundamentally new optimization approach for DPP learning.

**Significance:** The problem of learning DPP kernels is important for applications where modeling negative interactions between items is crucial, such as product recommendation, document summarization, and sensor placement. The proposed method achieves significant improvements (up to 16.5% relative gain in test log-likelihood) over the baseline on a real-world dataset. The asymptotic runtime advantage (O(T1nNk2 + T1T2N3) vs. O(T1nN3 + T1T2N3)) is substantial for larger N. The paper demonstrates that the EM approach is more robust to initialization and dataset changes, making it more practical for real-world applications. The results are likely to be of interest to researchers working with DPPs and to practitioners applying DPPs to real-world problems.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a clear technical contribution to DPP learning with a novel algorithm, strong theoretical justification, and compelling empirical results. The writing is excellent, the experiments are thorough, and the comparison with baseline methods is fair and comprehensive. The authors provide valuable insights into why the baseline method fails, which helps explain the observed performance improvements.

**Weaknesses:** The evaluation is limited to a single real-world dataset (baby registry recommendations), which somewhat limits the generalizability of the results. While the paper acknowledges this limitation, a broader evaluation across multiple domains would strengthen the significance claim. The paper doesn't provide a detailed comparison with other recent DPP learning methods beyond the baseline, though this is understandable given the paper's focus on a specific approach.

**Conclusion:** This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of DPP learning by presenting a novel EM-based algorithm that overcomes fundamental limitations of previous approaches. The technical soundness, clarity, and empirical validation are strong. The paper addresses a meaningful problem with a well-justified solution that is likely to be adopted by practitioners and researchers working with DPPs. The work is well-positioned to advance the state of the art in DPP learning and has the potential to impact applications across multiple domains.

Pro: Novel approach to a well-known problem in DPP learning, strong theoretical justification, significant empirical improvements over baseline, clear writing and organization, asymptotic runtime advantage.

Con: Limited evaluation on real-world datasets (only one dataset used), some aspects of initialization could be explored more thoroughly.

Overall, this paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission. It is technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant. I recommend acceptance.
