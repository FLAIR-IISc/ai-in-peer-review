- Problem: Learning the full, unconstrained DPP kernel from observed subsets is a hard, non-convex (conjectured NP-hard) problem that prior work avoided via restrictive assumptions.

- Main contribution: A novel EM algorithm that re-parameterizes the DPP marginal kernel via eigendecomposition and treats a subset of eigenvectors J as a latent variable.

- E-step: The posterior over J given an observed subset is itself a k-DPP, enabling efficient inference.

- M-step (eigenvalues): Eigenvalue updates have a convenient closed-form solution.

- M-step (eigenvectors): Eigenvector updates are handled by a gradient step on the Stiefel manifold to respect orthogonality.

- Practical approximation: The M-step uses a single gradient step for eigenvectors rather than full maximization, deviating from standard EM.

- Complexity: Algorithm includes an O(N^3) term (matrix exponential) plus O(nNk^2) operations, which may limit scalability to very large N.

- Empirical results: On Amazon baby registry data, EM is more robust than projected gradient ascent, yields up to 16.5% relative test log-likelihood gains, and is empirically faster.

- Strength — Significance & originality: Tackles a central open problem and moves beyond restrictive prior models for DPP learning.

- Strength — Technical soundness: Clever eigendecomposition reparameterization, sound E-step derivation, closed-form eigenvalue updates, and well-motivated manifold optimization for eigenvectors.

- Strength — Clarity: Paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear motivation and step-by-step derivations.

- Strength — Empirical validation: Thorough experiments, sensible baseline comparison, analysis of initialization/data effects, qualitative example, and runtime comparison.

- Weakness — Local optima: EM is still a local optimization method with no global optimality guarantees; solutions depend on initialization.

- Weakness — M-step approximation: Using a single gradient step for V weakens the connection to standard EM theory and its guarantees.

- Weakness — Scalability concerns: O(N^3) components could be a bottleneck for large ground sets; low-rank or Nyström-style approximations might be needed.

- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses a difficult central problem with a novel, technically sound method, clear writing, and strong empirical support.

- Arguments against acceptance: Relies on local optimization with no global guarantees and uses an approximate M-step for eigenvectors.

- Conclusion: Strong contribution, minor weaknesses, recommended for acceptance and likely to be influential.
