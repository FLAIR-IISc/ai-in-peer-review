This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from data via maximum likelihood estimation. The authors correctly note that this is a non-convex optimization problem, conjectured to be NP-hard, which has led prior work at NIPS and elsewhere to focus on more restricted, convex learning settings (e.g., Kulesza & Taskar, 2011) or Bayesian approaches with parametric kernels. The main contribution is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that directly learns the kernel's eigenvalues and eigenvectors. By introducing a latent variable corresponding to a selection of eigenvectors, the authors derive a tractable lower bound on the log-likelihood, leading to an effective optimization procedure that avoids the pitfalls of a naive projected gradient ascent baseline.

The paper's primary strengths are its originality, technical quality, and significance. The application of an EM algorithm to learn the full DPP kernel is a novel and elegant idea that directly tackles a key open problem in the field. The technical derivation is sound, cleverly leveraging the generative interpretation of DPPs as a mixture of elementary DPPs to formulate the E- and M-steps. The M-step updates for eigenvalues (closed-form) and eigenvectors (using optimization on a Stiefel manifold) are well-justified. The experimental results on a real-world product recommendation task are convincing, demonstrating that the proposed EM method is not only more robust but also significantly faster than the projected gradient ascent baseline, especially in settings where modeling diversity (i.e., the off-diagonal kernel entries) is crucial. The paper is also very clearly written and well-organized, making the complex derivation easy to follow.

The paper has few weaknesses. The experimental comparison is limited to a single baseline (projected gradient ascent). While this is the most direct competitor, including a comparison to a restricted learning model (e.g., learning only diagonal weights) could have helped quantify the practical benefits of learning the full kernel. Additionally, while the asymptotic runtime is better than the baseline for small `k`, the `O(N^3)` complexity for the eigenvector update could still be a bottleneck for very large ground sets, a point that could be discussed further.

Arguments for acceptance:
-   Provides a novel and principled algorithm for a fundamental, unsolved problem in DPP learning.
-   Technically strong, with a well-founded EM derivation.
-   Demonstrates clear and significant empirical improvements over a natural baseline in terms of performance, robustness, and speed.
-   The work is highly significant and likely to be widely used by researchers and practitioners applying DPPs.

Arguments against acceptance:
-   The experimental evaluation could be slightly broader.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor limitations. It is a high-quality, complete piece of work that significantly advances the state of the art in learning probabilistic models of diversity.
