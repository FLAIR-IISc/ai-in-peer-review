- Proposes an EM algorithm to learn the full DPP kernel matrix by reformulating learning in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors.  
- Cleverly sidesteps known NP-hardness by working with the eigendecomposition and deriving a tractable optimization procedure.  
- Builds on the L↔K eigendecomposition relationship and avoids the projection step that hinders naive gradient ascent.  
- Novel contribution compared to prior work that learned constrained kernel parametrizations or used strong priors.  
- Theoretical derivation appears sound, leveraging Jensen’s inequality and DPP properties.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient mathematical detail (with extra derivations in supplementary material).  
- Empirically validated on a product recommendation (baby registry) dataset, outperforming projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent).  
- Ablation study on initialization (Wishart vs. moment matching) gives useful practical insight.  
- Runtime analysis suggests potential scalability benefits versus K-Ascent.  
- Practical relevance is high given DPPs’ utility for diverse subset selection in recommendation tasks.  
- Implementation may be complex, especially the Stiefel manifold update for eigenvectors.  
- M-step requires trial-and-error step size selection; lacks a systematic or automatic strategy.  
- Scalability concerns remain—O(N^3) operations and experiments limited to N ≈ 71 may not generalize to much larger datasets.  
- Limited empirical comparisons beyond K-Ascent; broader comparisons (even high-level) to other DPP learning methods would strengthen evaluation.  
- Dataset description could include more detail on product categories and what constitutes negative interactions.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant advance, strong empirical results, clarity, and potential wide impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: implementation complexity and unresolved scalability issues.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions—add strategies for step-size selection, expand scalability discussion, and compare to additional DPP methods.
