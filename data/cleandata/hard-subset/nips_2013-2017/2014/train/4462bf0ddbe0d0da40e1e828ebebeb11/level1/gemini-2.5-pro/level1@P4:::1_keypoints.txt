- Problem: Learning the full kernel matrix of a DPP from observed subsets is a challenging, non-convex optimization, conjectured NP-hard.
- Novel contribution: Introduces an EM algorithm that re-parameterizes the kernel by its eigenvalues and eigenvectors to maximize data log-likelihood.
- Latent formulation: Uses a latent variable J selecting active eigenvectors via the DPP mixture representation, enabling an EM approach.
- E-step: Shows the posterior over J given an observed subset is a tractable k-DPP and derives a closed-form kernel for it.
- M-step: Provides a closed-form update for eigenvalues and updates eigenvectors with a single gradient step on the Stiefel manifold to maintain orthogonality.
- Empirical results: On a baby registry product recommendation dataset, EM achieves higher test log-likelihood, is faster, and is more robust to initialization than projected gradient baseline.
- Strength — Technical quality: EM formulation and tractable E-step are significant technical contributions that make learning practical.
- Strength — Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear motivation, background, and derivations.
- Strength — Originality: First practical algorithm for learning the full unconstrained DPP kernel via likelihood maximization using eigendecomposition and latent variables.
- Strength — Significance: Advances practical application of DPPs, improving model quality and runtime; code and data are publicly released.
- Weakness — Eigenvector M-step: The M-step only takes a single gradient step (GEM-style) rather than fully maximizing over eigenvectors; monotonicity but not full maximization is guaranteed.
- Weakness — Experimental scope: Evaluation is limited to a single dataset and domain (product recommendation), lacking other canonical DPP tasks.
- Weakness — Scalability: Runtime has O(N^3) components (eigendecompositions), which may limit applicability to very large ground sets.
- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses a fundamental problem, proposes a novel and effective EM solution, shows empirical improvements, and is well-presented with likely practical impact.
- Arguments against acceptance: Limited experimental diversity and the M-step’s partial optimization over eigenvectors.
