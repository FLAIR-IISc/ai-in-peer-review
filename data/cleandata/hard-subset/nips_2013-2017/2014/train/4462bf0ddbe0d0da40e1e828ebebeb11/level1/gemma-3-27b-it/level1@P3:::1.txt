This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP). The core idea is to reparameterize the kernel in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors, and then lower-bound the likelihood using Jensen’s inequality, leading to an iterative optimization procedure. The motivation is strong: learning the full kernel is conjectured to be NP-hard, and prior work has focused on more restrictive learning settings. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, and the mathematical derivations appear sound.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research on DPPs, particularly the work of Kulesza & Taskar (UAI 2012, NIPS 2012, Foundations and Trends 2012) which laid much of the groundwork for applying DPPs to machine learning. The authors correctly identify the limitations of previous approaches, such as the row-weight learning of Kulesza & Taskar (UAI 2012) which doesn't capture item similarity, and the L-ensemble method of Affandi et al. (ICML 2013) which loses the attractive computational properties of a single DPP. The paper also appropriately references related work on subset selection and diversity modeling, including applications to recommendation systems and document summarization. The connection to existing EM algorithms is also well-placed.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed EM algorithm represents a significant step towards learning the full kernel matrix of a DPP, addressing a long-standing challenge in the field. The reparameterization in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors is a clever and crucial contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations, particularly the lower-bounding of the likelihood and the derivation of the update rules, appear well-justified and rigorously presented.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the baby registry dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of the EM algorithm compared to projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent). The reported gains in test log-likelihood are encouraging, and the analysis of the performance based on the data marginals provides valuable insights.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are easy to follow, even for readers not intimately familiar with DPPs.
*   **Addressing Limitations:** The authors acknowledge and address potential issues such as the initialization sensitivity and the computational complexity of the algorithm, and offer practical solutions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** While the authors claim asymptotic speedup compared to gradient ascent, the practical runtime analysis is limited to a single dataset. A more detailed analysis of the runtime scaling with problem size (N, n) would be beneficial. A comparison with other methods beyond K-Ascent would also be valuable, although potentially difficult given the limited availability of methods capable of learning the full kernel.
*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** The performance of the EM algorithm is heavily dependent on the initialization strategy. The moments-matching initialization appears to mitigate some of the issues with the Wishart initialization, but a more systematic study of different initialization methods would be worthwhile. The paper also mentions projection problems with the K-Ascent method, but does not deeply analyze the sensitivity to initialization.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The discussion of the Stiefel manifold update is somewhat brief. While the simplification is understandable, a more detailed explanation of the practical implementation and potential challenges would be helpful. Also, handling the case where a lambda value becomes exactly 1 is a pragmatic detail, but it would be good to elaborate how it affects the optimization.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are confined to a single domain (baby registries). Evaluating the algorithm on a broader range of datasets and tasks would strengthen the conclusions.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant open problem in the DPP literature.
*   The proposed EM algorithm is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.
*   The work has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in DPP learning and applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The runtime analysis is limited, and the computational complexity remains a concern for large-scale problems.
*   The algorithm's performance is sensitive to initialization.
*   The experiments are limited to a single domain.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPPs. The proposed EM algorithm is a significant advance over existing methods, and the paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented. While the computational complexity and initialization sensitivity are legitimate concerns, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest expanding the runtime analysis, providing a more detailed discussion of the Stiefel manifold update, and exploring the sensitivity to initialization more thoroughly. I also encourage the authors to consider evaluating the algorithm on a broader range of datasets.
