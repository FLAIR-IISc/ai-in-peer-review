## Review of “Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes”

This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the full kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP), which is known to be NP-hard. The authors propose a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that avoids the problematic projection step required by naive gradient ascent methods, which often leads to degenerate solutions. Their approach cleverly reparameterizes the kernel in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors, enabling a more effective optimization procedure. The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing methods (scalar weighting, L-ensemble approaches, and Bayesian methods) and positioning their work as a significant step forward.

The strengths of this paper lie in its technical innovation and thoroughness. The derivation of the EM algorithm is well-explained, and the use of eigendecomposition is a smart move. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity, demonstrating the potential for asymptotic speedup compared to gradient ascent. The experimental evaluation on a product recommendation task is convincing, showing consistent gains over the baseline K-Ascent method, particularly in scenarios with stronger item interactions and limited data. The inclusion of ablation studies (Wishart vs. moment-matching initialization) adds further insight.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the theoretical justification is strong, the practical implementation details (e.g., step size selection in the EM algorithm) could benefit from more elaboration. The discussion of the Stiefel manifold update is somewhat brief, and it's unclear how sensitive the results are to the simplification of neglecting second-order information. The connection between the data characteristics (d metric) and EM’s performance, while interesting, feels somewhat speculative and could be strengthened with more rigorous analysis. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a comparison to more recent DPP learning methods, if any exist, beyond those already cited.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*   Clear theoretical justification and analysis.
*   Empirically validated improvements over existing methods.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Implementation details could be more thoroughly explained.
*   The connection between data characteristics and performance could be strengthened.
*   Limited comparison to very recent DPP learning methods.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of DPP learning. The proposed EM algorithm is a significant advancement, and the experimental results are compelling. Despite some minor weaknesses, the paper meets the criteria for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above.
