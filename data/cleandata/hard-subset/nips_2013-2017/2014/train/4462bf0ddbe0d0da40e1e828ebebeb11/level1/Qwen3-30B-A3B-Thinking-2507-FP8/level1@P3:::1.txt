

# Peer Review: "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper addresses a significant limitation in the learning of Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), a probabilistic model for set diversity. Previous work had been restricted to learning only partial parameters of the DPP kernel (e.g., scalar weights for each row of the kernel matrix [2], or linear combinations of DPPs with fixed kernels [3]), as learning the full kernel matrix was conjectured to be NP-hard [1]. The authors propose a novel EM-based algorithm that avoids the problematic projection steps required by gradient ascent methods, by parameterizing the kernel in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors. This approach enables learning the full kernel matrix without the degeneracy issues that plague naive gradient ascent methods. The paper relates well to recent NIPS work on DPPs, including Kulesza & Taskar's foundational work on DPPs [11, 12, 13], as well as Affandi et al.'s work on learning DPP parameters [8].

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with clear theoretical derivations. The authors develop a rigorous EM algorithm by changing parameterization to eigenvalues and eigenvectors, then applying Jensen's inequality to lower-bound the likelihood. The E-step is solved by recognizing the conditional distribution as a k-DPP, and the M-step provides closed-form updates for eigenvalues and gradient-based updates for eigenvectors. The experimental validation on a real-world baby registry dataset is thorough, with 13 categories showing consistent improvements (up to 16.5% gain in test log-likelihood) over the gradient ascent baseline. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach (e.g., initialization sensitivity) and provide theoretical justification for their algorithmic choices. The computational complexity analysis is sound, demonstrating asymptotic speed advantages over gradient ascent.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical progression from background to method to experiments. The mathematical derivations are detailed and accessible to readers familiar with DPPs and EM algorithms. The supplementary material provides additional technical details that would help reproducibility. However, the description of the Stiefel manifold update (Equation 21) could be clearer for readers unfamiliar with optimization on manifolds. The experimental setup is well-described, with appropriate dataset details and thorough analysis of performance across different initialization methods.

**Originality:** This work makes a clear contribution to the field by addressing the previously unaddressed problem of learning the full DPP kernel matrix. The key insight of parameterizing the kernel in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors to enable EM optimization is novel. The authors effectively distinguish their approach from prior work, noting that previous methods either restricted the parameter space [2, 3] or relied on Bayesian methods with parametric assumptions [8]. The paper's approach is significantly different from recent NIPS papers on DPPs (e.g., [15, 16, 17, 18, 19]), as it provides a new learning algorithm rather than applying DPPs to new domains.

**Significance:** The ability to learn the full kernel matrix significantly expands the applicability of DPPs to problems where modeling negative interactions between items is crucial (e.g., product recommendation, document summarization). The empirical results demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods, with consistent improvements in log-likelihood and runtime. The method's asymptotic speed advantage (O(nNk²) vs. O(nN³)) makes it particularly valuable for larger datasets. This work provides a solid foundation for future research in DPP learning and applications.

**Strengths:**
- Novel EM approach for learning full DPP kernel matrices
- Theoretical justification for avoiding projection steps
- Strong empirical results showing significant improvements over gradient ascent
- Clear analysis of when the method provides the greatest advantage (high negative interaction settings)
- Asymptotic runtime advantage demonstrated both theoretically and empirically

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited comparison to other recent DPP learning methods beyond gradient ascent (e.g., no comparison to the Bayesian approach in [8])
- Scalability to very large N (e.g., N > 10,000) is not addressed
- Limited theoretical analysis of EM convergence properties
- The paper could better explain why the EM approach is more robust than gradient ascent beyond the projection issue
- Numerical stability of the eigendecomposition for large matrices is not discussed

**Recommendation:** This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of DPP learning with a novel algorithm that addresses a long-standing limitation. The method is theoretically sound, well-validated experimentally, and has clear practical advantages. While some aspects of the analysis could be strengthened (particularly regarding scalability and convergence properties), the paper is of high quality and makes a meaningful advancement in the field. I recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Solves a well-known limitation in DPP learning (full kernel matrix learning)
- Novel approach that avoids degeneracy issues of previous methods
- Strong empirical results with clear improvements over baseline
- Theoretical and practical runtime advantages
- Well-written and clearly organized

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited comparison to other recent DPP learning methods
- Scalability analysis for large N is missing
- Limited theoretical convergence analysis for the EM algorithm

The paper is clearly within NIPS scope, addressing a core machine learning problem with significant theoretical and practical contributions. It advances the state of the art in DPP learning and has clear potential for application in diverse domains where set diversity is important. The work is a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
