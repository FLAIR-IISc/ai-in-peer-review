

# Review of "Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes"

This paper addresses the important problem of learning the full kernel matrix for Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs), a model for capturing set diversity. Previous work has been limited to restricted learning settings: either learning a single weight per row of the kernel matrix [2] or learning weights for linear combinations of DPPs with fixed kernels [3]. The authors propose a novel EM-based algorithm that avoids the problematic projection steps required by naive gradient ascent methods, which often produce degenerate (nearly diagonal) kernels that fail to model diversity. By working with the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix, they develop an optimization procedure that is more robust and asymptotically faster than gradient-based approaches. The paper demonstrates significant improvements (up to 16.5% relative gain in test log-likelihood) on a real-world product recommendation task using baby registry data from Amazon.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound approach that effectively addresses a well-documented limitation in DPP learning. The EM algorithm is carefully derived, with detailed mathematical justification for each step, including the E-step (Lemma 1) and M-step updates (Equations 18-20). The authors properly acknowledge the limitations of their approach, noting that it still requires good initialization and doesn't fully solve the NP-hardness conjecture for full kernel learning. The experimental validation is thorough, with multiple initialization strategies (Wishart vs. moment matching) and careful analysis of when the EM approach provides the most significant gains. The runtime analysis is convincing, showing the asymptotic advantage of EM over gradient ascent (O(T1nNk² + T1T2N³) vs. O(T1nN³ + T1T2N³)).

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The background section (Section 2) provides a concise yet comprehensive overview of DPPs, their properties, and the relationship between the kernel matrix L and marginal kernel K. The motivation for the EM approach is well-explained in Section 3, with a clear comparison to the limitations of gradient ascent (Section 3.1). The mathematical derivations are presented with sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results, and the algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-2) are precise. The experimental setup (Section 4) is well-described, with appropriate statistical measures and thorough analysis of results across different categories.

## Originality
This work makes a significant original contribution to the DPP literature. While Kulesza and Taskar (NIPS 2011, 2012) established the theoretical foundation of DPPs, and Affandi et al. (NIPS 2013) explored learning DPP parameters, this paper presents the first method for learning the full unconstrained kernel matrix without restrictive assumptions. The key innovation—using the eigendecomposition to develop an EM algorithm that avoids projection steps—is novel and directly addresses a gap in previous work. The paper properly contextualizes this contribution against prior NIPS work (e.g., Kulesza & Taskar 2011, 2012; Affandi et al. 2013), clearly articulating how this work differs from and extends previous approaches.

## Significance
The significance of this work is high for both theoretical and practical reasons. Theoretical significance lies in providing a practical method for a problem previously considered difficult (learning full DPP kernels), which advances the state of the art in probabilistic modeling. Practical significance is demonstrated through compelling results on a real-world product recommendation task, where the proposed method significantly outperforms the naive baseline. The paper also provides valuable insights into when the method is most beneficial (when negative item interactions are strong, as measured by the d metric). This work has clear potential for broader application in domains where modeling negative interactions between items is important (document summarization, sensor placement, etc.).

## Strengths
- Solves a meaningful problem in DPP learning that has been previously limited to restricted settings
- Provides a well-motivated, technically sound EM algorithm that avoids problematic projection steps
- Demonstrates significant practical improvements (up to 16.5% relative gain in log-likelihood) on a real-world task
- Clear theoretical analysis of runtime complexity and convergence properties
- Thorough experimental validation with careful analysis of results across different initialization strategies

## Weaknesses
- The paper doesn't fully address scalability to very large N (the experiments use N=71, but DPPs are often applied to much larger sets)
- The initialization strategy (Wishart vs. moment matching) is somewhat ad-hoc; the paper doesn't provide a principled way to choose between them
- The theoretical guarantees of the EM algorithm are limited; it's not clear if it converges to a global optimum or what the convergence rate is
- The comparison to other methods beyond the baseline (K-Ascent) is limited; it would be valuable to compare with other recent DPP learning approaches

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work makes a significant contribution to the DPP literature, addressing a key limitation in previous approaches with a technically sound and well-validated method. The paper is well-written, clearly explains its contribution in context of prior NIPS work, and demonstrates clear practical benefits. While there are some limitations regarding scalability and theoretical guarantees, these don't undermine the overall contribution, which is substantial and well-executed. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and will likely be valuable to researchers and practitioners working with DPPs.

Pro-acceptance arguments: Novel solution to a well-documented problem, strong experimental results with clear practical impact, technically sound derivation, well-written and well-organized.

Con-acceptance arguments: Limited scalability analysis, some ad-hoc aspects of initialization, theoretical convergence guarantees could be stronger.
