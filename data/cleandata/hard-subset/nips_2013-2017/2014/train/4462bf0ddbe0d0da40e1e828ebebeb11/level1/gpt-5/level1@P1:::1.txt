Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses learning the full kernel of a determinantal point process (DPP) from observed subsets by maximum likelihood. Since the log-likelihood is non-convex in the kernel entries and full learning is conjectured NP-hard, prior work has typically learned only restricted parameterizations (e.g., per-item weights/quality [Kulesza & Taskar UAI’11], mixtures of fixed kernels [Lin & Bilmes UAI’12], or Bayesian learning under a parametric form [Affandi et al. NIPS/ICML]). The authors propose a novel EM-style algorithm that reparameterizes the marginal kernel K via its eigendecomposition (eigenvalues and eigenvectors), introduces the latent subset of eigenvectors J (the “elementary DPP” index set), applies Jensen’s inequality to form a lower bound, and alternates:
- E-step: set q(J|Y) = pK(J|Y), which the authors show is a k-DPP with a computable kernel (conditional on |Y|).
- M-step: closed-form update for eigenvalues λj via k-DPP marginals, and a gradient step for eigenvectors V on the Stiefel manifold using a first-order update with line search.

They compare against projected gradient ascent on K (KA), which requires eigenvalue clipping to preserve PSD and λ ≤ 1. Experiments on a real Amazon baby registry recommendation dataset show EM provides higher test log-likelihoods (up to 16.5% relative gain in data-poor settings), is more robust to initialization, and is faster asymptotically and in practice for moderate N.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The latent-variable reformulation via the elementary DPP mixture is standard in DPP sampling and is cleverly repurposed for learning. The E-step is exact. The eigenvalue M-step is clean and efficient through k-DPP marginalization, yielding O(n N k^2) cost where k = max |Yi|. The eigenvector update is not a full maximization; the authors use a single first-order step employing a Stiefel manifold retraction and accept the step only if it increases the true likelihood (with an inner E-step to recompute q for the new parameters). This is a form of generalized EM (as per Neal & Hinton), but an explicit monotonicity/convergence guarantee is not stated; still, the line search on L mitigates instability in practice.
- Derivations: Key identities (e.g., conditional distribution being a k-DPP; eigenvalue update; initial gradient for V) are plausible and referenced to the supplement. Equation (20) being valid only for the first gradient step (q=p) is an important caveat; it would be useful to make the generalized-EM nature explicit and clarify whether later V-steps still ascend the bound or rely solely on likelihood checks.
- Experiments: The dataset is substantial and realistic; comparisons include two initializations (Wishart and moment-matching) and a lower-data regime, with clear analysis of when EM helps (strong off-diagonals). Runtime comparisons are provided. However, baselines are limited to KA; comparisons to the convex per-item weight model, mixtures of fixed L-ensembles, or Bayesian parametric approaches would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The background succinctly reviews DPPs, K–L relations, and the elementary DPP mixture. Algorithms are concisely presented. Some suggestions:
- Clearly state the algorithm is a generalized EM and discuss monotonicity of the lower bound vs. likelihood.
- Summarize the E-step lemma and k-DPP marginalization within the main text at a higher level (the supplement can retain details).
- Provide more guidance on numerical issues (ensuring λ < 1, conditioning of HYi, stability of the matrix exponential) and the cost of computing V̂ for QYi.

Originality:
Using the elementary DPP mixture and k-DPP conditional structure to derive an EM algorithm that learns the full eigenspectrum (λ and V) appears novel in the DPP learning literature. The combination with Stiefel manifold updates for eigenvectors is also new in this context. Prior ML work on DPPs has not provided a practical method to learn unconstrained kernels.

Significance:
Learning full DPP kernels is important for applications where negative interactions drive performance (diversity-sensitive recommendation, summarization, sensor placement). The proposed method offers a practical and robust alternative to naive projected gradient, with a better computational profile when k ≪ N. The empirical improvements on a real task and the runtime gains are promising. The main limitation is empirical scope: a single domain and lack of comparison to other learning paradigms beyond KA. Scalability to large N remains an open question due to O(N^3) components in the Stiefel update; discussing low-rank or Nyström approximations would broaden impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually novel and elegant EM formulation exploiting DPP eigenstructure and k-DPPs.
- Practical algorithm addressing known pitfalls of projected gradient (degenerate projections to near-diagonal K).
- Solid empirical gains in likelihood and runtime on a real dataset; thorough analysis of when EM helps.
- Clear exposition and publicly available code/data.

Cons for acceptance:
- No formal convergence/monotonicity guarantees beyond line-search on likelihood; partial M-step for V with an approximation (Eq. 20).
- Empirical evaluation limited to one application and one baseline; missing comparisons to other established DPP learning approaches.
- Scalability to large N not demonstrated; algorithm still requires O(N^3) operations per iteration for the manifold update.
- Some technical details deferred to supplement that are central to implementation.

Questions and suggestions:
- Can you provide generalized-EM convergence guarantees (e.g., monotone increase in L under your line-search scheme)?
- How sensitive is performance to the step-size schedule and the tolerance for λ close to 1?
- Could you include baselines for the convex per-item weight model and mixtures of fixed kernels, at least in a subset of categories?
- Discuss or demonstrate a low-rank variant (e.g., fixed-rank K, Nyström), which could reduce the O(N^3) term and improve scalability.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper makes a meaningful and original contribution to DPP learning with a practically effective algorithm and careful analysis; broader empirical comparisons and a clearer convergence discussion would strengthen it further.
