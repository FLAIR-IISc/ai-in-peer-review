

# Review: Expectation-Maximization for Learning Determinantal Point Processes

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in learning Determinantal Point Processes (DPPs): the inability to efficiently learn the full kernel matrix from data. Previous work had been limited to learning restricted parameterizations (e.g., single weights per row [2] or linear combinations of fixed kernel matrices [3]), while the full kernel learning problem was considered NP-hard [1]. The authors propose an EM-based approach that leverages the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix to avoid the problematic projection step required in naive gradient ascent methods (K-Ascent), which often results in degenerate solutions that fail to capture diversity.

The paper builds upon the DPP literature, including foundational work by Kulesza and Taskar [11, 12, 20] and recent NIPS papers on DPPs [13, 15, 19]. It clearly distinguishes itself from prior work by addressing the full kernel learning problem without restrictive assumptions, while maintaining the computational advantages of DPPs.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors derive a rigorous EM algorithm by reparameterizing the kernel using eigenvalues and eigenvectors, and they provide detailed derivations of the E-step and M-step updates. The theoretical justification for why the EM approach avoids the degeneracy issues of gradient ascent is compelling and well-explained. The experimental results on Amazon baby registry data are thorough, with multiple initialization strategies and comprehensive comparisons to K-Ascent. The paper correctly acknowledges limitations (e.g., both methods struggle with initial kernels with too many zeros) and provides meaningful analysis of when the EM approach provides the greatest advantage (in settings where negative interactions between items are important, as indicated by the d metric).

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The background section provides a comprehensive yet accessible introduction to DPPs, including their connection to marginal kernels and the key properties that make them useful for diversity modeling. The mathematical derivations are presented step by step with sufficient detail, making the paper accessible to a broad NIPS audience. The experimental setup is clearly described, and the results are presented in a way that directly supports the main claims. The authors provide pseudocode for both algorithms (Algorithms 1 and 2), which is particularly helpful for reproducibility.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant contribution to the DPP learning literature. While several NIPS papers have explored DPPs for various applications [13, 15, 19], none have proposed an efficient method for learning the full kernel matrix without restrictive assumptions. The EM approach using eigendecomposition is novel and addresses a key limitation in the field. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from previous work, citing relevant literature and explaining why prior methods are insufficient for full kernel learning. The authors' insight about the degeneracy of gradient ascent due to projection steps is particularly insightful and well-justified.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. DPPs have broad applications in machine learning (document summarization, recommendation systems, sensor placement, etc.), and the ability to learn the full kernel matrix from data greatly expands their applicability. The experimental results demonstrate clear practical benefits: up to 16.5% relative gains in test log-likelihood on a real-world product recommendation task, with the EM approach being both more robust and asymptotically faster than gradient ascent. The authors provide a concrete example (Figure 2) showing how the EM-trained DPP produces more reasonable recommendations than the gradient ascent method. The paper also opens up new research directions for DPP learning that could benefit many applications.

## Strengths vs. Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Novel EM approach to full kernel learning that avoids degenerate solutions
- Strong theoretical justification for the method
- Comprehensive experiments on a real-world dataset showing significant improvements
- Clear, well-organized writing with helpful pseudocode
- Demonstrated practical significance in product recommendation

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't explore the scalability of their method for extremely large N (e.g., N > 10,000), though the asymptotic runtime analysis suggests it would scale better than gradient ascent
- The initialization strategies (Wishart and moment matching) are somewhat limited; exploring more sophisticated initialization approaches could further improve performance
- The paper doesn't address how the EM approach would perform on datasets with very sparse subsets (e.g., where most subsets contain only 1 or 2 items)

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the DPP literature and addresses a significant gap in the existing work. The proposed EM approach is both theoretically sound and practically effective, with clear advantages over previous methods in both accuracy and efficiency. The experimental results are compelling, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. While there are some limitations in the scope of the experiments, these do not undermine the significance of the core contribution.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria at a high level: it's of high quality, clear, original, and significant. It represents a meaningful advancement in DPP learning that will be valuable to researchers and practitioners working with diverse set selection problems.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:** Novel approach to a fundamental problem in DPP learning, strong theoretical and empirical results, clear writing, significant practical impact, and a well-justified contribution to the field.

**Con-acceptance arguments:** Limited exploration of extreme scale scenarios and sparse data, but these are minor limitations for a paper of this quality.

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS. It represents a significant step forward in DPP learning that will be of interest to a broad audience in machine learning.
