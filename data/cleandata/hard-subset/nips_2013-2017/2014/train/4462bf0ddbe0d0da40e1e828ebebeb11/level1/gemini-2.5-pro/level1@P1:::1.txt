### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. The authors correctly note that maximizing the log-likelihood of the data is a non-convex problem, conjectured to be NP-hard. They situate their work effectively in the context of prior NIPS and related conference papers. Previous approaches have either learned a restricted, convex version of the problem (e.g., learning only a quality term for each item, as in Kulesza & Taskar, UAI 2011), or learned a mixture of DPPs with fixed kernels (Kulesza & Taskar, ICML 2011), which results in a model that is no longer a true DPP and loses some of its attractive computational properties. More recent work (Affandi et al., ICML 2013) has used Bayesian methods, but assumes a specific parametric form for the kernel.

The main contribution of this paper is a novel algorithm for learning the full, unconstrained kernel matrix. The key idea is to reparameterize the kernel via its eigendecomposition (eigenvalues and eigenvectors). By viewing the selection of a subset of eigenvectors as a latent variable, the authors cleverly frame the learning problem in an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework. This formulation allows them to derive an iterative optimization procedure that avoids the problematic projection step required by naive projected gradient ascent. The proposed EM algorithm provides closed-form updates for the eigenvalues and uses an elegant optimization technique on the Stiefel manifold for the eigenvectors, which naturally enforces the orthogonality constraint. The authors demonstrate their method's effectiveness on a real-world product recommendation task, showing it to be more robust and faster than a projected gradient ascent baseline.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation of the EM algorithm is sound and well-motivated. The insight to treat the eigenvector selection as a latent variable is the core technical contribution and is executed well. The subsequent steps, particularly identifying the posterior over the latent variables as a *k*-DPP (Lemma 1) to enable efficient computation in the E-step, and using a principled manifold optimization for the M-step update of the eigenvectors, are sophisticated and appropriate. The claims are strongly supported by the experimental results. The experiments are well-designed, comparing against a sensible baseline (projected gradient ascent) under different initializations and data regimes. The analysis of the baseline's failure mode (degenerating to a near-diagonal kernel due to projection) is convincing and clearly motivates the need for the proposed method. The work is presented as a complete and usable algorithm.

A minor weakness is that the M-step for the eigenvectors V is not a full optimization but rather a single gradient step. The authors state that the formula provided for the gradient (Eq. 20) is only exact at the beginning of the M-step (when q=p). While this pragmatic approach appears to work well in practice, it is a slight deviation from a classic EM coordinate ascent procedure. This could perhaps be clarified or discussed further.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for the problem is clear, the background on DPPs is concise yet sufficient, and the development of the algorithm is presented in a logical and easy-to-follow manner. The authors do an excellent job of first presenting the baseline, explaining its potential pitfalls, and then showing how their proposed EM algorithm elegantly circumvents these issues. The figures and tables are clear and effectively communicate the experimental results. The level of detail provided seems sufficient for an expert to reproduce the work, especially with the promise of supplemental material for detailed derivations.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While EM is a well-known meta-algorithm, its application to learning DPP kernels in this manner is novel. The specific formulation, using the eigendecomposition to introduce a latent variable and then deriving the corresponding E- and M-steps, is a significant and creative contribution. This approach is fundamentally different from previous attempts at DPP learning, which either simplified the problem to make it convex or abandoned the DPP structure. The use of manifold optimization for the eigenvector update is also a non-trivial and novel choice in this context that avoids the issues of standard projection methods. The paper is well-referenced and clearly delineates its contributions from prior art.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is very significant. DPPs are an elegant and powerful model for capturing diversity, but their application has been somewhat hampered by the difficulty of learning the kernel from data. This paper provides a practical, robust, and efficient algorithm for this fundamental task. By enabling the learning of a full, non-parametric kernel, this work makes DPPs much more applicable to a wide range of problems in machine learning, including product recommendation, document summarization, and diverse search results. The demonstrated advantages in both robustness (avoiding poor local optima) and speed over the naive baseline suggest that this EM algorithm could become a standard tool for practitioners and researchers working with DPPs. The problem it addresses is important, and the proposed solution advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

### Arguments for Acceptance (Pro/Con)

**Pro:**
*   Addresses the central, difficult, and important problem of learning the full kernel for a DPP.
*   Presents a novel and elegant EM-based algorithm that cleverly utilizes the kernel's eigendecomposition.
*   The technical approach is sound, employing sophisticated techniques like *k*-DPP marginalization and Stiefel manifold optimization to create an efficient and principled algorithm.
*   Empirically shown to be more robust and computationally faster than the natural projected gradient ascent baseline on a real-world task.
*   The work significantly enhances the practical utility of DPPs, likely encouraging wider adoption and future research.
*   The paper is extremely well-written and clear.

**Con:**
*   The M-step for the eigenvectors is an approximation (a single gradient step) rather than a full optimization of the auxiliary function. While it works well, it is a minor theoretical limitation.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh the minor weakness. It is a high-quality, original, and significant contribution that solves a key problem in a popular and growing area of machine learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
