Summary
The paper addresses maximum likelihood learning of determinantal point processes (DPPs), where the kernel is a PSD matrix L (or the marginal kernel K = L(L+I)−1). Prior work largely avoided full kernel learning because the likelihood is non-convex and conjectured NP-hard; instead, earlier approaches learned only per-item weights (convex) or weights over mixtures of fixed kernels, or imposed parametric forms with Bayesian inference. This paper proposes an EM-style algorithm that reparameterizes the kernel by its eigenvalues and eigenvectors and introduces a hidden variable J indexing subsets of eigenvectors (the known elementary-DPP mixture view). The E-step sets q(J|Y) = pK(J|Y), which is shown to be a k-DPP (k = |Y|) with a kernel that can be constructed efficiently. The M-step has a closed-form eigenvalue update using k-DPP marginals, and a gradient step for eigenvectors constrained on the Stiefel manifold. Compared to naïve projected gradient ascent on K (KA), the method avoids eigenvalue projection that can drive K toward near-diagonal solutions, and has better asymptotic complexity when average set size k ≪ N.

Experiments on a large Amazon baby registry dataset compare EM to KA under two initializations (Wishart and moment-matching). EM achieves higher test log-likelihood on most categories (median improvements up to 16.5% in low-data settings) and runs about 2.1× faster.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on foundational DPP work (e.g., Kulesza & Taskar), uses the mixture-of-elementary-DPPs representation and k-DPPs, and contrasts against convex per-row weight learning and fixed-kernel mixtures. It also situates against Bayesian parametric DPP learning. The use of manifold optimization for orthogonality constraints follows established techniques.

Strengths
- Clear, principled reformulation: The EM framework via the eigen-decomposition and hidden J is natural and well-grounded in known DPP structure.
- E-step tractability: Showing q(J|Y) is a k-DPP with an explicit kernel enables efficient computation of needed marginals and avoids intractable sums.
- M-step efficiency: Closed-form eigenvalue updates via k-DPP marginals; eigenvector updates respect the Stiefel constraint with a standard multiplicative update.
- Avoids projection pitfalls: Empirically addresses a real failure mode of projected gradient on K (degenerate near-diagonal solutions).
- Complexity advantage when k ≪ N: Derivations suggest O(n N k^2) per EM iteration for the main computations, versus O(n N^3) for KA’s inverses, plus comparable N^3 costs for decomposition/updates.
- Empirical validation: Real dataset, test likelihood improvements across multiple categories, robustness to initialization, and runtime gains. Public code and data are a plus for reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines: Comparisons focus on KA. It would be informative to include (i) convex per-row weight learning [e.g., Kulesza & Taskar], (ii) parametric/Bayesian approaches (Affandi et al.), and (iii) fixed-kernel mixtures, to show when full-kernel EM is preferable in practice.
- Scope of evaluation: Only one application domain and solely likelihood-based metrics. Additional tasks (e.g., recommendation quality, diversity metrics) or other datasets (image search, summarization) would strengthen claims of general utility.
- Convergence guarantees: The algorithm is effectively a generalized EM with a single gradient step for eigenvectors and step-size selection that checks likelihood after updating q. Formal monotonicity or convergence guarantees are not provided; discussion of conditions ensuring non-decrease in L would help.
- Scalability: Although asymptotically favorable when k is small, the algorithm still incurs N^3 costs per iteration due to manifold updates/exp, which may limit very large N. Practical scalability beyond N≈10^2–10^3 is not explored; low-rank or structured approximations are not evaluated.
- Technical details deferred to supplement: Key derivations (E-step kernel construction, complexity of marginals, eigenvector gradient) and numerical stability near λ→1 are in the supplement; summarized proofs or robustness notes in the main text would improve clarity.

Quality
- Technical soundness appears solid: the EM lower bound is standard; E-step solution and k-DPP characterization are plausible; eigenvalue updates are derived cleanly; eigenvector update uses established Stiefel manifold machinery. Some complexity claims hinge on specialized linear algebra (e.g., exploiting rank-k structure); providing more explicit algorithms would aid verification.
- Empirical methodology is reasonable, with careful initialization considerations and train/test splits. The fairness of line-search/step-size tuning across methods seems acceptable, though more details on hyperparameters would help.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable. The motivation for moving to eigen-parameterization and the failure mode of KA are clearly explained. Suggestions: briefly sketch the E-step kernel derivation and the cost-saving steps for marginals in the main text; add a short discussion on numerical handling when λ is near 1.

Originality
- The EM formulation for learning a full DPP kernel via eigenvalues/eigenvectors and the specific computational tricks for k-DPP-based updates are, to my knowledge, novel. Prior work did not offer a practical full-kernel MLE procedure.

Significance
- Full-kernel learning is an important open problem in DPPs. Providing a workable algorithm that improves likelihood and runtime over a strong naïve baseline is a meaningful contribution likely to influence subsequent work and applications.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled approach to a long-standing learning challenge for DPPs.
- Sound derivations with efficient updates leveraging k-DPP structure.
- Demonstrated empirical gains in both accuracy (test likelihood) and speed.
- Broad relevance to subset selection problems where diversity matters.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited baselines and datasets; unclear performance relative to other practical learners.
- No formal convergence guarantees; reliance on generalized EM and line search.
- Scalability to very large N not demonstrated; remaining N^3 components may be bottlenecks.
- Dependence on supplement for key technical details.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to convex per-row weight learning and Bayesian parametric methods.
- Include at least one additional application/dataset and a task-oriented metric.
- Provide a monotonicity argument or empirical evidence of consistent non-decreasing likelihood per iteration.
- Discuss or experiment with low-rank/structured variants to scale N.
- Move key derivations and a brief numerical stability discussion into the main paper.
