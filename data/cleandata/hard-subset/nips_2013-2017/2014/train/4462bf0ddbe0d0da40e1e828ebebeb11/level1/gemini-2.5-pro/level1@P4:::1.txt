This paper addresses the problem of learning the kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) from a collection of observed subsets. The authors correctly note that this is a challenging non-convex optimization problem, conjectured to be NP-hard. They situate their work in contrast to prior approaches which either learn restricted, convex versions of the problem (e.g., learning only item quality scores [2]) or learn ensembles of DPPs which lose the attractive computational properties of a single DPP [3].

The main contribution is a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for maximizing the data log-likelihood with respect to the full kernel matrix. The key idea is to change the parameterization from the kernel matrix entries to its eigenvalues and eigenvectors. By leveraging the mixture representation of a DPP, where a DPP is a weighted sum of elementary DPPs corresponding to subsets of eigenvectors [21], the authors introduce a latent variable `J` that selects which eigenvectors are active. This formulation naturally leads to an EM algorithm. The E-step involves computing the posterior probability of the latent variable `J` given an observed subset `Yi`. The authors show that this posterior is itself a k-DPP and provide a closed-form expression for its kernel. The M-step updates the parameters (eigenvalues and eigenvectors) to maximize the expected log-likelihood. The update for the eigenvalues is shown to be a simple, closed-form expression, while the eigenvectors are updated via a single gradient step on the Stiefel manifold to maintain orthogonality.

The proposed EM algorithm is compared against a projected gradient ascent baseline (K-Ascent) on a real-world product recommendation task using a baby registry dataset. The experiments demonstrate that the EM algorithm achieves higher test log-likelihood and is more robust to initialization, particularly in settings where diversity (off-diagonal kernel entries) is important. The authors also show that their method is computationally faster than the baseline.

### Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the learning problem within an EM framework is a creative and effective way to tackle the non-convexity and positive semi-definite constraints. The derivation of the E-step, which shows that the posterior over the latent variables is a tractable k-DPP, is a significant technical result that makes the algorithm practical. The M-step updates are well-motivated; the closed-form update for eigenvalues is elegant, and the use of gradient ascent on the Stiefel manifold for eigenvectors is an appropriate way to handle the orthogonality constraint. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against a sensible baseline under various conditions (different initializations, data-poor vs. data-rich settings) and providing both quantitative and qualitative results.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and positions the work with respect to prior literature. The background section provides the necessary definitions for DPPs concisely. The step-by-step derivation of the EM algorithm, from the change of variables to the final E and M steps, is logical and well-explained. The authors also provide clear intuition for the failure modes of the baseline projected gradient method, which strengthens the motivation for their approach.

**Originality:** The proposed method is highly original. To my knowledge, this is the first practical algorithm for learning the full, unconstrained kernel of a DPP by direct likelihood maximization. The central idea of re-parameterizing the kernel by its eigendecomposition and casting the problem in a latent variable framework is novel in this context. It cleverly transforms the difficult constrained optimization problem over a matrix into separate, more manageable updates for eigenvalues and eigenvectors, thereby avoiding the problematic projection step that hampers the baseline method.

**Significance:** This work represents a significant advance for the practical application of DPPs. The difficulty of learning the kernel has been a major bottleneck for using DPPs in machine learning. By providing an effective and efficient algorithm for this task, the paper opens the door for wider adoption of DPPs in applications that require modeling diversity, such as product recommendation, document summarization, and search results diversification. The demonstrated improvements in both model quality and runtime over a natural baseline underscore the practical importance of the contribution. The public release of code and data further enhances the paper's potential impact.

### Weaknesses

1.  **M-step for Eigenvectors:** The M-step does not fully maximize the objective with respect to the eigenvectors `V`; instead, it takes a single gradient step. While this is a common and valid approach in generalized EM (GEM) algorithms, the paper could be more explicit about this, clarifying that the algorithm guarantees monotonic improvement in the objective function `F` but not necessarily a full maximization within each M-step.

2.  **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are convincing but are confined to a single dataset and application domain (product recommendation). While the results are strong, demonstrating the algorithm's performance on other canonical DPP tasks, such as document summarization, would further bolster the claims of general applicability.

3.  **Scalability:** The algorithm's runtime complexity contains an O(N^3) term due to eigendecompositions and matrix exponentiation, where N is the size of the ground set. While the paper shows it is faster than the baseline for the tested problem sizes (N ≈ 71), this complexity may limit its applicability to problems with very large ground sets (N >> 1000). A brief discussion of this limitation or potential avenues for scaling (e.g., using approximations) would be welcome.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental, difficult, and important problem in the DPP literature: learning the full kernel matrix.
*   Proposes a novel and elegant EM-based solution that is technically sound and well-motivated.
*   The method empirically outperforms a strong baseline in terms of both model quality and computational efficiency on a real-world task.
*   The paper is very well-written, making a complex topic accessible.
*   The work is likely to have a significant impact on the use of DPPs in practice.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation is limited to one application domain.
*   The M-step for the eigenvectors is a single gradient ascent step, not a full maximization.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It makes a clear and significant contribution to the field by providing a novel, practical, and effective solution to an important open problem.
