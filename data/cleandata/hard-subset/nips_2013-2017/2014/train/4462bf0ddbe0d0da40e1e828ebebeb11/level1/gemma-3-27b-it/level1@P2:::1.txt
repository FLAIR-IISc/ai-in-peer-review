This paper addresses the challenging problem of learning the kernel matrix for a Determinantal Point Process (DPP) directly from data, moving beyond previous work that focused on restricted learning settings. The core idea is to reparameterize the kernel in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors and then apply an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm with a lower-bound on the likelihood. The authors demonstrate empirical gains on a product recommendation task compared to a projected gradient ascent approach.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the work within the existing literature on DPPs and subset selection. The background section provides a concise yet sufficient overview of DPPs and relevant mathematical notation. The description of the EM algorithm is detailed and logically presented, with clear explanations of each step. The experimental setup is well-defined, and the results are presented in a clear and compelling manner. The inclusion of runtime comparisons is a valuable addition.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed EM algorithm represents a significant step forward in DPP learning, tackling the full kernel matrix learning problem which was previously considered intractable. The reparameterization using eigenvalues and eigenvectors is a clever idea that enables the derivation of an effective optimization procedure.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical justification for the EM algorithm, based on lower-bounding the likelihood, appears sound. The derivations of the update equations for the eigenvalues and eigenvectors seem correct, and the computational complexity analysis is helpful.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, showing improvements in test log-likelihood compared to the baseline K-Ascent method. The analysis of the impact of the initialization strategy and the runtime comparison further strengthen the empirical validation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, even for readers not intimately familiar with DPPs. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are clear and concise.
*   **Significance:**  Learning DPPs effectively opens doors to better modeling of diversity and quality tradeoffs in various applications. The demonstrated gains in a product recommendation task suggest the potential for real-world impact.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Implementation:** While the theoretical analysis is clear, implementing the EM algorithm seems non-trivial, particularly the Stiefel manifold update for the eigenvectors. The paper doesn’t fully address the practical challenges of implementation.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are limited to a single application (product recommendation) and a relatively specific dataset. Exploring the performance of the algorithm on other datasets and tasks would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Dependence on Initialization:** The performance of the algorithm appears to be sensitive to the initialization strategy. While the authors explore two initialization methods, further investigation into the impact of different initializations could be beneficial.
*   **Justification of Simplifications:** The simplification of the eigenvector update step (using only one gradient step) is not thoroughly justified. A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs involved in this simplification would be helpful.
*   **Connection to Related Work:** While the paper cites relevant work, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the connections to other optimization techniques for non-convex problems, such as alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM).

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in DPP learning.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.
*   The potential impact of this work on various applications is significant.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The implementation complexity might be a barrier to adoption.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
*   The sensitivity to initialization is a concern.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of DPP learning. While there are some weaknesses, the novelty, technical soundness, and empirical validation outweigh these concerns. The paper addresses an important problem, proposes a novel and effective solution, and is clearly presented. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, specifically adding a discussion of practical implementation challenges and expanding the experimental evaluation.
