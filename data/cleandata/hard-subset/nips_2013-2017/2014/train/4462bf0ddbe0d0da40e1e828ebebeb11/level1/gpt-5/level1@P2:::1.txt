Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles the long-standing challenge of learning a full Determinantal Point Process (DPP) kernel from observed subsets by maximum likelihood. Prior work largely avoided the non-convexity and suspected NP-hardness of optimizing over the kernel entries [1] by either learning item-wise quality weights with a fixed similarity structure (a convex problem) [2], fitting mixtures/linear combinations of fixed kernels [3], or using parametric Bayesian approaches [8], each of which restricts expressiveness or sacrifices DPP properties. The authors propose a novel EM-style algorithm that reparameterizes the marginal kernel K via its eigenvalues/eigenvectors, introduces a latent subset of eigencomponents J using the known mixture-of-elementary-DPPs view [21], lower-bounds the log-likelihood via Jensen, and alternates: (i) an E-step computing q(J|Y) which is shown to be a k-DPP with a closed-form kernel; (ii) an M-step with a closed-form update for eigenvalues (posterior inclusion probabilities averaged over data) and a Stiefel-manifold gradient step for eigenvectors. They compare against naïve projected gradient ascent on K (“KA”). On an Amazon baby registry dataset, EM improves test log-likelihood over KA (up to 16.5% in low-data regimes) and is ~2× faster, particularly when off-diagonal structure (negative interactions) matters.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The EM construction leverages standard DPP identities and the elementary DPP mixture [21]. The E-step result (q(J|Y) is a k-DPP with kernel QY) is plausible and, with a proof in the supplement, appears sound. The eigenvalue M-step reduces to computing k-DPP marginals, which can be done via eigen-decomposition of QY, yielding O(n N k^2) complexity for k = max|Yi|. The eigenvector update uses a Stiefel manifold exponential map (first-order variant of [26]), with a line search that checks improvement in the true likelihood L, making the procedure a generalized EM [25]. One caveat is that only a single gradient step is taken per M-step, and the gradient formula (Eq. 20) is exact for q = p; this is an approximation but empirically seems effective. The authors acknowledge the need to clamp λ < 1 to avoid blow-up in QY; this is reasonable but breaks exact feasibility at the boundary.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are careful about initialization (Wishart vs moment matching), analyze regimes where KA fails due to projection-induced degeneracy, and report both accuracy (test log-likelihood) and runtime. However, evaluation is limited to one application domain. There are no comparisons to Bayesian DPP learning [8] or other recent scalable DPP learning methods (e.g., Nyström approximations [15]) that could provide broader context. Predictive metrics beyond log-likelihood (e.g., MAP subset quality or held-out item prediction given partial sets) would strengthen the case.
- Reproducibility: Algorithms, complexity analysis, and a code link are provided. Some key derivations are relegated to the supplement but the main steps are clear.

Clarity
The paper is well-written and well-organized. The motivation for eigen-parameterization and the failure mode of projected gradient is clearly explained. Algorithms 1–2 and complexity discussion are easy to follow. A few suggestions:
- State explicitly that the procedure is a generalized EM without monotonicity guarantees on the bound, but with monotonic increases in the true likelihood enforced via line search.
- Clarify computational costs of the E-step: what is the per-datapoint cost to form QY and diagonalize it, and are there opportunities to reuse computations across data with shared sizes?
- Provide more intuition for Eq. (20) and its restriction to the initial gradient (q = p).

Originality
The core novelty is applying EM to full-kernel DPP learning by introducing eigencomponent subsets J as latent variables and working in eigen-space. While the mixture interpretation and k-DPP machinery are known [3,21], using them to derive a practical EM algorithm with closed-form eigenvalue updates and Stiefel-manifold eigenvector updates is, to my knowledge, new. The paper differentiates itself from prior convex or parametric approaches by directly tackling the full, unconstrained kernel.

Significance
Learning expressive DPPs is important for many applications (summarization, recommendation, sensor placement). Providing a viable algorithm for full-kernel learning is valuable. The empirical gains (especially when diversity interactions matter and data are limited) and runtime improvements over KA indicate practical impact. Limitations include scalability to very large N (O(N^3) steps remain in the V update line search) and the focus on a single domain; nonetheless, this is a meaningful advance that many researchers/practitioners can build upon (e.g., combining with low-rank structure, featureized kernels, or priors).

Strengths
- Conceptually elegant EM using the eigen-decomposition and k-DPP posterior; closed-form eigenvalue updates.
- Avoids degeneracy from PSD projection in naïve gradient methods; more robust to initialization.
- Clear complexity advantages when k ≪ N; empirically faster than KA.
- Solid analysis of when EM helps (categories with stronger off-diagonal signal).
- Code and detailed algorithms provided.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited baselines and datasets; no comparison to Bayesian learning [8] or scalable approximations [15]; no task-based evaluations beyond log-likelihood.
- Convergence theory is limited (generalized EM with single-step V updates); sensitivity to step-size and re-E steps could be discussed further.
- No regularization or structural prior on K; potential overfitting risks in high-N, low-n regimes.
- Numerical stability near λ≈1 is handled ad hoc by clamping.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and principled algorithm for a key open problem in DPP learning.
- Demonstrated robustness and speed gains over a widely used baseline.
- Clear exposition and potential for broad impact in diverse ML applications.

Arguments against acceptance
- Narrow empirical scope; missing stronger baselines and predictive/task-based evaluations.
- Partial theoretical guarantees (approximate M-step, reliance on line search).
- Scalability to larger N not demonstrated.

Suggestions to authors
- Add a second dataset (e.g., document/image summarization) and a predictive evaluation.
- Compare to at least one Bayesian/parametric DPP learner and a low-rank/approximate method.
- Explore light regularization (e.g., nuclear norm or Frobenius penalty) and/or featureized kernels to reduce overfitting.
- Discuss convergence properties as a generalized EM and provide ablations on step-size/number of V updates per M-step.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper addresses an important and challenging problem with a novel, well-motivated algorithm, and provides empirical evidence of practical benefits. With broader evaluation and a bit more convergence discussion, this would be a very strong contribution.
