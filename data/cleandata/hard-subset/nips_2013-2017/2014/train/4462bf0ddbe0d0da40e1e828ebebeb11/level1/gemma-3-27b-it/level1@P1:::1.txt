This paper proposes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning the full kernel matrix of a Determinantal Point Process (DPP). The authors address a known NP-hardness result for DPP learning by cleverly reformulating the problem in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors, allowing them to derive a tractable optimization procedure. This is a significant contribution, as prior work focused on more constrained learning settings. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and empirically validated on a product recommendation task.

**Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

DPPs are powerful models for diverse subset selection, finding applications in numerous fields.  The core challenge lies in learning the kernel matrix *L* which parameterizes the DPP.  Direct maximum likelihood estimation is computationally intractable. Previous approaches, such as [2] and [3], tackled this by either learning row weights of *L* or learning weights for a fixed set of DPPs, sacrificing generality. Bayesian approaches [8] require prior assumptions on the kernel. This paper bypasses these limitations by working with the eigendecomposition of *L* and applying an EM-style algorithm. This builds on the known eigendecomposition relationship between *L* and the marginal kernel *K* [2], but crucially uses it to *avoid* the projection step that plagues naive gradient ascent (as pointed out by the authors). The paper effectively frames the learning problem as finding an appropriate eigenvalue decomposition, offering a fresh perspective. The authors clearly position their work within the existing literature, accurately highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of previous approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The EM algorithm presented is a novel approach to DPP learning, circumventing the limitations of previous methods.  The reformulation in terms of eigenvalues and eigenvectors is insightful.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the EM algorithm appears theoretically sound, leveraging Jensen's inequality and established properties of DPPs. The authors provide sufficient mathematical detail, though further details are relegated to the supplementary material.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the baby registry dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over projected gradient ascent (K-Ascent).  The ablation study regarding initialization strategies (Wishart vs. moment matching) provides valuable insights.
* **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise. The motivation and intuition behind each step are well-articulated.
* **Practical Relevance:** The product recommendation application is a compelling example of the DPP’s utility, and the experimental setup feels realistic.
* **Runtime Analysis:** The authors provide a runtime analysis comparing their EM algorithm to K-Ascent, indicating potential scalability benefits.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the theoretical framework is clear, implementing the EM algorithm, especially the Stiefel manifold update for the eigenvectors, might be challenging. The reliance on the supplementary material for some derivations adds to this complexity.
* **Step Size Selection:** The authors mention a trial-and-error approach for step size selection within the M-step. While acknowledged, a more systematic strategy for choosing the step size would strengthen the algorithm's robustness.
* **Scalability Discussion:** While runtime analysis is presented, a discussion of scalability to much larger datasets (beyond the N ~ 71 scale in the experiments) would be beneficial.  The O(N^3) components of the runtime could become limiting.
* **Limited Comparison:** The primary comparison is with K-Ascent.  Comparing to other related methods, even if they are more constrained (e.g., the method of [8]), would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
* **Dataset Details:** While dataset details are mentioned, more comprehensive information about the product categories and the nature of the "negative interactions" would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Significant Advance:** The proposed EM algorithm represents a significant advance in DPP learning, addressing a long-standing challenge in the field.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The empirical results demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness and robustness.
* **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and easy to follow.
* **Potential Impact:** The method has the potential to impact a wide range of applications that benefit from DPPs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Implementation Complexity:** The complexity of implementing the algorithm might limit its immediate adoption.
* **Scalability Concerns:**  The O(N^3) runtime components could be a bottleneck for very large datasets.  While addressed, more detailed discussion would be helpful.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel and effective solution to a challenging problem in DPP learning. The strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses. The algorithm is theoretically sound, empirically validated, and clearly explained.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors could consider: 1) adding a brief discussion of potential strategies for automatic step size selection, 2) expanding the discussion of scalability to larger datasets, and 3) briefly comparing performance to other DPP learning methods beyond K-Ascent (even if it's a high-level comparison).
