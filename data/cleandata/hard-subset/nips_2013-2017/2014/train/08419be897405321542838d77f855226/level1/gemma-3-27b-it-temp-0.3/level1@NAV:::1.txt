## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper explores the application of machine learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and propose using both n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to guide the search for equivalent, but less complex, expressions. The core idea is to learn from simpler identities to accelerate the discovery of more complex ones, circumventing the exponential search space inherent in brute-force approaches. This work builds upon existing research in symbolic computation (e.g., Maple, Mathematica [6, 27]) by incorporating learning, and draws inspiration from NLP techniques like n-gram modeling and RNNs [2, 22, 23]. The problem is well-motivated, addressing a long-standing challenge in automating mathematical reasoning.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a novel and interesting approach to a difficult problem. The attribute grammar framework provides a structured way to represent and manipulate symbolic expressions. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the learning-guided search, particularly for identities beyond the reach of brute-force methods. The discovery of previously unknown identities is a significant contribution. The code release ([https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning](https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning)) is excellent and promotes reproducibility. The learned representation of symbolic expressions using RNNs is a particularly promising aspect, potentially opening avenues for applying other ML techniques to symbolic domains.

**Weaknesses:** The scope of the grammar is limited (homogeneous polynomials of a single matrix), which restricts the applicability of the approach. While the authors acknowledge this, a more detailed discussion of potential extensions would be beneficial. The performance of the RNN is somewhat overshadowed by the n-gram models, and the reasons for this are not fully explored. The experimental evaluation focuses on a relatively small set of expression families. While the results are compelling within those families, it's unclear how well the approach would generalize to other types of mathematical expressions. The discussion of related work, while extensive, could be more focused on directly comparable approaches. For example, the connection to automated theorem proving [5, 9, 11] could be strengthened.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a challenging problem.
*   Demonstrated ability to discover previously unknown identities.
*   Strong experimental results within the defined scope.
*   Excellent code release.
*   Potential for broader impact in symbolic computation and AI.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of the grammar.
*   RNN performance is not clearly superior to n-grams.
*   Evaluation is limited to a few expression families.



**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. While the limitations are acknowledged, the novelty of the approach, the compelling experimental results, and the potential for future work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding the generalization of the approach and a more in-depth analysis of the RNN performance.
