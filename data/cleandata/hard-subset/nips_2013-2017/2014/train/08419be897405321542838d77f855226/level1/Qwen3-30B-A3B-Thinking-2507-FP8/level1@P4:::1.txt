

**Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"**

This paper introduces a framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The authors propose an attribute grammar-based approach to represent symbolic expressions and explore equivalent expressions with lower computational complexity. Given the exponential growth of the search space with expression complexity, they introduce two learning-based strategies: an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns from simpler expressions to guide the search. The paper demonstrates that these approaches can discover identities intractable for brute-force search, with examples reducing complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) or from exponential to polynomial time.

The work relates to attribute grammars (Knuth, 1968), symbolic computation engines (Maple, Mathematica), theorem proving (Cook, 1971), and NLP techniques (Luong et al., 2011; Socher et al., 2011). The authors properly contextualize their contribution as applying NLP-inspired techniques to a new domain rather than proposing entirely new algorithms.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a clear, well-structured technical approach to a meaningful problem.
- Experimental results (Fig. 3) convincingly demonstrate that learning-based strategies significantly outperform random search for complex expressions.
- The use of integer-based descriptors to avoid numerical issues in expression matching is a practical and well-justified solution.
- The framework demonstrates tangible benefits with concrete examples (e.g., reducing matrix operation complexity).
- The paper makes a compelling case for practical applications in machine learning (e.g., for dropout, RBM partition function approximation).

**Weaknesses:**
- The problem scope is significantly restricted (homogeneous polynomials of single matrix/vector variables), limiting generalizability.
- The paper doesn't adequately address how the framework would scale to more complex mathematical domains requiring additional grammar rules (e.g., trigonometric functions).
- While related work in theorem proving is acknowledged, the authors don't fully explain why previous approaches haven't succeeded for this specific problem, weakening the novelty claim.
- The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze why the RNN underperforms compared to n-grams for certain patterns, despite the authors' claim of novelty in applying RNNs to symbolic domains.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound with appropriate theoretical grounding. The paper provides sufficient experimental evidence to support claims, though the restricted problem formulation somewhat limits broader impact. The authors carefully address potential weaknesses (e.g., numerical issues via integer descriptors).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The examples (Examples 1 & 2) effectively illustrate the approach. Methodology is clearly explained, though some technical details about the RNN implementation could be more precise.

**Originality:** The application of n-gram models and recursive neural networks to mathematical identity discovery is novel. While the underlying techniques (n-grams, RNNs) are established, their application to this specific problem represents a meaningful contribution. The paper properly distinguishes itself from related work in symbolic computation.

**Significance:** The discovered identities have potential practical applications in machine learning. The framework demonstrates a promising approach to combinatorial search problems that could have broader implications for AI. The paper acknowledges the restricted scope but shows how it can be extended.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**
- Pro: Novel application of machine learning to a new domain, technically sound approach, clear experimental results demonstrating significant improvements over baseline methods, potential for practical applications.
- Con: Restricted problem scope, limited discussion of limitations, some technical details could be more precise, RNN approach doesn't outperform n-grams in evaluated cases.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by applying machine learning techniques to a domain where they've been underexplored. While the scope is limited, the approach is well-motivated and demonstrates clear improvements over baseline methods. The work advances the state of the art in combinatorial search by showing how learning can guide exploration of large symbolic spaces.

The primary limitation is the restricted problem formulation, but this is acknowledged by the authors and common in initial explorations of new techniques. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (quality), clearly written (clarity), applies machine learning to a new domain (originality), and demonstrates potential for practical impact (significance).

This paper should be accepted at NIPS for its innovative application of learning techniques to symbolic computation, its sound methodology, and its demonstrated ability to discover mathematically interesting identities that would be challenging to find through other means.
