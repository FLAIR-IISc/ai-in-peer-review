- Presents a novel method combining attribute grammars with learned strategies (n-gram, RNN) to guide search for efficient mathematical identities.  
- Frames identity discovery as search in a grammar-defined expression space and uses learning to direct that search.  
- Well-motivated connection to theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation; emphasizes learning over hand-designed heuristics.  
- Use of attribute grammars provides a structured framework for representing and manipulating symbolic expressions.  
- Technical approach is sound; n-gram and RNN methods are well-described and appropriately applied.  
- Experiments are reasonable and show learning approaches outperform brute-force search, especially for higher-degree expressions.  
- Integer representation modulo a prime is a clever fix for floating-point inaccuracies.  
- Ablation studies (n-gram orders, RNN dropout) are valuable and informative.  
- Paper lacks a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the search space and convergence/performance guarantees for the learning algorithms.  
- Claim of discovering “many new mathematical identities” needs stronger substantiation and broader comparison to mathematical literature.  
- Scope is limited (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix/vector), which restricts immediate applicability.  
- Writing is generally clear and organized; notation and examples help explain the approach.  
- RNN architecture description and a diagram could be clearer; connection between learned representation and search strategy needs strengthening.  
- Supplementary material is essential for understanding the specific identities discovered.  
- Combination of attribute grammars and ML for this problem is original and distinguishes the work from prior theorem-proving and symbolic computation approaches.  
- Reported identities appear new, bolstering originality, though verification is needed.  
- Potential impact is considerable: automated discovery of efficient identities can aid scientific computing and ML.  
- Overall recommendation: strong contribution with promising results; accept, but address theory, scope, and substantiation concerns.
