- Proposes an ML-guided framework that searches attribute-grammar expression trees to find analytically equivalent but computationally cheaper mathematical identities.
- Represents symbolic expressions as trees from an attribute grammar and checks equivalence using a numerical descriptor over a finite field.
- Tackles exponential search spaces by training models on solutions for simpler expressions to guide search for harder targets.
- Introduces two learning strategies: an n-gram model capturing common sub-patterns and a recursive neural network (RNN) learning continuous embeddings of expressions.
- Demonstrates the approach on multiple expression families, finding novel identities that reduce naive exponential or high-degree costs to O(n^2)/O(n^3) for some targets.
- Positions the work relative to automated theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation, highlighting its use of statistical learning rather than hand-crafted rules.
- Highlights the novelty and potential impact of automatically discovering efficient algorithms for scientific computing and ML.
- Uses an appropriate, coherent technical design (attribute grammar, numerical equivalence check, learned representation).
- Shows strong empirical results where learning-based methods outperform random search, validating the core idea.
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides sufficient methodological detail for replication.
- Evaluation is limited to homogeneous single-variable scalar polynomials, leaving generalizability to broader domains unclear.
- The RNN underperforms compared to the simpler n-gram model in the main task, and the paper lacks deep analysis of this failure.
- Unclear explanation of how the search finds sets of trees for linear-combination solutions versus generating individual candidate trees.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, highly original, well-presented work with significant potential despite scope and analysis limitations.
