- Proposes a framework to automatically discover computationally efficient equivalents of symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar and learned search.  
- Represents the search space of expressions via an attribute grammar and seeks analytically equivalent but lower-complexity forms.  
- Uses machine learning to learn search strategies because brute-force enumeration is infeasible.  
- Proposes two learned search strategies: a simple n-gram model and a more complex recursive neural network (RNN).  
- Trains models with curriculum learning, using solutions for simpler expressions to guide harder cases.  
- Uses a "numerical descriptor" (evaluations over a finite field Z_p) for fast, robust equivalence checking without symbolic manipulation.  
- Allows constructing solutions as linear combinations of multiple expression trees for complex targets.  
- Demonstrates effectiveness on five expression families, finding several new non-trivial identities and outperforming random search.  
- Paper is technically strong; components and methods are sound and well-integrated.  
- Numerical descriptors over Z_p are a clever, practical solution to equivalence checking.  
- Experimental design and curriculum setup are solid and results strongly support the central claim.  
- System discovered novel identities that significantly reduce complexity (e.g., exponential to polynomial).  
- Minor weakness: simpler n-gram models consistently outperform the RNN; deeper analysis of this discrepancy is lacking.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, with clear descriptions of methodology and experiments.  
- Minor clarity gaps: unclear how the set of M trees for linear systems is populated and lacks justification for the n-gram weighting (10^k).  
- Highly original: novel synthesis of attribute grammars with ML and learning continuous representations for symbolic expressions.  
- Potentially high significance: opens a new direction for ML in symbolic math with implications for RBMs, theorem proving, and program synthesis.  
- Limitation: current framework is restricted to homogeneous polynomials, which limits immediate generality.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, creative approach; strong empirical results; introduces continuous representations; very well written.  
- Rejection arguments: limited scope to homogeneous polynomials and RNN not outperforming simpler models weakens the deep-learning claim.
