This paper explores the application of machine learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent expressions using an attribute grammar and then employ n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to guide the search for equivalent, but lower-complexity, expressions. The work builds on attribute grammar concepts [14] and incorporates techniques from NLP like n-gram language models and RNNs [22, 23]. It aims to address a gap in existing symbolic computation systems (e.g., Maple [6], Mathematica [27]) which primarily rely on rule-based simplification and lack learning capabilities. The authors demonstrate successful identity discovery, including examples beyond manual derivation or exhaustive search, and provide code and data for reproducibility.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed framework, combining attribute grammars with learning techniques, is well-defined. The experimental setup is reasonable, and the reported results support the claims. The use of numerical and learned representations for symbolic expressions is well-motivated and appears effective. However, the integer-based descriptor using modulo arithmetic (Zp) feels a bit like a workaround for floating point precision issues, and the justification for choosing 'p' isn’t fully clear. The ablation studies comparing different n-gram sizes and dropout rates in the RNN are helpful. A minor weakness is the limited discussion of the computational cost of training the RNN, especially as expression complexity increases. It would be helpful to provide some details on training time and resource requirements. The reliance on a fixed cutoff time in experiments introduces potential bias; reporting the percentage of runs reaching the cutoff would strengthen the analysis.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. The descriptions of the attribute grammar and learning approaches are understandable. However, the presentation of Example 1 and 2 feels a bit abrupt; a more gradual introduction to the concepts would improve readability. The notation is mostly clear, although the use of several different subscripts (e.g., k, n, i, j) can occasionally be confusing. The description of the RNN architecture, while generally understandable, could benefit from a clearer visual representation (e.g., a diagram). A minor quibble is the use of Matlab-style syntax which may not be familiar to all readers.

**Originality:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for symbolic identity discovery is novel. While attribute grammars have been used in other contexts [10, 19, 26], their application to this particular problem, coupled with the learning component, is a significant contribution. The use of an RNN to learn a continuous representation of mathematical structures is also a novel idea. The authors adequately reference related work, including theorem proving [5, 9, 11], program induction [18, 28], and probabilistic programming [12, 20], properly positioning their work within the existing literature. The discovery of previously unknown mathematical identities further strengthens the originality claim.

**Significance:** The results are important. Discovering efficient mathematical identities can have a significant impact on computational performance in various applications. The demonstrated ability to reduce computational complexity from O(n^3) to O(n^2) or even O(n^2) from exponential time is impressive. The potential applications in areas like machine learning (e.g., optimizing RBM computations) further enhance the significance. The work opens up new avenues for research in automated symbolic computation and provides a promising approach for leveraging machine learning to tackle challenging problems in mathematics and computer science. The availability of code and data encourages further exploration and reproducibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of attribute grammars and machine learning.
*   Demonstrated ability to discover complex mathematical identities.
*   Potential for significant computational performance improvements.
*   Clear writing and well-defined experimental setup.
*   Availability of code and data for reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The integer-based descriptor feels like a workaround.
*   Limited discussion of RNN training costs.
*   Potential bias introduced by the fixed cutoff time in experiments.
*   Some notation could be improved for clarity.
*   The scope is limited to homogeneous polynomials with a single variable which restricts applicability.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and promising approach to a challenging problem. The technical contributions are significant, and the results are compelling. Despite some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh the drawbacks, and I recommend acceptance.
