

# Peer Review: Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors introduce an attribute grammar representation for symbolic expressions and develop two learning-based search strategies (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to efficiently navigate the combinatorially large space of possible expressions. The key insight is that learning from simpler expressions can guide the search for more complex ones, enabling the discovery of identities beyond brute-force reach. The work builds on attribute grammars (Knuth, 1968) and draws inspiration from NLP techniques like n-gram models and recursive neural networks (Socher et al., 2011; Luong et al., 2013), applying them to the symbolic computation domain. The paper demonstrates success on several families of polynomial expressions, including cases where naive evaluation would require exponential time but can be computed in polynomial time.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with careful attention to the computational challenges of symbolic identity discovery. The numerical representation using integer modulo p to avoid floating-point issues is particularly clever and well-justified. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with multiple families of expressions tested across varying degrees, and the results clearly demonstrate the superiority of learning-based approaches over random search for complex targets. The RNN representation learning (Section 4.2) is well-executed with appropriate architectural choices (identity initialization, rectified linear units, curriculum learning) that achieve high classification accuracy (≤5% error rate).

**Originality:** The core contribution—applying machine learning to guide the search for efficient mathematical identities—is highly original. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work in theorem proving (Bridge et al., 2013), symbolic computation engines (Maple, Mathematica), and attribute grammar applications (Cheung & McCanne, 2000). The authors properly cite the relevant NIPS work on recursive neural networks (Socher et al., 2011) while demonstrating a novel application to symbolic mathematics.

**Significance:** The work has significant potential impact. The discovered identities could improve computational efficiency for important ML problems (e.g., RBM partition function approximation, dropout implementation). The framework demonstrates a promising approach to guiding combinatorial search, with broader implications for AI problems involving combinatorial explosions. The paper effectively articulates these applications in Section 8.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with helpful examples (Examples 1 and 2) that illustrate the approach effectively. The technical details of the grammar framework, representation learning, and search strategies are presented in a logical flow. The supplementary material (referenced but not included here) appears to contain additional valuable examples.

## Weaknesses

**Scope Limitations:** While the paper demonstrates impressive results on specific polynomial families, the framework is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with a single matrix or vector input (Section 2). This limitation is acknowledged but not sufficiently addressed—extending to more general mathematical expressions would be necessary for broader impact. The authors mention potential extensions in Section 8, but these remain speculative.

**Evaluation of Learning Approaches:** The paper shows that n-gram models outperform the RNN for certain families (Fig. 3), but doesn't thoroughly explain why. The RNN is presented as a more sophisticated approach, yet the experimental results suggest it's less effective for the problems studied. A deeper analysis of when and why each approach succeeds would strengthen the paper.

**Comparative Baselines:** The paper compares learning-based approaches to random search but doesn't benchmark against other symbolic computation techniques or state-of-the-art simplification tools (e.g., Mathematica's Simplify function). This makes it difficult to assess the absolute performance gain.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**For Acceptance:**
- Novel application of machine learning to mathematical identity discovery
- Demonstrated success on previously undiscovered identities with significant computational savings
- Well-executed technical approach with strong experimental validation
- Clear connection to broader AI challenges (combinatorial search)

**Against Acceptance:**
- Limited scope of mathematical expressions considered
- RNN approach underperforms compared to n-grams for the problems studied
- Insufficient comparison to existing symbolic computation tools

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with clear novelty and technical merit. The framework represents a meaningful advance in applying machine learning to symbolic computation, and the discovered identities offer tangible computational benefits. While the scope is somewhat limited, the authors provide a compelling foundation that could be extended to broader mathematical domains. The paper is well-written and makes a significant contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the scope limitations more thoroughly and provide additional context about how the approach compares to existing symbolic computation tools. The work is of high quality, originality, and significance for NIPS, and would be valuable to the community.
