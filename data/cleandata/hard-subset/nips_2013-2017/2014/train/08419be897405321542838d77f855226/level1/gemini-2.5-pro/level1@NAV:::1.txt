This paper presents a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar and then search the space of possible expression trees for one that is analytically equivalent to a target expression but has lower computational complexity. Recognizing that brute-force search is intractable, the authors introduce two machine learning models to guide the search: a simple n-gram model and a more sophisticated recursive neural network (RNN). These models are trained on solutions found for simpler expressions to help discover identities for more complex ones. This work lies at the intersection of symbolic computation, program synthesis, and machine learning, relating to prior work in theorem proving but distinguishing itself through its data-driven, learning-based approach to discovering heuristics, rather than using hand-crafted rules. The application of NLP techniques, such as n-grams and RNNs (reminiscent of work by Socher et al. on parsing), to a symbolic mathematics domain is particularly novel for the NIPS community.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed framework is well-defined, and the experimental validation is thorough. The authors test their methods on five challenging families of expressions, demonstrating clear improvements over a random search baseline. The claims are well-supported by the results, and the authors are honest about the limitations, such as the current focus on homogeneous polynomials.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides clear motivating examples that make the problem and solution immediately understandable. The progression from the grammar framework to the representation and search strategies is logical and easy to follow.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The application of machine learning to guide the search for mathematical identities is a novel concept. While attribute grammars and RNNs are existing tools, their combination and application in this domain are new. The idea of learning a continuous vector representation for symbolic expressions is a significant contribution that could enable further applications of ML in symbolic domains.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this research is substantial. The framework could lead to tools for automatic code optimization in compilers and scientific computing libraries. The discovery of new, efficient identities for expressions relevant to machine learning (e.g., RBM partition function terms) is of direct interest to the community. More broadly, it presents a compelling demonstration of how learning can tackle combinatorial search problems in symbolic AI.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Highly novel and creative application of machine learning to a fundamental problem in symbolic computation.
*   Strong empirical results that clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed learning-based search strategies over a non-learning baseline.
*   Significant potential impact on both theoretical research (applying ML to mathematics) and practical applications (code optimization).

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The scope is currently restricted, primarily to homogeneous polynomials. While a necessary starting point, this limits immediate general-purpose use.
