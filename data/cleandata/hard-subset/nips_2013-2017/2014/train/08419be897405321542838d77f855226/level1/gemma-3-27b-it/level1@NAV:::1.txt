## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper explores the application of machine learning to discover efficient mathematical identities, specifically focusing on finding computationally cheaper equivalent expressions. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework to represent symbolic expressions and address the exponential search space by employing n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to guide the tree search. They demonstrate the ability to derive identities beyond what is achievable through brute-force or manual derivation, providing examples of reducing complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) and even achieving polynomial time complexity from exponential runtimes.

This work builds upon several areas. The use of attribute grammars (Knuth, 1968) for representing expressions is a solid foundation, and the authors correctly position their work concerning symbolic computation engines like Maple and Mathematica, highlighting the novelty of their learning-based approach. The connections to theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming are also well-placed. The borrowing of techniques from NLP (n-grams, RNNs – Luong et al., 2013; Socher et al., 2013) is a central and insightful contribution.  Recent work on learning heuristics (Bridge et al., 2014) is acknowledged, appropriately differentiating this work by learning the heuristics themselves, rather than hand-designing them.

**Strengths:** The core idea of leveraging learning to navigate the complex space of mathematical identities is novel and promising. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating the ability to discover identities that are non-trivial and offer significant computational improvements. The introduction of a learned representation for symbolic expressions using RNNs is particularly interesting and could have broader applications. The code release is a significant plus for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:** While the problem is clearly defined, the scope is somewhat restricted (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix/vector). The RNN results, while promising, are not consistently superior to n-gram models, and the reasoning behind the dropout rate selection could be elaborated. The experimental evaluation primarily focuses on a few specific families of expressions; exploring a wider range would strengthen the conclusions. The limitations of the learned representation are not fully discussed – what types of expressions might be particularly challenging for the RNN to represent effectively? Finally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of *why* the learned strategies succeed, beyond simply stating that they “capture common structure.” 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Novel application of machine learning to a challenging symbolic problem.
* Compelling experimental results demonstrating significant computational improvements.
* Interesting and potentially impactful learned representation of symbolic expressions.
* Open-source code.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
* Limited scope of the problem addressed.
* RNN performance is not consistently superior to simpler baselines.
* Lack of in-depth analysis of why the learned strategies work.



**Overall:** I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. This paper presents a significant contribution to the field, demonstrating a novel and effective approach to discovering mathematical identities. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
