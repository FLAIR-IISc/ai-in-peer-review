

# Peer Review: "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework to represent symbolic expressions as trees, and develop learning-based search strategies (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the exploration of the combinatorially large space of potential expressions. The key insight is that learning from simpler expressions can improve the search for more complex ones, enabling discovery of identities that would be intractable with brute-force methods.

The paper builds on several strands of work: attribute grammars (Knuth, 1968), symbolic computation engines like Maple and Mathematica, and NLP techniques including n-gram models and recursive neural networks. The authors make a compelling case for the novelty of applying these learning techniques to mathematical identity discovery, noting that existing symbolic computation systems are rule-based without learning capabilities. The closest related work appears to be Bridge et al. (2013) on learning heuristics for theorem proving, but the authors' approach differs significantly by learning directly from simpler expressions rather than using hand-designed heuristics.

**Quality:** The methodology is technically sound, with well-defined problem formulation and clear experimental design. The use of integer-based descriptors to avoid numerical issues in expression matching is clever and well-justified. The RNN representation learning (Section 4.2) is particularly impressive, with the authors providing detailed implementation insights (identity initialization, rectified linear units, curriculum learning) that contribute to high classification accuracy (≤5% error rate). However, the paper lacks sufficient theoretical analysis of why their learning approaches generalize well to more complex expressions. The experimental results demonstrate effectiveness but don't rigorously quantify the conditions under which the approach succeeds or fails. The restricted problem domain (homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree) is acknowledged but not thoroughly justified in terms of broader applicability.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good use of examples (particularly Examples 1 and 2) to illustrate the core concepts. The experimental results are presented clearly in Figure 3 and Table 2. However, some technical details could be improved: the exact structure of the RNN (beyond the brief description in Figure 2) and the implementation of the linear combination approach (Section 5) would benefit from more detail. The supplementary material (mentioned but not described in the main text) would be valuable for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The core idea of using machine learning to guide symbolic search for mathematical identities is genuinely novel in the NIPS context. The application of recursive neural networks to learn representations of symbolic expressions (Section 4.2) is particularly innovative and extends the use of RNNs beyond natural language processing. While the use of n-grams for sequence prediction in symbolic domains has precedents, the authors' specific application to mathematical identity discovery is new. The paper distinguishes itself well from related work in theorem proving and program induction.

**Significance:** The discovered identities (e.g., reducing O(n³) to O(n²) complexity) have clear practical potential, particularly for applications in machine learning where efficient computation of expressions like partition functions is important. The paper's connection to RBM partition function approximation (Section 8) demonstrates real-world relevance. However, the restricted problem domain (single matrix/vector, homogeneous polynomials) limits the immediate applicability. The authors acknowledge this in Section 8 but don't fully address how their framework could be extended to more general mathematical domains.

**Strengths:**
- Novel application of machine learning to mathematical identity discovery
- Well-designed experiments demonstrating the approach's effectiveness
- Effective use of integer-based descriptors to avoid numerical issues
- Intriguing demonstration of RNN learning continuous representations for symbolic expressions
- Practical relevance to computational efficiency in machine learning

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited theoretical analysis of why learning approaches generalize
- Restricted problem domain (homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree)
- Insufficient discussion of the approach's limitations and failure cases
- Some technical details (RNN structure, linear combination implementation) could be clearer
- Limited comparison to alternative symbolic search methods beyond random search

**Recommendation:** Accept

This paper makes a significant contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. While the problem domain is somewhat restricted, the approach is novel, well-executed, and demonstrates clear potential for practical applications. The authors provide a thorough experimental evaluation and insightful discussion of the approach's limitations and future directions. The work represents an important step toward integrating machine learning with symbolic reasoning, a promising direction for future AI research. The paper would be strengthened by more detailed theoretical analysis and a more thorough discussion of limitations, but these are minor concerns given the paper's overall quality and significance. The results are impressive, with the ability to discover identities beyond brute-force reach (e.g., the k=6 example in Example 2), and the approach has clear potential for broader application in mathematical computation.
