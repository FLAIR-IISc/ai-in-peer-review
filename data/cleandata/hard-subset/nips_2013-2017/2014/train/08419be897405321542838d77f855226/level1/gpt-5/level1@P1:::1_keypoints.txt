- Paper tackles automatic discovery of algebraically equivalent but cheaper expressions using an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic and elementwise operators.
- Expressions are expression trees that use the input exactly k times and produce a scalar; equivalences found via single trees or linear combinations.
- Exact equality is tested numerically using modular arithmetic descriptors over Z_p; linear combinations solved by Fw = t (mod p).
- Main challenge is exponential search space; solution is learned search strategies trained on simpler targets.
- Two learned strategies: n-gram models over subtrees and an RNN that learns embeddings then a policy for rule selection.
- Method discovers identities reducing O(n^3) to O(n^2) and finds polynomial-time forms for some exponential-sum families for small k.
- Experiments compare random, n-gram (n=1..5), and RNN strategies on several expression families.
- Use of attribute grammars and modular descriptors is a novel combination; linear-combination solving is elegant and efficient.
- Learned strategies are simple and effective; n-grams capture local patterns and RNN achieves low classification error (≤5%).
- Empirical results show substantial expansion of solvable instances versus random search, with clear wins on several families.
- Verification is a major concern: matching modulo a single prime is probabilistic and collision risk is not quantified.
- Paper should use multiple primes, CRT lifting, or final CAS checks and clarify how Z_p coefficients map back to Z/Q/R.
- Some complexity claims rely on restricting the grammar (e.g., removing matrix-matrix multiply), which is pragmatic but not a full optimization proof.
- Missing details hinder reproducibility: full grammar, cost model, number/choice of primes, false-positive bounds, scheduler specifics, and coefficient constraints.
- Figures/tables referenced are essential and must be complete in the camera-ready version.
- Originality: applying learned guidance (n-grams, RNNs) to symbolic identity discovery is novel and creative for the venue.
- Related work (superoptimization, CSE, Wilf–Zeilberger, polynomial identity testing) should be more fully acknowledged and cited.
- Significance: potentially impactful if identities are correct and generalize, with applications to compilers, CAS, and ML.
- Scope is limited (single variable, homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs) and hardest families remain unresolved beyond small k.
- Strengths include a clear grammar-based framework, effective learned search, low-error RNN embeddings, and public code/data.
- Weaknesses/suggestions: strengthen verification, compare to stronger baselines (beam/A*/MCTS, CAS, compiler optimizations), and provide runtime/speedup measurements and ablations.
- Recommend testing generalization (cross-family, multi-variable), clarifying cost model, and citing relevant PIT and sum-simplification literature.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel bridge between ML and symbolic computation, demonstrated nontrivial identity discovery, and clear methodology with substantial gains.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient correctness guarantees, limited evaluation and baselines, and scope restrictions partly enforced by grammar choices.
- Overall: compelling and original work that needs stronger correctness guarantees and broader evaluation; reviewer leans toward acceptance contingent on addressing concerns.
