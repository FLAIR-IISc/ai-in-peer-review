This paper presents a novel approach to discovering efficient mathematical identities by combining attribute grammars with machine learning techniques. The core idea – using learned strategies to guide the search within a grammar-defined space of expressions – is compelling and addresses a challenging problem. The authors effectively frame the problem as a search within a complex space, and the introduction of learning to direct this search is a significant contribution. The paper is well-motivated, clearly connecting to existing work in theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation, while highlighting the unique aspects of their approach (e.g., the use of learning rather than hand-designed heuristics). The application of attribute grammars to this problem is also a noteworthy aspect, providing a structured framework for representing and manipulating symbolic expressions.

**Quality:** The technical soundness of the paper is generally good. The use of attribute grammars and the proposed learning methods (n-gram and RNN) are well-described and appear appropriately applied. The experimental setup is reasonable, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the learning approaches, particularly for higher-degree expressions where brute-force search fails. However, the paper could benefit from a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the search space and the conditions under which the learning algorithms are guaranteed to converge (or at least, perform effectively). The numerical representation using integers modulo a prime number is a clever solution to address potential floating-point inaccuracies. The ablation studies comparing different n-gram orders and dropout rates in the RNN are valuable. The claim of discovering "many new mathematical identities" is bold and should be substantiated with a more comprehensive comparison to existing mathematical literature. The limitations regarding the scope (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix/vector) are acknowledged, which is positive.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, although some sections could be more concise. The notation is clear, and the explanations of the attribute grammar and learning methods are understandable. The examples (e.g., Example 1 and 2) are helpful for illustrating the problem and the proposed solution. However, the discussion of the RNN architecture could be further clarified with a more detailed diagram. The connection between the learned representation and the search strategy isn't immediately obvious and could be strengthened. The supplementary material is essential for understanding the specific identities discovered.

**Originality:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for discovering mathematical identities is novel. While attribute grammars have been used in various contexts, their application to this specific problem is a significant contribution. The use of RNNs to learn a continuous representation of symbolic expressions and then employ it for guiding the search is also a unique aspect. The paper effectively distinguishes itself from existing work in theorem proving and symbolic computation by emphasizing the learning-based approach. The reported identities appear to be new, further strengthening the originality of the work. 

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is considerable. Discovering efficient mathematical identities can lead to significant performance improvements in various applications, particularly in scientific computing and machine learning. The learned representations of symbolic expressions could also be valuable for other tasks involving symbolic manipulation. The demonstration that machine learning can effectively guide the search for efficient identities is a significant step towards automating mathematical discovery. The work addresses a challenging problem in a novel and effective way, and the results have the potential to be used by both researchers and practitioners. However, the current scope of the grammar (homogeneous polynomials) limits the immediate applicability of the approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and original approach combining attribute grammars and machine learning.
*   Effective in discovering efficient identities that are difficult to find manually.
*   Demonstrates the potential of machine learning for automating mathematical discovery.
*   Well-motivated and clearly written.
*   Experiments show significant improvements over brute-force search.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Theoretical analysis could be strengthened.
*   Scope of the grammar is limited to homogeneous polynomials.
*   The claim of discovering "many new mathematical identities" requires more rigorous substantiation.
*   The connection between the learned representation and search strategy could be clearer.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field and should be accepted. The authors present a novel and effective approach to a challenging problem, with promising results. While some aspects could be further strengthened (e.g., theoretical analysis, substantiation of novelty), the originality and potential impact of the work are significant. I rate it as **Accept**.
