Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the problem of automatically discovering algebraically equivalent but computationally cheaper expressions for given target expressions. It represents expressions via an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic and elementwise operators, builds expression trees that must use the input variable exactly k times and produce a scalar, and seeks equivalences by either matching a single tree or a linear combination of trees. Exact equality is checked numerically using modular arithmetic descriptors over Z_p; for more complex targets, a linear system Fw = t (mod p) identifies linear combinations of previously generated trees that match the target.

The key challenge is the exponential search space. The paper proposes learned search strategies trained on solutions to simpler targets: (i) n-gram models over subtrees, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees, first via supervised classification of equivalence classes and then as a policy (softmax on top of embeddings) to predict the next rule. The approach discovers identities that reduce O(n^3) to O(n^2) and, for some exponential naive sums (RBM-like families), finds polynomial-time expressions for small k. Experiments compare random, n-gram (n=1..5), and RNN strategies on several expression families.

This work intersects theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, but focuses on homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, avoiding undecidability. It relates to CAS simplification (Maple/Mathematica) but differs by optimizing computational cost and using learned guidance. It borrows from NLP: n-grams and RNNs over trees (cf. Socher et al.) to learn representations of symbolic structures. The numerical identity testing resembles randomized polynomial identity testing ideas (e.g., Schwartz-Zippel), though not cited.

Quality
- Methods are clearly described and reasonable. Attribute grammars and integer modular descriptors are standard ingredients used in a novel combination. The linear-combination step via solving Fw=t (mod p) is elegant and efficient.
- The learned search strategies are simple yet effective; n-grams capture repetitive local patterns; the RNN yields low classification error (≤5%) for equivalence-class prediction and provides a policy for rule selection.
- Empirical evidence shows substantial expansion of solvable instances compared to random search, with clear wins on families like sum(AAT)^k and sum((A.*A)AT)^k. For the most challenging RBM-2 family, gains are limited but RNN finds k=5 faster than random.
- However, verification rigor is a concern. Matching modulo a single large prime gives only probabilistic correctness. The paper should quantify collision probability, use multiple primes/sizes, or provide a path to exact proof (e.g., CRT lifting or a final CAS check). Also, when solving in Z_p, mapping coefficients back to Z or Q needs clarification to ensure the discovered linear combination is valid over reals.
- Complexity claims are sometimes enforced by restricting the grammar (e.g., removing matrix-matrix multiply), which guarantees O(n^2) if a solution is found. While pragmatic, this is not an optimization per se; more precise operation-count comparisons and measured runtime on numeric inputs would strengthen claims.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, with clear motivation, setup, and examples. The RNN training details (identity init, ReLU, curriculum, dropout) are helpful.
- Some missing details hinder reproducibility/interpretation: exact grammar (Table 1) and cost model; how coefficients from Z_p are interpreted; number of primes/samples used; bounds on false positives; scheduler specifics (e.g., greedy vs beam, any backtracking); and how linear combinations are constrained (integer/rational coefficients, sparsity).
- Figures/tables referred to (e.g., examples, Table 2/3, Fig. 3) are essential to judge scope; ensure they are complete in the camera-ready.

Originality
- The core idea—using learned guidance to discover computationally efficient algebraic identities within a grammar—is novel for NIPS. Using an RNN to learn continuous embeddings of symbolic expressions and deploying it as a search policy is creative and extends prior NLP uses of RNNs to a symbolic algebra setting.
- The n-gram strategy is straightforward but its application to symbolic identities is, to my knowledge, new.
- Related areas (superoptimization, compiler strength reduction/CSE, Wilf–Zeilberger for sum simplification, randomized polynomial identity testing) could be more fully acknowledged for positioning.

Significance
- If the identities are correct and generalize, this is impactful: it demonstrates that learned guidance can push the boundary of combinatorial symbolic search, with potential downstream use in compilers/CAS and in ML (e.g., RBM partition approximations, closed-form dropout-like computations).
- The current scope is restricted (single variable, homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs) and the hardest family remains largely unsolved beyond small k. Nevertheless, showing nontrivial speedups and discovering previously unknown identities is promising.
- The learned embedding of trees may be useful for broader symbolic ML tasks.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated problem with an elegant grammar-based framework.
- Effective learned search strategies; substantial empirical gains over random.
- RNN representation learning for symbolic expressions with low classification error.
- Public code/data and many reported identities.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Verification: quantify failure probability of modular checks; use multiple primes/sizes and/or integrate a final exact proof (symbolic CAS or PIT theory). Explain how Z_p coefficients are converted to integer/rational coefficients and validated over R.
- Baselines: compare to stronger search heuristics (beam search, A*, MCTS), CAS simplification, and compiler optimizations (CSE/strength reduction). Show that Mathematica/Maple do not find these identities.
- Evaluation: report time-to-solution distributions, number of nodes explored, and measured numeric speedups on random inputs with realistic BLAS costs. Provide ablations (curriculum, identity init, dropout rates).
- Generalization: test cross-family transfer (train on one family, test on another), and multi-variable targets. Discuss extending grammar while controlling search termination.
- Clarify the cost model and provide formal operation counts for representative identities.
- Cite relevant work on polynomial identity testing and algorithmic sum simplification.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, timely idea bridging ML and symbolic computation; strong fit for NIPS.
- Demonstrated discovery of nontrivial, efficiency-improving identities beyond brute force and (claimed) human derivation.
- Clear methodology; learned guidance substantially enlarges solvable scope.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficiently rigorous verification of equivalence; potential risk of false positives from modular matching and coefficient lifting issues.
- Limited evaluation breadth and strong baselines; n-gram often outperforms RNN, and hardest cases remain unsolved beyond small k.
- Scope restricted to a narrow class of expressions; complexity reductions partly enforced via grammar restrictions rather than optimized.

Overall assessment
A compelling and original contribution that opens a new direction for learned symbolic discovery. With stronger correctness guarantees and broader evaluation, this could be highly impactful. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing verification and evaluation concerns.
