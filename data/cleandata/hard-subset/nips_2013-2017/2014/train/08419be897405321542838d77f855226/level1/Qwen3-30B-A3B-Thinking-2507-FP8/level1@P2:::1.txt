

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The authors propose an attribute grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions, combined with learning techniques to guide the search for efficient versions of target expressions. The key insight is that while brute-force search becomes intractable for complex expressions, learning from simpler expressions can significantly improve the search strategy. The authors implement two learning approaches: n-gram models that capture common substructures in solutions, and a recursive neural network that learns continuous representations of symbolic expressions. The paper demonstrates that these methods can discover identities with significant computational complexity reductions (e.g., from O(n³) to O(n²)), which would be difficult or impossible to find manually or via exhaustive search.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a clear methodology and thorough experimental evaluation. The authors carefully address the combinatorial explosion problem by introducing a learning-based search strategy, and they provide convincing experimental results across multiple families of mathematical expressions. The attribute grammar framework is well-defined, and the numerical representation approach (using Zp†) effectively handles the equivalence checking problem. The experimental results in Section 7.2 show that the learning approaches consistently outperform random search for complex expressions, with the 3-gram model achieving particularly strong results for repetitive patterns (as demonstrated in Example 2). The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the challenges with the RBM-2 family, and provides appropriate error analysis. The representation learning approach (Section 4.2) demonstrates high classification accuracy (≤5% error rate), validating the effectiveness of the continuous embedding approach. The paper is careful to avoid overstating results and acknowledges the restricted scope of their problem formulation.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem statement to methodology to evaluation. The examples (particularly Examples 1 and 2) effectively illustrate the core concepts, and the notation is consistently applied throughout. The authors provide sufficient technical detail for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the work, though the supplementary material would be helpful for full reproducibility. The distinction between the different expression families and their computational complexities is clearly explained. The writing is precise and avoids unnecessary jargon, making the technical content accessible to the NIPS audience.

**Originality:** This paper makes a significant contribution by applying machine learning techniques to the domain of mathematical identity discovery, a problem that has been largely unexplored with statistical learning methods. While attribute grammars have been used in other domains (as cited in the related work), their application to discovering mathematical identities is novel. The use of n-gram models and recursive neural networks for this specific purpose represents a fresh approach to a challenging combinatorial search problem. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work in theorem proving, program induction, and NLP, particularly by emphasizing the learning aspect rather than hand-designed heuristics. The connection to NLP techniques (n-grams, recursive neural networks) is well-motivated and appropriately applied to this new domain.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a meaningful problem with practical implications for computational efficiency in machine learning and mathematics. The discovered identities could potentially improve the efficiency of algorithms like dropout (related to RBM-1 family) or RBM partition function computation (RBM-2 family). The framework demonstrates a general approach for using machine learning to guide combinatorial search, which is relevant to broader AI challenges. The paper successfully shows that their approach can discover identities that would be difficult to find manually or via brute-force search, as evidenced by the examples provided. The demonstration that a recursive neural network can effectively learn continuous representations of symbolic expressions opens up new possibilities for applying machine learning to symbolic computation.

**Strengths:** 
- Novel application of machine learning to mathematical identity discovery
- Clear demonstration of computational complexity improvements
- Effective integration of attribute grammars with learning techniques
- Strong experimental results showing clear advantages over random search
- Thorough analysis of limitations and failure cases (RBM-2 family)

**Weaknesses:**
- The problem is somewhat restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree and single matrix/vector inputs, though this is acknowledged in Section 8
- Limited theoretical analysis of why the learning approaches work well for certain expression families but not others
- The paper doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison with symbolic computation tools like Maple or Mathematica, which could have strengthened the significance argument
- The RBM-2 family results (where all approaches performed worse than random search) could have been analyzed in more depth

**Recommendation:** This paper makes a strong contribution to the field by demonstrating a novel application of machine learning to a challenging combinatorial search problem in mathematics. The technical approach is sound, the results are compelling, and the significance is well-justified. While the problem scope is somewhat restricted, the authors acknowledge this and provide a clear path for future work. The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, offering both practical insights for computational efficiency and a new direction for applying machine learning to symbolic domains.

Overall, this paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS. It is technically sound, clearly written, original in its application of machine learning techniques to mathematical identity discovery, and significant in its potential to impact computational efficiency in machine learning algorithms. The minor limitations noted are acknowledged by the authors and do not detract from the overall contribution.
