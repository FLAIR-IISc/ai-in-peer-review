Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes using machine learning to discover computationally efficient identities equivalent to given symbolic expressions. The approach represents expressions via an attribute grammar whose production rules carry complexity attributes. The system searches over grammar trees to produce expressions S such that S(V) = T(V) but at lower computational cost. Because exhaustive enumeration is infeasible, the authors learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets. Two learned strategies are studied: (i) n-gram models over subtrees, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous embedding of expressions and a softmax policy to predict the next grammar rule. Equivalence of expressions is tested by a numerical descriptor constructed from evaluations on random inputs modulo a prime; more complex targets are matched via integer linear combinations of tree descriptors solved modulo p.

The work connects to automated theorem proving and program induction but deliberately restricts to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with scalar outputs to avoid undecidability and unbounded search. It also relates to grammar-guided optimization and to NLP techniques (n-grams, RNNs) applied here to symbolic expressions. Compared to symbolic algebra systems (Maple, Mathematica), the focus is not simplification per se but discovery of lower-complexity forms, guided by learning rather than hand-crafted rules.

Strengths:
- A clear grammar-based framework with complexity attributes and a practical equivalence-testing mechanism that enables large-scale search and learning.
- Novel use of RNNs to learn continuous embeddings of algebraic expressions, with good classification accuracy on equivalence classes up to degree 6.
- Learning to guide search demonstrably expands the set and complexity of identities discovered compared to random search; n-gram strategies are particularly effective for families with repetitive patterns.
- Open-source implementation and concrete case studies, including matrix identities and exponential-sum families (RBM-related), showing nontrivial speed-ups.

Weaknesses:
- Equivalence testing and linear-combination solving are probabilistic (random evaluations modulo p). The paper lacks formal bounds on collision probability and clear guidance on choosing N and p to guarantee correctness with high probability. A brief theoretical analysis (e.g., polynomial identity testing bounds) would strengthen soundness.
- The mapping from solutions of Fw = t over Zp to exact integer coefficients in the target ring is not fully specified. It is unclear what coefficient set is permitted in constructing S from multiple trees, and whether mod-p solutions always correspond to realizable combinations within the grammar.
- Complexity accounting relies on rule attributes and, in some experiments, removal of certain rules (e.g., matrix-matrix multiply) to force O(n^2) constructions. This can bias the search and leaves open whether discovered expressions are close to optimal. Also, there is at least one inconsistency in Example 1’s stated complexity (O(n(m+p)) vs the derived O(m(n+p))).
- Experimental evaluation lacks stronger baselines (e.g., beam search, MCTS, or heuristic-guided CAS) and does not assess verification against symbolic systems or formal proofs. Claims of “previously undiscovered” identities are not independently validated.
- Scope is restricted (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, mainly single variable), and the approach struggles on the most challenging family ((RBM-2)k), with learned strategies not outperforming random beyond k=5.

Quality:
- Methods are mostly sound and implemented carefully. The RNN training details (identity initialization, ReLU, curriculum, dropout) are appropriate and produce low error in equivalence classification.
- The search framework is well defined, but the probabilistic equivalence and linear combination steps need theoretical guarantees or post hoc formal verification. More detailed ablations (e.g., effect of descriptor length N, prime p, and softmax training set size) would clarify robustness.
- Evaluation metrics (fraction of successful runs within a time cap) are reasonable for a discovery task but could be complemented by time-to-solution distributions and scalability analyses.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clearly written and well organized with motivating examples. However, important details are missing or under-specified: full grammar rule set and allowed scalar coefficients; exact complexity accounting per rule; bounds on descriptor collisions; and how solutions modulo p are turned into concrete symbolic identities. Correcting the minor complexity misstatement in Example 1 and expanding the main text with a representative set of discovered identities would help.

Originality:
- Applying learned search policies (n-grams and RNN embeddings) to discover lower-complexity mathematical identities within an attribute grammar is novel. Learning continuous embeddings for symbolic math structures and using them to guide constructive search is an original and interesting contribution for the NIPS community (2014 timeframe).

Significance:
- The approach demonstrates that learning can materially assist combinatorial search in a symbolic domain, which is of broad interest. The discovered identities for matrix expressions and symmetric polynomials illustrate potential practical benefits. However, the lack of formal verification and limited scope reduce immediate impact; the work is best viewed as an enabling step opening a research direction.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide probabilistic guarantees for equality testing (e.g., bounds via polynomial identity testing) and guidance on N and p selection; report observed collision checks.
- Clarify the coefficient domain for linear combinations and how mod-p solutions map to valid expressions; if necessary, add a lifting/refinement step to recover integer/rational coefficients.
- Correct complexity statements and detail complexity attribution per rule.
- Compare against stronger baselines (beam/MCTS; CAS with heuristics) and include verification of discovered identities by a CAS or by formal proofs.
- Report time-to-solution curves and scalability with k and grammar size; ablate n-gram order and RNN capacity.
- Expand to multi-variable settings in experiments, or discuss limitations explicitly.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and well-motivated problem setting with a clear framework.
- Innovative integration of learned representations and search in a symbolic domain.
- Empirical evidence that learning substantially improves discovery over random search in several families.
- Opens promising avenues for applying ML to symbolic computation and program optimization.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient theoretical guarantees for the core equivalence and combination steps; claims of new identities are not formally validated.
- Limited baselines and scope; mixed results on the hardest family.
- Some clarity gaps (grammar/coefficients/complexity) and a minor correctness issue in an example.

Overall, this is a creative and timely contribution with solid potential, but it would benefit from strengthened theoretical assurances and broader evaluation.
