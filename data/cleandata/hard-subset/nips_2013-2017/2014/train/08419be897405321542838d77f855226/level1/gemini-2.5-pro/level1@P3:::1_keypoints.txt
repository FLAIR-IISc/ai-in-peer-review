- Proposes a novel framework to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities by representing symbolic expressions as trees from an attribute grammar.  
- Tackles the combinatorial explosion of expression trees by guiding search with ML models trained on solutions to simpler problems.  
- Uses a numerical descriptor (evaluate on random inputs, modulo a prime) to efficiently check equivalence instead of slow symbolic manipulation.  
- Introduces two learned search strategies: an n-gram model learning common substructures and an RNN learning continuous embeddings to predict grammar rules.  
- Demonstrates the framework on polynomial families relevant to ML, finding new non-trivial identities that are much more efficient than naive forms.  
- Novel fusion of program induction/symbolic computation with NLP-style statistical learning (n-grams, recursive neural nets) is a fresh direction.  
- Originality and significance: highly novel, ambitious approach with potential impact on algorithm optimization, automated science, and compilers.  
- Technical quality: solid formal basis with attribute grammar, pragmatic numerical descriptor, and sensible curriculum-based learning.  
- Learned continuous representation: RNN embeddings for symbolic expressions show high classification accuracy and promise for broader ML tools on symbolic domains.  
- Clarity: paper is very well-written with intuitive examples and helpful figures illustrating trees and RNN architecture.  
- Limited scope: experiments restricted to homogeneous single-variable polynomials with scalar output, unclear scalability to broader math or richer grammars.  
- Empirical finding: simple n-gram models often outperform the more complex RNN, raising questions about RNN advantages and task suitability.  
- Complexity of RNN strategy: multi-stage pre-training plus classifier—unclear if end-to-end or fine-tuning was explored and whether that would improve results.  
- Question on numerical descriptor: need for clarification on choice of sample size N and theoretical/empirical collision probability analysis.  
- Question on basis diversity: how does learning encourage generation of diverse basis trees needed for linear combinations, beyond mechanisms like dropout?  
- Question on benchmarking: compare discovered Symk identities to established methods (e.g., Newton's sums) to contextualize solution quality.  
- Overall conclusion: excellent, technically solid, and original work likely to inspire follow-up research; strong recommendation for acceptance.
