- Proposes a framework using attribute grammars and learning-based search (n-grams, RNNs) to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities.
- Uses integer modulo p representation to avoid floating-point issues and enables robust symbolic evaluation.
- Learning from simpler expressions guides search for complex identities, enabling discoveries beyond brute-force search.
- Demonstrates success on several families of homogeneous polynomials where naive evaluation is exponential but can be reduced to polynomial time.
- Technical approach is sound with careful treatment of computational challenges and clever numerical representation.
- Experimental evaluation is thorough across multiple expression families and shows learning-based methods outperform random search for complex targets.
- RNN representation learning is well-designed (identity init, ReLU, curriculum) and achieves high classification accuracy (≤5% error).
- The core idea—applying ML to guide symbolic identity search—is original and differentiates from theorem proving and symbolic computation systems.
- Work has potential impact on ML problems (e.g., RBM partition function, dropout) and more generally on combinatorial search in AI.
- Paper is clear and well-organized, with helpful examples and supportive supplementary material.
- Scope is limited to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with single matrix/vector inputs, and extensions to broader expressions are speculative.
- Lack of analysis explaining why n-grams outperform RNNs for some problems weakens the evaluation of learning approaches.
- Missing benchmarks against state-of-the-art symbolic computation tools (e.g., Mathematica) makes absolute performance gains unclear.
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, demonstrated computational savings, solid technical execution, and relevance to AI combinatorial search.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited scope, RNN underperformance relative to n-grams, and insufficient comparison to existing symbolic tools.
- Overall: strong, novel paper with high quality and significance; recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to address scope and comparative baselines.
