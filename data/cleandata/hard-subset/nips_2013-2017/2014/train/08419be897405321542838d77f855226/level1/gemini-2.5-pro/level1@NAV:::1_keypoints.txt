- Core idea: Use an attribute grammar to represent symbolic expressions and search for analytically equivalent expression trees with lower computational complexity.  
- Search challenge: Brute-force search is intractable, so the paper introduces learned heuristics to guide search.  
- Models used: Two ML models—an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN)—guide the search process.  
- Training strategy: Models are trained on solutions for simpler expressions to generalize to more complex ones.  
- Positioning: Bridges symbolic computation, program synthesis, and ML; differs from theorem proving by learning heuristics instead of hand-crafted rules.  
- Novelty: Applying NLP techniques (n-grams, RNNs) and learning continuous vector representations for symbolic expressions is a novel contribution.  
- Quality: Technically sound with thorough experiments on five challenging expression families and clear improvement over random search.  
- Clarity: Exceptionally well-written and organized with motivating examples and a logical progression of ideas.  
- Originality: Highly original combination of existing tools applied in a new domain, enabling ML-driven symbolic reasoning.  
- Significance: Strong potential impact for automatic code optimization, scientific computing, and discovering efficient identities relevant to ML.  
- Pros for acceptance: Novel approach, strong empirical results, and meaningful theoretical and practical implications.  
- Cons against acceptance: Current scope is limited—primarily focused on homogeneous polynomials, restricting immediate generality.
