- Problem: Discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities by searching an attribute grammar of symbolic operators with learning-guided search.
- Representation/equivalence: Expressions as trees; equivalence checked via randomized numerical descriptors over Zp and solved with modular Gaussian elimination when linear combinations are needed.
- Search challenge: Exponential search space tackled by learning from simpler targets using (i) n-gram subtree statistics and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that embeds expressions.
- RNN training/use: RNN trained for equivalence-class classification, then used to score next-rule choices during search.
- Empirical results: Tested on five expression families (matrix polynomials, elementwise-matrix variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, RBM exponent sums) and found learned strategies discover identities beyond brute force.
- Performance gains: Often reduced O(n^3) to O(n^2) and transformed exponential-time sums to polynomial forms.
- Model comparison: n-gram models generally outperform the RNN.
- Relation to prior work: Bridges grammatical program induction and symbolic algebra; relates to continuous/tree representations (Collobert & Weston; Mnih & Hinton; Socher et al.) and randomized polynomial identity testing ideas.
- Baselines missing: Lacks comparisons to strong heuristic searches (beam search, MCTS), superoptimization-style search, and CAS rewrites plus common subexpression elimination.
- Theoretical gaps: Needs formal failure-probability bound for the Zp descriptor method and analysis of collision risk and required sample size N.
- Evaluation/metrics issues: Success metric (fraction solved within 10 minutes) should be complemented by time-to-solution distributions and ablation studies (curriculum, dropout, softmax-only vs joint finetuning).
- Clarity/details: Generally readable but grammar rules/complexity attributes and scheduler policy (greedy vs beam/backtracking) should be specified more precisely.
- Originality: High — novel use of learned tree embeddings and n-gram statistics to guide discovery in attribute grammars.
- Significance/limitations: Promising for compiler rewrites and ML computations, but scope is narrow (homogeneous polynomials, single variable) and RNN gains are limited.
- Pros for acceptance: Novel formulation, clear empirical gains over random search, reusable learned representations, public code/data, discovery of nontrivial identities.
- Cons against acceptance: Incomplete baselines, limited theoretical guarantees for equivalence testing, narrow grammar/target scope, and weaker evaluation metrics.
