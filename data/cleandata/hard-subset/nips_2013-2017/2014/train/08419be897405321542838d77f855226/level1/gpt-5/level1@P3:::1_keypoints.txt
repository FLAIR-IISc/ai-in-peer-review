- Paper proposes learning-guided search over an attribute grammar to find analytically equivalent, cheaper reformulations of expressions using randomized modular evaluation as descriptors and linear combinations Fw = t mod p to assemble multi-tree identities.
- Two learning strategies: n-gram models over frequent subtrees and an RNN that embeds expressions and provides a softmax rule policy; experiments show n-grams (3–5-grams) usually outperform random search and often outperform the RNN.
- Relation to prior work: connects to algebraic simplification, theorem proving, program synthesis/superoptimization, polynomial identity testing (Schwartz–Zippel not cited), and recursive embeddings from NLP.
- Strength — clear DSL and complexity accounting: concrete grammar, typing and cost model create a clean search space for optimization.
- Strength — scalable equality checking: randomized evaluation modulo a prime enables practical large-scale search and the linear-combination solver is an effective way to assemble identities.
- Strength — curriculum learning and simple models: learning from small to large targets is effective; n-grams are simple yet strong and RNN embeddings are an interesting continuous representation with good classification accuracy.
- Strength — empirical gains and release: experiments show learning expands reachable identity complexity and code/dataset are released.
- Weakness — probabilistic equality testing: correctness guarantees are probabilistic; the paper should formalize failure probability via Schwartz–Zippel (dependence on p, N, degree) and use multiple primes/CR to reduce error.
- Weakness — modular linear combinations: solutions of Fw = t mod p may not lift to integer/rational/grammar-expressible coefficients; clarify allowed coefficient domain, lifting method (multi-prime + rational reconstruction), and final verification.
- Weakness — missing baselines/ablations: compare to CAS simplifiers (Mathematica/Maple), hand-crafted rewrite heuristics or greedy/A* search, structural heuristics (beam search, MCTS), and include beam-width/search-depth ablations.
- Weakness — known algorithmic results ignored: Symk has O(nk) DP/Newton algorithms and RBM moments relate to known combinatorial identities; compare to these to properly calibrate gains.
- Weakness — scalability of enumeration-based RNN training: enumerating all expressions up to k=6 may not scale to larger grammars/degrees; discuss curriculum versus exponential blow-up and consider end-to-end policy learning (REINFORCE/imitation).
- Weakness — search strategy myopia: current scheduler appears greedy; beam search and canonicalization for commutative/associative nodes could improve performance and should be reported.
- Clarity — provide full grammar in main text: include explicit typing, arity, cost model, closure properties, and whether scalar constants/negative coefficients are allowed.
- Clarity — move representative discovered identities to main: include complexity analyses and empirical speedups (wall-clock or operation counts) rather than relegating to supplement.
- Clarity — detail hardware and parallelization: describe 16-core setup, how 10-minute cutoff scales with k and n, and parallel search behavior.
- Originality — novel angle: learning-to-search over a math DSL is novel for the venue, and continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions are an interesting contribution.
- Significance — potentially high impact if identities yield true polynomial-time evaluations for naively exponential expressions and are verified with high confidence; current strongest wins are for repetitive-pattern families where n-grams suffice.
- Pros for acceptance: well-motivated problem, clean DSL, convincing evidence that learning improves search, interesting RNN embeddings, and released code/data.
- Cons for acceptance: incomplete treatment of correctness (PIT and coefficient lifting), missing comparisons to strong baselines (CAS and DP for Symk), RNN underperformance and lack of end-to-end learning, and many details left to the supplement.
- Recommendation: weak accept conditional on strengthening correctness guarantees (PIT analysis, multi-prime verification and coefficient lifting), adding comparisons to known methods (especially for Symk), and moving key identities/analyses into the main paper.
