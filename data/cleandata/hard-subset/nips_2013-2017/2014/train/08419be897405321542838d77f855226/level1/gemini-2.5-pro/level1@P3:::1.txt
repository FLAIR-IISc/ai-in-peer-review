This paper presents a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions as trees generated from an attribute grammar. The main challenge is the combinatorial explosion in the space of possible expression trees. To overcome this, the authors propose guiding the search using machine learning models trained on solutions to simpler problems.

The authors introduce two key components to make this feasible. First, to efficiently check for equivalence between a candidate expression and the target, they eschew slow symbolic manipulation in favor of a numerical "descriptor." An expression is evaluated on a set of random inputs to produce a unique vector signature. Second, and central to the paper's contribution, they propose learning-based search strategies to navigate the vast tree space. They explore two main approaches: a simple n-gram model, which learns common sub-structures from previously found solutions, and a more sophisticated recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions and uses it to predict the next grammar rule to apply. The framework is demonstrated on several families of polynomial expressions relevant to machine learning, discovering new, non-trivial identities that are significantly more efficient than their naive counterparts.

This work connects several fields in a novel way. While program induction and symbolic computation are established areas, the application of modern statistical learning techniques, particularly those from natural language processing like n-grams and recursive neural networks, is a fresh and promising direction. It differs from systems like Mathematica by learning search heuristics from data rather than relying on hand-crafted rules. The concept of learning a continuous embedding for symbolic math, akin to word embeddings in NLP, is particularly intriguing and has parallels with recent work on deep learning for structured data (e.g., Socher et al., 2013).

**Strengths (Pros):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength is its originality. Applying machine learning, especially deep models, to the discovery of mathematical identities is a highly novel and ambitious goal. The potential impact is significant; a successful system of this kind could be a powerful tool for algorithm optimization, automated science, and compiler design. The fact that the system discovers new, non-trivial identities for complex expressions (e.g., related to RBM partition functions) is a strong testament to its power and significance.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sound and well-thought-out. The attribute grammar provides a solid formal basis. The use of numerical descriptors (modulo a prime) is a clever and pragmatic solution to the problem of equivalence checking, making the large-scale search tractable. The incremental, curriculum-based learning approach (solving for degree k, then using those solutions to train for k+1) is well-motivated and sensible.
3.  **Learned Representation for Symbolic Math:** The development of a continuous representation for symbolic expressions using an RNN is a valuable contribution in its own right. The high classification accuracy reported in Table 2 demonstrates that the RNN can capture the semantic equivalence of different symbolic forms. This opens up the possibility of applying a wide range of standard machine learning tools to symbolic domains, which has been a long-standing challenge.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. The introductory examples immediately build intuition for the problem and the solution. The methodology is explained logically, and the figures (e.g., Fig 1 and 2) are very helpful in illustrating the tree structures and the RNN architecture.

**Weaknesses (Cons):**

1.  **Limited Scope of Expressions:** The framework is currently demonstrated on a restricted class of expressions: homogeneous polynomials of a single variable with a scalar output. While this is a rich enough space to demonstrate the core ideas, it is unclear how the approach would scale to more general mathematics involving non-homogeneous polynomials, multiple distinct variables, different data structures (e.g., sparse matrices), or a more complex grammar with recursion or transcendental functions.
2.  **Relative Performance of n-grams vs. RNN:** A surprising result is that the simpler n-gram models often outperform the more complex RNN-based search strategy. While this doesn't diminish the overall success of the learning-based approach, it does raise questions about the specific advantages of the RNN in this task. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of why this might be the case. It is possible the specific problem families chosen have solutions with highly regular, repetitive local structures that are perfectly captured by n-grams, and the RNN struggles to learn these long-range dependencies or overfits.
3.  **Complexity of the Search Strategy:** The RNN-based search strategy involves a multi-stage process: pre-training the representation, then training a softmax classifier on top. It would be interesting to know if the authors explored end-to-end training or fine-tuning of the representation for the search task itself, which might yield better performance.

**Detailed Questions and Suggestions:**

*   The numerical descriptor relies on a sufficiently large number of random samples `N` to avoid collisions. Could the authors comment on how `N` was chosen and whether they have any theoretical or empirical analysis of the collision probability?
*   The solution for more complex targets often requires finding a linear combination of basis trees. This implies that the search must generate a sufficiently diverse and useful set of basis trees before the linear solver can succeed. How does the learning strategy encourage this diversity, especially when it is trained to greedily follow high-probability paths? The use of dropout in the RNN seems to be one mechanism, but this could be elaborated on.
*   For the symmetric polynomial family (Symk), which is well-studied, how do the discovered identities compare to known efficient methods like Newton's sums? A comparison would help benchmark the quality of the discovered solutions against established mathematics.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel and exciting research direction. It is ambitious, technically solid, and presents compelling results. The fusion of symbolic manipulation with modern machine learning is highly original and likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up work. Despite some limitations in scope and the surprising performance of the simpler n-gram model, the contributions are significant and the paper represents a clear advance. It is a perfect fit for NIPS and I strongly recommend acceptance.
