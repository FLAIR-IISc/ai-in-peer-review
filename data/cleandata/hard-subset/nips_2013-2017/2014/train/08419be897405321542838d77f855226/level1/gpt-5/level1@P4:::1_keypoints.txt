- Paper proposes learning-guided search over an attribute grammar to find lower-complexity symbolic expressions equivalent to given targets.  
- Expressions are tested for equivalence via numerical descriptors from evaluations modulo a prime; linear combinations use integer linear solves modulo p.  
- Two learned policies are studied: subtree n-gram models and an RNN embedding with a softmax rule predictor.  
- Work is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree and scalar outputs to avoid undecidability and unbounded search.  
- Clear grammar-based framework with complexity attributes and a scalable, practical equivalence-testing mechanism.  
- Novel use of RNNs to learn continuous embeddings of algebraic expressions with good equivalence classification up to degree 6.  
- Learning-guided search expands the set and complexity of discovered identities versus random search; n-grams excel for repetitive families.  
- Open-source implementation and case studies (matrix identities, exponential-sum/RBM families) showing nontrivial speed-ups.  
- Equivalence testing and linear-combination solving are probabilistic; paper lacks formal bounds on collision probability and guidance for choosing N and p.  
- Mapping from modular solutions to exact integer/rational coefficients is under-specified; coefficient domain and realizability within the grammar are unclear.  
- Complexity accounting can be biased by rule removals (e.g., forbidding matrix-matrix multiply) and contains at least one inconsistent example complexity statement.  
- Experimental baselines are weak; missing comparisons to beam search, MCTS, heuristic CAS, and independent validation of “new” identities.  
- Scope is limited (homogeneous, scalar, mainly single-variable) and struggles on hardest families ((RBM-2)k) beyond k=5.  
- Methods and implementation details (RNN training, curriculum, dropout) are mostly sound and produce low error in classification.  
- Probabilistic equivalence and combination steps need theoretical guarantees or post hoc formal verification; more ablations (N, p, training size) are needed to assess robustness.  
- Evaluation metric (fraction successful within time cap) is reasonable but should be complemented by time-to-solution and scalability analyses.  
- Paper is generally clear and well organized but omits important details: full grammar, allowed scalar coefficients, per-rule complexity, collision bounds, and how mod-p solutions yield concrete identities.  
- Novelty: applying learned search policies and continuous embeddings to discover lower-complexity mathematical identities is original and promising.  
- Significance: demonstrates learning can aid combinatorial symbolic search with practical examples, but limited scope and lack of formal verification reduce immediate impact.  
- Suggested improvements: provide polynomial identity-testing bounds and N/p guidance; clarify coefficient domains and add lifting to integers/rationals; fix complexity statements and detail per-rule costs.  
- Suggested improvements (cont.): compare to stronger baselines and verify identities with a CAS or formal proof; report time-to-solution curves, scalability, and ablate n-gram/RNN hyperparameters.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel problem framing, clear framework, successful integration of learned representations and search, and empirical gains over random search.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient theoretical guarantees for equivalence/combination, limited baselines and scope, mixed results on hardest problems, and some clarity/correctness gaps.  
- Overall: creative and timely contribution with solid potential that would benefit from stronger theoretical assurances, broader evaluation, and clarification of missing details.
