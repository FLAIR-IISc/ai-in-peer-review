- Proposes learning-guided search to discover computationally efficient identities for symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar with cost attributes.  
- Uses randomized equivalence testing via evaluations over a large finite field (Zp) to filter candidates.  
- Allows linear combinations of multiple grammar trees by solving modular linear systems.  
- Learns search policies from simpler targets using n-gram models over subtrees and an RNN embedding that predicts next grammar rules.  
- Experiments on multiple expression families (matrix polynomials, mixed elementwise/matrix, symmetric polynomials, RBM sums) show substantial gains over random search.  
- n-gram models often outperform the RNN, though the RNN produces useful continuous embeddings and sometimes finds complex identities faster.  
- Clear formulation of identity discovery as grammar-constrained search with explicit cost modeling.  
- Practical, scalable equivalence testing via random evaluation enables fast filtering and data generation.  
- Effective curriculum learning and RNN embeddings with high classification accuracy on equivalence classes.  
- Demonstrated re-discovery and extension of nontrivial efficiency identities; repeated patterns captured well by n-grams.  
- Open-source code and dataset provided for reproducibility.  
- Equivalence testing is probabilistic and lacks formal error bounds or a final symbolic verification step.  
- Evaluation metrics are limited (fraction of runs within 10 minutes); missing are time-to-solution distributions, node expansions, and stronger search baselines (beam, MCTS, A*, SAT/SMT).  
- RNN’s added value over simpler n-grams is mixed; signs of overfitting without dropout.  
- Scope is restricted (homogeneous polynomials, single variable, scalar output), limiting generality to broader symbolic/compilation tasks.  
- Claims of “previously undiscovered” identities lack external validation or formal proofs.  
- Mapping modular linear-system solutions back to integer/rational coefficients and controlling coefficient magnitudes is under-specified.  
- Overall technically sound implementation, but missing formal guarantees and symbolic verification weakens proof of correctness.  
- Clarity is good overall, but some implementation details (exact grammar, p and N values, coefficient recovery) are missing or scattered.  
- Novel combination of attribute grammars, finite-field randomized testing, and learned search policies—high originality.  
- Potentially significant for automated algebraic optimization and compilers if scaled and verified formally.  
- Suggested improvements: provide formal error bounds, report p and N, add deterministic checks, include time/node metrics and stronger baselines, explain coefficient recovery, and incorporate algebraic invariances.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel contributions, nontrivial discovered identities, strong empirical improvements, public code/data.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of formal guarantees and verification, limited baselines and metrics, strong scope restrictions.  
- Overall recommendation: weak accept — promising and original but needs stronger formal verification and baseline evaluation.
