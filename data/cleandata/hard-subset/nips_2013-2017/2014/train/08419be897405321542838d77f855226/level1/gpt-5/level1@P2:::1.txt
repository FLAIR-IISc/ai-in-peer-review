Summary
The paper proposes using learning-guided search to discover computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target symbolic expression. Expressions are represented via an attribute grammar whose rules encode both allowable operations and their computational costs. The search space (trees of grammar rules) grows exponentially, so the authors (i) introduce a randomized descriptor-based equivalence test using evaluations over a large finite field; (ii) allow linear combinations of multiple trees by solving a modular linear system; and (iii) learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets. Two strategies are considered: an n-gram model over subtrees and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions and predicts the next rule. Experiments on several families of expressions (matrix polynomials, elementwise/matrix mixes, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related exponential sums) show substantial gains over random search; n-gram models often outperform the RNN, which nevertheless yields useful representations and occasionally finds more complex identities faster.

Relation to prior work: The setting intersects theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming but avoids undecidability by restricting to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree and scalar outputs. Unlike rule-based symbolic engines (Maple/Mathematica), the focus is on complexity reduction and learning to guide search. The attribute-grammar use aligns with earlier grammar-based optimization, while the RNN draws from NLP work on tree-structured models and embeddings. Prior ML-for-theorem-proving work that learns to choose heuristics is acknowledged; here the heuristics are learned end-to-end from data.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling formulation of identity discovery as grammar-constrained search with explicit cost attributes.
- Practical, scalable equivalence testing via random evaluation over Zp, enabling fast filtering and data generation for learning.
- Effective use of curriculum and RNNs to learn continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions; high classification accuracy on equivalence classes indicates the representation is meaningful.
- Demonstrated ability to re-discover and extend efficiency identities (e.g., reducing O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)); repeated patterns across k captured well by n-grams).
- Open-source code and dataset support reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Equivalence testing is probabilistic; the paper lacks a formal error bound or a final symbolic verification step. Citing and applying Schwartz–Zippel–DeMillo–Lipton bounds would strengthen claims and guide choices of p and N.
- Evaluation metrics focus on “fraction of runs that succeed within 10 minutes”; missing are time-to-solution distributions, node expansions, and comparisons to stronger search baselines (e.g., beam search, MCTS, A*, or SAT/SMT-based enumeration).
- The strongest reported performance often comes from n-grams; the RNN’s added value over simpler models is mixed, and its learned softmax sometimes overfits without dropout.
- Scope restrictions are substantial (homogeneous polynomials, single variable, scalar output). While understandable, this limits demonstrated impact on general symbolic tasks and compiler-like scenarios.
- Claims about “previously undiscovered” identities lack external validation or symbolic proofs; better grounding would improve significance.
- The linear-combination solver over Zp may produce modular coefficients; the mapping back to integer/rational coefficients and constraints on coefficient magnitudes are under-specified.

Quality
Technically sound overall: the grammar design, modular equivalence testing, linear system approach, and learning strategies are reasonable and carefully implemented. However, the lack of formal probabilistic guarantees and of symbolic verification leaves a gap between “empirical equality on many random points” and “proof.” Experimental breadth is good within the chosen families, but baseline comparisons and ablations could be stronger (e.g., search strategies beyond uniform random; effect of grammar choices; impact of representation size l and p, N). The claim of complexity reduction is plausible and illustrated with explicit examples, but a systematic accounting of operation counts for discovered identities would add rigor.

Clarity
Generally well-written and easy to follow, with motivating examples and sensible organization. Useful training details are provided (initialization, activation choice, curriculum, dropout). Some key implementation details are missing or scattered (exact grammar rule set and attributes; values for p and N; how integer coefficients are recovered from modular solutions). Figures/tables are referenced appropriately, though some identities are relegated to the supplement. Suggestions:
- Add a short subsection formalizing the equivalence-testing error probability and chosen parameters.
- Clarify the scheduler (e.g., depth-first vs beam, tie-breaking, backtracking).
- Explicitly list grammar rules and cost model in the main text.

Originality
High. Combining attribute grammars, finite-field randomized identity testing, and learned search policies for identity discovery is novel for NIPS. The use of RNNs to embed symbolic expressions and to guide rule selection is an interesting and nontrivial adaptation of NLP techniques. The n-gram idea is simple but effective in this domain; the overall system integration is original.

Significance
Moderate to high. If scaled and verified formally, this line of work could influence automated algebraic optimization in ML systems and compilers and enable new applications of ML to symbolic reasoning. Demonstrated improvements for selected families are meaningful, but scalability and generality remain open. The fact that n-grams often outperform RNNs tempers the representational significance; nonetheless, the learned embeddings themselves may be valuable for other symbolic tasks.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide formal error bounds for the equivalence test and report the actual p, N used; optionally include a final deterministic check (e.g., polynomial identity testing with different primes or symbolic proof).
- Report time-to-solution and node expansions; add beam search and MCTS baselines.
- Discuss recovery of integer/rational coefficients from modular solutions; constrain coefficients (e.g., small integers) and show their sizes.
- Evaluate against strong baselines in symbolic simplification (e.g., Mathematica/Sympy with factorization/collect) and compiler optimizations (CSE/strength reduction) on overlapping tasks.
- Incorporate algebraic invariances (commutativity/associativity) into the model or data augmentation; analyze whether this helps the RNN.
- Analyze correlation between RNN classification accuracy and identity-discovery performance.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and timely intersection of ML and symbolic computation; clear technical contributions.
- Demonstrated discovery of nontrivial efficiency identities beyond brute-force reach.
- Solid empirical evidence that learned policies dramatically improve search over random.
- Public code/data; opens a path for follow-up work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of formal guarantees and symbolic verification; claims of novelty of identities not externally validated.
- Limited baselines and metrics; strongest gains often from simple n-grams.
- Strong scope restrictions; unclear generalization beyond selected families.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper is original and well-motivated, shows convincing empirical benefits of learning-guided search, and provides a promising representation for symbolic expressions. Strengthening the formal verification and baseline comparisons would make it a clear accept.
