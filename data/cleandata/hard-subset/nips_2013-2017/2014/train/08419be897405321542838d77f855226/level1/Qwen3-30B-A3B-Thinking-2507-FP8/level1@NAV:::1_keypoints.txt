- Introduces a novel ML-based framework to discover efficient mathematical identities.  
- Uses an attribute grammar to represent symbolic expressions.  
- Proposes two learned search strategies: n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs).  
- Key insight: learning from simpler identities guides search for more complex ones.  
- Thorough evaluation across multiple mathematical families (e.g., symmetric polynomials, RBM-related expressions).  
- Learned continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions via RNNs are promising and meaningful.  
- Results show significant improvements over random search overall.  
- n-gram models outperform RNNs on repetitive pattern cases (Example 2).  
- RNNs show potential for capturing more complex structures despite weaker performance in some cases.  
- Well-situated in related work, building on attribute grammars and NLP techniques.  
- Clearly differentiates from non-learning theorem provers and from program induction focused on logical equivalence.  
- Strengths: novel framework, strong empirical results, clear experiments, and practical applications.  
- Weaknesses: restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, limiting generality.  
- Weaknesses: the hardest family (RBM-2) showed no improvement over random search.  
- Weaknesses: limited comparison with recent symbolic AI/neural theorem prover approaches.  
- Clarity: generally good but some technical details need expansion.  
- Overall: high quality and originality, moderate-to-high significance; reviewer recommends acceptance.
