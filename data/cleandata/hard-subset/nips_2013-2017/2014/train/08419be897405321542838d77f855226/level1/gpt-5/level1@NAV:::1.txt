This paper tackles the discovery of computationally efficient mathematical identities by searching over an attribute grammar of symbolic operators and using learning to guide the search. Expressions are represented as trees; equivalence to a target is checked via randomized numerical descriptors over Zp, and complex targets may require linear combinations of trees solved with modular Gaussian elimination. To overcome the exponential search space, the authors learn search strategies from simpler targets: (i) n-gram statistics over subtrees and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions (trained for equivalence-class classification, then used to score next-rule choices). Experiments on five expression families (matrix polynomials, elementwise-matrix variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related exponential sums) show that learned strategies discover identities beyond brute force, often reducing O(n3) to O(n2) and exponential-time sums to polynomial forms. The n-gram models generally outperform the RNN.

Relation to prior work: The approach bridges grammatical program induction and symbolic algebra with learning-guided search, echoing NIPS work on continuous representations (Collobert & Weston; Mnih & Hinton) and recursive models for trees (Socher et al.). It complements theorem proving, probabilistic programming, and grammar-guided optimization, while borrowing ideas akin to randomized polynomial identity testing (Schwartz–Zippel) for fast equivalence checks; conventional CASs (Maple/Mathematica) are discussed as rule-based baselines without learning.

Quality: Technically solid overall, with a clear pipeline and sensible training curriculum. However, missing baselines are notable: comparisons to strong heuristic searches (beam search/MCTS), superoptimization-style search, or CAS rewrites plus common subexpression elimination. The Zp descriptor method would benefit from a formal failure-probability bound (PIT) and analysis of collision risk and required N. Success metrics (fraction of runs within 10 minutes) could be complemented by time-to-solution distributions and ablations (curriculum, dropout, softmax-only vs joint finetuning).

Clarity: Generally well organized and readable. The grammar and complexity attributes could be specified more precisely (full rule table, constraints), and the scheduler policy (greedy vs beam/backtracking) should be explicit.

Originality: High. Using learned tree embeddings and n-gram statistics to guide discovery of algebraic identities via attribute grammars is novel.

Significance: Promising. The discovered identities and the methodology could impact compiler algebraic rewrites and ML objective computations, though current scope (homogeneous polynomials, single variable) and limited RNN gains temper immediate impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and well-motivated formulation; clear empirical gains over random search
- Reusable learned representation for symbolic structures
- Public code/data and discovery of nontrivial identities

Cons against acceptance:
- Incomplete baselines and limited theoretical guarantees for equivalence testing
- Narrow scope of grammars/targets; RNN underperforms n-grams
- Evaluation metrics could be stronger and more comprehensive
