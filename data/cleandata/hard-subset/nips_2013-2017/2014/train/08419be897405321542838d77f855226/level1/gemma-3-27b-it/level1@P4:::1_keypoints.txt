- Core idea: Use attribute grammars plus n-gram models and RNNs to search for computationally efficient equivalent mathematical expressions.  
- Methodology: Represent expressions with an attribute grammar and guide search using language-model style n-grams and learned RNN embeddings.  
- Motivation: Addresses gap in symbolic systems (Maple, Mathematica) by introducing learning-based identity discovery beyond rule-based simplification.  
- Empirical results: Demonstrates discovery of nontrivial identities, including ones not found by manual derivation or exhaustive search, with provided code and data.  
- Technical quality: Framework is well-defined, experiments reasonable, and numerical/learned representations appear effective.  
- Integer descriptor concern: The Zp modulo integer-based descriptor seems like a workaround for floating-point issues and choice of p is not fully justified.  
- Ablations: Useful ablation studies on n-gram sizes and RNN dropout are included and informative.  
- Training cost: Limited discussion of RNN training time and resource requirements as expression complexity grows.  
- Experimental bias: Use of a fixed cutoff time may introduce bias; reporting percent of runs hitting the cutoff is suggested.  
- Writing and clarity: Generally well-written and organized with clear motivation and contribution statements.  
- Presentation issues: Examples 1 and 2 are introduced abruptly and would benefit from smoother exposition.  
- Notation: Multiple subscripts (k, n, i, j) can be occasionally confusing and could be streamlined.  
- RNN description: Architecture explanation is adequate but would benefit from a clear diagram or visual.  
- Syntax choice: Use of Matlab-style syntax may not be familiar to all readers and could hinder accessibility.  
- Originality: Novel combination of attribute grammars with machine learning for symbolic identity discovery.  
- RNN novelty: Using an RNN to learn continuous representations of mathematical structures is a noteworthy contribution.  
- Literature positioning: Related work in theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming is appropriately cited and positioned.  
- Significance: Potentially large impact by reducing computational complexity (e.g., O(n^3) to O(n^2) or exponential to polynomial) with applications in ML and beyond.  
- Reproducibility: Code and data availability encourages further research and validation.  
- Acceptance pros: Novel approach, demonstrated discovery capability, tangible performance improvements, clear writing/experiments, reproducible artifacts.  
- Acceptance cons: Zp descriptor feels ad hoc, insufficient training-cost discussion, potential cutoff bias, some notational clarity issues, limited scope to homogeneous single-variable polynomials.  
- Recommendation: Strong paper with significant contributions and results; reviewer recommends acceptance.
