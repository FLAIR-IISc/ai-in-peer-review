This paper explores the application of machine learning to discover mathematically equivalent, but computationally cheaper, expressions. The core idea is compelling: leverage learning to navigate the exponential search space of possible symbolic transformations. The authors frame this as a search within an attribute grammar, employing both n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to guide the exploration. The reported results are promising, showcasing the discovery of identities beyond manual derivation or brute-force search.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing symbolic computation systems (rule-based, lacking learning) and highlighting the potential of a learning-based approach. The related work section is comprehensive, covering theorem proving, program induction, probabilistic programming, and relevant NLP techniques like n-grams and RNNs, positioning the work appropriately within the broader landscape of symbolic AI. The problem definition is precise, focusing on polynomial simplification with reasonable constraints to make the problem tractable. The use of attribute grammars provides a formal framework for representing and manipulating symbolic expressions.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of machine learning, particularly RNNs, to the discovery of mathematical identities is a novel and potentially impactful idea.
* **Technical Soundness:** The framework based on attribute grammars and the detailed explanation of the learning approaches (n-grams and RNNs) demonstrate technical depth. The use of integer-based descriptors to avoid numerical issues is a smart engineering detail.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the learning approaches, particularly the n-gram models, in discovering identities that are difficult to find using other methods.  The quantitative results (success rates) are presented clearly and the examples provided are illustrative.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The figures and examples help to clarify the concepts.
* **Availability of Code:** Providing the code and data is a significant contribution that facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **RNN Performance:** While the RNN is a theoretically appealing approach, its performance appears to be consistently worse than the n-gram models. The reasons for this are not thoroughly investigated. The dropout hyperparameter tuning seems limited. It’s unclear why the RNN struggles to capture patterns that the n-grams easily find. A deeper analysis of the learned representations could provide valuable insights.
* **Limited Scope:** The focus on homogeneous polynomials with a single variable, while simplifying the problem, limits the applicability of the framework. The authors acknowledge this, but a discussion on the challenges of extending the approach to more general expressions would be helpful.
* **Search Strategy Details:** The description of the scheduler and how the scores from the learning strategies are used to select rules could be more detailed. It’s unclear how sensitive the results are to the specific scoring mechanism.
* **Computational Cost:** The experiments run for a fixed 10-minute cutoff. While reasonable, reporting the computational resources used more precisely (e.g., CPU cores, memory) would be helpful for reproducibility.  A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the search process itself would be beneficial.
* **Comparison to Existing Systems:** While the paper contrasts with symbolic computation engines like Maple and Mathematica, a more direct comparison of performance on a standardized set of simplification problems would strengthen the claim of improvement.  Specifically, could these systems *eventually* find the same identities given enough time?



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the learning-based search strategy.
* The code and data availability promotes reproducibility and further research.
* The work has the potential to impact areas such as symbolic computation, compiler optimization, and machine learning for scientific discovery.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The RNN performance is underwhelming and lacks sufficient analysis.
* The limited scope of the problem domain restricts the immediate applicability of the framework.
* The lack of a direct comparison to existing symbolic computation systems weakens the claim of improvement.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field, demonstrating the potential of machine learning to automate the discovery of mathematical identities. Addressing the weaknesses related to RNN analysis, scope limitations, and comparison to existing systems would further strengthen the paper. Specifically, I suggest the authors: (1) provide a more in-depth analysis of the RNN’s performance, including a visualization of the learned representations; (2) discuss the challenges of extending the framework to more general expressions; and (3) include a comparison with Maple or Mathematica on a benchmark of simplification problems.
