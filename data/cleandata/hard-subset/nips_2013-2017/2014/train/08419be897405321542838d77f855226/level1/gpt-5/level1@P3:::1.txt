Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes learning-guided search to discover analytically equivalent but computationally cheaper reformulations of target expressions. The core representation is an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic and elementwise operators, constrained to produce homogeneous, fixed-degree scalar polynomials in entries of an input matrix/vector. Equality checking between candidate expressions and the target is done via randomized evaluation over Zp (“descriptors”), avoiding expensive symbolic reasoning. For complex identities that generally require summing multiple trees, the method assembles a linear combination by solving Fw = t mod p. To counteract exponential search, two strategies are learned from solutions to easier targets: (i) an n-gram model over frequently occurring subtrees, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions and a softmax policy for rule selection. Experiments on several families ((Σ AAT)k, (Σ (A.*A)AT)k, elementary symmetric polynomials, and two RBM-moment–related sums) show that learned strategies, particularly 3–5-grams, substantially outperform random search; RNN helps on some harder cases but is generally weaker than n-grams.

The paper sits at the intersection of automated algebraic simplification/optimization, theorem proving, and program induction. Attribute grammars have been used for optimization in other domains (Cheung & McCanne; O’Neill et al.) and learning has guided theorem proving (Bridge et al.). The use of randomized evaluation modulo a prime is closely related to polynomial identity testing (PIT) via Schwartz–Zippel, though this is not cited. The learned continuous embeddings echo recursive models used in NLP (Socher et al., Luong et al.) and word embeddings (Collobert & Weston; Mikolov et al.). The overall problem is also related to superoptimization/program synthesis (e.g., genetic programming; stochastic superoptimization) and to classical algebraic complexity, but the paper’s learning-to-search angle over a math DSL is novel.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation with a concrete DSL and complexity attributes; the grammar provides a clean search space and complexity accounting.
- Pragmatic, scalable equality checking via randomized modular evaluation, enabling large-scale search/learning; the linear-combination solver is a neat way to assemble multi-tree identities.
- Learning from small to large (curriculum) is sensible and effective; n-grams are simple yet strong; the RNN embedding is an interesting contribution to continuous representations of symbolic expressions with good classification accuracy on equivalence classes.
- Empirical evidence that learning dramatically expands the reachable identity complexity, with code released.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
Quality/technical soundness:
- Correctness guarantees of equality testing: randomized descriptors over Zp imply only probabilistic soundness. Please formalize the failure probability (e.g., via Schwartz–Zippel bounds) as a function of p, evaluation set size N, and polynomial degree, and adopt multiple primes/Chinese remaindering to make errors negligible. Also discuss non-polynomial constructs (sum, transpose) in terms of resulting polynomials in entries to justify PIT applicability.
- Linear combinations over Zp: Solving Fw = t mod p can yield coefficients that do not correspond to integer/rational coefficients over the reals, or to a grammar-expressible sum (e.g., negative scalars). Clarify (i) which coefficient set is permitted in the grammar (integers/rationals?), (ii) how you lift modular solutions to exact coefficients (e.g., via multiple primes and rational reconstruction), and (iii) how you verify the final identity symbolically or with high-confidence randomized checks.
- Baselines and ablations: Beyond “Random,” it would strengthen the case to compare against (a) CAS simplifiers (Mathematica/Maple) with expand/collect/simplify and linear-algebra identities, (b) hand-crafted rewrite heuristics or cost-driven greedy search/A* with a heuristic (e.g., partial descriptor agreement), and (c) non-learned but structural heuristics (beam search, MCTS). Also include beam width and search depth ablations for your learned policies.
- Known results: For Symk, the naïve O(n^k) baseline is not representative; classical DP/Newton identities compute elementary symmetric polynomials in O(nk). Please compare against these to calibrate claimed gains. For RBM-1 and RBM-2, relate your results to known combinatorial/moment identities (e.g., Poisson–Binomial moments), and clarify when your identities match or improve known formulae.
- Scalability and coverage: The RNN is trained by enumerating all valid expressions up to k=6; discuss feasibility for larger grammars/degrees, and whether curriculum avoids exponential blow-up. Consider end-to-end policy learning for search (e.g., REINFORCE/imitation learning) rather than freezing φ and training a shallow softmax.
- Search strategy: The scheduler appears greedy; beam search could mitigate local myopia. Report performance with small beams. Also, n-grams likely benefit from simple canonicalization of commutative/associative nodes; describe any canonicalization used.

Clarity:
- Provide the full grammar (Table 1) in the main paper with explicit typing, arity, cost model, and closure properties (producing scalars; degree accounting). Clarify whether scalar constants and negative coefficients are allowed.
- Move at least a few representative “discovered” identities with complexity analysis from the supplement into the main text; quantify speedups empirically (wall-clock or operation counts).
- Detail hardware usage and parallelization (the 16-core setup) and how the 10-minute cutoff scales with k and n.

Originality:
- The idea of learning to guide algebraic identity discovery within a DSL is novel for NIPS and goes beyond rule-based CAS or theorem provers. The continuous embedding for symbolic expressions is also interesting, though the search component ends up favoring n-grams.

Significance:
- If the identities indeed yield polynomial-time evaluation of expressions that are naively exponential (RBM families) and are verified with high confidence, the potential impact is substantial for probabilistic models and compiler optimizations. However, the current strongest empirical gains are on families where repetitive local patterns arise and n-grams suffice; demonstrating clear wins on RBM-2 for k>5 or integrating with a downstream ML task (e.g., faster RBM partition approximation) would strengthen significance.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, well-motivated problem; clean DSL formulation and search framework.
- Convincing evidence that learning greatly improves search efficiency and discoverable complexity.
- Interesting RNN embedding of symbolic expressions with good classification accuracy.
- Released code and dataset.

Cons for acceptance:
- Incomplete treatment of correctness (probabilistic equality; modular lifting).
- Missing comparisons to strong known baselines (CAS; DP for Symk; heuristic search).
- RNN underperforms n-grams in search; end-to-end learning not explored.
- Many identities and details relegated to the supplement; limited evaluation on RBM-2.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on strengthening the correctness guarantees (PIT analysis, multi-prime verification and coefficient lifting), adding comparisons to known methods (especially for Symk), and including representative identities with explicit complexity analyses in the main paper. The core idea is timely and promising, and the learning-to-search results are compelling.
