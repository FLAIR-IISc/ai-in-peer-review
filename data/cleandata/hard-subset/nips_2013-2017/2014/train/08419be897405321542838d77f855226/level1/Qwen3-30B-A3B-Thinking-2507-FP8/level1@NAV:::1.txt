

This paper presents a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors introduce an attribute grammar approach to represent symbolic expressions and propose two learning-based search strategies—n-gram models and recursive neural networks—to guide the exploration of the combinatorially large space of possible expressions. The key insight is that learning from simpler identities can effectively guide the search for more complex ones, enabling the discovery of identities beyond brute-force reach.

The paper makes several strong contributions. The technical execution is sound, with thorough evaluation across multiple mathematical families (e.g., symmetric polynomials, RBM-related expressions). The learned representations for symbolic expressions are particularly interesting, demonstrating how recursive neural networks can capture meaningful continuous embeddings for mathematical structures. The results show significant improvements over random search, with the n-gram models outperforming the RNN for repetitive patterns (as seen in Example 2), while the RNN shows promise for more complex structures.

The work is well-positioned within the literature, building on attribute grammars (Knuth, 1968) and borrowing techniques from NLP (Socher et al., 2011). It clearly differentiates itself from theorem proving systems (like Maple/Mathematica) that don't use learning approaches, and from related work in program induction (Bridge et al., 2013) by focusing on computational complexity rather than logical equivalence.

Strengths include the practical significance of the discovered identities (e.g., potential applications in machine learning for efficient dropout computation), the clear experimental design, and the thoughtful discussion of limitations. Weaknesses include the restricted scope to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree (limiting broader applicability), and the fact that the most challenging family (RBM-2) showed no improvement over random search. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with recent symbolic AI approaches (e.g., neural theorem provers).

Quality: High (solid theoretical foundation, well-supported results)  
Clarity: Good (well-organized, but some technical details could be expanded)  
Originality: High (novel application of learning to symbolic search)  
Significance: Moderate to high (addresses a real computational problem with practical implications)

Pros: Novel framework, strong empirical results, clear problem statement, practical applications.  
Cons: Restricted scope, limited comparison with recent symbolic AI work, most challenging case remains unsolved.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the intersection of symbolic computation and machine learning, advancing the state of the art in automatic discovery of mathematical identities. I recommend acceptance.
