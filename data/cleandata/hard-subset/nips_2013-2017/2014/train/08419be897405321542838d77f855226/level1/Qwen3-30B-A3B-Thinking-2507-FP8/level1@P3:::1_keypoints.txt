- Proposes a framework to discover efficient mathematical identities using an attribute grammar to represent expressions and learning-guided search (n-gram models and RNNs).
- Key insight: learning from simpler expressions improves search efficiency for discovering complex identities that brute force cannot find.
- Builds on attribute grammars, symbolic computation systems, and NLP techniques, but differs by adding learning rather than hand-designed rules.
- Methodology is technically sound with a clear problem formulation and experimental design.
- Uses integer-based descriptors to avoid numerical matching issues, which is a clever and justified choice.
- RNN representation learning is well-implemented (identity initialization, ReLU, curriculum learning) and achieves high classification accuracy (≤5% error).
- Lacks sufficient theoretical analysis explaining why the learning approaches generalize to more complex expressions.
- Experiments show effectiveness but do not rigorously quantify the conditions under which the approach succeeds or fails.
- Problem domain is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, limiting immediate generality and not fully justified.
- Paper is well-organized and clear, with useful examples and clear presentation of results (Figure 3, Table 2).
- Some technical details are under-specified: exact RNN structure and the linear combination implementation need more explanation for reproducibility.
- Supplementary material is mentioned but not described in the main text and would aid reproducibility.
- Originality is strong: applying machine learning, especially RNNs, to symbolic mathematical identity discovery is novel in this context.
- Significance demonstrated by practical complexity reductions (e.g., O(n^3) → O(n^2)) and relevance to ML tasks like RBM partition function approximation.
- Strengths: novel ML application to symbolic search, solid experiments, robust integer descriptors, and effective RNN-based symbolic representations.
- Weaknesses: limited theoretical grounding, restricted domain, insufficient discussion of limitations/failure cases, missing technical clarity, and limited baselines beyond random search.
- Recommendation: Accept — the paper is a significant, well-executed step toward integrating learning with symbolic reasoning despite some limitations.
