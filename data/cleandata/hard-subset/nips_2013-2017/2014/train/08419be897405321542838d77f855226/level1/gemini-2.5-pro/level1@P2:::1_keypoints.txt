- Proposes a framework to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities by representing expressions as attribute-grammar trees and searching for lower-complexity equivalents.
- Addresses combinatorial search explosion by training ML models on solutions for simpler expressions to guide search on harder ones.
- Introduces two learning-based search strategies: an n-gram model over subtrees and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous vector representations of expressions.
- Uses modular-arithmetic numerical descriptors to efficiently and robustly check expression equivalence.
- Demonstrates on five families of complex polynomials, discovering new identities that reduce complexity from exponential/high-order polynomial to low-order polynomial.
- Experimental evaluation is solid, with source code provided and important RNN training tricks (identity init, curriculum learning) documented for reproducibility.
- Main limitation: experiments confined to single-variable homogeneous polynomials with scalar outputs, so generality is not yet shown.
- A deeper analysis is needed to explain why simpler n-gram models often outperform the more complex RNN in this domain.
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, with effective figures, tables, and implementation detail.
- Highly original synthesis of known components (grammars, n-grams, RNNs) applied novelly to symbolic mathematics; continuous embeddings for formulas are a notable contribution.
- Work is significant: finds practically useful new identities (e.g., related to RBM partition functions) and suggests broader applications (compilers, automated optimization).
- Pro: novelty, strong results, technical quality, clarity, and a promising new research direction.
- Con: limited experimental scope and insufficient investigation into the RNN vs. n-gram performance gap.
- Overall recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance.
