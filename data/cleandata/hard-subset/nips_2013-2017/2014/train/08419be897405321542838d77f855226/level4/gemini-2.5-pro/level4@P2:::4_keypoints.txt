- Summary: Proposes a framework using attribute grammars and learned models (n-grams, RNN) to discover computationally efficient symbolic identities by guiding search over expression trees.  
- Key contribution: Introduces an RNN-based continuous representation for symbolic expressions enabling gradient-based learning in symbolic mathematics.  
- Empirical result: Discovers non-trivial identities that reduce complexities (e.g., exponential or O(n³) to polynomial like O(n²)) for several expression families, including symmetric polynomials and RBM terms.  
- Novelty/significance: Creative application of machine learning/deep learning to symbolic identity discovery, opening avenues for learning-based symbolic methods.  
- Impact: Finds useful, complex identities with potential relevance to ML and symbolic computation.  
- Technical approach: Uses attribute grammars and two learning strategies (n-gram, RNN) with thorough empirical evaluation across five expression families.  
- Strength: Demonstrates superiority of learned strategies over random baselines on increasingly difficult families.  
- Weakness: Limited scope—experiments restricted to homogeneous single-variable scalar polynomials; extension to broader function classes is not addressed.  
- Weakness: Presentation clarity—system components (representations, search, linear solver) are intertwined and need a clearer, modular exposition.  
- Weakness: Lack of explicit complexity guidance—the models learn equivalent expressions but don't directly use computational complexity as an objective signal.  
- Question: Request for intuition/visualization of learned RNN embeddings (e.g., t-SNE) to see if semantically similar expressions cluster.  
- Question: Ask for formal justification of restrictions (why only homogeneous polynomials) and whether this is a fundamental or pragmatic choice.  
- Question: Inquiry about sensitivity to number of generated trees M before invoking the linear solver and principled stopping criteria.  
- Question: Request to verify a potential invalid discovered identity for RBM-1 when k=1 and n=1 in supplementary material.  
- Minor typos: Several small typographical errors noted (Lines 148, 289, 332, 338) to be fixed.  
- Conclusion: Overall strong and innovative work; novelty and results outweigh weaknesses, recommend acceptance.
