This paper explores the application of machine learning techniques to the discovery of efficient mathematical identities, a novel direction within the field. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and employ both n-gram models and recursive neural networks to guide the search for computationally simpler, yet equivalent, expressions. This work builds upon existing research in symbolic computation [14, 27], theorem proving [5, 9, 11], and program induction [18, 28], but uniquely integrates learning approaches into the process of identity discovery.

The primary strength of this paper lies in its originality. The combination of attribute grammars with machine learning for the purpose of automatically discovering mathematical identities represents a genuinely new approach. The authors demonstrate the potential of this approach by finding identities for specific expressions, some of which they claim are previously undiscovered, and provide a public repository for code and data [29]. The learned representation of symbolic expressions using RNNs is also a significant contribution, potentially bridging the gap between symbolic and continuous representations and opening avenues for applying further machine learning techniques. 

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the proposed methods enable the discovery of identities, the demonstrated performance gains, particularly for more complex expressions, appear limited. The experimental results focus on a relatively narrow set of expression families, and the scalability of the approach to more general mathematical expressions remains unclear.  The success of the n-gram model, outperforming the RNN in some cases, suggests the complexity of the problem may not fully leverage the representational power of the RNN. The paper acknowledges restrictions on the considered expressions (polynomials of a fixed degree with single or two variables) and doesn’t fully explore the impact of relaxing these assumptions. Further analysis on the computational cost and memory requirements of the approach, especially as expression complexity increases, would be beneficial.  While the authors provide comparisons to naive evaluation complexity, a comparison to existing symbolic computation engines like Maple [6] and Mathematica [27] would strengthen the contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty of the approach combining attribute grammars and machine learning for identity discovery.
*   Potential for discovering previously unknown mathematical identities.
*   Demonstrated feasibility of using learned representations for symbolic expressions.
*   Publicly available code and data for reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited performance gains for complex expressions.
*   Focus on a narrow set of expression families, raising concerns about generalizability.
*   Lack of comparison to existing symbolic computation engines.
*   Scalability of the approach remains uncertain.
*   The initial results, while positive, might be achievable through simpler, more direct mathematical reasoning for the limited expression set explored.



Overall, this paper presents an interesting and potentially promising direction for research. However, the limited scope of the experiments and the modest performance gains suggest that further development and evaluation are needed before it reaches the standard typically expected for publication at NIPS.
