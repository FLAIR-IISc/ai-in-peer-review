The paper introduces a novel approach for representing symbolic expressions and finding their mathematical identities using neural networks. Although the expressions studied are limited, I find the paper interesting and believe it could have a significant impact on symbolic software libraries.

One concern is that the problem statement emphasizes finding expressions with lower computational complexity, but the algorithm does not explicitly enforce this constraint. I understand that incorporating this constraint is not trivial, but I would like the authors to discuss possible solutions to limit the search space to such solutions.

Regarding the restrictions mentioned in Section 2, I wonder if the scheduler is the only limiting factor for handling more complicated mathematical expressions. I would appreciate more formal analysis or empirical experiments to justify these restrictions.

The paper's structure could be improved for better clarity. The stages of the approach are not clearly explained, making it difficult to follow.

Additionally, I noticed that there seems to be a constraint on the size of the vector/matrix, but it is not explicitly stated. For example, for k=1 and n=1, the expression for RBM-1 in the supplementary material appears to be invalid.

Some minor corrections are needed: "show for" (line 332), "has contains" (line 148), "is is" (line 289), and "by be" (line 338).
