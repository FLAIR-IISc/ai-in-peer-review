

This paper introduces two novel approaches for discovering simplified symbolic expression trees: one leveraging n-grams derived from sub-expression sequences, and another employing a recursive neural network (RNN) to generate continuous-space representations of trees. These representations predict likely subtree combination rules. As shown in Figure 3, the n-gram method outperforms the RNN on two of the three evaluated tasks, suggesting practical advantages for certain problem instances.  

**Quality:** The technical execution is sound, with rigorous empirical evaluation across multiple benchmarks. The methods are well-motivated within the context of symbolic computation, and the experimental design adequately supports the claims.  

**Clarity:** The paper’s core ideas are conceptually clear, but Section 7.2 (on implementation details) is poorly structured and difficult to follow, obscuring key aspects of the RNN approach. The overall exposition would benefit from tighter organization and more precise terminology, particularly in describing the n-gram construction and RNN training procedures.  

**Originality:** The work presents two distinct, non-trivial methodological contributions: the n-gram-based search heuristic and the RNN-driven prediction framework for symbolic trees. These approaches represent a meaningful departure from conventional symbolic optimization techniques.  

**Significance:** The paper’s significance is limited. While the methods address an interesting theoretical problem—efficiently searching for simplified expression trees—their practical relevance is unclear. The authors do not convincingly demonstrate why this specific search problem arises in real-world computational contexts where efficiency is critical (e.g., in compiler optimization, automated reasoning, or scientific computing). The problem formulation appears contrived, as practitioners seeking computational efficiency would typically rely on established techniques (e.g., algebraic simplification, common subexpression elimination, or numerical approximation) rather than the bespoke search mechanisms proposed here. Without a compelling case for the problem’s prevalence or the methods’ superiority over existing tools, the work’s impact remains uncertain.  

**Recommendation:** The paper makes a technically valid contribution to symbolic computation research but fails to establish meaningful significance. The methods are interesting in isolation, yet their utility for addressing tangible computational challenges is not substantiated. The clarity issues in Section 7.2 further diminish the paper’s accessibility. Given the high quality and originality, acceptance is possible *only if* the authors substantially revise the motivation to clarify the problem’s relevance and improve the exposition of the RNN approach. Without these changes, the paper’s contribution is unlikely to resonate with the broader NeurIPS community.
