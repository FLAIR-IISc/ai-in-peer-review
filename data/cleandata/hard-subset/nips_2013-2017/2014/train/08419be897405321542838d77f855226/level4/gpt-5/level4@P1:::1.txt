Summary and contribution:
The paper applies deep neural networks to the discovery of computationally efficient mathematical identities. The authors propose searching over symbolic expressions via a grammar and using learned guidance, trained on simpler instances, to find algebraically equivalent formulations with lower computational cost. This is an interesting and novel application of machine learning to a symbolic domain.

Strengths:
- The problem setting is intriguing and underexplored, and the paper presents a fresh angle on automated identity discovery.
- The idea of learning to guide search, rather than relying purely on hand-crafted heuristics or brute-force enumeration, is appealing.
- Framing the task as learning from simpler expressions to tackle more complex ones is conceptually sound.

Weaknesses:
- The empirical performance in discovering identities does not appear particularly strong given the narrowness of the setting. Even with a very limited set of inputs and target families, the results are mixed.
- The set of expressions considered is so restricted that it seems plausible one could design targeted mathematical rules or heuristics to find many of these identities more simply, without recourse to deep learning. This gives the impression of using a sledgehammer to crack a nut.
- The paper suggests the approach could extend to a broader range of expressions, but based on the reported performance, it is not clear that the current methodology would scale effectively or that such scaling would be sensible given the so-so initial results.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The high-level idea is reasonable, but the demonstrated effectiveness is modest. Given the constrained problem scope, stronger empirical evidence would be expected. As it stands, the approach does not convincingly outperform simpler alternatives for this niche.
- Clarity: The core idea is understandable, and the motivation is clear. However, the presentation would benefit from sharper positioning of the limitations and a more explicit discussion of when and why the method succeeds or fails.
- Originality: The application domain is novel and interesting. Learning to guide symbolic search is a useful direction, though the instantiation here feels preliminary.
- Significance: With the current results and limited scope, the impact appears modest. It is hard to see practitioners adopting the approach as-is, and the path to broader applicability is not convincingly demonstrated.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and appealing problem setting: learning to discover efficient symbolic identities.
- Clear motivation for learning-guided search over symbolic grammars.
- Potential to inspire future work at the ML–symbolic reasoning interface.

Cons for acceptance:
- Underwhelming empirical performance even in a highly constrained setting.
- Very limited set of expressions; hand-crafted heuristics might suffice, calling into question the need for deep learning here.
- Unclear scalability to richer grammars or more realistic mathematical domains.
- Overall, the work feels preliminary and below the bar for NIPS at this time.

Recommendation:
I find the paper interesting, but, given the limited scope and the unimpressive empirical gains in such a constrained scenario, I view it as slightly below the threshold for NIPS. A stronger case would require demonstrating clear advantages over simpler baselines in more varied and challenging settings, or compelling evidence that the approach scales and yields identities unlikely to be found by straightforward rule-based methods.
