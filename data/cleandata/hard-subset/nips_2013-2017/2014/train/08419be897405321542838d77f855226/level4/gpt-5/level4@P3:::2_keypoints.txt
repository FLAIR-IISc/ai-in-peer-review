- Paper proposes learning-guided search over an attribute grammar to discover computationally cheaper expressions equivalent to a target symbolic expression.
- Core methods: n-gram subtree frequency bias and an RNN that learns continuous embeddings φ(S) to predict next grammar rules.
- Matches targets by allowing linear combinations of candidate trees and solving via modular Gaussian elimination.
- Experiments on five families of scalar-output expressions with a 10-minute per-degree cutoff and curriculum learning.
- n-gram models generally outperform the RNN and reliably find several nontrivial cost-reducing identities; some exponential-sum cases reduced to polynomial in limited instances.
- Strength: clear, cost-aware problem formulation using attribute grammars to make the search space explicit.
- Strength: methodological novelty in continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions and robust modular descriptors for equivalence testing.
- Strength: solid engineering choices (identity initialization, curriculum, dropout) and practical expansion of solution space via linear combinations.
- Strength: evidence of nontrivial discoveries and public code/data release.
- Weakness: restrictive problem scope (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, mostly single-variable) and dependence on constrained grammars for some wins.
- Weakness: limited baselines—missing comparisons to beam search, MCTS, A*, and complexity-aware symbolic algebra systems.
- Weakness: RNN underperforms n-gram baselines; needs ablations (curriculum, initialization, dropout) and exploration of more structure-aware models and timing/calibration metrics.
- Weakness: modular equivalence testing lacks quantified collision probability, unspecified N and prime p, and unclear descriptor sample counts.
- Weakness: experimental reporting incomplete—operation counts, asymptotic complexities in main text, wall-clock speedups, time distributions, number of trees explored, and cross-family generalization missing.
- Weakness: claims of “new” identities and ML relevance are speculative without concrete verified identities or end-to-end ML applications.
- Clarity: paper is generally readable but has dense sections and notational/formatting issues; suggests moving long family definitions to appendix and unifying notation.
- Originality: high—novel integration of attribute grammars, modular descriptors, and learned guidance in this context.
- Significance: promising research direction but currently limited practical impact and narrow demonstrations.
- Pros for acceptance: innovative framing, reproducibility, and demonstrated extensions of brute-force search.
- Cons for acceptance: limited scope, weak empirical comparisons, underperforming neural method, and missing theoretical verification for modular testing.
- Overall recommendation: interesting and original but leans against acceptance pending stronger baselines, broader scope, better RNN validation, rigorous equivalence guarantees, and improved experimental reporting.
