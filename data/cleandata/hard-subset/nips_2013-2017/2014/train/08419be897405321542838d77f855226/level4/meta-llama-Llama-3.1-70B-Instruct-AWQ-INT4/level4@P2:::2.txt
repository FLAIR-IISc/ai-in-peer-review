This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities by leveraging machine learning techniques. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and develop two learning approaches to guide the search for efficient identities: an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN). The n-gram model learns patterns from simpler expressions, while the RNN learns a continuous representation of symbolic expressions. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by discovering new mathematical identities that offer significant reductions in computational complexity.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their framework and methods. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the related work, particularly in the areas of theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming. Additionally, the authors could provide more insight into the limitations of their approach and potential avenues for future research.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their methods. The use of n-grams and RNNs is well-motivated, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a range of tasks. However, the significance of the paper is somewhat limited, as the problem of discovering efficient mathematical identities may not be of broad interest to the NIPS community. Nevertheless, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of machine learning and symbolic computation.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, but some sections, such as Section 7.2, could be improved with more detailed explanations and examples. The originality of the paper is high, as the authors propose a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities. The significance of the paper, while limited, is still notable, as the authors demonstrate the potential of machine learning techniques in symbolic computation.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with some revisions to address the clarity and significance concerns mentioned above.
