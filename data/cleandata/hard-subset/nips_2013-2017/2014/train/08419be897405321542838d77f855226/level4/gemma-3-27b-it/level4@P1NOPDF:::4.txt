The paper introduces a novel application of neural networks: learning representations for symbolic expressions and discovering mathematical identities between them. Despite the limited scope of expressions currently considered, and the lack of a demonstrated path to scaling to more complex cases, I find this work interesting and believe it has the potential to significantly impact symbolic software libraries.

However, I have several concerns. The problem statement focuses on finding expressions with lower computational complexity, but the algorithm itself doesn’t incorporate any mechanism to enforce this constraint. While I understand this is a challenging problem, I would like to know if the authors have considered potential solutions to limit the search space to expressions exhibiting such improvements.

Furthermore, regarding the restrictions outlined in Section 2, is the scheduler genuinely the sole limiting factor preventing the handling of more complex mathematical expressions? I would have appreciated a more formal justification for these restrictions, or perhaps empirical evidence supporting their necessity.

The paper’s structure also hinders clarity. A more explicit and stepwise explanation of the methodology would significantly improve its readability.

Finally, the paper lacks discussion of constraints on vector/matrix sizes. For example, with k=1 and n=1, the expression for RBM-1 in the supplementary material appears invalid.

I also noted a few minor typos:

* Line 332: “show for” should be “show” or “shown for”
* Line 148: “has contains” should be “contains”
* Line 289: “is is” should be “is”
* Line 338: “by be” should be “be”

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and novel approach to representing symbolic expressions and identifying mathematical identities, but benefits from further clarification and consideration of practical constraints.
