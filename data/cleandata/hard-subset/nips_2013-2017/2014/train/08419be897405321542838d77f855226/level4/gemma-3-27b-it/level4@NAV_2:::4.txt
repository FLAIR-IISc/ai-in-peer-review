The paper introduces a novel framework based on attribute grammars and machine learning for discovering efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to learn a search strategy for exploring the space of possible symbolic expressions, guided by solutions found for simpler cases. This is a compelling application of machine learning to a traditionally symbolic domain, with potential implications for optimization in scientific computing and symbolic manipulation systems. The work is well-motivated, clearly connecting to areas like theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, while appropriately highlighting its distinct contributions and limitations relative to these fields. 

The strengths of the paper lie in its innovative combination of techniques – attribute grammars for representing expressions and both n-gram models and recursive neural networks for guiding the search. The empirical results are promising, demonstrating the ability to discover identities (e.g., reducing O(n3) to O(n2) complexity) that are beyond the reach of brute-force search or manual derivation. The provision of code and evaluation data fosters reproducibility and further research. The exploration of learned representations for symbolic expressions, analogous to word embeddings in NLP, is a particularly interesting and potentially impactful contribution.

However, there are several areas for improvement. The paper lacks a formal mechanism for *enforcing* computational complexity reduction during the search process. While the goal is to find more efficient expressions, the algorithm doesn’t explicitly prioritize solutions with lower complexity. Addressing this – perhaps through incorporating complexity estimates into the scoring function used by the search strategy – would strengthen the work. The justification for the restrictions outlined in Section 2 feels somewhat superficial. A more detailed discussion of why these restrictions are necessary and whether they could be relaxed, potentially through algorithmic modifications or increased computational resources, would be beneficial.  The paper’s structure could be improved for clarity.  A more step-by-step explanation of the overall process, from grammar definition to identity discovery, would enhance readability.  Finally, there appears to be an inconsistency regarding variable size constraints (e.g., k=1, n=1 for RBM-1 in the supplementary material).

Here's a list of arguments for and against acceptance:

**Pro:**
*   Novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation.
*   Demonstrated ability to discover previously unknown mathematical identities.
*   Sound technical approach combining attribute grammars and learning techniques.
*   Publicly available code and data.
*   Potentially impactful for optimization and symbolic software.

**Con:**
*   Lack of formal mechanism for enforcing computational complexity reduction.
*   Limited scope of expressions currently handled.
*   Justification for restrictions feels somewhat weak.
*   Paper structure could be improved for clarity. 
*   Minor inconsistencies/errors in the presentation (e.g. the variable size example).
