

This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars and machine learning. The authors represent symbolic expressions as trees generated via a grammar of mathematical operations, with the goal of finding equivalent expressions of lower computational complexity. Given the exponential growth of the search space, they propose two learning-based strategies: an n-gram model that captures recurring subexpression patterns from simpler solved identities, and a recursive neural network (RNN) trained to predict the next grammar rule for efficient expression construction. The approach demonstrates success on specific polynomial families (e.g., reducing O(n³) matrix operations to O(n²)), with identities discovered beyond brute-force reach. The work builds on attribute grammars (Knuth, 1968) and draws inspiration from NLP techniques (e.g., n-grams, RNNs for symbolic parsing), while making a novel contribution by applying these to mathematical identity discovery—a domain where prior work (e.g., Bridge et al., 2013; Maple/Mathematica) relies on rule-based simplification without learning.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The technical execution is strong. The attribute grammar framework is well-motivated, and the experimental design rigorously tests the learning strategies (Section 7.2). The RNN representation (Section 4.2) achieves high classification accuracy (≤5% error for k=3–6), and the n-gram approach consistently outperforms random search on repetitive patterns (e.g., Example 2, Fig. 3). The code and data availability (GitHub) enhances reproducibility.  
*Originality:* The integration of NLP-inspired learning methods (n-grams, RNNs) into symbolic computation for *discovering* identities—not just simplifying—represents a significant departure from prior work. While attribute grammars have been used for algorithm design (e.g., Cheung & McCanne, 2001), this is the first application to *automatically discover* novel identities via learning from simpler cases.  
*Clarity:* The core ideas are clearly articulated, though Section 7.2 (experimental families) could be streamlined with a table summarizing the five expression families (e.g., (ΣAAT)ᵏ, RBM-2ᵏ), their naive complexity, and key results. The numerical descriptor approach (Section 4.1) is particularly well-explained.  

**Weaknesses:**  
*Significance:* The primary concern is the narrow scope of the problem. The framework targets specific polynomial families (e.g., homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree k with single-matrix inputs), which may not align with common computational bottlenecks in practice. While the paper notes potential applications in ML (e.g., RBM partition function approximation, Section 8), these are not sufficiently developed to demonstrate *immediate* utility. A stronger justification is needed for why practitioners would adopt this method over established symbolic tools (e.g., Mathematica) or direct algorithmic design. The claim that identities are "previously undiscovered" is plausible, but without evidence of real-world impact (e.g., speedups in ML libraries), the significance remains limited.  
*Clarity (Minor):* The connection between the RNN’s learned representation (Section 4.2) and its role in the search strategy (Section 6.3) could be more explicit. A brief schematic of the RNN’s input/output flow would aid readers.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pros:*  
- Novel integration of NLP techniques (n-grams, RNNs) into symbolic identity discovery.  
- Demonstrated success on non-trivial identities beyond brute-force reach (e.g., Example 2 for k=6).  
- Rigorous evaluation of learning strategies (n-grams vs. RNN) with clear metrics (Fig. 3).  
*Cons:*  
- Problem scope is narrow (single-matrix homogeneous polynomials); broader applicability is speculative.  
- Limited evidence of real-world impact beyond theoretical efficiency gains.  
- Significance not convincingly tied to existing ML/compilers challenges (e.g., no benchmarks on real ML workloads).  

**Recommendation:**  
**Weak Accept**. The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the *methodology* of symbolic search, with clear novelty and quality. However, the significance is constrained by the narrow problem formulation. While the framework could inspire future work (e.g., compiler optimizations for ML, as noted in Section 8), the current results do not demonstrate sufficient immediate value to practitioners. To strengthen significance, the authors should: (1) explicitly benchmark their identities in a real ML context (e.g., accelerating a specific ML algorithm), and (2) discuss how the framework could generalize beyond the current grammar (e.g., via additional rules for trigonometric functions). Without these, the paper’s impact remains incremental for the NIPS audience.  

*Note:* The paper is well-suited for NIPS given its technical rigor and alignment with the conference’s focus on machine learning methods. The framework’s potential to bridge symbolic computation and learning is promising, but the current significance argument requires strengthening.
