This paper presents an application of deep neural networks (DNNs) to the novel problem of discovering mathematical identities. While the core idea is interesting, my overall assessment is that the work appears preliminary and may not yet meet the high standards for publication at NeurIPS. 

My primary concern lies with the performance of the proposed method. Even given the limited scope of the input expressions, the results in discovering identities are not particularly compelling. The expressions used for evaluation were drawn from a sufficiently narrow distribution that a targeted, rule-based approach – rather than a DNN – might have been more efficient and effective. The application of a DNN in this context feels somewhat disproportionate to the complexity of the task; it feels like using a sledgehammer to crack a nut.

The authors may argue that their approach is designed to scale to a broader and more complex range of expressions. However, it is unclear whether the current methodology would readily extend in a practical manner. Furthermore, given the modest initial results, the benefit of such scaling is not convincingly demonstrated. It remains uncertain whether applying a more powerful model to a more expansive expression set would yield significant improvements.

Despite these reservations, I recognize the potential interest in this line of research. However, based on the current presentation, I believe the work falls slightly below the threshold for publication at NeurIPS.
