This paper introduces two methods for discovering computationally simpler, equivalent symbolic expression trees. The first method is based on n-grams computed over sequences of sub-expressions. The second employs a recursive neural network (RNN) to generate continuous vector representations of expression trees. These vector representations are then used to predict the most likely rule for combining subtrees. Based on the results presented in Figure 3, the n-gram approach appears to outperform the RNN method on two of the three evaluation tasks.

Comments wrt specific review criteria:
Quality - High.
Clarity - The presentation is at times difficult to follow, and Section 7.2, in particular, would benefit from significant clarification.
Originality - High.
Significance - Low. The practical significance of this work is questionable. While the paper presents novel methods for discovering more computationally efficient symbolic expressions, the underlying problem feels contrived. It is unclear whether these techniques would be adopted in practice by those seeking to optimize computations. Consequently, the overall impact of the proposed methods is uncertain.
