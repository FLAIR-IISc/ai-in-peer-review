This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar and then search through the space of possible expression trees for one that is analytically equivalent to a given target expression but has a lower computational complexity. Recognizing that a brute-force or random search of this combinatorial space is intractable for non-trivial expressions, the authors propose using machine learning techniques to guide the search. Specifically, they train models on solutions found for simpler expressions to predict promising search paths for more complex ones. The two main learning approaches explored are a simple n-gram model over grammar rules and a more sophisticated recursive neural network (RNN) designed to learn a continuous representation of the symbolic expressions themselves.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The application of modern machine learning, particularly deep learning models like RNNs, to the domain of symbolic mathematics and formula discovery is highly novel and ambitious. This work opens up a fascinating and potentially impactful research direction at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation, which is a departure from the more common statistical pattern recognition tasks seen at NIPS. The idea of "learning to reason" or learning to manipulate symbolic structures is a fundamental challenge in AI, and this paper presents a concrete and well-executed attempt to address a part of it.

2.  **Novel Technical Contribution (Learned Representation):** The development of a continuous, vector-space representation for mathematical expressions using an RNN is a significant technical contribution. In the same way that word embeddings have enabled a wide range of NLP tasks, this learned representation could potentially unlock the application of many other machine learning methods to symbolic domains. The high classification accuracy reported in Table 2 demonstrates that the RNN is effectively capturing the semantic equivalence of different symbolic expressions.

3.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors define their problem clearly and propose a coherent framework based on attribute grammars to tackle it. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the core thesis of the paper: learning-guided search significantly outperforms a random baseline. As shown in Figure 3, for several families of expressions, the n-gram and RNN models are able to find complex identities for high-degree polynomials (e.g., k > 10) where the random strategy completely fails. This provides strong evidence that learning from simpler problems is a viable strategy for this task.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance):**

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The experiments are conducted on five specific and highly structured families of homogeneous polynomials. For several of these, such as the `(sum(AAT))^k` family, the resulting efficient identities exhibit a very regular, repetitive structure (as shown in Example 2). This regularity makes them particularly well-suited for the n-gram models, which excel at capturing local, repeated patterns. This raises the question of whether the impressive performance is a feature of the learning method or an artifact of the structured problem families chosen. It is unclear how well this approach would generalize to more diverse or less structured mathematical expressions, which might not lend themselves to a simple curriculum of increasing `k`.

2.  **Performance of the RNN Strategy:** While the RNN for learning an expression representation is a central and novel contribution, its performance as a *search strategy* is underwhelming compared to the much simpler n-gram models. Across most experiments in Figure 3, the 3-gram, 4-gram, and 5-gram models find solutions more reliably than the RNN. This discrepancy weakens the argument for using the more complex deep learning model for this specific discovery task. The paper would be strengthened by a deeper analysis of why the RNN underperforms here. Is it an issue of optimization, architecture, or does the task's nature simply favor the explicit, discrete history modeled by n-grams?

3.  **Potential for Simpler, Non-Learning Solutions:** For the families of expressions with highly regular solutions, one could argue that a human mathematician, after deriving the solutions for k=2 and k=3, could likely deduce a general rule. In this sense, applying a complex learning framework might feel like overkill ("using a sledgehammer to crack a nut"). While the automated nature of the discovery is a clear advantage, the true power of the approach would be better demonstrated on problems where the solutions lack obvious repetitive structure, forcing the model to learn more abstract principles. The challenging (RBM-2)k family is a step in this direction, but results there are inconclusive as no method finds solutions beyond k=5.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an interesting and highly original paper that explores a novel application area for machine learning. The core idea is compelling, and the framework is well-designed. The primary strengths are the novelty of the problem and the learned symbolic representation. The primary weakness is that the experimental evaluation, while demonstrating the failure of random search, is conducted on problem sets that seem perfectly matched to the capabilities of the simpler n-gram models, making the contribution of the more sophisticated RNN search strategy less clear.

Despite these weaknesses, the paper's novelty and potential long-term significance are high. It introduces a new way of thinking about combining symbolic and statistical methods and could inspire significant follow-up work. While the results could be seen as preliminary in terms of the breadth of mathematics covered, it is a strong proof of concept. I lean towards acceptance, as the new ideas and potential for future impact outweigh the current limitations in the scope of the evaluation.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by the experiments provided. It is a complete piece of work for the problem it sets out to solve.
**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The attribute grammar framework and the learning strategies are explained well.
**Originality:** Very high. The problem of learning to discover mathematical identities is new to the NIPS community, as is the application of RNNs for learning continuous representations of symbolic formulae.
**Significance:** Potentially high. If this framework can be generalized, it could have a significant impact on automated theorem proving, compiler optimization, and scientific discovery. The learned symbolic embeddings could be a valuable tool for the broader community.
