This paper introduces a novel use case for neural networks: learning representations for symbolic expressions and discovering their mathematical identities. Although the set of expressions studied is very limited and there is no evidence that the approach extends to more complex expressions, I find the paper interesting and potentially impactful for symbolic software libraries.

Major comments:
- Objective vs. algorithmic enforcement: The problem statement targets finding expressions with lower computational complexity, yet the algorithm does not enforce this constraint. I understand that doing so is nontrivial, but have the authors considered mechanisms to bias or constrain the search toward lower-complexity identities (e.g., cost-based regularization, pruning heuristics, or constrained decoding) to limit the search space accordingly?
- Restrictions in Section 2: Is the scheduler the only limiting factor preventing more complicated mathematical expressions? I would have liked to see a more formal justification or empirical experiments (e.g., ablations) motivating these restrictions and clarifying their necessity and impact.
- Paper organization: The structure is not easy to follow; the stages of the method should be explained more clearly and sequentially.
- Dimensionality constraints: Is there a constraint on the sizes of vectors/matrices? For example, for k=1 and n=1, the expression for RBM-1 in the supplementary material does not seem valid.

Minor issues and typos:
- Line 148: “has contains” → should be either “has” or “contains”
- Line 289: “is is” → “is”
- Line 332: “show for” → likely “show”
- Line 338: “by be” → please correct

Overall, the paper presents a novel approach to representing symbolic expressions and discovering their identities. Even though it is limited in the expressions it can currently handle and lacks evidence for scalability to more complex cases, it is an interesting contribution with potential relevance for symbolic computation libraries.
