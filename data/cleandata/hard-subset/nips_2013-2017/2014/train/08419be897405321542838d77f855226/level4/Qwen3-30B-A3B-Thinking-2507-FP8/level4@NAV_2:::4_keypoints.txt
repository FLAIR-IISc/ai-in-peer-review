- Novel framework combining attribute grammars with machine learning to discover efficient mathematical identities.
- Grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions guided by n-gram models and recursive neural networks.
- Extends Knuth’s attribute grammar ideas to the novel domain of mathematical identity discovery; creative interdisciplinary use of NLP techniques.
- Technically sound: experiments show discovery of previously unknown identities that reduce computational complexity (e.g., O(n³) → O(n²)).
- Clear application and examples for specific problem families ((ΣAAT)ᵏ and (Σ(A.*A)AT)ᵏ) demonstrating effectiveness.
- Significant contribution in learning continuous representations for symbolic expressions, enabling ML methods in symbolic domains.
- Major limitation: scope restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with a single matrix variable, limiting generalizability.
- Authors acknowledge scope limits but do not justify them or outline how to relax these restrictions in future work.
- Algorithmic concern: search procedure does not explicitly enforce lower computational complexity as a constraint.
- Lack of explanation/guarantee that discovered expressions actually have lower computational complexity rather than merely being equivalent.
- Presentation issues: transitions between grammar representation and learning approaches need clearer descriptions and visual aids.
- Section 5 (linear combination of trees) is particularly hard to follow and needs clearer examples.
- Limited exploration of practical applications (e.g., optimizations for ML algorithms) and no empirical/ formal scaling analysis to more complex expressions.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel integration of grammars+ML, demonstrated identity discoveries, proper literature grounding, potential integration into symbolic tools.
- Arguments against acceptance: overly restricted problem scope, no explicit complexity enforcement, unclear implementation details, limited discussion of generalization.
