- Introduces a novel framework combining attribute grammars and machine learning to discover efficient mathematical identities.
- Core idea: learn a search strategy for symbolic expression space guided by solutions from simpler cases.
- Well-motivated connection to theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, with distinct contributions and limits.
- Strength: innovative combination of attribute grammars with n-gram models and recursive neural networks to guide search.
- Empirical results show the method can discover nontrivial identities (e.g., reducing O(n^3) to O(n^2)) beyond brute-force or manual derivation.
- Provides code and evaluation data, supporting reproducibility and follow-up research.
- Interesting contribution: learned representations for symbolic expressions analogous to word embeddings in NLP.
- Major weakness: no formal mechanism to enforce or prioritize computational complexity reduction during search.
- Scope limitation: current system handles a limited class of expressions.
- Justification for the restrictions in Section 2 is superficial; needs deeper discussion on necessity and potential relaxation.
- Paper structure and exposition need improvement; a step-by-step walkthrough from grammar definition to identity discovery would aid clarity.
- Presentation inconsistency: variable size constraints (e.g., RBM-1 k=1, n=1) appear contradictory in the supplementary material.
- Minor typographical errors noted (p.332 "show for"→"shown for"; p.148 "has contains"→"has contained"/"contains"; p.289 "is is"→"is"; p.338 "by be"→"by").
