- Proposes a framework that learns search strategies over an attribute grammar to discover lower-complexity symbolic identities by representing expressions as trees.  
- Uses learned guidance from simpler targets via (i) n-gram models over subtrees and (ii) an RNN embedding that predicts next grammar rules.  
- Verifies equality with modular integer descriptors and composes complex identities by solving modular linear systems.  
- Experiments on matrix polynomials and sums over binary vectors show learned strategies (especially n-grams) greatly expand tractable search space and find substantial complexity reductions.  

- Strength: Original combination of attribute grammars with learned search strategies; code and data released.  
- Strength: Clear empirical evidence that learned guidance outperforms random search, enabling discoveries beyond brute force.  
- Strength: Clever use of modular descriptors and linear combination avoids expensive symbolic manipulation.  
- Strength: Learned continuous embeddings of expressions are a promising asset for ML-for-symbolic tasks.  

- Weakness: Ambiguous description of the RNN composition—notation suggests a neural tensor network; needs clarification or correction and justification/ablations.  
- Weakness: Unclear whether the RNN is used for classification at discovery time or only its embedding is used to score next-rule choices; specify components used during training vs. search and per experiment.  
- Weakness: Scheduler/strategy interface and proposal generation are underspecified—how next-rule candidates are enumerated, how multi-input rules combine branches, and which constraints prune expansions.  
- Weakness: n-gram scoring/backoff scheme (weighted sum with 10^k weights) lacks justification and sensitivity analysis.  

- Clarity: Problem setting restrictions (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, typically single matrix) should be stated up front and rationale for excluding operations (e.g., matrix-matrix multiply) summarized.  
- Clarity: Provide an end-to-end example of the search loop showing partial tree, strategy scores, rule selection, completion, and linear-solver invocation.  
- Clarity: Avoid conflating “classification” and “search”; explicitly state when the softmax is predicting next rules versus classes.  

- Originality: Novel application of n-grams and RNN embeddings to symbolic expression trees and a fresh grammar-based learned-search approach compared to CAS systems.  

- Significance: Promising empirical discoveries, but broader impact depends on extending to richer grammars and multi-variable targets given limited generalization on hardest families.  

- Pros for acceptance: Innovative ML+symbolic framework, strong empirical gains over random search, public code/data, and useful continuous embeddings.  
- Cons for acceptance: Ambiguities in model architecture and RNN role, underexplained search mechanics, restrictive target class and limited success on hardest tasks, presentation issues.  

- Suggestion: Clarify the RNN module (matrix vs. tensor), relate to neural tensor networks, and report ablations.  
- Suggestion: Provide pseudocode for the scheduler–strategy loop and the modular linear-system formation/solution.  
- Suggestion: Explain how two-input rules are applied with one branch (fixed random vector), justify the design, and report its impact.  
- Suggestion: Expand on n-gram smoothing/backoff and include sensitivity analysis.  
- Suggestion: Add a table summarizing which components (RNN embedding, softmax, n-gram) are used at training vs. discovery for each experiment.  
- Suggestion: Discuss descriptor collisions, and how N and p are chosen in practice.  

- Typos/minor fixes: list of specific corrections (e.g., “has contains a single matrix” → “contains a single matrix”; “listed in table Table 1.” → “listed in Table 1.”; fix multiple grammatical errors and Figure 3 label “TNN” → “RNN”).  

- Overall: Intriguing, creative, and empirically solid paper that would benefit from clarifications on model/search mechanics and tightened exposition; reviewer leans toward acceptance.
