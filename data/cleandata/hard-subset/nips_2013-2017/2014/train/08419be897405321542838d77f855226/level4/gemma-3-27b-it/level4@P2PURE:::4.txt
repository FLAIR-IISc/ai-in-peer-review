The paper presents a novel application of neural networks: learning representations of symbolic expressions and discovering mathematical identities between them. While the scope is currently limited to relatively simple expressions, and the authors do not demonstrate extensibility to more complex cases, I find the work interesting and believe it has the potential to benefit symbolic software libraries.

However, the problem statement focuses on finding expressions with lower computational complexity, yet the proposed algorithm does not explicitly enforce this constraint. While I recognize this is a challenging addition, I am curious whether the authors have considered approaches to limit the search space to solutions with desirable complexity properties.

Furthermore, concerning the restrictions outlined in Section 2, it is unclear whether the scheduler is the sole limiting factor preventing the application to more complex mathematical expressions. I would appreciate a more formal justification, or empirical evidence, supporting these restrictions.

The paper’s structure also hinders clarity; a more explicit explanation of each stage of the process would improve readability. 

Finally, the paper does not specify any constraints on the size of the vector or matrix representations, and an apparent inconsistency exists in the expression for RBM-1 (k=1, n=1) presented in the supplementary material.

Minor typos were noted on lines 332 ("show for"), 148 ("has contains"), 289 ("is is"), and 338 ("by be").
