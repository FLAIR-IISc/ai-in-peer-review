This paper presents a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is analytically equivalent to a given target expression but has a lower computational complexity. Recognizing that the search space grows exponentially, the authors propose guiding the search using machine learning models trained on solutions to simpler problems. Two learning-based search strategies are introduced: one based on n-gram models over sub-expressions, and a more sophisticated method using a recursive neural network (RNN) to learn a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions, which is then used to predict the next best grammar rule to apply.

The work is situated at an interesting and relatively underexplored intersection of symbolic computation and statistical machine learning. While it relates to broad areas like theorem proving and program induction, its specific focus on learning search heuristics for discovering computationally efficient identities appears novel. Unlike traditional symbolic computation engines that rely on predefined rule-based simplification, this work learns the simplification strategy from data. The use of techniques from natural language processing, such as n-grams and RNNs on tree structures, is a creative and well-motivated application to this symbolic domain.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality:** The paper's primary strength is its originality. Applying machine learning, particularly deep learning models like RNNs, to the problem of discovering mathematical identities is a novel concept. The idea of learning a continuous representation for symbolic expressions is compelling and could have broader applications beyond this specific task, potentially enabling a variety of symbolic reasoning problems to be tackled with modern machine learning tools.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sound. The attribute grammar provides a principled way to define the search space, and the two methods for verifying expression equivalence (numerical descriptors and the learned RNN representation) are well-conceived. The experiments are comprehensive, evaluating the methods on five distinct and non-trivial families of expressions with varying difficulty. The authors are transparent about the results, including cases where the learning-based approaches did not outperform the random baseline.

3.  **Potential Significance:** The core idea of using learned heuristics to navigate a combinatorial search space is significant and highly relevant to the NIPS community. While focused on mathematical identities, this "learning from simpler problems" paradigm could potentially be applied to other difficult AI problems characterized by combinatorial explosion, such as algorithm discovery or automated theorem proving.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity could be improved in a few key areas.
    *   The description of the five expression families in Section 7.2 is quite dense. The section would be much easier to understand if the authors provided a concrete example for a small value of `k` (e.g., k=2 or k=3) for *each* family. This would parallel the effective use of examples in the introduction and make the experimental setup more accessible.
    *   The training procedure for the RNN is split between Section 4.2 (representation learning) and Section 6.3 (search strategy). A more consolidated, step-by-step description of the two-stage training process (first pre-training for classification, then fine-tuning a new head for rule prediction) would enhance readability.

2.  **Practicality and Scope:** The most significant weakness concerns the practical utility and generality of the proposed system.
    *   The framework is demonstrated on a restricted class of problems: homogeneous polynomials of a single variable that produce a scalar output. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the challenges and potential paths toward generalizing the framework to handle a wider variety of mathematical structures (e.g., trigonometric functions, inequalities, non-polynomial expressions, multiple output dimensions).
    *   The learning approach relies on a curriculum of related problems of increasing difficulty (e.g., solving for `k` to help solve for `k+1`). It is unclear how this method would perform on a standalone, arbitrary expression where such a curriculum is not readily available. This currently limits its potential as a general-purpose tool within a compiler or symbolic algebra system.
    *   The primary experimental baseline is a random search strategy. While this effectively demonstrates the benefit of learned guidance, the significance of the results would be clearer with a comparison or at least a discussion in relation to more traditional search algorithms used in symbolic manipulation or automated reasoning (e.g., A* search with a hand-crafted heuristic, or methods from the constraint programming literature).

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

*   **Pro:** A highly original and creative application of machine learning to the symbolic domain of discovering mathematical identities.
*   **Pro:** The technical approach is sound, and the experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates the effectiveness of the learned search strategies over a random baseline.
*   **Pro:** The idea of learning continuous representations for symbolic expressions and using them to guide combinatorial search has significant potential for broader impact.
*   **Con:** The practical applicability of the system in its current form seems limited due to the restricted class of expressions it can handle and its reliance on a curriculum of related problems for training.
*   **Con:** Certain key sections, particularly the description of the experimental setup, could be improved for clarity with more concrete examples.

In summary, this is a very interesting and thought-provoking paper that introduces a novel direction of research. Its main contribution is a proof-of-concept demonstrating that machine learning can successfully learn to guide search in a complex symbolic domain. While the immediate practical utility is not fully established, the originality of the problem and the proposed techniques make it a valuable contribution to the machine learning community.
