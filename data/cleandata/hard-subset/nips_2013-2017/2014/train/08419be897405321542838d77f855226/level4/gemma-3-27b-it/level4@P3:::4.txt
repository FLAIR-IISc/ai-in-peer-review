This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities by combining attribute grammars with machine learning techniques. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions as trees constructed from grammar rules, and then use learned models (n-grams and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for equivalent expressions with lower computational complexity. This is a compelling approach to a challenging problem, bridging the gap between symbolic computation and machine learning. The paper is well-motivated, clearly identifying the limitations of existing methods and positioning this work as a significant step towards automating the discovery of mathematical simplifications.

The authors appropriately relate their work to existing areas such as theorem proving [5, 9, 11], program induction [18, 28], and probabilistic programming [12, 20], while clearly distinguishing their approach through its focus on polynomial simplification and the application of learning techniques. They also correctly position their contribution relative to symbolic computation engines like Maple [6] and Mathematica [27], highlighting the novelty of their learning-based search strategy. The related work section is thorough and demonstrates an awareness of relevant literature, including work in NLP [2, 3, 8, 15, 16, 22, 23, 25] and attribute grammars [10, 14, 19, 26].

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for this problem is a genuinely novel contribution. The application of RNNs to learn a continuous representation of mathematical structures is particularly interesting and opens up possibilities for further research.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach in discovering identities that are difficult or impossible to find manually or through brute-force search. The examples provided (e.g., reducing O(n<sup>3</sup>) to O(n<sup>2</sup>) complexity) are compelling.
*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The problem statement is well-defined, and the authors acknowledge the limitations of their current approach, specifically the restrictions on the types of expressions considered.
*   **Code Availability:**  The availability of code and evaluation data ([https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning](https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning)) is a major strength, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity Constraint:** The paper states the goal is to find expressions with lower *computational complexity*, but the algorithm doesn't explicitly enforce this constraint during the search. While acknowledged as non-trivial, a discussion of potential strategies to prioritize complexity reduction within the search space would strengthen the work.
*   **Restrictions & Generalizability:**  The current restrictions on the types of expressions (homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix variable) limit the applicability of the method. While the authors discuss these limitations, a more detailed justification, potentially with empirical analysis demonstrating the challenges of extending the framework, would be valuable.  Is the scheduler the primary limitation preventing generalization to more complex expressions, or are there other fundamental hurdles?
*   **Paper Structure & Clarity:** The paper's structure could be improved. The flow of ideas sometimes feels disjointed. A clearer separation of the framework components (grammar, representation, search strategy) with dedicated sections for each would enhance readability.  The description of the search process feels particularly dense.
*   **Minor Technical Issue:** The paper mentions a potential issue with the RBM-1 expression for k=1, n=1 in the supplementary material. Clarifying this point or addressing it in the revised paper is recommended. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in discovering useful mathematical identities.
*   The code availability promotes reproducibility and further research.
*   The work has the potential to impact areas such as symbolic computation and compiler optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The limitations of the current approach (restricted expression types) may limit its broader applicability.
*   The lack of explicit complexity optimization within the search algorithm is a weakness.
*   The paper's clarity and organization could be improved.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field. While the current limitations should be acknowledged, the novelty of the approach, the compelling empirical results, and the availability of code make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend **acceptance**, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the complexity constraint and improving the clarity of the presentation. Specifically, I would appreciate a more detailed discussion of how complexity might be incorporated into the search strategy and a clearer explanation of the scalability challenges involved in extending the framework to more general expressions.
