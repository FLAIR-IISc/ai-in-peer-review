Summary
This paper proposes a neural approach to learning representations for symbolic expressions and using them to discover mathematical identities. While the scope of expressions considered is fairly limited and there is no guarantee the method scales to richer algebraic structures, the problem is novel and potentially impactful, particularly for symbolic computation and optimization in scientific software.

Strengths
- Novel problem formulation: learning continuous representations for symbolic expressions to guide identity discovery.
- Potential practical impact on symbolic algebra systems and on simplifying expressions for lower runtime cost.
- The approach is intriguing and, if extended, could open a new line of work at the intersection of neural models and computer algebra.

Major Concerns and Questions
- Objective vs. algorithmic enforcement of computational complexity:
  The problem statement emphasizes finding expressions with lower computational complexity, but the current algorithm does not appear to enforce or optimize this explicitly. Please clarify:
  - How is “lower complexity” operationalized during search and selection?
  - Could you integrate a cost-aware objective (e.g., penalizing FLOPs, operation counts, tree depth/size, memory traffic) into training or candidate scoring?
  - Have you considered cost-informed search strategies (e.g., A* with cost heuristics, beam search with complexity-aware scoring, or Monte Carlo tree search with a reward that trades off correctness and cost)?
  - Can you incorporate operator-specific costs and hardware-aware latency models (or learned cost predictors) to better match the stated goal?
  Without some mechanism like the above, the method may discover equivalences but not preferentially select computationally cheaper ones.

- Scope restrictions and the scheduler in Section 2:
  The paper mentions restrictions and a scheduler; it is unclear whether the scheduler is the only limiting factor or if there are deeper grammatical/typing constraints.
  - Please formalize the grammar (operators, arities, associativity/commutativity, broadcasting rules) and the typing/dimensionality system used.
  - Provide empirical justification for the chosen limits (e.g., maximum depth, operator set, scheduler budget). Ablations varying these constraints would help clarify trade-offs between tractability and coverage.
  - Discuss failure modes: classes of identities your system cannot currently discover and which restriction is the bottleneck.

- Clarity and structure:
  The exposition is hard to follow. The pipeline would benefit from a clear staging with a running example:
  1) representation learning (training objective and data generation),
  2) candidate generation/search (grammar, scheduler, pruning criteria),
  3) equivalence testing/verification (symbolic vs. numeric checks, robustness),
  4) selection based on complexity or other criteria.
  A figure and concise pseudocode would greatly improve readability. Clearly state inputs/outputs at each stage.

- Dimensionality and validity of expressions:
  Are there explicit constraints or static checks for dimensions of vectors/matrices/tensors during search and verification? For example, in the supplementary material, the RBM-1 expression for k=1, n=1 appears dimensionally invalid. Please:
  - Specify the typing rules and shape inference used to prevent invalid compositions.
  - Correct or clarify the RBM-1 example and include additional examples covering edge cases (scalars, vectors, matrices) to illustrate the type system.

- Generalization beyond the studied expressions:
  The current scope is narrow. While a full proof of extensibility is unrealistic, a discussion of how the method could handle:
  - richer operator sets (e.g., non-commutative ops, matrix calculus, special functions),
  - larger expression trees,
  - noisy or approximate identities,
  would strengthen the contribution. Any preliminary results or negative findings would be valuable.

Suggestions for Improvement
- Integrate a complexity-aware scoring function during search; at minimum, penalize the number/type of expensive operations and tree size/depth.
- Explore a cost-regularized training objective or multi-objective selection to balance identity correctness and computational cost.
- Provide ablations on operator sets, search depth/beam width, and scheduler budgets.
- Add a formal grammar and type system, with static shape checks, and report the proportion of invalid candidates filtered.
- Improve organization and add a schematic overview of the pipeline with a running example.

Minor Issues and Typos
- Line 148: “has contains” → “contains” or “has”
- Line 289: “is is” → “is”
- Line 332: “show for” → “show” or “show that”
- Line 338: “by be” → “be”
- Supplement: RBM-1 for k=1, n=1 appears invalid; please correct or clarify dimensional assumptions.

Overall Assessment
An interesting and original direction with promising applications, but the current version underspecifies how computational complexity is handled and lacks clarity in the problem constraints and pipeline. I would be inclined to support acceptance if the authors can (i) make complexity a first-class objective in the method or provide strong empirical evidence that simpler identities are indeed preferred, (ii) formalize and justify the imposed restrictions, and (iii) significantly improve clarity and dimensional validity checks.
