This paper explores the application of machine learning techniques to discover efficient mathematical identities. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and aim to find analytically equivalent expressions with lower computational complexity. They propose two learning approaches – an n-gram model and a recursive neural network – to guide the tree search within this grammar, addressing the exponential growth of the search space. This work builds upon the areas of theorem proving [5, 9, 11], program induction [18, 28], and probabilistic programming [12, 20], but restricts its scope to polynomials of fixed degree, simplifying the computational challenges. The use of attribute grammars [14] is inspired by compiler construction and prior work in algorithmic problem solving [19, 26], while the n-gram model and RNN leverage techniques from natural language processing [2, 3, 8, 15, 16, 22, 23].

The paper’s core strength lies in its novel combination of symbolic computation, attribute grammars, and machine learning. The idea of using learning to guide the search for efficient mathematical identities is intriguing and represents a potentially fruitful direction for research. The authors demonstrate the ability to discover identities, in some cases exceeding manual derivation or brute-force search, and provide code and data for reproducibility. The learned representation of symbolic expressions using a recursive neural network is also a valuable contribution, potentially opening avenues for applying machine learning to other symbolic tasks.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the authors demonstrate success on specific examples, the significance of these results feels somewhat limited. The experiments focus on a relatively narrow set of expressions, and the performance gains, while present, aren’t always dramatic. The statement that the identities discovered are “previously undiscovered” should be carefully substantiated, as similar simplifications might exist in mathematical literature. The comparison to existing symbolic computation engines like Maple and Mathematica is brief, and a more thorough comparison outlining the advantages of this learning-based approach would strengthen the paper. The scalability of the approach is also a concern. The computational cost of exploring the search space still appears substantial, and it is unclear whether the learning methods will continue to effectively guide the search as the complexity of the target expressions increases. The ablation study comparing the different search strategies (n-gram vs. RNN) could be more detailed. While the n-gram models appear to outperform the RNN in some cases, a deeper analysis of why this is the case would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation.
*   Successful demonstration of identity discovery beyond brute-force methods.
*   Introduction of a novel framework based on attribute grammars.
*   Valuable contribution of a learned representation for symbolic expressions.
*   Publicly available code and data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of experiments and relatively modest performance gains.
*   Scalability concerns regarding the search space and learning methods.
*   Lack of detailed comparison to existing symbolic computation engines.
*   Potential for unsubstantiated claim of "previously undiscovered" identities.
*   The simplicity of the initial problems tackled raises concerns about the broader applicability of the approach.

**Overall:**

I believe this paper presents an interesting and novel idea, but its current form falls somewhat short of the high standards for NIPS publication. The experimental results, while positive, are not sufficiently compelling to demonstrate a significant breakthrough. The scalability and generalizability of the approach remain open questions. I recommend **weak reject**, but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit to a future conference. Specifically, expanding the experimental evaluation to a wider range of expressions, providing a more thorough comparison to existing methods, and addressing the scalability concerns would significantly strengthen the paper.
