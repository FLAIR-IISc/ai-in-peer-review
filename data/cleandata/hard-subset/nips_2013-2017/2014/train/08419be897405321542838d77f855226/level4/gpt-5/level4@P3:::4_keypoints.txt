- Proposes a framework to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities by searching symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar.  
- Checks expression equality via numerical descriptors evaluated on random inputs modulo a large prime.  
- Introduces learned search strategies: an n-gram model over subtrees and an RNN that predicts next grammar rules from continuous embeddings.  
- Allows linear combinations of multiple trees, solved by modular Gaussian elimination to match target descriptors.  
- Experiments show learned strategies outperform random search and often find identities reducing O(n^3) to O(n^2) or turning exponential-time into polynomial-time.  
- Novelty: framing identity discovery as learning-guided grammar search and using RNN embeddings for symbolic expressions.  
- Significance: can discover practical, nontrivial identities useful for code optimization and symbolic libraries; code released for reproducibility.  
- Technical quality: modular and coherent system design; practical use of numerical descriptor modulo a prime and thoughtful RNN training curriculum.  
- Empirics: strong evidence that learned strategies, especially higher-order n-grams, greatly expand solvable targets; RBM families are challenging stress tests.  
- Main weakness: search does not explicitly optimize computational complexity; complexity is only indirectly controlled via grammar pruning.  
- Equivalence guarantees are weak: collision probability of modulo-p testing is not quantified and needs formal justification or ablations.  
- Linear-combination coefficients unclear: paper must clarify allowed coefficient sets, lifting modular solutions to reals, and whether scalar constants are in the grammar.  
- Scope restricted to homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, and mainly single-variable inputs without systematic justification or sensitivity analysis.  
- RNN underperforms n-grams in many cases; need discussion on when continuous representations help and exploration of better architectures/learning signals.  
- Clarity: pipeline could be presented more linearly with a schematic and pseudocode for the scheduler/strategy interface.  
- Recommendations for improvement: include cost-aware search, formal Schwartz–Zippel-style bounds or multiple primes, coefficient-lifting procedure, and experiments relaxing scope.  
- Questions for authors: how are modular coefficients validated over reals, can complexity be incorporated into search objectives, and which components limit scalability to multiple variables?  
- Pros for acceptance: novel framing, demonstrated practical identity discovery, reusable continuous embedding idea, and released code.  
- Cons for acceptance: no explicit complexity optimization, limited theoretical equivalence guarantees, narrow scope, and RNN underperformance relative to n-grams.  
- Minor issues: several typos and need for consistent notation and clearer type/conformability specifications in examples.  
- Overall assessment: interesting and creative work that would benefit from clearer correctness and complexity treatment; current recommendation: weak accept.
