This paper presents a novel approach for learning representations of symbolic expressions to discover their mathematical identities using neural networks. While the method is interesting and could have a significant impact on symbolic software libraries, its application is currently limited to a narrow class of expressions. The authors do not demonstrate that the approach can be extended to more complex mathematical forms.

- The problem is framed as a search for expressions with lower computational complexity, yet the proposed algorithm does not appear to enforce this constraint. While acknowledging this is non-trivial, have the authors considered methods for restricting the search space to solutions that satisfy this complexity requirement?

- Regarding the restrictions on expression complexity mentioned in Section 2, it is unclear if the scheduler is the sole limiting factor. The paper would be strengthened by a more formal justification or empirical evidence for why these specific restrictions were chosen.

- The structure of the paper is difficult to follow. Clarity could be significantly improved by explaining the different stages of the proposed method more explicitly.

- Is there a constraint on the size of the vectors/matrices? For example, the expression for RBM-1 in the supplementary material does not seem to be valid for the case where k=1 and n=1.
