Summary
This paper explores the use of deep neural networks to discover mathematical identities, positioning the task as a new application area for DNNs. The problem is interesting and potentially impactful: automated discovery of identities could inform symbolic computation, theorem proving, and program synthesis. The authors present a system that learns from a restricted family of expressions and reports identity discovery performance on this controlled setting.

Strengths
- Novel and intriguing problem formulation that connects deep learning with symbolic mathematics.
- The paper takes a first step toward learning-based discovery of mathematical structure, which could open up a valuable research direction.
- The experimental setup appears straightforward and the writing is generally clear.

Weaknesses
- The experimental scope is very limited. The identities are drawn from a constrained set of operators and expression templates, which makes it hard to assess real-world utility.
- Reported performance is modest even within this restricted regime. Given the tight constraints on the search space, it is unclear that a neural approach is warranted; simpler symbolic methods (e.g., rule-based rewriting, term enumeration with pruning, or standard computer algebra systems) might solve these cases more reliably and efficiently.
- There is no strong baseline comparison. Without comparisons to symbolic search, algebra systems (e.g., Mathematica/SymPy), or hand-crafted heuristics tailored to the same grammar, it is difficult to judge the contribution of the proposed learning component.
- Scalability and generalization are not convincingly demonstrated. It is unclear how the method would extend to larger expression trees, richer operator sets, or identities that require multi-step reasoning. The paper does not provide evidence that the learned representations capture reusable structure beyond the specific training regime.
- Limited analysis. The paper could benefit from a deeper error analysis (e.g., which classes of identities fail, whether failures correlate with tree depth or operator types) and ablations that isolate what the network is actually learning versus memorizing.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen baselines. Include:
  - A symbolic enumeration or term-rewriting baseline with cost-based pruning.
  - Off-the-shelf computer algebra (simplify/FullSimplify) restricted to the same operator set and expression depth.
  - Heuristics derived from known algebraic rules applied systematically.
- Expand the benchmark to better test generalization:
  - Vary expression depth and breadth; report performance as a function of tree size.
  - Introduce new operators at test time to probe compositionality.
  - Evaluate on held-out identity families and out-of-distribution templates.
  - Provide time-to-discovery and sample efficiency metrics, not just success rates.
- Provide a clearer problem definition and evaluation protocol:
  - Precisely specify the grammar, distribution over expressions, and how positives/negatives are generated.
  - Define success criteria, confidence thresholds, and how candidate identities are verified.
- Include ablations and diagnostics:
  - Compare different expression encodings (sequence vs. tree-structured), architectures, and training losses.
  - Show sensitivity to training set size and noise.
  - Offer qualitative examples where the model discovers nontrivial identities not easily found by simple rules.
- Discuss scalability:
  - Analyze computational complexity and practical limits.
  - Outline a plausible path to richer algebraic domains (e.g., polynomials with higher degree, trigonometric/exponential operators, rational functions), or argue for hybrid neural-symbolic systems where the network guides a symbolic search.

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an appealing and underexplored application area. However, in its current form, the work feels preliminary. The empirical results are limited to a narrow setting and do not yet demonstrate a clear advantage over simpler, well-established symbolic methods. Claims about extensibility are not substantiated by experiments or analysis. With stronger baselines, broader and more challenging benchmarks, and deeper insight into what the network learns, this could become a compelling contribution. At present, I view it as slightly below the threshold for NIPS 2014.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline), with encouragement to resubmit after substantial strengthening of experiments, baselines, and analysis.
