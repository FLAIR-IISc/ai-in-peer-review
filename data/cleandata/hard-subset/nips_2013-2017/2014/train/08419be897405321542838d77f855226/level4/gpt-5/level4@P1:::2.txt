This paper proposes two methods for searching for simpler symbolic expression trees. The first uses n-grams computed over sequences of sub-expressions to bias the search toward promising patterns observed in prior solutions. The second trains a recursive neural network (RNN) to produce continuous representations of symbolic expression trees; these representations are then used by a classifier to predict which grammar rule is most likely to combine subtrees to match a target expression efficiently. According to Figure 3, the n-gram approach outperforms the RNN on two of the three evaluated tasks.

Comments with respect to the review criteria:

- Quality: High. The technical approach is sound and the methodology is clearly defined: learn from solutions to simpler targets to guide search on harder ones. The empirical comparison indicates that learned strategies can outperform random search, and that n-grams can be particularly effective relative to the RNN on the reported tasks.

- Clarity: The paper is somewhat hard to follow, especially Section 7.2. The presentation in this section is dense, and the flow between problem families, search strategies, and results could be more clearly organized to help the reader track what is being compared and why.

- Originality: High. Combining n-gram models and an RNN-based continuous representation to guide search over symbolic expression trees is novel in this context.

- Significance: Low. It is unlikely that practitioners seeking efficient computations would adopt this approach; the problem setup feels contrived, and the practical impact of discovering these identities via learned search is unclear. While the methods can find more computationally efficient symbolic expression trees, the case for real-world utility is not convincingly made.

Pros:
- Technically solid learning-based search strategies.
- Clear experimental evidence that learned guidance can outperform random search.
- Novel application of RNNs to produce continuous representations of symbolic expressions.

Cons:
- Clarity issues, particularly in Section 7.2, make the empirical narrative difficult to follow.
- Practical significance is unclear; the problem appears contrived, and it is doubtful that this will be used in practice for efficient computation.
- The n-gram approach appears to work better than the RNN on most tasks, raising questions about the added value of the more complex model in this setting.
