- Novel direction: applying machine learning/deep nets to automated discovery of mathematical identities.  
- Paper is preliminary and may not meet NIPS publication standards.  
- Main concern: demonstrated performance is on a constrained set of expressions, suggesting the problem is oversimplified for the methods used.  
- Results do not convincingly demonstrate scalability or benefit for more general expressions.  
- Method: attribute grammars combined with n-gram models and a recursive neural network — technically sound and clearly defined.  
- Implementation detail: integer-based descriptors to avoid numerical issues is a clever touch.  
- Representation learning: learned continuous representations for symbolic expressions via RNN is promising.  
- Experiments: tested on polynomial expansions and RBM-related terms with some complexity reduction but only modest gains.  
- Limited impact: discovered identities are not substantially more efficient or hard-to-find by standard mathematical manipulation.  
- Scalability concern: search space could become prohibitive for complex, less-structured expressions.  
- Evaluation insufficient: lacks comparisons to established symbolic systems (Mathematica/Maple) and alternative search strategies; search cut-off may limit findings.  
- Clarity issues: some experimental choices (n-gram weighting, grammar rule removal) need clearer rationale.  
- Related work: appropriately cites theorem proving, program induction, probabilistic programming, and NLP techniques but should discuss symbolic computation engines in more depth.  
- Recommendation: reject now but encourage revision with stronger scalability results, more thorough evaluation, and clearer experimental details.
