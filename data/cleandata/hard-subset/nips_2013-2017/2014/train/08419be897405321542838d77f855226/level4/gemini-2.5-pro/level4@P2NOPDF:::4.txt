This paper proposes a novel approach for discovering mathematical identities by learning distributed representations of symbolic expressions using neural networks. The core idea is to train a model to predict whether two expressions are equivalent, and then leverage the learned representations to search for new, potentially simpler, identities.

The application of neural networks to symbolic mathematics is an intriguing and creative direction. The proposed method presents a compelling way to tackle the challenging problem of identifying mathematical equivalences, a task fundamental to symbolic computation. If this approach can be extended to more complex mathematical domains, it could have a significant impact on the development of symbolic software libraries and automated theorem provers. The novelty of the application area makes this an interesting contribution.

However, the paper has several areas that could be significantly improved:

1.  **Clarity and Structure:** The paper's overall structure is difficult to follow. The different stages of the proposed method—data generation, model training, and identity search—are not always clearly delineated, which makes the pipeline challenging to understand. The exposition would be greatly enhanced by a clearer, step-by-step explanation of the methodology, perhaps supplemented with a diagram or a more explicit algorithmic breakdown.

2.  **Constraint on Computational Complexity:** A key motivation stated in the problem description is the discovery of expressions with lower computational complexity. However, the proposed algorithm does not appear to explicitly incorporate or enforce this constraint during the search for new identities. While this is admittedly a non-trivial task, the paper would be stronger if it discussed potential strategies for biasing the search towards computationally simpler expressions. For example, could a complexity metric be incorporated into the objective function or used as a filter during the search phase?

3.  **Justification of Limitations:** Section 2 outlines several restrictions on the types of expressions the system can handle. The justification for these limitations is not fully developed. For instance, is the scheduler the sole bottleneck preventing the application to more complex expressions, or are there other fundamental challenges? The paper would benefit from a more formal analysis or empirical evidence supporting the choice of these specific constraints. This would help the reader better understand the current boundaries of the method and the key obstacles to scaling it up.

4.  **Parameter Constraints:** There appears to be an unstated constraint regarding the dimensionality of the vectors and matrices. For example, the expression for RBM-1 provided in the supplementary material does not seem to be well-defined for the case where k=1 and n=1. Please clarify the valid range for these parameters or correct the provided expression if there is an error.

**Minor Issues and Typos:**
Some minor typos were found that should be corrected:
*   Line 148: "has contains" should be "contains" or "has".
*   Line 289: "is is" should be "is".
*   Line 332: "show for"
*   Line 338: "by be"

In summary, this paper introduces a promising and original direction for applying machine learning to symbolic mathematics. While the current work is limited in the scope of expressions it can analyze, and the paper could be improved in its clarity and justification of certain design choices, the core idea is compelling. I believe this work will be of interest to the community and could inspire further research at the intersection of neural networks and symbolic computation.
