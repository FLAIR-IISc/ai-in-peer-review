This paper introduces an interesting approach to discovering more efficient algebraic identities. A key contribution is demonstrating that distributed, recursive neural representations can effectively classify these identities. The authors propose a novel grammar framework and integrate machine learning techniques to guide the search for computationally simpler expressions, achieving results beyond what brute-force or manual derivation can accomplish.

The problem definition is somewhat restricted, focusing on homogeneous polynomials of a single matrix. While acknowledged by the authors, it’s worth noting this limitation in the context of broader applicability. 

Section 4.2 contains a slightly unclear explanation: “weight tensor W3 (of size lxlxl, so that the output is also size l).” This suggests the use of a neural tensor network rather than a standard linear network. It would be valuable to clarify this point and potentially discuss Bowman’s (2013) work, which demonstrates the benefits of tensor networks in similar tasks.

In Section 7.2, the interplay between the RNN and the different search strategies is not entirely clear. The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of how proposals are generated during the search process. A clearer description of the search mechanism would enhance understanding.

Several minor typographical errors were noted: "has contains a single matrix," "listed in table Table 1," "vector is the presented to the next operation," "The final vector is pass to a softmax classifier," and “as to how explore space of possible.” Additionally, the figure 3 label should be RNN, not TNN.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The authors address the problem of discovering mathematically equivalent but computationally cheaper expressions. They formulate this as a search problem within an attribute grammar. Due to the exponential growth of the search space, they employ machine learning—specifically n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs)—to guide the search. These models are trained on solutions to simpler expressions, enabling the discovery of identities for more complex cases. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach with several examples, including reductions in computational complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) and even from exponential to polynomial time.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of machine learning techniques, particularly RNNs, to the discovery of mathematical identities is a novel and promising direction.
*   **Effectiveness:** The experimental results demonstrate the ability to find identities that are beyond the reach of traditional methods. The reported reductions in computational complexity are significant.
*   **Clear Framework:** The attribute grammar provides a well-defined framework for representing and manipulating symbolic expressions.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors provide code and data, fostering reproducibility and further research.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments showcase that the proposed methods can significantly improve computational efficiency for certain expressions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Restricted Scope:** The focus on homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix limits the generality of the approach.
*   **Search Strategy Clarity:** The explanation of how the search strategy utilizes the learned models could be more detailed.
*   **RNN vs. Tensor Networks:** The use of tensor networks deserves discussion given their documented advantages in similar contexts (Bowman, 2013).
*   **Limited Generalization:** The performance on the most challenging family of expressions (RBM-2) suggests limitations in the generalization capabilities of the current approach.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to a challenging problem.
*   The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
*   The code and data availability promotes reproducibility and further research.
*   The work represents a significant step towards bridging the gap between machine learning and symbolic computation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The restricted scope of the problem limits the broader applicability of the proposed techniques.
*   The lack of clarity in the search strategy explanation hinders full understanding of the approach.
*   The limited performance on the RBM-2 family raises concerns about the robustness and generalization capabilities of the model.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to discovering mathematical identities. While the restricted scope and minor clarity issues are concerns, the significant contributions of the work outweigh these weaknesses. The paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS conference and has the potential to inspire further research in this area.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Clarify the use of tensor networks and discuss Bowman’s (2013) work.
2.  Provide a more detailed explanation of the search strategy.
3.  Address the limitations in generalization observed with the RBM-2 family.
4.  Correct the typographical errors.
