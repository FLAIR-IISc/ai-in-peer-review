

This paper introduces an attribute grammar framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities through machine learning techniques. The authors propose using n-gram models and recursive neural networks to guide the search for symbolic expression trees that compute equivalent results to a target expression but with lower computational complexity. The framework builds on Knuth's attribute grammar formalism [14] and adapts techniques from natural language processing, including n-gram models and recursive neural networks, to the domain of symbolic computation. This work extends previous applications of grammars in compiler design [19] and NLP [22, 23] to a novel optimization problem in symbolic mathematics.

The paper demonstrates significant technical quality, with well-justified claims supported by experimental results. The authors successfully apply machine learning to a combinatorially challenging search space, showing that learned search strategies outperform random approaches for complex expressions. The framework discovers previously unknown mathematical identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) or better.

The clarity of the paper is somewhat problematic, particularly in Section 7.2, where the experimental details and results for the different expression families become difficult to follow. While the paper's technical content is sound, the presentation of results and methodology could benefit from clearer organization and more detailed explanations.

The originality of the work is high, as the authors present a novel application of machine learning to symbolic identity discovery, extending attribute grammars to a learning framework for mathematical optimization. The paper makes clear connections to related work in theorem proving [5, 9, 11], program induction [18, 28], and NLP [22, 23], while establishing distinct contributions to the field.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited. While the technical approach is sound, the problem addressed appears somewhat contrived and unlikely to be of practical importance to researchers or practitioners seeking to perform efficient computations. The paper demonstrates the ability to find mathematical identities with lower complexity, but it's unclear whether these identities would be valuable in real-world applications. The most compelling potential applications mentioned (e.g., for dropout or RBM partition functions) remain speculative, as the paper doesn't demonstrate concrete implementation or performance gains in these specific contexts.

Strengths: High technical quality, novel application of learning techniques to symbolic computation, well-supported experimental results.

Weaknesses: Limited practical significance, unclear real-world applicability, presentation challenges in Section 7.2.

The paper's contribution to the field of machine learning is primarily methodological, introducing a framework for using learning to guide symbolic search. While the approach is technically sound and original, its impact may be limited to the niche problem of mathematical identity discovery rather than broader machine learning applications. The methods could potentially be valuable for compiler optimization or symbolic computation tools, but the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate this potential.
