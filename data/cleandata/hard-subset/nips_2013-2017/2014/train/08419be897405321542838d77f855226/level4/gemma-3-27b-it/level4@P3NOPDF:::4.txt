## Review of NIPS 2014 Submission – [Paper ID]

This paper introduces a novel application of neural networks – learning representations of symbolic expressions and discovering mathematical identities between them. While the scope of expressions currently considered is limited, and the potential for generalization to more complex cases remains unproven, I find the work interesting and believe it holds potential impact, particularly for symbolic software libraries and automated theorem proving.

The core motivation, as stated, is to identify expressions with reduced computational complexity. However, the proposed algorithm does not explicitly incorporate or enforce such a constraint during the search process. While acknowledging the difficulty of achieving this, I would like to understand if the authors have considered potential strategies for limiting the search space to favor computationally simpler solutions. A discussion of this, even if preliminary, would strengthen the paper.

The restrictions detailed in Section 2, particularly regarding the scheduler, raise questions about the primary limitations to scaling to more complex mathematical expressions. Is the scheduler truly the dominant factor, or are other aspects of the proposed framework equally limiting? The justification for these restrictions feels somewhat ad-hoc. I would appreciate a more formal argument, or ideally, empirical experiments demonstrating the impact of these constraints and motivating their specific choices. Exploring ablation studies where these restrictions are relaxed, even if performance degrades, could provide valuable insights.

The paper's structure currently hinders clarity. The flow between stages – representation, learning, and identity discovery – is not immediately obvious. A more explicit breakdown of the methodology, potentially through dedicated subsections within the methods section, would significantly improve readability. Clearly delineating each stage and its contribution to the overall process would benefit the reader.

Finally, the paper lacks clarity regarding potential size limitations of the vector/matrix representations. For instance, in the supplementary material, the expression for RBM-1 when *k*=1 and *n*=1 (page [page number in supplementary material]) appears to be invalid. Specifying any inherent constraints on these dimensions is crucial for understanding the practical applicability of the method.

Specific minor errors identified include:

*   Page 332: “show for” should be “show that” or “shown for”.
*   Page 148: “has contains” should be “contains”.
*   Page 289: “is is” should be “is”.
*   Page 338: “by be” should be “be”.

In summary, this paper presents a creative application of neural networks to a challenging problem. The novelty of the approach is commendable. However, addressing the concerns outlined above – particularly regarding the enforcement of computational complexity, the justification of restrictions, the clarity of presentation, and the discussion of size limitations – would significantly strengthen the contribution and impact of this work. Despite these weaknesses, I believe the paper warrants further consideration, and I am cautiously optimistic about its potential.
