This paper introduces an interesting solution to finding more efficient algebraic identities. It is very interesting to see that distributed, recursive neural representations can successfully classify these identities.

The problem definition seems quite restricted. The authors consider the restricted setting where the target expression T is a homogeneous polynomial of degree k, the variables V contain a single matrix or vector A, and the output O is a scalar. While these assumptions may seem quite restrictive, they still permit a rich family of expressions for the algorithm to explore.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions, which allows them to build trees that combine rules to form expressions involving the variables. They also propose two novel learning approaches, an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN), to learn from simpler expressions and guide the tree search.

The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework in discovering efficient identities. The results show that the learning approaches generally perform better than the random strategy for large values of k, with the 3-gram, 4-gram, and 5-gram models outperforming the RNN for some families of expressions.

However, there are a few minor issues with the paper. The explanation of the weight tensor W3 in Section 4.2 could be clarified. It is not clear if the authors use a neural tensor network instead of a simple linear network. Additionally, the proposal generation in the search strategy could be explained better.

There are also a few typos in the paper, including "has contains a single matrix", "listed in table Table 1", "vector is the presented to the next operation", "The final vector is pass to a softmax classifier", "as to how explore space of possible", and "The scheduler is is fixed". Furthermore, the difficulty of the task can be appreciated by looking at the examples in Figure 1, and the phrase "which can easily captured with" should be rephrased.

Lastly, the caption of Figure 3 should be corrected to read "RNN" instead of "TNN".

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper introduces a novel solution to finding more efficient algebraic identities using machine learning techniques.
* The proposed framework is well-explained and demonstrated to be effective in discovering efficient identities.
* The experiments show promising results, with the learning approaches outperforming the random strategy for large values of k.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The problem definition seems quite restricted, which may limit the applicability of the proposed framework.
* The explanation of some technical details, such as the weight tensor W3, could be clarified.
* There are a few minor issues with the paper, including typos and unclear explanations.

Overall, I believe that the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of machine learning and symbolic computation, and I recommend its acceptance.
