- Core contribution: applies machine learning to guide search for computationally efficient equivalent symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar and tree search.  
- Method: introduces two learned search heuristics — an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN) — trained on solutions to simpler problems to prune the exponential search space.  
- Representation: learns continuous vector embeddings for symbolic expressions via an RNN, bridging symbolic computation and NLP-style representation learning.  
- Strength — Originality: novel, creative application of data-driven ML to discovering mathematical identities, with the RNN embedding being particularly innovative.  
- Strength — Technical quality: technically sound framework with solid empirical results showing discovery of non-obvious, efficient identities beyond random search.  
- Strength — Significance: elegant bootstrapping approach (learn on simple problems to solve harder ones) addressing combinatorial search challenges.  
- Weakness — Scope/practical impact: evaluated on a restricted class of homogeneous polynomial expressions; unclear generalization to broader symbolic/compilation settings.  
- Weakness — Clarity: experimental setup (Section 7.2) is dense and would benefit from a clearer, step-by-step construction of k-th terms for each family.  
- Weakness — Relative performance: simpler n-gram models often outperform the RNN on the search task, raising questions about the RNN's practical advantage.  
- For acceptance: highly original ML-to-symbolic approach, novel learned representations, and strong empirical evidence of finding hard identities.  
- Against acceptance: limited demonstrated applicability to constrained expression classes and weaker-than-expected RNN performance relative to n-gram baselines.
