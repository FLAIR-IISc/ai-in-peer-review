

The paper proposes a novel application of neural networks to learn representations of symbolic expressions and identify their mathematical identities. While the scope of expressions considered is limited, the approach represents an interesting and potentially impactful contribution for symbolic computation libraries. However, several concerns require clarification before the work can be fully appreciated.

The problem statement emphasizes finding expressions with lower computational complexity, yet the algorithm does not enforce this constraint. The authors should address how the search space is restricted to favor such solutions, particularly given the stated goal. Without this, the practical relevance of the method for complexity-sensitive applications remains unclear.

Section 2 outlines restrictions on expression complexity, but it is ambiguous whether the scheduler is the sole limiting factor for scalability. The authors should provide either formal justification for these constraints or empirical evidence demonstrating their necessity—e.g., by showing how relaxing them leads to intractable search spaces or failed identity discovery. Without this, the rationale for the chosen limitations appears underdeveloped.

The paper’s structure impedes clarity. Key stages (e.g., representation learning, identity search) are not distinctly introduced or sequenced. A more explicit roadmap or modular explanation of the pipeline would significantly improve readability. For instance, the transition from symbolic expression parsing to neural representation training is abrupt, making it difficult to follow the methodological flow.

A technical concern arises regarding the vector/matrix size constraints. The RBM-1 expression in the supplementary material appears invalid for the case k=1, n=1 (as the dimensions do not align). The authors must clarify whether implicit size constraints exist or correct the example to ensure consistency.

Minor issues include:
- p. 332: "show for" → "show that"
- p. 148: "has contains" → "contains"
- p. 289: "is is" → "is"
- p. 338: "by be" → "by"

While the core idea is novel and promising for symbolic software, the current presentation lacks sufficient rigor to support its broader applicability. The work would benefit from:
1. Explicit integration of computational complexity constraints into the algorithm.
2. Stronger justification for expression complexity restrictions.
3. A clearer, more structured presentation of the methodology.
4. Correction of the RBM-1 example and typos.

Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper’s contribution and clarify its potential impact. As presented, the limited scope and unresolved methodological gaps prevent full endorsement, but the foundational concept merits further development.
