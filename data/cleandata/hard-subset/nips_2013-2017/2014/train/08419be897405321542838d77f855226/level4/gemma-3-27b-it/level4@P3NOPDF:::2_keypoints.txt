- Proposes two methods for finding simpler symbolic expression trees: an n-gram over sub-expression sequences and an RNN that learns continuous tree representations to predict rule applications.  
- Empirical result: n-gram approach outperforms the RNN on two of three tasks (Figure 3).  
- Quality: technical contributions are sound, well-executed, and experiments are reported in sufficient detail.  
- Clarity: overall moderate—Section 7.2 (RNN implementation/training) is opaque and needs clearer, more intuitive explanations and examples.  
- Clarity: rationale for RNN architectural choices (recursion strategy, hidden sizes) and inconsistent notation should be clarified.  
- Originality: high—novel combination of symbolic tree search with n-gram analysis and RNN-based tree representations.  
- Originality: applying RNNs to guide symbolic expression construction appears to be a new direction.  
- Significance: low—the motivation for optimizing computational simplicity over accuracy is not convincingly established and practical impact is unclear.  
- Significance: evaluation tasks seem contrived; authors should better justify task choice and real-world relevance (e.g., embedded systems, real-time control, program synthesis).  
- Evaluation: provide more details on the three tasks, target-function complexity, and selection rationale.  
- Baselines: compare to standard symbolic regression methods (e.g., genetic programming) to better benchmark performance.  
- Scalability & rules: discuss computational cost scaling with expression/search complexity and clarify whether the rule set is fixed or learnable and its impact on performance.  
- Overall: technically solid and original but limited by unclear motivation and presentation; addressing clarity and application relevance would strengthen the paper.
