This paper introduces a novel application of neural networks for learning distributed representations of symbolic mathematical expressions. The ultimate goal is to use these representations to discover new mathematical identities. While the scope of expressions currently handled is limited, the approach is intriguing and could have a significant impact on the field of symbolic computation and its integration into software libraries.

**Strengths:**
*   The primary strength of this work is its novelty. Applying neural network-based representation learning to the discovery of mathematical identities is a creative and promising research direction.
*   The paper presents an interesting proof-of-concept that demonstrates the potential for this approach, which could inspire further research in this area.
*   The potential application for improving symbolic software libraries is a well-motivated and impactful goal.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Constraint on Computational Complexity:** The problem statement specifies a goal of finding expressions with lower computational complexity. However, it is not clear how this objective is incorporated into the proposed algorithm or search procedure. The paper does not seem to describe a mechanism that explicitly enforces this constraint. Have the authors considered potential solutions for guiding the search toward simpler or more computationally efficient identities? For instance, could a complexity penalty be added to the loss function or used as a filter during the candidate search phase?

2.  **Justification for Expression Restrictions:** Section 2 discusses several restrictions on the complexity of the mathematical expressions the system can handle, attributing this primarily to the scheduler. Is the scheduler the sole bottleneck, or are there other architectural or theoretical limitations? The justification for the current set of restrictions feels underdeveloped. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more formal analysis or empirical evidence explaining why these specific limitations were chosen and outlining a path toward relaxing them for more complex expressions in future work.

3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The structure of the paper can be difficult to follow at times. The different stages of the proposed method—such as representation learning, candidate generation, and verification—are not always clearly delineated. A clearer exposition of the overall pipeline, perhaps with a dedicated section or diagram that walks the reader through each step, would greatly enhance the reader's understanding of the proposed system.

4.  **Parameter Constraints:** A specific question regarding the implementation details: Are there constraints on the size of the vectors and matrices in the expressions? For example, for the case where k=1 and n=1, the expression for RBM-1 provided in the supplementary material does not appear to be valid. Could the authors please clarify the valid domain for these parameters?

**Minor Comments and Typos:**
*   Line 148: "has contains" should be "has" or "contains".
*   Line 289: "is is" should be "is".
*   Line 332: "show for" seems grammatically incorrect; perhaps it should be "shown for" or rephrased.
*   Line 338: "by be" should likely be "to be" or "by".

In summary, this is an interesting and novel paper that introduces a promising new research direction. While its current capabilities are limited and there are open questions regarding its scalability, the core idea is valuable. The paper would be improved by addressing the points above, particularly regarding the enforcement of the complexity constraint and the clarity of the overall method.
