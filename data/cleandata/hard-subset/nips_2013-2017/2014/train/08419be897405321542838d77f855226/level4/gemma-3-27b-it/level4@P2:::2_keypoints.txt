- Introduces a novel framework combining attribute grammars and learned models (n-grams, RNNs) to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities.
- Represents symbolic expressions as trees from an attribute grammar and uses learned models trained on simpler expressions to guide search for lower-complexity equivalents.
- Demonstrates ability to find identities beyond brute-force search or manual derivation.
- Situates work relative to symbolic computation, theorem proving, program induction, and ML-for-reasoning, highlighting differences from rule-based engines like Maple/Mathematica.
- Novelty: unique combination of attribute grammars and ML, and use of RNNs for continuous representations of symbolic expressions.
- Technical soundness: clear problem definition, grammar, learning methods, and supportive experimental evaluation.
- Demonstrated results: compelling examples showing reduced computational complexity and better-than-brute-force performance.
- Code and data are publicly available, aiding reproducibility.
- Clarity issues: some sections (notably Section 7.2) are dense and need clearer explanations and narrative connections.
- Significance uncertain: practical impact and real-world applications of discovered identities are not well established.
- Limited scope: restriction to homogeneous polynomials and few variables limits generalizability; extensions are insufficiently explored.
- RNN performance concerns: n-gram models outperform RNNs, and the paper lacks deeper analysis explaining this gap.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically solid approach with strong results and reproducible artifacts that open new research directions.
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear practical significance, readability issues, and underperforming RNN component.
- Overall assessment: interesting and competent work deserving consideration, but needs revisions to improve clarity and discuss broader impact.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions focused on clarity, broader application discussion, and deeper analysis of RNN vs. n-gram performance.
