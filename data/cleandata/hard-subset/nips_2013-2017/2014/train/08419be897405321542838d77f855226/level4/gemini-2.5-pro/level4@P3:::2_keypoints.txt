- Paper proposes an attribute-grammar-based framework to search for computationally efficient, analytically equivalent symbolic expressions using learned guidance.  
- Expression representations: numerical evaluations over finite fields and learned recursive neural network (RNN) embeddings.  
- Search strategies: an n-gram model capturing local sub-expression frequencies and an RNN-based policy using learned embeddings.  
- Experiments on five expression families (including symmetric polynomials and RBM Taylor terms) show learning-guided search outperforms random search and can discover non-trivial identities.  
- Originality: novel application of machine learning to discover efficient mathematical identities and learn search policies over grammars.  
- Creative contribution: recursive neural network embeddings for symbolic expressions, linking NLP methods to symbolic computation.  
- Technical quality: well-realized end-to-end system with thorough experiments demonstrating clear gains from learning-based methods.  
- Significance: approach addresses learning-to-search in huge combinatorial spaces and could impact theorem proving, program synthesis, and compiler optimizations.  
- Clarity issue: paper is hard to follow in places (notably Section 7.2) and needs more intuitive, step-by-step examples for complex families.  
- Practicality/scope limited: demonstrated on scalar homogeneous polynomials with single-variable structures; unclear generalization to multi-variable, non-polynomial, or conditional expressions.  
- Lack of scalability discussion: paper should compare potential extension challenges and scalability versus mature symbolic engines (e.g., Mathematica, Maple).  
- Insufficient analysis: surprising result that simple n-grams outperform the RNN lacks deep investigation into causes (data regularity vs. RNN limitations).  
- Arguments for acceptance: highly original, technically sound, learns continuous expression representations, and discovers new complex identities.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited real-world applicability, unclear presentation/reproducibility, and inadequate analysis of why the n-gram baseline outperforms the RNN.  
- Overall judgment: strong and thought-provoking work with addressable weaknesses in clarity and scope, meriting acceptance if revised.
