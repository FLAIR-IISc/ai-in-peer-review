- Proposes two methods: an n-gram model over sub-expression sequences and an RNN that learns continuous vector representations to predict subtree-combining rules.  
- Experiments (Fig. 3) indicate the n-gram approach outperforms the RNN on 2 of 3 benchmarks.  
- Quality: technically sound methods and a rigorous experimental setup.  
- Clarity: paper is hard to follow in places; Section 7.2 is dense and needs an illustrative example and clearer linkage between high-level concepts and math.  
- Originality: high — novel application of n-gram models and recursive neural networks to symbolic expression simplification.  
- Significance: low — unclear practical advantage over optimized, heuristic-based simplification algorithms used in compilers and symbolic systems.  
- Problem formulation seems somewhat contrived and lacks a compelling practitioner motivation for adopting computationally intensive search methods.  
- Missing discussion of concrete real-world applications where these methods would be advantageous.  
- Lacks comparison against traditional simplification baselines to establish practical relevance.
