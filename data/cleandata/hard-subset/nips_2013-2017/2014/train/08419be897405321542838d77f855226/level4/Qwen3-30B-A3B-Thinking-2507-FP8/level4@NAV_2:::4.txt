

The paper introduces a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars combined with machine learning techniques. The authors present a grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions and demonstrate how n-gram models and recursive neural networks can guide the search for computationally efficient versions of target expressions, particularly for polynomial expressions involving matrix operations. This work builds upon Knuth's attribute grammar framework [14], which has been previously applied to algorithmic problems and bioinformatics, but extends it to the novel domain of mathematical identity discovery. The authors also draw inspiration from natural language processing techniques, applying n-gram language models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to symbolic computation, which represents a creative interdisciplinary application.

The paper's main strengths include its technical soundness in developing the attribute grammar framework and machine learning approaches, as evidenced by the experimental results showing the discovery of previously unknown identities that reduce computational complexity (e.g., from O(n³) to O(n²) for certain matrix operations). The authors provide a clear explanation of how the framework can be applied to specific problem families (such as (ΣAAT)ᵏ and (Σ(A.*A)AT)ᵏ), with concrete examples demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach. The paper also makes a significant contribution by learning continuous representations for symbolic expressions, which opens the door to applying numerous machine learning techniques to symbolic domains.

However, the paper has several limitations that need to be addressed. First, the problem scope is quite restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with a single matrix variable (Section 2), which limits the generalizability of the approach. The authors acknowledge these restrictions but do not provide sufficient justification for why they are necessary or how they might be overcome in future work. Second, while the paper states the goal is to find expressions with lower computational complexity, the algorithm itself does not explicitly enforce this constraint during the search process, which is a significant oversight. The authors should explain how they ensure the discovered expressions actually have lower complexity rather than just being mathematically equivalent.

The paper's structure could also be improved for better clarity. The explanation of the framework's stages (particularly the transition from the grammar representation to the learning approaches) would benefit from more detailed descriptions and possibly visual aids. The description of the linear combination of trees (Section 5) is particularly challenging to follow without clearer examples.

Regarding significance, the identities discovered could potentially be valuable for optimizing machine learning algorithms (such as for dropout or RBM partition function computation), but the paper doesn't sufficiently explore these practical applications. The paper also doesn't provide empirical evidence or formal analysis of how the approach scales to more complex expressions beyond the limited scope presented.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel integration of attribute grammars with machine learning for mathematical identity discovery
- Demonstrated success in discovering previously unknown identities that reduce computational complexity
- Properly situates the work within related literature in theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation
- Provides a framework with potential integration into symbolic computation tools and compilers

Arguments against acceptance:
- Problem scope is overly restricted to homogeneous polynomials with fixed degree and single matrix variable
- Algorithm does not explicitly enforce computational complexity constraints during search
- Paper structure is not sufficiently clear for readers to follow the framework's implementation details
- Limited discussion of how the approach might generalize to more complex mathematical expressions

The paper is interesting and potentially impactful for symbolic computation software libraries, but the limitations in scope and the lack of explicit complexity constraints in the algorithm need to be addressed before it can be considered a strong contribution to the field.
