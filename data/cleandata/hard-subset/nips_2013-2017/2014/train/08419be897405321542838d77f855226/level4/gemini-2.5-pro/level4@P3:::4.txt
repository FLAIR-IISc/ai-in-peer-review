This paper presents a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar and to search over the space of possible expression trees for one that is analytically equivalent to a given target expression but has a lower computational cost. Recognizing that a brute-force or random search is intractable for complex expressions, the authors introduce learning-based search strategies. These strategies, namely an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN), are trained on solutions found for simpler expressions to guide the search for more complex ones. A key contribution is the use of an RNN to learn a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions, allowing them to be classified and compared. The equivalence between expressions is checked either using a numerical descriptor based on evaluation at random points or by using the learned continuous representation.

The work relates to several fields, including automated theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation engines like Mathematica or Maple. However, it distinguishes itself through its central focus on using statistical machine learning to guide the discovery process. Unlike traditional rule-based systems, this framework learns effective search heuristics automatically from data, which is a significant and novel approach in this domain.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The application of modern machine learning techniques, particularly recursive neural networks, to the problem of symbolic identity discovery is highly original. The concept of learning a continuous representation for mathematical expressions is a creative idea that could open up new avenues for applying ML to symbolic domains. The potential impact is substantial; such a system could be integrated into compilers or scientific computing libraries to automatically optimize code, and it could aid researchers in discovering new, efficient formulas for problems in machine learning and beyond (e.g., the RBM partition function mentioned).

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sound. The authors clearly motivate the need for learning-based search and demonstrate its effectiveness empirically. The experiments are well-designed, showing a clear advantage of the n-gram and RNN strategies over a random baseline on several non-trivial families of expressions. The "curriculum learning" approach, where the system solves simpler problems to generate training data for harder ones, is an intelligent way to bootstrap the learning process.

3.  **Reproducibility:** The authors have made their code and evaluation data available, which is a significant strength that allows for verification of their results and encourages future work to build upon their framework.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Clarity and Organization:** While the individual components of the framework are explained, the overall structure of the paper can be difficult to follow. The interplay between the attribute grammar, the numerical and learned representations, the linear combination of trees, and the search strategies is complex. A high-level overview or a diagram illustrating how these components fit together would greatly improve the paper's clarity and make the system easier to understand.

2.  **Scope and Generalizability:** The framework is currently demonstrated on a restricted class of expressions: homogeneous polynomials of a single variable that produce a scalar output. While this is a reasonable starting point, the paper would be stronger with a more thorough discussion of the challenges involved in extending the system to a broader mathematical domain (e.g., expressions with trigonometric functions, recursion, or non-polynomial terms).

3.  **Enforcement of Complexity Reduction:** The problem statement is to find expressions with *lower* computational complexity. However, the search algorithm itself seeks analytical equivalence, while the efficiency gain comes from manually restricting the grammar (e.g., by removing the matrix-matrix multiply rule). This is a valid approach but could be made more explicit. It seems the system is not actively optimizing for complexity but rather searching for any solution within a pre-defined set of efficient operations.

**Detailed Questions and Suggestions**

*   Could the authors clarify how the complexity constraint is handled? Is it possible to incorporate computational complexity as a direct objective in the search strategy, rather than relying solely on restricting the grammar beforehand?

*   Regarding the restrictions mentioned in Section 2, what are the primary obstacles to handling more complex mathematical expressions? Is the scheduler the main bottleneck, or do the representation methods (numerical and learned) pose fundamental challenges for more general functions?

*   In the supplementary material, for the (RBM-1)k family with k=1 and n=1, the discovered identity appears to be `2*A` while the target expression is `sum(v_i * A_i)` which simplifies to `A_1` for n=1 and v=1. Could the authors verify this example?

**Overall Recommendation:**

This is a novel and interesting paper that introduces a promising new direction for applying machine learning to symbolic mathematics. The core ideas are creative and the results demonstrate the potential of the approach. Despite some limitations in scope and clarity, the work represents a significant contribution. I recommend acceptance.
