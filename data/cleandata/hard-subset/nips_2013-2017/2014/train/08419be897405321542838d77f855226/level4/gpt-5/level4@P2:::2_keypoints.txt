- Paper studies automatic discovery of algebraically equivalent but computationally cheaper expressions using an attribute grammar and mod-prime descriptors to test equality.
- Proposes learning-guided search with two policies: subtree n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) that predicts composition rules and embeds expression trees.
- Also supports solving for linear combinations of multiple expression trees to match targets.
- Experiments on several expression families show learned strategies find identities unreachable by random search under a fixed time budget.
- Higher-order n-grams outperform the RNN in most settings, though the RNN can be faster on some instances.
- RNN embeddings cluster expressions into equivalence classes with high accuracy, indicating learned continuous representations capture algebraic structure.
- Positioned between theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, focusing on fixed-degree polynomial-like expressions to avoid undecidability.
- Differs from computer algebra systems by explicitly optimizing for computational cost and learning search heuristics instead of hand-crafted rewrite rules.
- Strength: clear, novel problem formulation and technically interesting components (mod-prime descriptors, linear combos, curriculum training).
- Strength: practical training details, code release, and published discovered identities improve reproducibility.
- Empirical strength: learning-guided search substantially outperforms random search as expression complexity grows.
- Weakness: practical significance not convincingly demonstrated—no end-to-end wall-clock speedups or compiler-integrated use cases shown.
- Weakness: limited scope—homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, single-variable targets, and a restricted operator set; scalability unclear.
- Weakness: evaluation metric (fraction of successful runs within 10 minutes) does not quantify computation savings or runtime reductions versus tuned baselines.
- Weakness: comparisons are limited; missing stronger baselines like cost-aware CAS, evolutionary program search, or informed non-learned grammar search.
- Weakness: claims of “previously undiscovered” identities need formal verification via CAS proofs since mod-prime checks are not formal proofs.
- Weakness: RNN underperforms n-grams; paper lacks analysis of RNN failure modes and exploration of architectural improvements (e.g., attention).
- Clarity: mostly clear but Section 7.2 is dense; recommend restructuring expression-family descriptions, making the cost model explicit, and clarifying the strategy interface.
- Originality: high—learning-guided algebraic search with continuous symbolic embeddings is a novel contribution.
- Significance: currently moderate-to-low until broader applicability, practical speedups, or compiler/autodiff integration are demonstrated.
- Pros for acceptance: novel formulation, demonstrated benefit of learned heuristics, mod-p descriptors, learned embeddings, and reproducible artifacts.
- Cons for acceptance: limited practical utility, narrow problem scope, RNN weaker than simple n-grams, and lack of strong baselines and formal validation.
- Recommendation: borderline reject; paper is original and competent but needs clearer exposition, stronger comparisons, formal proofs, and real-world demonstrations to be compelling.
