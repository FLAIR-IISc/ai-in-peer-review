- Paper proposes using attribute grammars plus learning-based search (n-gram, RNN) to discover computationally efficient equivalent expression trees.
- Novelty: Applying modern ML, especially RNNs and continuous representations, to symbolic identity discovery is original and potentially impactful.
- Empirical strength: Demonstrates discovery of non-trivial, polynomial-time equivalents for expressions that are otherwise exponential, including symmetric polynomials and RBM-related terms.
- Completeness and reproducibility: Presents framework, learning methods, extensive experiments, and publicly releases code and data.
- Clarity/organization: Paper is hard to follow; relationships among grammar, representations, tree combinations, and search need clearer exposition and a high-level diagram.
- Constraint enforcement: Search does not explicitly optimize or guarantee lower computational complexity; some experiments rely on manual grammar restrictions.
- Generalizability: Framework is limited to restricted expression classes (homogeneous polynomials → scalar); discussion of obstacles to extending to more complex functions/data structures is lacking.
- Question: Need formal justification or empirical evidence for the specific problem restrictions in Section 2.
- Question: Clarify whether discovered identities depend on input vector/matrix size; an RBM-1 example in the supplement (k=1, n=1) appears invalid.
- Minor typos to fix: Line 148 "has contains"; Line 289 "is is"; Line 332 "show for"; Line 338 "by be".
