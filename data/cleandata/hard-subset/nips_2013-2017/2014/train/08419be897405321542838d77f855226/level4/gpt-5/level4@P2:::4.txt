Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient identities for symbolic expressions. The authors represent expressions using an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic operations, and search over trees that compose these operations to produce formulas equivalent to a target but with lower computational cost. Because exhaustive search is infeasible, they introduce learned search strategies: (i) an n-gram model over subtrees derived from previously discovered solutions, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions and predicts the next grammar rule to apply.

Two representations are used to test equivalence: a numerical “descriptor” based on evaluating expressions over random inputs modulo a large prime, and a learned RNN embedding trained to classify expressions into equivalence classes. Efficient identities are found either as single trees or linear combinations of trees via solving a linear system modulo a prime. Experiments cover five families of targets (e.g., sums of powers of AA^T, elementwise products with AT, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-inspired sums). Learning-based strategies substantially outperform random search in several families, with n-gram models often surpassing the RNN; the RBM-2 family remains challenging beyond k = 5. The work connects to prior efforts in theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, but differs in using learning to guide search over an attribute grammar. It also relates to recursive models in NLP and learned embeddings.

Strengths:
- Novel application of machine learning to symbolic manipulation: learning search heuristics and continuous representations for algebraic structures.
- Clear, modular framework: grammar, equivalence tests (numeric and learned), and guided search.
- Practical success on expressions beyond brute-force search, with code and data released.
- The use of Zp descriptors is a pragmatic way to avoid floating-point issues and integrate with linear solvers.
- Potential impact on compiler/CAS optimizations and connections to ML objectives (e.g., RBM partition function approximations).

Weaknesses and concerns:
- The objective “lower computational complexity” is not explicitly enforced during search. While some experiments remove matrix–matrix multiply to cap complexity, the strategy does not incorporate complexity-aware constraints or guarantees.
- Scope is narrow: homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, and often a single variable. Generalization to broader operators (trigonometric, recursion) is discussed but not demonstrated.
- Equivalence checking via random evaluation modulo p lacks a formal bound on collision probability or a symbolic verification fallback; the paper does not state guarantees that discovered identities are correct with high probability.
- The scheduler/strategy interface is under-specified for more general grammars (e.g., with recursion or variable-size outputs), and termination criteria are not formalized beyond depth/degree limits.
- Clarity and organization could be improved; the pipeline stages and their dependencies are sometimes hard to follow.
- Some experimental choices (e.g., 10-minute cutoff, success criterion, n-gram weighting) deserve more justification and ablations. The RNN underperforms n-grams in several cases; analysis of failure modes is limited.
- Potential issue in the supplementary RBM-1 example for k = 1, n = 1 (seems invalid as written); size constraints on vectors/matrices are not clearly stated.

Quality:
The methods are technically sound overall, and implementation details for the RNN training are helpful. However, the lack of complexity-aware search constraints and formal equivalence guarantees reduces the strength of the claims about “efficient” identities.

Clarity:
Readable but sometimes difficult to follow. The stages (grammar setup, descriptor construction, class clustering, RNN pretraining, strategy training, search/scheduler, linear solver) would benefit from a concise pipeline figure and a step-by-step algorithm description.

Originality:
High. Applying learned representations and n-gram/RNN strategies to guide search for algebraic identities over an attribute grammar is novel relative to prior work in theorem proving and NLP.

Significance:
Moderate to high potential if extended. Current results are promising within a limited domain; broader grammars and stronger guarantees could make this impactful for CAS/compilers and certain ML models.

Questions for the authors:
- How can complexity be incorporated directly into the strategy (e.g., as a penalty or constraint), beyond removing specific rules? Could the attribute (cost) guide beam search or act as a learned regularizer?
- What is the probability of false positives when using Zp descriptors? Do you validate discovered identities symbolically or with multiple primes?
- Are there constraints on n, m, p or shapes that must hold for all grammar rules? Please clarify the RBM-1 k = 1, n = 1 case.
- How are ties and termination handled in the scheduler for more general grammars (e.g., non-homogeneous, multiple outputs)?
- Why do n-grams outperform the RNN in several families? Did you try pretraining the RNN on a larger unlabeled expression set or using a deeper composition function?

Suggestions:
- Add a formal statement on descriptor collision probabilities and a verification step (e.g., cross-check with a second prime or a symbolic CAS) for identities reported.
- Integrate complexity into the search (e.g., A* with a complexity-aware heuristic, or a multi-objective score combining match likelihood and estimated cost).
- Provide a compact algorithmic overview and a pipeline diagram to improve readability.
- Include ablations for n-gram order, weighting schemes, and RNN architecture choices; report search efficiency (nodes expanded, time to first solution).
- Clarify size/shape assumptions for all operators and fix the potential RBM-1 example issue.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and promising approach to a hard combinatorial problem.
- Demonstrated discovery of nontrivial identities; released code/data.
- Bridges ML and symbolic computation with learned representations.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited scope and lack of complexity-aware constraints or formal guarantees.
- Mixed experimental outcomes (RNN often weaker than simple n-grams) with limited analysis.
- Clarity and presentation issues.

Overall:
An interesting and creative paper with clear potential. I recommend acceptance if the authors address the complexity constraint, equivalence guarantees, and clarity of the pipeline; otherwise, it is a strong borderline.
