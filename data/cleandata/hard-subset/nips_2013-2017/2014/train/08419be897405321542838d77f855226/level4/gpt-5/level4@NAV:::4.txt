Review- This paper presents a new application of neural networks: learning representations of symbolic expressions and discovering their mathematical identities. Although the set of expressions considered is quite limited and there is no evidence that the approach extends to more complex forms, I find the work interesting and potentially impactful for symbolic software libraries.
-The problem statement aims to find expressions with lower computational complexity, yet the algorithm does not appear to enforce this constraint. I recognize this is nontrivial, but have the authors considered ways to restrict the search space to such solutions?
-Regarding the restrictions in Section 2, is the scheduler the only bottleneck for handling more complex mathematical expressions? I would have liked a more formal treatment or at least empirical evidence motivating these constraints.
-The paper’s organization is not easy to follow; the stages would benefit from clearer exposition.
-Are there constraints on vector/matrix dimensions? For example, for k=1 and n=1, the RBM-1 expression in the supplementary material does not seem valid.
Some typos:
332: "show for"
148: "has contains"
289: "is is"
338: "by be"
The paper proposes a novel approach to representing symbolic expressions and identifying their mathematical identities. Even though the method handles only a limited class of expressions, it remains an interesting and original contribution.
