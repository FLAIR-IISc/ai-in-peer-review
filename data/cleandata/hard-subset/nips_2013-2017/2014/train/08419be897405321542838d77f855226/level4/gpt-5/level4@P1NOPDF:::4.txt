This paper introduces a novel use case for neural networks: learning representations for symbolic expressions and discovering mathematical identities. Although the class of expressions studied is very limited and there is no evidence that the approach extends to more complex expressions, I find the paper interesting. It could have meaningful impact on symbolic software libraries.

Major comments:
- The problem statement claims the goal is to find expressions with lower computational complexity, yet the algorithm does not appear to enforce such a constraint. I understand this is nontrivial, but have the authors thought about ways to restrict the search space to solutions with provably lower computational cost (e.g., through regularization, cost-aware scoring, or constrained decoding)?
- Regarding the restrictions in Section 2, is the scheduler the only limiting factor preventing application to more complicated expressions? I would have liked a more formal justification of these restrictions or empirical ablations demonstrating why they are necessary and how sensitive performance is to relaxing them.
- The paper’s structure is not easy to follow. It would help to present the stages of the method more clearly and sequentially, with explicit transitions and a brief summary of the role and inputs/outputs of each stage.
- Are there constraints on the sizes of vectors/matrices used in the expressions? For example, for k=1 and n=1, the expression for RBM-1 in the supplementary material does not seem to be valid. Please clarify the dimensionality assumptions and whether edge cases like k=1, n=1 are supported.

Minor comments and typos:
- Line 148: “has contains” → choose one (“has” or “contains”).
- Line 289: “is is” → “is”.
- Line 332: “show for” → likely “show that” or “show for the case of”.
- Line 338: “by be” → likely “by” or “be”; please correct.
- General proofreading would improve readability.

Overall, this is a novel approach to representing symbolic expressions and discovering their identities. Despite the current limitations in the scope of expressions handled and the absence of guarantees about extensibility to more complex cases, the work is promising and potentially impactful. Strengthening the connection between the stated objective (lower computational complexity) and the actual search procedure, clarifying the necessity and effect of the imposed restrictions, and improving the exposition would significantly enhance the paper.
