- Presents a novel framework that automatically discovers computationally efficient mathematical identities by representing symbolic expressions as trees from an attribute grammar.  
- Brute-force search is intractable, so the authors propose two learned search-guiding methods trained on solutions for simpler expressions.  
- First method: an n-gram model over sub-expressions used to guide search.  
- Second method: a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous vector embeddings of expression trees.  
- Empirical claim: learning-based strategies discover complex, non-obvious identities that reduce computation from exponential/high-order polynomial to lower-order polynomial time.  
- Originality: novel and creative application of ML (especially RNN embeddings) to symbolic identity discovery, bridging symbolic computation, program induction, and ML.  
- Technical quality: sound framework using attribute grammars and clever numerical/learned equivalence checks; experiments show clear advantage over random search.  
- Impressive results: non-trivial identities found that significantly lower computational complexity for several polynomial families.  
- Clarity issue: paper is hard to follow in places (especially experimental section 7.2) and descriptions of the five expression families are dense and need more intuition.  
- Significance/practicality concern: tested families feel contrived and connections to real-world problems or tool integration are speculative; unclear generality.  
- RNN effectiveness concern: simpler n-gram models often outperform the RNN, warranting deeper analysis of when the RNN is advantageous.
