Summary
The paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities by searching over an attribute grammar of symbolic operations. Expressions are represented as trees built from grammar rules; the goal is to find an expression S equivalent to a target T but with lower computational complexity. Because exhaustive enumeration is infeasible, the authors learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets. Two approaches are explored: (i) n-gram models over subtrees that bias the search toward frequently occurring patterns in previous solutions, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous embedding of symbolic expressions and then predicts the next rule to apply. Equality is checked via integer-valued descriptors modulo a large prime, and complex identities are formed as linear combinations of trees by solving a modular linear system. Experiments on several families of expressions (including matrix polynomials and sums over binary vectors) show that learned strategies greatly expand the tractable search space, sometimes yielding O(n^2) or O(n^3) algorithms for targets whose naive evaluation is O(n^3) or exponential. The paper relates to automated theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic algebra systems, and adapts ideas from NLP (n-grams, recursive nets) to symbolic computation.

Strengths
- Original combination of attribute grammars with learned search strategies to discover algebraic identities, with code and data provided.
- Clear demonstration that learned guidance (especially n-grams) can dramatically outperform random search, enabling identities beyond brute-force reach.
- Neat use of modular integer descriptors and linear combinations to verify and assemble solutions without costly symbolic manipulation.
- The learned continuous embeddings of expressions are interesting in their own right and could enable broader ML-for-symbolic tasks.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
Quality
- Section 4.2: The description “weight tensor W3 (of size l×l×l, so that the output is also size l)” suggests you use a neural tensor network rather than a simple linear transformation. If so, please clarify that this is a tensor composition module. If not, please correct the notation and explain how the bilinear/tri-linear interaction is implemented. Bowman showed neural tensor models can outperform vanilla RNNs on logical reasoning; discussing why you chose this architecture (and ablations) would strengthen the paper.
- Section 7.2: It is unclear whether the RNN is still used for classification at identity discovery time, or whether only its embedding is used to score next-rule choices while you evaluate different search strategies. Please make explicit which components are used at training vs. search, and for which families.
- Search proposal generation: The scheduler/strategy interface is underspecified. How are candidate next rules enumerated per partial tree? When a rule requires two inputs, how are branches selected/combined? Are there constraints beyond degree and scalar output that prune invalid or redundant expansions? A brief pseudocode or schematic would help.
- For the n-gram strategy, please clarify the backoff/combination scheme. You state a weighted sum of depths up to n with weights 10^k (k = depth). Justify this choice and report sensitivity if possible.

Clarity
- The problem setting is quite restricted: homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, scalar output, and typically a single matrix/vector variable (though Figure 1 uses two). The constraints are discussed later, but the limitations and how they affect generality should be stated up front. It would help to summarize which families required removing matrix-matrix multiply from the grammar and why.
- Provide a concise, end-to-end example of the search loop, showing: (i) partial tree state; (ii) strategy scores; (iii) rule selection; (iv) completion to degree k; and (v) invocation of the linear solver over accumulated trees.
- Minor confusion in Section 7.2 between “classification” and “search.” If the softmax atop the RNN is repurposed to predict next rules rather than expression classes, please say so explicitly and avoid calling it “classification” in that section.

Originality
- The application of n-grams and RNN embeddings to symbolic expression trees is novel and well-motivated by NLP analogies. The grammar-based search with learned guidance provides a fresh angle relative to rule-based CAS systems.

Significance
- The discovered identities and empirical evidence that learning enables tractable discovery are promising. However, given the current restrictions and the failure to generalize in the most challenging family ((RBM-2)k beyond k > 5), the broader impact will depend on demonstrating extensions to richer grammars and multi-variable targets.

Pros for acceptance
- Innovative and potentially impactful framework bridging ML and symbolic computation.
- Compelling empirical results showing learned search significantly outperforms random.
- Public release of code/data; discoveries include nontrivial reductions in complexity.
- Continuous embeddings of symbolic expressions are likely useful beyond this paper.

Cons for acceptance
- Important ambiguities in the model architecture (tensor vs. linear) and the exact role of the RNN during search.
- The search/scheduler mechanics and proposal generation are underexplained.
- Heavy restrictions on the target class, with limited success on the hardest family.
- Some presentation issues and typos (see below).

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the RNN module: specify whether operations use matrices or tensors; if tensors, relate to neural tensor networks and discuss why this choice was made. Consider reporting an ablation (with/without tensor interactions).
- Provide pseudocode for the scheduler–strategy loop and the formation/solution of the linear system over tree descriptors.
- Explicitly describe how two-input rules are applied when only one branch exists (the current text mentions using a fixed random vector; justify this design and its impact).
- Expand on the n-gram scoring and smoothing/backoff; include a brief sensitivity analysis.
- Add a concise table summarizing which components (RNN embedding, softmax, n-gram) are used at training vs. discovery time for each experiment.
- Discuss limitations and potential collisions of the Zp descriptor, and how N and p are chosen in practice.

Overall evaluation
An intriguing and creative paper with solid empirical evidence that learning can guide combinatorial symbolic search. With clarifications to the model and search procedure and tightened exposition, it would make a strong contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
