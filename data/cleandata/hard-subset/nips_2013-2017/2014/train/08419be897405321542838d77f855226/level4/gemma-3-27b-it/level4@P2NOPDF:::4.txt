This paper presents a novel application of neural networks to the problem of learning representations of symbolic expressions and discovering mathematical identities between them. Despite the current limitations in the complexity of expressions the method can handle, and the lack of a formal guarantee of scalability, I find the work interesting and believe it has the potential to impact symbolic software libraries.

However, several points require clarification and strengthening. The paper motivates the problem by seeking expressions with lower computational complexity, but the proposed algorithm does not explicitly enforce this constraint. While I acknowledge this is a challenging aspect, I would appreciate a discussion of potential approaches to limit the search space towards solutions with desired complexity properties. Have the authors considered methods for incorporating such a constraint into the learning process or search strategy?

Regarding the restrictions outlined in Section 2, it is unclear whether the scheduler is the *sole* limiting factor preventing application to more complex mathematical expressions. Further justification for these restrictions is needed – ideally through more formal analysis or empirical experimentation demonstrating their necessity. A clearer explanation of *why* these particular limitations are in place would be beneficial.

The paper's structure currently hinders clarity. The flow of ideas would be improved by a more explicit and organized presentation of the different stages of the approach. A more detailed breakdown of the methodology would greatly enhance readability and understanding.

Finally, I noted a potential constraint issue regarding vector/matrix sizes. Specifically, the expression for RBM-1 presented in the supplementary material for the case k=1, n=1 appears to be invalid. This warrants investigation and correction. 

I also identified a few minor typos that should be addressed:

*   Page 332: "show for" should be "show" or "shown for"
*   Page 148: "has contains" should be "has" or "contains"
*   Page 289: "is is" should be "is"
*   Page 338: "by be" should be "be"

Overall, this paper presents an intriguing initial step towards leveraging neural networks for symbolic manipulation. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding complexity constraints, justification of restrictions, and structural clarity, would significantly strengthen the manuscript.
