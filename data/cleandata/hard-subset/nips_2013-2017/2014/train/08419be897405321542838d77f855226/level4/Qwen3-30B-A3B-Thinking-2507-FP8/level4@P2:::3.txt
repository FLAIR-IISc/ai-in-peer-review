

This paper presents an interesting framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The authors introduce an attribute grammar-based approach to represent symbolic expressions and propose two learning-based search strategies (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to navigate the exponentially large search space of possible expressions. The framework demonstrates promising results in discovering identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) or better, including examples that appear to be previously undiscovered.

The work relates to several areas: attribute grammars (Knuth, 1968), symbolic computation engines (Maple, Mathematica), and machine learning approaches to symbolic reasoning (Bridge et al., 2013; Bowman, 2013). While previous work has explored grammars for specific domains (e.g., knapsack problems, bioinformatics), the authors make a novel application of grammars to mathematical identity discovery, particularly through the integration of learning methods. The paper also connects to NLP techniques (n-gram models, recursive neural networks) applied to symbolic computation, extending the work of Socher et al. (2013) and Bowman (2013).

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a significant contribution by applying machine learning to a problem that has traditionally been approached with hand-crafted heuristics. The demonstration that learning from simpler expressions can guide the discovery of more complex identities is compelling.
2. The experimental results show clear improvements over random search, particularly for moderate complexity targets (k=2 to k=15). The framework successfully discovers identities that would be challenging to find manually.
3. The paper provides a well-structured framework with clear problem definition and thorough experimental validation across multiple expression families.
4. The learned continuous representations for symbolic expressions (Section 4.2) are particularly interesting and potentially broadly applicable to other symbolic reasoning tasks.

Weaknesses:
1. The problem definition is quite restricted (single matrix/vector variable, homogeneous polynomials, scalar output), which limits the generalizability of the approach. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't sufficiently discuss how the framework could be extended to more general cases (e.g., multiple variables, non-homogeneous expressions), which would strengthen the significance of the work.
2. The technical explanation of the recursive neural network implementation is unclear in several places. Specifically, the description of the weight tensor (W3 of size l×l×l) is confusing—it's unclear whether this represents a neural tensor network or a standard RNN. Bowman (2013) showed neural tensor networks work better than RNNs for logical reasoning, so the authors should clarify whether they're using a tensor network or a standard RNN.
3. The explanation of the search strategy (Section 6) is insufficient. The paper states that "the scheduler receives scores for each rule from the strategy" but doesn't clearly explain how these scores are generated or how the strategy is updated incrementally as more solutions are found for simpler targets.
4. Several grammatical errors and unclear phrases need correction (e.g., "has contains a single matrix," "vector is the presented to the next operation," "the final vector is pass to a softmax classifier").

Quality: The paper is technically sound with appropriate experimental validation. The authors demonstrate that learning-based approaches significantly outperform random search for complex targets. However, the restricted problem setting limits the scope of the contribution. The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of the limitations of the current framework.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, but several technical explanations could be improved for better understanding. The description of the RNN implementation (Section 4.2) requires clarification, particularly regarding the weight tensor. The explanation of the search strategy (Section 6) needs more detail about how the learning approaches guide the search process.

Originality: The paper's core idea—using machine learning to guide the search for mathematical identities within a grammar framework—is novel. While attribute grammars have been applied to other domains (e.g., knapsack problems), the application to mathematical identity discovery with learning-based search strategies appears original. The authors appropriately cite related work in theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation.

Significance: The discovered identities offer concrete computational benefits for specific expression families. The approach could potentially be integrated into compilers or optimization tools to automatically replace inefficient computations with efficient equivalents. However, the restricted problem setting limits the immediate applicability of the results to broader machine learning contexts. The paper could strengthen its significance claim by discussing how the framework might be extended to more general mathematical problems or real-world applications (e.g., the connection to RBM partition functions mentioned in Section 8).

Overall, this is an interesting and technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. While the restricted problem definition limits the immediate applicability, the core idea of using learning to guide symbolic search is promising. With improved explanations of the technical details (particularly the RNN implementation), the paper would be significantly stronger.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem, with clear experimental validation of the proposed methods. The contribution advances the state of the art in applying machine learning to symbolic reasoning tasks. The weaknesses noted are primarily related to clarity and scope rather than technical soundness.
