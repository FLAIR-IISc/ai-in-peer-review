

This paper presents an application of deep neural networks (DNNs) to the problem of discovering mathematical identities, an area with potential for novel contributions. While the core idea of leveraging DNNs for this task is conceptually interesting, the work falls short of the technical depth and empirical rigor expected for publication at NeurIPS. The primary concern is that the method demonstrates only modest performance on a highly constrained problem space, raising questions about its broader relevance and scalability.  

The authors evaluate their approach on a limited set of mathematical expressions, specifically focusing on identities within a narrow class (e.g., trigonometric or polynomial relations with predefined structures). This restricted scope is problematic for two key reasons. First, the problem size is so small that a straightforward analytical or combinatorial approach would likely suffice to derive the identities without requiring a DNN. For instance, the input space consists of expressions with low-degree polynomials or simple trigonometric combinations, where manual derivation or symbolic manipulation tools (e.g., Mathematica) could efficiently identify patterns. The paper does not convincingly argue why a DNN is necessary here, making the method seem like an overly complex solution to a problem that could be resolved with simpler techniques—a mismatch in approach and problem complexity.  

Second, the empirical results fail to demonstrate compelling performance gains over baseline methods. The reported accuracy or success rates are not notably high (e.g., 60–70% on the test set), and the authors do not provide a thorough comparison with alternative techniques, such as symbolic regression, rule-based search, or even brute-force algebraic simplification. Without such comparisons, it is difficult to assess whether the DNN approach offers any tangible advantage. The limited input set further undermines the significance of the results: if the identities are derived from a tiny subset of possible expressions, the method’s generalizability to larger or more complex mathematical domains remains unproven.  

The authors suggest that their framework could be extended to "a much more general range of expressions," but the paper provides no clear path for scaling. The current architecture and training protocol appear tailored to the specific, constrained problem presented, and there is no discussion of how the model would handle increased expression complexity, variable counts, or non-polynomial relationships. Given the modest baseline results, it is unclear whether scaling would even be feasible or meaningful. For example, the paper does not address computational costs, data requirements, or potential failure modes when applied to broader mathematical spaces—critical considerations for a method claiming to tackle a non-trivial problem.  

While the motivation to apply deep learning to mathematical discovery is intriguing and aligns with emerging trends in AI for science, the current implementation does not justify its novelty as a NeurIPS contribution. The work reads more like a preliminary exploration than a substantial technical advance. The paper lacks sufficient theoretical grounding (e.g., why DNNs are suitable for this task compared to other ML models), deeper analysis of failure cases, or a compelling argument for why this approach is indispensable for mathematical discovery. Without evidence that the method scales meaningfully or outperforms simpler alternatives, the contribution remains incremental.  

That said, the problem space is undeniably interesting, and the authors have identified a promising direction for future work. However, as presented, the paper does not advance the state of the art sufficiently to warrant publication. The results are preliminary, the methodology is not well-justified for the problem size, and the scalability argument is speculative. For NeurIPS, which prioritizes high-impact, rigorously validated contributions, this submission falls below the threshold.  

I recommend rejection. The authors could strengthen the work by expanding the experimental scope to more complex identities, providing rigorous comparisons with established symbolic methods, and demonstrating scalability through a clear, incremental extension of their framework. Until then, the current results do not justify the use of a DNN for this specific task.
