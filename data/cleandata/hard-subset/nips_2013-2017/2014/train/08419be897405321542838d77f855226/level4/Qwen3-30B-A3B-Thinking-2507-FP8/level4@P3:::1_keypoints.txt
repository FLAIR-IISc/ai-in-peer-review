- Novel application: integrates attribute grammars with learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities.  
- Framework: expressions represented as trees with computational complexity as an attribute guiding search.  
- Methods: two learning-based search strategies proposed—n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) trained on simpler solutions.  
- Empirical success: demonstrates complexity reductions (e.g., O(n^3)→O(n^2)) on specific homogeneous polynomial families and finds previously undiscovered identities.  
- Connection to NLP: adapts n-grams and RNN parsing/embedding ideas to symbolic mathematical structures.  
- Strength — originality: combining learning with symbolic search and RNN continuous representations is a novel contribution.  
- Strength — validation: rigorous technical details and integer-based validation bolster experimental claims for tested families.  
- Strength — reach: framework can discover identities beyond brute-force search limits for some cases (e.g., k=15).  
- Weakness — limited scope: experiments restricted to homogeneous polynomials, single matrix/vector variables, and scalar outputs.  
- Weakness — generalization failure: RBM-2 family and some cases show RNN does no better than random, questioning scalability.  
- Weakness — marginal benefit: for simple cases n-gram heuristics suffice, so ML’s added value is not convincingly demonstrated.  
- Significance concerns: technical quality is strong but impact is constrained without evidence for broader classes or real-world integration.  
- Clarity/originality caveat: paper is well-written and novel in application, but overstates novelty given prior learning-to-search ideas.  
- Recommended improvements: expand to less-structured problems, compare against non-learning heuristics, and outline how grammars extend to richer operations.  
- Recommendation: promising foundational work but insufficiently general/significant for NIPS; recommend rejection and submission to a more specialized venue.
