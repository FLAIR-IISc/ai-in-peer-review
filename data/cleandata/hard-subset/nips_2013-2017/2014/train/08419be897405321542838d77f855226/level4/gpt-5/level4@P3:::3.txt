Summary
This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient identities that are analytically equivalent to a given target expression. The authors encode symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic operations and search the induced space of expression trees for alternatives of lower computational cost. To address the exponential growth of the search space, they learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets: (i) n-gram models over subtrees, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions and predicts the next grammar rule to apply. Equality is checked numerically via descriptors computed over random integer assignments modulo a large prime, and complex expressions may be formed as linear combinations of trees by solving Fw = t (mod p). Experiments show strong classification accuracy for the learned embeddings and discovery of identities in several families (e.g., reducing O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)) and reducing exponential sums to polynomial-time evaluations in some cases). The approach outperforms random search; n-grams often beat the RNN on repetitive structures.

Relation to prior work
The problem interfaces with theorem proving and program induction, but the authors avoid undecidability by restricting to homogeneous polynomials of bounded degree. Unlike symbolic CAS systems (Maple, Mathematica), which focus on algebraic simplification via hand-coded rules, this work learns heuristics from data. The use of attribute grammars for search connects to prior optimization-by-grammar approaches. The learned representation and search policy draw on NLP methods: n-grams and RNNs over trees (cf. Socher et al., Bowman), here applied to mathematical expressions.

Strengths
- Clear, modular framework: grammar, representations, linear-combination solver, and learned search strategies.
- Novel application of RNNs to learn continuous embeddings of symbolic expressions with high classification accuracy and useful transfer to search.
- Concrete, new identities with substantial complexity reductions, some beyond brute-force enumeration and difficult to derive by hand.
- Code and evaluation data are released, supporting reproducibility.
- The integer-modulo descriptor is a practical and efficient equality oracle in this setting.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Scope restrictions: single variable, homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs; many identities of practical interest involve multiple variables and non-polynomial operators. The paper hints at generalization but does not demonstrate it.
- Correctness guarantees: the equality test relies on random evaluation modulo a prime. A brief discussion using a formal bound (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel) on collision probability as a function of degree, descriptor length N, and prime size p would strengthen the claims.
- Linear combinations: Solving Fw = t (mod p) can produce dense integer coefficients modulo p. How are coefficients mapped back to standard integer/rational coefficients? Are solutions constrained (e.g., sparsity, small integers) to yield interpretable identities?
- Search procedure details: The scheduler/strategy split is appealing, but the proposal mechanism is underspecified. How are partial trees maintained (beam vs. greedy vs. DFS/BFS), how are two-branch rules fed candidates (b1, b2), how is backtracking handled, and what is the branching factor/beam width? Clarify stopping and deduplication.
- RNN architecture: For binary operators you use a third-order tensor W3 (size l×l×l). Is this an NTN-style bilinear composition or a parameter sharing scheme across operators? Do you use separate parameters per grammar rule? How are commutative/associative operations handled (e.g., parameter tying or input order canonicalization)?
- Empirical baselines: Comparison against strong symbolic baselines (e.g., Mathematica FullSimplify/Factor/Collect, Maple simplify/convert) and compiler-style algebraic rewrites/common subexpression elimination would contextualize the gains.
- Evaluation protocol: Counting any nonzero success over 10 runs as a “success” is permissive; the success-rate plots help, but aggregate statistics (median time-to-solution, solutions found per minute, number of trees evaluated) would better quantify efficiency.
- Design choices: Removing matrix-matrix multiply for some families guarantees O(n2) if any solution is found, but also biases the search space. Please justify this choice and discuss whether discovered identities still hold under the full grammar.

Quality
Technically sound overall with a thoughtful integration of grammar-based search and learned heuristics. However, the correctness guarantees for equality testing and the interpretation of modular linear solutions should be formalized. More detail on the search scheduler and RNN design would improve reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, but several key mechanisms (scheduler details, tensor composition, coefficient recovery) are under-explained. The experimental section is clear but could benefit from additional baselines and statistics. Typos listed below.

Originality
High. Learning to guide symbolic identity discovery under an attribute grammar, and using RNN embeddings for expressions, is novel relative to prior NIPS work and CAS practice.

Significance
Potentially impactful. The discovered identities are interesting and the approach could be integrated into compilers/CAS to automatically reduce computational cost. Current scope limitations temper immediate applicability; broader grammars and multi-variable settings would increase impact.

Pros
- Novel and well-motivated problem with clear relevance to ML/AI.
- Effective learned strategies that scale search beyond brute force.
- Strong learned representation for expressions.
- Public code/data.

Cons
- Restricted setting and limited baselines.
- Insufficient formal discussion of equality testing and coefficient recovery.
- Incomplete details on the search procedure and RNN architecture.
- n-gram often outperforms the RNN; the benefit of the more complex model is mixed.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide formal bounds on the probability of false equality under the Zp descriptor (in terms of degree and N), and report chosen p and N.
- Explain how modular solutions w are converted into integer/rational coefficients and whether sparsity or small-magnitude constraints are enforced.
- Detail the scheduler: tree growth policy, beam size, candidate pairing for binary ops, backtracking, and deduplication.
- Clarify parameterization: per-operator matrices/tensors, handling of commutativity/associativity, and whether “identity” initialization extends to third-order tensors.
- Add CAS baselines on representative instances and report time-to-solution and number of trees evaluated.
- Report ablations: RNN without dropout/curriculum, varying l, and effect of pretraining vs. training only the top softmax for search.
- Discuss extending to multiple variables and non-polynomial operators, even if only in a small-scale experiment.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a compelling and well-executed idea—learning to guide symbolic search for efficient identities—with promising results and released code. Addressing the clarity and rigor concerns above would substantially strengthen the contribution.
