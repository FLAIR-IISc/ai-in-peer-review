This paper presents a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities for symbolic expressions. The core contribution is to reframe this symbolic problem as a learning-guided search task. The authors represent mathematical expressions as trees using an attribute grammar. Given the exponential size of the search space, they propose two machine learning models—an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN)—to guide the search for equivalent but more efficient expressions. These models are trained on solutions found for simpler problems to generalize to more complex targets. The paper also introduces a novel application of an RNN to learn a continuous vector representation for symbolic expressions, enabling them to be compared and classified. The method is successfully applied to discover new, non-obvious identities for several families of polynomial expressions relevant to machine learning.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The application of modern machine learning techniques, particularly deep learning models like RNNs, to the classical domain of symbolic mathematics is highly original and thought-provoking. This work opens up a promising new direction for automated reasoning and symbolic computation, moving beyond traditional rule-based systems like Maple or Mathematica.
*   **Practical Potential:** The framework successfully discovers complex and useful identities that are non-trivial to derive by hand. This demonstrates a clear potential for practical impact, for instance, in optimizing numerical software libraries or as a component in next-generation compilers.
*   **Learning from Simple to Complex:** The curriculum-based learning approach, where models are trained on simpler problems to tackle progressively harder ones, is an elegant and effective way to manage the combinatorial complexity of the search space.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

*   **Enforcing the Complexity Constraint:** The problem statement specifies a search for expressions with lower computational complexity. However, the search algorithm itself does not appear to explicitly enforce this constraint. In some experiments, the authors manually remove the expensive matrix-multiply operation from the grammar to ensure any solution found is efficient. Could the authors comment on more general strategies for incorporating computational complexity directly into the search objective or as a constraint, rather than altering the grammar on a case-by-case basis?
*   **Scope and Generalizability:** The paper focuses on a restricted class of expressions (homogeneous polynomials over a single matrix or vector). While this is a reasonable starting point, the paper would be stronger with more discussion on the key challenges to extending this framework. For example, what are the primary obstacles—be it the grammar, the scheduler, or the representation—to handling more complex expressions involving trigonometric functions, recursion, or multiple distinct variables? A more formal justification for the current restrictions would also be helpful.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** While the individual components of the framework are described, the overall structure of the paper can be difficult to follow. A clearer, more sequential explanation of the entire workflow—from defining a target expression to employing the learned strategy to find an identity—would significantly improve readability.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper introduces a novel and interesting approach to a challenging problem in symbolic computation. Despite the current limitations in the scope of expressions it can handle and some issues with clarity, the core ideas are original and significant. The successful application of machine learning to guide symbolic search is a valuable contribution with the potential to inspire future work in this area.
