

This paper proposes a novel approach for discovering efficient algebraic identities using distributed, recursive neural representations. The core contribution—applying neural networks to classify algebraic identities—is conceptually interesting and addresses a non-trivial problem in symbolic computation. However, significant issues with clarity, technical justification, and presentation limit the paper's impact.

The problem scope appears unnecessarily narrow. The authors focus exclusively on identities involving three operands (e.g., associative or distributive laws), yet the broader context of algebraic identity discovery (e.g., for arbitrary expression trees or higher-order operations) is not discussed. Without justification for this restriction, the work’s relevance to wider symbolic reasoning tasks is unclear.

The technical description contains critical ambiguities. The phrase "weight tensor W3 (of size l×l×l, so that the output is also size l)" is confusing. Does this imply a neural tensor network (NTN) layer, or a modified linear layer with tensor parameters? Bowman et al. (2015) demonstrated that NTNs outperform RNNs for similar classification tasks, yet the paper fails to clarify whether the authors adopt an NTN or a standard RNN. This is particularly problematic in Section 7.2 ("Efficient Identity Discovery"), where the authors claim to use "the RNN" for classification while evaluating search strategies. If the model is an NTN, this misattribution undermines the technical narrative. The paper must explicitly state the network architecture and justify the choice relative to established baselines.

The search strategy for identity discovery is poorly explained. How are proposals generated? What constitutes the "space of possible identities" being explored? The description lacks algorithmic detail (e.g., are proposals sampled randomly, via heuristic rules, or via a learned policy?), making it impossible to assess the novelty or efficiency of the search. A concise pseudocode or clearer flowchart would resolve this.

Several minor issues detract from readability:
- "has contains a single matrix" → "contains a single matrix"
- "listed in table Table 1" → "listed in Table 1"
- "vector is the presented to the next operation" → "vector is presented to the next operation"
- "The final vector is pass to a softmax classifier" → "The final vector is passed to a softmax classifier"
- "as to how explore space of possible" → "how the space of possible identities is explored"
- "The scheduler is is fixed" → "The scheduler is fixed"
- "The difficulty of the task by be appreciated by looking" → "The task difficulty is evident from"
- "which can easily captured with" → "which can be easily captured with"
- Figure 3 incorrectly labels the model as "TNN" instead of "RNN."

The paper’s core idea—using neural representations to classify algebraic identities—is promising, but the execution falls short. The unclear architecture, unexplained search mechanism, and narrow scope prevent a strong evaluation of the method’s efficacy. Without addressing these points, the contribution remains speculative rather than demonstrated.

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept. The problem is novel and the neural approach is intriguing, but the paper requires substantial revisions to clarify its technical foundation, scope, and methodology before it can be considered for publication. The authors should: (1) explicitly define the network architecture (RNN vs. NTN) and justify it; (2) provide a detailed description of the identity search procedure; (3) broaden the problem context to justify the restricted scope; and (4) correct all typos and ambiguous phrasing. With these revisions, the paper could make a valuable contribution to symbolic AI.
