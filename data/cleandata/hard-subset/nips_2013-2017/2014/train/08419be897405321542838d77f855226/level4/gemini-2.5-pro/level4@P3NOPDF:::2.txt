This paper addresses the problem of simplifying symbolic expression trees to find more computationally efficient, yet equivalent, representations. The authors propose two distinct, learned-model-based approaches to guide the search for these simpler expressions. The first method employs an n-gram model computed over sequences of sub-expressions to predict promising simplification rules. The second, more novel approach, utilizes a recursive neural network (RNN) to learn continuous vector representations of symbolic expression trees. These embeddings are then used to predict the most probable rule for combining subtrees. The methods are evaluated on three symbolic simplification tasks, and the empirical results, particularly in Figure 3, suggest that the n-gram model outperforms the more complex RNN-based approach on two of the three tasks.

**Quality and Originality:**
The primary strength of this work lies in its originality. The application of machine learning techniques, particularly recursive neural networks, to guide the heuristic search for simplifying symbolic expressions is a novel and interesting research direction. The technical execution of the proposed models appears solid, and the paper introduces a creative way to frame a classic problem from symbolic computation within a modern machine learning context. The quality of the investigation is high, and the proposed methods are non-trivial.

**Clarity:**
Despite the novel ideas, the paper's clarity could be significantly improved. The exposition is, at times, difficult to follow, making it challenging for the reader to fully grasp the intricacies of the proposed models. Section 7.2, which describes the recursive neural network architecture and its application, is particularly dense. This section would benefit from a more detailed, step-by-step explanation of the model's forward pass and how the representations are used for prediction, perhaps supplemented with a clearer diagram or pseudo-code. Improving the clarity of the presentation would make the paper's contributions much more accessible.

**Significance:**
A more fundamental concern is the significance and motivation of the problem as framed. The paper aims to find "computationally efficient" expressions, but the connection to real-world performance gains is not well-established. It is unclear what specific computational settings would benefit from this type of offline, search-based simplification over the highly-optimized, established methods used in compilers or computer algebra systems.

The paper would be much stronger if it could provide a compelling use case or demonstrate a scenario where existing symbolic simplification heuristics fail and these learned methods provide a tangible advantage. For example, are there specific domains of physics or engineering where expressions are notoriously difficult to simplify and where this search-based approach could uncover novel, efficient forms? As it stands, the problem feels somewhat disconnected from a practical need, which limits the potential impact of the proposed solutions. While the methods are technically interesting, their applicability remains in question.

**Additional Comments:**
The observation from Figure 3 that the simpler n-gram model outperforms the more complex RNN model on two of the three tasks is noteworthy and warrants more discussion in the paper. Why might this be the case? Is the RNN overfitting, is the dataset too small to train such a complex model effectively, or is the learned continuous representation not capturing the essential structural properties needed for this task? A deeper analysis of this result would add significant value and insight.
