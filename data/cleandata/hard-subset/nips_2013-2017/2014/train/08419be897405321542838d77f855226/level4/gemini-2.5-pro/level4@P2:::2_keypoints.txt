- Core idea: Use an attribute grammar to represent expression space and search for analytically equivalent, lower-complexity expressions guided by learned heuristics.  
- Methods: Two learning-based search strategies — n-gram models over sub-expressions and an RNN that learns continuous vector representations to predict grammar rules.  
- Novelty: Original application of machine learning, especially deep learning, to discover mathematical identities and learn simplification strategies.  
- Representation: Learning a continuous vector embedding for symbolic expressions is compelling and potentially broadly useful.  
- Technical soundness: Framework and equivalence-checking methods (numerical descriptors and RNN) are well-conceived and implemented.  
- Experiments: Comprehensive evaluation on five non-trivial expression families with transparent reporting, including negative results.  
- Significance: The "learn from simpler problems" paradigm could generalize to other combinatorial AI problems like theorem proving or algorithm discovery.  
- Clarity issues: Description of the five expression families is dense and would benefit from concrete small-k examples.  
- Presentation issue: RNN training procedure is split across sections and should be consolidated into a clear two-stage description (pretrain then fine-tune for rule prediction).  
- Scope limitation: Demonstrated only on homogeneous single-variable polynomials producing scalars, limiting immediate practical applicability.  
- Curriculum dependence: Relies on curricula of related problems (e.g., k → k+1), so performance on standalone arbitrary expressions is unclear.  
- Baseline comparison: Primary baseline is random search; comparison or discussion with traditional search algorithms (e.g., A*, constraint programming) is missing.  
- Suggested improvements: Add concrete examples for each family, consolidate training description, discuss generalization paths, and compare to stronger baselines.  
- Overall verdict: Interesting, technically solid proof-of-concept with high originality and potential impact but limited generality and some clarity and baseline-comparison gaps.
