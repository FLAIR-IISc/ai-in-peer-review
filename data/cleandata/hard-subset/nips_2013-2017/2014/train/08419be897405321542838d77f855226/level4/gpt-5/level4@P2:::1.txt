Summary
The paper proposes using learning to discover computationally efficient identities for symbolic expressions. The authors define an attribute grammar over matrix/vector operations and search over expression trees that are analytically equivalent to a target but cheaper to evaluate. Because exhaustive enumeration is infeasible, they train two strategies to guide search: (i) n-gram models over subtrees and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings for expressions and predicts the next grammar rule. Equivalence is checked numerically using integer descriptors modulo a large prime; more complex identities are formed as linear combinations of trees via modular Gaussian elimination. Experiments on several families—e.g., sums over powers of AAT, elementwise products, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-inspired exponential sums—show that learned strategies can find identities that random search cannot, sometimes reducing complexity from O(n3) to O(n2) or turning exponential-time naive evaluations into polynomial-time computations. The RNN embedding also classifies expressions into equivalence classes with ≤5% error.

Relation to prior work
The work intersects theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, but focuses on polynomial-time symbolic search within a constrained grammar. It contrasts with rule-based CAS systems (Maple/Mathematica) that simplify but do not explicitly optimize for computational cost, and with heuristic selection approaches in automated reasoning. It adapts ideas from NLP, using n-grams and RNNs over trees akin to work by Socher et al., but in a symbolic-math domain.

Strengths
- Novel problem formulation: learning to discover lower-cost identities in a symbolic domain.
- Clean framework combining attribute grammars, numeric equivalence checking (mod prime), and linear combinations of trees.
- Learning-from-simpler-cases paradigm is well-motivated and effective in some families; n-gram models capture recurring local structures.
- The RNN-based representation achieves low classification error on equivalence classes, suggesting meaningful embeddings of symbolic expressions.
- Public code and clear examples (e.g., reducing sum(sum(A*B)) to O(n(m+p)) and patterns for powers of (A*B)).

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Empirical performance is mixed. For the hardest family ((RBM-2)k), learned strategies do not outperform random search beyond k=5; overall, n-gram models often outperform the proposed RNN during search.
- Evaluation lacks baselines against strong symbolic rewrite/optimization systems (e.g., CAS simplification with cost-aware rewrites, compiler-like common subexpression elimination, or specialized identities for symmetric polynomials). Even if those systems don’t target complexity reduction explicitly, a comparison with a simple cost model would contextualize gains.
- The numerical equivalence check is practical, but the probability of descriptor collisions and the choice of N and prime p are not rigorously analyzed; a bound or empirical collision study would strengthen correctness claims. Symbolic verification of final identities would be reassuring.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written, but some implementation details are terse: the exact grammar rules (full table), scheduler policy, and how multi-branch choices are handled would benefit from elaboration. Report the distribution of training examples across degrees and classes used for the RNN.
- Complexity accounting could be clearer (e.g., what operations count as O(n2) vs O(n3), and whether constants are significant in practice).

Originality
- The combination of attribute grammars with learned search and continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions is original. That said, the search strategies themselves (n-gram, softmax-on-embeddings) are relatively simple; stronger search (beam search, MCTS, A* with learned heuristic, or equality-saturation-style exploration) might yield larger gains.

Significance
- Potential impact is intriguing (compiler integration, efficient approximations for ML objectives like RBM partition functions), but current scope is narrow: homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, and mostly single-variable inputs. It remains unclear how well the approach scales to richer operator sets, multiple variables, or non-polynomial functions.
- Claims of discovering “previously undiscovered” identities warrant careful validation (e.g., symbolic proof or literature cross-checks), especially for well-studied families like symmetric polynomials.

Concrete suggestions
- Add baselines with CAS systems plus a simple cost model and/or hand-designed rewrite heuristics; include runtime and success-rate comparisons.
- Use more powerful search (beam width >1; top-k rule expansions; MCTS) combined with the learned scoring function.
- Provide analysis of descriptor collision risks; optionally verify final identities symbolically.
- Report ablations for RNN architecture (tree-LSTM, deeper encoders), training curricula, dropout rates, and beam sizes.
- Expand scope modestly (multi-variable targets) and quantify scaling with grammar size.
- Clarify the complexity model and provide end-to-end wall-clock speedups for representative n.

Pros
- Novel and timely direction: learning-guided search for symbolic optimization.
- Clear framework and publicly available code.
- Demonstrated reductions in complexity on several families; interpretable patterns captured by n-grams.
- Meaningful learned embeddings of symbolic expressions.

Cons
- Limited scope and scalability; hardest cases remain unsolved beyond small k.
- RNN underperforms simpler n-gram models in search; overall gains are modest.
- Missing comparisons to strong symbolic/optimization baselines and limited analysis of correctness guarantees.
- Several engineering choices (scheduler, descriptors) lack rigorous justification.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper presents an interesting idea and a promising framework, but the empirical gains are limited, the scope is narrow, and key comparisons and analyses are missing. With stronger baselines, improved search, broader coverage, and clearer correctness and complexity analyses, this work could become a compelling NIPS submission.
