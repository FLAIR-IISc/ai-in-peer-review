This paper explores the application of machine learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and propose two learning-based search strategies – an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN) – to navigate the exponentially growing space of possible expression trees. The goal is to find expressions analytically equivalent to a target but with lower computational complexity.

The core idea builds upon existing work in symbolic computation and program induction (e.g., [5, 18, 28]), but distinguishes itself by leveraging learning to guide the search process. The use of attribute grammars ([14]) provides a structured representation for symbolic expressions, allowing for systematic exploration, while the n-gram and RNN approaches aim to address the inherent scalability issues of brute-force search. The RNN-based continuous representation of symbolic expressions is a particularly novel contribution, potentially bridging the gap between symbolic and learning-based approaches. Related work in theorem proving [9, 11] and probabilistic programming [12, 20] are acknowledged, but the authors rightly point out the limitations of those approaches in this specific context.  Recent work leveraging learning for reasoning [4] is also appropriately noted.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars with machine learning techniques for automated discovery of mathematical identities is a novel and interesting approach. The application of a recursive neural network to learn continuous representations of symbolic expressions is a particularly strong contribution.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed framework appears technically sound, with a clear problem definition and a well-defined grammar. The experimental evaluation, while limited in scope, demonstrates the effectiveness of the learning-based search strategies in several cases.
* **Empirical Results:** The examples provided (e.g., reducing O(n³) to O(n²)) showcase the potential of the approach to uncover significant computational improvements.  The discovery of previously unknown identities is a noteworthy achievement.
* **Code Availability:** The authors have made their code and evaluation data publicly available ([https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning]), which greatly facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity:** While the overall concept is clear, certain sections (particularly Section 7.2 and the description of the RNN implementation in Section 4.2) are dense and could benefit from clearer explanations and more illustrative examples. The notation could also be more consistent.
* **Significance:** The practical impact of this work is somewhat limited. While discovering efficient identities is valuable, the specific scenarios considered (polynomial simplification) may not be broadly applicable to real-world problems. The motivation for focusing on homogeneous polynomials of a fixed degree feels somewhat arbitrary.
* **Limited Scope:** The experiments are focused on a relatively small set of expression families. It is unclear how well the proposed approach would generalize to more complex mathematical expressions or different domains.
* **RNN Performance:** The n-gram models consistently outperform the RNN in the presented experiments. The reasons for this discrepancy are not fully explored, and the potential of the RNN seems underexploited.  More detailed analysis of the RNN's performance and potential improvements could strengthen the paper.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a challenging problem.
* The technical contributions are solid and the experimental results are encouraging.
* The work has the potential to open up new avenues of research at the intersection of symbolic computation and machine learning.
* The code release promotes reproducibility and further investigation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The practical significance of the work is questionable.
* The clarity of the presentation could be improved.
* The scope of the experiments is limited.
* The RNN performance is underwhelming.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and technically sound contribution to the field. While the practical significance of the work may be limited and the clarity could be improved, the novelty of the approach and the encouraging experimental results warrant serious consideration.  I believe this paper represents a valuable addition to the NIPS conference, particularly for researchers interested in symbolic computation, machine learning, and program induction.

**Recommendation:**  **Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper with the request that the authors address the clarity issues and provide a more thorough discussion of the RNN's performance and potential limitations.  Expanding the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of expression families would also strengthen the paper.
