1. Interesting idea applying DNNs to mathematical identity discovery but lacking technical depth and empirical rigor for NeurIPS.  
2. Evaluated on a highly constrained problem space (low-degree polynomials, simple trigonometric relations).  
3. Problem size is small enough that analytic or symbolic methods could likely solve it, questioning the need for a DNN.  
4. Empirical performance is modest (≈60–70% accuracy), not clearly compelling.  
5. No thorough comparisons with alternative methods (symbolic regression, rule-based search, brute-force algebraic simplification).  
6. Limited input set undermines claims of generalizability to broader or more complex mathematical domains.  
7. Paper offers no clear path or modifications to scale the model to increased expression complexity or variable counts.  
8. Fails to discuss computational costs, data requirements, or potential failure modes when scaling.  
9. Lacks theoretical justification for why DNNs are suitable here and insufficient analysis of failure cases.  
10. Reads as a preliminary exploration and an incremental contribution, not a substantial advance.  
11. Recommendation: reject; authors should expand experiments, compare rigorously with symbolic methods, and demonstrate scalability.
