Paper introduces a framework to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities by representing expressions with an attribute grammar and searching expression trees.

Main contribution is ML-guided search (n-gram over subexpressions and a recursive neural network) to make brute-force search tractable.

RNN learns continuous vector representations for symbolic expressions and achieves high accuracy on an expression classification task.

Framework successfully discovers novel, non-trivial identities (some relevant to ML); authors released code for reproducibility.

Strength: novel and significant application of modern ML (especially recursive/tensor nets) to symbolic mathematics, bridging statistical and formal methods.

Strength: technically sound experiments with clear comparisons against random baselines across challenging expression families.

Strength: paper is generally well-written and well-motivated, with effective examples and introduction.

Weakness: description of the RNN architecture is ambiguous — appears to be a tensor network (RNTN) and should be explicitly clarified and justified with citations.

Weakness: mechanics of the search process are unclear — the interplay between the scheduler and strategy needs a concrete step-by-step example and definition of returned scores.

Weakness: connection between representation learning (classification pretraining) and the search policy is not fully explained; need to clarify how φ(S) is reused and how the new softmax is trained.

Weakness: problem scope is restricted to single-variable homogeneous polynomials with scalar outputs; paper should discuss challenges in generalizing beyond this restricted setting.

Arguments for acceptance: original approach, demonstrated discovery of useful identities, and valuable learned continuous representations for symbolic expressions.

Argument against / concern: insufficient methodological detail (exact RNN architecture and search mechanics) affecting reproducibility.

Recommendation: accept with revisions to clarify the RNN architecture, search algorithm, and the linkage between representation pretraining and the search policy.

Minor typos and phrasing errors noted throughout (e.g., "has contains a single matrix" → "contains a single matrix", other similar fixes).
