- Summary: Proposes a framework that represents expressions with an attribute grammar and uses learned models to guide search for computationally efficient mathematical identities.
- Methods: Introduces n-gram and recursive neural network (RNN) strategies trained on simpler problems to guide search over expression trees.
- Results: Finds non-trivial, sometimes novel identities (e.g., for elementary symmetric polynomials and RBM partition terms) that yield large complexity speedups.
- Strength — Originality: Novel application of learning-based search to discover mathematical identities with potential impact on compilers and scientific code.
- Strength — Learned representation: Uses an RNN to produce continuous embeddings of symbolic expressions, showing high classification accuracy and broad interest.
- Strength — Empirical validation: Learning-guided searches (n-gram and RNN) outperform random baselines and scale to problems with huge search spaces.
- Weakness — RNN clarity: The RNN/composition function is ambiguously described (tensor vs. standard composition); architecture choice and comparisons to prior models need clarification.
- Weakness — Search process clarity: Interaction between scheduler and strategy, proposal generation, and scoring lacks formal description or pseudocode, hurting reproducibility.
- Weakness — Using RNN in search: The transition from classifier training to using the RNN for next-rule prediction is unclear (how the additional softmax is trained and on what data).
- Weakness — Limited scope: Problem restricted to homogeneous single-variable scalar polynomials; paper should discuss extending to multiple variables, non-polynomial functions, and vector/matrix outputs.
- Weakness — Result analysis: Strong n-gram performance over RNNs needs deeper analysis—whether due to repetitive local structure or RNN training/architecture limits.
- Minor issues: Multiple typographical and grammatical errors throughout the paper that should be corrected.
- Conclusion: Overall a strong, creative paper with significant contributions and addressable clarity/scope issues; reviewer recommends acceptance.
