This paper explores the application of machine learning techniques, specifically deep neural networks, to the discovery of efficient mathematical identities – a novel area of research. While the core idea is interesting, my assessment is that the current work represents preliminary exploration and may not yet meet the high standards for publication at NIPS. 

The primary concern revolves around the demonstrated performance. The identities discovered appear within a constrained set of expressions, suggesting the problem may be overly simplified for the complexity of the proposed methods. It feels akin to employing a computationally intensive approach for tasks readily solvable through direct mathematical reasoning. While the authors suggest potential scalability to more general expressions, the current results do not convincingly demonstrate that such scaling is feasible or beneficial.

The paper introduces a framework based on attribute grammars, combined with n-gram models and a recursive neural network, to search for computationally less complex, yet equivalent, expressions. The experimental evaluation focuses on a few families of expressions – polynomial expansions and RBM-related terms – and shows performance gains in reducing computational complexity. However, the benefits are not substantial enough to warrant a NIPS publication at this stage. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of machine learning to the automated discovery of mathematical identities is a genuinely new direction.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed framework, based on attribute grammars and machine learning, appears technically sound, with a clear problem definition and methodology. The use of integer-based descriptors to address numerical issues is a clever detail.
*   **Representation Learning:** The exploration of a learned continuous representation for symbolic expressions using a recursive neural network is a potentially valuable contribution, opening the door to applying broader machine learning techniques to symbolic computation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Impact:** The discovered identities, while correct, are not dramatically more efficient than existing approaches or readily discoverable through standard mathematical manipulation.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** It’s unclear how well the current approach would scale to more complex and less structured mathematical expressions. The complexity of the search space could quickly become prohibitive.
*   **Insufficient Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation lacks comparison to established symbolic computation systems (e.g., Mathematica, Maple) and alternative search strategies. The cut-off time for search could also be a limiting factor.
*   **Clarity:** The description of some experimental details (e.g., the weighting scheme for n-grams, the rationale behind specific grammar rule removal) could be more detailed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a genuinely novel problem with a well-defined approach.
*   The exploration of a learned representation for symbolic expressions is a potentially significant contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The demonstrated results are not sufficiently impactful to justify publication at NIPS.
*   The scalability of the approach remains unproven.
*   The evaluation lacks thorough comparison to existing methods.

**Related Work:** The paper adequately cites relevant work in theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, as well as related techniques in natural language processing. However, a more detailed discussion of existing symbolic computation engines and their limitations would be beneficial.

**Overall:**

This paper presents an interesting exploration of a novel research direction. However, the current results do not demonstrate sufficient impact or scalability to warrant acceptance to NIPS. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to continue developing this line of research and submit a revised version with more compelling results and a more thorough evaluation. Further work on addressing scalability concerns and demonstrating the benefits on more complex mathematical problems would be crucial.
