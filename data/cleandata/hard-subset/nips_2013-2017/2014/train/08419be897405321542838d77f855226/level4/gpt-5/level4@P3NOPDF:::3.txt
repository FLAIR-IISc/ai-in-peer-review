Summary
The paper proposes a learning-based approach to discover more efficient algebraic identities, using distributed, recursive neural representations to classify equivalences and guide search. The idea is interesting and timely: combining neural composition with symbolic algebra could yield practical efficiency gains and offers a novel angle on program optimization.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: finding shorter/faster algebraic expressions is broadly useful.
- The use of recursive neural models to encode expressions is compelling and, empirically, seems to classify identities with good accuracy.
- The paper begins to separate two key components—classification (equivalence prediction) and search (identity discovery)—and explores different search strategies.

Weaknesses and clarifications needed
- Problem scope: The current formulation appears quite restricted (e.g., operator set, expression depth/arity, allowable rewrites). Please define precisely:
  - The language of expressions (operators, constants, variables), any type system (scalars, vectors, matrices), and constraints (associativity/commutativity allowed?).
  - The cost model defining “efficiency” (operation count, FLOPs, tree size, latency proxy) and how ties are broken.
  - How ground truth equivalence is established (symbolic checker, random testing, formal derivation).
- Model specification: The description “weight tensor W3 (of size l × l × l, so that the output is also size l)” is unclear.
  - Are you using a neural tensor network (NTN/RNTN) layer as in Socher et al., with a bilinear tensor contraction plus affine term? If so, please provide the exact composition equation (e.g., o_k = sum_{i,j} W3[k,i,j] h_i h_j + W2 h + b) and activation.
  - If not an NTN, explain how a 3rd-order tensor yields an l-dimensional output from inputs of what shape. Also describe parameter sharing across tree nodes.
  - Please specify initialization, regularization (e.g., weight decay, dropout), and gradient handling (e.g., clipping).
- Relation to prior work: Bowman et al. report strong gains for tensor networks over standard RNNs on compositional semantics. Given your task is also compositional, please justify the chosen architecture and, if feasible, include a direct comparison (RNN vs NTN/RNTN) or an ablation to isolate what drives performance.
- Section 7.2 (Efficient Identity Discovery):
  - It is unclear whether the classifier used in the search loop is the same RNN/NTN trained earlier or a different model. Please state the architecture used during search, how scores are computed, and whether the model is updated online.
  - Proposal generation needs a precise description: What are the proposal operators (e.g., local rewrites, subtree replacements, random expansions)? How are they sampled? Are proposals constrained to preserve type/dimensions? Is there a learned proposal policy?
  - Search strategy details: beam width, rollout depth, pruning criteria, duplicate detection, and termination conditions. Do you enforce equivalence during search (via a verifier) or rely on the classifier’s scores? How do you prevent cycling?
  - Evaluation: Report success rate, solution cost, and search effort (time/nodes explored). Include baselines (random search, greedy with heuristic costs, symbolic simplifiers) and ablate the classifier to show its contribution.
- Generalization and robustness:
  - Train/test split: How do you avoid template leakage (e.g., near-duplicate trees across splits)? Please report generalization to longer or structurally different expressions than those seen in training.
  - Sensitivity to noise in equivalence labels, and to distribution shift (new operators or operand dimensions).
  - Reproducibility: provide dataset generation details, hyperparameters, and code or pseudocode for the full pipeline.
- Significance: The approach is promising, but the current experiments do not yet demonstrate end-to-end utility (e.g., measurable FLOP reductions on a representative benchmark). Consider including a case study showing practical wins.

Specific questions for the authors
- Does W3 implement a full bilinear tensor contraction as in RNTN, and if so, is there also a linear term W2 and bias? If not, please clarify the contraction and its input dimensionalities.
- In Section 7.2, is the same trained classifier used to score nodes during search? Are scores calibrated (e.g., temperature-scaled) to be comparable across depths?
- How are proposals guaranteed to be well-typed (especially for matrix/tensor operators)? Do you maintain shape annotations at nodes?
- What is your ground-truth equivalence oracle? Exact algebraic reasoning, randomized testing, or both? If randomized, what are the failure rates?
- How do you define and measure “efficiency,” and does the metric correlate with real runtime on hardware?

Overall assessment
Interesting problem and a creative use of recursive neural representations. The classification results suggest real promise. However, the method section needs clearer specification (especially the tensor layer), and the search procedure requires a more rigorous and transparent description with stronger baselines and ablations. With these clarifications and expanded experiments—particularly a clear comparison between RNN and tensor variants and a well-documented search pipeline—the paper would be significantly strengthened. As it stands, I view this as borderline, leaning toward accept if the authors can address the methodological clarity and evaluation gaps.
