This paper presents a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar and to search over the space of possible expression trees for one that is analytically equivalent to a given target expression but has lower computational complexity. Recognizing that the search space grows exponentially, the authors propose using machine learning techniques—specifically n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN)—to guide the search. These models are trained on solutions found for simpler expressions to learn heuristics that can accelerate the discovery of more complex identities.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this work lies in its ambitious and creative goal. Applying modern machine learning techniques, particularly deep learning, to the domain of symbolic mathematics is a highly novel research direction. The problem of discovering efficient identities is fundamental and has the potential for significant impact in areas ranging from compiler optimization to theoretical machine learning (e.g., finding efficient ways to compute partition functions). This is a genuinely new application area for techniques like RNNs.

2.  **Novel Technical Contribution:** The proposal to learn a continuous, vector-space representation for symbolic expressions using an RNN is a significant contribution in its own right. This approach, analogous to word embeddings in natural language processing, could open the door for various gradient-based learning methods to be applied to symbolic computation, a domain traditionally governed by discrete, rule-based systems. The reported classification accuracy for identifying equivalent expressions suggests this is a promising representation.

3.  **Well-Designed Framework:** The overall framework, combining an attribute grammar for generation with a learning-based search strategy, is elegant and well-motivated. The authors clearly explain the components, from the numerical representation for checking equivalence to the training procedure that uses a curriculum learning approach (solving simpler problems first). The inclusion and comparison of multiple strategies (random, n-gram, RNN) is a mark of good scientific practice.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The main weakness is that the experimental validation is confined to a small number of highly structured families of expressions (homogeneous polynomials of a single variable). For some of these families, the discovered efficient identities exhibit simple, repetitive patterns (as shown in Example 2). This raises the concern that the problem, as formulated in the experiments, might not be complex enough to justify the sophisticated learning machinery proposed. A simpler, pattern-matching heuristic might have been sufficient for these specific cases. The work would be much more compelling if it demonstrated success on a more diverse set of problems, perhaps involving multiple variables, non-homogeneous polynomials, or different mathematical domains.

2.  **Unclear Scalability and Generalization:** While the learning-based approaches outperform the random baseline, the performance gains are not always dramatic, especially for the more complex RNN model which is often outperformed by simpler n-gram models. On the most challenging problem family (RBM-2), the learning methods failed to find solutions beyond k=5, performing no better than the random strategy. This calls into question how well these learned heuristics scale as the problem complexity increases. The paper speculates about extending the framework to include trigonometric functions or recursion, but it does not address the substantial technical challenges this would introduce (e.g., managing a much larger search space, ensuring termination, and validating equivalence for non-polynomial expressions).

3.  **Performance of the RNN Model:** The RNN is presented as a key contribution for learning a rich representation of expressions. However, in the identity discovery task, it is frequently outperformed by the much simpler 3-gram model. This suggests that for the problems tested, capturing local, shallow structure was more important than learning a deep, compositional representation. While the RNN's performance on the expression classification task is impressive, its utility in the main search task is less clear from the current results. It would be helpful to analyze why the RNN struggles relative to n-grams and to explore architectures or training procedures that might better leverage its representational power for search.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper introduces a fascinating and highly original approach to a challenging problem in symbolic mathematics. The idea of learning to guide a search through the space of mathematical expressions is ambitious and potentially impactful. The concept of a continuous embedding for symbolic formulas is a valuable contribution.

However, the current work feels preliminary. The empirical results, while demonstrating a proof of concept, are not strong enough to fully support the claims of the method's effectiveness. The limited and structured nature of the test problems makes it difficult to assess whether the proposed learning framework provides a general solution or simply excels at identifying the repetitive patterns present in the chosen examples. The fact that simpler n-gram models often outperform the more sophisticated RNN also weakens the argument for the necessity of such a complex model.

Overall, I find this to be a thought-provoking and creative paper, but its empirical foundations are not yet solid enough for a NIPS publication. The work shows significant promise, but it would require a more thorough and challenging experimental evaluation to be convincing.

**Recommendation:** Borderline Reject.
