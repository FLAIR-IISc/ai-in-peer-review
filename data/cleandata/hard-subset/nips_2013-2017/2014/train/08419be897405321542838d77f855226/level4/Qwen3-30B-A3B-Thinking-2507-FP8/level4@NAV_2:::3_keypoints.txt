- Novel framework combining attribute grammars with machine learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities.
- Grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions with two learning-guided search methods: n-gram models and recursive neural networks.
- Builds on compiler attribute grammars and applies NLP-inspired models (n-grams, RNNs) to symbolic computation.
- Demonstrated effectiveness in case studies, including converting exponential-time matrix expressions to polynomial-time algorithms.
- Clear problem motivation and practical demonstration of discovering previously unknown identities (supplementary material).
- Well-executed experiments showing learning-based approaches significantly outperform random search on complex expressions (Section 7.2).
- Learned representations for symbolic expressions could enable broader machine-learning applications in symbolic computation.
- Problem definition is overly restrictive (homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with a single matrix variable), limiting applicability.
- Description of the recursive neural network is unclear—use of a size l×l×l weight tensor suggests a tensor network, contradictory to the RNN claim; needs clarification and justification (e.g., vs. Bowman).
- Search strategy exposition is insufficient: unclear how the RNN generates next-rule proposals, how the scheduler/strategy operate, and how vectors are transformed between operations.
- The transition from RNN classification to using the representation for identity discovery (Section 6.3) is underexplained.
- Paper contains multiple minor errors/typos and a figure label mistake (Figure 3 should reference RNN, not TNN).
- Overall: promising and valuable contribution but requires clearer technical details and reconsideration/justification of the restrictive problem setup.
