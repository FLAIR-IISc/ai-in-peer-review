The paper introduces a novel approach for representing symbolic expressions and finding their mathematical identities using neural networks. Although the expressions studied in this work are limited, I find the paper interesting and believe it could have a good impact on symbolic software libraries.

One of the primary concerns with the paper is that the problem statement emphasizes the need for expressions with lower computational complexity. However, this constraint is not explicitly enforced in the proposed algorithm. I understand that incorporating this constraint is not trivial, but I would have liked the authors to discuss possible solutions for limiting the search space to such solutions. This discussion would strengthen the paper by providing a clearer direction for future work.

Another point of concern is the restrictions mentioned in Section 2. The authors attribute these restrictions primarily to the scheduler. However, I would have liked to see a more formal analysis or empirical experiments to justify these restrictions. It is unclear whether the scheduler is the only limiting factor for handling more complicated mathematical expressions. A more thorough discussion of these restrictions would provide valuable insights into the applicability of the proposed approach.

The structure of the paper could be improved for better clarity. The stages of the proposed method are not clearly explained, making it difficult to follow the authors' reasoning. Breaking down the method into more distinct stages and providing a clearer explanation of each stage would enhance the overall readability of the paper.

Additionally, I would like to point out a few technical concerns. Is there a constraint on the size of the vector/matrix used in the algorithm? For instance, the expression for RBM-1 in the supplementary material appears to be invalid for k=1 and n=1. Clarifying this point would help to avoid potential confusion.

Finally, there are a few typos that need to be corrected: "show for" (page 332), "has contains" (page 148), "is is" (page 289), and "by be" (page 338).

Overall, the paper presents an interesting approach to learning representations for symbolic expressions and finding their mathematical identities. Addressing the concerns mentioned above would strengthen the paper and provide a clearer direction for future research in this area.
