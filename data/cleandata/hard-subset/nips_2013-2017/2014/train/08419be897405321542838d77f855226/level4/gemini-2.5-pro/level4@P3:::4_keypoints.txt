- Presents a framework to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities by representing expressions with an attribute grammar and searching for analytically equivalent, lower-cost expression trees.

- Introduces learning-based search strategies (n-gram model and recursive neural network) trained on solutions to simpler expressions to guide search for harder ones.

- Key contribution: using an RNN to learn continuous vector representations of symbolic expressions for classification and comparison.

- Expression equivalence is checked via numerical evaluation at random points or via the learned continuous representation.

- Distinguishes itself from automated theorem proving and symbolic engines by using statistical ML to learn search heuristics rather than relying on rule-based systems.

- Strength — Originality/Significance: novel application of RNNs to symbolic identity discovery with potential impact for compilers and scientific computing.

- Strength — Technical Quality: sound framework, well-designed experiments showing n-gram and RNN outperform random baseline; uses curriculum learning effectively.

- Strength — Reproducibility: authors provide code and evaluation data.

- Weakness — Clarity/Organization: paper’s overall structure and interactions between components are hard to follow; a high-level diagram is needed.

- Weakness — Scope/Generalizability: experiments restricted to homogeneous single-variable polynomials; needs discussion on extending to broader mathematical domains.

- Weakness — Complexity Enforcement: system searches for equivalence but does not directly optimize computational complexity, relying on manual grammar restrictions for efficiency gains.

- Question: Clarify whether computational complexity can be incorporated as a direct objective in the search rather than via grammar restrictions.

- Question: Explain main obstacles to handling more complex expressions—scheduler vs numerical/learned representations.

- Request: Verify a supplementary RBM-1 example where discovered identity seems inconsistent for k=1, n=1.

- Minor typos: Line 148 "has contains"→"contains"; Line 289 "is is"→"is"; Line 332 "show for"→"shown for"; Line 338 "by be The paper"→"by the paper".

- Overall recommendation: Novel, promising work with significant contribution; recommend acceptance despite scope and clarity issues.
