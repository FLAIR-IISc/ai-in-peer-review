- Summary: Proposes neural-guided discovery of efficient algebraic identities using recursive/distributed representations to classify equivalences and guide search.

- Strength: Clear, broadly useful motivation for finding shorter/faster algebraic expressions.

- Strength: Recursive neural models appear effective at encoding expressions and classifying identities with good empirical accuracy.

- Strength: Paper separates classification (equivalence prediction) from search (identity discovery) and explores multiple search strategies.

- Weakness: Problem scope is underspecified—need precise language of expressions (operators, constants, variables, type system, associativity/commutativity rules).

- Weakness: Cost model is unclear—define “efficiency” (operation count, FLOPs, tree size, latency proxy) and tie-breaking rules.

- Weakness: Ground-truth equivalence method is unspecified—clarify symbolic checker vs randomized testing vs formal proof.

- Weakness: Model/tensor layer is poorly specified—clarify whether W3 implements an NTN/RNTN bilinear contraction, the exact composition equation, input/output shapes, and parameter sharing across tree nodes.

- Weakness: Missing training details—specify initialization, regularization (weight decay/dropout), gradient handling (clipping), and other hyperparameters.

- Weakness: Relation to prior work is weak—justify architecture choice vs RNN/NTN literature and include comparisons or ablations (RNN vs tensor).

- Weakness: Search-loop clarity lacking—state whether the same trained classifier is used during search, how scores are computed, and whether the model is updated online.

- Weakness: Proposal generation is underspecified—define proposal operators, sampling method, type/dimension constraints, and whether a learned proposal policy exists.

- Weakness: Search strategy details missing—report beam width, rollout depth, pruning, duplicate detection, termination, verifier use vs classifier reliance, and cycle prevention.

- Weakness: Evaluation is incomplete—report success rates, solution costs, search effort, include baselines (random/greedy/symbolic), and ablations showing classifier contribution.

- Weakness: Generalization and robustness not addressed—avoid template leakage in splits, test on longer/structurally different expressions, and analyze sensitivity to label noise and distribution shift.

- Weakness: Reproducibility needs work—provide dataset generation, hyperparameters, code or pseudocode for the pipeline.

- Weakness/Significance: Experiments do not show end-to-end utility; include a case study demonstrating measurable FLOP/runtime reductions on representative benchmarks.

- Specific question: Does W3 implement a full bilinear tensor contraction and is there also a linear term W2 and bias?

- Specific question: Is the same trained classifier used to score nodes during search and are scores calibrated across depths?

- Specific question: How are proposals kept well-typed (shape annotations/typing guarantees for matrix/tensor ops)?

- Specific question: What is the ground-truth equivalence oracle and if randomized testing is used, what are its failure rates?

- Specific question: How is “efficiency” defined and how well does it correlate with actual runtime on hardware?

- Minor issues/typos: Multiple grammatical fixes and Figure 3 label correction (e.g., “has contains a single matrix” → “contains a single matrix”; “TNN” → “RNN” if appropriate).

- Overall assessment: Interesting and promising approach, but needs clearer method specification (tensor layer), a rigorous, transparent search pipeline, stronger baselines/ablations, and practical evaluation; borderline paper leaning toward accept if addressed.
