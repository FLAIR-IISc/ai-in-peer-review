- Proposes a framework to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities by representing symbolic expressions with an attribute grammar and searching for lower-complexity equivalents.
- Uses learned models (n-gram and a recursive neural network) to guide an otherwise intractable search over expression trees.
- Trains models on solutions to simpler identities so they can generalize to discover complex identities beyond exhaustive search.
- Connects program induction and formal grammars with modern ML techniques, drawing parallels to RNNs and learned embeddings in NLP.
- Original and significant: applying deep learning to symbolic identity discovery is novel and yields previously unknown, useful formulas with practical implications.
- Learned representation: the RNN produces a continuous vector embedding for symbolic expressions with high classification accuracy, enabling ML techniques in symbolic domains.
- Technical quality: combines grammar-based search, modular-arithmetic-based equivalence checking, and learning-guided search in a well-designed, effective framework.
- Experiments are thorough, comparing learning-based strategies to random baselines across challenging expression families.
- Clarity issues: the proposal-generation mechanism and the scheduler–strategy interaction are not fully detailed, hindering reproducibility.
- Architectural ambiguity: the described RNN (mention of an l×l×l weight tensor) resembles an RNTN—paper should clarify the exact composition function and relate to prior tensor models.
- Usage unclear: it is not explicitly stated how the pre-trained RNN representation is used during identity discovery (e.g., whether the original C-way head is removed).
- Scope limitations: problem restricted to homogeneous single-variable scalar polynomials; paper lacks detailed discussion on extending to multi-variable, non-polynomial, or non-scalar cases.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a novel cross-disciplinary problem, discovers identities beyond brute force, introduces a broadly useful continuous symbolic representation, and provides strong experimental support.
- Arguments against acceptance: incomplete implementation details may impede reproducibility, and the current problem scope is narrow with limited generalization discussion.
- Minor issues and typos: inconsistent terminology for the recursive model and several grammatical/typographical errors throughout (specific line edits suggested).
