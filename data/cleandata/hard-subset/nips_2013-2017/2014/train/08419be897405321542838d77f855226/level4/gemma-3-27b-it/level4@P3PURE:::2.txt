This paper presents two methods for discovering simpler symbolic expression trees. The first approach leverages n-grams computed over sequences of sub-expressions, while the second employs a recursive neural network to generate continuous space representations of these trees. These learned representations are subsequently used to predict the most likely rule for combining subtrees. Based on the results presented in Figure 3, the n-gram method appears to outperform the RNN approach on two of the three evaluated tasks.

Regarding the specific review criteria:

Quality – High.
Clarity – The paper suffers from a lack of clarity, particularly in Section 7.2, which makes it difficult to follow the details of the methodology.
Originality – High.
Significance – Low. The problem addressed feels somewhat contrived, and it’s unclear if the efficient computation of symbolic expression trees, as explored in this work, would be relevant to practical applications. Consequently, the significance of the proposed methods is questionable. It is unlikely that researchers seeking efficient computations in general would pursue this specific line of investigation.
