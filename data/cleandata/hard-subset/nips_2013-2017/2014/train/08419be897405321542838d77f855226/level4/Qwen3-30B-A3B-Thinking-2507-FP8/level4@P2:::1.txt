

This paper introduces an attribute grammar framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities, using machine learning to guide tree search beyond brute-force limits. The authors apply n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) trained on simpler expressions to discover identities like O(n³) → O(n²) matrix computations (e.g., sum(sum(A*B)) → sum((sum(A,1)*B)',1)), with claims of novel identities for specific polynomial families.  

**Quality**: The technical approach is sound, with clear experimental design (Section 7.2). However, the claimed efficiency gains are limited to highly constrained cases (e.g., fixed-degree homogeneous polynomials with single variables). The RNN’s 5% error rate (Table 2) is promising, but the framework’s efficacy is unconvincing for broader applications: the most challenging family (RBM-2) yields no solutions for k > 5, and the RNN only slightly outperforms random search (438s vs. 100s for k=5). Crucially, the paper does not rigorously compare against simpler combinatorial approaches (e.g., pattern-based rule extraction for symmetric polynomials), undermining the "sledgehammer" argument.  

**Clarity**: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with effective examples (Examples 1–2) and thorough notation. The grammar definition (Section 3) and numerical descriptor (Section 4.1) are particularly well-explained. Minor issues include the abrupt shift to "integer-based descriptor" without motivation and the supplementary material’s reliance on readers to verify identities.  

**Originality**: The core contribution—applying n-grams/RNNs to symbolic identity discovery—is derivative. Attribute grammars (Knuth, 1968) and their use in search (e.g., [7, 19]) are well-established, and the NLP-inspired RNN adaptation (Socher et al., 2013) is straightforward. The novelty is overstated: the framework’s "novelty" stems from *applying* existing techniques to a new domain, not from the techniques themselves. The claim of "previously undiscovered identities" lacks evidence (e.g., no comparison to Mathematica/Maple’s simplification capabilities).  

**Significance**: The significance is weak. The restricted scope (single-matrix homogeneous polynomials, fixed k) limits applicability. While the RBM/dropout connections (Section 8) are intriguing, they remain speculative—no implementation or empirical validation is provided. The paper advances the state of the art only in the narrow context of its constrained grammar, not in the broader goal of symbolic computation.  

**Strengths**:  
- Clean integration of attribute grammars with ML for search guidance.  
- Effective demonstration of continuous representations for symbolic expressions (Section 4.2).  
- Well-executed experiments on manageable cases (e.g., matrix identities in Examples 1–2).  

**Weaknesses**:  
- Overstated novelty: Leverages existing NLP/grammar techniques without sufficient innovation.  
- Limited scope: Results confined to trivially solvable cases; no evidence of scalability.  
- Underwhelming performance: RNNs only marginally outperform random search (Fig. 3), and key families (RBM-2) fail entirely.  
- Significance unsupported: Speculative applications (RBM/dropout) lack concrete validation.  

**Conclusion**: While the framework is technically competent and the problem is interesting, the paper fails to demonstrate a meaningful advance over prior work. The claimed "novel identities" are not rigorously verified as previously unknown, and the approach’s limited scope and marginal gains do not justify its publication at NIPS. The work is better suited for a systems or application-focused venue (e.g., ICML for the ML techniques, or a symbolic computation workshop). It does not meet NIPS’ bar for significant, broadly impactful contributions to machine learning.  

**Recommendation**: Reject. The paper’s incremental nature and unconvincing results do not align with NIPS’ emphasis on transformative contributions.
