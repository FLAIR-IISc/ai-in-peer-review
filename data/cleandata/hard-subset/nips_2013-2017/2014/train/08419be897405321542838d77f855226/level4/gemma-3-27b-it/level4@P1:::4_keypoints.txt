- Novel application: uses machine learning (recursive neural nets) to learn representations of symbolic expressions and discover mathematical identities.
- Limited scope: experiments restricted to a specific class of polynomials and assume a single matrix/vector variable.
- Scalability concern: no proof or demonstration that the approach scales to more complex expressions.
- Potential impact: could benefit symbolic software libraries by finding more efficient expressions.
- Complexity objective mismatch: problem targets lower computational complexity but the algorithm does not explicitly enforce or optimize for it during search.
- Request for methods: authors should discuss ways to constrain the search to solutions with provably lower complexity.
- Section 2 restrictions: unclear whether the scheduler is the main limiting factor; needs formal justification or empirical analysis.
- Presentation clarity: paper structure and progression of ideas are hard to follow; a clearer breakdown of stages is needed.
- Validity question: possible constraint on vector/matrix sizes—RBM-1 expression in supplement may be invalid for k=1, n=1.
- Empirical strengths: demonstrates effectiveness at finding identities difficult to find manually for certain expression families.
- Well-defined framework: problem and attribute-grammar-based exploration are clearly specified.
- Generalizability concern: approach’s applicability to broader classes of expressions is not shown.
- Efficiency concern: unclear whether discovered identities are genuinely more efficient in practice due to lack of complexity optimization.
- Minor corrections: typos at lines 148, 289, 332, and 338 need fixing.
- Overall verdict: interesting and original work with significant potential, but limited scope and missing complexity guarantees raise acceptance concerns.
