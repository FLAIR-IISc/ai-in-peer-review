### Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

**Summary**

This paper presents a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to represent mathematical expressions as trees using an attribute grammar. The primary challenge is the combinatorial explosion in the number of possible trees (i.e., expressions) for a given target. To address this, the authors propose using machine learning to guide the search through this vast space.

The paper introduces and evaluates two learning-based search strategies: an n-gram model that captures common sub-expressions from previously found solutions, and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions. These models are trained on solutions to simpler problems to guide the search for more complex identities. The framework is successfully applied to several families of expressions, including elementary symmetric polynomials and terms from the partition function of an RBM, discovering non-trivial and reportedly new identities that offer significant computational speedups (e.g., from O(n^k) or exponential to polynomial time). The work draws connections to program induction, theorem proving, and techniques from natural language processing, notably the use of RNNs for structured data.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a challenging and important problem at the intersection of symbolic computation and machine learning. Applying learning-based search to discover mathematical identities is a highly novel idea. If this framework can be generalized, it could have a significant impact on optimizing code in scientific computing, compilers, and machine learning libraries. The discovery of new, efficient identities for complex expressions (e.g., related to RBMs) is a strong demonstration of the method's potential.

2.  **Novel Technical Contribution (Learned Representation):** A key contribution is the successful application of a recursive neural network to learn a continuous embedding for symbolic mathematical expressions. The high classification accuracy reported demonstrates that this representation effectively captures the semantic equivalence of different expressions. This technique could be of independent interest to the community, enabling the application of various continuous-space machine learning models to symbolic domains.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments clearly show that the learning-based search strategies (both n-gram and RNN) significantly outperform a random search baseline, especially as the complexity of the target expression increases. The framework successfully finds identities for problems with massive search spaces where naive approaches would fail, validating the central hypothesis of the paper.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Clarity of the Methodology:** Several key components of the method could be explained more clearly.
    *   **RNN Architecture:** The description of the RNN's composition function is ambiguous. The paper states, "applies a weight tensor W3 (of size l x l x l, so that the output is also size l)". This suggests the use of a neural tensor network, not a standard RNN with a simple linear layer. Related work by Bowman [3] has shown that tensor networks can be more effective for logical reasoning tasks. The authors should clarify the exact architecture, justify its choice, and explicitly compare it to alternatives like the models in Socher et al. [23] or Bowman [3].
    *   **Search Process:** The interaction between the "scheduler" and the "strategy" is not fully detailed. It is unclear how proposals are generated and scored at each step of the tree construction. A more formal description or a pseudocode algorithm for the search loop would greatly improve the paper's reproducibility and clarity.
    *   **Using the RNN for Search:** The transition from training the RNN as a classifier to using it as part of the search strategy is confusing. Section 6.3 explains that a new softmax layer is trained on top of the learned representations `φ(b1)` and `φ(b2)` to predict the next rule. This critical step needs to be elaborated on. How is this second model trained? Is it trained on the same data as the original representation learner?

2.  **Restricted Problem Scope:** The problem definition is currently limited to homogeneous polynomials of a single variable that produce a scalar output. While this is a reasonable starting point for such an ambitious project, the paper would be stronger if it included a more thorough discussion of the challenges and potential paths toward generalizing the framework to handle multiple variables, non-polynomial expressions (e.g., trigonometric, exponential), and vector or matrix outputs.

3.  **Analysis of Results:** The n-gram models, particularly 3-gram to 5-gram, often outperform the more complex RNN model. This is an interesting and somewhat counter-intuitive result. The authors should provide more discussion on why this might be the case. Does it suggest that the solutions for the chosen expression families possess a highly repetitive local structure that is well-captured by n-grams? Or does it point to potential limitations or suboptimal training of the current RNN architecture for this generative task?

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong, creative, and thought-provoking paper that introduces a promising new direction for applying machine learning to symbolic mathematics. The strengths in originality, technical contribution, and empirical validation are significant. The main weaknesses relate to the clarity of the methodology and a limited discussion of the problem's scope. These issues are addressable through revision. I believe the work is of high quality and would be of great interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
