- Introduces a framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using an attribute-grammar representation of symbolic expressions.
- Proposes two learning-based search strategies to handle exponential search growth: an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN) for continuous expression representations.
- Demonstrates the approach across several expression families, finding identities beyond brute-force search and manual derivation.
- Key contributions: grammar framework for symbolic search, integration of learning to guide search, novel use of RNNs for mathematical structures, and discovery of new computationally advantageous identities.
- Paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments, clear problem formulation, and a proper random-search baseline.
- Includes thorough ablation studies comparing n-gram orders and RNN variants.
- Experimental setup pays appropriate attention to numerical representation and solution verification.
- RNN achieves up to 95% classification accuracy, supporting the effectiveness of the learned representation.
- Clarity issues: Sections 4 and 7.2 need better organization and more concrete examples.
- Technical descriptions of the attribute grammar and learned representation are somewhat hard to follow without additional examples or visualizations.
- Supplementary material contains valuable examples that should be referenced more explicitly in the main text.
- Originality: novel application of ML (n-grams and RNNs) to discovering mathematical identities, distinct from prior theorem-proving and symbolic methods.
- Significance: discovered identities have practical implications (e.g., RBM partition functions) and potential for integration into compilers/optimization frameworks.
- Broader relevance: advances methods for learning search strategies in combinatorially large spaces relevant to AI.
- Pros: novel ML–symbolic integration, demonstrated discovery of unknown identities, strong technical execution, and clear practical potential.
- Cons: clarity of some sections, scope currently focused on homogeneous polynomials, and practical impact needs further real-world validation.
- Overall recommendation: strong paper with significant contributions; reviewer recommends acceptance.
