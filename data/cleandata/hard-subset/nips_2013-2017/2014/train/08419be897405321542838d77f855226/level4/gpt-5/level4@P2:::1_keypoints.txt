- Proposes learning-guided search over an attribute grammar to find computationally cheaper, analytically equivalent symbolic matrix/vector identities.
- Uses n-gram subtree models and an RNN embedding to guide rule selection; checks equivalence numerically via integer descriptors modulo a large prime and combines trees via modular Gaussian elimination.
- Experiments on multiple families (sums of AAT powers, elementwise products, symmetric polynomials, RBM-like sums) show learned strategies can find identities that random search misses, sometimes reducing complexity (e.g., O(n^3)→O(n^2) or exponential→polynomial).
- RNN embeddings classify equivalence classes with ≤5% error, and n-gram models capture recurring local structures effectively.
- Novel problem framing: optimizing symbolic expressions for computational cost using learned search within a constrained grammar.
- Clean integration of attribute grammars, numeric equivalence checks, and linear combinations of trees; public code and clear examples provided.
- Empirical performance is mixed: hardest family ((RBM-2)^k) resists improvement beyond k=5; n-gram often outperforms the RNN during search.
- Lacks baselines against strong symbolic rewrite/optimization systems, compiler optimizations, or simple cost-model comparisons to contextualize gains.
- Numerical equivalence check lacks rigorous collision analysis for descriptors and choice of prime; symbolic verification of final identities is suggested.
- Implementation details are terse: full grammar, scheduler policy, multi-branch handling, and training data distribution for the RNN are under-specified.
- Complexity accounting needs clarification (what counts as O(n^2) vs O(n^3), and practical constant factors); report wall-clock speedups.
- Search strategies are simple; stronger methods (beam search, MCTS, A*, equality saturation) could improve results.
- Scope is narrow (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, mostly single-variable); scalability to richer operators, multiple variables, and non-polynomial functions is unclear.
- Claims of novel identities need careful validation against literature or symbolic proofs.
- Concrete suggested improvements: add CAS and cost-model baselines, use more powerful search (beam/MCTS), analyze descriptor collision risks and symbolically verify identities, ablate RNN architectures/training, expand to multi-variable targets and report scaling.
- Pros: novel direction, clear framework, public code, demonstrated complexity reductions, and meaningful symbolic embeddings.
- Cons: limited scope and scalability, RNN underperforms simpler n-grams, missing strong baselines and rigorous correctness/complexity analyses, and some engineering choices lack justification.
- Recommendation: borderline reject — interesting idea but needs stronger baselines, improved search, broader coverage, and clearer correctness and complexity analyses to be compelling.
