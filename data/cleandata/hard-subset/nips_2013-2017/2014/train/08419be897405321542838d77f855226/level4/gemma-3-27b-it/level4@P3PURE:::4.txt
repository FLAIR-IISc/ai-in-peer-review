The paper introduces a novel application of neural networks: learning representations for symbolic expressions and discovering their mathematical identities. While the scope of expressions currently studied is limited, and there is no formal proof of extensibility to more complex cases, I find the work interesting and believe it has the potential to significantly impact symbolic software libraries.

However, the paper has several areas for improvement. The problem statement focuses on identifying expressions with lower computational complexity, yet the proposed algorithm does not explicitly enforce this constraint. While I recognize this is a non-trivial challenge, I would like to know if the authors have considered potential solutions to limit the search space to expressions with desirable complexity properties.

Regarding the restrictions outlined in Section 2, the paper attributes limitations on handling more complex mathematical expressions primarily to the scheduler. I would appreciate a more rigorous justification for these restrictions – either through formal analysis or empirical experiments demonstrating their necessity. 

Furthermore, the paper’s structure hinders clarity. A more explicit and organized presentation of the different stages involved would significantly improve readability.

Finally, the paper lacks discussion regarding constraints on vector/matrix size. For example, the expression for RBM-1 in the supplementary material (for k=1, n=1) appears to be invalid.

I also noted a few minor typos: 
* Line 332: "show for" should be revised.
* Line 148: “has contains” should be corrected.
* Line 289: “is is” should be corrected.
* Line 338: “by be” should be corrected.

Despite these concerns, the paper presents a novel approach for representing symbolic expressions and finding their mathematical identities, even if limited in its current scope.
