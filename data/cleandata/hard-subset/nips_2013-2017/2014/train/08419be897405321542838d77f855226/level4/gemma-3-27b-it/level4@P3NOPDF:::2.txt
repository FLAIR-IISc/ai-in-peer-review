This paper introduces two novel approaches to discovering simpler symbolic expression trees: a method leveraging n-grams computed over sequences of sub-expressions, and a recursive neural network (RNN) designed to learn continuous space representations of these trees. The RNN aims to predict likely rule applications for combining subtrees, guiding the search process. Initial results, as presented in Figure 3, suggest the n-gram based approach outperforms the RNN on two out of three evaluated tasks.

**Quality:** High. The technical contributions appear sound and well-executed. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of both symbolic regression and neural network techniques. The experimental setup seems reasonable, and the reported results are presented with sufficient detail for verification.

**Clarity:** Moderate. While the core ideas are intriguing, the paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in Section 7.2, which details the RNN implementation and training procedure. The explanation of how the continuous representations are utilized for rule prediction feels somewhat opaque. Providing more intuitive explanations, potentially with illustrative examples, would significantly improve comprehension. Expanding on the rationale behind specific architectural choices within the RNN (e.g., the choice of recursion strategy, hidden unit sizes) would also be beneficial. Furthermore, the notation throughout the paper could be more consistent and clearly defined.

**Originality:** High. The combination of symbolic expression tree search with both n-gram analysis and recursive neural networks represents a novel contribution. The application of RNNs to this specific problem domain is, to the best of my knowledge, a new direction. The n-gram approach, while utilizing established techniques, is applied in a creative manner to optimize symbolic expressions.

**Significance:** Low. The primary limitation of this work lies in the perceived significance of the problem it addresses. The motivation for explicitly searching for *computationally* simpler symbolic expressions, rather than simply more accurate ones, is not convincingly established. While the authors argue for the importance of efficiency, the scenarios where this optimization would be truly impactful remain unclear. The tasks used for evaluation (presumably described elsewhere, but not sufficiently detailed within this review context) appear somewhat contrived, and it’s difficult to envision a practical application where these techniques would demonstrably outperform existing symbolic regression methods focused on accuracy. 

The paper would benefit from a more compelling discussion of the potential real-world applications of finding computationally efficient symbolic expressions. Are there specific domains (e.g., resource-constrained embedded systems, real-time control) where this optimization is crucial? Demonstrating a link to such applications would dramatically increase the perceived significance of the work. Even exploring the connection to program synthesis or code optimization could strengthen the argument.

**Further Comments & Questions:**

*   **Evaluation:** The paper mentions three tasks. Providing more details about these tasks – their characteristics, the complexity of the target functions, and the rationale behind their selection – is essential. How do the tasks reflect the intended use cases?
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** The paper would be strengthened by a comparison to more standard symbolic regression techniques (e.g., genetic programming) on the same tasks, even if these baselines are not explicitly optimized for computational efficiency. This would provide a better benchmark for assessing the performance of the proposed methods.
*   **Scalability:** How do the computational costs of the n-gram and RNN approaches scale with the complexity of the symbolic expressions and the size of the search space? This is a critical consideration given the goal of improving computational efficiency.
*   **Rule Set:** Is the set of allowed rules fixed, or can it be learned/evolved? The impact of the rule set on the performance of both methods should be discussed.



**Overall:** This is a technically sound and original paper with a potentially interesting approach to symbolic regression. However, its impact is limited by the lack of a clear motivating application and the somewhat contrived nature of the evaluation tasks. Addressing the clarity issues, particularly in Section 7.2, and providing a more compelling justification for the problem’s significance would significantly strengthen the paper.
