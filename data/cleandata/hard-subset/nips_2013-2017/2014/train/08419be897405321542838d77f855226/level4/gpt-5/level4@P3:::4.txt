Summary
This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities by searching over symbolic expressions defined by an attribute grammar. Equality between expressions is checked via a numerical descriptor computed by evaluating expressions over random inputs modulo a large prime. Because exhaustive search over expression trees is exponential, the paper introduces two learned search strategies trained on solutions to simpler targets: (i) an n-gram model over subtrees and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous representations of expressions and predicts the next rule. The method also allows linear combinations of multiple trees, solved by modular Gaussian elimination, to match a target descriptor. Experiments on several families of expressions (e.g., sums over products of AAT, elementwise variants, symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related exponential sums) show that learned strategies significantly outperform random search, often discovering identities that reduce O(n^3) computations to O(n^2), and occasionally replacing exponential-time evaluations with polynomial-time equivalents. The paper relates to theorem proving, program induction, symbolic algebra systems, and NLP-style representation learning (e.g., Socher et al.) but claims a novel focus on learning to guide identity discovery.

Strengths
- Originality: Framing identity discovery as learning-guided grammar search is novel. The use of an RNN to learn continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions, then leveraging them for search, is especially interesting and may open new avenues for applying ML in symbolic domains.
- Significance: The approach can discover nontrivial identities with practical implications for code optimization and symbolic libraries, particularly in linear algebra and structured polynomial expressions. The availability of code aids reproducibility.
- Quality: The modular design (grammar, equality checking, linear combination solver, learned strategies) is technically coherent. The numerical descriptor modulo a prime is a practical alternative to full symbolic equivalence checking. The curriculum training and engineering choices for the RNN are thoughtful.
- Empirics: Clear evidence that learned strategies (especially higher-order n-grams) dramatically expand the scope of solvable targets compared to random search. The RBM-1/2 families are ambitious and informative stress tests.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Complexity objective not enforced: While the goal is to find lower-complexity identities, the search does not explicitly optimize or constrain computational cost; instead, complexity is indirectly controlled by pruning grammar rules (e.g., removing matrix–matrix multiply). A principled cost-aware search (e.g., cost-augmented scoring, A* with admissible cost estimates, or beam search with a complexity prior) would better align the algorithm with the stated objective and enable fairer comparisons across grammars.
- Equivalence guarantees: The numerical descriptor over Zp reduces false mismatches but does not quantify collision probability. A more formal justification (e.g., via Schwartz–Zippel bounds and/or multiple independent primes) and ablations on N and p would strengthen confidence that discovered identities are correct over the intended field (typically reals).
- Linear combinations and admissible coefficients: The linear system Fw = t (mod p) can yield arbitrary integer coefficients modulo p. The paper should clarify: (i) what coefficient set is permitted in the final identity (e.g., small integers, rationals), (ii) how modular solutions are lifted to valid coefficients over reals, and (iii) whether the grammar allows scalar multiplication by constants. Without this, some matches may be artefacts of modular arithmetic rather than usable identities.
- Scope restrictions: The method focuses on homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, scalar outputs, and primarily single-variable inputs. The paper asserts these restrictions but does not systematically justify them or show empirical sensitivity to relaxing them (e.g., multiple variables, mixed degrees, non-polynomial ops). It is not clear whether the scheduler is the main bottleneck versus representational or equality-checking limits.
- RNN versus n-grams: In many families, higher-order n-grams outperform the RNN. Discussion on when continuous representations help (e.g., non-repetitive structures) versus when they underperform, and whether better architectures (TreeLSTMs, attention over partial trees) or training signals (contrastive/metric learning) would close the gap, would be valuable.
- Clarity and organization: The pipeline (from grammar, to equality check, to linear combinations, to search and learning) could be presented more linearly with a schematic and pseudocode for the scheduler/strategy interface.

Quality
- Technically sound overall, but the lack of explicit complexity optimization and the unresolved issues around modular equivalence and coefficient lifting leave gaps. More rigorous treatment of collision probability and constraints on identity coefficients is recommended.

Clarity
- The paper is readable but could be better organized. Suggested improvements:
  - Add a high-level flow diagram and pseudocode for the scheduler and strategy scoring.
  - Clearly define the cost model and how complexity attributes are used (or not) during search.
  - State assumptions on matrix/vector dimensions and conformability throughout. Provide explicit constraints on n, m, p in all examples/grammars.
  - Consolidate equality-checking details and provide recommended default parameters (N, p, number of primes).

Originality
- Strong. While related to theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic algebra, the learned search over attribute grammars for identity discovery, coupled with continuous expression embeddings, is a novel contribution.

Significance
- Moderate to high, contingent on tightening correctness guarantees and expanding scope. The approach could influence compiler optimizations and symbolic libraries and may motivate further ML for symbolic computation.

Questions for the authors
- How do you prevent the linear solver from returning coefficients that are invalid over reals once lifted from mod p? Do you constrain w (e.g., to small integers) and verify equality over multiple primes or in rational arithmetic?
- Can you incorporate complexity into the search objective (e.g., penalize rules by their attribute cost, maintain Pareto fronts of (match score, cost))? Any preliminary results?
- Is the scheduler the primary barrier to handling multiple variables and mixed-degree expressions, or do representation/equality checks also limit scalability?
- What are the dimensionality assumptions in RBM-1/2 and linear algebra families? For k=1, n=1 in RBM-1 (supplement), the expression appears ill-typed—please clarify.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and promising framing of identity discovery.
- Demonstrated ability to find nontrivial, efficiency-improving identities beyond brute force.
- Reusable idea of learned continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions.
- Released code and clear empirical methodology.

Cons for acceptance
- Objective (lower complexity) is not explicitly optimized.
- Limited theoretical guarantees for equivalence; coefficient handling unclear.
- Scope is narrow; empirical justification for restrictions is limited.
- RNN underperforms simple n-grams in key settings; architectural justification could be stronger.

Overall assessment
An interesting and creative paper that advances learning-guided search in symbolic domains. With clearer treatment of correctness and complexity, and improved exposition, I would lean toward acceptance. Current version: weak accept.
