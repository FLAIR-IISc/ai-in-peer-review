

The paper proposes a novel neural network approach for learning representations of symbolic expressions and identifying their mathematical identities. While the work addresses an interesting problem with potential applications in symbolic computation libraries, it is limited to a narrow class of expressions, and the authors provide no theoretical justification for scalability to more complex cases. Despite these constraints, the core idea merits attention, particularly for its potential to enhance symbolic software. Below, I outline key concerns requiring clarification.

First, the problem statement emphasizes minimizing computational complexity, yet the algorithm imposes no such constraint during search. The authors acknowledge this challenge but do not discuss how to restrict the search space to low-complexity solutions. Without addressing this, the practical utility of the method remains questionable. A brief discussion of potential strategies—such as incorporating complexity penalties into the loss function or leveraging domain-specific heuristics—would strengthen the contribution.

Second, Section 2 introduces restrictions on expression complexity but offers insufficient justification. The authors state these constraints arise from "scheduler limitations," yet it is unclear whether this is the sole factor or if deeper mathematical or representational barriers exist. The paper would benefit from either a formal analysis of these constraints or empirical validation (e.g., experiments demonstrating how expression size impacts performance). Without this, the scope of the method appears arbitrarily defined.

Third, the paper’s structure impedes clarity. The methodology is presented as a sequence of "stages," but the transitions between them are not explicitly delineated. For instance, the shift from representation learning to identity discovery lacks a clear narrative thread. A more organized exposition—perhaps with a high-level workflow diagram or subsections explicitly mapping stages to algorithmic components—would greatly improve readability and help readers grasp the technical flow.

Fourth, a potential issue arises in the supplementary material (RBM-1 expression, k=1,n=1). The derivation appears invalid under these parameters, suggesting either a typographical error or an unaddressed edge case. The authors should verify this example or clarify the conditions under which the expression holds.

Minor grammatical errors and typos also require correction:  
- Line 332: "show for" → "show that"  
- Line 148: "has contains" → "contains"  
- Line 289: "is is" → "is"  
- Line 338: "by be" → "to be"  

The paper’s core innovation—using neural networks to discover symbolic identities—holds promise for symbolic computation, particularly in contexts where manual derivation is error-prone or inefficient. However, the current presentation does not sufficiently establish its generality or robustness. To elevate this work, the authors should:  
1. Explicitly address computational complexity constraints in the algorithm,  
2. Provide justification for the chosen expression restrictions,  
3. Reorganize the methodology for clearer stage delineation,  
4. Resolve the supplementary material discrepancy.  

Without these revisions, the paper risks being perceived as a narrow technical exercise rather than a broadly applicable technique. If addressed, the work could make a meaningful contribution to the symbolic AI and automated reasoning communities. As presented, it is a promising but incomplete step toward scalable symbolic identity discovery.
