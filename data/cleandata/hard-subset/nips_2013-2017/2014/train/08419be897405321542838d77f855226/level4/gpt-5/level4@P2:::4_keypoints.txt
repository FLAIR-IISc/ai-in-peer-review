- Proposes a framework that searches an attribute grammar of linear-algebraic operators to find computationally cheaper equivalent symbolic expressions.  
- Introduces learned search strategies: an n-gram model over subtrees and an RNN embedding that predicts next grammar rules.  
- Uses two equivalence tests: numeric Zp descriptors (random evaluation modulo a prime) and a learned RNN embedding for equivalence classification.  
- Finds efficient identities as single trees or linear combinations by solving linear systems modulo a prime.  
- Experiments on five target families; learning-guided search outperforms random search, with n-grams often beating the RNN; RBM-2 remains hard beyond k=5.  
- Novel application of ML to symbolic manipulation, combining learned heuristics and continuous representations for algebraic structures.  
- Clear, modular pipeline (grammar, equivalence tests, guided search) with code and data released.  
- Zp descriptors are a pragmatic choice to avoid floating-point issues and to enable modular linear solving.  
- Potential impactful applications for compilers/CAS optimizations and some ML objectives.  
- Search does not explicitly enforce or optimize computational complexity; complexity-aware constraints or guarantees are missing.  
- Scope is narrow (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, often single variable) and not demonstrated on broader operator sets.  
- Equivalence checking via random Zp evaluations lacks formal collision probability bounds and symbolic verification fallback.  
- Scheduler/strategy interface is under-specified for more general grammars (recursion, variable-size outputs) and termination criteria are informal.  
- Paper organization and clarity could improve; pipeline stages and dependencies are sometimes hard to follow.  
- Experimental choices (timeouts, success criteria, n-gram weighting) need more justification and ablation; RNN failure modes are under-analyzed.  
- Possible error or underspecification in the supplemental RBM-1 example (k=1, n=1); operator size/shape constraints unclear.  
- Methods are technically sound but weakened by lack of complexity-aware search and formal equivalence guarantees.  
- Originality is high: combining n-gram/RNN learned strategies with attribute-grammar search is novel relative to theorem proving and program induction.  
- Significance is moderate-to-high if extended; promising results within a limited domain.  
- Key questions: how to incorporate complexity into the strategy, Zp false-positive rates and validation, shape/size constraints, scheduler tie/termination rules, and why n-grams outperform the RNN.  
- Suggested fixes: add formal descriptor collision bounds and verification step, integrate complexity into search (A* or multi-objective), provide algorithmic overview/pipeline diagram, and include ablations for n-gram/RNN choices and search metrics.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel approach, demonstrated nontrivial identity discovery, released code/data, bridges ML and symbolic computation.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited scope, no complexity guarantees, mixed experimental outcomes with limited analysis, and clarity/presentation issues.  
- Minor typos listed (e.g., "has contains" → "contains"; "is is" → "is"; "show for" → "show that"; "by be" → "be").  
- Overall: interesting and creative work; recommend acceptance if authors address complexity constraints, equivalence guarantees, and pipeline clarity, otherwise borderline.
