The paper introduces a novel application of machine learning, specifically learning representations for symbolic expressions to discover mathematical identities. While the scope of expressions considered is limited, and there’s no proof of scalability to more complex cases, I find the work interesting and believe it has the potential to impact symbolic software libraries.

The problem statement focuses on finding expressions with lower computational complexity. However, the algorithm doesn’t explicitly enforce this constraint during the search. Have the authors considered possible methods to limit the search space to solutions with demonstrably lower complexity? It would be valuable to see some discussion of this.

Regarding the restrictions mentioned in Section 2, is the scheduler the primary limiting factor when moving to more complex mathematical expressions? I would appreciate a more formal justification, or empirical analysis, supporting these limitations.

The paper’s structure could be improved for clarity. The progression of ideas isn’t always easy to follow, and a more explicit breakdown of the stages involved would be beneficial. 

Is there a constraint on the size of the vector/matrix involved? For example, for k=1 and n=1, the expression for RBM-1 in the supplementary material appears invalid.

Here’s a summary of arguments for and against acceptance:

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of machine learning techniques, particularly recursive neural networks, to the discovery of mathematical identities is a novel approach.
*   **Potential Impact:** The ability to automatically discover more efficient expressions could be valuable for optimizing symbolic computations in software libraries and other applications.
*   **Empirical Results:** The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach in finding identities that are difficult or impossible to discover manually, particularly for certain families of expressions.
*   **Well-defined Problem:** The authors have clearly defined the problem and introduced a framework based on attribute grammars that allows for a systematic exploration of possible solutions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Scope:** The current approach is limited to a specific class of polynomials and assumes a single matrix or vector variable. The generalizability to more complex expressions is not demonstrated.
*   **Lack of Complexity Constraint:** The algorithm doesn’t actively optimize for computational complexity, making it unclear whether the discovered identities are genuinely more efficient in practice.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper could benefit from a more structured and clearly articulated presentation of the methodology and results.
