

This paper presents a novel application of machine learning to the discovery of computationally efficient mathematical identities, using attribute grammars to represent symbolic expressions and learning techniques to guide the search for equivalent, lower-complexity forms. The authors introduce a grammar-based framework where expressions are built as trees, with computational complexity as an attribute. They then propose two learning-based search strategies—n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs)—trained on solutions for simpler expressions to discover identities for more complex targets. The method demonstrates success on specific polynomial families (e.g., reducing O(n³) to O(n²) for matrix expressions), with examples of previously undiscovered identities. The work draws connections to NLP techniques (e.g., n-grams, RNNs for parsing) and attribute grammars (Knuth, 1968), though it is the first to apply these to mathematical identity discovery.

**Strengths:**  
The paper makes a compelling case for the *novelty* of its approach. While attribute grammars have been used in compiler design and algorithm optimization (e.g., Cheung & McCanne, ICIP 2002), the integration of learning to guide symbolic search is original. The RNN-based continuous representation of symbolic expressions (Section 4.2) is particularly significant—it bridges symbolic computation and neural networks, enabling techniques like embeddings (e.g., Mikolov et al., NIPS 2013) to be applied to mathematical structures. The experimental results for homogeneous polynomials (e.g., Families 1–2 in Section 7.2) are technically sound, with clear complexity reductions (e.g., Example 2 for k=6) and rigorous validation via integer-based descriptors (Section 4.1). The framework’s ability to discover identities beyond brute-force reach (e.g., k=15 for Family 1) demonstrates *significant* potential for practical applications, such as optimizing machine learning operations (e.g., dropout approximations, RBM partition functions, as discussed in Section 8).

**Weaknesses:**  
The primary limitation is the *restricted scope* of the problem. The framework assumes homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree k, a single matrix/vector variable, and scalar outputs (Section 2). While the authors acknowledge this (Section 8), the experiments only test this narrow class. For instance, the RBM-2 family (exponential sums) fails for k>5, and the RNN strategy performs no better than random search here—contradicting the paper’s claim that learning scales to complex cases. This raises questions about whether the approach generalizes beyond the authors’ carefully curated examples. Furthermore, the "sledgehammer" critique in the draft is partially valid: for simpler identities (e.g., k=2–4), the n-gram strategy is trivially effective (e.g., repeating patterns in Example 2), and hand-derived rules might suffice without learning. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate that ML *adds value* over simpler heuristics for the tested cases.

**Quality and Significance:**  
The technical quality is strong for the scope presented (e.g., RNN training details in Section 4.2, numerical validation in Section 4.1), but the *significance* is constrained by the narrow problem domain. The discovered identities are mathematically interesting, but their utility depends on whether the framework can extend to broader classes (e.g., non-homogeneous polynomials, trigonometric functions). The authors’ claim that this "naturally lends itself to integration with compilers" (Section 8) remains speculative without evidence of scalability. The paper does not address how the grammar would adapt to more complex structures (e.g., recursion, non-polynomial operations), which is critical for real-world applicability. While the method is a promising *step* toward learning-based symbolic discovery, it does not yet advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way for the field at large.

**Clarity and Originality:**  
The paper is well-written and clearly structured. Originality is strong in the *integration* of NLP techniques (n-grams, RNNs) with symbolic search, though the grammar itself is standard (Knuth, 1968). Related work (Section 1.1) appropriately cites foundational NIPS papers (e.g., Socher et al., NIPS 2011; Mikolov et al., NIPS 2013) and distinguishes the work from theorem-proving approaches (e.g., Bridge et al., JAR 2014). However, the paper overstates the novelty of the *application*; the core idea of using learning to guide search in combinatorial spaces is not new (e.g., Bengio’s curriculum learning, ICML 2009), though its adaptation to mathematical identities is fresh.

**Recommendation:**  
This work is innovative in methodology but limited in scope and impact. It makes a valuable contribution to the *technique* of learning-driven symbolic search, but the results do not yet justify its significance for NIPS. The framework’s success on a constrained set of examples is promising, but the lack of evidence for scalability (e.g., RBM-2 failure, no extension to non-polynomial cases) prevents it from being a compelling advance. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:  
1) Expand experiments to less structured problems (e.g., non-homogeneous polynomials),  
2) Compare against non-learning heuristics (e.g., rule-based pattern matching) to quantify ML’s marginal gain,  
3) Provide a clearer path to broader applicability (e.g., how grammar rules could be extended).  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro:* Novel integration of NLP techniques with symbolic search; rigorous demonstration of complexity reduction for specific cases; strong RNN representation for symbolic expressions.  
*Con:* Overly restricted problem domain; marginal gains over simple heuristics for tested cases; insufficient evidence of scalability to more complex or general problems.  

This paper is a solid *foundation* for future work but currently falls short of NIPS’s standard for significance and general impact. It would be better suited for a venue focused on specific applications (e.g., ML for scientific computing) rather than a top-tier general conference. I recommend rejection.
