This paper explores a new application of neural networks: learning representations for symbolic expressions and using them to uncover analytically equivalent identities.

Although the class of expressions considered is quite restricted and there is no demonstration that the method extends to more complex settings, the work is interesting and could have meaningful impact on symbolic math software libraries.

- The problem statement aims to find expressions with reduced computational complexity, yet the algorithm does not explicitly enforce such a constraint. I recognize this is nontrivial, but have the authors considered mechanisms to bias or restrict the search to lower-complexity candidates to narrow the search space?

- Regarding the constraints in Section 2, is the scheduler the primary limiting factor for handling more general mathematical expressions? I would have appreciated either a more formal justification or empirical evidence motivating these restrictions.

- The paper’s organization is somewhat difficult to follow; clarifying the stages of the pipeline and how they connect would improve readability.

- Are there dimensionality assumptions on vectors/matrices? For instance, for k=1, n=1, the RBM-1 expression in the supplementary material appears invalid.

Some typos:
332: show for
148: has contains
289: is is
338: by be

Overall, the paper presents a new approach to representing symbolic expressions and discovering their mathematical identities. While the current scope is limited, it is an interesting and novel direction.
