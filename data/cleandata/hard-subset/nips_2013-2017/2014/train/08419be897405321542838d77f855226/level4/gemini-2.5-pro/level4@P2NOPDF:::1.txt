This paper proposes a novel application of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) to the problem of discovering mathematical identities. The core idea of leveraging machine learning for symbolic mathematics is intriguing and represents a creative direction for research.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this work lies in its novelty. Applying data-driven techniques like DNNs to a domain traditionally governed by formal logic and symbolic manipulation is an interesting and potentially impactful line of inquiry. The paper is well-motivated in exploring a new frontier for machine learning applications.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the novelty, the work in its current form feels preliminary and may not yet meet the high standard for publication at NIPS. My main concerns are as follows:

1.  **Limited Empirical Performance:** The method's effectiveness in discovering identities, as demonstrated in the experiments, is not particularly compelling. The results do not yet provide strong evidence that the proposed DNN-based approach is a robust or powerful tool for this task.

2.  **Constrained Problem Scope:** The evaluation is conducted on a highly restricted set of mathematical expressions. Given this limited scope, the choice of a complex DNN model feels like an over-engineered solution. For the narrow class of identities explored, it is plausible that a simpler, more direct algorithmic or rule-based method could achieve the same or better results with far greater efficiency and interpretability. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a justification for using a learning-based approach over such alternatives within this constrained setting.

3.  **Unclear Path to Scalability:** A key motivation for using a machine learning approach in this domain would be its potential to generalize to a much broader and more complex space of expressions where human intuition or brute-force search fails. However, the paper does not present a clear or convincing argument for how the proposed method would scale to more general cases. The modest performance on the initial, simplified problem set does not build confidence that this approach would be successful when faced with a more diverse and challenging search space. A more thorough discussion of the scalability challenges and the architectural or methodological adaptations required to address them is needed.

**Conclusion:**
In summary, while the paper introduces an interesting and novel research direction, the contributions feel incremental at this stage. The combination of a narrowly defined problem, underwhelming empirical results, and unresolved questions about the method's scalability and generalizability suggests that the work is not yet mature enough for a top-tier venue like NIPS. I believe this is a promising line of inquiry, but it requires more substantial development and more convincing empirical validation before it is ready for publication.

I had noted several typos and minor grammatical errors in a previous reading. I would encourage the authors to perform a careful proofread of the manuscript.
