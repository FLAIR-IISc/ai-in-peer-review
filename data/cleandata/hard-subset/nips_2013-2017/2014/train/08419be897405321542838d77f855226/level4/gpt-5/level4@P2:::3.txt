Summary
The paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given symbolic expression. Expressions are generated from an attribute grammar over matrix/vector operations; each tree corresponds to a candidate expression with known computational cost. Because exhaustive search is infeasible, the authors learn strategies from simpler targets to guide exploration for harder ones. Two representations enable fast equivalence checking and learning: (i) a numerical descriptor over evaluations modulo a large prime, and (ii) a learned continuous embedding from a recursive neural network (RNN) trained to classify expression trees into equivalence classes. To handle complex targets, linear combinations of trees are solved via modular Gaussian elimination to match the target’s descriptor. Three search strategies are compared: random, n-gram over subtrees, and an RNN-guided softmax predicting the next rule. Experiments on several families (including sums over powers of AAT, elementwise variants, symmetric polynomials, and RBM-inspired series) show that learned strategies can find identities beyond the reach of random search, sometimes reducing naive cubic or exponential costs to quadratic or even linear-time formulations. Code and data are released.

Strengths
- Clear and novel framing: casting identity discovery as guided search in an attribute-grammar space.
- Effective blend of symbolic and statistical techniques: modular arithmetic descriptors for fast equivalence checks and a recursive network that learns useful embeddings of expressions.
- Demonstrated practical impact: nontrivial identities that reduce asymptotic cost, with examples that would be tedious or intractable by hand or brute force.
- Good use of curriculum learning and dropout to make the RNN viable on deep trees.
- Open-sourced implementation.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Scope is quite narrow: homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, typically over a single variable, and a limited grammar. This should be discussed more explicitly in terms of what breaks when relaxing assumptions.
- Section 4.2 architecture: you state “weight tensor W3 (of size l × l × l, so that the output is also size l).” Please clarify whether this is a recursive neural tensor network (as in Socher et al.) or a standard recursive network with operator-specific bilinear/linear parametrizations. If tensors are used broadly, a brief comparison with tensor vs non-tensor variants would help, especially since prior work (e.g., Bowman) suggests tensor models can outperform plain RNNs on logical reasoning tasks.
- Section 7.2: It is unclear whether the RNN is still used to guide search (as a strategy) or only for pretraining/classification, while n-gram is used for the main results. Please state, per family and per k, which strategy discovered each identity.
- Proposal generation: The scheduler/strategy interface is under-specified. Please detail how candidate rules are enumerated and ranked, whether there is beam search, backtracking, or multi-starts, and how two-branch rules are proposed/combined. Pseudocode would greatly improve clarity.
- Correctness guarantees: modular descriptors avoid floating-point issues, but a single prime may allow collisions. Do you verify matches across multiple primes or with a final symbolic check? Also clarify how you set N (number of evaluation points) relative to the number of linear equations to ensure solvability and avoid spurious matches.
- Evaluation depth: since n-gram often outperforms the RNN for larger k, more analysis would be useful (e.g., failure modes of RNN strategy, ablations on embedding dimensionality, or ways to inject locality into the RNN to better capture repetitive patterns).
- Grammar choices: You sometimes remove matrix–matrix multiplication to enforce O(n2) solutions. Please justify this design more fully and discuss its impact on completeness—could it exclude valid efficient identities that transiently require matmul?

Quality
- Generally sound, with a pragmatic equivalence check and a workable RNN training recipe. However, the paper would benefit from clearer algorithmic specifications and stronger correctness checks (e.g., multiple primes or symbolic verification of final identities).
- Complexity claims for discovered identities are plausible; providing explicit cost analyses for a few representative discoveries would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- The high-level narrative is clear, but several crucial implementation details (scheduler, proposal scoring, training data generation per k, and the exact RNN operator parametrization) need tightening. Pseudocode and a figure of the scheduler–strategy loop would help.
- See the typo list below.

Originality
- Applying attribute grammars plus learned guidance to symbolic identity discovery appears novel, and the RNN-based embedding of algebraic expressions is an interesting contribution that parallels NLP tree models.

Significance
- If generalized and integrated into compilers or CAS systems, the approach could impact how we optimize algebraic code and certain ML computations (e.g., RBM terms, dropout-related sums). Current scope and mixed performance on the hardest families limit near-term breadth, but the direction is promising.

Pros for acceptance
- Innovative formulation with practical wins on nontrivial identities.
- Solid combination of symbolic and learned components; code available.
- Empirical evidence that learning significantly outperforms random search.

Cons for acceptance
- Narrow scope and several underspecified algorithmic details.
- n-gram often exceeds RNN performance; limited analysis of why.
- Limited guarantees against equivalence collisions; verification procedure unclear.

Overall
A creative and potentially impactful paper that convincingly shows learning can guide symbolic identity discovery. I recommend acceptance if the authors clarify the model architecture and scheduler/proposal mechanism, and strengthen correctness verification and the analysis of search strategies.
