This paper introduces a novel framework leveraging machine learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions as trees built from an attribute grammar, and then utilize learned models – specifically n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) – to guide the search for equivalent expressions with reduced computational complexity. The learned models are trained on simpler expressions to improve the search strategy for more complex ones. Results demonstrate the ability to derive identities beyond the reach of brute-force search or manual derivation.

This work builds on previous research in symbolic computation, theorem proving (e.g., [5, 9, 11]), and program induction [18, 28]. Unlike these areas, which often grapple with undecidability, this paper restricts the problem to homogeneous polynomials of a fixed degree, creating a manageable search space. The authors also draw connections to existing symbolic computation engines like Maple and Mathematica [6, 27], highlighting that these systems primarily focus on simplification using rule-based approaches rather than learning-based discovery. The use of attribute grammars [14] is also situated within existing work in compiler construction and optimization problems [19, 26, 10], while acknowledging the novelty of applying them specifically to mathematical formula discovery with learning. Recent applications of machine learning to reasoning [4] are also discussed, contrasting their heuristic-based approach with the learned search strategies proposed here. Finally, the work relates to NLP techniques such as n-gram models [16] and recursive neural networks [22, 23], demonstrating a thoughtful engagement with relevant literature.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for discovering mathematical identities is a unique approach. The application of a recursive neural network to learn continuous representations of symbolic expressions is also a significant contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The framework appears technically well-founded, with a clear problem definition, a defined grammar, and a description of the learning methods. The experimental evaluation provides evidence supporting the effectiveness of the approach.
*   **Demonstrated Results:** The paper provides compelling examples of discovering identities that reduce computational complexity, and demonstrates performance beyond brute-force or manual approaches, as seen in Examples 1 & 2.
*   **Availability of Code:** The publicly available code and evaluation data ([https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning](https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning)) are a valuable asset for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** While generally well-written, some sections, particularly Section 7.2, are dense and could benefit from more detailed explanations and clearer connections to the broader narrative.
*   **Significance:** The practical impact of discovering these identities, while interesting, remains unclear. The problem, though well-defined, feels somewhat contrived. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not fully address potential real-world applications where such efficient identities would be directly beneficial. The paper might benefit from speculating on how these discoveries could be applied to other fields, such as optimization or numerical analysis.
* **Limited Scope:** The restrictions imposed on the problem (homogeneous polynomials, single/few variables) limit the immediate generalizability of the framework. While acknowledged, the discussion regarding potential extensions could be more detailed.
* **RNN Performance:** The n-gram models consistently outperformed the RNN in the experiments, raising questions about the effectiveness of the RNN in this specific domain. A deeper analysis of why the RNN struggled compared to the n-gram approach would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*   The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods in discovering non-trivial mathematical identities.
*   The availability of code and data promotes reproducibility and encourages further research.
*   The work opens up new avenues for applying machine learning to symbolic computation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The practical significance of the work is uncertain.
*   The clarity of certain sections could be improved.
*   The RNN approach did not perform as well as the n-gram models.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and technically competent contribution. While the practical significance of the work is debatable, the novelty of the approach and the demonstrated results make it worthy of consideration. The clarity issues can be addressed through revisions. I believe that with some improvements, this paper could be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions, focusing on improving clarity and discussing the potential broader impact of the research. A more detailed analysis of the RNN performance compared to the n-gram model would also be beneficial.
