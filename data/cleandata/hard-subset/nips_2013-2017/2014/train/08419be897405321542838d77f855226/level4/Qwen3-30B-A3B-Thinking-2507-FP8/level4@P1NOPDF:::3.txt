

This paper presents an interesting approach to discovering more efficient algebraic identities, demonstrating that distributed, recursive neural representations can successfully classify such identities. However, the problem scope appears overly restricted, and key methodological details require clarification.

The paper's definition of the weight tensor $W_3$ (size $l \times l \times l$, producing an output of size $l$) is ambiguous. It is unclear whether the authors employ a neural tensor network (NTN) rather than a simple linear layer. Bowman et al. (2015) established that NTN architectures significantly outperform standard RNNs for such classification tasks; this context should be explicitly addressed to justify the model choice.

Section 7.2 ("Efficient Identity Discovery") raises significant methodological concerns. The paper evaluates different search strategies but fails to clarify whether the RNN remains the classification backbone during this process. Specifically, the proposal generation mechanism for the search space is inadequately explained. A clearer description of how candidate identities are generated and evaluated would strengthen the contribution.

Several typos and phrasing issues impede readability:
- "has contains a single matrix" → "contains a single matrix"
- "listed in table Table 1" → "listed in Table 1"
- "vector is the presented to the next operation" → "vector is presented to the next operation"
- "The final vector is pass to a softmax classifier" → "The final vector is passed to a softmax classifier"
- "as to how explore space of possible" → "how to explore the space of possible"
- "The scheduler is is fixed" → "The scheduler is fixed"
- "The difficulty of the task by be appreciated by looking" → "The task's difficulty becomes apparent when examining"
- "which can easily captured with" → "which can be easily captured with"

Additionally, Figure 3 incorrectly labels the model as "TNN" instead of "RNN," which contradicts the text. This error must be corrected.

The core idea—using neural representations for identity discovery—is promising, but the current presentation lacks sufficient technical precision. The restricted problem formulation and insufficient methodological details prevent a clear assessment of the approach's novelty and generality. The authors should explicitly address Bowman et al.'s findings, clarify the RNN's role during search, and rigorously detail the proposal mechanism. Without these revisions, the contribution's significance remains difficult to evaluate.
