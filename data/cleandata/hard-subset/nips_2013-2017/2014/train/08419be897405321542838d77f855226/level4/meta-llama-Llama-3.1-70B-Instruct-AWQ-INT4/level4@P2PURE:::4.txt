The paper introduces a novel approach for representing symbolic expressions and finding their mathematical identities using neural networks. Although the expressions studied are limited, I find the paper interesting and believe it could have a good impact on symbolic software libraries.

However, there are a few concerns that need to be addressed. The problem statement emphasizes the importance of finding expressions with lower computational complexity, but the algorithm does not enforce this constraint. I understand that incorporating such a constraint is not trivial, but I would like the authors to discuss possible solutions to limit the search space to such solutions.

Regarding the restrictions mentioned in Section 2, it is unclear whether the scheduler is the only limiting factor for more complicated mathematical expressions. I would have liked to see a more formal analysis or empirical experiments to justify these restrictions.

Furthermore, the structure of the paper is not easy to follow, and the stages of the approach could be explained more clearly.

Additionally, I noticed that there is no mention of constraints on the size of the vector/matrix. For instance, the expression for RBM-1 in the supplementary material appears to be invalid for k=1 and n=1.

Some minor errors were also found: "show for" (page 332), "has contains" (page 148), "is is" (page 289), and "by be" (page 338).
