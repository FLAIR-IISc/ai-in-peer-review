This paper presents a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions as trees derived from an attribute grammar. Since a brute-force search over the space of possible expression trees is intractable for all but the simplest cases, the authors propose two machine learning methods to guide the search. These methods are trained on solutions found for simpler expressions to learn which search paths are most promising for more complex targets. The first search strategy is a relatively simple n-gram model over sub-expressions, while the second is a more sophisticated recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation of symbolic expression trees. The authors demonstrate that these learning-based strategies can discover complex, non-obvious identities for several families of polynomial expressions, reducing their computational complexity from exponential or high-order polynomial time to a lower-order polynomial time.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Originality:** The paper's primary strength is its originality. Applying machine learning techniques, particularly recursive neural networks, to guide the search for symbolic mathematical identities is a highly novel and creative approach. This work sits at an interesting intersection of symbolic computation, program induction, and machine learning. The concept of learning a continuous embedding for symbolic expressions, analogous to word embeddings in NLP, is a significant conceptual contribution that could open up symbolic domains to a wider range of learning-based tools.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sound. The use of an attribute grammar to define the search space is well-founded, and the numerical and learned representations for checking expression equivalence are clever solutions to the challenges of symbolic manipulation. The experiments are well-designed, demonstrating the method's ability to tackle problems of increasing complexity and showing a clear advantage over a random search baseline.
*   **Impressive Empirical Results:** The framework successfully discovers non-trivial efficient identities for complex expression families, some of which have naive computational complexities that are exponential in the input size. The discovery of polynomial-time equivalents for these expressions is a strong proof of concept for the power of this approach.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is difficult to follow in places, particularly in the experimental section (7.2). The descriptions of the five families of expressions are very dense. It would significantly aid the reader if the authors provided more intuition for why these specific families were chosen and what they represent. A clearer, more step-by-step explanation of their construction would improve the paper's readability and allow readers to better appreciate the difficulty of the tasks.
*   **Significance and Practicality:** The main weakness of the paper is its limited demonstrated significance. The problem of finding efficient identities for the specific, and seemingly contrived, families of expressions feels somewhat disconnected from pressing, real-world problems. While the authors suggest potential applications (e.g., integration into compilers, simplifying RBM partition functions), these connections remain speculative. The paper would be much more impactful if it could demonstrate its utility on a well-known, difficult expression from a mainstream application area or provide a more concrete roadmap for how this framework could be integrated into practical tools. As it stands, it is unclear whether this is a general-purpose tool or one that works only on a narrow set of problems.
*   **Effectiveness of the RNN:** According to the results in Figure 3, the simpler n-gram models (particularly 3-, 4-, and 5-grams) consistently outperform the more complex RNN model on most tasks. While the RNN's ability to learn a representation is interesting, its inferior performance as a search strategy raises questions about its practical advantage for this specific problem. The paper would benefit from a deeper analysis of why the simpler model succeeds and where the more powerful RNN might be expected to have an edge.

In summary, this is a highly original and thought-provoking paper that introduces a promising new direction for applying machine learning to symbolic mathematics. The technical contributions, especially the learning of continuous representations for expressions, are significant. However, the paper is held back by clarity issues in the experimental section and, more importantly, by the uncertain practical significance of the specific problem it solves. While the work is of high quality and originality, its overall impact is currently limited by the contrived nature of the examples.
