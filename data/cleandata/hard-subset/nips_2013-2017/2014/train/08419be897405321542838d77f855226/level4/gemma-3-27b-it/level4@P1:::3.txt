**Summary:**

The paper explores the use of machine learning to discover efficient mathematical identities, framing the problem as a search through an attribute grammar. The authors introduce a novel grammar framework and demonstrate that learning strategies, specifically n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN), can guide this search, enabling the discovery of identities beyond the reach of brute-force or manual derivation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for this specific problem is novel. The application of a recursive neural network to learn a continuous representation of mathematical structures is also a significant contribution.
*   **Demonstrated Success:** The paper demonstrates the ability to discover non-trivial identities, including those offering significant computational reductions (e.g., O(n<sup>3</sup>) to O(n<sup>2</sup>)). These identities, as claimed, are often not readily apparent and difficult to find manually.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The problem is well-defined, and the constraints (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix variable) are clearly stated, allowing for a manageable search space.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments cover several families of expressions and compare different search strategies, providing a solid evaluation of the approach.
*   **Availability of Code and Data:** The release of code and evaluation data promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Restricted Scope:** The problem is restricted to a specific class of expressions (homogeneous polynomials). While understandable for initial exploration, this limits the immediate applicability of the method.
*   **Lack of Clarity in Certain Explanations:** Some explanations, such as the description of the weight tensor in the RNN, are not clear and could benefit from more detail. The proposal generation within the search process needs a more detailed explanation.
*   **RNN Performance:** The n-gram models consistently outperform the RNN in the experiments. This raises questions about the effectiveness of the RNN in this context and warrants further investigation. The reasons for this discrepancy are not fully explored.
*   **Limited Generalization:** While the RNN learns a representation, its performance is limited, especially for complex expressions (RBM-2 family).
*   **Dependence on Grammar:** The efficiency of the method is heavily dependent on the design of the attribute grammar.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a challenging problem with a novel and promising approach.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in discovering efficient identities.
*   The code and data release facilitates reproducibility and further research.
*   The work has the potential to impact areas such as symbolic computation, compiler optimization, and machine learning for mathematical reasoning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The restricted scope of the problem limits the immediate applicability of the method.
*   The RNN performance is underwhelming compared to the n-gram models.
*   Some explanations lack clarity and could be improved.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. While the scope is restricted, the novelty of the approach, the demonstrated success in discovering efficient identities, and the availability of code and data make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. The weaknesses are not severe enough to warrant rejection, but addressing them in a revised version would strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, focusing on clarifying the explanations, further analyzing the RNN performance, and discussing potential directions for expanding the scope of the method.
