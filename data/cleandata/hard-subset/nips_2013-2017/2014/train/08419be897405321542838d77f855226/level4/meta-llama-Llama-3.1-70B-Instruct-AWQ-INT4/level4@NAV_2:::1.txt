This paper explores how machine learning techniques can be applied to the discovery of efficient mathematical identities. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and propose two novel learning approaches to guide the search for efficient identities. The first approach uses an n-gram model, while the second utilizes a recursive neural network (RNN). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their framework by discovering complex identities that are beyond the reach of brute-force search or human derivation.

The paper relates to previous work in theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming. However, the authors limit their domain to the computation of polynomials with fixed degree k, avoiding issues of undecidability and the halting problem. The use of attribute grammars is inspired by their application in compiler construction, but the authors are not aware of any previous work using grammars for discovering mathematical formulas.

The paper's main contributions include a novel grammar framework for finding efficient versions of symbolic expressions, the integration of machine learning techniques into this framework, and the discovery of many new mathematical identities that offer significant reductions in computational complexity.

The authors evaluate their framework using five different families of expressions, chosen to fall within the scope of their grammar rules. They apply three different search strategies: a random strategy, an n-gram model, and an RNN. The results show that the learning approaches generally perform better than the random strategy for large values of k, with the n-gram models outperforming the RNN for certain families of expressions.

Strengths of the paper include its novelty, as it applies machine learning techniques to a new domain, and its potential impact, as the discovered identities could be used to improve the efficiency of various computational tasks. However, the paper's weaknesses include its limited evaluation, as the authors only consider a small set of expressions, and its lack of clear scalability, as it is unclear how the approach would perform on more complex expressions.

Arguments for acceptance include the paper's novelty, its potential impact, and its well-written presentation. Arguments against acceptance include the limited evaluation, the lack of clear scalability, and the fact that the approach may not be necessary for the specific expressions considered.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their framework and results. However, the paper's clarity could be improved by providing more details on the implementation of the RNN and the n-gram model. Additionally, the authors could benefit from a more thorough evaluation of their framework, considering a wider range of expressions and comparing their approach to other methods.

Overall, while the paper is interesting and novel, its limited evaluation and lack of clear scalability make it a borderline case for acceptance.
