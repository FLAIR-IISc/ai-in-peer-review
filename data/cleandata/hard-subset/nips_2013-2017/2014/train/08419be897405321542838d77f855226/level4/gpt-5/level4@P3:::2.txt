Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes learning-guided search methods to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities that are equivalent to a target symbolic expression but cheaper to evaluate. The core representation is an attribute grammar over matrix/vector operations, where each rule carries a computational cost. Expressions are built as trees of grammar rules up to a specified degree k. Because exhaustive search over trees is exponential and quickly intractable, the authors introduce two learned strategies to guide the search:

- n-gram strategy: counts the frequency of subtrees (up to depth n) seen in solutions to simpler targets and biases the search toward frequently occurring substructures. This is analogous to n-gram language models in NLP.

- RNN strategy: learns a continuous embedding φ(S) of expression trees via a recursive neural network trained to classify expressions into equivalence classes (identified using randomized numerical evaluation modulo a large prime). A softmax atop φ predicts the next rule to apply when combining subtrees during search. Training uses curriculum learning, identity-based initialization, and dropout.

To match a target expression, the system builds a set of candidate trees and (crucially) allows linear combinations of tree outputs, solved via modular Gaussian elimination against the target’s descriptor vector. Experiments evaluate five families of scalar-output expressions (e.g., sums of powers of AAT, elementwise-structured variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, and sums over binary vectors akin to RBM partition terms). With a 10-minute per-k cutoff and curriculum over k, the n-gram models generally outperform the RNN, reliably discovering efficient identities in some families (e.g., reducing O(n3) to O(n2) or, for exponential sums, to polynomial in limited cases). The work is positioned relative to theorem proving, program induction, probabilistic programming, and symbolic algebra systems (Maple/Mathematica), emphasizing that those systems simplify but do not explicitly target complexity reduction, and do not learn heuristics.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation: given a grammar and a target expression, find an equivalent but cheaper expression; the attribute grammar framework makes the search space explicit and cost-aware.
- Methodological novelty: learning continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions and using them to guide program search; simple, effective n-gram baselines; modular arithmetic descriptors to robustly test equivalence.
- Solid engineering: practical details (identity initialization, ReLUs, curriculum, dropout) are convincingly motivated and empirically useful; the linear-combination-of-trees approach expands the reachable solution space.
- Evidence of nontrivial discoveries: examples that transform O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)) and repeated-pattern reductions in powers of (A*B) show the approach can recover identities beyond brute force; code and data are released.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Significance and scope: The problem setting is restrictive (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, primarily single-variable A) and several “wins” rely on removing matrix-matrix multiply from the grammar to force O(n2) solutions. It remains unclear how often such identities arise in practice or whether the approach scales to broader classes of symbolic problems. Consider expanding at least one family to multiple variables in the main text and demonstrating an identity that would be practically relevant in ML or numerical computing, plus real runtime speedups.
- Baselines: The comparison set is limited (random vs n-gram vs RNN). Please compare to:
  - Strong non-learned search baselines (beam search, MCTS, A* with cost heuristics).
  - Symbolic algebra systems with complexity-aware simplification (e.g., Mathematica’s ComplexityFunction or user-defined rewrite systems), even if they do not optimize complexity by default.
- RNN underperformance: n-grams outperform the RNN on most families (Fig. 3), suggesting the current embedding does not capture the necessary structural regularities. An ablation showing the impact of curriculum, identity initialization, and dropout on search success would clarify where the bottleneck is. Also consider more structure-aware models (e.g., different composition functions per operator, gated/compositional variants) and report calibration/time-to-solution, not just success fraction.
- Equivalence testing: The modular evaluation approach is elegant, but the paper should quantify the probability of collision (false equality) given N and prime p, and state the actual N and p used. A short lemma or reference (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel-style bounds) would improve rigor. Also clarify how many descriptor samples are needed for the linear system in Section 5.
- Experimental reporting: 
  - Provide operation counts and asymptotic complexities for discovered identities in the main paper (not only the supplement), and measure empirical wall-clock speedups on numeric instances as n grows.
  - Report time distributions (median and quartiles) to solution under each strategy, not just success rate, and include the number of trees explored.
  - Clarify whether training and testing for the RNN use the same families (curriculum over k within a family) and evaluate cross-family generalization (train on some families, test on others).
- Claims of “new” identities and ML relevance: The paper asserts discovery of many new identities and suggests potential for closed-form dropout/RBM partition computations. These are intriguing but speculative; temper the claims or provide concrete, vetted identities with proofs or independent verification. For ML relevance, demonstrate an end-to-end application (e.g., approximate RBM partition term with learned identity and show accuracy/speed trade-offs).

Comments by review criterion:
- Quality: Technically sound overall, with thoughtful design choices and convincing small-scale demonstrations. However, the evaluation lacks stronger baselines and more comprehensive measurements of efficiency and generalization.
- Clarity: Generally readable, but Section 7.2 is dense. Suggestions: move lengthy family definitions to an appendix while keeping more worked examples in the main text; unify notation (A’, AT), fix formatting (e.g., O(()n3)), and provide a concise table of grammar rules with costs and constraints.
- Originality: High. The integration of attribute grammars, modular descriptors, and learned guidance (especially continuous embeddings of symbolic expressions) is creative and, to my knowledge, novel in the NeurIPS literature of that time.
- Significance: Currently limited. The approach is promising as a research direction in learning-guided program synthesis and symbolic reasoning, but the demonstrated practical impact is narrow, and the RNN method underperforms strong but simple n-gram heuristics.

Pros for acceptance:
- Innovative framing and techniques at the intersection of ML and symbolic computation.
- Public code/data; reproducibility.
- Demonstrates that learning can materially extend brute-force search in a challenging discrete space.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited scope and practical relevance; several results rely on constrained grammars.
- Weak empirical comparisons and underwhelming performance of the main neural method.
- Missing probability bounds and detailed verification for modular equivalence matching.

Overall: Interesting and original work with solid ideas and some compelling examples, but with questions about practical significance and incomplete evaluation. I lean against acceptance in its current form, but I would be enthusiastic about a revision addressing the baseline, evaluation, and clarity issues above.
