This paper presents an intriguing approach to discovering more efficient algebraic identities using distributed, recursive neural representations. The successful classification of these identities using this framework is a noteworthy contribution. However, several aspects of the work require clarification and further development.

The primary limitation lies in the restricted nature of the problem definition. While focusing on a specific domain allows for in-depth exploration, the generalizability of the proposed method to broader algebraic structures remains unclear. A discussion of these limitations and potential avenues for expansion would be beneficial.

A key point of confusion arises in the description of the network architecture. The text states, "weight tensor W3 (of size lxlxl, so that the output is also size l)." It is unclear whether this refers to the utilization of a neural tensor network (NTN) rather than a standard linear network. Given Bowman’s earlier work demonstrating the superior performance of NTNs in similar tasks compared to RNNs, explicitly stating the network type and justifying this architectural choice is crucial. The connection to Bowman’s findings should be made more explicit.

Section 7.2, “Efficient Identity Discovery,” lacks clarity regarding the classification mechanism. It’s unclear whether the RNN is still employed for classification while evaluating different search strategies. Further explanation is needed to delineate the roles of the neural network and the search algorithm within this section.

The process of generating proposals during the search phase is also poorly explained. A more detailed description of the proposal generation methodology, perhaps with a concrete example, would significantly improve understanding. How are potential identities sampled or constructed? What criteria are used to guide the search?

The paper contains several minor errors that detract from its polish. These include: "has contains a single matrix," should be corrected to “contains a single matrix”; “listed in table Table 1” should be “listed in Table 1”; “vector is the presented to the next operation” should be “vector is presented to the next operation”; “The final vector is pass to a softmax classifier” should be “The final vector is passed to a softmax classifier”; “as to how explore space of possible” should be “as to how to explore the space of possible”; “The scheduler is is fixed” should be “The scheduler is fixed”; and “The difficulty of the task by be appreciated by looking” should be “The difficulty of the task can be appreciated by looking.” 

The difficulty of the task and the benefits of the proposed approach are not fully conveyed. Providing comparative results against existing methods, even if limited to the scope of this specific problem, would strengthen the paper’s impact. The statement "which can easily captured with" is vague and requires further elaboration.

Finally, Figure 3 appears to incorrectly label the network as a “TNN” when, based on the description, it appears to be an RNN. This should be corrected.

Overall, the paper presents a promising direction for research. Addressing the points raised above, particularly clarifying the network architecture and the search procedure, will substantially enhance its clarity and impact. A more thorough discussion of the limitations and potential for generalization would also be valuable.
