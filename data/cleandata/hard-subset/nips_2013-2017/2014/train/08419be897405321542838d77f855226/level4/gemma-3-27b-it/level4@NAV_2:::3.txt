This paper introduces an interesting solution to finding more efficient algebraic identities. It is very interesting to see that distributed, recursive neural representations can successfully classify these identities.

The problem definition seems quite restricted, though the authors acknowledge this and discuss potential avenues for generalization. The paper is well-motivated, framing the problem within the context of symbolic computation and limitations of existing approaches like theorem proving and symbolic computation engines. The use of attribute grammars provides a structured framework for exploring the space of possible identities.

The technical soundness of the work appears strong. The approach of combining machine learning with a grammar-based search is novel and promising. The numerical and learned representations for symbolic expressions are well-explained, and the results in Section 7.1 demonstrate the effectiveness of the RNN-based representation for expression classification. The experimental setup seems reasonable, with clear comparisons between different search strategies and ablations. The authors are careful to evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, and the code and data availability is excellent. 

However, certain aspects could benefit from further clarification. For example, the description of the “weight tensor W3 (of size lxlxl, so that the output is also size l)” is unclear. Does this mean you use a neural tensor network instead of a simple linear network? Bowman [3] showed that tensor networks often perform better in these types of tasks. Furthermore, the explanation of how proposals are generated during the search process (Section 6) could be more detailed. It is unclear how the learning strategies guide the tree search beyond simply assigning scores to grammar rules. 

There are a few minor issues with writing and presentation. For instance, the phrase "has contains a single matrix" appears in the text. Similarly, there is a repetition of “Table” (“listed in table Table 1.”). There are a few other small errors like “vector is the presented to the next operation” and “The final vector is pass to a softmax classifier”, and "as to how explore space of possible". The scheduler is fixed, but it is written as "The scheduler is is fixed". Additionally, the difficulty of the task can be appreciated by looking, rather than “by be appreciated by looking”. Finally, Figure 3 appears to refer to TNN, but the paper focuses on RNN.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of machine learning to symbolic identity discovery.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach, particularly for certain families of expressions.
*   Well-defined problem and clear presentation of the method.
*   Availability of code and data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The problem is restricted to homogeneous polynomials, limiting the broader applicability of the method.
*   Some aspects of the approach (e.g., the search strategy) could be more clearly explained.
*   The RNN-based approach is not consistently superior to simpler methods like n-grams.
*   The RBM-2 family remains challenging, suggesting limitations in the current approach.

**Overall:**

This is a solid paper that presents a novel and promising approach to a challenging problem. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS conference.
