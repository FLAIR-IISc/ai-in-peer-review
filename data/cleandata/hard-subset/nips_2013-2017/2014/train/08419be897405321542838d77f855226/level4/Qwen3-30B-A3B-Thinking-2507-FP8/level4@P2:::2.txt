

This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors propose an attribute grammar-based approach to represent symbolic expressions, where the goal is to find alternative expressions that compute the same result as a target but with lower computational complexity. To address the exponential growth of the search space, they introduce two learning-based strategies: an n-gram model that captures patterns in successful solutions for simpler expressions, and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous representations of symbolic expressions to guide the search. The paper demonstrates their approach on several families of mathematical expressions, showing that their methods can discover identities beyond the reach of brute-force search or manual derivation.

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) a grammar framework for symbolic expression search, (2) integration of learning techniques to guide the search process, (3) novel application of RNNs to learn continuous representations of mathematical structures, and (4) discovery of new mathematical identities with significant computational improvements.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments. The authors provide a clear problem formulation, properly evaluate their methods against a random search baseline, and include thorough ablation studies (comparing n-gram models of different orders and RNN variants). The experimental setup is reasonable, with appropriate attention to numerical representation and solution verification. The paper demonstrates strong technical execution, with the RNN achieving up to 95% classification accuracy on symbolic expressions (Table 2), confirming the effectiveness of their learned representation. The methods are well-motivated and appropriately applied to the problem.

Clarity: The paper would benefit from improved organization, particularly in Sections 4 and 7.2. The technical details of the attribute grammar (Section 3) and the learned representation (Section 4) are somewhat difficult to follow without more concrete examples. Section 7.2, which describes the experimental families, would be clearer with a more structured presentation of the problem setup and results. The authors could improve clarity by providing more visualizations of the discovered identities (beyond the limited examples in the paper) and by better explaining the relationship between the grammar rules and the discovered identities. The supplementary material appears to contain valuable examples that could be referenced more explicitly in the main text.

Originality: The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by applying machine learning techniques to the problem of discovering mathematical identities. While attribute grammars have been used in other domains (e.g., compiler construction, bioinformatics), their application to discovering mathematical identities with learning-based search strategies is new. The use of n-grams over symbolic operations and the adaptation of recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of mathematical structures are particularly innovative. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from related work in theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation engines, which typically rely on hand-designed heuristics rather than learning.

Significance: The significance of this work is stronger than the draft review suggests. The paper demonstrates how their framework can discover identities with practical implications for machine learning applications. For instance, the (RBM-1)k and (RBM-2)k families relate directly to computing partition functions for Restricted Boltzmann Machines, a problem with significant practical importance in machine learning. The identities discovered could potentially lead to closed-form versions of dropout or efficient computation of RBM partition functions. More broadly, the framework represents a step toward general-purpose symbolic computation tools that could be integrated into compilers or optimization frameworks, potentially benefiting a wide range of computational tasks. The paper's approach to learning search strategies for combinatorial problems has broader relevance to AI, where combinatorial explosions frequently occur.

Pro/Con Acceptance:
Pro:
- Novel integration of machine learning techniques with symbolic computation
- Demonstrated success in discovering previously unknown mathematical identities
- Strong technical execution with proper evaluation
- Clear potential for practical applications in machine learning
- Contribution to the broader AI problem of guiding search in combinatorially large spaces

Con:
- Some sections (particularly 4 and 7.2) would benefit from improved clarity
- The problem scope is somewhat restricted to homogeneous polynomials, though the authors discuss generalization potential
- The practical impact, while promising, requires further validation in real-world machine learning applications

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The framework is well-designed, the experiments are thorough, and the results are compelling. The authors have successfully demonstrated how machine learning can be used to guide search in combinatorially complex symbolic spaces, with potential applications in computational mathematics and machine learning. The significance of the work is substantial, particularly for applications involving efficient computation of mathematical expressions in machine learning contexts. I recommend acceptance.
