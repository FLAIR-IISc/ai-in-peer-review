

This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors propose an attribute grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions and demonstrate how learning from simpler expressions can guide the search for complex identities that would be intractable via brute-force enumeration. Specifically, they develop two learning approaches: an n-gram model that leverages frequent subexpression patterns, and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous representations of symbolic trees to predict optimal grammar rule combinations. The framework successfully discovers identities with significant complexity reductions (e.g., from O(n³) to O(n²) for matrix expressions), which the authors claim are previously unknown. The work bridges symbolic computation with machine learning, applying techniques from NLP (like n-grams and RNNs) to mathematical discovery, and contributes new embeddings for symbolic structures.

**Quality**: The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The experimental design is rigorous, with controlled comparisons across search strategies (random, n-gram, RNN) on five distinct expression families. The use of integer-based descriptors (Zp) to avoid numerical instability in equivalence checks is well-justified, and the RNN training details (curriculum learning, identity initialization, dropout) address critical overfitting concerns. The classification accuracy (≤5% error for k=3–6) validates the representation learning. The framework’s ability to discover identities beyond brute-force reach (e.g., Example 2 for k=6) is convincingly supported by results. However, the paper could better address potential limitations—e.g., the scope of expressions is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, and the grammar excludes matrix-multiply operations for efficiency guarantees in some cases. The claim that discovered identities are "previously undiscovered" would be strengthened by explicit comparison to existing literature on similar expressions (e.g., the Symk family, which is well-studied in combinatorics [24]).

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-structured but suffers from minor clarity issues in Section 7.2 (expression families). The description of the (RBM-2)ᵏ family, for instance, uses dense notation without sufficient contextualization (e.g., "v and h are binary n-vectors" without explaining their role in the RBM context). A brief example of the target expression for k=2 would help readers grasp the problem. The transition from grammar rules (Table 1) to the search strategy (Section 6) could be smoother; explicitly stating *why* the grammar rules enable complexity control (e.g., "removing matrix-multiply ensures O(n²) solutions") would clarify the design rationale. The supplementary material references are helpful but could be integrated more explicitly into the main text.

**Originality**: This work is highly original. While attribute grammars have been used in compiler design [14] and algorithm optimization [19,26], their application to *discovering* mathematical identities—combined with learning-based search strategies—represents a novel contribution. The paper correctly situates itself against related work in theorem proving [5,9,11], program induction [18,28], and symbolic computation tools like Mathematica [27], emphasizing that prior systems lack learning-based search. The use of RNNs for symbolic expression embeddings (paralleling word embeddings [8,16]) is also a fresh application. The authors convincingly argue that their work is distinct from Bridge et al. [4] (which uses learning to select heuristics, not discover identities) and from NLP grammar applications [15,22,23] (which operate on linguistic, not mathematical, structures).

**Significance**: The significance claim requires careful consideration. While the framework demonstrates a technically impressive method for discovering novel identities, the practical utility for real-world computational tasks is not sufficiently justified. The paper notes potential applications (e.g., optimizing dropout or RBM partition functions), but these remain speculative. Current computational workflows rarely require *discovering* identities via this framework—practitioners typically leverage known identities (e.g., for matrix multiplication) or use symbolic tools like Mathematica for simplification [27]. The problem formulation focuses on *finding* identities, not *applying* them; thus, the primary contribution is methodological rather than directly impactful for practitioners. The authors could strengthen significance by: (1) quantifying the potential speedup in specific ML tasks (e.g., "Our identity for RBM-2ᵏ reduces partition function computation from O(2²ⁿ) to O(n³), enabling RBM training on datasets with n > 100"); (2) comparing their discovered identities to known ones (e.g., "Our O(n²) identity for ∑ᵢⱼ(AAᵀ)ᵏ matches existing results for k=2 but extends to k=6"); or (3) demonstrating integration with an existing ML system (e.g., as a compiler optimization pass).

**Overall Assessment**: This is a technically strong paper with high novelty and clear methodological contributions. The learning-based search strategy effectively extends the scope of discoverable identities beyond brute-force limits. However, the significance is currently overstated relative to practical computational needs. The authors should refine their discussion to clarify *when* this approach offers tangible advantages over existing symbolic computation tools or known identities. The experimental results are convincing, but the narrative should better align the technical contribution with concrete applications to justify broader impact.

**Recommendation**: **Weak Accept**. The work is a valuable technical contribution to the intersection of symbolic computation and machine learning, with clear potential for future applications. With minor revisions to strengthen the significance discussion and improve clarity in Section 7.2, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The current version is strong but could better articulate why this method matters to the broader ML community.
