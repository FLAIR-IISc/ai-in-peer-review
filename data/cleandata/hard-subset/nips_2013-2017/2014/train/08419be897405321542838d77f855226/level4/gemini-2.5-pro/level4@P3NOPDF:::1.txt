This paper proposes a novel application of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) to the task of discovering mathematical identities. The core idea is to train a network to determine the equivalence of symbolic expressions, thereby identifying new relationships. The primary strength of this work lies in its novelty. Applying modern machine learning techniques to the domain of symbolic mathematics is an intriguing and potentially fruitful research direction, and the authors are commended for exploring this unconventional application.

However, while the direction is interesting, the current work feels preliminary and does not yet meet the threshold for publication at this venue. The main concerns are detailed below:

1.  **Limited Empirical Performance on a Constrained Problem:** The method's performance in discovering identities is not sufficiently compelling. This is particularly concerning given that the evaluation was conducted on a very limited and highly structured set of input expressions. For such a constrained domain, one would expect a specialized model to achieve very high accuracy. The modest results presented undermine the central claim of the paper's effectiveness.

2.  **Questionable Necessity of the Approach:** The simplicity of the problem space raises questions about the suitability of using a DNN. For the narrow class of expressions considered, it seems highly probable that a simpler, more direct algorithmic or rule-based method could be designed to discover these identities with greater accuracy, efficiency, and interpretability. The current application feels akin to using a sledgehammer to crack a nut; it does not convincingly demonstrate a scenario where a learning-based approach is necessary or superior to traditional symbolic manipulation.

3.  **Unsubstantiated Claims of Generalizability:** A key motivation for this work is the potential for such a method to scale to more complex and diverse mathematical domains where identities are not easily found. However, the paper provides little evidence to support the claim that the proposed approach would generalize effectively. It is not clear how the current architecture and representation would handle a richer set of mathematical operators, nested functions, or fundamentally different algebraic structures. The lackluster performance on a simple case does not inspire confidence in the method's potential to tackle more challenging, open-ended discovery tasks, which would be the true test of its value.

In conclusion, while the paper introduces a fascinating idea, the execution and evaluation are not yet mature enough for a top-tier publication. The work in its current form is more of a proof-of-concept on a toy problem than a demonstration of a powerful new technique. For this paper to be compelling, the authors would need to either: a) demonstrate overwhelmingly strong performance on the current problem, making a case for the DNN's surprising effectiveness, or b) apply the method to a more complex and meaningful class of expressions where traditional methods fall short, thereby clearly motivating the need for a machine learning solution.
