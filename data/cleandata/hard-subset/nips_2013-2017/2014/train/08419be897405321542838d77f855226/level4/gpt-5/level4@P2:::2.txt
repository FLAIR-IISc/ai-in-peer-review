Summary
The paper studies how to automatically discover algebraically equivalent but computationally cheaper expressions. It formulates the search over expressions using an attribute grammar of matrix/vector operators and evaluates whether a candidate equals a target via descriptors computed over random integer inputs modulo a large prime. Because the space of trees grows exponentially, the authors propose learning-guided search strategies trained on solutions to simpler targets: (1) an n-gram model over subtrees, and (2) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees and, on top, a softmax to predict the next composition rule. The system also solves for linear combinations of multiple trees to match targets. Experiments on several expression families (e.g., powers of sums of AAT, elementwise variants, symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related sums) show that learned strategies find identities unreachable by random search under a fixed time budget. In most settings, higher-order n-grams outperform the RNN, though the RNN can be faster on some instances. The paper also reports high accuracy for an RNN classifier that clusters expressions into equivalence classes, suggesting the learned embeddings capture algebraic structure.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned between theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, but focuses on fixed-degree polynomial-like expressions to avoid undecidability. Unlike computer algebra systems that simplify via rewrite rules, this framework explicitly optimizes for computational cost and learns search heuristics from data rather than hand-crafting them. It borrows ideas from NLP (n-grams; recursive networks for tree structures) and from grammar-based optimization.

Strengths
- Clear, novel problem formulation: learning to guide algebraic search toward lower-cost identities within an attribute-grammar framework.
- Technically interesting components: numerical modulo-prime descriptors to test equivalence efficiently; linear combinations of trees; curriculum learning for RNN training; and practical training tricks reported in detail.
- Empirical evidence that learning improves search: on multiple families, n-grams and, to a lesser extent, the RNN substantially outperform random search as complexity grows.
- Potentially broad impact of learned continuous representations of symbolic expressions, which could enable other ML methods in symbolic domains.
- Code availability and a collection of discovered identities increase reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Practical significance is not convincingly demonstrated. The paper argues possible applications (e.g., dropout-like sums, RBM partition function terms), but does not show end-to-end wall-clock speedups on real workloads or compiler-integrated use cases.
- Scope is restricted: homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, primarily single-variable targets, and a limited operator set. It is unclear how well the approach scales to richer grammars or non-polynomial operators.
- Evaluation methodology relies on “fraction of successful runs within 10 minutes.” While this reflects search difficulty, it does not quantify resulting complexity savings versus tuned baselines or report runtime reductions in practice.
- Comparisons are limited. Stronger baselines could include: (i) computer algebra systems with cost-aware rewrite strategies; (ii) heuristic or evolutionary program search; and (iii) non-learned but informed grammar-guided search.
- Claims of “previously undiscovered” identities would benefit from verification via CAS proof or citations; modulo-prime equality checks, while practical, do not constitute formal proofs.
- The RNN strategy underperforms n-grams on most families; further analysis of failure modes and architectural choices (e.g., attention over subtrees, better compositional operators) would strengthen the case for learned embeddings as a search policy.

Comments with respect to review criteria
- Quality: Generally solid. The attribute-grammar framework, modulo-prime descriptors, and learning strategies are carefully described, and experiments are thorough within the chosen scope. However, formal verification of discovered identities and stronger baseline comparisons are missing.
- Clarity: Mostly clear, but Section 7.2 is dense. Consider restructuring the description of expression families, making the cost model explicit, moving more identities into the main text, and clarifying the scheduler/strategy interface with a concise algorithmic summary.
- Originality: High. Learning-guided search for algebraic efficiency identities with continuous embeddings of symbolic expressions is a fresh angle that bridges ML and symbolic computation.
- Significance: Currently moderate to low. The methods are interesting, but the paper stops short of demonstrating practical impact. Showing real-world speedups, broader operators, or integration with compilers/autodiff would substantially raise significance.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel and well-executed formulation of a challenging search problem.
- Demonstrated benefit of learned heuristics over random search.
- Methodological contributions (mod p descriptors; learned embeddings for trees).
- Relevance to the growing intersection of ML and symbolic reasoning.

Cons:
- Limited evidence of practical utility; no end-to-end speedups.
- Narrow problem scope and operator set.
- Weaker-than-expected performance of the RNN policy versus simple n-grams.
- Lack of strong baselines and formal verification of identities.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper is original and technically competent, but its practical significance is not yet established, and the evaluation lacks strong baselines and formal validation. With clearer exposition, stronger comparisons, formal proofs (or CAS verification), and demonstrations of real-world computational gains or broader applicability, this work could become a compelling contribution.
