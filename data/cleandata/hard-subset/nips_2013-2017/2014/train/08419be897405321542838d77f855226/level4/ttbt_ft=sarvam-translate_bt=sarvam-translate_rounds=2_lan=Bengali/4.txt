This paper proposes a new practical application of neural nets, which involves learning representations for symbolic expressions and finding their mathematical equivalents.

Although the expressions discussed here are very limited, and there is no evidence that they can be extended to more complex expressions, I found this paper interesting. And I think it could have a positive impact on symbolic software libraries.

- The problem description states that we are looking for expressions with low computational complexity. But the algorithm does not impose such a limitation anywhere. I understand that it is not easy, but have the authors taught about possible solutions to limit the search space for such solutions?
- Regarding the restrictions mentioned in section 2, is scheduling the only limitation for more complex mathematical expressions? I would have liked to see more formal or empirical tests for determining these restrictions.
- Also, the structure of the paper is not easy to follow. It would have been better if the stages were explained more clearly.
- Are there any limitations for vector/matrix sizes? For example, the expression for RBM-1 for k=1, n=1 does not seem valid.
