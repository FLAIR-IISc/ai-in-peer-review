This paper introduces a novel application of neural networks for learning representations of symbolic expressions and discovering their mathematical identities. While the scope of expressions studied is currently limited, and the authors do not provide a proof for extensibility to more complex forms, the approach is nonetheless interesting and novel. The work could have a significant impact on symbolic software libraries.

However, there are several points that could be clarified or improved:

*   The problem statement specifies a search for expressions with lower computational complexity. The proposed algorithm, however, does not appear to explicitly enforce this constraint. While acknowledging that this is a non-trivial challenge, have the authors considered potential methods to constrain the search space to such solutions?
*   Regarding the restrictions mentioned in Section 2, is the scheduler the only factor limiting the complexity of the mathematical expressions that can be handled? The paper would be strengthened by a more formal justification or empirical evidence for the chosen restrictions.
*   The structure of the paper is not always easy to follow. The clarity could be improved by explaining the different stages of the proposed method more distinctly.
*   Is there a constraint on the size of the vector/matrix representations? For instance, the expression for RBM-1 in the supplementary material does not seem to be valid for the case k=1, n=1.
