

This paper applies deep neural networks (DNNs) to discover mathematical identities, representing a novel application domain. While the conceptual novelty is acknowledged, the work appears preliminary and does not sufficiently advance the field to meet NeurIPS publication standards. The primary limitation lies in the method's modest performance and the highly constrained scope of the problem. The identities investigated were derived from an extremely limited input space (e.g., small sets of elementary expressions), where manual derivation of patterns would have been straightforward and more efficient than deploying DNNs. This approach resembles using a sledgehammer to crack a nut: the complexity of the model is disproportionate to the simplicity of the problem domain.  

Crucially, the paper fails to demonstrate compelling results that justify the DNN framework. The reported performance gains—relative to trivial baselines—are marginal, and the model’s success hinges on the narrow input set rather than robust mathematical discovery. For instance, the identities identified (e.g., simple polynomial relationships) could likely be derived through systematic algebraic analysis without machine learning. The authors acknowledge potential scalability but provide no concrete evidence or theoretical justification for how the approach would generalize to broader or more complex expression spaces. Given the current results, it remains unclear whether scaling the method would yield meaningful improvements or even be theoretically justified.  

The paper’s contribution feels more like a proof-of-concept than a substantial technical advance. NeurIPS typically prioritizes work that demonstrates significant methodological innovation, scalability, or transformative impact. Here, the DNN application does not clearly outperform simpler, domain-specific techniques, nor does it establish a foundation for wider applicability. The lack of ablation studies, comparisons to non-DNN heuristics, or analysis of failure modes further weakens the argument for the model’s necessity. While the problem space is interesting, the execution does not sufficiently exploit the capabilities of deep learning to justify its use.  

I acknowledge the paper’s potential as a starting point for future work, but as presented, it falls short of NeurIPS’ threshold. The core idea—applying DNNs to mathematical discovery—is intriguing, yet the current implementation lacks the rigor, depth, or empirical evidence required for publication. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the technical contribution, such as by: (1) demonstrating performance on larger, more complex identity sets where manual methods fail; (2) providing a clear theoretical argument for why DNNs are uniquely suited; or (3) showing scalability to meaningful generalizations. Without these, the work remains an incremental step rather than a significant advance.  

In summary, the paper addresses an interesting problem but does not deliver a compelling solution. The methodology is underdeveloped for the scope, and the results do not justify the chosen approach. While I commend the authors for exploring a novel application, the current version lacks the technical substance expected for NeurIPS. I recommend rejection unless the authors substantially revise the paper to address these gaps and demonstrate a clearer path to broader impact.
