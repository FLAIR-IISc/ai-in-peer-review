- Core idea: Represent symbolic expressions with an attribute grammar and search for analytically equivalent, lower-cost expression trees.  
- Method: Use ML to guide search by training on solutions for simpler expressions; evaluate an n-gram model over grammar rules and a recursive neural network (RNN) embedding.  

Strengths:
- High originality/significance: Novel application of ML (including deep learning) to symbolic mathematics and formula discovery.  
- Learned representation: RNN-based continuous embeddings for expressions are a notable technical contribution with high classification accuracy.  
- Empirical validation: Learning-guided search (n-gram and RNN) substantially outperforms random search and finds complex identities (e.g., high-degree polynomials) where random fails.

Weaknesses:
- Limited evaluation scope: Experiments use five highly structured polynomial families, raising concerns about generalization to less regular expressions.  
- RNN underperformance: The RNN often trails much simpler n-gram models as a search strategy; reasons for this are not deeply analyzed.  
- Overkill for some families: Regular, repetitive solutions could likely be generalized by humans, so learning may be unnecessary for those cases; the hardest family yields inconclusive results beyond small k.

Overall assessment:
- Verdict: Interesting, original proof-of-concept with strong potential; novelty and learned representation justify acceptance despite evaluation limits.  
- Quality/clarity: Technically sound, well-supported experiments, and clearly written.  
- Originality/significance: Very high originality and potentially high long-term impact if generalized.
