The paper introduces a novel approach for representing symbolic expressions and finding their mathematical identities. Although it is limited in the expressions it can handle, it is an interesting and novel approach.

The problem statement clearly defines the objective of finding expressions with lower computational complexity. However, the algorithm does not explicitly enforce this constraint. It would be beneficial to discuss potential solutions to limit the search space to such solutions. For instance, the authors could explore incorporating complexity metrics into the search strategy or using pruning techniques to eliminate expressions that exceed a certain complexity threshold.

The restrictions mentioned in Section 2, such as limiting the target expression to homogeneous polynomials of degree k and considering only a single matrix or vector A, seem reasonable. However, it would be helpful to provide more formal or empirical justification for these restrictions. Are there specific challenges or limitations that arise when considering more general mathematical expressions? How do these restrictions impact the applicability of the approach to real-world problems?

The structure of the paper could be improved for better clarity. The authors may consider breaking down the methodology into more distinct stages, providing clear headings and summaries of each section. This would facilitate a smoother reading experience and enable readers to more easily follow the development of the approach.

Regarding the implementation details, it is unclear whether there are constraints on the size of the vector/matrix. For example, the expression for RBM-1 in the supplementary material appears to be invalid for k=1 and n=1. Clarifying these constraints would be beneficial for reproducibility and understanding the limitations of the approach.

Minor typos were found on pages 332 ("show for" instead of "show"), 148 ("has contains" instead of "has"), 289 ("is is" instead of "is"), and 338 ("by be" instead of "by").

Overall, the paper presents a unique and promising approach to representing symbolic expressions and finding mathematical identities. With some revisions to address the mentioned concerns, it has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field.
