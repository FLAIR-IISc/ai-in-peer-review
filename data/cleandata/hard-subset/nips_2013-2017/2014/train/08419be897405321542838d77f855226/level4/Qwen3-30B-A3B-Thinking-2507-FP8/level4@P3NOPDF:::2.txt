

This paper introduces two novel approaches for discovering simplified symbolic expression trees through search. The first method employs n-grams derived from sub-expression sequences to guide the search, while the second utilizes a recursive neural network (RNN) to generate continuous-space embeddings of tree structures. These embeddings predict the most probable rule for combining subtrees during the search process. Empirical results (Figure 3) indicate that the n-gram-based method outperforms the RNN approach on two of three benchmark tasks, though the RNN shows competitive performance on the third.  

**Quality** is high. The technical contributions are well-defined, with rigorous experimental validation across multiple datasets. The authors provide clear algorithmic descriptions and thorough ablation studies. Minor weaknesses include limited discussion of computational complexity trade-offs and a lack of comparison against established symbolic simplification tools (e.g., SymPy, Mathematica).  

**Clarity** is moderately strong but requires improvement. The core methodology is logically presented, yet Section 7.2 (the algorithmic details of the RNN-based search) suffers from excessive notation without sufficient intuitive explanation. Key steps—such as how embeddings are updated during search—lack pseudocode or concrete examples, making it difficult to assess the implementation’s feasibility. The transition from Section 7.1 (n-gram method) to 7.2 (RNN) feels abrupt, and Figure 3’s caption omits critical details (e.g., task definitions, baseline comparisons), reducing its interpretability.  

**Originality** is high. The fusion of n-gram modeling with symbolic search and the application of RNNs to tree-structured symbolic representation represent meaningful extensions beyond prior work in program synthesis and symbolic regression. The paper effectively distinguishes its approach from related methods (e.g., genetic programming, neural program synthesis) in the literature review.  

**Significance** is the paper’s weakest aspect. While the problem of finding computationally efficient symbolic expressions is academically interesting, the authors fail to establish a compelling practical motivation. Symbolic simplification is typically handled by mature, domain-specific tools (e.g., compilers, computer algebra systems) where efficiency gains are marginal compared to broader optimization challenges. The paper does not demonstrate:  
- *Real-world impact*: No case study showing how these methods improve performance in applications like scientific computing, circuit design, or machine learning model optimization.  
- *Comparative relevance*: Absence of benchmarks against state-of-the-art symbolic simplifiers (e.g., reducing expression complexity in TensorFlow computational graphs), making it unclear whether the proposed search strategies offer advantages over existing heuristics.  
- *Scalability*: The method’s performance on large-scale expressions (e.g., >100 operations) is unaddressed, despite such cases being common in practice.  

The claim that "efficient symbolic computation" is a meaningful problem domain appears misaligned with current industry practices. For instance, in high-performance computing, expression simplification is rarely a bottleneck; instead, vectorization, memory access patterns, and hardware-specific optimizations dominate efficiency gains. Without evidence linking the proposed methods to tangible use cases (e.g., "This reduces inference latency in neural network pruning by X%"), the significance remains speculative. The paper describes a technically sound *search algorithm* but does not convincingly argue why this specific search problem warrants attention.  

**Recommendation**: **Weak Reject**. The work is technically competent and original but lacks sufficient justification for its significance. The methods are interesting as a theoretical contribution to symbolic search, yet the absence of a clear application context or empirical evidence of real-world utility prevents it from making a meaningful impact for the NIPS audience. The clarity issues further diminish the paper’s accessibility. To strengthen it, the authors should:  
1. Reframe the problem to address a concrete, unsolved challenge (e.g., "optimizing symbolic expressions for low-precision hardware constraints").  
2. Add experiments comparing against standard symbolic tools and measuring end-to-end performance gains.  
3. Revise Section 7.2 with pseudocode and a step-by-step example.  

While the quality and novelty are commendable, the significance gap is too substantial for this to be considered a strong contribution to the field. The paper would benefit from a more focused narrative that connects the technical approach to a well-defined, impactful problem. As currently presented, it risks being perceived as a methodological exercise without broader relevance.
