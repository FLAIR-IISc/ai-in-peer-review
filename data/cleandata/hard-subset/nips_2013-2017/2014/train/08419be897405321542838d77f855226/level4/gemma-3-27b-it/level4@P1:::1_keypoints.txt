- Paper applies machine learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities using an attribute grammar for symbolic expressions.
- Uses n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) to guide tree search and learn from simpler expressions to tackle complex ones.
- Restricts scope to polynomials of fixed degree to avoid undecidability issues present in broader theorem proving/program induction.
- Demonstrates the ability to derive identities beyond manual derivation or brute-force search, including complexity reductions (e.g., O(n^3) to O(n^2)).
- Leverages prior work on attribute grammars and draws inspiration from NLP techniques (n-grams, RNNs).
- Provides publicly available code and evaluation data, aiding reproducibility.
- Learned continuous RNN representation of symbolic expressions is an interesting and potentially generalizable contribution.
- Experimental scope is limited and focused on specific families of expressions, raising generalizability concerns.
- Performance gains from learning-based strategies are sometimes modest, with n-gram models occasionally outperforming the RNN.
- Examples appear somewhat contrived and may not reflect applicability to realistic or complex mathematical problems.
- Success seems to rely on repetitive/structured patterns, leaving scalability to less structured or novel problems unproven.
- The claim that some discovered identities are previously undiscovered is bold and needs a more thorough literature review.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel ML application, demonstrated computational improvements, interesting RNN representation, and public artifacts.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited experiments, modest/uneven gains, unclear scalability, and possibility that simpler rule-based methods could match results.
- Overall assessment: promising proof-of-concept with sound technical work but insufficient scope and impact for a top-tier conference; reviewer leans toward rejection but encourages revision/submission to a workshop or specialized venue.
