This paper explores the application of machine learning techniques to the discovery of efficient mathematical identities. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and propose two novel learning approaches, an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN), to guide the search for efficient identities.

The paper relates to previous work in theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, but focuses on a restricted setting where the target expression is a homogeneous polynomial of degree k. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several families of expressions, including those related to machine learning, such as the partition function of a Restricted Boltzmann Machine (RBM).

The strengths of the paper include its novelty, as it applies machine learning techniques to a new domain, and its potential for broad applicability in symbolic computation. The use of a recursive neural network to learn a continuous representation of mathematical structures is particularly interesting.

However, there are also some weaknesses. The performance of the method in discovering identities is not particularly impressive, even with a limited set of inputs. The expressions used in the experiments are from a restricted set, and it is not clear how easily the approach would scale to more general expressions. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach but do not provide a clear plan for addressing these limitations.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. However, the evaluation of the method is limited, and the authors could benefit from more thorough experimentation and analysis.

Regarding clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear introduction to the problem and their approach. However, some sections, such as the description of the RNN, could be improved with more detail and explanation.

In terms of originality, the paper is novel in its application of machine learning techniques to the discovery of mathematical identities. However, the approach is not entirely new, as it builds on previous work in natural language processing and machine learning.

Regarding significance, the paper has the potential to contribute to the field of symbolic computation and machine learning. However, the impact of the paper is limited by the restricted setting and the lack of impressive results.
