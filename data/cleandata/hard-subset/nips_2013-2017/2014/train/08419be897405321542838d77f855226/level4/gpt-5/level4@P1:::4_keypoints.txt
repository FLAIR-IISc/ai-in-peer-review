- Paper proposes learning-based discovery of efficient mathematical identities using an attribute grammar to represent symbolic expressions.  
- Two learned search strategies are introduced: an n-gram model over subtrees and an RNN that embeds expressions and predicts next grammar rules.  
- Models are trained on solutions to simpler targets and guide search to find cheaper equivalent expressions for more complex targets.  
- Empirical results show successful complexity reductions for matrix expressions, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related sums, outperforming random or exhaustive search.  
- Builds on attribute grammars and borrows n-gram/RNN ideas from NLP; differs from rule-based systems (Maple/Mathematica) by learning heuristics from data.  
- The RNN-based continuous representation of symbolic expressions is a notable and novel contribution.  
- Strength: creative application of ML (especially RNNs) to represent symbolic expressions and guide identity search.  
- Strength: potential impact for symbolic libraries and compilers by automating discovery of efficient formulations.  
- Strength: shows that learning from simpler expressions expands the identities discoverable for harder targets.  
- Weakness: no explicit mechanism shown to enforce or verify lower computational complexity during search.  
- Weakness: restrictive problem scope (homogeneous polynomials, single-variable, scalar outputs) without sufficient justification or ablation.  
- Weakness: paper organization and exposition are confusing; pipeline stages should be presented more clearly.  
- Weakness/question: validity/constraints of the RBM-1 example need clarification (e.g., sizes of vectors/matrices).  
- Evaluation: quality — interesting and generally sound but lacks explicit complexity guarantees; clarity — needs reorganization; originality — high; significance — potential but limited by scope and lack of guarantees.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel framing and method, discovery of nontrivial identities, potential downstream impact.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited scope, no explicit enforcement/proof of lower complexity, unclear exposition and some ambiguous examples.  
- Minor issues/typos noted (e.g., "has contains" → "contains", "is is" → "is", "show for" → "show how to", "by be" → "by").  
- Overall: promising and novel approach to learning symbolic-expression embeddings and discovering efficient identities, but needs clearer exposition, broader scope or justification, and mechanisms to ensure computational efficiency.
