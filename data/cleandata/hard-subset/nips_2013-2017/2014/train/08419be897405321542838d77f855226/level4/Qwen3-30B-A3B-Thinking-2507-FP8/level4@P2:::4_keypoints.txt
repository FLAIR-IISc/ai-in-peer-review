- Presents a novel method for discovering mathematical identities by combining attribute grammars with learned search policies instead of brute-force enumeration.  
- Uses n-gram models and recursive neural networks to learn from simpler expressions and guide search toward efficient representations.  
- Demonstrates effectiveness on matrix operations and polynomial expressions, finding identities beyond brute-force capabilities.  
- Introduces integer-based numerical descriptors to avoid floating-point comparison issues.  
- Experimental results are technically sound and show learning-based search outperforms random search for complex identities.  
- Paper does not clearly enforce or incorporate computational complexity constraints in the search algorithm.  
- Scope is limited to single matrix/vector targets and homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, reducing broader applicability.  
- Presentation is generally clear but Sections 5 and 6 need more detailed examples; visualizations of grammar trees and search would help.  
- Learned representation (Section 4.2) is sound but should be made more accessible to readers unfamiliar with recursive NNs.  
- Supplementary material contains useful examples (e.g., k=6) that should be integrated or better referenced in the main text.  
- Original combination of attribute grammars and ML for symbolic computation is novel and distinguishes the work from theorem proving and program induction.  
- Could strengthen related work discussion by contrasting with prior learning-for-symbolic-computation efforts (e.g., Bridge et al., 2013).  
- Significant practical implications for symbolic computation and some ML tasks (e.g., efficient RBM partition computations), but impact is constrained by current assumptions.  
- Strengths: clear demonstration that learning guides identity discovery, effective integer descriptors, and discovery of novel efficient identities.  
- Weaknesses: lack of explicit complexity enforcement, restrictive expression assumptions, and paper structure/visibility of supplementary examples.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—clarify complexity enforcement, discuss limitations and extensions, improve clarity and visualizations, and fix noted typos.
