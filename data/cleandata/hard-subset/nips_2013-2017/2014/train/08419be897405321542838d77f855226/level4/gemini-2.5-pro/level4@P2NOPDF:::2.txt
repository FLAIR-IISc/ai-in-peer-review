This paper presents two methods for searching for computationally simpler, equivalent symbolic expression trees. The first approach employs an n-gram model over sequences of sub-expressions to guide the search process. The second method utilizes a recursive neural network (RNN) to learn continuous vector representations of the expression trees. These representations are then used to predict the most probable rules for combining subtrees. The experimental results, particularly those in Figure 3, suggest that the n-gram-based approach outperforms the RNN model on two of the three benchmark tasks.

**Comments on Review Criteria:**

**Quality: High.** The proposed methods are technically sound and the experimental setup appears rigorous.

**Clarity: Moderate.** While the overall goal is clear, the paper is difficult to follow in places. Section 7.2, in particular, is dense and would benefit from a more detailed explanation or an illustrative example to clarify the implementation details of the RNN-based model. The exposition could be improved by more clearly bridging the high-level concepts and their concrete mathematical formulations.

**Originality: High.** The application of both n-gram models and recursive neural networks to the problem of symbolic expression simplification is novel.

**Significance: Low.** The practical significance of this work is not well-established. The paper addresses the problem of finding more computationally efficient forms of symbolic expressions, but it is unclear whether the proposed search-based methods offer a tangible benefit over the highly optimized, heuristic-based simplification algorithms used in modern compilers and symbolic computation systems. The problem formulation feels somewhat contrived, and the paper lacks a compelling argument for why a practitioner would choose these computationally intensive search methods over existing, established techniques. The significance of the paper would be greatly enhanced by including a discussion of specific real-world applications where these methods would be advantageous and a comparison against more traditional simplification baselines.
