The paper introduces a novel approach for representing symbolic expressions and finding their mathematical identities, leveraging the capabilities of neural networks. Although the expressions studied are limited, the concept is intriguing and has the potential to positively impact symbolic software libraries.

One concern is that the problem statement emphasizes the need for expressions with lower computational complexity, yet this constraint is not explicitly enforced in the algorithm. The authors may want to consider discussing possible solutions to limit the search space to such solutions, as this aspect is not trivial.

Regarding the restrictions mentioned in Section 2, it is unclear whether the scheduler is the sole limiting factor for more complicated mathematical expressions. Providing more formal or empirical evidence for setting these restrictions would strengthen the paper. For instance, the authors could discuss the results of experiments that led to the imposition of these constraints.

The structure of the paper could be improved for better clarity. Breaking down the stages of the approach and explaining each component more clearly would enhance the overall flow of the paper.

Additionally, it would be beneficial to clarify whether there are constraints on the size of the vectors or matrices used. For example, in the supplementary material, the expression for RBM-1 when k=1 and n=1 appears to be invalid.

Minor errors were found, including typos on pages 332 ("show for" instead of "shows for"), 148 ("has contains" instead of "has"), 289 ("is is" instead of "is"), and 338 ("by be" instead of "by"). Addressing these issues would further improve the paper's overall quality.
