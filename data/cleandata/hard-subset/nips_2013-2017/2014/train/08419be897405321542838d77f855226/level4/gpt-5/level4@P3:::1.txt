Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes learning-based methods to discover algebraic identities that are equivalent to a given target expression but cheaper to evaluate. The authors formalize a space of symbolic expressions with an attribute grammar (following Knuth [14]) whose rules carry computational complexity attributes. Because naïve enumeration of grammar trees is exponential in the expression degree, they learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets and use these strategies to guide exploration for harder targets.

Two core ingredients support this pipeline:
- Representations for expressions: (i) a numerical descriptor based on evaluations over random inputs in Zp to test equivalence efficiently and (ii) a learned continuous embedding φ(S) via a recursive neural network (RNN) trained to classify expressions into equivalence classes (using the numerical test to prepare labels).
- Search strategies: a random baseline; n-gram models that score subtrees based on frequency in prior solutions; and an RNN-based policy that, given embeddings of partial trees, predicts the next grammar rule to apply. Solutions may be linear combinations of trees; coefficients are solved by modular Gaussian elimination.

Experiments consider five families, including sums over products of matrices (e.g., ∑ AAT terms), elementwise variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, and sums arising in RBM partition functions. The learned strategies dramatically enlarge the range of solvable degrees within a fixed time budget relative to pure random search. In most families the n-gram strategies outperform the RNN policy; for the hardest family (RBM-2), no method finds identities beyond k=5 within the time limit, though the RNN is faster than random at k=5. The paper relates to theorem proving [5,11], program induction [18,28], probabilistic programming [12,20], and NLP embeddings (e.g., [8,16,23]). Compared to CAS tools like Maple/Mathematica [6,27], the approach targets computational efficiency rather than symbolic simplification rules, and differs by learning heuristics from data.

Strengths
- Novel application: Casting identity discovery and complexity reduction as a learned search over an attribute grammar is original and timely. The use of φ(S) to embed symbolic expressions parallels word/sentence embeddings in NLP, opening potential for broader ML on symbolic structures.
- Clear framework: The separation of grammar, representation, and strategy is well-articulated. Handling linear combinations via modular arithmetic is practical and efficient for equivalence checks and coefficient solving.
- Empirical signal: On several families, learned strategies substantially outperform random search, enabling discovery beyond brute force and ad hoc human derivations; code/data availability supports reproducibility.
- Careful RNN engineering: The curriculum, identity initialization, ReLU activations, and dropout are well-motivated and yield high classification accuracy on expression equivalence (≤5% error for k up to 6).

Weaknesses
- Limited scope of expressions: The current grammar and problem setting (homogeneous polynomials in a single variable, scalar outputs) are quite restrictive. It remains unclear how readily the approach extends to multiple variables, mixed operators, or recursion, and how search will scale as the grammar enlarges.
- Modest search gains from deep learning: Across families, n-gram strategies often outperform the RNN policy for identity discovery. This weakens the case that the learned continuous representation materially improves search versus simpler statistics over subtrees.
- Evaluation breadth: The paper does not compare against strong non-learning search baselines (e.g., beam search, A*, MCTS/UCT, or term-rewriting/equality-saturation heuristics). Nor is there a clear head-to-head with CAS simplification plus cost-directed rewrites in the same operator set.
- Significance and impact: While several identities reduce apparent asymptotic cost, the paper provides limited analysis of novelty relative to known linear-algebraic identities, or demonstrations of downstream impact (e.g., speedups in ML models or compilers). For RBM families, results are mixed; the hardest case remains largely unsolved.
- Validation of correctness: Equivalence testing via random Zp evaluations is efficient but probabilistic; the paper would benefit from formal verification or post hoc symbolic checks of discovered identities to eliminate any (even if unlikely) collision risk.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The components are sound and well-integrated; the modular arithmetic approach is clever. However, the superiority of the learned RNN policy over simpler n-gram heuristics is not compelling in the current experiments.
- Empirical rigor: Time caps, repeated runs, and success fractions are reasonable, but stronger baselines, ablations (e.g., beam width, exploration vs. exploitation, descriptor size N, choice of prime p), and analysis of failure modes would improve confidence.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized; figures and examples are helpful. Some typos and notational inconsistencies remain. A brief, formal statement of the search scheduler (e.g., greedy vs. stochastic sampling, beam size) would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- The learning-to-search formulation for algebraic identity discovery using attribute grammars and learned embeddings is novel.

Significance
- Potentially high if extended, but current scope limits practical impact. Demonstrations in compilers or ML pipelines would strengthen the case.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add stronger non-learning baselines: beam/A*/MCTS guided by exact or learned cost-to-go; term-rewriting/equality-saturation tailored to your grammar and cost model.
- Provide formal verification or symbolic cross-checking of discovered identities, at least for representative cases.
- Analyze scaling: report complexity and success as grammar size increases; include multi-variable expressions and non-homogeneous polynomials.
- Show end-to-end benefits: compile a small numeric kernel or ML layer using discovered identities and measure speedups.
- Clarify scheduler details and consider exploration mechanisms (softmax sampling, beam search) rather than strictly greedy selection.
- Expand comparisons with CAS tools using the same operator set and a cost-aware objective.
- Provide more detailed ablations on the RNN embedding (dimension l, curriculum schedule, descriptor size N, prime p) and why it underperforms n-grams in search.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel problem framing and methodology at the intersection of ML and symbolic computation.
- Demonstrated ability to discover nontrivial identities beyond brute force.
- Reusable representation learning for symbolic expressions with high classification accuracy.
- Open-source code/data.

Arguments against acceptance
- Restricted scope; uncertain scalability to richer grammars and multi-variable settings.
- n-gram heuristics often outperform the RNN, weakening the deep learning contribution.
- Missing comparisons to strong non-learning search/planning and rewriting baselines.
- Limited evidence of practical impact or formal verification of identities.

Recommendation
Interesting and creative work with a clear vision, but empirical strength and breadth are currently below the NIPS bar. I lean toward rejection in its present form. With stronger baselines, broader scope, and end-to-end demonstrations or formal verification, this line of work could be impactful in a future submission.
