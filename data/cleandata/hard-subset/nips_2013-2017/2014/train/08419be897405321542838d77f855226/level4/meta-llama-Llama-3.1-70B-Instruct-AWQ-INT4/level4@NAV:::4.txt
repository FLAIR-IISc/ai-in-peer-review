The paper presents a novel application of neural networks, focusing on learning representations of symbolic expressions and identifying their mathematical identities. Although the expressions explored in this study are limited in scope, and it remains unclear whether this approach can be generalized to more complex expressions, the paper's potential impact on symbolic software libraries is noteworthy.

A concern arises from the problem statement, which emphasizes the pursuit of expressions with lower computational complexity. However, this constraint is not explicitly enforced in the proposed algorithm. It would be beneficial for the authors to discuss potential strategies for limiting the search space to solutions that meet this criterion.

The restrictions outlined in Section 2 appear to be largely dictated by the scheduler. It would be helpful to provide more formal justification or empirical evidence supporting these limitations, as it is unclear whether the scheduler is the sole limiting factor for more complex mathematical expressions.

The paper's structure could be improved for better clarity. A more explicit explanation of the stages involved would enhance the overall flow.

Additionally, it is unclear whether there are constraints on the size of vectors or matrices. For instance, the expression for RBM-1 in the supplementary material appears to be invalid for k=1 and n=1.

Minor errors were also noted: "show for" (page 332), "has contains" (page 148), "is is" (page 289), and "by be" (page 338).
