- Core idea: represent symbolic expressions with an attribute grammar and search for equivalent, lower-complexity expressions using learned search strategies.  
- Methods: guide intractable brute-force search with learned n-gram and recursive neural network (RNN) policies trained on solutions to simpler expressions.  
- Novelty: RNN learns continuous vector representations of symbolic expressions, adapting NLP techniques to symbolic math.  
- Evaluation: tested on five families of polynomial expressions (homogeneous, single-variable, scalar-output).  
- Results: discovered several non-trivial, computationally efficient identities, including polynomial-time equivalents for expressions with naive exponential evaluation.  
- Strength: original and significant application of ML to symbolic mathematics with potential for automated identity discovery and code optimization.  
- Strength: technical contribution in using recursive neural nets to obtain distributed representations of mathematical expressions.  
- Strength: empirical success in finding new efficient identities beyond naive search.  
- Weakness: narrow evaluation scope (only homogeneous single-variable polynomials), limiting claims of generality to broader classes of math expressions.  
- Weakness: scalability issues—performance degrades as target complexity increases (e.g., no benefit for RBM-2 when k>5) and RNN often underperforms simpler n-gram models.  
- Weakness: limited justification that learning-based search is necessary—many discovered identities have simple recursive patterns that symbolic pattern-matching systems might find directly.  
- Overall: promising and creative work but preliminary; insufficient experimental breadth and scalability to justify acceptance in current form.
