- Proposal: Learn search strategies over an attribute grammar to discover computationally cheaper algebraic identities equivalent to a target expression.
- Representations: Use numerical descriptors over Zp for equivalence testing and an RNN-learned continuous embedding φ(S) trained to classify equivalence classes.
- Search strategies: Compare random search, n-gram subtree frequency models, and an RNN policy that predicts grammar rules; solve linear combinations via modular Gaussian elimination.
- Experiments: Tested on five families (matrix sums, elementwise variants, symmetric polynomials, RBM partition sums); learned strategies extend solvable degrees under time limits.
- Empirical result: n-gram strategies often outperform the RNN policy; RNN is faster than random for the hardest case at k=5 but overall fails on the hardest family beyond k=5.
- Strength — novelty: Casting identity discovery as learned search over attribute grammars with embeddings is original and timely.
- Strength — framework: Clear separation of grammar, representation, and strategy; efficient handling of linear combinations with modular arithmetic.
- Strength — empirical signal: Learned methods outperform random search in several families and code/data are provided.
- Strength — RNN engineering: Curriculum, initialization, activations, and dropout yield high classification accuracy (≤5% error up to k=6).
- Weakness — scope: Grammar and problem setting are restrictive (homogeneous single-variable scalar outputs); unclear extension to multi-variable or richer operators.
- Weakness — limited DL benefit: n-gram heuristics often beat the RNN, weakening claims about the learned continuous representation’s utility for search.
- Weakness — evaluation breadth: Missing comparisons to strong non-learning search baselines (beam/A*/MCTS, term-rewriting, equality-saturation) and CAS cost-directed rewrites.
- Weakness — impact: Limited analysis of novelty relative to known linear-algebraic identities and little demonstration of downstream speedups or practical impact.
- Weakness — correctness validation: Equivalence testing via random Zp evaluations is probabilistic; formal verification or symbolic checks are needed.
- Quality: Components are sound and integrated, but the RNN policy’s superiority is not compelling and more ablations/baselines are required.
- Clarity: Generally well-written with useful figures, but some typos, notational inconsistencies, and missing scheduler details hinder reproducibility.
- Originality: The learning-to-search formulation for algebraic identity discovery is novel.
- Significance: Potentially high if extended, but current limitations reduce practical impact; end-to-end demonstrations would help.
- Suggestions: Add stronger non-learning baselines, formal verification of identities, scaling to larger grammars/multi-variable cases, end-to-end speedup demos, clarify scheduler and exploration, compare with CAS, and provide more RNN ablations.
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel framing, ability to discover nontrivial identities, reusable embeddings, and open-source release.
- Arguments against acceptance: Restricted scope, n-gram superiority over RNN, missing strong baselines and verification, and limited evidence of practical impact.
- Recommendation: Interesting and creative but currently below the conference bar; reviewer leans toward rejection pending stronger baselines, broader scope, and verification.
