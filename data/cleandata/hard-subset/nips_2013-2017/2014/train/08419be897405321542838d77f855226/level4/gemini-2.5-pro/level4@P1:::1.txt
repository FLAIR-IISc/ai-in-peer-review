This paper introduces a machine learning framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar and then search for an equivalent expression to a given target that has lower computational complexity. Recognizing that a brute-force search over the grammar tree is intractable, the authors propose guiding the search using learned strategies. These strategies, namely an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN), are trained on solutions from simpler expressions to find solutions for more complex ones. A key part of the RNN approach is the novel idea of learning a continuous vector representation for symbolic expressions. The authors test their framework on five families of polynomial expressions and demonstrate the discovery of several new, efficient identities that are beyond the reach of naive search.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a novel and ambitious problem: applying modern machine learning techniques to the domain of symbolic mathematics. This is a creative and significant departure from the standard statistical tasks typically seen at NIPS. The prospect of automatically discovering new mathematical identities or optimizing code at a symbolic level is highly compelling and could open up a new line of research.

2.  **Novel Technical Contributions:** The use of a recursive neural network to learn a distributed representation of a mathematical expression is a particularly interesting contribution. This is a clever adaptation of techniques from natural language processing and has the potential to make symbolic computation more accessible to a wide range of gradient-based learning methods.

3.  **Demonstrated Success:** The framework is not merely a theoretical proposal; it successfully discovers several non-trivial identities. Finding polynomial-time equivalents for expressions whose naive evaluation is exponential (e.g., the RBM-related families) is a strong demonstration of the system's potential practical value.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** A primary weakness is that the framework is only evaluated on a very restricted class of problems: homogeneous polynomials of a single variable that produce a scalar output. While this is a reasonable starting point, it makes it difficult to assess how the approach would generalize to the broader and more complex world of mathematical expressions (e.g., those involving trigonometry, recursion, inequalities, or more varied data structures). The claims of general applicability would be much stronger if the method were tested on a more diverse set of problems.

2.  **Scalability and Performance Concerns:** While the learning-based approaches outperform a random baseline, their performance seems to decline significantly as the complexity of the target expression (indicated by `k`) increases. For the most challenging problem family, (RBM-2), none of the learning methods provided an advantage over random search for `k > 5`. This raises questions about the scalability of the current learning strategies. Furthermore, the simpler n-gram models frequently outperform the more sophisticated RNN model. This may suggest that the structures of the discovered identities in these experiments were primarily composed of local, repetitive patterns that n-grams are well-suited to capture, while the RNN struggled to learn a more generalizable representation.

3.  **Justification of Learning-Based Approach:** For some of the expression families tested, the resulting efficient identities exhibit a clear and simple recursive structure (e.g., Example 2). One might argue that for such patterned solutions, a dedicated symbolic algebra system with pattern-matching rules could have discovered them more directly, without requiring a complex learning apparatus trained on simpler instances. The paper would benefit from a discussion of why such non-learning alternatives are insufficient, which would help to better motivate the necessity of the proposed ML-based search.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This is a thought-provoking and highly original paper that explores a fascinating new application for machine learning. The core ideas are creative, and the preliminary results, including the discovery of new identities, are promising.

However, the work in its current form feels preliminary. The main concerns are the narrow scope of the experimental validation, which leaves the generalizability of the approach an open question, and the mixed results on scalability, where even the proposed learning methods fail on more complex problems. Because of these limitations, the paper does not yet feel like a complete and convincing piece of work.

While I find the direction of research very interesting, I believe the paper is slightly below the acceptance threshold for NIPS. It would be a much stronger submission if the authors could demonstrate the framework's effectiveness on a wider and more challenging variety of mathematical expressions, thereby providing more convincing evidence of its general utility and scalability.
