Summary
This paper explores learning-based discovery of efficient mathematical identities. The authors represent symbolic expressions with an attribute grammar and search for compositions that are analytically equivalent to a target expression but cheaper to compute. Because brute-force search over grammar trees is exponential, they propose two learned strategies—an n-gram model over subtrees and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions and predicts the next grammar rule. Trained on solutions to simpler targets, these strategies guide the search toward efficient identities for more complex expressions. The paper reports successes on several families, including matrix expressions, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related sums, with identities that reduce computational complexity beyond what random or exhaustive search can typically find.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on attribute grammars and borrows ideas from NLP (n-gram models and RNNs applied to tree structures). It is distinct from rule-based symbolic systems like Maple/Mathematica by learning search heuristics from data rather than relying on hand-designed rules. The RNN-based continuous representation of symbolic expressions is particularly novel in this context.

Strengths
- Novelty: A creative application of machine learning, especially RNNs, to learn representations for symbolic expressions and to guide search for identities.
- Potential impact: Could be valuable for symbolic software libraries and compilers by automatically discovering or substituting more efficient formulations.
- Empirical insight: Demonstrates that learning from simpler expressions can widen the scope of identities discoverable for more complex targets, beyond random search.

Weaknesses and questions for the authors
- Enforcement of efficiency: The problem statement seeks expressions with lower computational complexity, but I do not see where the algorithm explicitly enforces or verifies such a constraint during search (beyond removing certain rules in some experiments). Have the authors considered mechanisms to restrict or bias the search space to solutions with provably lower complexity?
- Scope restrictions (Section 2): The paper limits targets to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, single-variable settings, and scalar outputs. Is the scheduler the only limiting factor preventing extension to richer mathematical expressions, or are there other bottlenecks (e.g., grammar design, descriptor size, training data availability)? More formal justification or empirical ablations for these restrictions would strengthen the paper.
- Organization and exposition: The structure is difficult to follow in places. A clearer staged explanation of the pipeline (grammar design → representation/descriptor → search/scheduler → training data generation → learned strategies → linear combination solver) would improve readability.
- Validity of RBM-1 example: Is there a constraint on the sizes of vectors/matrices? For k=1, n=1, the RBM-1 expression in the supplementary material does not seem valid. Clarification would help.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: The approach is interesting and generally sound, with promising results. However, the absence of an explicit mechanism to ensure lower complexity during search is a gap relative to the stated objective.
- Clarity: The paper could be better organized; the stages of the method and the role of each component (scheduler vs. strategy) should be presented more systematically.
- Originality: High. Learning continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions and using them to guide identity discovery is novel.
- Significance: Potentially significant, particularly for symbolic computation and compiler optimizations. That said, the current scope is limited, and the lack of guarantees or stronger controls on complexity may limit immediate practical impact.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel problem framing and methodology.
- Demonstrated ability to discover nontrivial identities beyond brute-force or random search.
- Potential downstream impact in symbolic libraries and program optimization.

Cons:
- Limited scope (homogeneous polynomials, single variable, scalar outputs) without sufficient justification or ablation.
- No explicit enforcement or proof of lower complexity in the algorithmic loop.
- Exposition needs tightening; some examples/constraints are unclear (e.g., RBM-1 edge case).

Minor issues and typos
- Line 148: “has contains” → “contains”
- Line 289: “is is” → “is”
- Line 332: “show for” → “show how to” or “show”
- Line 338: “by be” → “by”

Overall
The paper presents a novel and interesting approach to learning representations of symbolic expressions and using them to discover efficient identities. Although the current scope is limited and some algorithmic and expository aspects need clarification—especially how computational efficiency is ensured during search—it is a promising direction with potential impact on symbolic computation and optimization tools.
