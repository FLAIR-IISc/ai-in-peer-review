- Paper proposes two search methods for simpler symbolic expression trees: an n-gram model over sub-expression sequences and a recursive neural network (RNN) for continuous representations.
- Learned representations predict the next rule for combining subtrees to guide the search.
- Experiments (Fig. 3) show the n-gram approach outperforms the RNN on two of three expression families.
- Technical approach is sound, well-motivated, and described in sufficient detail.
- Use of attribute grammars integrated with machine learning is well-justified.
- Presentation is generally clear, but Section 7.2 needs clearer explanation and some refinements.
- Notation is consistent and examples effectively illustrate concepts; more intuitive explanations of grammar rules/search would help.
- The combination of attribute grammars with learning for discovering mathematical identities is novel.
- Applying an RNN to learn continuous representations of mathematical structures is a significant contribution.
- Significance is moderate: discovering new identities with reduced complexity is noteworthy but practical impact is uncertain.
- Potential applications include compiler optimization, approximation algorithms, and simplifying computations in ML models.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, original, promising methodology, though practical significance of specific discoveries is unclear.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel and technically sound, discovers new identities, integrates ML into symbolic reasoning, has application potential, and is well written.
- Arguments against acceptance: problem may be contrived with unclear practical impact, Section 7.2 needs improvement, and RNN underperforms the simpler n-gram model.
