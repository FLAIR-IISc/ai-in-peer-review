

This paper proposes an interesting application of machine learning techniques to the problem of discovering efficient mathematical identities, using attribute grammars to represent symbolic expressions and machine learning models (n-gram and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for computationally efficient versions of target expressions. The authors demonstrate their framework on a limited set of polynomial expressions, showing it can discover identities that reduce computational complexity for certain expressions.

The paper makes several contributions: (1) a grammar framework for finding efficient versions of symbolic expressions, (2) integration of machine learning techniques to guide the search based on simpler expressions, (3) a novel application of recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of mathematical structures, and (4) discovery of new mathematical identities with significant computational improvements.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with good technical details on the attribute grammar representation, numerical and learned representations of symbolic expressions, and the search strategies. The experimental section is thorough for the limited scope of the problem, showing results for four families of expressions with increasing complexity.

The primary strength of the paper lies in its novel application of machine learning to a domain that has not been extensively explored. The use of attribute grammars to formalize the search space and the application of n-gram models and RNNs to guide the search are conceptually interesting. The demonstration of continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions has parallels with NLP techniques, and the approach to learning representations for mathematical structures is a promising direction.

However, there are several significant concerns that prevent this paper from being a strong candidate for NIPS:

1. The scope of the problem is extremely limited. The authors focus on a specific class of homogeneous polynomials with very restricted input structures (single matrix or vector, scalar output). The families of expressions tested (e.g., (ΣAAT)k, (Σ(A.*A)AT)k) are quite narrow, and the paper acknowledges this limitation in Section 8. The mathematical identities discovered, while novel for these specific cases, do not seem to represent a major advance in mathematical knowledge or have broad applicability.

2. The performance gains appear modest relative to the complexity of the approach. For example, the paper shows that for k=6, the approach can discover an identity that computes in O(nm) time instead of O(nmp) for naive evaluation. However, the search space for k=6 is only 9785 trees (as shown in Example 2), which is not particularly large. The paper acknowledges that "the majority of these examples are too complex to be found manually or by exhaustive search," but this claim is questionable given the manageable search space size.

3. The paper's claim that "using a sledgehammer to crack a nut" is particularly relevant. The problem being solved is quite small-scale, and the machine learning approach is significantly more complex than the problem warrants. The authors demonstrate that for their specific examples, n-gram models perform better than the RNN, suggesting that simpler statistical methods might suffice for the limited scope of problems considered.

4. The scalability of the approach is not convincingly demonstrated. The paper shows results up to k=15 for some families, but the search space grows exponentially with k (as noted in Table 3), and the paper acknowledges that for the most challenging family (RBM-2)k, the approach performs worse than random search for k>5. The authors do not provide convincing evidence that their approach would scale to more complex mathematical problems.

5. The significance of the results is questionable. While the discovered identities do offer computational improvements for the specific cases tested, the paper does not demonstrate how these would impact real-world machine learning applications beyond the limited scope of their examples. The connection to dropout and RBM partition functions mentioned in Section 8 is intriguing but remains speculative without concrete demonstrations of how the identities would be used in practice.

In summary, while the paper presents an interesting concept and makes some technical contributions to the application of machine learning to symbolic computation, the scope of the problem is too narrow, the performance gains are modest relative to the complexity of the approach, and the significance of the results is not convincingly demonstrated. The paper feels more like a promising preliminary exploration than a substantial contribution to the field that would be appropriate for NIPS.

Given the four criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance), the paper scores well on Clarity and has some Originality, but scores poorly on Quality (due to limited scope and modest results) and Significance (due to narrow applicability and lack of demonstrated impact). The paper is interesting but falls short of the threshold for publication at NIPS.

I recommend rejection, as the work does not yet represent a sufficiently strong contribution to the field.
