This paper presents a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar and then search over the space of possible expression trees to find one that is analytically equivalent to a target expression but has lower computational complexity. As a brute-force search is intractable, the main contribution is the application of machine learning techniques to guide this search. The authors propose two learning-based search strategies: an n-gram model over sub-expressions and a recursive neural network (RNN). These models are trained on solutions found for simpler expressions and then used to find solutions for more complex targets. A key component of the RNN approach is its ability to learn a continuous vector representation for symbolic expressions, which is demonstrated to be effective in a standalone expression classification task. The framework is successfully used to discover new, non-trivial identities for several families of expressions, some of which are relevant to machine learning.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The application of modern machine learning techniques, particularly recursive neural networks, to the domain of symbolic mathematics is highly novel and significant. It bridges the gap between statistical pattern recognition and formal symbolic manipulation. The discovery of new, complex identities (e.g., polynomial-time equivalents for expressions that are naively exponential to compute) demonstrates the power of the approach and could have a real-world impact, especially if integrated into compilers or symbolic math packages. The potential applications mentioned for RBM partition functions or closed-form versions of dropout are compelling.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The idea of using a learned continuous representation for symbolic expressions is powerful, and the high classification accuracy reported in Section 7.1 provides strong evidence for its effectiveness. The experimental design is solid, clearly comparing the proposed learning-based search strategies against a random baseline across several challenging families of expressions. The authors are also transparent, having made their code available for reproducibility.

3.  **Clarity:** For the most part, the paper is well-written and motivated. The introduction and examples effectively convey the problem and the high-level approach.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

While the overall direction is excellent, several key methodological details could be clarified to strengthen the paper.

1.  **Clarity of the RNN Architecture:** The description of the recursive neural network in Section 4.2 is ambiguous. The paper mentions a "weight tensor W3 (of size l x l x l)" for binary operations. This architecture sounds more like a Recursive Neural Tensor Network (RNTN) than a standard RNN. It would be beneficial for the authors to clarify this. If it is indeed a tensor network, they should state so explicitly and perhaps briefly discuss why this choice was made, potentially citing related work like Bowman (2013), who demonstrated the effectiveness of tensor networks over standard RNNs for learning logical reasoning.

2.  **Clarity of the Search Process:** The interplay between the "scheduler" and the "strategy" in Section 6 could be explained more concretely. While the roles are defined, a step-by-step example of how a single expression tree is expanded would be very helpful. For instance, how does the scheduler present the current state of the tree to the strategy, and what is the precise form of the scores returned by the strategy that leads to the selection of the next grammar rule?

3.  **Connection Between Representation Learning and Search:** The link between the expression classification experiment (Section 7.1) and the identity discovery experiment (Section 7.2) is not fully explained. The RNN is first trained as a C-way classifier. Then, for the search task, the final softmax layer is removed. It is stated that a *new* softmax layer (with weights U) is trained on top of the frozen φ(S) representations to predict the next grammar rule. This should be made more explicit. Clarifying that the representation learning is essentially a pre-training step for the search-policy network would improve reader understanding.

4.  **Restricted Problem Scope:** The problem definition is currently limited to homogeneous polynomials of a single variable with a scalar output. While this provides a controlled environment for demonstrating the method, it is quite restrictive. The authors acknowledge this in the discussion, but a more detailed comment on the specific challenges that would arise when relaxing these assumptions (e.g., handling undecidability with more general functions, managing a more complex state space) would add depth and provide a clearer roadmap for future work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper tackles a challenging and important problem with a highly original approach that combines symbolic methods with modern machine learning.
*   **Pro:** The successful discovery of novel and complex mathematical identities is a significant result.
*   **Pro:** The work on learning continuous representations for symbolic expressions is a valuable contribution in its own right and could enable further research in this area.
*   **Con:** Key details of the methodology, particularly the exact RNN architecture and the mechanics of the search process, are not explained clearly enough for full understanding and reproducibility.

**Conclusion:**
This is a very interesting and innovative paper that presents a promising new direction for applying machine learning to symbolic problems. The strengths in originality and significance are substantial. The main weaknesses relate to a lack of clarity in some methodological sections. These weaknesses are addressable, and I would recommend acceptance, with the strong encouragement that the authors revise the paper to clarify the points raised above.
