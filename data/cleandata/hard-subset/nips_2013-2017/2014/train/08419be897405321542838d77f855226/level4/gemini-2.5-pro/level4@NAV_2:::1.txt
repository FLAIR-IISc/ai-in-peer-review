This paper proposes a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar and to search through the space of possible expression trees for one that is analytically equivalent to a target expression but has a lower computational complexity. Recognizing that the search space grows exponentially, the authors introduce learning-based strategies to guide the search. Specifically, they employ n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) to learn from solutions to simpler expressions, thereby enabling the discovery of identities for more complex targets that are intractable for brute-force or random search. This work relates to the fields of automated theorem proving and program induction but distinguishes itself by applying statistical machine learning techniques rather than relying on predefined rules or heuristics. The use of an RNN to learn a continuous embedding for symbolic expressions is a particularly interesting parallel to recent successful techniques in natural language processing, such as word and sentence embeddings.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novelty. Applying modern machine learning, particularly deep learning models, to the symbolic problem of discovering mathematical identities is an ambitious and creative research direction. It opens up a new potential application area for these techniques. The discovery of several new, non-trivial identities (e.g., providing polynomial-time computations for expressions with naive exponential complexity) serves as a strong proof of concept for the framework's potential.
*   **Novel Representation:** The proposed use of a recursive neural network to learn a continuous vector representation for symbolic expressions is a significant contribution. This idea, analogous to word embeddings in NLP, could have broad applicability, potentially making the symbolic computation domain more accessible to a wide variety of gradient-based learning methods. The high classification accuracy reported for this representation demonstrates its effectiveness.
*   **Framework Quality:** The overall framework is well-conceived, integrating several components—attribute grammars for generation, numerical and learned methods for equivalence checking, and learning-based search—into a cohesive system.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized, successfully explaining a complex, multi-part system and its motivation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope:** The experiments, while demonstrating the principle, are confined to a relatively restricted class of expressions: homogeneous polynomials of a single matrix or vector. This raises questions about the generality of the approach. It is not clear how well the framework would perform with a more diverse grammar that includes, for instance, trigonometric or exponential functions, or handles a wider range of algebraic structures.
*   **Efficacy of Learning Models:** The experimental results show that while the learning-based strategies consistently outperform random search, the performance of the more sophisticated RNN model is somewhat underwhelming, as it is often outperformed by simpler n-gram models. On the most challenging problem family ((RBM-2)k), none of the learning methods provided a significant advantage over random search for higher-degree polynomials. This suggests that the current models are most effective at capturing repetitive local patterns (which n-grams do well) and may struggle to generalize to discover more complex, non-repeating structural identities.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** While the paper successfully tackles problems with a large search space, it is unclear how the approach would scale if the grammar of mathematical operators were significantly expanded. A larger grammar would not only increase the branching factor at each step of the search but might also require a more sophisticated scheduling mechanism than the one presented.

In summary, this paper introduces a highly original and thought-provoking approach to a challenging problem. The application of machine learning to discover new mathematical knowledge is an exciting direction, and the idea of learning a continuous representation for symbolic expressions is a valuable contribution. However, the work in its current form feels somewhat preliminary. The limited scope of expressions considered and the mixed results of the more advanced learning model suggest that significant challenges remain in scaling this approach to a more general-purpose tool for mathematical discovery. Despite these limitations, the novelty of the ideas and the promising proof-of-concept results make this an interesting contribution to the field.
