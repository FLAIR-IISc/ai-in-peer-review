The paper introduces a novel approach for representing symbolic expressions and finding their mathematical identities using neural networks. Although the expressions studied are limited, I find the paper interesting, and I believe it could have a good impact on symbolic software libraries.

One concern is that the problem statement emphasizes the goal of finding expressions with lower computational complexity, but the algorithm does not explicitly enforce this constraint. I understand that incorporating such a constraint is not trivial, but I would like the authors to discuss possible solutions to limit the search space to such solutions.

Regarding the restrictions mentioned in Section 2, I question whether the scheduler is the only limiting factor for handling more complicated mathematical expressions. I would have liked to see more formal analysis or empirical experiments to justify these restrictions.

Furthermore, I found the structure of the paper to be difficult to follow. It would be beneficial to explain each stage more clearly to improve the overall clarity of the presentation.

Additionally, I noticed that the paper does not mention any constraints on the size of the vector or matrix. For instance, the expression for RBM-1 in the supplementary material appears to be invalid for the case k=1 and n=1.

Finally, I identified a few typos: "show for" (line 332), "has contains" (line 148), "is is" (line 289), and "by be" (line 338).
