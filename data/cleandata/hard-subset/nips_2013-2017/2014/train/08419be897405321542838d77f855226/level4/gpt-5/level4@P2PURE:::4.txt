The paper presents a novel application of neural networks: learning representations for symbolic expressions and discovering their mathematical identities. Although the class of expressions studied is quite limited and there is no proof that the approach extends to more complex expressions, the work is interesting and could have a positive impact on symbolic computation libraries.

- The problem statement emphasizes finding expressions with lower computational complexity, yet the algorithm does not appear to enforce this constraint. I understand this is nontrivial, but have the authors considered mechanisms to restrict or bias the search space toward such solutions?

- Regarding the restrictions in Section 2, is the scheduler the only factor limiting applicability to more complex mathematical expressions? I would have liked a more formal justification, or at least empirical evidence, for these restrictions.

- The structure of the paper is not easy to follow; the stages would benefit from a clearer, step-by-step explanation.

- Is there a constraint on vector/matrix dimensions? For example, when k=1 and n=1, the RBM-1 expression in the supplementary material does not seem valid.

Typos:
- Line 332: "show for"
- Line 148: "has contains"
- Line 289: "is is"
- Line 338: "by be"

Overall, the paper introduces a novel approach to representing symbolic expressions and discovering their mathematical identities. Despite its limited scope, it remains an interesting and novel contribution.
