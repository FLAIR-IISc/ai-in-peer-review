This paper presents a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core contribution is the application of machine learning to guide the search for equivalent but less complex symbolic expressions. The authors represent mathematical formulas within an attribute grammar and then search the tree-like space of operator compositions. Recognizing that this search space grows exponentially, making brute-force or random search infeasible for complex targets, they introduce two learning-based search strategies: an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN). These models are trained on solutions to simpler problems to learn heuristics that effectively prune the search space for more difficult expressions. The work effectively bridges concepts from symbolic computation with techniques from natural language processing, where n-gram models and RNNs have been successfully used to model structured data. The idea of learning a continuous vector representation for symbolic expressions is particularly interesting and aligns with broader efforts in representation learning for structured domains.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality:** The paper's main strength lies in its novelty. The application of data-driven, statistical machine learning to the classical symbolic problem of discovering mathematical identities is a highly creative and promising direction. The use of a recursive neural network to learn a continuous embedding for mathematical expressions is particularly innovative and could open up new avenues for applying modern machine learning tools to symbolic reasoning and manipulation tasks.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sound and the empirical evaluation is solid. The authors demonstrate that their learning-based approaches can discover complex, non-obvious identities for several challenging families of expressions, far exceeding the capabilities of a random search baseline. The high classification accuracy achieved by the RNN on the expression representation task (Section 7.1) provides strong support for the validity of this approach. The discovery of efficient polynomial-time formulas for expressions whose naive evaluation is exponential (e.g., the RBM-related families) is an impressive proof of concept.
*   **Significance of Approach:** The paper addresses a core AI problem—search in a combinatorially large space—with a compelling learned-heuristic approach. The methodology of bootstrapping from solutions to simpler problems to solve more complex ones is elegant and could be inspirational for other domains facing similar combinatorial challenges.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Scope and Practical Impact:** A key weakness is the limited scope of the problem setting, which raises questions about the broader significance of the results. The framework is demonstrated on a restricted class of expressions (homogeneous polynomials involving a single matrix or vector). It is not clear how these methods would generalize to the more heterogeneous and complex expressions found in general-purpose compilers or symbolic algebra systems. The paper would be more impactful with a discussion on the path to applying this framework in a more general context and the challenges that would need to be overcome.
*   **Clarity:** While the paper is mostly well-written, the description of the experimental setup in Section 7.2 is dense and somewhat difficult to follow. A more detailed, step-by-step definition of how the k-th term is constructed for each expression family would greatly improve readability and allow the reader to better appreciate the complexity of the optimization tasks.
*   **Relative Performance of Models:** The results in Figure 3 show that the simpler n-gram models often outperform the more sophisticated RNN model. While the RNN is a more novel contribution, its superior performance is not consistently demonstrated on the search task itself, which slightly tempers the claims about its practical utility for this specific problem compared to simpler baselines.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    1.  Presents a highly original and creative application of machine learning to a symbolic computation domain.
    2.  The technical approach, particularly the learned representation for expressions, is novel and potentially impactful beyond this specific application.
    3.  Provides strong empirical evidence of discovering complex, non-trivial mathematical identities that are intractable for naive search methods.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    1.  The practical significance is somewhat unclear, as the method is evaluated on a constrained class of expressions, and its path to broader applicability is not fully articulated.
    2.  The simpler n-gram baseline is more effective than the novel RNN approach on several of the reported tasks.
