- Introduces an attribute-grammar framework and two learning-based search strategies (n-gram, recursive NN) to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities.
- Demonstrates reductions from O(n^3) to O(n^2) or better, including some apparently novel identities.
- Situates work within attribute grammars, symbolic computation engines, and ML-based symbolic reasoning / NLP techniques.
- Strength: Applies learning to replace hand-crafted heuristics, using simpler expressions to guide discovery of more complex identities.
- Strength: Experimental results clearly outperform random search for moderate complexity targets (k=2–15) and find challenging identities.
- Strength: Presents a well-structured framework with clear problem definition and thorough experimental validation across multiple expression families.
- Strength: Learned continuous representations for symbolic expressions are interesting and potentially widely applicable.
- Weakness: Problem setting is highly restricted (single matrix/vector variable, homogeneous polynomials, scalar output), limiting generalizability.
- Weakness: RNN implementation description is unclear—particularly the W3 weight tensor—and it’s ambiguous whether a tensor network or standard RNN is used.
- Weakness: Search strategy explanation is insufficient; how rule scores are generated and updated is not clearly described.
- Weakness: Paper contains several grammatical errors and unclear phrases that need correction.
- Assessment: Technically sound with appropriate validation, but limited scope reduces broader impact.
- Clarity: Generally well organized, but key technical sections (RNN details, search strategy) require clearer explanations.
- Originality: Novel application of learning-guided grammar search for mathematical identity discovery.
- Significance: Discovered identities have concrete computational benefits and potential compiler/optimization use, but broader applicability needs discussion of extensions.
- Recommendation: Accept.
