### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities for a given target symbolic expression. The core idea is to represent mathematical expressions as trees derived from an attribute grammar. The search space of possible expression trees grows exponentially, making brute-force or random search impractical for complex targets. To address this, the authors propose using machine learning models—specifically n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN)—to learn a search strategy. These models are trained on solutions found for simpler expressions and then used to guide the search for more complex ones, effectively learning common structural motifs in efficient identities. A key contribution is the novel use of an RNN to learn a continuous vector representation for symbolic expressions, which enables the use of gradient-based learning in this symbolic domain. The authors demonstrate their framework by discovering several new, non-trivial identities for families of expressions, including those related to symmetric polynomials and terms from the RBM partition function, reducing their complexity from exponential or O(n³) down to polynomial time (e.g., O(n²)). This work connects ideas from program induction and symbolic computation with modern machine learning techniques from natural language processing, such as n-gram models and recursive neural networks.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novelty. The application of machine learning, particularly deep learning models like RNNs, to the symbolic task of discovering mathematical identities is a creative and promising research direction. The idea of learning a continuous representation for symbolic expressions is itself a significant contribution that could enable a wide range of learning-based approaches in symbolic mathematics.

2.  **Important Results:** The framework is not merely a theoretical proposal; it successfully discovers new, complex, and useful mathematical identities. The ability to find polynomial-time equivalents for expressions that are naively exponential to compute (e.g., the RBM-related families) is a powerful demonstration of the method's potential impact, particularly for the machine learning community.

3.  **Solid Technical Approach:** The use of an attribute grammar to structure the search space is well-founded. The two proposed learning methods (n-grams and RNNs) are appropriately chosen, drawing insightful parallels with their use in natural language processing. The empirical evaluation is thorough, testing the framework on five distinct families of expressions of increasing difficulty and clearly demonstrating the superiority of the learned strategies over a random baseline.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection)**

1.  **Limited Scope:** The framework is currently demonstrated on a restricted class of expressions: homogeneous polynomials of a single variable with a scalar output. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper would be stronger with a more detailed discussion of the key challenges and potential solutions for extending the framework to more general mathematics (e.g., expressions with trigonometric or exponential functions, multiple variables, non-scalar outputs, or recursion).

2.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper's structure can be difficult to follow at times. The different components of the system—the representation of expressions (numerical vs. learned), the search for candidate trees (the strategies), and the use of a linear solver to combine trees—are intertwined. A clearer, more modular explanation of how these pieces fit together, perhaps with a flowchart or a more structured overview in the introduction, would significantly improve readability.

3.  **Explicit Complexity Guidance:** The problem is defined as finding expressions with *lower computational complexity*. While the experiments achieve this by sometimes constraining the grammar (e.g., removing matrix-matrix multiplication), the search strategies themselves do not appear to explicitly use complexity as a signal to guide the search. The models learn to find *equivalent* expressions, which happen to be efficient due to the grammar's constraints. It is unclear how the method would perform if the grammar contained both efficient and inefficient rules capable of forming a solution. A discussion on incorporating the complexity attribute directly into the learning objective or reward signal would be valuable.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

*   The learned RNN representation for symbolic expressions is very interesting. Table 2 shows impressive classification accuracy. Could you provide more intuition on what the learned representation `φ(S)` captures? For instance, have you tried visualizing the embeddings (e.g., with t-SNE) to see if semantically similar (but not identical) expressions cluster together?

*   Regarding the restrictions in Section 2: a more formal justification for these choices would be helpful. For instance, why are only homogeneous polynomials considered? Is this a fundamental limitation of the numerical representation scheme or a choice made to keep the search space manageable?

*   The linear combination of trees (Section 5) is a crucial step for finding more complex identities. How sensitive is the overall system to the number of trees `M` generated before the solver is called? Is there a principled way to decide when to stop generating trees and attempt to solve the system?

*   Could you clarify a potential ambiguity regarding the expression for the RBM-1 family in the supplementary material? For the case where k=1 and the input vector `A` has size n=1, the discovered identity does not appear to be valid. Please verify this and comment.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong, innovative paper that introduces a promising new bridge between symbolic computation and machine learning. The strengths—particularly the novelty of the approach and the significance of the discovered identities—outweigh the weaknesses related to scope and clarity of presentation. The work is likely to inspire follow-up research and could have a substantial impact on both symbolic mathematics software and the optimization of computations within the machine learning field. I recommend acceptance.
