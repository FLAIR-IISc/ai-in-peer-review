- Proposes a novel framework that searches attribute-grammar-generated expression trees to find analytically equivalent but computationally cheaper identities.  
- Uses ML (n-gram models and an RNN) trained on simpler solutions to guide search across an exponentially large space.  
- High originality and potential impact (e.g., compiler optimization, ML), applying deep learning to symbolic mathematics.  
- Technical contribution: learning continuous vector-space embeddings for symbolic expressions via an RNN, with promising classification accuracy.  
- Well-designed overall framework: attribute grammar, numerical equivalence checks, curriculum learning, and multiple baseline comparisons.  
- Experiments are limited to small, highly structured families (single-variable homogeneous polynomials), which may be too simple to justify the complex methods.  
- Simpler pattern-matching heuristics might suffice for the tested cases; lack of diverse problem domains (multi-variable, non-homogeneous, non-polynomial).  
- Scalability and generalization are unclear: gains over random are not always large, and methods fail on the hardest family (RBM-2) beyond k=5.  
- The RNN often underperforms a simple 3-gram in the search task, calling into question its practical utility and requiring further analysis.  
- Overall assessment: creative and promising but preliminary with insufficient empirical support; recommendation: borderline reject.
