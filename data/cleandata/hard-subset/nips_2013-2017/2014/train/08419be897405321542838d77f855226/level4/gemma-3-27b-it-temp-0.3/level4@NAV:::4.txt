The paper presents a new application of neural networks: learning representations of symbolic expressions and discovering mathematical identities between them. Despite the current limitations in the complexity of expressions addressed, and the lack of a formal guarantee of scalability, I find the work intriguing and believe it holds potential benefits for symbolic computation software.

Regarding the stated goal of identifying expressions with reduced computational complexity, the algorithm does not currently incorporate any mechanisms to enforce this constraint. While acknowledging the difficulty, I would be interested to know if the authors have considered strategies for limiting the search space to prioritize solutions with lower complexity.

Concerning the restrictions outlined in Section 2, it is unclear whether the scheduler is the sole limiting factor preventing the handling of more complex mathematical expressions. I would appreciate further justification for these restrictions, ideally through formal analysis or empirical investigation.

The paper’s structure currently hinders clarity; a more explicit and sequential explanation of the different stages would improve readability. 

Finally, the paper lacks discussion of constraints on vector/matrix size. For instance, the expression for RBM-1 in the supplementary material (for k=1, n=1) appears to be invalid.

I also noted a few minor typos: 
Line 332: “show for”
Line 148: “has contains”
Line 289: “is is”
Line 338: “by be”

Overall, this is a novel approach to representing symbolic expressions and identifying mathematical identities, though currently limited in scope.
