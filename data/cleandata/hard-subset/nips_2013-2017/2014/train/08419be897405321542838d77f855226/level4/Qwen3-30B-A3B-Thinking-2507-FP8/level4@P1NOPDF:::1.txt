

This paper applies deep neural networks (DNNs) to the novel task of discovering mathematical identities, and while the core idea is intriguing, the work represents preliminary research that fails to meet NIPS's publication standards. The method demonstrates unremarkable performance in identifying identities, particularly given the highly constrained input space—expressions were drawn from an extremely limited set of examples. In such a narrow domain, deriving closed-form rules through conventional mathematical analysis would likely be more efficient and effective than deploying DNNs, making the approach feel like an overkill for the problem size.  

The authors suggest their framework could generalize to broader expression classes, but the current results provide no clear path to scalability. The modest performance on the presented task raises questions about whether extending the method would yield meaningful gains or even be theoretically justified. Without evidence of significant advantages over simpler, domain-specific techniques, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative.  

I acknowledge the paper's potential as a starting point for future work, but its current execution—lacking substantial empirical validation, theoretical grounding, and clear evidence of broader applicability—falls short of NIPS's expectations. The work is interesting, but it is slightly below the threshold for publication.
