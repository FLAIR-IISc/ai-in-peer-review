This paper explores the novel application of deep neural networks (DNNs) to the problem of discovering mathematical identities. While the core idea is interesting, my overall assessment is that the work, in its current form, represents a promising but preliminary investigation that may not yet meet the high standards for publication at NeurIPS. 

My primary concern lies with the performance demonstrated. Even with a deliberately constrained input space, the method does not achieve particularly compelling results in identity discovery. The expressions used for evaluation were limited in scope, to the extent that developing a targeted, rule-based approach might have been a more efficient and effective solution. The current approach feels somewhat akin to using a complex tool for a relatively simple task. 

The authors rightly suggest the potential for scaling this method to more general and complex expressions. However, it is unclear whether the current architecture and training methodology would readily translate to such a broader domain. Moreover, given the modest initial results, the benefit of such scaling is questionable. It’s not evident that increased complexity would necessarily yield significant improvements, or justify the computational cost.

Specifically, it would be helpful to understand:

*   **Baseline Comparison:** How does the performance of the DNN compare to simpler symbolic manipulation techniques or automated theorem provers, even on this limited dataset? Establishing a clear baseline would better contextualize the contribution.
*   **Scalability Analysis:** A more detailed discussion of the challenges associated with scaling the approach – both in terms of computational resources and architectural adaptations – would be valuable. What modifications would be necessary to handle significantly more complex expressions?
*   **Representation Learning:** The paper would benefit from a deeper analysis of *what* the DNN is learning. Is it identifying fundamental mathematical structures, or simply memorizing patterns within the limited training set? Visualizing the learned representations could offer insights.
*   **Error Analysis:** A thorough error analysis could reveal patterns in the identities the DNN struggles with, suggesting potential avenues for improvement.

Despite these concerns, the exploration of DNNs in this domain is a valuable endeavor. The concept of leveraging neural networks for symbolic reasoning is intriguing, and this paper takes a first step in that direction. However, at this stage, the work feels somewhat underdeveloped and below the threshold for NeurIPS publication. Further investigation, coupled with a more rigorous evaluation and analysis, could substantially strengthen the contribution.
