- Proposes a novel framework that represents symbolic expressions with an attribute grammar and searches for analytically equivalent, lower-complexity expression trees.  
- Recognizes exponential growth of the search space and introduces learning-based strategies to guide the search.  
- Uses n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) trained on simpler solutions to discover identities for more complex targets.  
- Applies an RNN to learn continuous embeddings of symbolic expressions, drawing an analogy to NLP word/sentence embeddings.  
- Distinguishes itself from automated theorem proving and program induction by relying on statistical learning rather than fixed rules or heuristics.  
- Strength: highly original application of machine learning/deep learning to discover mathematical identities, with non-trivial new identities found.  
- Strength: the RNN-based continuous representation for symbolic expressions is a significant, broadly applicable idea with high reported classification accuracy.  
- Strength: well-integrated framework combining attribute grammars, equivalence checking, and learning-guided search.  
- Strength: paper is clearly written and well-organized.  
- Weakness: experiments are limited to homogeneous polynomials of a single matrix/vector, raising questions about generality.  
- Weakness: RNN performance is underwhelming compared to simpler n-gram models and offers limited advantage on the hardest problem family.  
- Weakness: unclear scalability when expanding the grammar of mathematical operators or increasing search branching.  
- Summary: promising and creative proof-of-concept but preliminary, with significant challenges remaining to scale and generalize the approach.
