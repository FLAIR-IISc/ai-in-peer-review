This paper presents two methods for finding simpler, more computationally efficient symbolic expression trees. The first approach uses n-grams computed over sequences of sub-expressions to guide the search. The second method employs a recursive neural network to learn continuous vector representations of the symbolic trees. These representations are then used to predict the optimal rule for combining subtrees. Notably, the results presented in Figure 3 suggest that the n-gram model outperforms the RNN on two of the three evaluation tasks.

**Comments on specific review criteria:**

**Quality:** High.

**Clarity:** The manuscript's clarity could be improved. The exposition is sometimes difficult to follow, with Section 7.2 being particularly challenging to understand.

**Originality:** High.

**Significance:** Low. The main weakness of this paper is its limited practical significance. While the proposed methods for searching for more computationally efficient symbolic expression trees are novel, the problem itself seems contrived. It is difficult to envision a practical scenario where these techniques would be adopted by practitioners seeking to perform efficient computations. As a result, the overall impact and relevance of the work are unclear.
