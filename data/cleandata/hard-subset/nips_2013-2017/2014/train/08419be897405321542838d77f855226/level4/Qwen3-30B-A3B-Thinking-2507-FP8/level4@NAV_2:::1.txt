

This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques applied to attribute grammars. The authors propose a grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions, combined with learning approaches (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for computationally efficient equivalents of target expressions. The work demonstrates how training on simpler expressions can enable discovery of complex identities that would be intractable through brute-force search.

The paper makes several noteworthy contributions: a grammar framework for symbolic expression search, integration of learning techniques to guide this search, a novel application of recursive neural networks for continuous representation of mathematical structures, and the discovery of previously unknown mathematical identities with significant computational complexity reductions.

In terms of quality, the technical approach is sound and well-structured. The authors provide clear experimental results showing the effectiveness of their learning approaches for specific families of expressions. However, the scope of the experiments is limited to a restricted class of polynomial expressions, and the improvements over brute-force search appear modest for the complexity of the learning approaches employed. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with alternative approaches for the same problem space.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with clear explanations of the grammar framework and learning approaches. The notation is consistent, and the examples provided (particularly Examples 1 and 2) effectively illustrate the core concepts. Some additional detail on the limitations of the approach would strengthen the presentation.

The originality of the work is moderate. While the application of attribute grammars to mathematical identity discovery is novel, the learning techniques themselves (n-grams and RNNs) are well-established in other domains. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from related work in program induction and symbolic computation, particularly regarding how the proposed method differs from existing symbolic manipulation systems like Maple or Mathematica.

Regarding significance, the paper demonstrates a technically interesting application of machine learning to a novel domain, but the practical impact appears limited to a narrow class of mathematical expressions. The claimed computational complexity improvements (e.g., O(n³) to O(n²)) are promising for the specific cases studied, but it is unclear how broadly these results would generalize to more complex mathematical problems. The potential applications to machine learning (e.g., dropout, RBM partition functions) are mentioned but not thoroughly explored.

Strengths:
- Novel application of machine learning to mathematical identity discovery
- Clear demonstration of the grammar framework
- Effective use of n-grams and RNNs for guiding search
- Discovery of new mathematical identities with computational complexity benefits

Weaknesses:
- Limited scope of experiments (restricted to specific polynomial families)
- Learning approaches appear over-engineered for the problem size
- Insufficient comparison with alternative methods
- Limited discussion of generalizability to broader mathematical domains
- The claimed significance of the results is not fully supported by the experimental results

While the paper presents an interesting idea with potential for future work, the current results do not demonstrate sufficient advancement of the state-of-the-art to warrant publication at NIPS. The work would benefit from more extensive experiments on a wider range of mathematical problems, stronger comparisons with alternative approaches, and a more compelling argument for the broader significance of the results. The current version appears to be preliminary work that would be better suited for a more specialized venue focused on symbolic computation rather than a general machine learning conference like NIPS.
