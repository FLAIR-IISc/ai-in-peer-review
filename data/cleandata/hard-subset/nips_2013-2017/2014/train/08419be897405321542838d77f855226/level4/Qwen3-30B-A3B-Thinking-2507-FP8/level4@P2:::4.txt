

This paper presents an innovative approach to discovering mathematical identities using machine learning techniques, specifically applying attribute grammars and neural networks to search for computationally efficient representations of symbolic expressions. The work builds on the attribute grammar framework introduced by Knuth (1968) and extends it with machine learning techniques to guide the search for efficient expressions, rather than relying on brute-force enumeration. The authors demonstrate how n-gram models and recursive neural networks can learn from simpler expressions to discover more complex identities, with applications to matrix operations and polynomial expressions.

**Quality:** The paper makes a technically sound contribution with a clear methodology for discovering mathematical identities. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the learning approaches in finding efficient identities beyond brute-force search capabilities. The authors properly evaluate both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, acknowledging limitations in the scope of expressions they can handle. The numerical representation using integer-based descriptors effectively avoids the numerical issues inherent in floating-point comparisons. However, the paper does not sufficiently address how the computational complexity constraint is enforced during the search process. While the paper states the goal is to find expressions with lower computational complexity, the algorithm itself does not explicitly incorporate this constraint, which is a significant gap. The authors should clarify how they ensure the discovered expressions actually meet the complexity requirements, rather than just being algebraically equivalent.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized, though the presentation of the framework could be improved. The problem statement (Section 2) and the attribute grammar definition (Section 3) are clear, but the explanation of the linear combinations of trees (Section 5) and the search strategy (Section 6) could benefit from more detailed examples. The paper would be significantly improved with clearer visualizations of the grammar trees and the search process. The supplementary material appears to contain valuable examples (like Example 2 for k=6), but these should be more prominently featured in the main paper. The description of the learned representation (Section 4.2) is technically sound but could be made more accessible to readers unfamiliar with recursive neural networks.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant contribution by applying machine learning to the problem of discovering mathematical identities, which has been largely unexplored in the literature. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work in theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation engines. The application of n-gram models and recursive neural networks to learn search strategies for symbolic expressions represents a novel combination of techniques. The authors cite relevant prior work in attribute grammars (Knuth, 1968) and NLP applications (Socher et al., 2013), but they could strengthen their position by more explicitly contrasting with the limited prior work on learning for symbolic computation (Bridge et al., 2013).

**Significance:** The paper addresses a meaningful problem with practical implications for symbolic computation systems. The discovered identities offer significant computational complexity reductions for specific expressions, which could potentially improve the efficiency of mathematical software libraries. The approach has promising applications in machine learning contexts, such as efficiently computing partition functions for RBMs or implementing dropout more efficiently. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the restrictive assumptions made about the target expressions (single matrix/vector, homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree). The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how these limitations might be overcome to broaden the applicability of the framework.

**Strengths:** The paper's main strengths include the clear demonstration of how learning can guide the search for mathematical identities, the effective use of integer-based descriptors to avoid numerical issues, and the compelling experimental results showing the superiority of learning-based approaches over random search for complex identities. The discovery of novel identities (e.g., the efficient computation of (∑(A.*A)AT)k) is particularly impressive.

**Weaknesses:** The most significant weakness is the lack of explicit enforcement of computational complexity constraints during the search process. The paper also doesn't sufficiently address how the restrictions on expression complexity (single matrix/vector, homogeneous polynomials) limit the framework's applicability. The paper's structure could be improved to make the progression of ideas more transparent, and the supplementary material should be referenced more explicitly in the main text.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to an interesting problem with clear potential applications. The main issues to address are:
1. Explicitly explain how computational complexity constraints are enforced during the search process.
2. Provide a more thorough discussion of the framework's limitations and potential extensions to broader mathematical domains.
3. Improve the clarity of the paper's structure with better visualizations of the search process and grammar trees.
4. Address the typos noted in the draft review (332: "show for" should be "show", 148: "has contains" should be "contains", 289: "is is" should be "is", 338: "by be" should be "by").

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation, and with the suggested revisions, it would be a strong addition to the NIPS proceedings.
