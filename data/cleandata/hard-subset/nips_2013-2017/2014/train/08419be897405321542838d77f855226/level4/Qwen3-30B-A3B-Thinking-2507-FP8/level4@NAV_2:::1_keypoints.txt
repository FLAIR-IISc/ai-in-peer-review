- Introduces a grammar-based framework and learning-guided search (n-grams, recursive neural nets) to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities.  
- Demonstrates that training on simple expressions can enable discovery of complex identities infeasible by brute force.  
- Key contributions: attribute-grammar representation, learned search guidance, RNN-based continuous representations, and discovery of new identities with reduced complexity.  
- Technical approach is sound and experiments show effectiveness on specific expression families.  
- Experimental scope is narrow—limited to a restricted class of polynomial expressions.  
- Improvements over brute-force search are modest relative to the complexity of the learning methods used.  
- Lacks thorough comparisons with alternative methods and existing symbolic systems (e.g., Maple, Mathematica).  
- Clarity is generally good with consistent notation and illustrative examples, but more detail on limitations is needed.  
- Originality is moderate: novel application of grammars, but uses standard ML techniques without sufficiently distinguishing from program induction/symbolic computation prior work.  
- Significance appears limited to narrow cases; unclear generalization to broader mathematical problems despite promising O(n³)→O(n²) examples.  
- Strengths: novel ML application to identity discovery, clear grammar framework, effective n-gram/RNN guidance, and discovery of useful identities.  
- Weaknesses: limited experimental breadth, potentially over-engineered learning for problem size, insufficient baselines/comparisons, and weak generalizability argument.  
- Recommendation: current results are preliminary and better suited to a specialized symbolic-computation venue rather than NIPS without more extensive experiments and stronger comparative evidence.
