

This paper introduces an interesting framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors propose an attribute grammar-based approach to represent symbolic expressions, with the goal of finding equivalent expressions of lower computational complexity. The key innovation lies in using machine learning to guide the search for these identities, rather than relying solely on brute-force enumeration. The authors introduce two learning approaches—n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN)—that leverage solutions to simpler expressions to discover identities for more complex targets.

The paper's contributions are significant in demonstrating how learning can be integrated into symbolic computation problems. The authors show that their framework can discover mathematical identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) or even from exponential to polynomial time, which would be challenging to find through manual derivation or exhaustive search. For instance, they demonstrate how to compute expressions like ∑∑(A*B)^k efficiently for k up to 6, where the naive approach would require exponential time.

In terms of originality, this work represents a novel application of machine learning to mathematical identity discovery. While attribute grammars have been used in other domains (e.g., compiler construction, bioinformatics), the authors are correct that their application to discovering mathematical formulas using learning techniques is new. The closest related work is Bridge et al. (2013) on learning heuristics for theorem proving, but the authors' approach differs in that it learns from simpler expressions rather than using hand-designed heuristics. The paper also makes an important contribution by demonstrating how to learn continuous representations for symbolic expressions, which could enable broader application of machine learning to symbolic computation.

The quality of the work is generally strong, with thorough experiments across multiple families of expressions. The authors demonstrate that their learning approaches (particularly n-grams) outperform random search for complex targets, with success rates increasing as the complexity of the target expression grows. The framework is well-structured, with clear definitions of the problem, grammar, and representation techniques. The numerical and learned representations for symbolic expressions are particularly well-considered, with the authors addressing potential numerical issues through the use of integer-based descriptors.

However, the paper has some limitations. The problem definition is quite restricted to homogeneous polynomials of a single matrix or vector, which limits the general applicability of the framework. While the authors acknowledge this in Section 8, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how to extend the framework to broader mathematical domains. Additionally, the paper contains some unclear technical details regarding the RNN implementation. For instance, the description of the weight tensor W3 (size l×l×l) is confusing—it's not clear whether this represents a neural tensor network rather than a standard RNN. The authors should clarify this, as Bowman (2013) has shown that tensor networks often outperform standard RNNs for similar tasks.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, but could be improved in a few areas. The explanation of the search strategy (Section 6) is somewhat opaque. The paper should better clarify how the scheduler and strategy components interact, particularly how the RNN-based strategy generates proposals for the next grammar rule to apply. The draft review also identified several grammatical errors and typos that need correction (e.g., "has contains," "vector is the presented," "The scheduler is is fixed").

The significance of this work is notable, particularly for computational mathematics and machine learning. The identities discovered could potentially be used to optimize computation in areas like dropout in neural networks or computing partition functions for restricted Boltzmann machines. The framework's ability to automatically discover efficient identities represents a significant step toward applying machine learning to symbolic computation problems.

In summary, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The core idea of using learning to guide the search for mathematical identities is novel and well-executed, with strong experimental results demonstrating significant computational improvements. While the problem scope is somewhat restricted, the framework's architecture suggests potential for extension to broader mathematical domains. The paper would benefit from clearer technical explanations of the RNN implementation and more detailed discussion of the limitations and potential extensions of the framework.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel application of learning techniques to mathematical identity discovery
- Demonstrates significant computational improvements over brute-force approaches
- Well-structured framework with strong experimental validation
- Potential for broader impact in computational mathematics and machine learning

Arguments against acceptance:
- Problem definition is quite restricted (single matrix/vector, homogeneous polynomials)
- Some technical details regarding the RNN implementation are unclear
- Limited discussion of how to extend the framework to more general mathematical domains

Overall, this paper presents a compelling and well-executed approach to a challenging problem, with clear significance for computational mathematics. With minor revisions to clarify technical details and expand on limitations and extensions, it would be a strong contribution to NIPS.
