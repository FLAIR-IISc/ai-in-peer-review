- Summary: Proposes learning-guided search over an attribute grammar to discover computationally cheaper algebraic identities using modular-evaluation descriptors and an RNN embedding with modular Gaussian elimination for linear combinations.

- Strength: Novel framing casting identity discovery as guided search in attribute-grammar space.

- Strength: Effective hybrid of symbolic (modular descriptors, Gaussian elimination) and statistical (RNN embeddings) techniques for fast equivalence checking and guidance.

- Strength: Demonstrated practical impact—found nontrivial identities reducing asymptotic costs (e.g., cubic→quadratic/linear) on several families.

- Strength: Good training tricks (curriculum learning, dropout) to make the RNN work on deep trees.

- Strength: Code and data released.

- Weakness: Narrow scope—restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, usually single-variable, and a limited grammar; unclear how methods generalize when assumptions are relaxed.

- Weakness: Ambiguous RNN architecture—unclear if W3 is a full tensor (RNTN) or standard recursive parametrization; request comparison tensor vs non-tensor variants.

- Weakness: Unclear role of RNN in final search—need per-family/k reporting of which strategy (RNN, n-gram, random) discovered each identity.

- Weakness: Proposal-generation/scheduler interface under-specified—missing details on rule enumeration, ranking, beam search/backtracking, multi-starts, and two-branch rule combination; pseudocode requested.

- Weakness: Correctness guarantees insufficient—single-prime modular checks risk collisions; need multi-prime or final symbolic verification and clarification of N (evaluation points) vs number of linear equations.

- Weakness: Evaluation depth issues—n-gram often outperforms RNN for larger k; more analysis/ablations on RNN failure modes and embedding dimensionality suggested.

- Weakness: Grammar design choices (e.g., removing matmul) need justification and discussion of completeness and whether they exclude valid efficient identities.

- Quality: Generally sound algorithmically but would benefit from clearer algorithmic specs and stronger verification (multiple primes or symbolic checks); include explicit cost analyses for representative discoveries.

- Clarity: High-level narrative is clear but crucial implementation details (scheduler, proposal scoring, training data per k, exact RNN operator parametrization) need tightening; pseudocode and scheduler–strategy figure recommended.

- Originality: Applying attribute grammars with learned guidance to identity discovery is novel; RNN-based embeddings for algebraic expressions are an interesting contribution.

- Significance: Promising for compiler/CAS optimizations and some ML computations, but current narrow scope and mixed hardest-case performance limit near-term breadth.

- Pros for acceptance: Innovative formulation, practical wins on nontrivial identities, solid symbolic+learned combination, and public code.

- Cons for acceptance: Narrow scope, underspecified algorithmic details, n-gram often beating RNN, and limited guarantees against equivalence collisions.

- Minor comments/typos: Multiple wording and typographical errors require fixes (examples listed by reviewer: “has contains a single matrix” → “contains a single matrix”, “listed in table Table 1.” → “listed in Table 1.”, Fig.3 label “TNN” → “RNN”, etc.).

- Overall recommendation: Creative and potentially impactful; recommend acceptance conditional on clarifying RNN architecture, scheduler/proposal mechanism, and strengthening correctness verification and search-strategy analysis.
