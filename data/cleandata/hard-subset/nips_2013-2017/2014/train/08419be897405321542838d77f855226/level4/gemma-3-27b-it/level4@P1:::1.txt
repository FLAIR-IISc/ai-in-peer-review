This paper explores the application of machine learning techniques to the discovery of efficient mathematical identities. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and aim to find computationally simpler expressions equivalent to a target, but with lower complexity. Recognizing the exponential growth of the search space, they propose using n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) to guide the tree search, learning from simpler expressions to tackle more complex ones. They demonstrate the ability to derive identities beyond manual derivation or brute-force search.

This work builds upon existing research in theorem proving [5, 9, 11], program induction [18, 28], and probabilistic programming [12, 20], but limits its scope to polynomials of fixed degree, avoiding the inherent undecidability issues of those broader fields. The approach leverages attribute grammars [14], previously used in compiler construction and other optimization problems [19, 26, 10], but uniquely applies them to the discovery of mathematical formulas with a learning component. The authors also draw inspiration from natural language processing techniques, utilizing n-gram models and RNNs similar to those employed in NLP [15, 22, 23, 3].

The strengths of this paper lie in its novel application of machine learning to symbolic manipulation and identity discovery. The grammar framework provides a structured approach to exploring the space of possible expressions. The experimental results demonstrate the ability to find efficient identities for specific cases, achieving reductions in computational complexity (e.g., O(n3) to O(n2)). The provided code and evaluation data ([https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning](https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning)) promote reproducibility. The learned representation of symbolic expressions using an RNN is a particularly interesting contribution, potentially opening avenues for applying other machine learning tools to this domain.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. While the demonstrated complexity reductions are valuable, the scope of the experiments is relatively limited. The focus on specific families of expressions raises questions about the generalizability of the approach. The performance gains achieved with the learning-based strategies are not always substantial, and in some cases, the n-gram models appear to outperform the more complex RNN. The examples provided, while illustrative, are somewhat contrived, and it is unclear how readily the method could be applied to more realistic or complex mathematical problems. The success appears to heavily rely on the specific structure of the identities being sought (e.g. repetitive patterns), and the scalability of the approach to genuinely novel or less structured problems is not convincingly demonstrated. The statement that the identities discovered are, “as far as we are aware, previously undiscovered,” seems bold and would benefit from a more thorough literature review.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of machine learning to the discovery of mathematical identities.
*   Demonstrated ability to find efficient expressions, offering computational improvements.
*   Interesting use of an RNN for learning a continuous representation of symbolic expressions.
*   Publicly available code and evaluation data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of experiments and focus on specific expression families.
*   Modest performance gains with learning-based strategies in some cases.
*   Scalability to more general or complex problems is not adequately demonstrated.
*   Potential for simpler, rule-based approaches to achieve similar results for the considered examples.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable approach to mathematical identity discovery. However, the limited scope, modest performance gains, and questions about scalability raise concerns about its readiness for a top-tier conference like NIPS. While the technical contributions are sound, the paper currently feels more like a promising proof-of-concept than a fully developed, impactful contribution. I lean towards rejection, but would encourage the authors to consider submitting a revised version to a workshop or a more specialized conference.
