This paper presents a novel application of neural networks: learning representations of symbolic expressions and discovering corresponding mathematical identities. Although the class of expressions considered is quite restricted and there is no demonstration that the approach scales to more complex cases, the idea is interesting and could have meaningful impact on symbolic mathematics software.

Strengths
- Clear and timely problem motivation: bridging neural learning with symbolic algebra.
- Potential practical impact for computer algebra systems and symbolic libraries.
- The representation-learning perspective on identities is novel and thought-provoking.

Main concerns and questions
- Objective/constraint mismatch: The problem statement emphasizes finding expressions with lower computational complexity, yet the algorithm does not appear to enforce or explicitly optimize for computational cost. Could the authors incorporate cost-aware mechanisms (e.g., operation-count regularization, FLOP/time/memory-based cost models, MDL-style complexity penalties, or beam search constrained by a complexity budget)? Even a simple heuristic (such as penalizing expression length or depth) would better align the method with the stated objective and help limit the search space.
- Restrictions in Section 2: Is the scheduler the only limiting factor preventing more complex expressions, or are there other architectural or training constraints (e.g., typing, arity, non-commutativity, broadcasting rules)? It would help to provide either a formal justification for the chosen restrictions or empirical ablations demonstrating the necessity and impact of each restriction on performance and tractability.
- Clarity and organization: The current presentation is hard to follow. Please make the pipeline/stages explicit and easy to track. Suggestions:
  - Number and name the stages (e.g., representation learning, candidate generation, verification).
  - Provide a concise algorithm box or flow diagram.
  - Include a small running example that threads through all stages.
  - Summarize inputs/outputs and assumptions per stage (e.g., typing rules, dimensionality constraints).
- Dimensionality and typing constraints: Are there explicit constraints on vector/matrix dimensions to ensure validity of composed operations? For example, for k=1, n=1, the RBM-1 expression in the supplementary material appears invalid under standard dimensionality rules. Please check and clarify the assumptions about broadcasting, indexing, and type-checking, and consider adding a simple type system or dimension-checking rule set that guarantees well-formed expressions.
- Generalization and scope: While the paper acknowledges limited expressivity, it would strengthen the work to either:
  - Provide evidence of generalization to a broader class of identities, or
  - Clearly delineate the current boundary of expressivity and share a roadmap (e.g., necessary scheduler or representation changes) for scaling up.

Minor issues and typos
- Line 148: “has contains” → “contains”
- Line 289: “is is” → “is”
- Line 332: “show for” → consider “show that” (or rephrase depending on context)
- Line 338: “by be” → “by”
- Please also check for duplicated or redundant sentences in the concluding remarks; one instance reads like a repetition of the main claim.

Overall assessment
- Novelty: High within the niche of learning representations for symbolic identities.
- Technical depth: Promising but currently limited by restrictive assumptions and lack of explicit cost modeling.
- Clarity: Needs improvement, especially in the pipeline description and dimensionality/type constraints.
- Impact: Potentially significant for symbolic computation if the approach can be extended and aligned with computational cost objectives.

Recommendation: Borderline accept. I lean positive due to the novelty and potential impact, contingent on clarifying the pipeline, addressing the complexity objective, and tightening the dimensionality/type constraints.
