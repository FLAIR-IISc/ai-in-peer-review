Summary:
The paper proposes two approaches for learning to search over symbolic expression trees to discover simpler, more computationally efficient equivalents. The first approach uses n-gram models computed over linearizations of sub-expressions to guide which rewrite rules to apply. The second uses a recursive neural network (RNN) to embed trees into continuous representations, which are then used to predict the most promising rule for combining or rewriting subtrees. Experiments on three tasks indicate that the n-gram approach outperforms the RNN on two of them (as shown in Figure 3).

Strengths:
- Originality: Applying data-driven models (n-grams and recursive neural networks) to guide symbolic rewriting and expression simplification is a novel and interesting direction.
- Technical quality: The methods are clearly defined at a high level, and the evaluation appears systematic. The comparison between a simple probabilistic model and a learned compositional representation is informative.
- Insight: The empirical finding that an n-gram model can outperform an RNN in this setting is a useful contribution that can inform future model choices and data requirements for learned rewrite guidance.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Significance and motivation: The practical significance is unclear. The tasks and problem setup feel contrived, and the paper does not convincingly argue that the proposed methods would yield meaningful gains in real-world settings (e.g., compiler optimization, computer algebra systems, or program synthesis). Without stronger motivation or demonstrations on realistic benchmarks, the impact is limited.
- Baselines: The comparison set appears narrow. It would be important to compare against strong, domain-relevant baselines such as established rule-based simplifiers (e.g., from computer algebra systems), common-subexpression elimination and peephole optimization heuristics, or search guided by handcrafted cost functions.
- Clarity and detail: The paper is at times difficult to follow, particularly Section 7.2. Key details are either missing or buried, including how trees are linearized for the n-gram model, the n-gram order and smoothing strategy, the precise RNN architecture and training setup, how rewrite rules are generated and constrained, and the mechanics of the search procedure (e.g., beam size, stopping criteria, tie-breaking).
- Analysis: The paper does not offer sufficient analysis to explain why the RNN underperforms on most tasks. Is the performance gap due to data scarcity, model capacity, training labels, or the inductive biases of the linearization? Error analysis and ablations (e.g., effect of linearization order, rule set size, beam width) would strengthen the conclusions.
- Evaluation metrics and efficiency: If the goal is computational efficiency, the evaluation should quantify end-to-end speedups or complexity reductions, not just rewrite success rates. Reporting runtime, number of rule applications, and asymptotic or empirical cost reductions would make the results more compelling.

Questions for the authors:
- How are training labels obtained for predicting rewrite rules? Do you have gold sequences or only end states? If the latter, how is credit assigned to intermediate decisions?
- What linearization strategy is used to construct n-grams from trees (preorder, postorder, bracketed strings), and how sensitive are results to this choice?
- What is the exact grammar/rule set? Are all rules semantics-preserving? How do you ensure search remains within the equivalence class of the original expression?
- What are the beam width, scoring function, and stopping criteria for the search? How do you trade off predicted likelihood vs. estimated cost reductions?
- How large are the training sets, and how do they compare to the parameter counts of the RNN? Did you try pretraining, regularization, or alternate architectures (e.g., TreeLSTM)?
- Can you provide comparisons to existing symbolic simplifiers or compiler optimizations on shared benchmarks, and report wall-clock improvements?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Strengthen the motivation with concrete use cases (e.g., compiler peephole optimizations, differentiable programming, automatic code generation), and demonstrate benefits on realistic workloads.
- Expand the baseline suite to include rule-based systems and heuristic search strategies commonly used in compilers and CAS tools.
- Clarify Section 7.2 and add a concise, self-contained description of the search algorithm, including pseudocode and all hyperparameters.
- Provide ablations and error analyses to isolate the contributions of the model, the rule set, the linearization, and the search parameters.
- Report efficiency metrics: runtime, number of rewrites, achieved complexity reductions, and end-to-end speedups where applicable.
- Discuss why the n-gram model outperforms the RNN on most tasks and what this implies about data requirements and inductive biases in this domain.

Ratings:
- Quality: High. The technical ideas are sound and the empirical procedures are generally reasonable, though additional analysis and baselines are needed.
- Clarity: Moderate. The paper is readable overall but several sections, especially Section 7.2, need clearer exposition and more detail.
- Originality: High. Learning-guided symbolic rewriting with both n-grams and recursive neural networks is novel and timely.
- Significance: Low to Moderate. Without stronger motivation and demonstrations on realistic problems, the broader impact is limited.

Overall assessment:
A technically solid and original paper with interesting empirical observations, especially the relative strength of the n-gram approach. However, the current presentation leaves the real-world significance unclear, the evaluation lacks key baselines and efficiency metrics, and Section 7.2 needs substantial clarification. I encourage the authors to tighten the exposition, broaden the evaluation, and better motivate the practical value of learned guidance for symbolic expression simplification.
