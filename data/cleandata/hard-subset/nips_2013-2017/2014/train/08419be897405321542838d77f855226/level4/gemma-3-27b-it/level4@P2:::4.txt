This paper presents a novel application of machine learning to the discovery of efficient mathematical identities. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and leverage both n-gram models and recursive neural networks to guide a search for computationally cheaper, yet equivalent, formulations of target expressions. While the scope is currently limited to a specific class of polynomial expressions, the approach demonstrates promising results and opens up interesting avenues for future research, potentially impacting symbolic software libraries and optimization tools.

The core idea is well-motivated, building on the observation that machine learning excels at pattern recognition and applying this capability to the symbolic domain. The proposed framework, utilizing attribute grammars, provides a structured way to explore the space of possible expressions. The use of learned models to direct the search, rather than relying on brute-force or random exploration, is a key contribution, enabling the discovery of identities beyond manual derivation or exhaustive search. The authors provide compelling examples, including derivations of expressions with reduced computational complexity (e.g., O(n<sup>3</sup>) to O(n<sup>2</sup>)). 

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The restriction to homogeneous polynomials of degree *k* with a single matrix variable, while simplifying the problem, feels somewhat arbitrary and the justification (Section 2) is not entirely convincing. It's unclear whether the scheduler alone limits scalability to more complex expressions or if other factors are at play. More discussion or empirical analysis of these limitations would be valuable. Additionally, the paper’s structure could be improved for clarity. The flow between sections feels disjointed, and a more explicit breakdown of the methodology would enhance readability. For instance, a clear algorithmic description alongside the conceptual explanation of the search strategy would be beneficial. 

A potential concern is the lack of explicit enforcement of computational complexity reduction within the algorithm. While the goal is to find more efficient expressions, the search is not explicitly constrained to prioritize lower complexity.  It would be interesting to see a discussion of how such constraints could be integrated into the search process.  Furthermore, the impact of vector/matrix size is not adequately addressed.  An example in the supplementary material (k=1, n=1 for RBM-1) appears invalid, suggesting a possible oversight in handling edge cases. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of machine learning to discover mathematical identities is a novel and potentially impactful idea.
* **Promising Results:** The demonstrated ability to derive more efficient expressions, beyond human capability, is significant.
* **Well-Defined Framework:** The attribute grammar provides a structured approach to exploring the space of symbolic expressions.
* **Clear Presentation of Examples:** The provided examples effectively illustrate the benefits of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope:** The current restrictions on the type of expressions considered limit the generalizability of the approach.
* **Lack of Complexity Constraints:**  The search algorithm does not explicitly prioritize lower computational complexity.
* **Structural Clarity:** The paper’s organization could be improved for better readability.
* **Edge Case Handling:** Potential issues with edge cases and vector/matrix sizes need further investigation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a novel and challenging problem in symbolic computation.
* The proposed approach demonstrates promising results and offers a new perspective on identity discovery.
* The work is well-written and clearly presents the key ideas and contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The limited scope and lack of exploration of potential generalizations may raise concerns about the broader impact of the work.
* The lack of explicit complexity constraints in the search process is a potential weakness.
* The paper could benefit from a more structured presentation and clearer explanation of the methodology.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its limitations, I believe this paper represents a worthwhile contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, coupled with the promising results, makes it worthy of acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the clarity of the presentation and further discussion of the limitations and potential extensions of the work. Specifically, the authors should elaborate on the restrictions of their approach, consider incorporating complexity constraints into the search process, and address the potential issues with edge case handling.
