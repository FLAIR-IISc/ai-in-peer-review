

The paper introduces an innovative framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors propose a grammar-based approach to represent symbolic expressions and apply n-gram models and recursive neural networks to guide the search for efficient versions of target expressions. This work represents a novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation, bridging the gap between traditional symbolic manipulation systems and modern learning-based approaches.

The paper's main contributions include: (1) a grammar framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities, (2) integration of machine learning techniques to guide the search process, (3) a novel application of recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of mathematical structures, and (4) discovery of previously unknown mathematical identities with significant computational complexity reductions. The authors demonstrate their approach on several families of polynomial expressions, showing reductions from O(n³) to O(n²) complexity for certain targets.

Regarding quality, the paper presents a technically sound approach with well-designed experiments. The authors carefully evaluate both the strengths and weaknesses of their method, including thorough comparisons of different search strategies. The experimental results clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of their learning-based approaches compared to random search, particularly for complex expressions. The paper provides sufficient details for reproduction, including code and data available on GitHub. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the computational complexity claims are well-supported by examples.

The paper shows strong originality by applying machine learning techniques to a domain where they have been largely unexplored. While there is prior work on symbolic computation and theorem proving (e.g., [5, 9, 11]), the authors' focus on learning search strategies for efficient expression discovery represents a significant novelty. The attribute grammar framework, though inspired by compiler construction [14], is applied in a novel way to this optimization problem. The paper also makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating how recursive neural networks can learn continuous representations for symbolic expressions, opening the door for applying other machine learning techniques to symbolic domains.

The significance of this work is substantial for both theoretical and practical reasons. The discovered identities have potential applications in machine learning libraries (e.g., for more efficient implementation of dropout or RBM partition function approximations), and the framework could potentially be integrated with compilers or other optimization tools. The paper demonstrates that machine learning can effectively guide combinatorial search in symbolic domains, which could inspire similar approaches for other AI problems involving combinatorial explosions.

However, several concerns should be addressed. First, the paper's scope is quite limited to homogeneous polynomials with specific structural constraints (Section 2). While the authors acknowledge these limitations, the paper does not sufficiently discuss how these constraints might impact broader applicability or provide evidence that the approach could be extended to more complex expressions. The paper states that "the framework can easily handle multiple variables" (Section 2), but this claim is not substantiated with examples or experiments.

Second, the search strategy section (Section 6) lacks sufficient clarity. The relationship between the scheduler, strategy, and grammar tree construction is not well-explained, making it difficult to understand how the learning approaches actually guide the search process. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of how the RNN and n-gram models specifically influence the search path.

Third, the paper doesn't adequately address how the approach might handle expressions outside its current scope. For instance, the discussion of RBM-1 and RBM-2 families (Section 7.2) shows promising results, but the paper doesn't clarify how these results might generalize to more complex expressions or whether the framework could be extended to handle them.

The paper has minor issues with clarity and presentation. Some sections (particularly Section 6) are difficult to follow, and the paper would benefit from clearer organization and more explicit transitions between ideas. There are also minor grammatical errors and typos (e.g., "show for" on page 332, "has contains" on page 148).

Despite these concerns, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating how machine learning can be applied to symbolic computation in a meaningful way. The experiments are well-designed and provide convincing evidence of the approach's effectiveness. The discovered identities represent genuine new contributions to the mathematical literature, with practical implications for computational efficiency.

For acceptance consideration, the paper's strengths include its novel approach, strong technical execution, and significant computational improvements demonstrated in the experiments. The main weaknesses are the limited scope of the problem considered and insufficient discussion of how the framework might be extended to more general mathematical expressions.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation with clear technical contributions. While the scope is limited, the results are promising and the framework could potentially be extended to broader applications. The paper would benefit from clearer exposition of the search strategy and more thorough discussion of the framework's limitations and potential extensions. Given these considerations, I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address these concerns in the final version.
