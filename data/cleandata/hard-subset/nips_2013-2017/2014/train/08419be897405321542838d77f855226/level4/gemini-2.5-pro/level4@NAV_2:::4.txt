This paper explores the application of machine learning techniques to the discovery of computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions within an attribute grammar framework, generating expression trees that are equivalent to a target expression but have lower computational complexity. As a brute-force search of the exponentially large tree space is intractable, the authors propose using learning-based search strategies. These strategies, including an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN), are trained on solutions from simpler expressions to guide the search towards promising candidates for more complex targets. This approach is related to work in automated theorem proving and program induction, but distinguishes itself by its focus on computational efficiency rather than logical proof and its novel use of statistical learning methods, like those from NLP, to guide the symbolic search.

**Strengths**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The application of modern machine learning, particularly recursive neural networks, to discover new mathematical identities is highly novel. Learning a continuous representation for symbolic expressions is a creative idea that could open up the field of symbolic computation to a wider range of learning-based approaches. The potential impact is significant, as the discovered identities could be integrated into compilers or symbolic math libraries to automatically optimize code.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates the discovery of several non-trivial identities for complex expression families (e.g., symmetric polynomials and RBM-related terms) that are beyond the reach of brute-force search. Finding polynomial-time equivalents for expressions that are naively exponential to compute is a powerful result.
*   **Completeness:** The work is a complete research contribution, presenting the framework, the learning methods, and extensive experiments. The public release of code and data is commendable and enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper's structure can be difficult to follow. The relationship and flow between the core components—the attribute grammar, the numerical and learned representations, the linear combination of trees, and the search strategies—could be explained more clearly. A high-level diagram illustrating how these parts fit together in the overall system would greatly improve readability.
*   **Constraint Enforcement:** The problem is defined as finding expressions with *lower* computational complexity. However, the search algorithm itself does not seem to explicitly optimize for or enforce this. For some experiments, this is achieved by manually restricting the grammar (e.g., removing matrix-matrix multiplication). It is unclear how the framework would guarantee more efficient solutions in a more general setting. A discussion on potential methods to bias the search towards lower-complexity solutions would strengthen the paper.
*   **Scope and Generalizability:** The framework is currently limited to a restricted class of expressions (homogeneous polynomials of a single variable resulting in a scalar). While this is a necessary simplification to make the problem tractable, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the specific challenges in extending the framework. For instance, what are the primary obstacles—be it the scheduler, the representation, or the grammar itself—to handling more complex functions or data structures?

**Questions and Detailed Comments**
*   Regarding the restrictions mentioned in Section 2: a more formal justification or empirical evidence for why these specific restrictions were chosen would be helpful.
*   Is there a constraint on the size of the input vector/matrix for the discovered identities to hold? For example, the supplementary material's expression for the RBM-1 family with k=1 and n=1 does not appear to be valid. Please clarify.
