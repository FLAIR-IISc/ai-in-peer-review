- Introduces two methods for discovering simplified symbolic expression trees: an n-gram-guided search and an RNN that embeds tree structures to predict subtree-combination rules.  
- Empirically, the n-gram method outperforms the RNN on two of three benchmarks; the RNN is competitive on the third.  
- Quality is high: clear technical contributions, rigorous experiments, and thorough ablation studies.  
- Minor quality weaknesses: limited discussion of computational-complexity trade-offs and no comparisons to established symbolic tools (e.g., SymPy, Mathematica).  
- Clarity is moderate but problematic: Section 7.2 is overly notational and lacks intuition, pseudocode, and concrete examples.  
- Transition from the n-gram method to the RNN approach is abrupt; Figure 3’s caption omits task definitions and baseline details.  
- Originality is high: novel combination of n-gram modeling with symbolic search and use of RNNs for tree-structured symbolic representations.  
- Significance is weak: paper fails to demonstrate compelling practical motivation or real-world impact.  
- Lacks comparative relevance: no benchmarks against state-of-the-art symbolic simplifiers or demonstrations in frameworks like TensorFlow.  
- Scalability concerns: performance on large-scale expressions (e.g., >100 operations) is not evaluated.  
- Claims of “efficient symbolic computation” seem misaligned with industry priorities (vectorization, memory, hardware), with no evidence of tangible benefits (e.g., latency reductions).  
- Recommendation: Weak reject—technically competent and novel but insufficiently justified in significance and hindered by clarity issues.  
- Suggested fixes: reframe toward a concrete, impactful problem; add experiments vs. standard symbolic tools and end-to-end performance metrics; provide pseudocode and step-by-step examples for the RNN method.
