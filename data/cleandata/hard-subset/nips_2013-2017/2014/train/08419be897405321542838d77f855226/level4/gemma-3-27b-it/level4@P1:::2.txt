This paper describes two methods for searching for simpler symbolic expression trees. One of these is based on the use of n-grams computed over sequences of sub-expressions, and the other uses a recursive neural network to create continuous space representations of symbolic expression trees. These representations are then used to predict the next rule likely to be used in combining subtrees. The experimental results, shown in Figure 3, suggest that the n-gram approach outperforms the RNN on two of the three tested families of expressions.

**Comments with respect to specific review criteria:**

**Quality:** High. The technical approach appears sound and well-motivated. The authors provide sufficient detail to understand the proposed methods and the experimental setup. The use of attribute grammars and the integration of machine learning techniques are well-justified.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clear, though some sections could benefit from further refinement. Section 7.2, in particular, could be more clearly explained. The notation is generally consistent, and the examples help illustrate the core concepts. Providing more intuitive explanations of the grammar rules and the search process would improve readability.

**Originality:** High. The combination of attribute grammars with machine learning techniques for discovering mathematical identities is novel. While related work exists in theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, the authors effectively position their work as distinct, focusing on a specific problem domain and employing learning to guide the search process. The application of a recursive neural network to learn continuous representations of mathematical structures is also a significant contribution.

**Significance:** Moderate. While the problem itself might be considered somewhat contrived, the demonstrated ability to discover previously unknown mathematical identities with reduced computational complexity is noteworthy. The potential applications in areas like compiler optimization or approximation algorithms could be significant. The authors acknowledge potential applications to machine learning models themselves (e.g., simplifying dropout calculations). However, the practical impact of these discoveries remains to be fully demonstrated.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and original approach to the discovery of efficient mathematical identities. The authors effectively leverage attribute grammars and machine learning to tackle a challenging combinatorial search problem. While the direct practical significance of the discovered identities is uncertain, the methodology itself is promising and could have broader implications. The clarity of the presentation could be improved in certain sections, but the overall quality of the work is high.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach.
*   Demonstrated ability to discover new mathematical identities.
*   Successful integration of machine learning techniques into a symbolic reasoning framework.
*   Potential for applications in compiler optimization and related fields.
*   Well-written and clearly presented, despite some areas for improvement.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The problem addressed is somewhat contrived, and the practical impact of the results is not immediately obvious.
*   Section 7.2 could be more clearly explained.
*   The RNN approach does not consistently outperform the simpler n-gram model.
