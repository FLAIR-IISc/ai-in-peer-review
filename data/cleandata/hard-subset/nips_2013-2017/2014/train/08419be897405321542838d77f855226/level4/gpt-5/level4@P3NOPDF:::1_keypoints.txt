- Paper explores using deep neural networks to discover mathematical identities, framing a novel application of DNNs to symbolic math.
- Novel and intriguing formulation connecting deep learning with symbolic mathematics and potential impact on symbolic computation and program synthesis.
- Clear writing and a straightforward experimental setup presenting a first-step system learned on a restricted family of expressions.
- Experimental scope is very limited: identities come from a constrained operator set and template family, limiting real-world relevance.
- Reported performance is modest even within the constrained regime, raising doubt that a neural approach is necessary or superior to simple symbolic methods.
- No strong baselines are provided (missing comparisons to symbolic enumeration, term-rewriting, or computer algebra systems like Mathematica/SymPy).
- Scalability and generalization are unproven: unclear extension to larger trees, richer operators, or multi-step reasoning; learned representations’ reusability is not shown.
- Limited analysis and diagnostics: lacks error breakdowns, correlation with tree depth/operator types, and ablation studies to distinguish learning vs. memorization.
- Suggest adding symbolic enumeration/rewriting and off-the-shelf algebra baselines plus heuristic baselines tailored to the grammar.
- Suggest expanding benchmarks: vary tree size, introduce novel operators at test time, evaluate held-out families and out-of-distribution templates, and report time-to-discovery/sample efficiency.
- Recommend clearer problem definition and protocol: specify grammar, expression distribution, positive/negative generation, success criteria, and verification procedures.
- Advise ablations and diagnostics: compare encodings/architectures/losses, sensitivity to training size/noise, and qualitative nontrivial identity examples.
- Request discussion of scalability: computational complexity, limits, and a roadmap to richer algebraic domains or hybrid neural-symbolic approaches.
- Overall assessment: promising but preliminary work; empirical limits and lack of baselines undermine claims of extensibility and advantage.
- Recommendation: Reject (borderline) with encouragement to resubmit after substantially strengthening experiments, baselines, and analysis.
