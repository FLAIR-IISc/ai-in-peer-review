- Proposes a framework that searches an attribute-grammar space for computationally cheaper expressions equivalent to a target expression.  
- Encodes expressions with a linear-algebraic attribute grammar and searches expression trees for lower-cost alternatives.  
- Learns search strategies from simpler targets using subtree n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) embedding that predicts next grammar rules.  
- Checks equality numerically via descriptors from random integer assignments modulo a large prime; forms complex expressions as linear combinations by solving Fw = t (mod p).  
- Experiments show strong embedding classification accuracy and discovery of nontrivial identities that reduce complexity in several families; n-grams often outperform the RNN on repetitive structures.  
- Relates to theorem proving and program induction but restricts scope to homogeneous polynomials of bounded degree to avoid undecidability.  
- Differs from CAS systems by learning heuristics rather than using hand-coded simplification rules; connects to grammar-based optimization and NLP methods for tree representations.  
- Strength: clear, modular design combining grammar, representations, linear-combination solver, and learned search strategies.  
- Strength: novel use of RNNs to learn continuous embeddings of symbolic expressions with good transfer to search.  
- Strength: discovers concrete, nontrivial identities with substantial computational reductions; code and data released.  
- Strength: practical integer-modulo descriptor serving as an efficient equality oracle.  
- Weakness: restricted scope—single variable, homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs—limits immediate applicability.  
- Weakness: equality testing lacks formal collision bounds (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel) relating degree, descriptor length N, and prime p.  
- Weakness: recovery of integer/rational coefficients from modular solutions is unclear—sparsity and interpretability not addressed.  
- Weakness: search procedure underspecified—proposal mechanism, partial-tree maintenance, branching/beam strategy, backtracking, stopping, and deduplication need detail.  
- Weakness: RNN architecture details unclear—third-order tensor parameterization, per-rule parameters, and handling of commutativity/associativity need clarification.  
- Weakness: missing comparisons to strong CAS and compiler-style baselines; evaluation metrics are permissive and lack aggregate efficiency statistics.  
- Weakness: design choices (e.g., removing matrix-matrix multiply) may bias search space and should be justified.  
- Quality: technically sound integration of grammar-based search and learned heuristics, but needs formalization of equality guarantees and coefficient interpretation.  
- Clarity: generally well written, but key mechanisms (scheduler, tensor composition, coefficient recovery) are under-explained.  
- Originality: high—novel combination of learned guidance for symbolic identity discovery and RNN embeddings for expressions.  
- Significance: potentially impactful for compilers/CAS, but scope limitations temper immediate practical impact.  
- Pros: novel problem, effective learned search strategies, strong representations, public code/data.  
- Cons: restricted setting, insufficient formal treatment of modular equality and coefficient recovery, incomplete search/RNN details, mixed benefit of RNN over n-grams.  
- Key suggestions: provide formal bounds on modular-equality failure, explain modular-to-integer coefficient recovery and sparsity constraints, detail scheduler and search mechanics, clarify RNN parameterization and handling of algebraic properties, add CAS baselines and time-to-solution metrics, and report ablations and small-scale extensions to multiple variables/non-polynomial ops.  
- Lists several typos and editorial fixes.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept; promising and well-executed but needs clarity and rigor improvements to strengthen the contribution.
