1. Novel application: uses neural networks to learn representations of symbolic expressions and identify mathematical identities—interesting and potentially impactful but limited in scope.

2. Main concern: paper emphasizes finding lower-complexity expressions but the algorithm does not enforce or favor lower computational complexity, making practical relevance unclear.

3. Ambiguity in Section 2: unclear whether the scheduler alone limits expression complexity; authors should provide formal justification or empirical evidence for the imposed restrictions.

4. Method clarity: paper structure impedes readability—key stages (representation learning, identity search) are not distinctly introduced or sequenced; transition from parsing to training is abrupt.

5. Technical error: RBM-1 example in supplementary material appears invalid for k=1, n=1 due to dimension mismatch; clarify implicit size constraints or correct the example.

6. Minor typos to fix: p.332 "show for"→"show that"; p.148 "has contains"→"contains"; p.289 "is is"→"is"; p.338 "by be"→"by".

7. Requested improvements: explicitly integrate complexity constraints into the algorithm, justify expression complexity restrictions, restructure the methodology for clarity, and correct the RBM-1 example and typos.

8. Overall verdict: foundational idea is promising but current presentation and unresolved methodological gaps limit endorsement; merits further development.
