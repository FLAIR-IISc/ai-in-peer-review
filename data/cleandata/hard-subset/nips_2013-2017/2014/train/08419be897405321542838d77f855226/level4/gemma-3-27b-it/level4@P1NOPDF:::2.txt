This paper presents two methods for discovering simpler symbolic expression trees. The first approach leverages n-grams computed over sequences of sub-expressions, while the second employs a recursive neural network to generate continuous space representations of these trees. These representations are then utilized to predict the most likely rule for combining subtrees. Based on the results presented in Figure 3, the n-gram approach appears to outperform the RNN on two out of three tasks.

**Comments with respect to specific review criteria:**

**Quality:** High.

**Clarity:** The paper is somewhat difficult to follow, particularly Section 7.2. The explanations in this section could benefit from further elaboration and a more streamlined presentation.

**Originality:** High. The combination of techniques and the application to symbolic expression tree simplification demonstrate a novel approach.

**Significance:** Low. It’s questionable whether these methods would be applicable to real-world, performance-critical computations. The problem itself feels somewhat contrived. This work focuses on identifying more computationally efficient symbolic expression trees, but the broader significance of achieving this remains unclear. The practical impact of simplifying expression trees in this manner is not convincingly demonstrated.
