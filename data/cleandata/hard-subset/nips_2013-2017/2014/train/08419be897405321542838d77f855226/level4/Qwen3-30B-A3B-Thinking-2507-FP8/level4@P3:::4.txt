

**Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"**

This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors propose an attribute grammar approach to represent symbolic expressions and apply n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to guide the search for equivalent expressions with lower computational complexity. The key innovation lies in learning search strategies from simpler expressions to discover complex identities that would be intractable via brute-force search.

The work builds upon several relevant areas: attribute grammars (Knuth, 1968) for symbolic representation, n-gram language models (common in NLP), and recursive neural networks (Socher et al., 2013). The authors clearly relate their contributions to these fields while highlighting how their approach differs from previous work in symbolic computation (e.g., Maple, Mathematica) and theorem proving (Bridge et al., 2013). The paper demonstrates the framework's effectiveness on several polynomial families, discovering identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) or better.

**Strengths:**

1. **Originality (High):** The paper presents a genuinely novel application of machine learning techniques to symbolic computation, specifically using learning to guide the search for mathematical identities. The discovery of previously unknown identities (e.g., for matrix operations and symmetric polynomials) demonstrates the framework's potential impact. The authors clearly distinguish their work from related areas, particularly highlighting the absence of learning approaches in previous symbolic computation systems.

2. **Significance (High):** The results have clear potential applications in computational mathematics and software libraries. The identities discovered could enable more efficient implementations of operations in machine learning (e.g., for RBM partition functions) and other domains. The framework's potential for integration with compilers or optimization tools represents a significant contribution to practical computational mathematics.

3. **Quality (Moderate-High):** The experimental evaluation is thorough, with clear demonstration of the framework's capabilities across multiple polynomial families. The RNN representation learning (Section 4.2) is particularly well-executed, with appropriate attention to implementation details (e.g., initialization strategies, curriculum learning). The results (Section 7.2) convincingly demonstrate the superiority of learning-based approaches over random search for complex expressions.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Clarity (Moderate):** The paper's structure would benefit from clearer organization. The explanation of the grammar framework and search strategy (Section 6) is somewhat dense and could be better scaffolded for readers unfamiliar with attribute grammars. The relationship between the grammar rules, search strategy, and computational complexity constraints needs more explicit articulation. For instance, the paper states the goal is to find expressions with lower complexity, but the algorithm itself doesn't enforce this constraint—this critical point needs clarification.

2. **Quality (Moderate):** While the framework works well within its restricted domain (polynomials of fixed degree), the paper does not adequately address how the approach might scale to more complex mathematical structures (e.g., trigonometric functions, recursive expressions). The limitations section (Section 8) mentions these challenges but lacks concrete analysis of why the current approach cannot be extended. The authors should provide more formal discussion of the theoretical boundaries of their method.

3. **Significance (Moderate):** The paper's focus on a restricted class of expressions (homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree) limits its immediate applicability. While the authors acknowledge this in Section 2, they could better justify why this specific domain is both practically important and sufficiently general to demonstrate the framework's value. The connection to machine learning applications (e.g., dropout, RBM partition functions) is promising but requires more detailed explanation.

**Specific Comments:**

- The paper should clarify how the framework ensures discovered identities have lower computational complexity, rather than just being equivalent. This is a critical aspect of the problem statement that isn't adequately addressed in the algorithm description.

- The restrictions mentioned in Section 2 (e.g., homogeneous polynomials, scalar output) should be justified more thoroughly. The authors should discuss whether these restrictions are inherent to the approach or merely practical limitations, and how they might be relaxed in future work.

- The paper's structure would benefit from clearer section headings and more explicit signposting of the framework's components. The relationship between the RNN representation (Section 4.2) and the search strategy (Section 6.3) is particularly confusing in its current form.

- The supplementary material contains the discovered identities, but the paper should include at least one detailed example of a particularly interesting identity to better illustrate the framework's capabilities.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation
- Demonstrated success in discovering previously unknown mathematical identities
- Strong experimental results showing significant computational complexity improvements
- Clear connection to practical applications in machine learning and computational mathematics

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited scope (restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree)
- Insufficient discussion of theoretical boundaries and limitations
- Clarity issues in explaining the framework's components
- Lack of formal analysis of how the approach might scale to more complex mathematical structures

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-executed and innovative paper that makes a significant contribution to the application of machine learning in symbolic computation. The framework's ability to discover efficient mathematical identities beyond the reach of brute-force search represents a meaningful advancement. While the restricted domain of the current work is a limitation, the approach is sufficiently general to inspire future extensions to broader mathematical domains. The paper would benefit from clearer exposition of the framework and more thorough discussion of its limitations, but these are minor concerns for a high-quality contribution to NIPS.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity issues and strengthen the discussion of the framework's boundaries. The work has significant potential to influence both theoretical and applied computational mathematics.
