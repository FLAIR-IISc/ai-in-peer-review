## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and novel approach to automating the discovery of mathematical identities. The combination of attribute grammars with machine learning, specifically n-gram models and RNNs, to navigate the search space is innovative and yields promising results. The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains a complex idea. However, the experimental evaluation, while demonstrating significant improvements, lacks a deeper analysis of the model's limitations and generalization capabilities.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Related Work:**

The authors tackle the challenging problem of automating the discovery of mathematically equivalent but computationally cheaper expressions. They propose a framework built around attribute grammars, which define the allowed operations, and employ machine learning to intelligently search this grammar space. This is a crucial step, as a brute-force search quickly becomes intractable. The paper positions itself correctly within the landscape of theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, highlighting its specific contribution: a learning-guided search within a defined symbolic domain. The related work section is thorough, accurately placing the work relative to existing approaches in symbolic computation, NLP, and program synthesis. The connection to attribute grammars, though less common in modern ML, is appropriately emphasized. 

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of combining attribute grammars with machine learning for this task is genuinely novel. The application of RNNs to learn continuous representations of symbolic expressions is particularly intriguing.
* **Significant Results:** The paper demonstrates impressive results, discovering identities offering substantial computational speedups (O(n3) to O(n2) and even exponential to polynomial time). Example 1 and 2 are particularly convincing.
* **Clear Framework:** The attribute grammar provides a structured and well-defined search space, which is essential for applying machine learning techniques. The explanation of the grammar and search process is clear and logical.
* **Practicality:** The release of code and data (https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning) is a major strength, fostering reproducibility and further research.
* **Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, despite the technical complexity of the topic.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Generalization Analysis:** While the results are compelling for the tested families of expressions, the paper lacks a robust analysis of generalization. It's not clear how well this approach would scale to significantly different types of mathematical expressions (e.g., involving trigonometric functions, integrals, or more complex algebraic structures).  The limitations mentioned in Section 8 are a good start, but a more quantitative assessment of the scope of the framework is needed.
* **RNN Performance vs. n-grams:** The RNN, while promising as a continuous representation learner, appears to be consistently outperformed by simpler n-gram models in the experiments. The authors do not thoroughly investigate why this is the case. Is it due to the limited training data, the architecture of the RNN, or inherent limitations in representing the underlying structure of these identities with a recurrent network? Further analysis is needed.
* **Modularity of the Learned Rules:** It’s unclear how easily the learned strategies (n-grams or RNN weights) could be transferred to different but related expression families. Does learning from (∑ AAT)k significantly improve performance on (∑ (A.∗A)AT)k, or is retraining required for each family? 
* **Search Strategy Cut-off:**  The fixed 10-minute cut-off time simplifies the experiments but might mask the true potential of the learning strategies. Some identities might be discovered *eventually* with longer runtimes, but the evaluation doesn't capture this.  A runtime curve showing the success rate as a function of time would be more informative.
* **Descriptor Choice:** The integer-based descriptor using modulo p is a clever solution to numerical instability. However, the choice of ‘p’ and the impact of ‘p’ on the success rate are not discussed. Is there a risk of collisions even with large p?



**4. Originality:**

The work is highly original in its combination of techniques. While attribute grammars and machine learning have been used individually in related areas, their integration for automated identity discovery is a novel contribution. The specific application of RNNs to learn representations of symbolic expressions within this framework also adds to the originality.

**5. Significance:**

The potential significance of this work is considerable. Automating the discovery of mathematical identities could have a substantial impact on a variety of fields, including scientific computing, optimization, and even the development of more efficient machine learning algorithms themselves (as highlighted by the authors). The ability to find identities that are too complex for humans to discover manually is a particularly exciting prospect.

**6. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel and innovative approach.
* Demonstrates significant computational improvements.
* Clear framework and well-written paper.
* Released code and data for reproducibility.

**Con:**

* Limited analysis of generalization to different expression families.
* RNN performance lags behind n-grams; reason unclear.
* Lack of modularity assessment for learned strategies.
* Fixed runtime cut-off might mask potential improvements.



**7. Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

*   **Expand the experimental evaluation:** Include a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach and its potential generalization to different types of mathematical expressions.
*   **Analyze RNN performance:** Investigate why the RNN consistently underperforms the n-gram models and explore potential improvements to the RNN architecture or training procedure.
*   **Assess modularity:**  Experimentally evaluate the transferability of learned strategies between expression families.
*   **Consider runtime curves:** Provide runtime curves showing the success rate as a function of time, rather than relying solely on a fixed cut-off.
*   **Discuss the choice of 'p'** and the potential for collisions in the integer-based descriptor.



This is a high-quality paper with significant potential, and addressing these minor revisions will further strengthen its contribution to the field.
