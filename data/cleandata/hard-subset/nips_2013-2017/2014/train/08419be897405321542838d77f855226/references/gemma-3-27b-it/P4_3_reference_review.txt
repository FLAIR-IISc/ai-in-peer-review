This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities based on attribute grammars and machine learning. The core idea is to represent expressions as grammar trees and then leverage learning to guide the search for efficient alternatives to a target expression. The authors explore both n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to learn a search strategy, demonstrating improvements over random search, and reporting the discovery of previously unknown identities.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing the challenging problem of automating mathematical discovery. It correctly identifies the limitations of existing symbolic computation systems and the potential of applying machine learning to this domain. The proposed framework, combining attribute grammars with learning-based search, is a significant contribution. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, particularly for expressions where naive evaluation is computationally expensive. The inclusion of code and evaluation data further enhances the reproducibility and impact of the work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for discovering mathematical identities is a novel approach.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The framework appears to be technically sound, with a clear problem definition, a well-defined grammar, and a reasonable search strategy. The use of integer-based descriptors to address numerical stability is a good engineering choice. The RNN architecture is justified with references to related work.
*   **Significance:** The potential impact is high. Automated discovery of efficient mathematical identities could lead to performance improvements in various applications. The discovered identities themselves are a valuable contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts. The examples provided help to illustrate the approach. The notation is standard and well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scope Limitations:** The paper explicitly restricts the domain to homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix variable. While this simplifies the problem, it limits the generalizability of the approach. The discussion of these limitations in Section 2 and Section 8 is helpful, but the framework's performance on more complex expressions remains unclear.
*   **RNN Performance:** While the RNN shows promise, the n-gram models consistently outperform it in the presented experiments. A more thorough analysis of the RNN's performance and potential improvements would be beneficial. The hyperparameter tuning for the RNN isn’t detailed.
*   **Scalability:** The experiments are limited to relatively small matrix sizes (n). The scalability of the approach to larger matrices or more complex expressions is not fully addressed. The computational cost of the linear system solver (Gaussian elimination) could become a bottleneck for larger problems.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to existing symbolic computation engines (Maple, Mathematica) is limited to stating that they don't use learning approaches. A more direct comparison of performance on the discovered identities would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound framework for discovering mathematical identities.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, particularly for challenging expressions.
*   The discovered identities are a valuable contribution to the field.
*   The code and data availability promote reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The scope of the problem is limited to a specific class of expressions.
*   The RNN's performance is not competitive with the n-gram models.
*   The scalability of the approach to larger problems is unclear.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of automated mathematical discovery. The novelty of the approach, the technical soundness of the framework, and the potential impact of the results outweigh the limitations. The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Recommendation:**

Accept. The paper is suitable for NIPS. I would encourage the authors to address the scalability concerns and provide a more thorough analysis of the RNN's performance in a future work.
