This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar, where each rule corresponds to a mathematical operation with an associated computational complexity. Since a brute-force or random search over the grammar tree is intractable for complex target expressions, the authors propose using machine learning to guide the search. The main contribution is to learn a search strategy by training models on solutions to simpler expressions, and then using these models to find solutions for more complex targets. The paper explores two learning-based strategies: an n-gram model that captures common sub-expressions from previous solutions, and a Recursive Neural Network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions. To verify if a candidate expression is equivalent to the target, the authors propose an efficient numerical representation based on evaluating expressions on random inputs modulo a prime number. The framework is demonstrated on several families of polynomial expressions, discovering new, non-trivial identities that reduce computational complexity from exponential or high-order polynomial to low-order polynomial time.

This work relates to several fields. It is a form of program induction or synthesis, but with a specific focus on optimizing computational complexity rather than general program generation. Unlike traditional symbolic computation systems (e.g., Mathematica, Maple) which rely on hand-crafted rules, this work's main novelty lies in its use of statistical machine learning to learn search heuristics automatically. The application of techniques from natural language processing, such as n-gram models and recursive neural networks for learning on tree structures, to the domain of symbolic mathematics is a creative and promising connection. The learned continuous representations for mathematical expressions are analogous to word embeddings in NLP, a parallel the authors correctly identify.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality:** The primary contribution of this work is its high degree of originality. The application of machine learning, particularly techniques inspired by NLP, to guide the search for mathematical identities is a novel and compelling idea. Framing the problem as a search over an attribute grammar and then learning a search policy is an elegant formulation. The discovery of previously unknown and computationally significant identities is a strong testament to the power of the approach.
*   **Significance:** The potential impact of this research is substantial. The framework could serve as a powerful tool for automated algorithm discovery and optimization, with potential applications in scientific computing, compiler design, and machine learning itself (as suggested by the RBM-related examples). The development of a learned continuous representation for symbolic expressions is a significant contribution on its own, potentially enabling a wide range of machine learning techniques to be applied to symbolic domains that have traditionally been difficult to access.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The use of an attribute grammar to define the search space is appropriate. The numerical representation for checking expression equality (evaluation modulo a prime) is a clever and pragmatic solution that avoids the complexities of symbolic manipulation and the precision issues of floating-point arithmetic. The experimental design is solid, demonstrating a clear performance gain from the learned search strategies over a random baseline on a series of progressively harder problems. The results are concrete and impressive.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction, particularly with Examples 1 and 2, does an excellent job of motivating the problem and outlining the solution. The distinct components of the framework—grammar, representation, and search strategy—are explained in a logical sequence.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope:** The framework, as presented, is restricted to a specific class of problems: finding scalar-valued, homogeneous polynomial identities involving a single variable. The authors acknowledge these limitations, but the paper would be stronger with a more detailed discussion of the challenges involved in extending the framework. For instance, how would the grammar and search handle non-polynomial functions, recursion, or conditional logic? The success of the current method relies on a tightly constrained search space, and its scalability to more general mathematical discovery remains an open question.
*   **RNN Performance:** A key contribution is the novel application of an RNN to learn a representation and guide the search. However, in the main identity discovery experiments (Figure 3), the simpler n-gram models consistently outperform the RNN strategy. While the RNN's success on the expression classification task validates the learned representation, its suboptimal performance on the primary search task is a notable weakness. The authors suggest this is because the solutions have repetitive local patterns well-suited for n-grams, but a more in-depth analysis of why the more powerful RNN model fails to capture or generalize these patterns better would strengthen the paper.
*   **Clarity on Linear Combinations:** The mechanism for combining multiple trees (Section 5) is a crucial component for finding complex identities, but its interaction with the tree search strategy could be explained more clearly. It is not entirely clear whether the search generates a static pool of trees that are then solved for, or if the linear solver is called iteratively as new trees are generated. More detail on this process would improve the reader's understanding of the complete system.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  Presents a highly novel and creative approach to a challenging problem at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation.
2.  Demonstrates impressive, concrete results by discovering new and useful mathematical identities.
3.  The proposed learned representation for symbolic expressions is a significant contribution with potentially broad applicability.
4.  The paper is well-executed, technically sound, and clearly written.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

1.  The demonstrated scope of the method is currently limited to a restricted class of polynomial expressions.
2.  The more sophisticated RNN model, a key methodological contribution, is outperformed by simpler n-gram baselines in the main experimental task.

In summary, this paper introduces a novel, significant, and well-executed piece of research. The strengths, particularly the originality of the core idea and the impressive results, substantially outweigh the weaknesses, which primarily point toward avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the current contribution. The work opens up a promising new direction for applying machine learning to automated scientific discovery.
