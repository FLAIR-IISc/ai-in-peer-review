

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions as trees using attribute grammars, and then employ machine learning techniques (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for efficient versions of target expressions that compute identical results with lower computational complexity.

The authors' approach is conceptually bold and highly original. They frame the problem as a search within a grammar tree space, where learning from simpler expressions helps discover more complex identities. The paper demonstrates the framework's ability to find identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) for certain matrix operations, and from exponential time to polynomial time for some combinatorial expressions. The authors present both theoretical justification and empirical validation across multiple families of mathematical expressions.

The novelty of this work is compelling. While symbolic computation and theorem proving have been studied extensively, the application of machine learning to guide the search for efficient mathematical identities represents a significant conceptual leap. The authors successfully bridge concepts from natural language processing (n-gram models, recursive neural networks) with symbolic mathematics, creating a new interdisciplinary approach. The learned continuous representation for symbolic expressions (Section 4.2) is particularly elegant and potentially applicable to broader symbolic AI problems.

The paper makes several strong contributions:
1. A novel grammar framework for finding efficient symbolic expressions
2. A demonstration of how machine learning can be integrated into symbolic search
3. A novel application of recursive neural networks to learn representations of mathematical structures
4. The discovery of previously unknown mathematical identities with significant computational benefits

The experimental results (Section 7.2) are impressive, showing that learning-based search strategies significantly outperform random search for complex expressions. For example, the n-gram approach reliably discovers solutions for the first two families of expressions, while the RNN approach is particularly effective for the RBM-2 family (finding k=5 solutions consistently faster than random search). The authors provide clear examples (Examples 1 & 2) that effectively illustrate the approach's capabilities.

The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately, noting the restricted scope (homogeneous polynomials of degree k with single matrix/vector) and the challenges with the RBM-2 family. The discussion (Section 8) thoughtfully connects the work to potential applications in machine learning (e.g., for dropout and RBM partition functions) and broader implications for symbolic computation.

The quality of the paper is high. The framework is well-defined, the methodology is sound, and the experiments are thorough. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility (including code availability). The technical presentation is clear and well-organized, with helpful examples that make the abstract concepts accessible.

The significance of this work is substantial. It opens a new direction for applying machine learning to symbolic computation, potentially enabling more efficient algorithms across multiple domains. The ability to automatically discover mathematical identities with reduced computational complexity could have practical impact in fields like machine learning, where computational efficiency is crucial. The paper also makes a compelling case for the broader applicability of their approach to other symbolic domains.

While the scope is somewhat restricted (as acknowledged by the authors), this limitation is reasonable for an initial exploration of the approach. The paper's focus on a well-defined problem space actually strengthens its contributions, as it allows for rigorous evaluation of the proposed methods.

The main weakness is the limited evaluation of the framework's generalizability beyond the specific mathematical domains explored. The RBM-2 family results (where no solutions were found for k>5) suggest clear limitations that warrant further investigation. However, this doesn't diminish the novelty and significance of the core approach.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I find this paper exceptionally compelling. It introduces a genuinely new direction in machine learning with clear potential for impact. The paper's focus on a novel application of learning techniques to symbolic computation is exactly the kind of bold, interdisciplinary work that advances the field.

The paper's contributions are sufficiently distinct from previous work. While the authors correctly cite related work in theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, they clearly differentiate their approach as focusing on learning to guide symbolic search for efficiency, rather than rule-based simplification or theorem proving.

I recommend acceptance. The paper's core idea is highly novel, its implementation is sound, and its potential impact is significant. The authors have demonstrated a promising new direction for machine learning applications in symbolic computation, with concrete results that validate their approach. The minor limitations noted are expected for an initial exploration of this novel direction, and the paper provides clear pathways for future work to address them.

This paper represents a significant step forward in applying machine learning to symbolic problems, and its novel approach to guiding symbolic search through learned strategies makes it a strong candidate for NIPS.
