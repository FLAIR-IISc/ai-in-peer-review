

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars and machine learning. The authors address the problem of finding expressions that compute the same result as a target expression but with lower computational complexity. They introduce a grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions and use machine learning to guide the search for efficient solutions, training models on simpler expressions to solve more complex ones. The approach combines n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to learn search strategies, demonstrating success on several families of mathematical expressions where the naive computation would be O(n³) or exponential, but the discovered identities reduce complexity to O(n²) or O(n³).

The work builds on attribute grammars (Knuth, 1968) and draws inspiration from NLP techniques like n-gram language models and recursive neural networks (Socher et al., 2011). The authors position their work against related areas including theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation engines like Maple and Mathematica, noting the lack of learning-based approaches in these domains.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness and Quality:** The paper presents a well-structured technical approach with clear methodology. The attribute grammar framework is well-defined and properly motivated. The use of integer-based descriptors (Zp) to avoid numerical issues is a clever technical solution. The RNN training details (weight initialization, curriculum learning, dropout) demonstrate careful consideration of practical implementation challenges. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with ablation studies comparing n-gram models (n=1 to 5) and RNNs with different dropout rates against random search across multiple expression families.

**Originality:** The core contribution of applying machine learning to guide combinatorial search in symbolic computation is novel. The paper clearly articulates how their approach differs from related work in theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation. The discovery of new mathematical identities (e.g., the pattern in Example 2 for k=6) that reduce computational complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) represents a significant technical contribution. The continuous representation of symbolic expressions via RNNs is particularly innovative.

**Significance:** The paper demonstrates concrete applications with potential impact in machine learning (e.g., efficient computation of dropout or RBM partition functions). The framework could be integrated into compilers or optimization tools, representing a practical contribution. The work also advances the broader field by showing how learning can guide combinatorial search, which has implications beyond mathematical identities.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized with clear examples (Examples 1 and 2) that effectively illustrate the concepts. The notation is consistent and well-defined. The figures (Fig. 1-3) are helpful for understanding the grammar trees and experimental results. The supplementary material appears to provide additional details on the discovered identities.

## Weaknesses

**Scope Limitations:** The problem is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree k with a single matrix or vector variable. While the authors acknowledge this limitation and note that the framework could be extended with additional grammar rules, the current scope limits the applicability to a narrower class of problems. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how these restrictions impact the generalizability of their approach.

**Comparison to Existing Work:** The comparison to symbolic computation engines like Maple and Mathematica is somewhat superficial. A more detailed analysis of how their approach differs from existing simplification techniques in these systems would strengthen the position statement.

**RNN Generalization:** The paper notes that the RNN approach shows overfitting issues requiring dropout, and that patterns without simple repetitive structure are difficult to generalize. While they acknowledge this limitation, a more thorough analysis of when the RNN approach fails compared to n-gram models would be valuable.

**Scalability:** The experiments are limited to k up to 15 with a 10-minute time limit. The paper doesn't address how the approach would scale to much larger problems or more complex grammars that might be needed for broader mathematical applications.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound and well-executed paper that makes a novel contribution to the intersection of symbolic computation and machine learning. The core idea of using learning to guide combinatorial search in symbolic domains is both innovative and well-executed, with clear evidence of the approach's effectiveness through the discovered mathematical identities. The framework has significant potential for practical applications in computational efficiency for machine learning.

The paper is clearly written and well-structured, with appropriate comparisons to related work. While the problem scope is somewhat limited, the authors acknowledge this and position their work as a foundation for future extensions. The technical contributions (continuous representation of symbolic expressions, learning search strategies) are significant and directly relevant to NIPS.

The main weaknesses are the restricted problem scope and the lack of deeper analysis of the RNN's generalization limitations. However, these do not undermine the core contribution, which is both novel and technically sound.

## Recommendation

The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field by demonstrating how machine learning techniques can be applied to guide combinatorial search in symbolic domains. The technical execution is strong, the results are convincing, and the work has clear potential for both theoretical and practical impact. The limitations noted are acknowledged and do not detract from the paper's overall quality.

I recommend acceptance.
