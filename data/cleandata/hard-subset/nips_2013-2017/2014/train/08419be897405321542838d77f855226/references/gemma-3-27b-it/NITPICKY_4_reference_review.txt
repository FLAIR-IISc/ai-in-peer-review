This paper proposes a framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars and machine learning. The core idea – leveraging learning to guide the search within a grammar-defined space of expressions – is interesting, and the reported results, particularly the discovery of previously unknown identities, are potentially significant. However, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet pervasive, issues in presentation, technical detail, and experimental rigor that, taken together, lead me to recommend rejection.

The introduction is overly broad in its initial claims about applying ML to symbolic settings. While the goal is valuable, framing it as a limitation *despite* ML’s success in perception is misleading; the challenges are fundamentally different. The contributions list is also somewhat inflated. While the novel grammar framework is a contribution, simply *integrating* ML techniques is not novel in itself. The RNN application is more substantial, but presenting *all* identified identities as a major contribution feels excessive.

My primary concern lies with the technical details. The attribute grammar itself (Table 1) lacks sufficient explanation regarding the associated complexity metrics. What constitutes the “complexity” attributed to each rule? This ambiguity makes understanding the optimization process difficult. The numerical representation (Section 4.1) introduces a modulo operation to avoid floating-point issues, which is commendable, but the choice of prime ‘p’ is unexplained and crucial for preventing collisions.  The RNN architecture is described superficially. The initialization strategy is stated as “identity plus noise”, but the magnitude of the noise is unspecified. Similarly, the selection of l=30 is attributed to cross-validation, but results are not presented, and the reasoning for this specific dimensionality is unclear.  The dropout rate justification is similarly lacking in supporting evidence. The description of backpropagation “to the input” is vague and could be clarified.

The experimental setup also raises concerns. The choice of families of expressions appears somewhat arbitrary, and the justification for selecting them is weak.  While stating that the families are ordered by “difficulty”, there’s no objective measure of this difficulty.  Furthermore, the statement that removing the matrix-multiply rule guarantees O(n²) complexity is incorrect; other operations within the grammar still have O(n²) or O(n³) costs. The experimental results (Fig. 3) are presented without sufficient statistical significance testing. Showing only fraction of successful runs without standard deviations or confidence intervals is inadequate. The comparison between the n-gram and RNN approaches lacks a detailed analysis of why n-grams perform better on certain families.  Simply stating that n-grams capture repetitive patterns is insufficient. The claim that the RNN found solutions faster for the (RBM-2)k family is quantified with large standard deviations (100±12 vs 438±77), weakening the claim.

Numerous minor issues detract from the paper’s overall quality.  The notation is inconsistent (e.g., using both O() and O(n²) to represent complexity). The examples (Example 1 and 2) are poorly formatted and lack clarity. The reference list contains several errors and inconsistencies (e.g. lacking volume/issue numbers). There are multiple instances of grammatical errors and awkward phrasing throughout the manuscript. The link to the Github repository is present, but its contents were not fully explored during review.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea of using ML to guide the search for efficient identities is potentially valuable.
*   The reported discovery of novel identities demonstrates the framework's feasibility.
*   The learned representation of symbolic expressions using RNNs is a novel contribution.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Lack of rigor in technical details and experimental setup.
*   Numerous minor errors in presentation and writing.
*   Overstated claims regarding contributions and significance.
*   Insufficient statistical analysis of experimental results.
*   Ambiguity in complexity metrics and optimization process.



In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting idea and some promising initial results, the numerous technical shortcomings and lack of rigor prevent me from recommending acceptance.  A substantial revision addressing these issues would be required for reconsideration. The paper, as it stands, does not meet the quality standards expected for NIPS.
