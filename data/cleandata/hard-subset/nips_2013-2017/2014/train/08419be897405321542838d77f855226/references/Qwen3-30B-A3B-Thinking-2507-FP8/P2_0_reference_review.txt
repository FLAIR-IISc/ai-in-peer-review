

# Review for "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

## Summary

This paper presents a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors address the problem of finding an expression S that computes the same result as a target expression T but with lower computational complexity. They propose using attribute grammars to represent symbolic expressions and develop a search strategy guided by machine learning models (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) that learn from solutions to simpler expressions. The framework demonstrates significant improvements over random search, discovering identities that reduce complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) or better for several families of expressions.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) a grammar framework for efficient identity discovery, (2) integration of machine learning into this framework, (3) a novel application of recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of mathematical structures, and (4) the discovery of new mathematical identities with practical computational benefits.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The authors clearly define the problem, provide a rigorous framework based on attribute grammars, and develop appropriate machine learning strategies for guiding the search. The experimental setup is well-designed, with multiple expression families tested across different degrees (k=2 to k=15), and results are presented with appropriate statistical measures (success rates across 10 runs). The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the difficulty with the RBM-2 family, and provides reasonable explanations.

The claims are well-supported by the experiments. For example, Table 2 shows strong classification accuracy (≤5% error) for the RNN representation, and Figure 3 demonstrates clear superiority of learning-based approaches over random search for complex identities (e.g., the (ΣAAT)^k family). The authors are careful to note that the approach works best for expressions with repetitive structures (which n-gram models capture well) but struggles with more complex patterns.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and well-organized. The introduction clearly states the problem, motivation, and contributions. The technical sections (attribute grammar, representation learning) are explained with sufficient detail and supported by examples (like Examples 1 and 2). The figures (Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Fig. 3) effectively illustrate key concepts and results.

The paper provides enough detail for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the work. The authors clearly explain their notation, describe the grammar rules, and provide details about the learning approaches (e.g., the RNN architecture, curriculum learning, and training details). The supplementary material (referenced as [29]) is mentioned as containing additional identities, which is helpful for readers interested in specific examples.

## Originality

This work demonstrates significant originality. The core idea of using machine learning to guide the search for efficient mathematical identities within an attribute grammar framework is novel. While attribute grammars have been used in other domains (e.g., compiler construction, bioinformatics), this is the first application to mathematical identity discovery.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work in theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation. As noted in Section 1.1, "In contrast, our approach does not use hand-designed heuristics, instead learning them automatically from the results of simpler expressions." The application of recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of symbolic expressions is also novel, with the authors providing a detailed explanation of their RNN architecture and training procedure.

The paper effectively cites relevant prior work and positions its contributions within the broader context of NLP (e.g., n-gram models, recursive neural networks), theorem proving, and symbolic computation.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. The discovered identities have practical implications for machine learning, as the authors note: "The identities we have discovered could potentially be used to give a closed-form version of dropout, or compute the RBM partition function efficiently (i.e. in polynomial time)." This is not just theoretical; it directly addresses computational bottlenecks in real machine learning applications.

The framework's ability to discover identities that are "too complex to be found manually or by exhaustive search" represents a meaningful advancement over previous approaches. The paper demonstrates that learning can effectively guide search in combinatorially large spaces, which has broader implications beyond mathematics (as noted in Section 8: "This could have broad applicability in allowing machine learning tools to be applied to symbolic computation").

The work also advances the state of the art by showing that machine learning can be applied to guide search in discrete combinatorial spaces, which is a core challenge in AI. This has potential applications in compiler optimization, program synthesis, and other domains where combinatorial search is a bottleneck.

## Strengths

1. **Novel problem formulation**: The paper identifies a meaningful problem (discovering efficient mathematical identities) that has not been previously addressed with machine learning.
2. **Strong experimental results**: The results demonstrate clear superiority of learning-based approaches over random search for complex expressions, with significant complexity reductions.
3. **Well-structured framework**: The attribute grammar framework is well-conceived and provides a solid foundation for the search process.
4. **Practical relevance**: The paper makes a compelling case for the real-world applicability of the discovered identities to machine learning problems.
5. **Clear limitations**: The authors acknowledge the approach's limitations (e.g., with the RBM-2 family) and discuss future directions thoughtfully.

## Weaknesses

1. **Problem scope limitations**: The paper restricts the problem to homogeneous polynomials of degree k with a single matrix/vector input. While the authors acknowledge this in Section 2, the limitations could be more thoroughly discussed in terms of how they constrain the approach's applicability to broader mathematical domains.
2. **Lack of theoretical analysis**: The paper provides empirical results but lacks theoretical analysis of why the approach works well for some families but not others (e.g., the RBM-2 family). A deeper analysis of the relationship between the structure of the target expression and the learnability of the search strategy would strengthen the paper.
3. **Limited discussion of computational costs**: The paper doesn't sufficiently discuss the trade-off between the computational cost of training the learning models versus the benefits of the discovered identities. For practical deployment, this is an important consideration.
4. **Underdeveloped connection to broader ML applications**: While the paper mentions potential applications to dropout and RBM partition functions, it doesn't provide concrete examples of how these identities would be integrated into existing ML pipelines.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and makes a significant contribution to the field. The problem is well-motivated, the approach is novel, and the results are compelling. The paper's strengths clearly outweigh its weaknesses.

The paper advances the state of the art in several ways: by demonstrating how learning can guide search in combinatorial spaces, by providing a new framework for mathematical identity discovery, and by discovering identities with practical computational benefits. The results are reproducible, and the approach has clear potential for broader application.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In Section 8 (Discussion), expand the discussion of practical applications beyond the brief mention of dropout and RBM partition functions. Provide more specific examples of how the discovered identities could be integrated into existing machine learning frameworks.

2. Consider adding a brief theoretical analysis of the learnability of the search strategy based on the structure of the target expressions. For instance, why do n-gram models work well for the (ΣAAT)^k family but not for RBM-2?

3. In Section 7.2, more clearly articulate the trade-off between the computational cost of training the learning models and the benefits of the discovered identities. This would help readers assess the practical utility of the approach.

4. In the introduction, explicitly state the limitations of the current approach and how they might be addressed in future work. This would help position the work more clearly within the broader context of the field.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of machine learning and symbolic computation. The work has the potential to influence both theoretical and practical aspects of machine learning, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
