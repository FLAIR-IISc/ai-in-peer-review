This paper presents a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is equivalent to a given target but has lower computational complexity. As a brute-force or random search is intractable for complex targets, the main contribution is the use of machine learning to learn an effective search strategy. The authors propose training models on solutions found for simpler expressions to guide the search for more complex ones. Two main learning approaches are explored: n-gram models that capture common local patterns in solution trees, and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions to predict the next best grammar rule to apply. The framework is successfully applied to several families of polynomial expressions, discovering new, efficient identities for problems with naive complexities ranging from polynomial to exponential.

The work is situated at an interesting intersection of symbolic computation, program induction, and machine learning. It differentiates itself from classical symbolic systems like Mathematica by its focus on learning search heuristics rather than using hand-crafted rules. The application of NLP techniques, particularly n-gram models and RNNs on tree structures (similar to Socher et al.), to the domain of symbolic mathematics is a creative and promising direction. The concept of learning a continuous embedding for mathematical expressions is particularly notable, drawing a powerful analogy to word embeddings in NLP.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a challenging and important problem: optimizing symbolic computation. The approach of framing this as a learning-guided search problem over a grammar is highly original. If this framework can be generalized, it could have a significant impact on scientific computing, compiler design, and machine learning itself (e.g., by finding efficient ways to compute complex objective functions or partition functions as suggested by the RBM examples). The discovery of new, non-trivial mathematical identities is a very strong and impressive result.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sound and well-designed. The use of a numerical representation (evaluating expressions on random inputs modulo a prime) to efficiently check for expression equality is a very clever and practical solution that avoids the complexities of symbolic provers. The RNN architecture for learning expression embeddings is well-motivated, and the authors demonstrate a high level of care in the training process, noting crucial details like identity weight initialization and curriculum learning.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The introductory examples are highly effective at communicating the core problem and the potential of the method. The separation of concerns—grammar definition, expression representation, and search strategy—makes the overall system understandable. The experiments are clearly described, and the results are presented effectively.

4.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors test their framework on five distinct and non-trivial families of expressions, including some directly relevant to the NIPS community (e.g., related to RBMs). The inclusion of a random search baseline clearly demonstrates the value of the learning-based strategies. The comparison between n-gram models and the more sophisticated RNN model is insightful.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Performance of RNN vs. n-grams:** A somewhat surprising result is that the simpler n-gram models (particularly 3, 4, and 5-grams) often outperform the RNN. While the paper reports this result honestly, it lacks a discussion of *why* this might be the case. Is it because the solutions for these specific families exhibit strong local, repetitive patterns that are perfectly suited for n-grams? Or does it suggest a limitation in the current RNN architecture or training scheme? A deeper analysis of this result would strengthen the paper, as the learned continuous representation is presented as a major contribution, yet the simpler model often works better in practice.

2.  **Scope and Generalizability:** The current work is restricted to a specific class of expressions: homogeneous polynomials with scalar outputs. The authors acknowledge this limitation but could elaborate more on the key challenges in extending the framework. For instance, how would the grammar and search handle trigonometric functions, recursion, or conditional logic? The introduction of such rules might create non-terminating trees, requiring a more sophisticated scheduler. While the current scope is sufficient for a first paper, a discussion of the path toward a more general-purpose "identity discoverer" would be valuable.

3.  **Role of the Linear Combination Step:** Section 5 describes combining multiple trees linearly to match the target. This seems crucial for finding more complex identities. It would be helpful if the authors could provide more insight into its practical usage. For example, for the more difficult problems, roughly how many basis trees (`M`) are typically generated by the search strategy before the linear solver finds a solution? Does this number grow rapidly with `k`?

**Summary of Recommendation:**

This is an excellent paper presenting a highly novel and creative application of machine learning to a fundamental problem in symbolic computation. The core ideas are elegant, the execution is technically sound, and the results are impressive, including the discovery of new mathematical identities. The paper is well-written and makes a significant contribution that is likely to inspire future work in this direction. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to opportunities for deeper analysis and discussion of future challenges rather than fundamental flaws in the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
