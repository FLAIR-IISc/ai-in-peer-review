- Proposes an attribute grammar + machine learning framework to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities.
- Represents symbolic expressions as sequences of grammar rules and uses n-gram models and RNNs to guide search.
- Uses solutions to simpler expressions to train models that improve search for more complex expressions (transfer of search knowledge).
- Demonstrates significant complexity reductions on multiple expression families (e.g., O(n^3) → O(n^2) for matrix ops).
- Technically sound with well-structured experiments, clear problem definition, and appropriate baselines and metrics.
- Strong experimental design covering multiple expression families (RBM-1, RBM-2, Symk, etc.) and a range of complexities (k=2..15).
- Acknowledges limitations and reports failure modes (RBM-2 particularly challenging) and scope restrictions.
- Exceptionally well-written and organized; notation and mathematical formulations are clear and reproducible.
- Integer-based descriptor and RNN architecture are clearly described; supplementary material expected to aid reproducibility.
- Novel contribution: first to combine attribute grammars with learning to discover mathematical identities.
- Distinguishes itself from theorem proving, symbolic computation, and program induction by learning search strategies rather than hand-crafted heuristics.
- Significant practical impact: discovered identities yield concrete speedups relevant to ML tasks (e.g., dropout, RBM computations).
- Shows broader methodological value: learning to guide combinatorial search could apply beyond identity discovery.
- Weaknesses: restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree and lacks deeper theoretical analysis of why n-grams beat RNNs.
- Additional weaknesses: limited analysis of RNN performance adequacy, unexplored causes of RBM-2 failures, and missing supplementary identities.
- Recommendation: Strong Accept — novel, significant, well-executed work with reasonable scope limitations.
