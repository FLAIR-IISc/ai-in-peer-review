This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The central idea is to define a search space of expressions using an attribute grammar and then use machine learning techniques to guide the search, which would otherwise be intractable. The authors train models (n-grams and a recursive neural network) on solutions found for simpler expressions to discover identities for more complex targets. The paper claims the discovery of several new identities for specific families of polynomial expressions, some of which are related to computations in machine learning.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses an interesting and ambitious problem: applying machine learning to the symbolic domain of mathematical identity discovery. This is a novel direction that deviates from typical statistical pattern recognition tasks.
*   The concept of using a curriculum learning approach—solving simpler problems to generate training data for more complex ones—is a sensible and effective strategy for bootstrapping the search process.
*   The work provides a concrete proof-of-concept, demonstrating that the framework can find non-trivial simplifications for several families of complex polynomial expressions, reducing their computational complexity from exponential or high-order polynomial to a lower-order polynomial.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, while presenting a novel idea, suffers from significant limitations in its evaluation and positioning relative to existing work, raising doubts about the robustness and generalizability of the proposed method.

1.  **Insufficient Baselines and Comparison to Established Methods:** The primary baseline for the search strategy is a random search within the grammar. This is a very weak baseline. The field of symbolic computation has existed for decades, and established Computer Algebra Systems (CAS) like Mathematica and Maple are highly optimized for expression simplification. The authors dismiss these systems as "rule-based" and not using learning, but this does not excuse the lack of empirical comparison. A convincing demonstration of the proposed method's utility would require showing that it can discover efficient identities that these established, highly-engineered systems cannot. Without such a comparison, the claim that the discovered identities are novel and that the learning-based approach is superior remains unsubstantiated.

2.  **Highly Constrained Problem Setting:** The framework is restricted to a very narrow class of problems: finding scalar-valued, homogeneous polynomials involving a single matrix variable. The grammar itself is small and hand-crafted for this domain. While the authors suggest it could be extended, there is no evidence presented that the approach would be viable for more general mathematics involving different functions (e.g., trigonometric, exponential), data structures, or logical constraints. This makes the work appear more like a solution to a carefully curated set of puzzles rather than a general framework for discovering mathematical identities. The claim of discovering "mathematical identities" feels overstated; the results are more akin to finding algorithmic shortcuts for specific polynomial forms.

3.  **Performance of the Proposed Learning Models:** A key contribution is the application of a recursive neural network (RNN) to learn a continuous representation of expressions and guide the search. However, the experimental results in Figure 3 show that simpler n-gram models (particularly 3-, 4-, and 5-grams) consistently outperform the more complex RNN approach across most problem families. This significantly undermines the motivation for using the sophisticated RNN machinery. The paper does not provide a satisfactory analysis of why the simpler, well-established n-gram technique is more effective. The complexity of the RNN model, including a host of training heuristics (identity initialization, curriculum learning, specialized learning rates), suggests it may be brittle and difficult to apply more broadly.

4.  **Evaluation and Claims:** The evaluation is conducted on five hand-picked families of expressions. While these examples are non-trivial, this selective testing does not provide a comprehensive picture of the method's capabilities or failure modes. The claim of discovering "previously undiscovered" identities is a strong one that is difficult to verify and requires more than a cursory dismissal of existing symbolic computation literature. Furthermore, the practical impact of these new identities is alluded to (e.g., for RBMs) but not demonstrated in any downstream application.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Quality:** The technical soundness of the proposed system within its narrow scope seems adequate. However, the experimental methodology is weak due to the absence of strong baselines from the symbolic computation field. The claims about novelty and superiority are not rigorously supported by the evidence provided. The work feels incomplete without a more thorough comparison to the state of the art.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written. The core ideas are explained clearly, although the interplay between the numerical representation (for checking equality) and the learned RNN representation (for guiding search) could be better delineated.
*   **Originality:** The application of machine learning, particularly sequence and tree models, to guide a symbolic search for mathematical identities is a novel and original concept. The use of an RNN to embed symbolic expressions is also innovative.
*   **Significance:** The potential significance of this line of work is high. A robust system for automatically finding efficient computational recipes would be a major contribution. However, the current paper presents a preliminary proof-of-concept in a highly restricted domain. Given the methodological weaknesses, particularly the lack of comparison to established CAS tools and the fact that simpler n-gram models outperform the novel RNN, the immediate significance of the contribution is limited. Other researchers are more likely to build on the general idea than on the specific (and seemingly fragile) RNN-based implementation.

In summary, the paper introduces an intriguing and novel research direction. However, its claims are not supported by a rigorous, comparative evaluation against established methods in symbolic computation. The proposed sophisticated learning model (RNN) is largely outperformed by a much simpler baseline (n-grams), questioning a central part of the technical contribution. While the idea is promising, the work in its current form is not yet a mature contribution and does not convincingly demonstrate an advance over the state of the art.
