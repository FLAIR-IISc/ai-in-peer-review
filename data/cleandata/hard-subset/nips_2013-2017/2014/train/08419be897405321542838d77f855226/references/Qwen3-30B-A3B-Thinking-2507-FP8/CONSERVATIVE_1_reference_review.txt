

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper proposes a novel approach to discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning to guide a grammar-based search for symbolic expression optimization. The authors frame the problem as finding alternative expressions that compute the same result as a target expression but with lower computational complexity, using attribute grammars to represent expressions and machine learning models (n-gram and RNN) to guide the search.

The paper's main contribution is a framework that uses learning to improve the search strategy for finding efficient identities, with the claim that their approach can discover identities too complex for manual discovery or brute-force search. The authors demonstrate examples where expressions originally requiring O(n^3) time can be computed in O(n^2) time, and provide results showing their learning-based search strategies outperform random search for higher-degree expressions.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the proposed framework. The numerical representation using integer modulo p is a clever solution to the equivalence checking problem. The experimental results for lower-degree cases (k ≤ 5) are promising, and the learned representations for symbolic expressions show reasonable classification accuracy (≤5% error rate for k=3-6).

**Weaknesses:** Despite these strengths, the paper falls short on several critical dimensions required for NIPS acceptance.

First, the methodology is built on highly restrictive assumptions (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix variable, scalar output) that severely limit the applicability of the approach. The authors acknowledge these limitations but fail to convincingly demonstrate why these restrictions don't prevent the framework from being useful in practical machine learning scenarios. The connection to "dropout" and "RBM partition function" is speculative without concrete evidence of how their discovered identities would actually be used in these contexts.

Second, the validation is inadequate. The paper claims to have discovered "previously undiscovered" identities, but provides no comprehensive mathematical proof that these identities are indeed novel or correct. The examples provided (like Example 2) are specific to their grammar and don't demonstrate generalizable patterns. The paper also fails to compare against established symbolic computation tools like Mathematica or Maple, which can simplify expressions but may not explicitly seek lower complexity.

Third, the learning approach appears to be a solution in search of a problem. For the families of expressions they consider, the patterns they identify (like the repetitive structure in Example 2) are simple enough that they could be discovered through straightforward pattern recognition without machine learning. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that machine learning is necessary for these discoveries, especially since the n-gram models outperform the RNNs in their experiments.

Fourth, the paper lacks rigorous analysis of failure cases. For the most challenging family (RBM-2)k, none of their approaches performed better than random search for k > 5, yet this is presented as a minor footnote rather than a significant limitation. The paper also doesn't analyze when their approach would fail or what the theoretical guarantees (if any) are for the discovered identities.

Finally, the paper overstates the significance of its contributions. The claim that these are "previously undiscovered" identities is unsubstantiated without a thorough literature review of similar work in symbolic computation. The connection to practical machine learning applications is speculative rather than demonstrated.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents an interesting application of machine learning to a symbolic computation problem, but the methodological limitations, lack of thorough validation, and overstatement of significance prevent it from meeting NIPS's standards for a high-quality contribution. The framework is too narrowly defined to be generally applicable, and the benefits of the learning approach aren't convincingly demonstrated beyond the specific examples provided.

I recommend rejection. The authors should substantially broaden the scope of their experiments, provide more rigorous validation of their discovered identities, and demonstrate practical utility in real-world machine learning scenarios before reconsidering for publication at NIPS. The core idea of using machine learning to guide symbolic search has potential, but this particular implementation lacks the necessary depth and validation to be considered a significant contribution to the field.
