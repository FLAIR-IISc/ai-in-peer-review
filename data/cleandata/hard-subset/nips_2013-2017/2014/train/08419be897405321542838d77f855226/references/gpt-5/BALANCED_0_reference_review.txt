Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target symbolic expression. The search space is defined by an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic and element-wise operations; each expression corresponds to a grammar tree. Exact equality to the target is checked not by symbolic manipulation but by numerical evaluation over many random inputs in a finite field Zp; for complex identities a linear combination of multiple trees is learned by solving Fw = t (mod p). Because exhaustive enumeration becomes intractable as degree k grows, the authors learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets: (i) n-gram models over subtrees, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings φ(S) of expressions; a softmax on top of φ predicts the next rule to apply during search.

Experiments consider five families of targets (matrix polynomials like sum(sum((A A^T)^•)), element-wise variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, and exponential sums related to RBM partition functions). The paper reports that learning-guided search dramatically expands the complexity of solvable targets. In particular, it rediscovers identities like sum(sum(A*B)) reducible from O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)), and finds O(nm) forms for sum(sum((A*B)^k)). The learned RNN achieves ≤5% error on classifying expressions into equivalence classes up to degree k=6. Across families, 3–5-gram models generally outperform the RNN-guided strategy; for the hardest RBM-2 family, no method scales beyond k=5 within a 10-minute budget, though RNN finds k=5 faster than random. The work relates to theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, and draws on NLP techniques (n-grams; recursive networks for trees). It differs from CAS simplifiers (e.g., Maple/Mathematica) by explicitly optimizing computational cost and using learned heuristics rather than fixed rules.

Strengths
- Clear, compelling problem: discovering more efficient symbolic forms can have real computational impact and relevance to ML (e.g., RBM-like sums).
- Well-motivated grammar-based search with explicit complexity attributes; linear combinations of trees broaden expressiveness.
- Clever use of numerical equivalence via random evaluations over Zp to avoid expensive symbolic proofs; practical and integrates with learning.
- Learning-guided search significantly improves over random and enables discoveries unlikely by brute force; training on simpler instances is natural and effective.
- RNN-derived continuous embeddings for algebraic expressions are interesting in their own right; reported low classification error suggests meaningful structure is captured.
- Open-sourced code and data (claimed), aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Correctness guarantees are underdeveloped. The approach relies on randomized polynomial identity testing via evaluation over Zp, but the paper does not provide bounds on the probability of collision (false positives/negatives) as a function of p, N, and degree/size of expressions. Incorporating a formal statement and proof sketch (e.g., DeMillo-Lipton/Schwartz-Zippel) would strengthen confidence. Similarly, clarify that solving Fw=t (mod p) implies polynomial identity over reals with high probability under appropriate randomness assumptions.
- “Novel identities” are verified empirically; it would be reassuring to confirm a subset with a CAS or to provide formal derivations once candidates are found.
- Experimental methodology is limited. Success is measured by “fraction of runs within 10 minutes,” but there is no ablation on search budget, beam width, or scheduler variants (e.g., beam search, MCTS) nor comparisons to non-learning heuristics used in superoptimization and program synthesis. Report wall-clock speedups on representative matrix sizes, not only asymptotic complexity changes.
- The RNN, while a key contribution, underperforms n-grams on most families. There is little analysis diagnosing failure modes or showing cases where learned embeddings truly enable generalization beyond n-gram repetition. The classification task (≤5% error) is only indirectly tied to search performance; an ablation linking classifier quality to identity discovery success would be helpful.
- Scope restrictions (single variable, homogeneous degree, scalar outputs) limit applicability; the authors acknowledge this but do not quantify how often real-world ML expressions fit these constraints.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written. However, several crucial hyperparameters are underspecified in the provided text (e.g., exact p and N, grammar details in Table 1, scheduler details for multi-branch construction, and how two-branch decisions are made when more than two continuations are possible).
- The linear system solving modulo p and its conditioning merits more detail (e.g., when is F full rank; regularization; multiple solutions).
- Figures showing example trees are helpful; providing at least one nontrivial found identity in the main text (not only in supplementary) would help readers.

Originality
- Applying learned guidance (n-grams, RNN) to attribute-grammar search for algebraic identities appears novel and timely. The idea of learned continuous embeddings for algebraic trees is original for this domain. That said, the finding that simple n-grams outperform the RNN raises questions about the added value of the neural component in its current form.

Significance
- If correctness is better justified and the scope broadened, this line of work could impact automatic algebraic optimization, compilers, and ML model acceleration. As presented, the demonstrated gains are promising but mainly within constrained families; the hardest class (RBM-2) remains largely unsolved.
- The embedding of expressions could catalyze further ML-for-symbolic work.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theoretical guarantees for the randomized equality test and for the linear-combination solver; specify p and N, and give error probabilities for the degrees considered.
- Validate discovered identities with a CAS and/or provide formal proofs for representative cases; include at least one substantial identity in the main paper.
- Compare against stronger search baselines: beam search, MCTS/UCT, A* with learned costs, equality saturation (e-graphs) with a learned cost model.
- Analyze why the RNN underperforms n-grams; consider contrastive/self-supervised objectives for φ, graph neural networks over expression DAGs, or joint training of φ and the policy head end-to-end. Add ablations on curriculum, identity initialization, dropout, and dimensionality l.
- Report practical runtime/memory speedups on concrete sizes and evaluate generalization across families (train on one family, test on another).
- Relax assumptions (multiple variables, non-homogeneous polynomials, non-scalar outputs) and discuss termination strategies for richer grammars.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a meaningful and underexplored problem with a clear ML angle.
- Introduces a practical framework that discovers nontrivial efficiency-improving identities.
- Provides an intriguing continuous representation of symbolic expressions.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient theoretical treatment of correctness for the randomized equivalence and linear combination steps.
- Experimental evaluation lacks strong baselines and practical runtime comparisons; scope is narrow.
- The core neural component does not consistently outperform simple n-grams; limited insight into when/why it helps.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper is promising and could become impactful with stronger correctness guarantees, broader and more rigorous empirical evaluation, and clearer evidence that the learned representation materially improves search beyond simple statistical patterns. The authors’ framework and ideas are valuable; the suggested additions would significantly strengthen the case for NeurIPS.
