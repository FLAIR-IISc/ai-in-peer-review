This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then use machine learning to guide the search through this space. The authors explore two main learning approaches: n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN), which are trained on solutions to simpler problems to find solutions for more complex target expressions. The paper demonstrates the discovery of efficient identities for several families of polynomial expressions, some of which are claimed to be novel.

The paper tackles an interesting and challenging problem: applying statistical machine learning to the symbolic domain of mathematics. The core ideas—using a grammar to structure the search and employing learning to prune the enormous search space—are compelling. The application of an RNN to learn a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions is a particularly novel contribution with potential for broader impact. However, the paper is marred by a significant number of clarity issues, methodological ambiguities, and questionable experimental design choices that undermine the strength of its contributions.

**Strengths:**
*   **Novel Problem Formulation:** The application of machine learning to discover efficient mathematical identities is a creative and promising research direction, bridging the gap between symbolic computation and statistical pattern recognition.
*   **Learned Representations for Symbols:** The development of a continuous, distributed representation for mathematical expressions using an RNN is a key contribution. This could enable a wide range of ML techniques to be applied to symbolic manipulation tasks, much as word embeddings did for NLP.
*   **Curriculum Learning:** The strategy of training models on simpler expressions (lower degree k) to solve more complex ones is logical and well-motivated.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper suffers from a lack of rigor in its presentation and methodology.

*   **Clarity and Formatting:**
    *   The manuscript contains several typographical errors (e.g., `O(()n3)` in Section 3) and formatting inconsistencies. The references are poorly formatted, with missing information (e.g., [1] is missing the year and page numbers), which suggests a lack of care in preparation.
    *   The notation is often confusing. In Example 1, the efficient formula `sum((sum(A, 1) * B)’, 1)` is not standard. The second `sum` call appears to operate on a transposed row vector, and the purpose of the final `, 1` argument is unclear.
    *   The presentation of formulas in Example 2 is nearly unreadable. A recursive definition or more compact notation would have been far more effective than listing out five extremely long, parenthesis-laden expressions.

*   **Methodological Ambiguities:**
    *   **Problem Scope:** Section 2 states that the paper restricts its focus to expressions with a single variable `A`, yet the primary motivating example (Example 1) involves two matrices, `A` and `B`. This is a significant inconsistency; the method is not evaluated on the very problem used to introduce it.
    *   **Numerical Representation:** The reliability of the numerical descriptor is not analyzed. The authors state `N` must be "sufficiently large" but provide no guidance or analysis on how `N` relates to the polynomial degree or the probability of expression collision (aliasing).
    *   **RNN Architecture:** Key details of the RNN are underspecified. The application of a 3D tensor `W3` is denoted `(W3 * a) * a`, which is ambiguous. Furthermore, the claim of initializing weights to the "identity" is not explained for the tensor weights, where the concept of an identity is non-trivial. The choice to use a "fixed random vector" for padding unary operations in the RNN strategy (Section 6.3) seems arbitrary and unjustified compared to a zero or learned padding vector.
    *   **Linear Combinations:** When the linear system in Section 5 yields multiple solutions, the paper does not specify the criterion for choosing among them (e.g., lowest complexity, fewest terms).

*   **Experimental Design Flaws:**
    *   A major weakness is the decision to manually remove the matrix-multiplication rule from the grammar for two of the experimental families. The paper claims the framework "discovers" an efficient O(n^2) solution to an O(n^3) problem, but this discovery is trivial if the O(n^3) operation is forbidden from the outset. This significantly overstates the system's learning capability; it is not learning to avoid the expensive operation, but is simply prevented from using it.
    *   The results in Figure 3 show that simpler n-gram models consistently outperform the more sophisticated RNN model. While this is an interesting result, its implications are not sufficiently discussed. It calls into question whether the complexity of the RNN approach is justified for this task.
    *   The negative result for the (RBM-2)k family, where no learned method surpasses the random baseline for k > 5, highlights the limitations of the proposed techniques, which should be discussed more frankly.

*   **Unsupported Claims:**
    *   The paper claims the discovery of "many new mathematical identities," but their novelty and significance are not established. A comparison to the output of established symbolic algebra systems like Mathematica or Maple would be necessary to substantiate this claim.
    *   The connection to practical applications like a "closed-form version of dropout" or efficient computation of the RBM partition function (Section 8) is highly speculative and not directly demonstrated by the experiments.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper introduces a novel and potentially impactful research direction at the intersection of ML and symbolic mathematics.
2.  The idea of learning a distributed representation for symbolic expressions is a valuable contribution on its own.
3.  The framework, despite its flaws, does succeed in finding non-trivial identities that are difficult to find by random search.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The paper is poorly written, with numerous typos, notational ambiguities, and sloppy referencing that detract from its scientific quality.
2.  There are significant methodological gaps and unjustified design choices (e.g., inconsistent problem scope, removal of grammar rules, random vector padding).
3.  The experimental design contains a crucial flaw (manually removing the mat-mul rule) that undermines one of the central claims of the paper.
4.  The overall impression is that of a preliminary proof-of-concept rather than a complete and rigorous scientific contribution suitable for a top-tier conference like NIPS.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a fascinating line of inquiry, the execution is flawed. The lack of clarity, methodological rigor, and questionable experimental choices prevent a clear assessment of the contributions. The sheer number of minor and major issues suggests the work is not yet ready for publication. I therefore recommend rejection, but I would encourage the authors to revise the paper substantially to address these concerns, as the core ideas are certainly worthy of investigation.
