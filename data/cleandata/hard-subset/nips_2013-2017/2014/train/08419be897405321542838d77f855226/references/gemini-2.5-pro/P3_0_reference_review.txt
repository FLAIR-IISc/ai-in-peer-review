This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then learn an efficient search strategy to explore this space. This contrasts with brute-force or random search, which quickly becomes intractable. The authors propose and evaluate two learning-based search strategies: an n-gram model over sub-expressions and a recursive neural network (RNN) trained to predict promising grammar rules. A key component of their system is a numerical representation of expressions, which allows for fast equality checking by evaluating expressions on random inputs modulo a large prime. The models are trained in a curriculum learning fashion, using solutions from simpler problems to guide the search for more complex ones. The authors demonstrate their method on several families of polynomial expressions, discovering new, efficient identities that are O(n^2) or O(n^3) instead of naive implementations that are O(n^k) or even exponential.

### Strengths

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a challenging and important problem at the intersection of symbolic computation and machine learning. The application of learning techniques to guide search in a formal grammar for mathematical discovery is highly novel. This work opens up a promising new research direction. The potential impact is significant, with possible applications in compiler optimization, scientific computing, and accelerating computations within machine learning itself (e.g., the RBM partition function example). The discovered identities are a concrete contribution, but the primary contribution is the framework itself.

2.  **Technically Sound and Creative Methodology:** The overall framework is well-conceived and executed. Several components are particularly clever:
    *   The use of an attribute grammar is an elegant way to formalize the search space.
    *   The numerical "fingerprinting" of expressions using evaluation over a finite field (Zp) is a very practical solution to the slow and complex problem of symbolic equivalence checking.
    *   The idea of training on simpler expressions to solve more complex ones (curriculum learning) is a natural and effective fit for this problem.
    *   The application of an RNN to learn a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions is a novel contribution in its own right, paralleling the success of word embeddings in NLP and potentially enabling a wide range of learning-based approaches for symbolic reasoning.

3.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, testing the framework on five distinct families of expressions with increasing difficulty. The results convincingly demonstrate that the learned search strategies (both n-gram and RNN) significantly outperform the random baseline, especially as the complexity of the target expressions (and thus the search space) grows. The inclusion of results for various n-gram models and RNN configurations provides a good sense of their relative performance. The paper is honest about the limitations, noting where the methods failed to find solutions for very high-degree polynomials (e.g., RBM-2 for k > 5).

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Limited Scope of the Grammar and Problem Setting:** The current work is restricted to a specific class of expressions: homogeneous polynomials with a scalar output, typically involving a single matrix variable. While this is a rich enough space to demonstrate the power of the approach, the paper could do more to discuss the challenges of extending this framework. How would one incorporate division, trigonometric/exponential functions, or recursion? These would introduce issues like non-termination and a much more complex search space. The current grammar is hand-crafted; a more general system would need principles for automatically constructing or extending grammars.

2.  **Analysis of RNN vs. n-gram Performance:** A surprising result is that the simpler n-gram models often outperform the more sophisticated RNN. The paper notes this but offers little analysis as to why. It is likely that for the families of expressions tested, the optimal solutions exhibit highly regular, repeating local patterns (as seen in Example 2). N-gram models are exceptionally good at capturing such local, compositional structures. The RNN, while more powerful in theory, might be harder to train or might overfit to the global structure of the training trees rather than generalizing the local patterns. A deeper discussion on this point would strengthen the paper and provide valuable insights for future work in this area.

3.  **Clarity on the Linear Combination Step:** The paper describes using a linear solver to find a combination of generated expression trees that matches the target. However, the details of how the set of `M` trees are generated and managed are sparse. Is there a fixed budget for `M`? How does the performance depend on the size of this set? Is this ever a computational bottleneck? A bit more detail on the interplay between the tree generation strategy and the linear combination solver would improve clarity.

4.  **Learned Representation Details:** The training of the RNN for expression classification relies on several specific "tricks" (identity initialization, high learning rate for the final layer, etc.). While it is commendable that the authors share these details, which are crucial for reproducibility, the section would be stronger with more intuition or analysis about *why* these techniques are necessary. For instance, the identity initialization is justified by citing its use in deep linear networks, which helps information flow. This is good, but further connecting this to the specific structure of symbolic expression trees would be beneficial.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that is creative, technically strong, and addresses a problem of significant interest. It successfully demonstrates that machine learning can be used to guide search in a symbolic domain to make novel mathematical discoveries. The combination of ideas from formal grammars, numerical methods, and deep learning is compelling. While the current scope is somewhat limited, the work lays a solid and exciting foundation for future research. The paper is well-written, the experiments are convincing, and the contributions are of high quality and originality, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
