Summary
This paper tackles the automated discovery of computationally efficient mathematical identities for a given target expression. The authors formalize the search space using an attribute grammar over symbolic matrix/vector operations, and represent candidate expressions as grammar trees. Because exhaustive enumeration is infeasible for higher degrees, they learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets. Two learning approaches are explored: (i) n-gram models over subtrees, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous embedding of expressions and, on top of that, a softmax policy that predicts the next grammar rule to apply. Equivalence checking between candidate and target expressions is conducted numerically via evaluations over random inputs in a finite field Z_p, and linear combinations of multiple trees are solved via modular Gaussian elimination to match targets that require sums of subexpressions. Experiments on several expression families (e.g., sums over powers of A A^T, elementwise variants, symmetric polynomials, and exponential sums reminiscent of RBM partition functions) show that learning-guided search finds identities that reduce naive cubic or exponential complexity to O(n^2)–O(n^3), with n-grams often outperforming the RNN. The authors claim many discovered identities are novel and release code.

Relation to prior work
The paper sits at the intersection of symbolic computation, program induction, and learned search. Unlike rule-based simplification in CAS (Maple/Mathematica), it explicitly seeks lower-complexity rewrites and replaces hand-designed heuristics with learned guidance. Compared to learning in theorem proving (e.g., heuristic selection) and probabilistic programming, the scope is narrower but more tractable (homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree), allowing systematic exploration. Methodologically, the use of attribute grammars for optimization has precedent in other domains, but learning embeddings for symbolic expressions via RNNs and using them to guide identity discovery is novel for this task. The work also borrows ideas from NLP (n-grams, recursive networks), adapting them to algebraic trees.

Strengths
- Clear and interesting problem formulation with potential practical impact (compiler/optimizer backends, efficient evaluation of certain ML quantities).
- Well-motivated use of attribute grammars with complexity annotations; the search space is explicit and auditable.
- Clever, practical equivalence checking using random finite-field evaluations to accelerate search; linear-combination solver enables composing multiple subtrees.
- Learning-based guidance substantially expands the tractable regime versus random search; simple n-gram strategies are surprisingly strong.
- RNN-based embeddings for expressions are demonstrated to classify equivalence classes with low error, a potentially reusable representation.
- Code/data release improves reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Correctness guarantees are probabilistic: matching over Z_p at random points can produce false positives/negatives. The paper should quantify collision probabilities (e.g., via Schwartz–Zippel) and recommend multi-prime, multi-sample verification or a final symbolic check.
- The linear-combination step solves F w = t mod p. It is unclear how w maps back to permissible symbolic operations: Are arbitrary integer coefficients allowed in the grammar? Are coefficients constrained (e.g., to small integers) and realizable via available rules? This mapping needs clarification to ensure that a solution in Z_p corresponds to a valid identity over reals/integers using permitted operations.
- Evaluation emphasizes “fraction of runs that find a solution under a 10-minute cap,” but lacks systematic complexity/cost savings quantification across families and sizes, head-to-head comparisons with CAS simplification/rewriting or other search heuristics, and ablations on scheduler components.
- The RNN policy underperforms n-grams in several cases; there is little analysis on when the learned embedding helps, what structures it captures, and how to improve it.

Clarity
- The grammar is central; however, Table 1 is not included here, and the paper should give a precise, self-contained list of rules, their types, and their attribute costs. Explain which rules are disabled in certain families and why.
- Better explain the descriptors: choice of p and N, their effect on error rates and linear system solvability, and the exact tolerance strategy for equality in Z_p.
- Clarify how multiple-branch states are handled when predicting the next rule (e.g., ordering, handling commutativity/associativity, and whether canonicalization is used).
- Provide more worked examples translating a linear combination solution back into a single expression built from the grammar.

Originality
- The application of learned guidance (n-grams and especially RNN embeddings) to symbolic identity discovery within an attribute grammar seems novel. That said, comparisons to optimization via term rewriting, algebraic circuits, common subexpression elimination, or existing compiler peephole optimizations are sparse.

Significance
- Potentially useful for automatic algebraic optimization in ML/tensor programs, and as a proof of concept for learned search over symbolic spaces. However, the breadth of discovered identities and comparisons to strong baselines are limited; significance would be strengthened by demonstrations on real workloads or integration in an optimizer.

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting, well-scoped problem with compelling examples (e.g., O(n^3) → O(n^2); exponential sums to polynomial-time forms).
- Demonstrates that simple learned statistics (n-grams) can greatly improve search in symbolic spaces; RNN embeddings for expressions are promising.
- Reproducible, with code and datasets.
- Opens a path for ML-driven symbolic manipulation beyond hand-crafted heuristics.

Cons for acceptance
- Incomplete correctness story (finite-field verification and linear combination mapping).
- Limited baselines and quantitative evaluation of end-to-end complexity gains vs established tools.
- RNN component underexplored; weaker than n-grams in many settings.
- Strong restrictions (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, mostly single variable) and heavy reliance on grammar pruning (e.g., disallowing matrix multiply) may bias what identities are discoverable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide formal probabilistic guarantees for equivalence checking (Schwartz–Zippel), use multiple primes and random draws, and add a final symbolic verification step.
- Explicitly constrain and document allowed coefficients in linear combinations; ensure solutions correspond to realizable expressions in the grammar (e.g., by incorporating scalar-multiply-by-constant rules or restricting w to small integers and verifying).
- Add baselines: CAS simplification (Maple/Mathematica), heuristic search without learning, and dynamic programming/common-subexpression baselines on polynomial circuits.
- Report concrete complexity and runtime reductions for discovered identities across problem sizes; include numerical stability considerations.
- Analyze the RNN embedding: visualize clusters, probe generalization across k, ablate curriculum, identity initialization, and dropout; consider beam search/MCTS guided by the policy and a learned value function.
- Extend experiments to multi-variable targets and non-homogeneous polynomials, or clearly argue why the current scope is representative.
- Clarify the grammar, the scheduler, and the mapping from solved linear combinations back to final expressions with multiple worked cases.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents a novel and timely approach with promising results and a reusable idea (learned embeddings for symbolic expressions), but would benefit from stronger correctness guarantees and more rigorous empirical comparisons. If the authors address the verification/mapping issues and add stronger baselines and analyses, the contribution would be solid for NeurIPS.
