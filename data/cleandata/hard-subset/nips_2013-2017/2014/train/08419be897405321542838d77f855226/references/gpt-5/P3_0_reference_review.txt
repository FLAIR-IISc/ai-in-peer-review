Summary
The paper tackles the problem of finding computationally efficient equivalents of given symbolic expressions. The authors define an attribute grammar over matrix/vector operations and search the space of grammar trees to produce expressions that are provably equivalent to a target but asymptotically cheaper. Because exhaustive search is infeasible, they introduce learned search strategies: (i) n‑gram statistics over successful subtrees and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees (pretrained via a classification task over equivalence classes, then used with a softmax policy to predict the next rule). Equivalence checking avoids slow symbolic manipulation by evaluating expressions over many random assignments in Zp (large prime) to form descriptors; for harder targets, they solve for linear combinations of trees via modular Gaussian elimination. Experiments on five expression families (matrix polynomials, elementwise variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, and two RBM-inspired exponential sums) show that learned strategies substantially outperform random search; n‑gram generally outperforms the RNN for the families studied. The paper claims discovery of new identities and makes code/data available.

Strengths
- Interesting and timely problem at the interface of ML and symbolic computation, with potential compiler and numerical-algebra applications.
- Clear framework combining attribute grammars, randomized equivalence tests, and learning-guided search. The mod‑p descriptor is a practical idea that scales better than symbolic equality.
- Novel use of RNNs to embed algebraic expression trees; the classification accuracies reported (≤5% error up to degree 6) suggest the learned representation captures meaningful structure.
- Empirical evidence that learned policies dramatically enlarge the tractable search space; n‑grams capture repetitive algebraic motifs effectively (e.g., the k‑step patterns in Example 2).
- Public release of code and identities enhances reproducibility.

Weaknesses and questions
- Correctness guarantees are underspecified. The approach relies on randomized equivalence checking over Zp and many random inputs, but the paper does not quantify the probability of collision or provide a bound via, e.g., Schwartz–Zippel. Without such bounds (or a secondary formal verification), the “discovered” identities remain probabilistic.
- Linear combinations over Zp: It is unclear how modular coefficients translate to valid real/rational coefficients in the final identity. Do you perform rational reconstruction or verify the resulting linear combination symbolically over the reals? Are the coefficients constrained to be small integers? As written, a solution w in Zp may not correspond to a meaningful or intended expression over R.
- Evaluation emphasizes success rates under a 10‑minute cap but lacks strong baselines. There is no comparison to modern computer algebra systems (Maple/Mathematica/SymPy) configured for factorization/collecting/common-subexpression elimination, nor to search/synthesis methods such as superoptimization, equality saturation, or theorem provers with E‑graphs. Even if those tools are not designed for the exact setting, a head-to-head on the families considered would contextualize gains.
- The removal of matrix-multiplication rules for two families biases the search to O(n^2) and may preclude identities that do require some matrix multiplies but still yield asymptotic improvements. This design choice should be justified and its impact quantified.
- The RNN’s contribution to search is modest: n‑grams outperform RNN on several families, and the paper provides little analysis of failure modes or when the learned embedding is actually beneficial. The link between the pretraining objective (equivalence class classification) and the downstream search policy is indirect; ablations would help.
- Claims of discovering “previously undiscovered” identities are not substantiated in the main text. It is essential to list at least a representative subset in the paper, provide human‑readable proofs or mechanized verification, and quantify the complexity reductions achieved.
- Scalability is limited on the hardest family ((RBM‑2)k), where learning does not improve beyond k=5. The paper would benefit from a discussion of why, and from method changes that break through this ceiling (e.g., improved exploration, value functions, beam/MCTS).

Quality
- Methodologically sound overall, but missing theoretical guarantees of correctness and a bridge from modular linear solutions to real-domain identities. The complexity analyses for discovered formulas are plausible but not rigorously derived in general. Experimental methodology is systematic, yet baseline coverage is thin.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; the grammar design and scheduler/strategy split are clear. Important details are missing or relegated to supplements: the exact grammar (Table 1), proof/verification protocol for identities, coefficient reconstruction, and more thorough complexity accounting. The RNN architecture and training heuristics are clearly described.

Originality
- Combining attribute grammars with learned guidance for algebraic identity discovery is novel, as is using an RNN to embed symbolic expressions for this task. The mod‑p descriptor approach in this setting is a nice engineering contribution. The n‑gram idea is simple but effective.

Significance
- Potential impact is moderate to high if correctness can be guaranteed and the approach scaled; automatic discovery of more efficient formulas is valuable in scientific computing and ML. As presented, the empirical gains are promising but confined, and the absence of formal verification and stronger baselines limits immediate practical adoption.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a probabilistic correctness analysis (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel bounds) for the descriptor-based equivalence test, with concrete choices of N and p giving failure probabilities.
- Explain how modular linear solutions are lifted to integer/rational coefficients and verified over the reals; include a symbolic or mechanized verification step for all reported identities.
- Report a subset of identities in the main paper with proofs and explicit operation counts; compare against CAS outputs and equality-saturation/superoptimization baselines.
- Explore stronger search: beam search or MCTS over grammar rules; combine policy (your learned strategy) with a value estimator of “distance to target.”
- Analyze when the RNN helps relative to n‑grams; consider training the RNN end-to-end for search (policy learning) rather than via a classification proxy.
- Remove the restriction on matrix-multiply when possible, and analyze the tradeoff.

Pro/Con for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses an important and underexplored problem with a clever ML–symbolic hybrid.
- Clear empirical improvements over random search; code released.
- Novel RNN embedding of symbolic expressions.

Cons:
- Lacks formal verification and modular-to-real coefficient reconstruction.
- Weak baselines; limited scalability on hardest tasks.
- Main claims about new identities not substantiated in the paper body.

Recommendation: Weak reject (borderline). With added correctness guarantees, stronger baselines, and clearer presentation of identities and complexity proofs, this would be a strong submission.

Confidence: High.
