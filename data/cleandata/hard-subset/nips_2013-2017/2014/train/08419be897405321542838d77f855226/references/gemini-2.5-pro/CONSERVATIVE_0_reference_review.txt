### **Summary**

This paper introduces a framework for discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to define a search space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then use machine learning to guide the search towards valid and efficient identities. The authors represent symbolic expressions numerically by evaluating them on random inputs, allowing for fast equivalence checking without resorting to slow symbolic manipulation. To overcome the intractable nature of a brute-force or random search in the grammar tree, the paper proposes learning a search strategy. Specifically, n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) are trained on solutions discovered for simpler expressions (e.g., lower-degree polynomials) to predict promising search directions for more complex targets. The paper demonstrates this framework by discovering several new, efficient identities for specific families of polynomial expressions, some of which are relevant to machine learning computations.

The work sits at the intersection of symbolic computation, program induction, and machine learning. While symbolic engines like Mathematica or Maple perform expression simplification, they are typically rule-based and do not employ statistical learning to discover new identities of lower complexity. The proposed learning-guided search is the main point of departure from this prior work. The use of an RNN to learn a continuous representation for symbolic expressions is analogous to recent work in natural language processing (e.g., Socher et al., 2013) that learns representations for sentences from their parse trees.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper presents a complete system and demonstrates its effectiveness on a set of non-trivial problems. The approach of using numerical "descriptors" computed over a finite field to check for expression equality is a clever and practical solution to bypass the bottleneck of symbolic manipulation. The experimental results clearly show that the learning-based strategies (n-gram and RNN) significantly outperform a random search baseline, successfully finding identities for expressions of a complexity that random search cannot handle within the time limit.

However, the methodological soundness and evaluation have several weaknesses:
1.  **Weak Baselines:** The primary baseline for the search strategy is a random walk through the grammar tree. This is a very weak baseline. For a search problem of this nature, comparisons against more established search heuristics from AI and program synthesis literature would be necessary for a convincing evaluation. Methods like Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), beam search, or other best-first search algorithms would have provided a much stronger point of comparison and better contextualized the performance of the learning-based models.
2.  **Underperformance of the Novel Model:** A key contribution is presented as the novel application of an RNN to learn a continuous representation of mathematical structures. However, in the main results (Figure 3), this novel and more complex model is consistently outperformed by simpler, standard n-gram models (specifically 3-, 4-, and 5-grams). The paper offers no analysis as to why this might be the case. This result undermines the claimed contribution of the RNN, suggesting that for the structured, repetitive patterns found in the solutions, a simple frequency-based model is more effective.
3.  **Limited Scope and Generalization:** The framework is evaluated on a very restricted class of problems: homogeneous polynomials of a single matrix or vector that evaluate to a scalar. While the authors suggest this could be extended, the paper provides no evidence of its applicability beyond this narrow domain. The claims about finding efficient Taylor series approximations or handling trigonometric functions are speculative. The failure to find solutions for the most complex family ((RBM-2)k) for k > 5 further highlights the current limitations of the approach.
4.  **Lack of Rigor on Key Details:** The numerical representation relies on evaluating expressions on `N` random points, where `N` must be "sufficiently large" to avoid collisions. The paper provides no analysis or justification for the choice of `N`, nor does it discuss the probability of collisions, which is a critical point for the correctness of the method. Similarly, several training details for the RNN (e.g., identity initialization, specific learning rates) and the n-gram model (e.g., the `10^k` weighting) feel ad-hoc and are not well justified.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and organized. The core concepts are explained clearly, and the use of examples in the introduction is highly effective at illustrating the problem and the nature of the discovered solutions. The overall framework is easy to understand.

**Originality:**
The main contribution—using machine learning to guide a search for mathematical identities—is novel. While the constituent parts (grammars, n-gram models, RNNs) are well-established, their application to this symbolic discovery problem is new and interesting. The discovery of previously unknown identities is a concrete and original result. The idea of learning a continuous embedding for symbolic expressions is also an original application of a technique that has proven powerful in other domains.

**Significance:**
The potential significance of this work is high. An automated system that can discover more efficient ways to compute mathematical expressions could have a substantial impact on scientific computing, compilers, and the optimization of machine learning algorithms. The paper tackles a difficult and important problem.

However, the demonstrated significance is limited by the aforementioned issues of scope and evaluation. The discovered identities are for highly specific families of expressions constructed by the authors. It is not clear whether this approach would be effective on the kind of complex, heterogeneous expressions encountered in real-world scientific or engineering code. The claims of potential application to computing the RBM partition function or providing a closed form for dropout are intriguing but remain speculative. The primary significance at this stage is in proposing a new research direction, rather than providing a widely applicable tool.

### **Arguments for and against acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel and challenging problem at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic reasoning.
*   The core idea of training a search policy on solutions to simpler problems is elegant and demonstrates success on its own terms.
*   The paper presents a complete, functional system that discovers new, non-trivial mathematical identities, which is a tangible contribution.
*   The application of representation learning to symbolic mathematics is an interesting direction that could inspire future work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation relies on a weak random search baseline, failing to compare against more relevant and established search algorithms.
*   The main novel learning method proposed (the RNN) is shown to be less effective than simpler, standard n-gram models, weakening the paper's ML contribution.
*   The problem domain is very narrow, and claims about the generalizability and practical impact of the framework are not sufficiently supported by evidence.
*   Key methodological details concerning the correctness of the numerical representation and the justification for model design choices are not rigorously addressed.

Overall, while the paper presents an interesting and original idea, its contributions are undermined by a limited evaluation and a lack of methodological rigor. The fact that a standard n-gram model outperforms the more sophisticated RNN raises questions about the depth of the machine learning contribution for this specific task.
