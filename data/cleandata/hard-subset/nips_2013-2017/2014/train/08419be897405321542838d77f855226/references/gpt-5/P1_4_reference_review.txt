Summary
The paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target expression. Expressions are generated using an attribute grammar whose rules carry computational cost. Exact equality to the target is checked via numerical descriptors computed over random inputs in a large finite field (mod prime), avoiding floating-point issues. For complex targets, the space of grammar trees explodes; to guide search, the authors train strategies from solutions to simpler instances: (i) n-gram models over subtrees and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees and a softmax policy to score next-rule choices. For targets requiring linear combinations of trees, they solve a modular linear system to combine tree descriptors into the target. Experiments on several families (matrix polynomials, elementwise-matrix polynomials, elementary symmetric polynomials, and sums over binary vectors akin to RBM terms) show learned strategies substantially outperform random search, with n-grams often beating the RNN. The authors claim many identities (notably reducing O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)) and O(n3) to O(n2)) that they believe are new.

Relation to prior work
The approach sits at the intersection of program synthesis and symbolic reasoning (attribute grammars, automated theorem proving), probabilistic programming, and NLP-inspired learning. It distinguishes itself from computer algebra systems (Maple, Mathematica), which focus on algebraic simplification but not explicitly on minimizing computational complexity, and from rule-based heuristic search by learning the search policy. The use of RNNs to embed symbolic expressions parallels work on tree-structured representations in NLP, but adapted here to mathematical operator trees. Relevant additional prior work not cited includes the SPIRAL system (Püschel et al.) for deriving fast signal transforms via algebraic rewrites and search, and randomized polynomial identity testing via the Schwartz–Zippel lemma, which provides theoretical guarantees for the numerical equivalence test the paper relies on.

Strengths
- Ambitious and original goal: learning to discover efficient algebraic identities, bridging ML and symbolic computation.
- Clear formulation with an attribute grammar, enabling explicit control over permitted operations and their costs.
- Clever and practical equality test using finite-field evaluation; avoids brittle floating-point behavior and integrates well with learning.
- Two complementary learned search strategies; the RNN embedding is interesting in its own right (high-accuracy classification of equivalence classes).
- Empirical evidence that learning dramatically expands the tractable search space; discovery of nontrivial identities across several families.
- Released code and data, increasing reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited scope: homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, often single variable and scalar output. Generalization beyond this grammar is discussed but not demonstrated.
- Evaluation methodology is weak in places: success is measured as “found at least once within 10 minutes across 10 runs,” without stronger baselines (e.g., state-of-the-art rewrite/superoptimization systems, Mathematica/Maple heuristic simplifiers, or search with domain-specific heuristics). The removal of matrix–matrix multiply for some families biases the search space.
- The RNN strategy underperforms n-grams on several families; the benefits of learned embeddings over simpler frequency models are not convincingly established.
- No formal correctness guarantees for identities: reliance on finite-field randomized testing is reasonable, but there is no analysis of collision probability versus N and p, nor a proof certificate output. This weakens the “new identities” claim.
- Complexity claims are sometimes coarse. The cost accounting for the discovered identities (e.g., sum reductions, repeated matrix-vector products) should be rigorously analyzed and benchmarked on actual problem sizes; comparisons to naive and to CAS-produced expressions would be helpful.
- Lack of cross-family generalization tests; training on simpler k within a family may not demonstrate transfer to structurally different targets.

Quality
Technically sound in its core components: grammar-based generation, modular linear solving, and learning-guided search. The experimental setup shows clear trends but would benefit from stronger baselines, tighter success metrics, and more rigorous correctness and complexity analyses. Important implementation details (full grammar in Table 1, precise scheduler mechanics, resource usage as k grows) could be elaborated.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with motivating examples. The RNN description is clear, including critical training tricks. Some sections could be improved by including the full grammar, more precise pseudocode for the scheduler/strategy interaction, and a consolidated description of hyperparameters and dataset sizes for the classification task.

Originality
High. Applying learned policies and continuous embeddings to guide symbolic identity discovery is novel, as is the specific combination with attribute grammars and finite-field descriptors. The idea of using learned representations for symbolic math trees is timely and opens avenues for broader applications.

Significance
Potentially significant: automatic discovery of efficient identities is relevant to compilers, autodiff frameworks, and probabilistic modeling (e.g., RBM partition approximations). Even if current scope is narrow, the approach may inspire future systems that blend ML with symbolic optimization. The immediate practical impact is tempered by limited generalization and the lack of formal proof outputs.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide bounds (via Schwartz–Zippel) for the probability of false positives in the modular descriptor test given chosen p and N? How do you select p and N in practice?
- Do you produce proof certificates (e.g., rewrite sequences) once a candidate is found, to enable formal verification? If not, could you integrate an SMT/CAS step post hoc?
- How often do identities found with the “no matmul” constraint rely on identities that would be unnatural if matmul were allowed? Could you compare against a grammar that includes matmul and then measures actual cost?
- Can the RNN policy transfer across families (train on one, test on another)? Any evidence of learned algebraic notions beyond n-gram patterns?
- How do your methods compare to CAS simplification with cost-aware heuristics and to systems like SPIRAL on relevant domains?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add theoretical guarantees for the equality test and report p, N, and collision probabilities.
- Include stronger baselines (CAS with cost heuristics, heuristic rewrite systems) and report time-to-solution distributions, not just fraction of successes.
- Provide formal proofs or certificates for key identities in the supplementary material.
- Analyze and report exact operation counts of discovered identities versus baselines.
- Explore cross-family generalization and hybrid strategies (e.g., n-gram + RNN, MCTS).
- Discuss relation to and differences from SPIRAL and superoptimization for algebraic programs.

Pros
- Novel problem formulation and approach.
- Demonstrated scalability via learning-guided search.
- Interesting RNN embedding of symbolic expressions.
- Public code/data.

Cons
- Narrow scope and constraints.
- Weak baselines and success criteria; limited formal guarantees.
- n-grams often outperform RNN; unclear added value.
- Claims of novelty of identities lack verification/proofs.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents an original and promising direction, with solid initial results and clear potential impact. Strengthening empirical comparisons and adding formal correctness guarantees would substantially improve the work.
