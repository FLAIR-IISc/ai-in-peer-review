**Summary**

This paper presents a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to define a space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is equivalent to a given target but has a lower computational complexity. As the search space is combinatorially large, the main contribution is the use of machine learning to guide the search. The authors propose learning a search strategy by training models on solutions to simpler, lower-degree expressions. They explore two learning approaches: simpler n-gram models that capture local structural patterns, and a more ambitious recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions to predict the next search step. The authors demonstrate their framework by discovering several new, non-obvious identities for families of expressions, including some related to models in machine learning, which offer significant polynomial-time speedups over naive exponential-time evaluations.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper is its outstanding novelty and ambition. The authors tackle a fundamental problem at the intersection of symbolic computation, program synthesis, and machine learning. Framing mathematical discovery as a learning-guided search problem is a bold and exciting direction for the field.

1.  **A Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** While symbolic simplification tools like Mathematica exist, they are typically rule-based and do not explicitly optimize for computational complexity in the way this paper does. The idea of *learning* to find these efficient forms is a significant conceptual leap. The potential applications in algorithm design, compiler optimization, and even theoretical machine learning (e.g., finding closed-form versions of complex sums) are immense.

2.  **Creative Application of Machine Learning:** The paper's most compelling contribution is the application of modern machine learning techniques to this symbolic domain. The transfer of ideas from natural language processing is particularly clever.
    *   The use of an RNN to learn a continuous embedding for symbolic expressions is a fantastic idea. Analogous to word2vec for language, this approach could unlock a vast range of gradient-based methods for symbolic reasoning tasks that have traditionally been resistant to them. The high classification accuracy reported for this representation is a strong proof of concept.
    *   The curriculum learning approach—training on simpler problems (lower `k`) to solve harder ones—is an elegant way to bootstrap the system and mimics how humans might build up mathematical intuition.

3.  **Concrete and Surprising Discoveries:** The paper does not just present a framework; it uses it to discover what appear to be new and useful mathematical identities. Finding polynomial-time equivalents for expressions that are naively exponential (the Sym-k and RBM families) is a very strong result. This demonstrates that the system is not just solving a toy problem but is capable of producing genuinely valuable and non-obvious mathematical insights that are beyond the reach of random search or unassisted human intuition.

**Weaknesses**

While the core ideas are excellent, the execution and validation have some limitations. However, in my view, these are acceptable flaws given the novelty of the work.

1.  **Limited Scope of the Grammar:** The framework is currently restricted to a fairly small class of expressions (homogeneous polynomials of a single variable with scalar output). This is an understandable simplification for a first paper on a new topic, but it does limit the immediate applicability. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the challenges involved in extending the grammar to include, for example, trigonometric functions, conditional logic, or recursion.

2.  **RNN Performance:** It is somewhat disappointing that the more sophisticated RNN model is frequently outperformed by the simpler n-gram models. This suggests that either the discovered identities have a highly repetitive local structure that is well-suited to n-grams, or that the RNN architecture and training scheme are not yet optimized for this task. While the authors are transparent about this result, it slightly undermines the case for the more complex model. That said, the fact that the RNN *can* learn a meaningful representation at all is a significant achievement.

3.  **Scalability:** The method fails to find solutions for the most complex family (RBM-2) for `k > 5`. This highlights the immense difficulty of the underlying combinatorial search problem and shows that while learning helps, it has not fully tamed the exponential explosion. This is not so much a flaw as a realistic appraisal of the current state of the art, which this paper successfully advances.

**Recommendation: Accept**

Arguments for acceptance:
*   **Highly Original:** The core concept of learning to discover mathematical identities is new, exciting, and opens up a promising research avenue.
*   **Conceptually Significant:** The successful creation of a continuous, learned representation for symbolic math is a major conceptual contribution that could have impact far beyond this specific application.
*   **Strong Results:** The discovery of new, non-trivial identities provides compelling evidence that the approach is not just a theoretical exercise but a powerful tool for discovery.
*   **Bold Vision:** This is exactly the kind of high-risk, high-reward paper that NIPS should encourage. It prioritizes a groundbreaking idea over safe, incremental progress.

Arguments against acceptance:
*   The current framework is limited to a narrow class of mathematical expressions.
*   The most sophisticated learning model (RNN) was not consistently the best performer, indicating room for methodological improvement.

I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper introduces a genuinely new idea and demonstrates its potential with impressive results. The weaknesses are primarily limitations of scope that are natural for an initial foray into a new research area. The conceptual novelty and the boldness of the approach far outweigh the incomplete validation. This work has the potential to inspire a great deal of follow-up research on bridging the gap between modern machine learning and symbolic reasoning.
