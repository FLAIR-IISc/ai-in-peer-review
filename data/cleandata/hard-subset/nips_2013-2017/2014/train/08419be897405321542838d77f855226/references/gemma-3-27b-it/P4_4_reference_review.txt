This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities, leveraging attribute grammars and machine learning to navigate the search space. The core idea is to represent expressions as grammar trees and use learned strategies (n-grams and RNNs) to guide the exploration of these trees, aiming to find equivalent expressions with lower computational complexity. The work is well-motivated, addressing a challenging problem at the intersection of symbolic computation and machine learning.

The paper's strengths are numerous. The proposed framework is conceptually sound and provides a structured approach to a traditionally manual process. The application of machine learning to guide the search within the grammar tree is a significant contribution, overcoming the limitations of brute-force enumeration. The authors demonstrate the framework’s effectiveness through compelling examples, including cases where they discover identities previously unknown. The inclusion of code and evaluation data is commendable for reproducibility. The use of integer-based descriptors to mitigate floating-point issues is a practical and important detail. The related work section is comprehensive and accurately positions the contribution relative to existing approaches in theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming. The experimental validation, including ablation studies with different search strategies, is well-executed.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the experimental results are promising, the scope of evaluated families of expressions is relatively limited. The performance gains with the RNN are not consistently superior to the n-gram models, raising questions about the benefits of the more complex RNN architecture, particularly given the need for significant training data. The explanation of why certain strategies perform better for specific families could be more in-depth. The parameter selection for the RNN (e.g., the dimension *l*) feels somewhat arbitrary, despite being determined via cross-validation, and a more detailed justification would be beneficial. The paper acknowledges restrictions such as only handling single variable polynomial expressions, and the scalability to more complex scenarios with multiple variables or functions remains an open question. The explanation of the dropout application within the RNN training could be clearer. The presentation of the identities discovered in the supplementary material is crucial but its availability is only hinted at, and direct inclusion in the main text for key examples would enhance clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for efficient identity discovery is a novel approach.
*   **Significance:** The discovery of previously unknown identities with reduced computational complexity has the potential to impact various fields, particularly those involving heavy mathematical computation.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The framework is well-defined, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed methodology.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Scope:** The experiments are currently limited to a specific set of mathematical expression families.
*   **RNN Performance:** The RNN does not consistently outperform simpler n-gram models, questioning its added value.
*   **Scalability:** The potential scalability to more complex mathematical scenarios is unclear.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a compelling and innovative approach to a challenging problem. While there are some limitations regarding the scope and performance of certain components, the novelty, significance, and technical soundness of the work are substantial. The paper offers a valuable contribution to the field and warrants acceptance, potentially with minor revisions to address the identified weaknesses. A stronger emphasis on scalability and a more detailed analysis of the RNN performance would further strengthen the paper.
