### Summary

This paper presents a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities for a given target symbolic expression. The core idea is to define a search space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then use machine learning to guide the search, which would otherwise be intractable due to a combinatorial explosion of possibilities. The authors propose learning a search strategy by training models on solutions found for simpler expressions, embodying a curriculum learning approach. They explore two main learning methods: n-gram models over sub-expressions and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous representation of symbolic expressions to predict the next best grammar rule to apply. A key component is an efficient numerical method for verifying the equivalence of expressions using modular arithmetic. The authors demonstrate their framework by discovering novel, efficient identities for several families of complex polynomial expressions, including some relevant to machine learning, such as terms in the Taylor series of an RBM partition function.

### Quality

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and well-executed piece of work.

**Strengths:**
1.  The proposed framework is well-conceived. The combination of an attribute grammar to define the search space, a numerical method for fast equivalence checking, and a learning-based search strategy is a coherent and powerful approach to a very difficult problem.
2.  The experimental methodology is solid. The authors test their approach on five distinct families of expressions of increasing difficulty. The comparison against a random search baseline clearly demonstrates the value of the learning-based strategies. The incremental, curriculum-style training (using solutions for degree `k` to train for `k+1`) is a sensible and effective way to tackle the scaling complexity.
3.  The technical details provided, such as using integer arithmetic modulo a prime (`Zp`) to avoid floating-point errors and numerical overflow, show a high degree of care in the implementation. Similarly, the detailed description of how the RNN was trained (identity initialization, curriculum learning, etc.) adds to the work's reproducibility and credibility.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The description of how linear combinations of trees are handled in Section 5 is somewhat brief. While the use of Gaussian elimination to solve `Fw = t` is clear, the process of generating the set of `M` trees is not fully specified. It is unclear how many trees are generated before the solver is invoked or how this interacts with the search strategy and the fixed time cutoff. A more detailed explanation of this part of the scheduler would strengthen the paper.
2.  The paper states that for two of the expression families, the matrix-matrix multiply rule was removed from the grammar to *ensure* that any found solution would be efficient. While pragmatic, this feels like a hand-engineered constraint that sidesteps part of the discovery problem. An interesting ablation study would be to see if the system could discover the O(n^2) solutions even with the O(n^3) rule available, perhaps by incorporating the computational cost attribute directly into the learning objective.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The core concepts are introduced intuitively and motivated with clear examples.

**Strengths:**
1.  The introduction is excellent. Example 1 (`sum(sum(A*B))`) provides a simple, compelling illustration of the problem, and Example 2 (`sum(sum((A*B)^k))`) effectively motivates the need for a learned search strategy over random search.
2.  The distinction between the different components of the system (grammar, representation, search strategy) is clear. The progression from the simple random strategy to the more complex n-gram and RNN models is logical.
3.  The figures and tables are informative and support the text well. Figure 3, in particular, provides a concise and convincing summary of the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The title of Section 4, "Representation of Symbolic Expressions," is slightly ambiguous. It covers two distinct concepts: a numerical representation for *verifying equivalence* (4.1) and a learned representation for *guiding search* (4.2). A clearer distinction or section titling could prevent potential confusion about the roles of these two representations.

### Originality

The paper is highly original and presents a novel intersection of machine learning, symbolic computation, and program synthesis.

**Strengths:**
1.  The primary contribution—using machine learning to guide a combinatorial search for mathematical identities—is a novel and powerful idea. It marks a significant departure from traditional rule-based symbolic computation systems (e.g., Maple, Mathematica) and simple brute-force or random search methods.
2.  The application of a recursive neural network to learn a continuous embedding for symbolic mathematical expressions is a significant and original contribution. This draws an insightful parallel to work in NLP and opens up the historically discrete domain of symbolic math to a wide array of continuous optimization and representation learning techniques.
3.  The work successfully discovers previously unknown identities for complex expressions (e.g., for the RBM families), demonstrating that the approach is not just a theoretical novelty but a practical tool for discovery.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are important and likely to have a substantial impact on both the machine learning community and other fields.

**Strengths:**
1.  The paper addresses a fundamental problem in AI: managing combinatorial search in a discrete, symbolic space. The successful application of learning to prune this search space offers a promising direction for other challenging AI tasks.
2.  The discovered identities themselves are significant. Finding polynomial-time equivalents for expressions that are naively exponential to compute (like the RBM families) could have direct practical applications in machine learning, potentially enabling more efficient inference or training algorithms.
3.  The concept of a learned continuous representation for symbolic expressions is a foundational contribution that could be built upon by many other researchers. It provides a "vector space model" for mathematics, analogous to word embeddings in NLP, which could enable new research in areas like automated theorem proving, equation solving, and educational tools.
4.  The framework itself is a potential stepping stone towards automated scientific discovery, where a system can not only verify proofs but also propose novel, useful conjectures (in this case, identities).

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   **High Novelty and Impact:** The paper introduces a genuinely new paradigm for symbolic computation, leveraging modern machine learning in a domain traditionally governed by deterministic rules. The potential for discovering new, useful mathematics is very high.
*   **Strong Technical Contribution:** The development of an RNN for symbolic expression embedding is a significant contribution on its own, with broad applicability beyond the immediate task.
*   **Convincing Empirical Results:** The method is shown to dramatically outperform a random search baseline and successfully discovers complex, non-obvious identities, validating the entire approach.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a complex topic accessible and its contributions easy to understand.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

*   **Limited Scope of Current Application:** The framework is currently restricted to a specific class of expressions (homogeneous polynomials). While the authors acknowledge this, the extension to more general mathematics is non-trivial and remains future work.
*   **Minor Lack of Detail:** Some implementation details, particularly around the linear solver for combinations of trees, could be more thoroughly explained.

### Recommendation

This is a strong paper that presents a creative, well-executed, and potentially high-impact line of research. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The combination of a novel problem formulation, a clever technical solution, and impressive empirical results makes it an excellent fit for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
