- Proposes a framework to discover computationally efficient algebraic identities using an attribute grammar over linear-algebra primitives and learned search policies.  
- Learns search strategies from simpler targets via n-gram subtree statistics and an RNN embedding/classifier used to predict grammar rules during search.  
- Handles linear combinations of expression trees by solving modular linear systems and reconstructing coefficients.

- Clear and relevant problem formulation with practical applications in symbolic optimization and ML.  
- Innovative combination of attribute grammars, randomized identity testing, and learned policies bridging PL, NLP, and ML.  
- RNN embedding for symbolic expressions is interesting and achieves low classification error up to k=6.  
- Demonstrated empirical wins: reduces complexity (e.g., O(n^3)→O(n^2), exponential→polynomial for small k) on several expression families.  
- Code and training details are provided, aiding reproducibility.

- Equivalence checking relies on randomized evaluation over Zp without quantified collision bounds or use of standard results (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel) and lacks details on number of tests/primes.  
- Modular solutions for linear combinations may yield coefficients not realizable in the grammar (non-integer, negative, large); lifting and validation over the reals are not specified.  
- Cost model encodes complexity via allowed grammar rules, conflating grammar design with true optimality and ignoring constants and memory effects.  
- Evaluation uses "found within 10 minutes" and restarts, conflating model quality with compute budget; more rigorous metrics and stronger search baselines are missing.  
- Limited generalization: training on smaller k within families; cross-family generalization not shown and RNN underperforms n-grams on hard cases.  
- Missing comparisons to strong symbolic baselines (e-graphs/equality saturation, CAS, compiler optimizations, tensor contraction tools).  
- Claims of novel identities lack formal proofs or independent CAS verification.

- Method is technically plausible but needs tighter formalization (probabilistic testing bounds, coefficient constraints, separation of search bias from grammar).  
- Paper is generally well written, but grammar definition, coefficient constraints, and search scheduler details need clarification.  
- The combination of attribute grammars with learned search appears novel; RNN representation is an original contribution.  
- Potentially impactful for symbolic optimization and ML compilers, but current scope (scalar outputs, single variable, homogeneous polynomials) limits immediate practical impact.

- Suggested fixes: provide collision probability bounds, use multiple primes/CRT or rational reconstruction, and validate identities with independent CAS.  
- Specify allowed coefficient sets for linear combinations and show how modular solutions are lifted and validated over the reals.  
- Compare empirically to equality-saturation/e-graphs and CAS heuristics, and try stronger search methods (beam search, MCTS, A*).  
- Explore joint training or RL/IL for the RNN policy, and report cross-family generalization experiments.  
- Clarify search scheduler (beam width, restarts) and make complexity accounting precise.

- Pros: novel direction, reproducible methodology, and convincing empirical wins on nontrivial cases.  
- Cons: limited formal guarantees, restricted evaluation and baselines, and RNN underperforms simple n-grams on hardest tasks.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept if verification and coefficient handling are addressed; otherwise borderline.
