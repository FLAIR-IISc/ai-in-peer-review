This paper presents a fascinating and ambitious attempt to apply machine learning to the discovery of mathematical identities, a traditionally symbolic domain. The core idea – using learned strategies to navigate a grammar-defined search space for more efficient expressions – is genuinely novel and, if successful, could open up exciting new avenues for automated mathematical reasoning and optimization. The reported results, particularly the discovery of identities not previously known (as claimed), are compelling. While the methodology isn’t flawless and the validation isn’t exhaustive, the sheer novelty and potential impact warrant strong consideration.

The attribute grammar framework provides a solid foundation, and the exploration of both n-gram and RNN-based search strategies is well-motivated. The learned representation of symbolic expressions using RNNs is particularly intriguing and represents a significant contribution in itself, bridging the gap between symbolic and continuous learning paradigms. The examples provided (sums of matrices) are illustrative, and the reported complexity reductions are substantial. The availability of code and data further strengthens the paper.

However, several aspects could be improved. The experimental evaluation, while showcasing success in specific cases, feels somewhat limited. The choice of families of expressions seems somewhat arbitrary, and a more systematic comparison against existing symbolic computation systems (Mathematica, Maple) would be valuable – even if those systems fail to find the same identities. The performance of the RNN is curiously surpassed by simple n-gram models in some instances. This deserves deeper investigation. Why do n-grams work well for certain problems, while the more expressive RNN struggles? Is this an issue of training data, network architecture, or something else? More analysis of the learned RNN representations would also be helpful, perhaps visualizing the embeddings or exploring their relationship to known mathematical properties. 

The paper states that the restricted setting (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix/vector variable, scalar output) is adopted to manage the search space. This is reasonable, but a more detailed discussion of the limitations and potential extensions to more general cases is needed.  The claim that the discovered identities are “previously undiscovered” is strong and requires more rigorous justification. A more thorough literature review, specifically targeting the families of expressions explored, would bolster this claim. The section on related work, while present, could be more expansive and better contextualize the contributions within existing research on automated theorem proving and symbolic regression.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **High Novelty:** The application of machine learning to discover mathematical identities is a genuinely new and exciting direction.
* **Potential Impact:** Successful automated identity discovery could have significant implications for optimization, compiler design, and mathematical reasoning.
* **Technical Soundness (Generally):** The core framework (attribute grammars + learned search) is well-defined and appears technically sound.
* **Promising Results:** The reported identity discoveries are compelling and demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.
* **Open Source:** Code and data availability facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is somewhat narrow in scope and lacks systematic comparison to existing systems.
* **RNN Performance:** The surprising underperformance of the RNN compared to n-grams requires further investigation.
* **Strong Claim Validation:** The claim of discovering “previously undiscovered” identities requires stronger justification.
* **Missing Discussion:** A more in-depth discussion of limitations and extensions to more general settings would be beneficial.



**Overall:** Despite some limitations, the novelty and potential impact of this work are substantial. The paper presents a bold and promising new approach to a challenging problem. I recommend **acceptance**, with a request for minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly concerning the experimental evaluation, RNN performance analysis, and justification of the “previously undiscovered” claim. The reviewers should particularly value the innovative spirit of the work, and the willingness to tackle a problem that sits at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation.
