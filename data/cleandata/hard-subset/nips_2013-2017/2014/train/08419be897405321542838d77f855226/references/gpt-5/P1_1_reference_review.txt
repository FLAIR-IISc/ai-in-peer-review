Summary
The paper tackles the discovery of computationally efficient mathematical identities by searching over symbolic expressions defined by an attribute grammar. The target is a homogeneous polynomial of fixed degree in one variable (primarily matrices/vectors), and the goal is to find an equivalent expression of lower asymptotic cost. The framework enumerates grammar trees and, when needed, linear combinations of trees; it checks equivalence via numerical descriptors computed over Zp for random inputs. To make search tractable, the authors learn strategies from simpler instances: (i) n-gram models over grammar subtrees and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions and a softmax head to predict the next grammar rule. Experiments on five families of targets show that learning-guided search can discover identities unreachable by random search within a fixed budget. They report nontrivial speedups (e.g., O(nm) vs O(nmp) for sum(sum(A*B)) and O(n2) vs O(n3) or even improvements from exponential to polynomial time for certain constructed families). They also demonstrate an RNN that classifies expression equivalence classes with low error, suggesting the learned embeddings capture symbolic structure.

Relation to prior work
The work bridges theorem proving/program induction/probabilistic programming and NLP-style learning over grammars. Unlike CAS systems (Maple/Mathematica), which simplify but do not target complexity reduction, this framework explicitly searches for lower-complexity equivalents and learns heuristics. Compared with prior use of attribute grammars for optimization and NLP n-grams/RNNs, the novelty lies in applying these to symbolic algebra search and learning continuous embeddings of mathematical expressions. The paper cites related uses of RNNs for parsing and logic, and learning for heuristic selection in theorem proving.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation with practical implications (compiler-like algebraic optimization; potential links to ML objectives like RBM partition functions and dropout).
- Elegant use of an attribute grammar with complexity attributes; linear combinations of trees extend expressivity beyond single trees.
- Two complementary representations for equality checking and learning: fast randomized Zp descriptors and learned RNN embeddings.
- Learning to guide search significantly extends the range of solvable targets; n-grams and RNNs both help, with n-grams strong on repetitive structures.
- Implementation details for training the RNN (identity init, ReLUs, curriculum, dropout) are useful; code availability increases reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Correctness guarantees: equality checking is probabilistic via evaluations modulo a prime. The paper asserts descriptors “uniquely characterize” expressions without formal bounds (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel-style guarantees, multiple primes). Risk of false positives/negatives is not quantified.
- Verification of “new” identities: proofs are not provided; reliance on randomized testing and supplementary material is insufficient for high-stakes claims.
- Limited baselines: comparisons are only to random and n-gram; no search/learning baselines like beam search, MCTS, A*, program synthesis with cost heuristics, or specialized CAS transformations targeting complexity.
- Scope limitations: grammar is restricted to homogeneous polynomials and a narrow operator set; for some families the matrix-multiply rule is removed to “guarantee” efficiency, which may bias search and exclude valid solutions that do require matmul but are still asymptotically optimal.
- RNN scheduler underperforms n-grams on several families; analysis of failure modes and ablations (e.g., end-to-end fine-tuning of the RNN during policy learning) would help.
- Evaluation metrics: primary metric is fraction of runs finding a solution within 10 minutes; more comprehensive measures (time-to-solution distributions, node expansions, solution diversity, and actual wall-clock speedups on representative sizes) would strengthen the empirical case.
- Reproducibility gaps: Table 1 (grammar) and some hyperparameters/dataset sizes per k are not fully specified in the text; details of the linear solver and handling of ill-conditioning modulo p are brief.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with solid engineering. The main scientific gap is in formal correctness guarantees for the equivalence test and proof of discovered identities. Empirical claims are plausible, but the lack of stronger baselines and proof mechanisms weakens the case for broad superiority. The learned RNN representation for equivalence classification is convincing with ≤5% error up to k=6.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The examples help. Suggestions:
- Include the full grammar (Table 1) and explicit complexity annotations in the main text.
- Provide a formal statement and probability bound for descriptor-based equality; discuss using multiple primes and input distributions.
- Clarify training protocol across k (dataset sizes, splits, number of solutions retained).
- Summarize key discovered identities in the main paper (at least a few representative ones with proof sketches).

Originality
High. Framing identity discovery as learned search over an attribute grammar, using continuous embeddings of symbolic expressions, is novel. The combination of linear combinations of trees with learned policies is also interesting.

Significance
Moderate to high. The approach pushes on a central AI challenge—learned guidance in combinatorial search—and offers a potentially useful tool for symbolic optimization. Impact will increase with stronger correctness guarantees and broader grammars. Even the negative result on RBM-2 beyond k=5 is informative about current limits.

Questions for the authors
- What are the theoretical collision probabilities for the Zp descriptor approach? Do you use multiple primes or input distributions to drive error to negligible levels?
- Can you post-verify discovered identities with a CAS or a formal proof system?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of grammar and to removing matmul? Could beam search or MCTS narrow the gap where RNN underperforms n-grams?
- Have you tried end-to-end training of the RNN with the softmax head on the next-rule task, possibly with curriculum and reinforcement learning?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, well-motivated problem and method
- Demonstrated ability to discover nontrivial identities
- Insightful learned representation for symbolic expressions
- Open-source implementation

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of formal correctness guarantees and proof of identities
- Limited baselines and analysis of failures
- Narrow grammar scope; engineered constraints (e.g., removing matmul)
- RNN scheduler not clearly superior to simple n-grams

Reproducibility
Code is promised; still, include full grammar, seeds, primes, and datasets.

Overall recommendation
7 (accept). Innovative and promising direction with solid results; strengthen proof guarantees and comparisons.

Reviewer confidence
High on ML aspects; moderate on symbolic algebra verification.
