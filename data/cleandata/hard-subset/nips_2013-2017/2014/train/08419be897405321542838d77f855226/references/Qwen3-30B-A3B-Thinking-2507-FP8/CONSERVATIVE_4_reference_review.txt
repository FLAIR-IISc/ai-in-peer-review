

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques applied to attribute grammars. The authors frame the problem as finding alternative expressions that compute the same result as a target expression but with lower computational complexity. They introduce a grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions, and propose using n-gram models and a recursive neural network to guide the search through the space of possible expressions. The authors claim to discover identities that reduce complexity from O(n^3) to O(n^2) for certain polynomial expressions, with examples including matrix operations that would normally require matrix-matrix multiplication but can be computed more efficiently.

The paper builds on attribute grammars [14] for symbolic expression representation, which is a well-established technique. The authors then apply n-gram language modeling and recursive neural networks (RNNs) from natural language processing to this domain. While the application of NLP techniques to symbolic computation is an interesting idea, the paper lacks sufficient justification for why these approaches would be effective for this specific problem.

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good technical explanations of the attribute grammar framework and the learned representations. The examples provided (like Example 1 and Example 2) effectively illustrate the approach. The demonstration of continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions is a potentially useful contribution that could enable other machine learning approaches to be applied to symbolic domains.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of rigorous comparison to established methods:** The paper only compares to random search, but doesn't compare to established symbolic computation methods or theorem provers. For example, systems like Mathematica or Maple [6, 27] have been used for symbolic simplification for decades, and the paper doesn't explain why their approach would outperform these or why machine learning is needed for this problem.

2. **Overstated novelty:** The paper claims to discover "previously undiscovered" identities, but doesn't provide sufficient evidence of novelty. The examples given (like the k=6 case) appear to be simple pattern repetitions of solutions for smaller k values, which suggests the problem may be too constrained to be meaningful. The paper doesn't adequately compare to existing work on symbolic computation or mathematical identity discovery.

3. **Limited scope and restrictive assumptions:** The problem is limited to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with a single matrix/vector input and scalar output. This severely restricts the applicability of the approach. For example, the authors state "we limit our domain to computation of polynomials with fixed degree k" - this seems like a problem definition chosen to make their approach work, rather than addressing a meaningful problem domain.

4. **Lack of theoretical justification:** The paper provides no theoretical analysis of why the n-gram models or RNNs would be effective for this task. The empirical results show that these methods outperform random search for certain families of expressions, but don't explain why they work or why they're better than other approaches.

5. **Questionable practical significance:** The claimed computational savings (e.g., from O(n^3) to O(n^2)) are based on their specific grammar rules rather than fundamental mathematical insights. The paper doesn't demonstrate that these savings translate to meaningful speedups in practice, or that these identities are useful for real-world machine learning applications.

6. **Insufficient validation:** The paper only evaluates on a small set of artificial mathematical families (e.g., (sum(AAT))^k, (sum(A.*A)AT)^k). It doesn't test on real-world mathematical problems that would benefit from computational efficiency improvements.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents an interesting application of machine learning techniques to a symbolic computation problem, but falls short of the rigorous validation and significant contribution expected at NIPS. The approach is not sufficiently novel compared to existing symbolic computation methods, and the experimental evaluation is limited and lacks comparison to established baselines. The claimed "discovered" identities appear to be simple extensions of patterns found in simpler cases, rather than meaningful mathematical discoveries. The restrictive assumptions make the results difficult to generalize.

While the idea of using machine learning to guide search in combinatorial spaces is interesting, the paper fails to demonstrate that this approach provides significant advantages over existing symbolic computation techniques. Without proper comparison to established methods and evidence of broader applicability, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative.

I recommend rejection. The paper would need substantial revision to address these concerns, including: 1) comparison to established symbolic computation methods, 2) more rigorous theoretical justification for the approach, 3) demonstration of practical impact on real-world problems, and 4) broader evaluation beyond the narrowly defined problem domain. As it stands, the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate that machine learning is a meaningful approach to this problem, rather than simply a more complicated way to solve a problem that can be addressed with existing symbolic techniques.
