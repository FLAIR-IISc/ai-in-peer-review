Summary
This paper tackles the problem of discovering computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target symbolic expression. The authors model expressions with an attribute grammar (capturing both compositional structure and operation costs), and search over grammar trees to find low-complexity re-expressions. To scale beyond brute-force/random search, they learn strategies from solutions to simpler targets: (i) n-gram models over subtrees and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions and predicts the next grammar rule. Equivalence checking is performed via randomized evaluation over Z_p (mod a large prime), and complex targets are handled as linear combinations of trees by solving Fw ≡ t (mod p). Experiments on several families (matrix polynomials, elementwise-multiplied variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related exponential sums) show substantial complexity reductions (e.g., O(n^3)→O(n^2); polynomial-time forms for targets with naive exponential cost for small k). The learned strategies dramatically expand the solvable regime versus random search, with n-grams often outperforming the RNN, and code/data are released.

Relation to prior work
The paper bridges symbolic computation, program induction, and ML for search guidance. Unlike CAS simplifiers (Maple/Mathematica), which apply fixed rules, the approach learns heuristics from data. It is adjacent to NLP work on RNNs over parse trees, but applied to symbolic math. It also relates to randomized polynomial identity testing (PIT) and to grammar-based search/optimization. I am not aware of prior ML work learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities in this way.

Strengths
- Bold and novel direction: learning to guide discovery of new, computationally efficient identities. This is an exciting bridge between ML and symbolic mathematics.
- Clear framework: attribute grammar with explicit operation costs, randomized equivalence checking, and linear combination solver.
- Learning improves reach: n-gram and RNN strategies trained on simpler cases reliably find more complex identities that random/brute force cannot.
- Continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions: the RNN achieves low classification error on equivalence classes, suggesting a reusable representation that could enable further ML tools in symbolic domains.
- Empirical evidence of impactful complexity reductions, including identities that are hard to find manually; open-source release supports reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Equivalence checking and correctness guarantees: The approach relies on randomized evaluation mod p. While practical, the paper lacks a formal bound on collision probability and guidance on selecting p and N relative to expression degree/size. Connecting to Schwartz–Zippel/DeMillo–Lipton PIT would strengthen correctness claims.
- Linear combinations mod p: Solving Fw ≡ t (mod p) may admit modular solutions that do not correspond to small-integer coefficients in the reals/integers. There is no discussion of coefficient domains, uniqueness, or rational reconstruction to lift modular solutions. A final symbolic verification step would allay concerns.
- Comparisons: No quantitative comparison against CAS simplification or classical algebraic techniques (e.g., common subexpression elimination, polynomial factorization) to contextualize the gains, even if those systems do not target complexity reduction explicitly.
- Scope and generality: Experiments largely restrict to homogeneous polynomials in a single variable and scalar outputs; while the authors discuss extensions, the practical scalability to richer grammars (recursion, transcendental functions, multi-variable interactions) is not evaluated.
- RNN vs n-gram: Despite the representational promise, n-grams often outperform the RNN on the hardest discovery tasks. More analysis on when/why the RNN helps, and stronger search integration (e.g., beam search, MCTS, or RL over the grammar with the RNN as a policy/value function), would be useful.
- RBM-2 limits: For the most challenging family, methods did not beat random beyond k=5. Understanding failure modes and providing insight into what’s missing (model capacity, training curriculum, search breadth) would be valuable.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Provide probabilistic bounds for the equivalence test (cite PIT) and specify how p and N are chosen to control error with respect to degree and number of variables. Consider adding a post hoc symbolic verification to confirm discovered identities.
- Clarify coefficient constraints in Fw ≡ t (mod p): are integer coefficients required? How large can they be? Consider rational reconstruction or Chinese Remainder Theorem to recover integer/rational coefficients, and report them explicitly.
- Add baselines: e.g., CAS simplification (with/without factoring), heuristic CSE, or a beam search with simple hand-designed heuristics, to quantify the value of learning-based strategies.
- Analyze generalization: how well do models trained up to degree k generalize to k+Δ without retraining? Provide learning curves, ablations (curriculum, dropout rates, identity initialization), and sensitivity to the grammar choice.
- Discuss integration with modern program synthesis/search (e.g., beam/MCTS, learned cost-to-go) and whether the RNN embedding can serve as a learned equivalence canonicalizer.
- Include explicit lists of grammar rules and operation cost models in the main text for reproducibility.

Quality: Generally sound and well-executed, with honest discussion of limitations. The main technical concerns lie in the rigor of identity verification and coefficient recovery, which are addressable.

Clarity: Clear and well-organized; the Matlab-style notation and examples help. Some key implementation details (grammar table, parameter choices for p and N) should be moved from supplemental to main text.

Originality: High. Learning to discover efficient mathematical identities and continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions are novel contributions.

Significance: High. The work opens a promising direction for ML-guided symbolic discovery and compiler-like optimization of algebraic code. Even partial success on exponential-sum families is notable.

Recommendation: Accept (strong novelty and potential impact outweigh the weaknesses). Confidence: moderate.
