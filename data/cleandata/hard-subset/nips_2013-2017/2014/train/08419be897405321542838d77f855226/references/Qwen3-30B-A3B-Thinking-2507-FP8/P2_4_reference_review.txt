

# Review: "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The authors propose representing mathematical expressions as grammar trees defined by attribute grammars, where each operation has an associated computational cost. They then use learning techniques (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for efficient equivalent expressions that reduce computational complexity. The approach is demonstrated on several families of homogeneous polynomial expressions, showing reductions from O(n³) to O(n²) complexity for some cases. The paper also introduces a continuous representation for symbolic expressions using recursive neural networks, enabling the application of machine learning techniques to symbolic domains.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with a well-structured methodology. The attribute grammar framework is appropriately designed for the problem domain, and the learning components are properly integrated into the search strategy. The authors provide clear experimental validation, demonstrating that their learning-based search strategies significantly outperform random search for complex expressions (Figure 3). The numerical representation using integer-based descriptors (Zp) effectively solves the problem of equivalence checking without symbolic manipulation.

The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately, noting that their approach struggles with the most complex family (RBM-2)^k, where no solutions were found beyond k=5. The experimental design is sound, with multiple runs at each degree and appropriate comparison to the random strategy. The authors also provide detailed ablation studies of different learning approaches (n-gram models of varying order and RNNs with different dropout rates).

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement is precise, and the key concepts (attribute grammars, numerical representation, learned representations) are well-explained with helpful examples (Examples 1 and 2). The relationship between the grammar rules and computational complexity is clearly articulated. The paper would benefit from more detailed description of the experimental hyperparameters, but the current level of detail is sufficient for reproducibility.

The figures effectively illustrate the grammar trees and the discovered identities, making the technical content accessible. The connection between the grammar framework and NLP techniques (n-grams, RNNs) is well-motivated and clearly explained.

## Originality

This work makes a significant original contribution by applying machine learning to guide the search for efficient mathematical identities. While attribute grammars have been used in other domains (e.g., compiler construction, bioinformatics), their application to discovering mathematical identities is novel. The paper properly situates itself within related work in theorem proving, program induction, and NLP, clearly differentiating their approach from previous efforts.

The most original contributions are:
1. Using learning to guide search for efficient mathematical identities rather than relying on hand-designed heuristics
2. The application of recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of symbolic expressions
3. The demonstration that learning from simpler expressions can help discover more complex ones

The paper avoids claiming novelty for the grammar concept itself, instead focusing on its novel application to this specific problem.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. The demonstrated complexity reductions (from O(n³) to O(n²) and from exponential to polynomial time) have concrete implications for computational efficiency in mathematics and machine learning. The connection to practical applications (e.g., the RBM partition function and dropout approximation) is particularly compelling, suggesting potential real-world impact.

The paper advances the state of the art by showing that machine learning can be effectively applied to symbolic domains, a field that has been largely untouched by statistical learning approaches. The framework could be integrated with compilers or optimization tools to automatically replace inefficient computations with efficient equivalents.

## Strengths

1. Novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation for discovering efficient mathematical identities
2. Clear demonstration of complexity improvements for specific expression families
3. Effective integration of NLP techniques (n-grams, RNNs) to a new domain
4. Well-structured experimental validation showing clear advantages over random search
5. The continuous representation of symbolic expressions via RNNs is a valuable contribution

## Weaknesses

1. The problem is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, which limits applicability to broader mathematical domains
2. The connection to practical machine learning applications (dropout, RBM partition function) remains somewhat theoretical without concrete implementation results
3. The approach fails for the most complex expression family (RBM-2)^k beyond k=5, which is a significant limitation
4. The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of computational complexity in the experimental section

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the application of machine learning to symbolic domains. The framework is well-designed, the experiments are sound, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over baseline approaches. While the problem scope is somewhat restricted, the approach represents a novel and promising direction for applying machine learning to mathematical discovery.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), presents a novel combination of techniques (Originality), and addresses a problem with significant potential impact (Significance). The limitations are acknowledged and appropriately discussed.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limited discussion of practical implementation details for the machine learning applications mentioned.

**Overall Rating: Strong Accept**
