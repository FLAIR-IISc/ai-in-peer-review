Summary
The paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally more efficient equivalents of given symbolic expressions. Expressions are generated by an attribute grammar whose rules are annotated with computational costs. Instead of brute-force enumeration, the authors learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets: (i) n-gram models over grammar subtrees and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions and, on top, a softmax policy to predict the next rule. Equivalence checking is done numerically by evaluating expressions over random inputs in a finite field Z_p to obtain descriptors; for complex targets, linear combinations of trees are solved via Gaussian elimination over Z_p. Experiments show the learned strategies find identities that reduce complexity (e.g., O(nm p) to O(n(m+p)); exponential sums for RBM-like expressions to O(n^2)–O(n^3) under the grammar), with n-grams generally outperforming the RNN in success rate and speed beyond small k. The authors claim many identities are novel; code is provided.

Relation to prior work
The work intersects theorem proving, program synthesis, superoptimization, and symbolic algebra. It differs from CAS systems (Maple/Mathematica) by optimizing for explicit computational complexity rather than algebraic simplification via hand-designed rewrite rules, and from prior learning in theorem proving by learning search directly from solved instances. The use of attribute grammars for optimization is known in other domains; applying them to identity discovery with learned guidance appears novel. The RNN-based embedding of symbolic expressions parallels NLP tree models and word embeddings, and is a plausible contribution to symbolic ML.

Strengths
- Addresses an important, difficult problem (search over combinatorial symbolic spaces) with a clear framework that integrates learning.
- Concrete, well-motivated grammar-based representation with cost attributes and a practical equivalence-checking mechanism (finite-field descriptors).
- Demonstrates nontrivial identities with substantial asymptotic speedups; examples illustrate patterns the learned search can exploit.
- Provides two complementary learned strategies; n-grams are simple yet effective; the RNN representation is an interesting contribution in its own right (high multi-class accuracy on equivalence-class prediction).
- Code and data are released, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Correctness guarantees are empirical. Equivalence is established via random evaluations modulo a prime; no explicit probabilistic bounds (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel) are provided, nor cross-checking with multiple primes/rational arithmetic. Solutions to linear systems mod p could be spurious over the integers; this should be addressed.
- Evaluation is primarily “fraction of runs that find a solution within 10 minutes.” There is little analysis of wall-clock run time beyond the cutoff, no ablation on descriptor size N or prime choice, and limited reporting of absolute speedups on actual numerical computations.
- The grammar is restrictive (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, often single variable) and sometimes intentionally excludes matrix-matrix multiply to bias search to O(n^2). This makes success more likely but also limits generality; it is unclear how often such constraints hide useful solutions outside the grammar.
- Claims of novelty of identities are plausible but not substantiated with proofs or citations; at minimum, a verification pipeline (e.g., symbolic proof or multi-prime checking with reconstruction) should be described.
- The learned RNN generally underperforms n-grams on more complex families; the added representational machinery’s practical impact on discovery is limited in the current setup.
- Some important implementation details are missing or only sketched: grammar table, distribution of random inputs, size of p and N, how linear combinations are mapped back to standard forms, and scheduler details (e.g., greedy vs beam, handling of branching/stopping).
- The connection to ML applications (e.g., dropout or RBM partition function) is suggestive but not experimentally demonstrated.

Quality
Technically sound overall with a coherent algorithmic pipeline. The finite-field descriptor approach is reasonable and efficient, but the lack of formal error bounds and verification across primes is a notable gap for a paper whose main claim is equality of symbolic expressions. Experimental methodology is appropriate for search problems, but could be strengthened with more rigorous comparisons (e.g., beam search baseline, different schedulers), time-to-solution curves, and ablations (effect of curriculum, dropout, representation size). The paper is a complete piece of work, though proofs and guarantees are limited.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized; the problem setting and methods are explained clearly. However, some necessary details are missing, forcing the reader to infer key choices. Including the complete grammar, numerical parameters (N, p), and more explicit algorithmic pseudocode for the scheduler would improve reproducibility. It would help to report more identities in the main text (beyond the two examples) with human-readable forms and complexity analyses.

Originality
The combination of attribute-grammar search with learned guidance for discovering efficient identities is novel in the ML community. The use of an RNN to learn continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions is not new per se, but its application here is interesting and adds value. The paper distinguishes itself from prior CAS and theorem proving work by aiming at computational efficiency via learned search.

Significance
Moderately high. If robust, the approach could influence program synthesis, compiler optimization, and symbolic ML. The empirical results show meaningful improvements in the tractable regime of the grammar. However, the scope restrictions and lack of formal guarantees temper the impact, as does the stronger performance of simpler n-grams over the RNN. Still, the idea of learning-guided symbolic search is timely and impactful.

Suggestions and questions
- Provide probabilistic guarantees for finite-field equivalence testing (e.g., bound failure probability via Schwartz–Zippel) and validate with multiple primes; describe how to lift mod-p solutions to integer coefficients.
- Report absolute runtime speedups for evaluated identities on actual matrices/vectors, not only asymptotic complexity.
- Include the full grammar and more implementation details (N, p, distributions, scheduler, beam width).
- Compare greedy scheduling with beam search or MCTS; include an ablation for curriculum learning.
- Expand experiments on multi-variable targets and non-homogeneous polynomials to assess generality.
- Analyze why n-grams outperform the RNN and whether hybrid strategies help.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated problem; practical framework with learning-guided search.
- Demonstrates nontrivial new identities and asymptotic savings.
- Code release; interesting RNN embedding of symbolic expressions.

Cons
- No formal correctness guarantees; potential for false positives mod p.
- Evaluation limited in rigor; restricted grammar and biased rule sets.
- RNN’s contribution to discovery is weaker than simpler n-grams.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents a creative and promising direction—learning to guide symbolic search for efficiency—with convincing case studies and open-source code. Strengthening the verification story and broadening the empirical evaluation would make it a clear accept.
