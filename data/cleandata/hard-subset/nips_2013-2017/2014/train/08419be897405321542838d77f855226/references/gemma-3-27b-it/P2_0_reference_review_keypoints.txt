- Overall: Novel, technically sound work; reviewer recommends strong accept with minor revisions.  
- Goal: Automate discovery of computationally efficient equivalent mathematical expressions.  
- Method: Uses attribute grammars combined with learning (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide search.  
- Scope: Focused on homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with one or two variables to keep search tractable.  
- Positioning: Distinguished from rule-based symbolic engines (Mathematica/Maple) by learning the search strategy.  
- Novelty: Combining grammars and ML, and applying RNNs to symbolic parse trees is a fresh contribution.  
- Technical soundness: Framework is well-defined; integer-based descriptors address floating-point issues.  
- Experiments: Ablation study compares random, n-gram, and RNN search strategies; results show meaningful complexity reductions (e.g., O(n^3)→O(n^2)).  
- Demonstrations: Claims discovery of previously unknown identities with convincing example cases.  
- Clarity and organization: Well-written and logically organized; technical details mostly accessible.  
- Reproducibility: Code and evaluation data are publicly released.  
- Main limitation: Strong restrictions on problem class (fixed-degree homogeneous polynomials, few variables) limit general applicability.  
- RNN concerns: RNNs do not consistently outperform n-gram models, especially on simpler families; evidence for RNN advantages is limited.  
- Experimental detail lacking: Insufficient reporting on hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity for learning models.  
- Explainability gap: Paper shows identities are found but offers limited intuition for why models discover specific identities.  
- Evaluation summary: Quality strong, clarity good, originality strong, significance good but constrained by scope.  
- Acceptance arguments: For — novel approach, discovered identities, solid experiments, code release; Against — limited scope and uneven RNN gains.  
- Suggested revisions: Broaden limitations discussion, deeper RNN/hyperparameter analysis, provide intuition for discovered identities, and clarify grammar rules (e.g., visual aids).
