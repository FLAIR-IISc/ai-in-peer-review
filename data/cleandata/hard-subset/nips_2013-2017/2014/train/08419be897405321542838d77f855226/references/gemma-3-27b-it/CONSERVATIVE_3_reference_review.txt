This paper proposes a framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars and machine learning. The core idea is to learn a search strategy within the grammar to navigate the exponential space of possible expression rewrites, aiming for computationally cheaper equivalents of a target expression. While the problem itself is interesting, and the application of machine learning to symbolic manipulation is a potentially valuable direction, I find the paper’s presentation and validation insufficient to warrant acceptance to NIPS.

The primary weakness lies in the limited and somewhat contrived experimental setup. The authors evaluate their approach on a set of polynomial expressions, acknowledging the restrictions this imposes. However, even within this constrained domain, the scaling of the methods is not convincingly demonstrated. The experiments largely focus on demonstrating successful rewrites for small to medium-sized expressions. While achieving O(n^2) complexity from O(n^3) targets is a positive result (Example 1), the generalizability and real-world impact of such optimizations remains unclear. The choice of families – while mathematically well-defined – feels constructed to showcase the method’s capabilities rather than addressing truly challenging, practical problems. For instance, the RBM-related examples, despite their computational complexity, don’t directly translate to impactful improvements in machine learning practice *as presented*.

The comparison to existing symbolic computation systems (Mathematica, Maple) is superficial. The authors correctly point out that these systems don't *learn* search strategies, but they fail to adequately address whether their learned approach consistently outperforms or even matches the efficiency of highly optimized rule-based systems for the presented problem class. A direct, head-to-head comparison is missing.

Methodologically, the reliance on a fixed cut-off time (10 minutes) for the search introduces a bias. The reported success rates, while informative, are dependent on this arbitrary time limit. A more robust evaluation would involve measuring the computational cost of finding a solution (or failing to do so) as a function of expression complexity. The statement that the cut-off has “little effect” on the results lacks sufficient justification.

The machine learning components – n-grams and RNNs – are presented without a deep exploration of their respective strengths and weaknesses in this context. The RNN, while potentially powerful, is outperformed by simple n-gram models in some cases. This suggests that the representation learning is not yet sufficiently sophisticated to capture the essential structural properties of the expressions. The choice of hyperparameters (e.g., dropout rate) seems somewhat arbitrary and lacks thorough justification. The explanation of the RNN architecture feels hand-wavy in places, and the detailed description of its implementation is lacking.

The numerical representation of expressions, while a clever workaround for symbolic comparison, introduces potential numerical instability and the need for large prime numbers. While the authors address this, the overhead associated with this representation and its impact on performance is not fully discussed.

**Strengths:**

*   The idea of combining attribute grammars with machine learning to guide symbolic manipulation is novel and potentially promising.
*   The learned representation of symbolic expressions via the RNN is a potentially valuable contribution.
*   The authors provide code and data, facilitating reproducibility (though the complexity of the setup may still pose a barrier).

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited scope of evaluation and contrived problem families.
*   Lack of comparison to established symbolic computation systems.
*   Insufficient analysis of the scaling behavior and computational cost.
*   Limited exploration of the machine learning components.
*   Arbitrary cut-off time and its impact on results.
*   Superficial discussion of numerical stability and overhead.



**Arguments for Acceptance:** Novelty of combining grammars and learning; potentially useful learned representation.
**Arguments for Rejection:** Limited scope and impact; lack of comparison to existing systems; insufficient validation; contrived experimental setup.

**Overall:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the core idea has merit, the execution and validation are insufficient to meet the standards for NIPS. The paper feels more like a proof-of-concept than a substantial contribution to the field. Significant revisions, including a more thorough experimental evaluation, comparison to existing systems, and a deeper analysis of the machine learning components, would be necessary for reconsideration. The authors need to demonstrate that their approach offers a genuine advantage over existing techniques and addresses a meaningful problem in a scalable way.
