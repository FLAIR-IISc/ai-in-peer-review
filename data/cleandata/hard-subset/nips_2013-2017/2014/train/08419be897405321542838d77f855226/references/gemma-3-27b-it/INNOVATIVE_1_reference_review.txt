This paper presents a fascinating and ambitious approach to discovering mathematical identities using a grammar-based framework guided by machine learning. The core idea – leveraging learning to navigate a complex search space of symbolic expressions – is highly novel and, if successful, could have a significant impact on automated theorem proving, symbolic computation, and potentially even algorithm discovery. The reported results, demonstrating the recovery of more efficient expressions for certain computations, are impressive and suggest a promising direction.

The strength of the paper lies in its boldness. It tackles a notoriously difficult problem – symbolic manipulation – with a genuinely new technique. The combination of attribute grammars with learned search strategies, particularly the recursive neural network component for learning expression representations, is a significant contribution. The experimental results, while limited in scope, are compelling. Discovering identities manually that offer reductions in computational complexity, as claimed, is a strong indicator of success.  The availability of code and data is also commendable.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. While the experimental evaluation demonstrates the method's effectiveness on specific examples, it lacks a comprehensive benchmark against existing symbolic computation systems (Maple, Mathematica). A more rigorous comparison would strengthen the claims. The scope of the grammar is currently limited to homogeneous polynomials. While the authors acknowledge this, the potential for extending the grammar to handle more general expressions remains unclear. The effectiveness of the different learning strategies (n-grams vs. RNN) appears to vary depending on the complexity of the target expressions. The reasons for this are not fully explored.  The reliance on a fixed cutoff time for the search strategy feels somewhat arbitrary and could benefit from a more principled stopping criterion.  The discussion of the learned representations, while interesting, doesn't delve deeply into *why* the RNN is effective; a more detailed analysis of the learned features could provide valuable insights.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **High Novelty:** The approach is genuinely novel and represents a significant departure from traditional symbolic computation techniques.
*   **Potential Impact:** Successful automated discovery of mathematical identities has broad implications across multiple fields.
*   **Promising Results:**  The reported discoveries of computationally efficient expressions, even on a limited set of examples, are encouraging.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The framework, while complex, appears logically sound and well-implemented.
*   **Open Source:** The availability of code and data encourages reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Scope:** The current grammar restricts the applicability of the method to a specific class of expressions.
*   **Lack of Benchmarking:** The absence of a comprehensive comparison to existing symbolic computation systems weakens the claims.
*   **Incomplete Analysis:** The reasons behind the varying performance of different learning strategies are not fully understood.
*   **Somewhat Arbitrary Search Termination:** The fixed cutoff time feels ad-hoc.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the identified weaknesses, I strongly believe this paper warrants acceptance. The novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh the current limitations. The authors have taken a bold step towards addressing a challenging problem with a creative and technically sound approach.  Minor flaws in the experimental validation and analysis can be addressed in future work.  The fact that the system is able to discover previously unknown mathematical identities is a compelling demonstration of its potential.  I would categorize this as a "high-risk, high-reward" submission, and I am optimistic that it will stimulate further research in this exciting area.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
