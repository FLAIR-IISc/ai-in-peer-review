## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and ambitious approach to discovering mathematical identities by combining attribute grammars with machine learning. The core idea – leveraging learning to guide the search within a grammar-defined space of expressions – is strong and the results, while not universally successful, are impressive, especially the demonstration of discovering previously unknown identities. The paper is well-written and clearly explains a complex system. However, the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, and the performance of the learned strategies doesn’t consistently outperform simpler approaches across all problem families. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the challenging problem of automating the discovery of mathematical identities, specifically focusing on finding computationally efficient equivalent expressions. The authors frame this as a search problem within a space defined by an attribute grammar and employ machine learning (n-gram models and RNNs) to improve the efficiency of this search.  The key contribution lies in learning a search strategy based on successful solutions from simpler expressions, enabling the discovery of identities for more complex cases.

The related work section is thorough and accurately places the paper within the context of theorem proving, program induction, symbolic computation, and NLP. It effectively highlights the novelty of integrating learning into a grammar-based search for mathematical identities, differentiating the approach from existing rule-based symbolic computation engines and limited prior work in applying ML to similar problems (Bridge et al. [4]). The connections drawn to NLP techniques like n-gram models and RNNs for parsing and representation learning are also well-justified.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for symbolic identity discovery is a genuinely novel approach.  It bridges the gap between traditional symbolic manipulation and data-driven learning.
* **Impressive Results:**  The discovery of previously unknown identities with significant computational improvements (O(n3) to O(n2), exponential to polynomial time) is a substantial achievement. Examples 1 & 2 are compelling.
* **Sound Methodology:** The attribute grammar framework provides a well-defined search space and allows for controlled exploration of possible expressions. The use of numerical and learned representations to compare expressions is clever and addresses the practical challenges of symbolic comparison.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the framework, including the attribute grammar, learning strategies, and experimental setup.  The examples effectively illustrate the core concepts.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors provide code and evaluation data, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Experimental Scope:**  The experimental evaluation focuses on a relatively small number of expression families. While these families are well-chosen to demonstrate the potential of the approach, it’s unclear how well it generalizes to other types of mathematical expressions.
* **Inconsistent Learning Performance:** The RNN consistently underperforms compared to n-gram models in the experiments. The authors acknowledge this but don’t provide a thorough analysis of why. It would be valuable to explore potential reasons (e.g., limitations of the RNN architecture, insufficient training data, difficulty of learning long-range dependencies).
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The importance of dropout rate and the initial weight initialization in the RNN suggests sensitivity to hyperparameters.  A more systematic exploration of hyperparameter settings would strengthen the results.
* **Scalability Concerns:** While the learning approaches improve performance for more complex expressions, the exponential growth of the search space remains a fundamental challenge.  The cut-off time of 10 minutes could be limiting for very difficult targets, making it hard to definitively say that a solution doesn’t exist.  Discussion of the inherent limitations of the approach to scalability would be helpful.
* **Grammar Limitations:** The current grammar, while well-defined, is limited to homogeneous polynomials. While the authors acknowledge this, exploring potential extensions to handle a broader class of expressions would be valuable for future work.



**4.  Originality:**

The work is highly original in its combination of techniques. While attribute grammars and machine learning are individually well-established, their integration for this specific problem is novel. The application of recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of symbolic expressions, inspired by NLP techniques, is also a significant contribution.  The discovered identities themselves are novel, as far as the authors are aware.

**5. Significance:**

The potential impact of this work is considerable. Automating the discovery of mathematical identities could have applications in a wide range of fields, including scientific computing, optimization, and even computer algebra systems.  The demonstrated ability to find more efficient algorithms for common operations could lead to significant performance improvements in many applications.  The learned representation of symbolic expressions could also facilitate the application of machine learning techniques to other symbolic reasoning tasks.

**Arguments Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel and ambitious approach with potentially significant impact.
* Demonstrates the discovery of previously unknown identities.
* Sound methodology and clear presentation.
* Code and data available for reproducibility.

**Con:**

* Limited experimental scope.
* RNN performance consistently inferior to n-gram models.
* Scalability remains a significant challenge.




**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I would encourage the authors to:

*   Expand the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of expression families.
*   Provide a more in-depth analysis of why the RNN underperforms compared to n-gram models.
*   Discuss the limitations of the approach regarding scalability and potential strategies for addressing them.
*   Briefly address the potential for extending the grammar to handle a wider class of expressions.
