

# Peer Review: "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper presents an interesting approach to discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques applied to attribute grammars. The authors propose a framework that uses grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions and learns search strategies to efficiently explore the combinatorial space of possible expressions, with the goal of finding versions with lower computational complexity.

## Summary and Context

The paper tackles a well-defined problem in symbolic computation: finding equivalent expressions with lower computational complexity. While related to theorem proving and program induction, the authors appropriately restrict their focus to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, avoiding undecidability issues. They make a novel contribution by applying machine learning (n-grams and RNNs) to guide the search within a grammar-based representation, rather than using hand-designed heuristics. This is a thoughtful extension of NLP techniques (n-grams, RNNs) to a new domain.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Application of Learning Techniques:** The core contribution—applying n-gram models and RNNs to learn search strategies for symbolic computation—is genuinely novel. The learned representation of symbolic expressions (Section 4.2) is particularly impressive, with the RNN achieving ≤5% error rate on classification tasks.

2. **Concrete Results:** The paper demonstrates significant complexity reductions (e.g., O(n³) → O(n²)) for specific polynomial families, with examples that are genuinely non-trivial and likely undiscovered manually. The examples (especially Example 2) clearly illustrate the approach's value.

3. **Technical Rigor:** The numerical representation using integer modulo p is clever and solves a critical practical problem (avoiding floating-point errors). The curriculum learning approach for RNN training shows careful attention to practical implementation details.

4. **Clear Problem Formulation:** The restricted setting (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix/vector) is well-justified and makes the problem tractable while still being meaningful for many applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope and Generalization:** The restriction to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree significantly limits applicability. The paper acknowledges this in Section 8 but doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how to extend to more general mathematical expressions (e.g., with trigonometric functions or recursion). The failure on the (RBM-2)^k family (Figure 3) highlights this limitation.

2. **Lack of Comprehensive Comparison:** The paper compares different search strategies (random, n-gram, RNN) but doesn't benchmark against alternative approaches to symbolic computation (e.g., symbolic algebra systems like Mathematica). The "success" metric (finding any solution within 10 minutes) is somewhat arbitrary and doesn't quantify how much better the discovered identities are compared to alternatives.

3. **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** The paper lacks formal analysis of why the learning approaches work or their theoretical guarantees. The observation that n-grams outperform RNNs for repetitive patterns (Example 2) is interesting but not analyzed.

4. **Implementation Details:** The linear combination approach (Section 5) is somewhat opaque. It's unclear how many trees are typically needed to form a solution, and the paper doesn't address potential numerical issues in the linear system beyond the modulo p representation.

## Overall Assessment

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound and well-executed, with good experimental results. However, the paper would benefit from more rigorous analysis of the limitations and a more comprehensive comparison with alternative approaches.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, with clear examples. Some technical details (e.g., the linear combination approach) could be clarified.

**Originality:** The core idea of using machine learning to guide search in symbolic computation is novel. The specific application to discovering efficient mathematical identities is a significant contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation.

**Significance:** The discovered identities could have practical applications in machine learning (e.g., for efficient computation of dropout or RBM partition functions). However, the restricted scope limits broader significance. The framework's potential for integration with compilers is promising but not demonstrated.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The core idea of using learning to guide search in symbolic spaces is novel and promising. However, the restricted scope and lack of comprehensive comparison with alternative approaches prevent it from being a major breakthrough.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1) More thorough discussion of limitations and how to extend the approach
2) Comparison with alternative symbolic computation methods
3) Analysis of why certain approaches work better than others

While the current results are impressive within the restricted setting, the broader significance would be enhanced by demonstrating applicability to more general mathematical expressions.

**Decision: Accept** (with minor revisions to address the limitations noted above)

The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear results that advance the state of the art in applying machine learning to symbolic computation. The restricted scope is justified and makes the problem tractable, and the discovered identities are genuinely interesting. The paper's main limitation is its narrow scope, which is acknowledged by the authors but not sufficiently addressed in the evaluation. With minor revisions to clarify the limitations and potential extensions, this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.
