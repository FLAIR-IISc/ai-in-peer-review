- Proposes a framework to discover computationally efficient algebraic identities using an attribute grammar with rule costs and finite-field numerical descriptors for equality testing.
- Generates expressions with an attribute grammar and checks exact equality via evaluations modulo a prime to avoid floating-point errors.
- Uses learned search strategies trained on simpler instances: subtree n-gram models and an RNN embedding with a softmax policy for rule choice.
- Handles targets that are linear combinations of trees by solving a modular linear system over descriptors.
- Experiments on several polynomial families show learned strategies greatly outperform random search; n-grams often beat the RNN.
- Authors claim discovery of many nontrivial and possibly new identities (e.g., reducing O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)) and O(n^3) to O(n^2)).
- Situates work at intersection of program synthesis, symbolic reasoning, probabilistic programming, and NLP-style tree embeddings.
- Distinguishes itself from CAS (Maple/Mathematica) by explicitly optimizing computational complexity rather than purely algebraic simplification.
- Missing relevant prior work citations, notably SPIRAL (fast transform derivation) and Schwartz–Zippel (polynomial identity testing).
- Strength: ambitious and original goal bridging ML and symbolic computation.
- Strength: clear attribute-grammar formulation allowing control over operations and costs.
- Strength: practical finite-field equality test that integrates well with learning.
- Strength: two complementary learned strategies; RNN embedding shows high classification accuracy of equivalence classes.
- Strength: empirical evidence that learning expands tractable search space and code/data are released.
- Weakness: narrow scope—focused on homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, usually single variable and scalar output.
- Weakness: weak evaluation methodology and baselines (no CAS, SPIRAL, or domain-specific heuristics), and removing matrix–matrix multiply biases results.
- Weakness: RNN often underperforms n-grams; unclear benefit of learned embeddings over simple frequency models.
- Weakness: no formal correctness guarantees or analysis of collision probability for modular tests; no proof certificates for identities.
- Weakness: coarse or insufficiently rigorous complexity claims and lack of benchmarking on real problem sizes.
- Weakness: no cross-family generalization experiments to show transfer beyond training instances.
- Quality: technically sound core components but experimental setup needs stronger baselines, metrics, and implementation detail disclosure.
- Clarity: generally well written; RNN section clear, but full grammar, scheduler pseudocode, and hyperparameters should be included.
- Originality: high—novel combination of learned policies, embeddings, attribute grammars, and finite-field descriptors.
- Significance: potentially significant for compilers, autodiff, and probabilistic modeling, but practical impact limited by scope and lack of formal verification.
- Questions: request Schwartz–Zippel bounds for false-positive probability and choice of p and N.
- Questions: ask whether proof certificates or CAS/SMT verification are produced post-hoc.
- Questions: how often “no matmul” results rely on unnatural constraints and how results compare when matmul is allowed.
- Questions: can the RNN policy transfer across families or capture algebraic structure beyond n-grams?
- Questions: comparisons requested against CAS cost-aware simplification and systems like SPIRAL.
- Suggestions: add theoretical guarantees for equality tests and report p, N, and collision probabilities.
- Suggestions: include stronger baselines, time-to-solution distributions, and operation-count benchmarks against CAS.
- Suggestions: provide formal proofs/certificates for key identities and analyze exact operation counts.
- Suggestions: explore cross-family generalization and hybrid search strategies (e.g., n-gram + RNN, MCTS).
- Pros: novel problem formulation, demonstrated scalability with learning, interesting RNN embedding, public code/data.
- Cons: narrow scope, weak baselines and verification, n-grams outperform RNN casting doubt on embedding benefits, claimed novel identities lack formal verification.
- Recommendation: weak accept—promising and original but needs stronger empirical comparisons and formal correctness guarantees.
