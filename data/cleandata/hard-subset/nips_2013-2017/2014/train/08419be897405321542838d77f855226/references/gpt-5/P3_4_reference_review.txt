Summary
The paper proposes a framework to discover computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given symbolic target expression. Expressions are generated by an attribute grammar; equivalence to the target is checked numerically using randomized evaluation over a finite field Zp. For complex targets, brute-force enumeration is intractable, so the authors learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets: (i) n-gram models over subtrees, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees and a softmax “policy” predicting the next grammar rule. Since many targets require linear combinations of subexpressions, the method solves a linear system over Zp to identify combinations of generated trees matching the target. Experiments on several expression families (matrix identities, symmetric polynomials, and exponentially large RBM sums) show that learning-guided search substantially outperforms random search in many cases; n-grams often outperform the RNN. The paper also reports an RNN classification accuracy ≤5% error on equivalence classes of expressions up to degree 6. The authors claim discovery of many new identities and release code.

Strengths
- Interesting and timely problem: ML-guided symbolic synthesis/identity discovery is underexplored and relevant to ML systems, compilers, and automated reasoning.
- Clear framing with attribute grammars and a practical pipeline: randomized equivalence testing over Zp, linear combination solver, learned policies.
- The RNN-based embedding of symbolic expressions is novel and potentially reusable beyond this paper.
- Empirical evidence that training on simpler instances improves search on harder ones; n-grams and RNN reduce search time and increase success rate relative to random.
- Reproducibility: code available; implementation details provided (curriculum, ReLU, identity initialization, dropout).

Weaknesses and questions
- Soundness/equivalence checking: The paper relies on randomized testing over Zp but does not quantify the probability of false positives/negatives (e.g., via Schwartz–Zippel-type bounds) or specify how p and N are chosen relative to degree and expression size. Moreover, solving Fw = t mod p admits coefficients in Zp; how are solutions lifted to integer/rational coefficients to certify identities over reals/integers? Are coefficients constrained (e.g., to {0,1} or small integers) or cross-validated across multiple primes?
- Linear combinations: It is not clear how the computed weights map to syntactically valid expressions within the grammar (e.g., availability of addition and scaling operators, and whether coefficients outside {−1,0,1} are permitted). Without constraints, modular solutions may not correspond to valid real-valued identities.
- Evaluation design: Success is defined as any non-zero count over 10 random runs within a 10-minute cutoff. This masks variability and does not compare against strong symbolic baselines (Mathematica/Maple simplification, known symmetric polynomial recurrences, or problem-specific derivations). It would help to report time-to-first-solution distributions, number of trees explored, and memory usage.
- Generality and scope: The setting is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, scalar outputs, and mostly a single variable. For some families the matrix-matrix multiply rule is removed to force O(n^2) solutions; this limits expressivity and may bias results. It is not shown how the approach scales with larger grammars or non-polynomial operators.
- RNN utility: Although the learned embedding achieves low classification error, the n-gram strategy often outperforms the RNN in discovery. The paper does not analyze why, nor whether alternative architectures (e.g., tree-LSTMs) or better training objectives (policy/value learning, reinforcement learning) would help.
- Claims about applications: The discussion suggests potential closed-form dropout or efficient RBM partition estimation, but experiments do not substantiate these claims. For RBM-2, no solutions were found for k>5, and learning did not beat random.

Quality
- The methodology is mostly sound and well-motivated, but the lack of formal guarantees for the randomized equivalence check and the lifting of modular linear solutions is a concern. Empirical evaluation demonstrates benefits of learning over random search on several families, but comparisons to established algebraic tools are missing, and reporting could be more rigorous (variance, ablations, search statistics).

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. However, the paper relies on figures/tables (grammar rules, example trees) not shown here; ensure all necessary details are in the main text. Clarify the linear combination procedure (constraints on coefficients, mapping to grammar operations) and provide a brief analysis of collision probabilities in Zp-based descriptors.

Originality
- Combining attribute grammars with learned search policies for identity discovery is novel. The learned continuous embedding for expression equivalence is also original in this domain. Related work is cited appropriately, though comparisons to symbolic algebra systems and program synthesis heuristics could be strengthened.

Significance
- The work opens a promising direction for ML-guided symbolic search and may influence compilers and automated theorem proving. However, current empirical scope and the reliance on restricted grammars limit immediate impact. Strengthening soundness guarantees and showing clear wins over symbolic baselines would increase significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide probabilistic guarantees on equivalence testing (choice of p, N) and describe a procedure to validate identities across multiple primes or rational arithmetic to eliminate spurious matches.
- Specify and constrain coefficients in the linear combination step and ensure they correspond to valid grammar operations; consider integral or sparse solutions (e.g., via ILP or L1 regularization) and verify over Q.
- Add baselines from Mathematica/Maple simplification and known identities (e.g., elementary symmetric polynomial recurrences), and report search statistics (nodes explored, time-to-solution).
- Explore stronger search strategies: beam/MCTS, reinforcement learning with success rewards, or value networks to estimate remaining distance-to-target; analyze why n-grams outperform the RNN.
- Demonstrate scalability with larger grammars and multi-variable targets; include at least one end-to-end application.

Recommendation: Weak accept
Pros:
- Novel and interesting problem formulation and methodology
- Clear empirical evidence that learning helps guide combinatorial search
- Reusable embedding idea; code released

Cons:
- Incomplete soundness/validation story for modular equivalence and linear combinations
- Limited baselines and evaluation rigor; ambitious application claims not supported
- RNN underperforms n-grams in key cases; analysis lacking

With clarifications on correctness guarantees and stronger comparisons, this could be a solid contribution.
