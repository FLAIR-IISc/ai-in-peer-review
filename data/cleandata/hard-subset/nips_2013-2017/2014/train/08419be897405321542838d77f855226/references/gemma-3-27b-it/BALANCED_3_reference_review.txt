This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities, leveraging attribute grammars and machine learning to navigate the search space. The core idea of representing symbolic expressions as grammar trees and employing learned strategies to explore them is compelling and tackles a challenging problem at the intersection of symbolic computation and machine learning. The authors demonstrate the ability to uncover identities offering significant computational improvements, some of which are reportedly previously unknown.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars with machine learning for symbolic identity discovery is a genuinely novel approach.  Existing methods in symbolic computation (e.g., Maple, Mathematica) rely on rule-based systems and lack the learning component.
*   **Significant Results:** The reported speedups (e.g., O(n<sup>3</sup>) to O(n<sup>2</sup>)) are substantial and demonstrate the potential impact of the approach.  The claim of discovering previously unknown identities is strong and, if verified, represents a significant contribution.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The use of attribute grammars provides a structured way to represent and manipulate symbolic expressions. The exploration of both n-gram and RNN-based search strategies is well-motivated. The numerical representation for checking equivalence, while potentially having limitations, is a pragmatic solution.
*   **Thorough Experiments:** The experiments cover multiple families of expressions, and the ablation study comparing different search strategies is insightful. The inclusion of a supplementary material detailing the discovered identities is commendable.
*   **Clear Writing & Code Release:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts. The release of code and evaluation data promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability Concerns:** While the framework works well for the examples presented, the scalability to very complex expressions remains unclear. The exponential growth of the search space, even with learning, is a fundamental limitation. The reliance on a fixed cut-off time in the experiments doesn't fully address this.
*   **Grammar Limitations:** The restricted setting (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix/vector variable) limits the generality of the approach.  While acknowledged by the authors, the extent to which the framework can be extended to handle more general expressions is not fully explored.
*   **RNN Performance:** The RNN-based approach doesn't consistently outperform the n-gram models, particularly for simpler expressions.  The paper doesn’t thoroughly investigate *why* the n-gram models perform better in certain cases or how the RNN architecture might be improved. The dropout rate selection feels somewhat arbitrary.
*   **Numerical Stability:** The reliance on modulo arithmetic to address numerical precision issues is a potential workaround rather than a fundamental solution. It's not fully clear how robust this approach is to different problem settings or numerical ranges.
*   **Limited Comparison to Existing Techniques:**  While the authors acknowledge symbolic computation engines, a more direct comparison (even if limited by the scope of the framework) to their performance on the tested expressions would strengthen the evaluation. For example, timing how long Mathematica takes to simplify the presented examples.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a challenging problem.
*   The results demonstrate the potential for significant computational improvements.
*   The methodology is sound and well-motivated.
*   The code release and clear writing enhance the impact of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Scalability remains a significant concern.
*   The RNN-based approach doesn’t consistently outperform simpler baselines.
*   The restricted scope of the framework limits its immediate applicability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The core idea is innovative, the results are promising, and the presentation is clear. However, the scalability concerns and the limitations of the RNN approach prevent me from recommending unconditional acceptance.  The paper addresses a difficult problem and offers a valuable contribution to the field.

**Recommendation:**

**Weak Reject.**  I recommend rejection in its current form, but strongly encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically:

*   **Scalability Analysis:**  Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the approach and discuss potential strategies for mitigating scalability issues (e.g., parallelization, more sophisticated search heuristics).
*   **RNN Improvement:**  Investigate why the RNN performs poorly compared to n-grams and explore alternative RNN architectures or training procedures. Perhaps pretraining on a larger dataset of mathematical expressions.
*   **Scope Extension Discussion:** Expand the discussion on extending the framework to handle more general expressions, outlining the challenges and potential solutions.
*   **Comparison to Existing Tools**: Provide a small comparison between the performance on these examples and that of existing symbolic computation software.

Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
