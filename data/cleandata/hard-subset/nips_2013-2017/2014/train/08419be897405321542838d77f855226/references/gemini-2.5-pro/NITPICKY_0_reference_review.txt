This paper presents a framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities for a given target symbolic expression. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is equivalent to the target but has lower computational complexity. As exhaustive search is intractable, the authors propose using machine learning to guide the search. They train models (n-grams and a recursive neural network) on solutions for simpler expressions to learn a search strategy for more complex targets. The authors demonstrate the discovery of efficient identities for several families of polynomial expressions, some of which are claimed to be novel.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses an interesting and challenging problem: applying machine learning to symbolic mathematics, a domain where such techniques are not commonplace. The goal of automatically finding more efficient algorithms for computation is highly significant.
*   The overall framework, combining attribute grammars with a learned search strategy, is conceptually elegant. The idea of using solutions to simpler problems to bootstrap the discovery of more complex ones (a form of curriculum learning) is sound.
*   The application of a recursive neural network to learn a continuous embedding for mathematical expressions is a novel contribution in this specific domain and could have broader applications, analogous to word embeddings in NLP. The reported classification accuracy for these embeddings suggests they are effective.
*   The discovery of polynomial-time equivalents for expressions that are naively exponential to compute (e.g., for the RBM-related families) is an impressive result, assuming the identities are indeed novel and correct.

**Weaknesses and Detailed Comments:**

Despite the novel ideas, the paper is undermined by a significant number of methodological shortcuts, a lack of technical precision, and numerous formatting and typographical errors. My recommendation is ultimately for rejection, as these issues collectively compromise the quality and rigor of the work.

**1. Quality and Technical Soundness:**

*   **Constrained Search Space:** A major methodological concern is the admission in Section 7.2 that for two of the five expression families, the matrix-matrix multiply rule was removed from the grammar. This ensures that any discovered solution is *a priori* more efficient (at most O(n^2)) than the O(n^3) naive evaluation. This approach does not "discover" an efficient identity from the full space of possibilities; rather, it finds an identity within a pre-selected, efficient subspace. This significantly weakens the central claim of the paper and should have been highlighted as a major limitation much earlier.
*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** The motivating example (Example 1) involves two matrices, A and B. However, the paper then states that "the rest of the paper considers targets based on a single variable." This is a bait-and-switch. The generalization from one to multiple variables is non-trivial, and failing to evaluate this more general case limits the demonstrated power of the framework.
*   **Ambiguous Technical Details:** The n-gram model uses a "weighted linear combination of the score from all depths up to n," with a weighting of 10^k. This weighting factor is presented without any justification, empirical or theoretical. Similarly, the tensor operation in the RNN (`(W3 * a) * a`) uses overloaded `*` notation without a clear definition, hindering reproducibility. The list of "important details" for RNN training suggests the process may be brittle.
*   **Contradictory Results:** In Section 7.2, the authors state for the (RBM-2)k family that "none of our approaches performed better than the random strategy". In the very next sentence, they claim the RNN found the k=5 solution "consistently faster" than random search. "Better" is not well-defined here, and the statements appear contradictory.

**2. Clarity and Presentation:**

The paper suffers from a pervasive lack of attention to detail.
*   **Formatting Inconsistencies:** There are numerous formatting errors that detract from the paper's professionalism.
    *   Big-O notation is inconsistently spaced, e.g., `O ( n3 )` vs. the standard `O(n^3)`.
    *   Footnote markers are frequently detached from the word they refer to (e.g., `l = 30‡`, `10 minutes§`). The asterisk for the footnote on homogeneous polynomials in Section 2 is missing from the main text.
    *   The bibliography contains systematic errors, with many entries ending in stray commas (e.g., `ICML,, ,`).
*   **Typos:** There are several typos. Section 3 contains `O(()n3)`. The formula for k=6 in Example 2 appears to have mismatched parentheses (13 opening, 12 closing). The description of the `(sum AAT)k` family in Section 7.2 contains a stray letter 'b' in the exponent `bk/2c`, which is presumably a typesetting error for a floor function.
*   **Notational Sloppiness:** The expression `Fw = t mod Zp` in Section 5 should likely be `mod p`.

**3. Originality and Significance:**

*   While the high-level idea is significant, the claim of discovering "new mathematical identities" is not rigorously substantiated. For well-studied objects like symmetric polynomials (`Symk`), it is unlikely these efficient forms are truly novel. The authors should provide more context or proof of novelty.
*   The work on learning embeddings for symbolic expressions builds directly on prior work (Socher et al., Bowman et al.), with the novelty being mainly in the application domain.

**4. Anonymity:**

The paper violates the double-blind review process. It includes a public GitHub link in the introduction and a self-citation [29] that points to an arXiv preprint of the same paper, explicitly listing the authors' names. This is a serious breach of conference policy.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel and important problem at the intersection of ML and symbolic computation.
*   The core idea of learning a search strategy over a grammar is promising.
*   The demonstration of learning continuous representations for symbolic expressions is valuable.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Major methodological flaw in constraining the search space to find "efficient" solutions.
*   Serious breach of the double-blind review policy.
*   Experimental validation is limited to a simplified single-variable case, despite a multi-variable motivating example.
*   The paper is rife with minor formatting errors, typos, and imprecise technical descriptions, indicating a lack of care in its preparation.
*   Claims of novelty for the discovered identities are not sufficiently supported.

In conclusion, while the paper contains the seeds of a very interesting research direction, the execution is flawed. The combination of a major methodological shortcut, a breach of anonymity, and a general lack of rigor and polish in the manuscript leads me to recommend rejection. The authors are encouraged to address these significant issues and resubmit their work to a future conference.
