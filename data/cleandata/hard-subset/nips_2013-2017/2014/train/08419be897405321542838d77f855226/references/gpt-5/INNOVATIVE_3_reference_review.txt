Summary
This paper proposes a learning-guided framework to discover algebraically equivalent but computationally cheaper identities for a given target expression. Expressions are generated by an attribute grammar over linear algebra and elementwise operators; equality is checked via integer-valued descriptors (evaluations over random inputs modulo a large prime). For complex targets, a single tree rarely suffices, so the method solves for linear combinations of tree outputs via modular linear algebra. To tame the combinatorial search, the authors learn strategies from solutions to simpler targets: (a) n-gram frequencies over subtrees and (b) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings for expressions and a softmax policy for the next rule. Experiments show dramatic complexity reductions (e.g., O(nmp) → O(n(m+p)) and O(n^3) → O(n^2)) and discovery of identities for several families, with learning-based strategies outperforming random search on larger degrees; interestingly, n-grams often outperform the RNN.

Relation to prior work
This sits at the intersection of theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming but restricts to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, avoiding undecidability. Unlike CAS simplifiers (Maple/Mathematica), the method explicitly optimizes computational cost and uses learned guidance rather than fixed rewrite heuristics. It relates to grammar-based synthesis/evolutionary programming and to superoptimization (e.g., stochastic search over program spaces), but targets symbolic algebra and complexity reduction. The RNN embedding of symbolic expressions is akin to compositional representations used in NLP.

Strengths
- Novel direction: framing identity discovery and complexity reduction as grammar search guided by learned policies is fresh and ambitious. Using continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions is particularly creative.
- Practical payoff: finds identities that reduce asymptotic complexity for nontrivial families, including cases that would be intractable by naive evaluation (e.g., exponential sums reduced to polynomial-time forms for small k).
- Methodological ingenuity: integer-modulo descriptors for robust equivalence checking are simple and effective; linear combination of trees is an elegant extension beyond single-tree matches.
- Clear incremental curriculum: learn from small k to tackle larger k; opens a path to curriculum-driven symbolic reasoning.
- Reproducibility: code and datasets are provided.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation limited/sparse baselines: Compared only to random and internal learned strategies. Missing comparisons to classical search (e.g., A*/MCTS with cost-aware heuristics), stochastic superoptimization, or CAS with cost models.
- Claims of novelty of identities and broad implications (e.g., “closed-form dropout” or efficient RBM partition estimates) are intriguing but under-validated. No formal proofs of correctness beyond high-probability modular checks; no empirical runtime benchmarks on actual hardware; no demonstration of end-to-end downstream gains.
- Scope restrictions: grammar is limited (homogeneous polynomials, mostly single variable; often removing matrix-multiply to force O(n^2)). Generality to richer operators, multiple variables, or recursion is discussed but not demonstrated.
- RNN underperformance: The learned RNN often trails n-grams on the hardest families and can overfit; limited analysis of why and how to improve (e.g., ablations on identity initialization, curriculum, dropout schedule).
- No guarantees: No bounds on collision probability in Zp setup; no completeness or optimality guarantees; stopping criteria and coverage relative to search space growth are not analyzed.

Quality
Technically sound as a heuristic search-and-learn framework; components are well motivated and integrated. The identity checks via Zp are reasonable but would benefit from using multiple primes and reporting collision probabilities. The experimental protocol (10-minute cutoff, success fractions) is transparent but thin for establishing significance against plausible baselines. Claims about discovering “previously undiscovered” identities need corroboration (e.g., citation checks or formal proofs provided in supplement).

Clarity
Generally well written and easy to follow. Figures/examples are helpful. Some missing details hinder reproduction: precise grammar (Table 1 not included here), primes and descriptor sizes N, policy/scheduler pseudocode, linear-system solver details, and hyperparameters. A clearer description of the search loop and stopping conditions would help.

Originality
High. Learning to guide symbolic identity discovery and the use of RNN embeddings for algebraic expressions are novel and potentially high-impact. The framing via attribute grammars plus linear combinations is an interesting synthesis.

Significance
Moderate to high. If extended to richer grammars/operators and strengthened evaluation, this line could catalyze ML-for-symbolic-optimization research and practical compiler/CAS integrations. Even with current limitations, the demonstration that learning can push beyond brute-force/random search in this domain is valuable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen equivalence checking: multiple primes, report collision bounds; optionally incorporate SMT/rational arithmetic checks for final candidates.
- Add baselines: MCTS/A*, cost-aware term rewriting, stochastic superoptimization; report wall-clock speedups on real matrices/vectors.
- Provide or sketch formal proofs for a subset of discovered identities; verify claims of novelty.
- Analyze why n-grams outperform the RNN; explore value functions or beam search; consider policy-gradient/MCTS hybrids.
- Expand to multi-variable grammars and richer operators; show one end-to-end application (e.g., replacing a costly kernel in a ML pipeline).

Pros
- Bold, original idea; compelling conceptual contribution.
- Demonstrated nontrivial complexity reductions; open-source code.
- Bridges continuous ML and discrete symbolic reasoning.

Cons
- Limited empirical rigor and baselines; some claims under-validated.
- Narrow grammar/scope; RNN does not clearly beat simpler models.
- No formal guarantees or proofs for reported identities in the main text.

Recommendation: Accept (novelty/vision outweigh current experimental limitations)
Confidence: 4/5
