- Proposes learning-guided search over grammar-based symbolic expressions to discover computationally efficient identities for homogeneous polynomials.  
- Novelty: applies n-gram models and RNNs to guide grammar-based search; RNN embedding yields ≤5% classification error.  
- Demonstrates concrete complexity reductions (e.g., O(n³) → O(n²)) with non-trivial, likely manually undiscovered examples (notably Example 2).  
- Technical rigor: uses integer modulo p representation to avoid floating-point errors and curriculum learning for RNN training.  
- Problem formulation is clear and well-justified by restricting to homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix/vector.  
- Limited scope/generalization: restriction to fixed-degree homogeneous polynomials reduces applicability and fails on families like (RBM-2)^k.  
- Lacks comprehensive comparison: no benchmarking against symbolic algebra systems (e.g., Mathematica) and uses an arbitrary success metric (solution within 10 minutes).  
- Limited theoretical analysis: no formal guarantees or explanation for why learning approaches work or why n-grams can outperform RNNs on repetitive patterns.  
- Opaque implementation details: the linear combination approach is unclear—uncertain typical number of trees required and potential numerical issues beyond modulo p.  
- Overall quality: technically sound with solid experiments, but would benefit from deeper analysis and broader comparisons.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized, though some technical aspects (e.g., linear combination) need clearer exposition.  
- Originality: novel application of machine learning to guide symbolic computation search; a meaningful contribution to the intersection of ML and symbolic computation.  
- Significance: discovered identities have practical ML relevance, but narrow scope limits wider impact; potential compiler integration not demonstrated.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address limitations, comparisons, and analytical explanations.  
- Suggested revisions: more discussion of limitations and extensions, comparisons with alternative symbolic methods, and analysis of why certain approaches perform better.
