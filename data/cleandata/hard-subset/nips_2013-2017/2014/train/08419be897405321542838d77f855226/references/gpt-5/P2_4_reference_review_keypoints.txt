- The paper searches for computationally cheaper algebraic identities equivalent to a target expression using an attribute grammar over linear/matrix ops with complexity annotations.  
- Exhaustive search is infeasible, so the authors learn search strategies from simpler targets using n-gram subtree statistics and a recursive neural network (RNN) embedding that predicts next grammar rules.  
- Equivalence checking is done numerically by evaluating expressions on random integer inputs modulo a large prime and solving Fw = t mod p to find linear combinations of trees.  
- Experiments on five problem families show learned strategies greatly improve finding low-cost formulas, sometimes reducing O(n^3) or exponential costs to O(n^2) or O(nm).  
- The RNN classifies expression equivalence with ≤5% error up to degree 6 and provides embeddings for guided search, but n-grams often outperform it for identity discovery.  
- The problem connects theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming but is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree and scalar outputs.  
- Using attribute grammars for optimization is consistent with prior AG-based work; applying them specifically to mathematical identity discovery appears novel.  
- The RNN over expression trees parallels tree-structured models from NLP; contrasts with CAS systems which optimize differently and don't target cost reduction.  
- Missing comparisons to GP/evolutionary program induction, MCTS, beam search, and other modern search baselines.  
- Strength: novel formulation of learning to discover cost-reducing algebraic identities within a grammar.  
- Strength: methodological combination of grammars, modular arithmetic checking, linear solving, and learned search strategies.  
- Strength: empirical evidence that learning enables discovery unreachable by random/brute force within time limits; code/data promised.  
- Strength: demonstration that local structural regularities are learnable (n-grams) and that neural embeddings can classify equivalence classes.  
- Strength: potential impact for compiler-level algebraic optimization and ML-symbolic computation crossover.  
- Weakness: weak correctness guarantees — equivalence validated only by single-prime random testing without formal proofs or CAS verification.  
- Weakness: narrow scope — single matrix variable, homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, and excluded operations; some results required hand-removing matrix-multiply.  
- Weakness: limited empirical rigor on practical utility and novelty of discovered identities, and minimal analysis of complexity optimality.  
- Weakness: thin baselines — no CAS with custom rules, GP, MCTS, or constraint-solver comparisons; limited ablation on scheduler/search hyperparameters.  
- Weakness: RNN underperforms simple n-grams in main task with limited analysis of when the RNN helps.  
- Weakness: informal cost model that ignores constants, memory, and precise dimensional/operator costs.  
- Quality: technically sound but needs stronger theoretical/empirical validation for equivalence checking and coefficient reconstruction.  
- Quality: linear system over Zp requires justification that mod-p coefficients map to true integer/rational identities.  
- Quality: more ablations and time-to-solution distributions for the scheduler/training are needed.  
- Clarity: generally well-written; grammar, representation, and scheduler explained, but grammar rules and complexity model should be explicit in main text.  
- Clarity: clarify coefficient domains for linear combinations and mapping from mod-p solutions to integer/rational coefficients.  
- Clarity: expand on RNN dataset construction, avoid leakage, and clarify train/test splits across equivalence classes.  
- Originality: high for problem framing and combining tools; applying RNNs to expression trees for identity discovery is novel.  
- Significance: moderate — promising but broader impact depends on stronger guarantees, scalability, and applicability beyond limited grammars.  
- Reproducibility: reasonable with released code/data, but missing search hyperparameters, seeds, and full identity lists hinder reproducibility.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel direction, clear empirical improvement with learning, useful technical ideas, and public code.  
- Cons for acceptance: insufficient correctness guarantees, narrow scope, sparse baselines, limited analysis of RNN vs n-grams, and unclear practical significance.  
- Questions for authors: have identities been verified over Q/R with multiple primes or CAS proofs and rational reconstruction?  
- Questions for authors: what are the actual coefficient domains (integers/rationals) and is there regularization to avoid spurious mod-p fits?  
- Questions for authors: can you provide ablations with beam search, MCTS, or GP baselines?  
- Questions for authors: any evidence of utility in real compiler/ML pipelines or real workloads?  
- Recommendation: weak reject — promising idea but lacks strong verification, broader baselines, and evidence of practical impact.
