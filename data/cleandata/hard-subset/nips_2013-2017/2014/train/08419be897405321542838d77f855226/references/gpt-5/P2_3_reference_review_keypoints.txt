- Proposes an attribute-grammar framework to discover computationally efficient identities for symbolic matrix/vector expressions.  
- Uses two key techniques: fast equivalence checking via random evaluation mod a large prime and learned search strategies (n-gram subtree models and an RNN embedding + policy).  
- Experiments show learned search finds lower-cost identities (often O(n^2) vs O(n^3) or polynomial vs exponential) where random search fails; code and data are released.  

- Soundness: leveraging polynomial identity testing mod p is standard, but the paper lacks quantification of false positive/negative probabilities and justification for mapping modular solutions back to real/integer coefficients.  
- Technical care: RNN training and search setup are well-documented, but complexity accounting is ad hoc and removing matrix-matrix multiply biases the search space.  
- Evaluation: success metric (fraction of runs finding solutions within 10 min) is reasonable, but comparisons to CAS/compilers and validation that identities are novel are missing; hardest family (RBM-2) remains unsolved for larger k.  

- Clarity: generally well-written with helpful examples, but missing the full grammar table, precise tensor shapes/mappings, a clear cost attribute definition, corrected linear-system dimensions, and a derivation for Example 1.  
- Originality: novel integration of attribute grammars, modular identity testing, and learned search policies; components are known but their combination for this task is new.  
- Significance: method has promising potential for algebraic optimization in ML/compilers with demonstrated gains, but scope is limited (homogeneous single-variable polynomials, scalar outputs) and scalability/representation gains are mixed.  

- Pro: clear, reproducible problem formulation and released code/data.  
- Pro: efficient equivalence checking and modular linear-combination solver.  
- Pro: learning-guided search significantly outperforms random; n-grams capture useful structure and RNN achieves low classifier error.  

- Con: probabilistic correctness of identity testing is not quantified and lacks robust post-verification.  
- Con: restricted grammar and removed costly rules may preclude valid efficient identities.  
- Con: weak baselines—no comparison to Mathematica/Maple, compiler passes, or known dynamic programs; claims of novelty are unverified.  
- Con: RNN often underperforms n-grams and lacks analysis of when learned representations help.  
- Con: minor clarity issues (linear system dimensions, mapping from mod p coefficients to actual integers).  

- Suggestion: include Schwartz–Zippel-based collision probability analysis, choice of p/N, and a post-verification step (multiple primes or symbolic checks).  
- Suggestion: report time-to-solution distributions and ablate scheduler/search strategies, cost models, and grammar choices (with/without matrix multiply).  
- Suggestion: compare to CAS tools and compiler algebraic optimizations on the same targets.  
- Suggestion: analyze RBM-2 failures and try stronger search methods (MCTS, beam search), reinforcement learning, or graph neural networks over expression DAGs.  
- Suggestion: fix linear-system dimensionality ambiguity and describe how modular coefficients are converted to integer/real coefficients.  

- Reproducibility: code/data provided and many training details listed; ensure the repository includes the full grammar table, dataset construction, and exact hyperparameters.  
- Overall: solid, novel integration with promising results but needs stronger theoretical guarantees, baselines, and clarity — overall recommendation: weak accept (Quality 6/10, Clarity 7/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 6/10).
