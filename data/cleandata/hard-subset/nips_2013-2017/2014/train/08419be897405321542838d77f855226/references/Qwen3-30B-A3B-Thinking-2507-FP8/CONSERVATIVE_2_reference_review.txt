

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning to guide a grammar-based search. The authors frame the problem as finding an expression S(V) = T(V) with lower computational complexity than a given target expression T(V), using attribute grammars to define valid expression structures and machine learning models (n-gram and RNN) to guide the search through the grammar tree space.

The paper's main contribution is a novel application of learning methods to guide the search for mathematical identities within a constrained grammar framework. The authors demonstrate this approach on several families of homogeneous polynomials, claiming to discover identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n^3) to O(n^2), and from exponential time to polynomial time.

**Quality and Methodological Soundness:** The paper demonstrates technical competence in implementing the attribute grammar framework and the RNN-based representation learning. The numerical representation using integer modulo arithmetic to avoid floating-point issues is well-justified. The experimental setup is reasonable for the constrained problem domain. However, the methodology has significant limitations: the problem is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with single matrix/vector inputs, which severely limits the scope of the approach. The paper fails to demonstrate that the machine learning component is necessary - the patterns discovered (e.g., repetitive structures in Example 2) could likely be captured with more straightforward pattern-matching techniques rather than learning. The paper also lacks sufficient comparison against existing symbolic computation tools like Mathematica or Maple, which already handle some expression simplification, though not explicitly for complexity reduction.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good explanations of the grammar framework and the RNN representation. The examples (especially Example 2) effectively illustrate the approach. However, the paper does not sufficiently explain why the learning approach is superior to simpler alternatives that might exist within the constrained problem domain.

**Originality:** The core idea of using grammars for symbolic search is not new (attribute grammars have been used in compiler construction and other domains since the 1960s). The application to mathematical identity discovery is somewhat novel, but the machine learning techniques used (n-grams and RNNs) are standard in NLP and applied here with minimal innovation. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate how their approach differs from or improves upon existing symbolic computation methods. The "discovered" identities appear to be specific to the constrained problem domain and do not represent fundamental mathematical breakthroughs.

**Significance:** The significance of the work is questionable. The problem addressed is highly specialized, and the claimed computational improvements (e.g., from O(n^3) to O(n^2)) are modest within the broader context of computational mathematics. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate practical impact - the identities discovered would likely have limited applicability outside the specific constrained cases examined. The paper claims potential applications to dropout and RBM partition functions, but provides no evidence that these would actually be useful in practice. The most significant contribution appears to be the RNN representation for symbolic expressions, but this is presented as a minor component rather than the main focus.

**Critical Weaknesses:** The paper's most serious weakness is its lack of rigorous validation. The authors claim to discover "previously undiscovered" identities, but provide no evidence that these identities are indeed novel (beyond the specific constrained cases they examine). The paper does not establish whether the discovered identities are truly more efficient in practice or simply mathematically equivalent with no computational advantage. The comparison against baseline search strategies (like random search) is limited to the specific grammar and problem constraints they define, without exploring whether simpler approaches might work just as well. The paper also fails to address whether the patterns they're learning could be captured through symbolic pattern recognition without machine learning.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper demonstrates technical competence and makes a minor contribution to the application of learning methods in symbolic computation, it lacks sufficient novelty, rigor, and significance for NIPS. The problem is too narrowly defined, the approach is not substantially different from existing techniques, and the claimed contributions are not convincingly validated. The paper presents an interesting engineering application of standard machine learning techniques to a constrained problem, but this does not constitute a significant scientific contribution to the field of machine learning.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**
- Pro: Well-structured paper with clear technical implementation; demonstrates a novel application of RNNs to symbolic representation
- Con: Problem domain is overly constrained; claims of novelty are unsubstantiated; lacks comparison against existing symbolic computation methods; machine learning component appears unnecessary for the problem; claimed computational improvements are modest and limited to specific cases; insufficient validation of claims

The paper would be more appropriate for a domain-specific conference (e.g., symbolic computation or programming languages) than for NIPS, where the focus should be on more general machine learning contributions with broader impact.
