## NIPS Review: Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and interesting approach to automatically discovering efficient mathematical identities. The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning to navigate the search space is compelling, and the demonstrated results – particularly the discovery of identities not previously known – are significant. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, and the RNN component underperforms the simpler n-gram approach, the core contribution is strong and warrants acceptance.

**Summary:** The authors tackle the problem of automatically finding computationally cheaper ways to evaluate mathematical expressions. They formulate this as a search problem within an attribute grammar, representing expressions as compositions of rules.  Exhaustive search is intractable, so they employ machine learning (n-grams and RNNs) to guide the search process, training on solutions to simpler instances. They demonstrate success in reducing the complexity of several expressions, including discovering previously unknown identities, and provide a codebase for reproducibility.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of machine learning to symbolic expression simplification is a genuinely novel idea. While related work exists in theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation, the integration with attribute grammars and the learning-guided search strategy are unique.
* **Significance:** The discovery of previously unknown mathematical identities, and the demonstrated reductions in computational complexity (O(n^3) to O(n^2), exponential to O(n^2/3)) are substantial results.  The potential application to optimization within compilers and machine learning libraries (as suggested by the authors) is promising.
* **Technical Soundness:** The framework, based on attribute grammars, is well-defined and appears sound. The numerical representation for matching expressions is a clever workaround for the challenges of symbolic comparison. The RNN architecture is standard and appropriately applied.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the core concepts effectively. The examples provided (especially Example 1 & 2) are helpful in understanding the approach. The inclusion of code and data further enhances reproducibility.
* **Representation Learning:**  The exploration of a learned, continuous representation of symbolic expressions via an RNN is an interesting contribution in itself, potentially opening doors for further application of machine learning techniques in this domain.

**Weaknesses:**

* **RNN Performance:** The RNN-based search strategy consistently underperforms the n-gram models, even with dropout. The paper doesn’t fully delve into *why* this is the case. While the authors mention overfitting, a more detailed analysis of the RNN’s learned representations and how they relate to the solution space would be valuable.  The relatively small dimensionality of the RNN embedding (l=30) might also be a contributing factor.
* **Limited Experimental Scope:** While the five families of expressions are well-chosen, the evaluation is somewhat limited in scope. Expanding the set of expressions, exploring different degrees *k*, and performing a more rigorous comparison of the n-gram and RNN strategies (e.g., with hyperparameter tuning) would strengthen the results.
* **Scaling:** The success seems heavily reliant on the ability to train the learning strategies on simpler expressions. The (RBM-2)k family demonstrates the limitations of this approach when the solution space becomes highly complex. It’s unclear how well this framework would scale to even more complex identities.
* **Grammar Limitations:** The restricted setting (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix) is acknowledged, but a more detailed discussion of the challenges involved in relaxing these restrictions would be helpful. The authors hint at difficulties with recursion, but a more concrete analysis would be beneficial.
* **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The success rates in Fig. 3 are presented without any error bars beyond the standard deviation of runtimes. Formal statistical tests to determine the significance of the improvements achieved by the learning strategies would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a challenging problem with potential for significant impact.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the framework in discovering previously unknown mathematical identities.
* The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning is a clever and well-executed idea.
* The codebase and data are readily available, facilitating reproducibility and future research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The RNN-based search strategy underperforms the simpler n-gram approach, raising questions about its effectiveness.
* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.
* The framework’s scalability to more complex identities remains uncertain.



**Overall Recommendation:** **Accept**.  

Despite the weaknesses, the strengths of this paper outweigh them. The novelty and significance of the work are compelling. The authors have identified an important problem and proposed a promising solution. Addressing the weaknesses (particularly a deeper analysis of the RNN performance and a more comprehensive evaluation) could further strengthen the paper, but it is already a valuable contribution to the field. I believe this work is well-suited for presentation at NIPS.
