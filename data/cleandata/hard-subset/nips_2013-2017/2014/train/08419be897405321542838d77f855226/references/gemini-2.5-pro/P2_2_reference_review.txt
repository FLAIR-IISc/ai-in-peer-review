### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities for a given target symbolic expression. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is equivalent to the target but has a lower computational complexity. Recognizing that a brute-force or random search of the combinatorially large grammar tree is intractable for complex expressions, the authors introduce a machine learning approach to guide the search. The system is trained on solutions found for simpler expressions to learn effective search strategies for more complex ones. The paper explores two main learning-based strategies: an n-gram model that captures common sub-expressions in successful solutions, and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions to predict the next best grammar rule to apply. The authors demonstrate their framework on several families of polynomial expressions, discovering new, efficient identities that reduce complexity from exponential or high-order polynomial time to low-order polynomial time.

### Review

This paper presents a highly original and ambitious approach to a challenging problem at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The proposed framework is elegant, and the results are impressive, demonstrating a successful application of machine learning to guide search in a vast, discrete, and symbolic space.

**Quality: High**
The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the problem within an attribute grammar framework is clear and appropriate. The two proposed methods for representing and verifying expressions are clever:
1.  The numerical representation using evaluation over a finite field (Zp) is a smart, practical solution to avoid the complexities of symbolic manipulation and the numerical instability of floating-point arithmetic.
2.  The learned representation using a recursive neural network is a significant contribution in its own right. Training an RNN to map symbolic expression trees to a continuous space where equivalence can be checked is a novel idea with potential applications beyond this specific task. The authors are careful to detail the training tricks required to make this work (identity initialization, curriculum learning, etc.), which adds to the work's credibility.

The experimental evaluation is solid. The authors test their method on five non-trivial families of expressions, including some inspired by problems in machine learning (RBM partition function). The results clearly demonstrate that the learning-based search strategies (n-gram and RNN) significantly outperform the random search baseline, especially as the complexity of the target expression increases. The authors are also honest about the limitations, noting when their methods failed to find solutions for the most challenging family (RBM-2 for k > 5).

**Clarity: Good**
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation with concrete examples that make the problem and the proposed solution easy to grasp. The core components of the system—the grammar, the expression representations, and the search strategies—are explained logically.

However, the clarity could be improved in a few areas:
*   **RNN Architecture Details:** While the general idea of the RNN is clear, some implementation details are sparse, which could hinder reproducibility. For instance, the handling of unary vs. binary operations in the softmax prediction layer (stating that for a single branch, `b2` is a "fixed random vector") could be elaborated upon. More details on the hyperparameters and training regimen would also be beneficial.
*   **Linear Combinations:** The mechanism for finding linear combinations of trees is a crucial part of the framework for complex targets. This is explained briefly in Section 5, but its practical impact and frequency of use in the experiments are not fully detailed.

**Originality: Excellent**
The work is highly original. While program synthesis and symbolic computation are established fields, the application of modern machine learning techniques—specifically n-gram models and deep recursive neural networks—to guide the discovery of efficient mathematical identities is novel. The idea of learning from solutions to simpler problems to solve more complex ones (a form of curriculum learning) is elegant and powerful. The development of a continuous embedding for mathematical expressions using an RNN is a standout contribution, drawing a compelling parallel to word embeddings in NLP but for a structured, symbolic domain. This work clearly differs from traditional computer algebra systems (like Mathematica or Maple), which rely on curated, rule-based simplification, not statistical learning.

**Significance: High**
The potential impact of this research is significant.
*   **Practical Applications:** The automatic discovery of efficient formulae could be integrated into compilers or scientific computing libraries to optimize code at a symbolic level, potentially yielding speedups beyond what traditional optimizers can achieve. The discovered identities for RBM-related expressions, while still for Taylor series terms, point towards a tantalizing possibility of finding polynomial-time computations for problems thought to be intractable.
*   **Research Direction:** This paper opens up a promising new research direction. It demonstrates that learning-guided search is a viable strategy for tackling combinatorial problems in symbolic domains. The learned representation for expressions could enable a wide range of ML techniques to be applied to problems in formal reasoning, theorem proving, and program synthesis. It effectively bridges the gap between statistical machine learning and symbolic AI.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novelty and Ambition:** The paper tackles a difficult, important, and novel problem with a creative and compelling approach.
2.  **Strong Technical Contributions:** The dual representation scheme (numerical and learned) and the learning-guided search framework are both well-conceived and executed. The RNN for symbolic expressions is a particularly valuable contribution.
3.  **Convincing Empirical Results:** The experiments show dramatic improvements over a random baseline and successfully discover non-trivial, previously unknown identities.
4.  **Clarity of Motivation:** The paper is well-motivated and easy to understand at a high level, making it accessible to a broad NIPS audience.

**Weaknesses/Areas for Improvement:**
1.  **Analysis of Model Performance:** The n-gram models often outperform the more sophisticated RNN model. The paper briefly hypothesizes this is due to the repetitive local structure of the solutions. This is a crucial point that warrants deeper analysis. A more thorough investigation into *why* and in what contexts the simpler model excels would strengthen the paper's conclusions.
2.  **Limited Scope of the Grammar:** The current grammar is restricted to a small set of polynomial operations. While sufficient for the chosen experiments, this limits the immediate applicability. A discussion of the challenges involved in extending the grammar (e.g., to include trigonometric functions, recursion, or conditional logic) would be welcome.
3.  **Reproducibility:** As mentioned under "Clarity," providing more explicit details about the RNN architecture and training would enhance the paper's value and allow other researchers to build upon this work more easily.

**Recommendation:**
This is an excellent paper that is a strong candidate for acceptance, potentially for an oral presentation. It is creative, technically deep, and opens up exciting avenues for future research. The weaknesses are minor and mostly relate to areas for future exploration or a need for slightly more detail, rather than fundamental flaws in the work.
