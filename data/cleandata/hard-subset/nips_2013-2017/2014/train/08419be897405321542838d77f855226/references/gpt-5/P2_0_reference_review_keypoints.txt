- Proposes a framework to discover computationally efficient equivalents of symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar with cost annotations, learned search strategies, and finite-field equivalence checking.  
- Learns search strategies via two methods: n-gram models over grammar subtrees and an RNN that produces continuous embeddings with a softmax next-rule policy.  
- Equivalence checking is done by evaluating expressions on random inputs modulo a prime p to get descriptors; linear combinations solved via Gaussian elimination over Z_p.  
- Experiments report discovery of nontrivial identities with substantial asymptotic speedups; many identities claimed novel and code is released.  
- Positions work at the intersection of theorem proving, program synthesis, superoptimization, and symbolic algebra, focusing on computational complexity rather than algebraic simplification.  
- Using attribute grammars with learned guidance for identity discovery appears novel in the ML community.  
- Strength: tackles a hard combinatorial search problem with a clear, integrated learning-based framework and practical equivalence-checking.  
- Strength: concrete grammar-based representation with cost attributes, complementary learned strategies, and reproducible code release.  
- Strength: demonstrates real asymptotic improvements and patterns the learned search exploits.  
- Weakness: correctness guarantees are empirical only; no formal probabilistic bounds (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel) or multi-prime/rational verification to rule out false positives.  
- Weakness: evaluation metric (fraction of runs finding a solution within 10 minutes) omits detailed wall-clock analysis, ablations on descriptor size/prime choice, and absolute runtime speedups on real computations.  
- Weakness: grammar is restrictive (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, often single variable) and sometimes excludes operations (e.g., matrix-matrix multiply), limiting generality and possibly biasing results.  
- Weakness: novelty claims for discovered identities are not substantiated with proofs, citations, or a verification pipeline to lift mod-p solutions to integer/rational forms.  
- Weakness: learned RNN underperforms simpler n-gram models on complex families, reducing practical impact of the RNN component.  
- Weakness: several implementation details are missing or sketched only (full grammar, distribution of random inputs, values of p and N, mapping linear combos back, scheduler specifics).  
- Weakness: suggested ML applications (dropout, RBM partition functions) are only suggestive and not experimentally demonstrated.  
- Quality: technically coherent and reasonable pipeline, but lacks formal error bounds and stronger experimental baselines/ablations (beam search, schedulers, curriculum effects).  
- Clarity: generally well written, but missing critical details and more human-readable identity examples in the main text to aid reproducibility.  
- Originality: novel combination of attribute-grammar search with learned guidance; RNN embedding application is interesting though not fundamentally new.  
- Significance: moderately high potential impact for synthesis and compiler optimization if robustness and verification are strengthened; current scope and guarantees temper immediate impact.  
- Suggestion: provide probabilistic guarantees (Schwartz–Zippel), validate with multiple primes and integer reconstruction, and describe lifting mod-p solutions to integer coefficients.  
- Suggestion: report absolute runtime speedups on actual matrices/vectors, include full grammar and parameter settings, and compare greedy scheduling with beam search or MCTS.  
- Suggestion: perform ablations on descriptor size, prime choice, curriculum learning, and representation size; expand experiments to multi-variable and non-homogeneous polynomials.  
- Pros: clear motivation, practical framework, discovered identities with asymptotic savings, open-source code, and an interesting RNN embedding study.  
- Cons: lacks formal correctness guarantees, limited experimental rigor and generality, and the RNN delivers weaker results than simple n-grams.  
- Overall recommendation: Borderline accept — promising and creative direction but needs stronger verification and broader empirical evaluation to be a clear accept.
