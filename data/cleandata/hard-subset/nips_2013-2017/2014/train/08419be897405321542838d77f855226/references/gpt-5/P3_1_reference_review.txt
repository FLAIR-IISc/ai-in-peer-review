Summary
The paper proposes a framework to automatically discover computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target symbolic expression. The search space is defined by an attribute grammar over linear algebraic primitives (e.g., matrix multiply, transpose, elementwise multiply, summation), with rule attributes encoding coarse computational complexity. To avoid exponential blow-up from brute-force enumeration, the authors learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets: (i) n-gram statistics over subtrees; and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous embedding of expression trees, first trained to classify expressions into equivalence classes (using randomized numerical equality testing modulo a large prime), then used with a softmax to predict the next grammar rule during search. More complex targets are often represented as linear combinations of trees, with coefficients solved by modular linear algebra. Experiments across several expression families (including sums over AAT, elementwise-squared variants, symmetric polynomials, and exponential sums arising in RBMs) show that learned strategies substantially outperform random search, that n-grams often outperform the RNN policy, and that the system can find identities lowering complexity from O(n3) to O(n2), and from exponential to polynomial for small k.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation: discovering equivalent, lower-cost expressions for a given target is both interesting and practically relevant (symbolic optimization, compilers, ML models).
- Innovative combination: attribute grammars + randomized identity testing + learned search policy is a compelling blend of ideas from PL, NLP, and learning.
- RNN representation of symbolic expressions is interesting in its own right; the classification results (≤5% error up to k=6) suggest nontrivial structure is being captured.
- Demonstrated wins: convincing examples include reducing sum(sum(A*B)) and higher-degree variants, and polynomial-time approximations to exponential sums for RBM-related families (for small k).
- Reproducibility: code link provided; training details (curriculum, init, activations, learning rates) are made explicit.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Soundness of equivalence checking: The approach relies on randomized evaluation over Zp. The paper claims a “unique descriptor,” but does not quantify collision probability nor cite standard bounds (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel). For matrices and noncommutative operations, formal guarantees and how many random instances are used per check should be stated, and multiple primes/CRTs or rational reconstruction could strengthen correctness.
- Linear combinations of trees: Solving Fw ≡ t (mod p) can yield modular coefficients not realizable in the grammar (e.g., non-integer, large magnitude, or negative coefficients). The paper should specify coefficient constraints (e.g., small integers, nonnegativity) and how modular solutions are lifted/validated over the reals. Otherwise, there is a risk of spurious modular identities.
- Cost model and optimality: Complexity is encoded at the rule level and controlled by disallowing certain rules (e.g., block matrix-multiply removed to force O(n2)). This ensures low asymptotic cost but conflates “what exists in the grammar” with true optimality. No analysis of minimality or competitive ratio is given, and constants/memory traffic are ignored.
- Evaluation design: Success is reported as “found within 10 minutes” with 10 random restarts. This conflates model quality with compute budget and randomness. More rigorous metrics (nodes expanded to solution, sample complexity, beam vs greedy, wall-time distributions) and ablations (e.g., beam search, MCTS, or A* over the same grammar) would strengthen the empirical claims.
- Generalization: Training uses solutions from smaller k within the same family. Cross-family generalization is not demonstrated. The RNN underperforms n-grams on the hardest families; the gap suggests the representation/policy learning is not yet competitive with very simple local statistics.
- Baselines: No comparison to strong symbolic baselines (e.g., equality saturation/e-graphs and rewrite systems; compiler strength reduction and CSE; Maple/Mathematica rewrite heuristics configured to reduce cost; tensor contraction order optimization). Even if these tools do not target the same cost model, a qualitative/quantitative comparison would situate the contribution.
- Claims of novelty of identities: The paper claims previously undiscovered identities without formal proofs or external validation. At minimum, verification with multiple primes and independent CAS checks should be reported; ideally, provide formal derivations for key discoveries.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, but several key components would benefit from formalization and tighter guarantees: probabilistic identity testing bounds; constraints and lifting for linear-combination coefficients; clearer separation of search heuristics from grammar-induced biases. Empirically, the methodology is plausible and often effective, but evaluation is limited and the strongest learned method is n-grams, not the RNN.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. Important implementation details (RNN init, curriculum, dropout) are specified. However, the grammar definition (Table 1) and coefficient constraints in Section 5 should be explicit. Search scheduler details (pure greedy? restarts? any beam width?) should be clarified. Complexity accounting should be made precise.

Originality
The combination of attribute grammars with learning-guided search for algebraic identity discovery appears novel. The learned continuous representation for symbolic expressions is an original application of RNNs. Related work on equality saturation and symbolic regression could be better discussed.

Significance
Potentially impactful for symbolic optimization and ML systems (e.g., fast evaluation of structured sums, compiler-like optimizations). As a first step, it shows that ML can guide discrete symbolic search. However, the current scope (scalar outputs, single variable, homogeneous polynomials) and evaluation limit immediate practical impact.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide collision probability bounds for the randomized equality test, and validate identities with multiple primes/instances; consider rational reconstruction to certify coefficients.
- Specify allowed coefficient set in linear combinations and how modular solutions are lifted and verified over reals.
- Compare against equality-saturation/e-graph-based systems and CAS heuristics targeted at cost reduction.
- Explore stronger search (beam/MCTS) and joint training of the RNN representation with the policy; consider RL or imitation learning from discovered proofs.
- Report cross-family generalization experiments.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and timely direction: learning to guide symbolic identity discovery.
- Interesting, reproducible methodology with clear wins on nontrivial cases.
- Bridges ML and PL/symbolic computation; may spur follow-up work.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited formal guarantees; potential for spurious identities via modular arithmetic.
- Restricted scope and evaluation; missing strong baselines.
- Main learned model (RNN) lags simple n-grams on hardest tasks.

Recommendation: Weak accept (if authors address verification and clarify coefficient handling), otherwise borderline.

Confidence: High (expert in symbolic ML and program synthesis).
