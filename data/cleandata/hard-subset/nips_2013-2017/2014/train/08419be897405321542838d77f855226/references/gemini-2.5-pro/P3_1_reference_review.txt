**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities for a given symbolic target expression. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is equivalent to the target but has a lower computational complexity. Recognizing that a brute-force or random search is intractable for complex expressions, the authors propose using machine learning to learn an effective search strategy. The training data for this learned strategy comes from solutions discovered for simpler expressions, embodying a curriculum learning approach. The paper explores two main learning methods: n-gram models over sub-trees of expressions and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions and uses it to predict the next grammar rule to apply. The authors demonstrate the framework's effectiveness by discovering new, non-trivial identities for several families of expressions, including some relevant to machine learning, that offer significant computational speedups (e.g., from O(n^3) to O(n^2), or exponential to polynomial time).

**Strengths**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a challenging and important problem at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The core idea of *learning to guide search* in the space of mathematical expressions is highly innovative. This work moves beyond traditional statistical pattern recognition and demonstrates a compelling application of modern machine learning to a structured, symbolic domain. The potential implications are significant, ranging from automated optimization in compilers to aiding in scientific discovery.

2.  **Elegant and Sound Methodology:** The overall framework is well-conceived.
    *   The use of an attribute grammar provides a principled and extensible way to define the search space.
    *   The "numerical descriptor" method for checking expression equality (by evaluating on random inputs modulo a large prime) is a very clever and pragmatic solution to bypass the slowness and complexity of symbolic manipulation engines.
    *   The proposal to learn a continuous embedding for symbolic expressions using an RNN is a major conceptual contribution in its own right. Similar to how word embeddings revolutionized NLP, this could unlock the application of many gradient-based learning methods to symbolic mathematics.
    *   The incremental training process, where solutions to simpler problems inform the search for more complex ones, is a natural and effective use of curriculum learning.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Experimental Results:** The experiments provide strong evidence for the framework's efficacy.
    *   The discovery of new, complex, and useful identities for five different families of expressions is a powerful proof-of-concept. The fact that the system found efficient polynomial-time formulas for expressions with naive exponential complexity is particularly impressive.
    *   The empirical comparison between random search, n-gram models, and the RNN-guided search clearly demonstrates the value of the learning-based approach, which consistently solves problems of much higher complexity than the baseline.
    *   The high accuracy of the RNN classifier for expression equivalence validates the quality of the learned symbolic representation.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Limited Scope of the Grammar:** The current work is restricted to homogeneous polynomials, primarily involving a single variable, and a fixed set of operations (matrix multiplication, summation, transpose, etc.). While this is a reasonable and necessary simplification for a foundational paper, it leaves open significant questions about generalizability. The authors could strengthen the paper by discussing the key challenges in extending the framework to a richer set of operations (e.g., trigonometric functions, logarithms, conditional logic, recursion) and how core components, like the equality check, would need to adapt.

2.  **Analysis of Learned Models:** While the n-gram and RNN models are shown to be effective, the paper provides limited insight into *why* they work and what they have learned. The n-gram models surprisingly outperform the more sophisticated RNN on several tasks. This suggests that the solutions may contain simple, repeated local motifs that are easily captured by counting n-grams. A more in-depth discussion of this trade-off would be beneficial. Furthermore, for the RNN, an analysis of the learned representation space (e.g., using t-SNE to visualize the embeddings of different expression classes) could provide valuable insights into whether the network is capturing meaningful algebraic or structural properties of the expressions.

3.  **Details of the RNN-Guided Search:** The description of how the trained RNN is used as a search strategy could be slightly more explicit. While the training process is clear (predicting the next rule), the testing/search process could be elaborated. For example, how does the scheduler handle the probabilistic outputs from the softmax? Does it always pick the max probability (greedy), or does it sample? This detail is important for understanding the exploration/exploitation trade-off in the search.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, exciting, and potentially high-impact line of research. The authors combine several clever ideas into a coherent and effective framework for a genuinely difficult problem. The work is technically sound, the experiments are compelling, and the results are significant, including the discovery of new mathematical identities. While the scope is currently limited, it lays a strong foundation for future work applying machine learning to automated symbolic discovery. The weaknesses are minor and mostly relate to opportunities for deeper analysis and discussion of future challenges. The paper is well-written, original, and addresses a topic of great interest to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
