Summary
The paper proposes a framework to automatically discover computationally efficient identities equivalent to given symbolic target expressions. It defines an attribute grammar over matrix/vector operations and searches the space of grammar trees to find expressions with lower computational cost. Two key technical ideas support tractable search: (1) a fast equivalence check by evaluating expressions over random assignments modulo a large prime (numerical descriptors), extended to linear combinations of trees via a modular linear system; and (2) learned search strategies trained on simpler targets, including n-gram models over subtrees and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions and a softmax policy to choose grammar rules. Experiments on several expression families show that learning-guided search finds efficient identities (often O(n^2) vs O(n^3) or polynomial vs exponential) at degrees where random search fails. The authors release code/data and report high accuracy for the RNN-based expression classifier.

Quality
- Soundness: The overall pipeline is sensible. Using random evaluation over finite fields to test polynomial identity is a standard trick; extending to linear combinations via modular Gaussian elimination is appropriate. However, the paper does not quantify the probability of false positives/negatives in equivalence testing. A reference to and bound via the Schwartz–Zippel lemma (and a discussion of p, N choices) would strengthen correctness. The mapping from solutions mod p back to integer/real coefficients is not discussed; while equality over Zp implies equality over reals for polynomials with small coefficients with high probability, this deserves justification.
- Technical care: Many practical training details for the RNN are carefully documented. The search setup (scheduler vs strategy) is clear. Complexity accounting is somewhat ad hoc: for some families the authors remove matrix-matrix multiply from the grammar to ensure O(n^2) complexity, which biases the search space and may rule out efficient formulas that still judiciously use matrix multiplication. A more principled cost model or explicit ablation would help.
- Evaluation: Success is measured as fraction of runs finding a solution within 10 minutes as degree increases, which is reasonable for a hard search problem. However, comparisons to existing simplification/optimization tools (e.g., Mathematica/Maple simplification, compiler algebraic passes, or known dynamic programs for symmetric polynomials) are missing, and there is no proof that identities are “new.” The hardest family (RBM-2) remains unsolved beyond k=5.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written, and the core ideas are traceable. Figures/examples (e.g., Examples 1–2) help. Important missing or underspecified items: the full grammar table (only referenced), exact tensor shapes and operation mapping for all grammar rules, and a precise description of the cost attributes. The dimensions in the linear system (F as M×N vs N×M) are ambiguous and should be corrected. A brief derivation/proof sketch for Example 1 would aid readers less comfortable with index manipulations. A short discussion on how coefficients from modular solving are converted to actual integer coefficients in the final identity is needed.

Originality
- Combining attribute grammars with learned policies for identity discovery appears novel in this domain, as does learning continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions tailored to this task. While the components (n-grams, RNNs on trees, random evaluations mod p) are known, their integration for automated discovery of low-complexity identities is original and interesting. Related work is broadly covered, though adding references on polynomial identity testing (Schwartz–Zippel) and on algebraic simplification/strength-reduction in compilers would situate the contribution better.

Significance
- If robust, the approach could impact algebraic optimization in ML models and compilers, especially for expression families where naive evaluation is expensive. The demonstrated gains (e.g., reducing O(n^3) to O(n^2)) on some families are meaningful. That said, current scope is limited (homogeneous polynomials in one variable, scalar outputs, restricted grammars), and the strongest learning model (n-grams) sometimes outperforms the RNN, tempering the novelty of the representation learning aspect. The inability to handle RBM-2 beyond k=5 suggests scalability challenges. Overall, the work advances an underexplored direction with moderate immediate impact and high potential.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation with a concrete, reproducible framework.
- Clever and efficient equivalence checking and linear-combination solver.
- Learning-guided search substantially outperforms random on several families; n-grams capture useful structure; RNN embeddings for expressions achieve low classification error.
- Code and data are released; practical training tips are provided.

Cons
- Probabilistic correctness of identity testing is not quantified; no mechanism to verify against false positives beyond empirical checks.
- Limited grammar/scope; removal of costly rules to force O(n^2) may preclude discovering some valid efficient identities.
- Weak baselines: no comparison to CAS/compilers or known identities; claims of “new” identities are not validated.
- RNN often underperforms n-grams; limited analysis of when/why learned representations help.
- Minor clarity issues (dimensions in linear system; mapping from mod p to real coefficients).

Suggestions and questions
- Add a section quantifying collision probability (Schwartz–Zippel), choices of p and N, and a post-verification step (e.g., symbolic verification for small sizes or multiple primes) to rule out false positives.
- Report time-to-solution distributions and ablations on scheduler (e.g., beam search), cost models, and grammar choices (with/without matrix multiply).
- Compare against Mathematica/Maple simplification and compiler algebraic passes on the same targets.
- Analyze failures on RBM-2; consider stronger search (MCTS, beam search), reinforcement learning or bandit policies, and graph neural networks over expression DAGs.
- Clarify the linear system dimensions and how modular coefficients map to final integer coefficients.

Reproducibility
- Good: code/data link provided; many training details listed. Please ensure the grammar table, dataset construction, and exact hyperparameters are included in the repository.

Overall assessment
- Quality: 6/10 (sound approach; needs stronger theoretical guarantees and baselines)
- Clarity: 7/10 (mostly clear; some missing details)
- Originality: 8/10 (novel integration of ideas in a new domain)
- Significance: 6/10 (promising direction; current impact moderate)

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper opens an interesting line of research at the intersection of learning and symbolic algebra, demonstrates nontrivial results, and provides resources. Addressing the correctness and evaluation gaps would strengthen it further.
