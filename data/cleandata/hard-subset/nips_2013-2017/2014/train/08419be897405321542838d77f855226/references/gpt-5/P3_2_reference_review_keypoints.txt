- Summary: Proposes learning-guided search over an attribute grammar to find algebraically equivalent, lower-cost matrix/vector expressions using randomized modular evaluation and linear combinations of grammar trees.

- Method: Uses integer descriptors via random evaluations mod a large prime to test equivalence, and solves F w = t mod p to find linear combos of candidate trees.

- Search learning: Trains n-gram models over subtrees and an RNN embedding + softmax policy (pretrained by multi-class classification) to guide grammar expansion.

- Experiments: Evaluated on expression families (AA^T sums, elementwise squares, symmetric polynomials, RBM sums); learned strategies outperform random search, n-grams often beat RNN, RNN helps on some hard cases.

- Example result: Shows nontrivial complexity reductions like sum(sum(A*B)) from O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)).

- Strength: Clear conceptual contribution combining attribute grammars with learned policies for cost-aware symbolic search.

- Strength: Practical randomized equivalence checking is simple and integrates well with learning.

- Strength: Extends expressivity by allowing linear combinations of grammar trees, not just single-tree matches.

- Strength: Uses curriculum learning (increasing degree k) to reuse learning signal effectively.

- Strength: RNN-based continuous embeddings of algebraic expressions are novel and potentially broadly useful.

- Strength: Empirically demonstrates substantial search efficiency gains over random and discovers some nontrivial identities.

- Weakness: No formal error bounds for randomized identity testing or guidance on number/choice of primes and samples, risking spurious identities.

- Weakness: Solving mod-p linear systems may yield modular coefficients that don't correspond to true integer/rational coefficients; coefficient recovery/verification is not addressed.

- Weakness: RNN evaluation measures memorization within equivalence classes rather than generalization; transfer tests holding out classes are missing.

- Weakness: Comparison only to random search; missing baselines from equality saturation, cost-aware rewrite systems, superoptimization, MCTS, beam/A* search, etc.

- Weakness: Search strategy appears greedy; no experiments on beam width, lookahead, backtracking, or exploration-exploitation sensitivity.

- Weakness: Scope limited to homogeneous single-variable polynomial families in experiments; unclear scalability to multiple inputs/operators and broader domains.

- Weakness: Key implementation details omitted from main text—exact grammar, cost model, descriptor size N, primes, solver sparsity/magnitude constraints, and recovery of integer coefficients.

- Weakness: Most discovered identities are relegated to supplement; few complex/new identities shown in main text, and scalability on RBM family limited to k≤5.

- Quality: Methodologically sound at a high level but lacks theoretical assurances for testing and coefficient recovery and misses strong empirical baselines and ablations.

- Clarity: Generally readable with good examples, but important implementation and training-details (grammar, cost model, RNN pretraining, curriculum) are insufficiently specified.

- Originality: Novel combination of attribute grammars and learned policies for identity discovery; RNN embeddings are interesting though related to existing tree-embedding work.

- Significance: Promising direction for learned symbolic optimization, but current evaluation and scope limit immediate impact; potential to be influential if validated further.

- Suggestions: Provide formal error analysis (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel style), use multiple primes and a verification phase (symbolic or multi-prime checks).

- Suggestions: Compare to at least one cost-aware rewrite baseline and non-greedy search methods (beam/A*/MCTS), and report sensitivity to beam width/search budget.

- Suggestions: Evaluate RNN transfer by holding out equivalence classes/families during pretraining and compare embeddings to n-grams on held-out data.

- Suggestions: Clarify grammar and cost model, descriptor parameters, and how modular solutions are translated to integer/rational coefficients.

- Suggestions: Explore invariance-aware modeling (canonicalization or invariant architectures) to handle commutativity/associativity and reduce sample complexity.

- Recommendation: Borderline accept (6) — interesting and novel but needs stronger validation, clearer methodology, and comparisons to established baselines.
