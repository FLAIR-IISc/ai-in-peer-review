This paper explores the application of machine learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities, framed within an attribute grammar. The core idea – using learned strategies to navigate the search space of possible symbolic transformations – is interesting, but the paper presents a somewhat overblown assessment of its contributions and lacks sufficient rigor in its evaluation to warrant strong positive endorsement.

The attribute grammar approach itself is well-established (as the authors acknowledge), and its use as a search space for symbolic manipulations is not entirely novel. The novelty lies in the integration of learning techniques to guide that search. However, the performance gains reported rely heavily on specific expression families, and the comparatively strong performance of n-gram models over the more sophisticated RNN is somewhat surprising and deserves deeper investigation. The claim of discovering “many new mathematical identities” feels overstated; finding equivalent expressions via algebraic manipulation, even if computationally aided, doesn't necessarily equate to discovering *new* mathematical truths. More convincing demonstration of genuine novelty beyond simple re-arrangements is required.

**Strengths:**

*   **Interesting Problem:** Applying machine learning to symbolic manipulation is a challenging and potentially fruitful area.
*   **Clear Framework:** The attribute grammar provides a well-defined structure for exploring the search space.
*   **Demonstrated Improvement:** The learning-based search strategies do outperform random search in some cases, particularly for expressions amenable to n-gram capture.
*   **Code Release:** Availability of code and data is excellent and facilitates reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Overstated Claims:** The repeated emphasis on “discovery” and “novelty” feels unsubstantiated. Many of the “efficient identities” seem like straightforward algebraic simplifications.
*   **Limited Scope:** The experiments are restricted to a narrow family of polynomial expressions. The applicability to more general symbolic domains is unclear. The restrictions outlined in Section 2 are significant and limit the generalizability of the findings.
*   **RNN Performance:** The relatively poor performance of the RNN compared to simple n-grams is concerning. The authors provide some explanation regarding dropout, but a more detailed analysis of the RNN’s learning process and the features it captures would be beneficial. Why does a simpler model outperform a more complex one?
*   **Evaluation:** The evaluation primarily focuses on whether a solution is *found* within a time limit, not on the *efficiency* of the search process itself. A more thorough analysis of the search trajectories and computational cost would be helpful. Specifically, how does the number of trees evaluated scale with k?
*   **Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to existing symbolic computation systems (Mathematica, Maple) is cursory. A more direct comparison, demonstrating that the proposed method can outperform these systems on comparable tasks, would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** There's limited theoretical grounding for why these learning methods should be effective in this domain. A more formal analysis of the search space and the properties of the learned strategies would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel application of machine learning to a challenging problem in symbolic computation.
*   The framework is well-defined and the code is publicly available.
*   The results demonstrate a clear improvement over random search in some cases.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The claims of novelty are overstated.
*   The scope of the experiments is limited.
*   The performance of the RNN is disappointing.
*   The evaluation is insufficient to fully assess the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
*   The paper lacks a strong theoretical foundation and a thorough comparison to existing approaches.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper has some merit but falls short of the standards for strong acceptance at NIPS. The core idea is interesting, but the execution and evaluation are not sufficiently rigorous. The overblown claims and limited scope detract from the potential contribution. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to significantly expand the experimental evaluation, provide a more thorough analysis of the RNN’s behavior, and tone down the claims of novelty. A direct comparison to established symbolic computation systems is essential. A more theoretically grounded analysis would also be a significant improvement.
