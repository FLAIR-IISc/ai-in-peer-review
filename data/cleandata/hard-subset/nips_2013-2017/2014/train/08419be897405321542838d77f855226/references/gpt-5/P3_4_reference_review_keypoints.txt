- Proposes a framework to discover computationally efficient symbolic identities by generating expression trees from an attribute grammar and checking equivalence via randomized evaluation over Zp.  
- Learns search policies from simpler targets using n-gram subtree models and an RNN embedding with a softmax policy to guide tree generation.  
- Handles targets requiring linear combinations by solving linear systems over Zp to find matching combinations of generated trees.  
- Experiments on matrix identities, symmetric polynomials, and RBM sums show learned search outperforms random search; n-grams often beat the RNN.  
- RNN embedding for symbolic expressions is novel and potentially reusable.  
- Clear pipeline and practical implementation details; code released for reproducibility.  
- Empirical evidence supports curriculum learning: training on simpler instances improves search on harder ones.  
- Weakness: randomized Zp testing lacks quantified collision/error bounds and no guidance on choice of p and number of evaluations.  
- Weakness: lifting modular solutions to integer/rational coefficients is not explained; unclear how modular weights map to valid real-valued identities.  
- Weakness: unclear whether computed linear combinations correspond to syntactically valid grammar expressions or permit arbitrary coefficients.  
- Evaluation concern: success metric (any non-zero success in 10 runs within 10 minutes) masks variance; lacks time-to-first-solution, nodes explored, and memory statistics.  
- Baselines missing: no comparison to symbolic algebra systems (Mathematica/Maple) or known analytic recurrences.  
- Scope limited to homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, mostly single-variable; some grammar rules were artificially removed potentially biasing results.  
- RNN underperforms n-gram in many tasks; paper lacks analysis of why and exploration of alternative architectures or RL-based objectives.  
- Application claims (e.g., dropout or RBM partition estimation) are not substantiated by experiments; RBM results were limited.  
- Methodology generally sound but weakened by lack of formal guarantees for randomized testing and modular linear solution lifting.  
- Clarity is good overall but the paper should better explain the linear-combination procedure and collision probability analysis.  
- Originality: novel combination of attribute grammars with learned search policies and continuous embeddings for expression equivalence.  
- Significance: promising direction for ML-guided symbolic search but current empirical scope and restricted grammars limit immediate impact.  
- Suggestions: provide probabilistic guarantees for equivalence testing, validate across multiple primes or rationals, and explain coefficient constraints and lifting procedures.  
- Suggestions: add strong symbolic baselines, report detailed search statistics, explore beam/MCTS or RL strategies, and analyze why n-grams outperform the RNN.  
- Suggestions: demonstrate scalability to larger grammars, multi-variable targets, and at least one end-to-end application.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — novel and interesting but needs stronger soundness guarantees, baselines, and evaluation rigor.
