

# Review: Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities

## Summary
This paper introduces a framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars and machine learning. The authors propose a grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions, where each grammar rule has an associated computational complexity (attribute). They develop numerical and learned representations for symbolic expressions to efficiently compare expressions and identify equivalent forms. The core contribution is the application of machine learning techniques (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for efficient expressions within the grammar space, rather than relying on brute-force enumeration. The framework is evaluated on discovering identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) or from exponential to polynomial time for various polynomial families.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound framework with well-motivated components. The use of integer-based descriptors (Zp) to avoid numerical issues in expression comparison is a thoughtful implementation detail. The RNN representation learning is carefully implemented with appropriate initialization, activation functions, and curriculum learning. The experimental design is robust, with multiple runs (10) per condition and a fixed 10-minute cutoff, providing reliable success rate measurements. The authors are careful to evaluate both strengths (e.g., 3-gram outperforming random search for complex identities) and weaknesses (e.g., failure to discover solutions for k > 5 in the RBM-2 family).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement, grammar definition, representation learning, and search strategies are logically presented with consistent notation. The examples (Examples 1 & 2) effectively illustrate the problem and solution. The section on representation learning includes detailed implementation specifics (weight initialization, activation functions, curriculum learning) that would enable reproduction. The connection between NLP techniques (n-grams, RNNs) and symbolic computation is clearly explained.

**Originality:** This work makes a novel contribution by applying machine learning to guide the search for efficient mathematical identities within an attribute grammar framework. While attribute grammars have been used in other domains (e.g., compiler construction, knapsack problems), the authors correctly identify that this is the first application to discovering mathematical identities. The use of RNNs to learn continuous representations of symbolic expressions is also novel in this context. The paper clearly differentiates from related work in theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation systems like Maple and Mathematica.

**Significance:** The discovered identities have meaningful computational implications (e.g., reducing complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) for certain matrix operations), with potential applications in machine learning (e.g., efficient computation of dropout and RBM partition functions). The framework represents a promising direction for applying machine learning to symbolic computation, which has been underexplored. The paper demonstrates that learning can successfully guide combinatorial search in a problem setting where brute-force enumeration is intractable.

## Weaknesses

**Scope Limitations:** The paper restricts the problem to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree k with a single matrix or vector as input. While the authors acknowledge these limitations in Section 8, the discussion of how to extend to more general cases (e.g., with recursion or trigonometric functions) is superficial. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed analysis of how these restrictions impact the generality of the approach.

**Performance on Challenging Cases:** The (RBM-2)k family presents a significant challenge, where no solutions were discovered for k > 5, and the RNN only marginally outperformed random search for k = 5. The paper lacks a thorough analysis of why this family is so difficult to handle with the current framework, which would help identify limitations and guide future work.

**Comparison with Symbolic Computation Systems:** While the paper contrasts with systems like Maple and Mathematica, it doesn't provide quantitative comparisons of the discovered identities' efficiency or novelty against what these systems could achieve. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the significance claim.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The framework is well-designed, the implementation details are thoughtful, and the results demonstrate significant advantages over random search for complex identities. The paper's novelty in applying machine learning to guide combinatorial search for efficient mathematical identities is well-supported by the experimental results.

The work is significant because it demonstrates a concrete application of machine learning to a fundamental problem in symbolic computation, with potential practical applications in machine learning algorithms. While the restricted problem setting is acknowledged, the framework provides a solid foundation for future work on more general mathematical discovery.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), presents a novel approach (Originality), and addresses a meaningful problem with practical implications (Significance).

## Recommendation

Accept. The paper makes a novel contribution to the field with strong technical execution and significant potential impact. The limitations noted are acknowledged by the authors and do not detract from the core contribution. The framework opens a promising direction for applying machine learning to symbolic computation problems that have traditionally been approached with rule-based or brute-force methods.
