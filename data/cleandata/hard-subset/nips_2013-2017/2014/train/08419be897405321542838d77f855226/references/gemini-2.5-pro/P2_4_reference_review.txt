### **Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is symbolically equivalent to a given target but has a lower computational complexity. As a brute-force or random search of the grammar tree is intractable for complex targets, the authors propose using machine learning to learn an effective search strategy. Specifically, they train models (n-grams and a Recursive Neural Network) on solutions found for simpler expressions to guide the search for more complex ones. The method is shown to discover new, non-trivial identities for several families of expressions, including some related to common computations in machine learning, reducing their complexity from exponential or high-order polynomial to low-order polynomial time.

**Qualitative Evaluation**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The proposed framework is well-conceived, combining classical ideas from computer science (attribute grammars) with modern machine learning techniques. The method for checking expression equality is particularly clever; using numerical evaluation over a finite field (integers modulo a prime) is a pragmatic and effective way to sidestep the challenges of symbolic manipulation while maintaining correctness. The experimental evaluation is strong, clearly demonstrating the superiority of the learned search strategies over a random baseline on a variety of challenging expression families. The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach, such as the restricted grammar and the failure to find solutions for the most complex RBM-related family beyond k=5.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear, and the introductory examples (Example 1 & 2) are highly effective at motivating the problem and illustrating the core idea. The description of the attribute grammar, expression representations, and search strategies is easy to follow. The inclusion of a link to the code is a commendable step towards reproducibility. A minor point for improvement would be to provide more intuition for the series of specific techniques required to make the RNN training work (identity initialization, curriculum learning, disparate learning rates). While these details are crucial for implementation, the paper could better explain *why* the RNN is so sensitive and why this particular combination of "tricks" is necessary to stabilize its representation learning.

**Originality:** The originality of this work is its primary strength. The application of machine learning to guide combinatorial search in the symbolic mathematics domain is highly novel. While program synthesis and theorem proving are established fields, the explicit goal of finding *computationally efficient* identities, and using ML trained on simpler problems to do so, is a fresh and exciting direction. The use of an RNN to learn a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions is a creative adaptation of techniques from natural language processing and represents a significant conceptual contribution. The work is well-situated within the related literature, clearly distinguishing itself from classical symbolic engines like Mathematica/Maple and from other work in program induction.

**Significance:** The potential significance of this research is very high. The results are not merely theoretical; the paper reports the discovery of new, useful mathematical identities that offer substantial computational speedups. This has immediate practical implications. The framework could be integrated into compilers or scientific computing libraries to automatically optimize code. Furthermore, the successful application to expressions related to RBMs and dropout suggests this approach could directly contribute to accelerating machine learning algorithms. Beyond these direct applications, the paper provides a powerful demonstration of how machine learning can be used to tackle challenging combinatorial search problems that are central to artificial intelligence. This work is likely to inspire follow-up research in applying similar learning-guided search techniques to other symbolic domains, such as algorithm discovery or theorem proving.

---

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem (expression optimization) with a highly original approach that blends grammars, numerical methods, and modern machine learning. The successful discovery of new, complex identities is a significant achievement.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The choice of expression families is non-trivial, and the results clearly show that the learning-based strategies dramatically outperform the random baseline, pushing the boundary of what is discoverable.
3.  **Creative Use of ML Techniques:** The adaptation of both n-gram models and Recursive Neural Networks to this symbolic domain is clever. The idea of learning a continuous representation for mathematical expressions is particularly powerful and could have applications beyond this specific task.
4.  **Clear Problem Formulation:** The framework of using an attribute grammar to define a search space and then learning a policy to explore it is elegant and well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Scope of the Grammar:** The current work is restricted to homogeneous polynomials. While the authors acknowledge this, the extension to a more general grammar (including trigonometric, exponential, or conditional operations) would introduce significant challenges not addressed here (e.g., managing non-terminating trees, a much larger search space). This limits the immediate general-purpose applicability of the system.
2.  **Analysis of Model Performance:** The n-gram models consistently outperform the more complex RNN model. This is a surprising and interesting result that warrants more discussion. Is this because the discovered solutions possess a highly regular, repetitive local structure that n-grams are perfectly suited to capture? Does it suggest a fundamental mismatch between the current RNN architecture and this type of symbolic reasoning? A deeper analysis would strengthen the paper's scientific contribution by offering more insight into the nature of the problem.
3.  **RNN Training Complexity:** The successful training of the RNN relied on a number of specific heuristics. While their inclusion is good for reproducibility, the lack of deeper justification makes it seem less like a principled approach and more like a bag of tricks. A more thorough ablation study or analysis of why these are needed would be beneficial.

**Recommendation:**

This is an excellent paper with a novel idea, strong execution, and significant results. It opens up a promising new research direction at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The weaknesses identified are primarily limitations on the current scope and opportunities for deeper analysis, rather than fundamental flaws in the work presented. This paper would be a valuable and thought-provoking addition to the NIPS program. I strongly recommend acceptance.
