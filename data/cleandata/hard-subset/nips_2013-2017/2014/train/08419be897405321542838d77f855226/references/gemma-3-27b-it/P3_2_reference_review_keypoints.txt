- Presents a framework to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars guided by learning.
- Uses n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to guide search, learning from solutions to simpler expressions to speed discovery for harder ones.
- Demonstrates significant empirical speedups (e.g., O(n^3) to O(n^2)) on polynomial expression benchmarks and finds non-obvious identities.
- Positions work well within theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming literature and contrasts with rule-based simplifiers.
- Attribute grammars are appropriately used to structure the search space and the paper connects to related grammar- and NLP-based methods.
- Novel combination of attribute grammars and machine learning to discover, not just select, identities.
- Methodologically sound: structured search, clever integer-based numerical descriptors to avoid floating-point errors.
- Empirical results are compelling, with illustrative examples and figures that aid understanding.
- Clear writing and organization; code and data are released for reproducibility.
- Promising application potential in compiler optimization and ML (e.g., efficient RBM computations).
- Limitation: grammar restricted to homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix variable, reducing general applicability.
- RNN underperforms compared to simple n-gram models; reasons for this gap are not deeply analyzed.
- Scalability concerns for high-degree polynomials (k >> 6) due to exponential search growth; 10-minute search cutoff seems arbitrary.
- Insufficient detail on hyperparameter tuning (learning rates, dropout, n-gram order) harms reproducibility.
- Difficulty: RBM-2 family solutions not found beyond k=5, indicating limits on handling complex dependencies.
- Arguments for acceptance: high novelty, ability to discover non-trivial identities, solid experiments, clear presentation, open-source code, and potential impact.
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability limits, RNN performance issues, and restrictive grammar constraints.
- Overall assessment: strong paper with significant potential; recommended for acceptance with minor revisions.
- Suggested revisions: deeper analysis of RNN vs n-grams, expanded discussion on scalability and mitigation strategies, more hyperparameter details, explanation of RBM-2 challenges, and justification of the 10-minute cutoff.
