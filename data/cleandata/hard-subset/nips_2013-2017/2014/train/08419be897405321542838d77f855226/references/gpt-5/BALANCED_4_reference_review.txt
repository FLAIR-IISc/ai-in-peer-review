Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given symbolic target expression. Expressions are generated by an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic and elementwise operations; each tree uses the variable A exactly k times and must yield a scalar. Because exhaustive search is intractable, the authors learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets. Two strategies are explored: (i) n-gram statistics over subtrees observed in prior solutions, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees; a softmax on top of the RNN predicts the next rule during search. Equality between candidate expressions and the target is checked numerically by evaluating on random inputs in Zp (integers mod a large prime), and for harder targets, linear combinations of trees are solved via modular linear algebra to match the target descriptor. Experiments on five expression families (matrix polynomials, Hadamard variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, and two RBM-derived families) show that learned strategies enable discovery of identities (often O(n^2) rather than O(n^3), or polynomial rather than exponential). N-grams generally outperform the RNN. Code and supplementary identities are provided.

The work is related to theorem proving and program induction but avoids undecidability by restricting to bounded-degree polynomials. It builds on attribute grammars (Knuth) and grammar-guided search used in optimization and GP, and borrows from NLP: n-gram modeling and RNNs over trees (Socher et al.). It differs from symbolic algebra systems (Maple/Mathematica) that simplify but do not aim at computational complexity reduction nor use learning.

Strengths
- Clear and interesting problem formulation: find algebraic rewrites that provably compute the same quantity but with lower complexity.
- Clever use of Zp descriptors for fast equivalence testing and linear combination solving; practical and scalable compared to full symbolic normalization.
- Systematic framework combining grammar-constrained search with learned guidance; curriculum and identity initialization choices in the RNN are thoughtful.
- Empirical evidence that learning substantially increases the tractable search horizon; the examples are nontrivial, and code is released.
- The idea of continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions is appealing and could seed follow-on work linking ML and symbolic computation.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation
Quality
- Correctness verification: Equality testing via evaluation in Zp is reasonable (cf. Schwartz–Zippel), but the paper does not quantify collision probabilities, choice of p and N, or use multiple primes/inputs to bound false positives/negatives. For linear combinations, solutions to Fw = t mod p may not lift uniquely to ℤ/ℚ; the paper should describe rational reconstruction/CRT and how final identities are verified symbolically.
- Baselines: The search evaluation lacks strong non-learning baselines such as beam/A* search, MCTS, or rewrite-based equality saturation. Comparisons to CAS simplification plus algebraic complexity heuristics (common subexpression elimination, associativity/commutativity reordering, contraction-order optimization) are missing. For Symk, dynamic-programming formulations are known; a comparison would calibrate gains.
- Metrics: Success is counted if any of 10 runs finds a solution under a 10-minute cutoff. This mixes exploration and solution quality; more informative would be time-to-first-solution distributions, success under fixed compute budgets, and asymptotic/constant-factor improvements measured in wall-clock time on real implementations.
- Learning components: The RNN underperforms n-grams on most families; ablations on curriculum, embedding dimension, dropout, and the greedy vs. stochastic policy would clarify where the representation helps. The policy is greedy; beam or sampling might help escape local optima.
- Claims about “previously undiscovered” identities are not substantiated; some examples (e.g., sum(sum(A*B))) reducing to O(n(m+p)) are standard.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable with helpful examples, but some key details are missing/unclear: the exact grammar (Table 1) and complexity attributes; how the scheduler handles rule arity/typing and termination; how linear-combination coefficients are interpreted over reals; and how numerical equivalence thresholds are chosen.
- The discussion of RBM families is suggestive; however, claims about approximating dropout or RBM partition functions would benefit from concrete demonstrations.

Originality
- The combination of attribute grammars, learned guidance, and modular-evaluation identity testing for complexity reduction is novel within ML venues. The RNN embedding for symbolic expressions is an interesting application of tree RNNs beyond NLP. That said, grammar-guided program search and symbolic rewriting have a long history; positioning against that literature could be strengthened.

Significance
- The problem is important and underexplored in ML. The framework can inspire future methods bridging ML and symbolic optimization. Immediate practical impact is tempered by the restricted domain (homogeneous polynomials, single variable, scalar outputs), reliance on hand-chosen families, and limited demonstrations of real-world speedups. The fact that simple n-grams often suffice suggests the learning story is promising but not yet compelling.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide formal correctness guarantees: use multiple primes and independent random inputs; quantify collision bounds; describe lifting modulo solutions to rational coefficients and provide symbolic proofs (e.g., via CAS verification) for reported identities.
- Strengthen baselines: beam/A* or MCTS over the grammar; rewrite-system search; CAS with heuristic complexity objectives. Compare to known dynamic programs for Symk.
- Improve search: combine the learned policy with beam search or stochastic sampling; add a value function estimating “distance to solvability”; cache/memoize partials.
- Report practical speedups: implement both naive and discovered formulas and measure wall-clock and memory across n,m,p, including constant factors.
- Broaden scope or justify constraints: multi-variable polynomials, non-homogeneous expressions, and additional operators; clarify how the approach would extend.
- Provide a catalog of discovered identities with proofs and discuss where they overlap with known results.

Arguments for acceptance
- Interesting, well-motivated problem with a coherent framework and code release.
- Learning demonstrably expands the search frontier; some identities are nontrivial.
- Bridges ML and symbolic computation; may stimulate follow-up work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient rigor in correctness verification and bold claims about novelty.
- Weak baselines and evaluation metrics; limited evidence of practical impact.
- RNN component underperforms simpler n-grams; ablation/analysis limited.
- Narrow scope and heavy reliance on curated families.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper addresses a meaningful problem with a creative framework and promising early results, but the empirical evaluation and rigor around correctness/novelty need strengthening. With the suggested improvements, this could become a strong contribution.
