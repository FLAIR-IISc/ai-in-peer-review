This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient versions of mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar, where each rule has an associated computational complexity. Because a brute-force or random search over the grammar tree is intractable for complex expressions, the authors propose learning a search strategy. This strategy is trained on solutions found for simpler expressions to guide the search towards promising expressions for more complex targets. The paper explores two main learning approaches: n-gram models that capture common sub-expressions from previous solutions, and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions and uses it to predict the next grammar rule to apply. This work relates to several fields, including automated theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation. However, its main novelty lies in the application of modern statistical machine learning techniques, particularly those inspired by natural language processing (e.g., n-grams, recursive networks for compositional structure), to the problem of symbolic optimization. Unlike traditional rule-based systems like Mathematica or Maple, this approach learns search heuristics from data rather than relying on hand-crafted rules.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The proposed framework is well-conceived, from the use of an attribute grammar to structure the problem to the clever use of numerical descriptors over finite fields (Zp) for robust and efficient equivalence checking. This numerical checking method is a practical solution to the bottleneck of symbolic manipulation. The second proposed representation, a learned continuous vector via an RNN, is also a significant technical contribution. The authors provide crucial details for training this network successfully (e.g., identity initialization, curriculum learning), which demonstrates a careful and thorough implementation. The experiments are well-designed, testing the framework on several non-trivial families of expressions, including some with exponential complexity (e.g., related to RBM partition functions). The results convincingly support the central claim that learning-guided search significantly outperforms random search.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The problem is clearly defined in the introduction, and the proposed components (grammar, representations, search strategies) are explained logically in subsequent sections. The use of examples, such as Example 1 and 2, is effective in illustrating both the problem and the nature of the solutions discovered. The diagrams of the grammar trees and the RNN architecture (Figures 1 and 2) are helpful aids to understanding. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly.

One area that could be improved for clarity is the description of the linear combination step (Section 5). It is not immediately clear how the set of M trees used to form the matrix F is selected. Are all trees generated so far included in the system? If so, this could become computationally demanding. A few more details on the practical implementation of this step would enhance the paper's reproducibility.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While attribute grammars and search are not new, their combination with modern machine learning techniques (n-grams and especially recursive neural networks) to discover novel mathematical identities is, to my knowledge, a new and exciting direction. The concept of learning a continuous representation for symbolic mathematical expressions, analogous to word embeddings in NLP, is a powerful idea that could have applications beyond this specific task. The paper also demonstrates the discovery of new identities for complex expressions (as detailed in the supplement), which is a concrete testament to the method's originality and power. The related work is adequately cited, and the authors do a good job of positioning their contribution with respect to prior work in automated reasoning and program synthesis.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is significant. The framework presents a generalizable approach for optimizing symbolic computations that could find applications in scientific computing, compiler design, and machine learning itself. For instance, the paper notes that the discovered identities for the "RBM-2" family could lead to polynomial-time approximations for the RBM partition function, a notoriously difficult problem. More broadly, this work serves as a compelling proof-of-concept that data-driven machine learning can make meaningful contributions in highly structured, symbolic domains traditionally dominated by formal methods. It opens up a promising new research avenue at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic mathematics. The learned representations for expressions could enable a wide range of ML tools to be applied to symbolic problems.

**Strengths:**
*   **Novelty and Ambition:** The paper tackles a challenging and fundamental problem with a highly original approach, successfully bridging symbolic methods with modern deep learning.
*   **Strong Technical Contributions:** The framework is well-designed, featuring two clever solutions (numerical and learned) for expression representation and equivalence checking. The application of an RNN to learn compositional representations of mathematical formulas is a standout contribution.
*   **Convincing Empirical Results:** The experiments clearly show that the learned search strategies significantly outperform a random baseline on a variety of difficult expression families, leading to the discovery of new, useful identities.
*   **Potential for High Impact:** The ideas presented could influence future work in automated optimization, compiler design, and the application of ML to scientific discovery.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Limited Scope of the Grammar:** The current work is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of a single variable. While this is a reasonable starting point, a discussion of the challenges involved in extending the grammar to a richer set of operations (e.g., trigonometric functions, conditionals, recursion) would strengthen the paper. For instance, how would equivalence be checked, and how would the search deal with non-terminating expressions?
*   **Under-performance of the RNN:** It is interesting that the simpler n-gram models often outperform the more sophisticated RNN. The paper notes this but lacks a deep analysis of why. Is the RNN architecture not well-suited for capturing the simple, iterative patterns present in the solutions, or is it an optimization issue? A brief discussion of this result would add valuable insight. The use of dropout is a good step, but perhaps the model still struggles to generalize from `k` to `k+1` when the underlying pattern is a simple repetition that n-grams capture naturally.
*   **Role of the Learned Representation:** The role of the learned RNN representation in the final system could be clarified. It is used to generate scores for the RNN search strategy, but the primary equivalence checking seems to rely on the numerical representation. It would be interesting to know if the learned vectors `φ(S)` could be used more directly in the search, perhaps as a heuristic for "closeness" to the target representation, to further guide the search.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that is novel, technically strong, and presents significant results on a challenging problem. It opens up an exciting new direction for research. Despite some minor weaknesses regarding the current scope and the analysis of the RNN's performance, the strengths far outweigh them. I strongly recommend acceptance.
