Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a grammar-based framework for discovering computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target symbolic expression. Expressions are generated via an attribute grammar whose rules are annotated with computational complexity. Since exhaustive search quickly becomes infeasible, the authors learn search policies from solutions to simpler problems. They explore two families of learned strategies: n-gram models over subtrees and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees and, on top of that, a rule-selection softmax. To check equivalence efficiently, they avoid symbolic algebra and use randomized numerical descriptors over a large prime field; for complex targets, they solve for linear combinations of candidate trees via modular linear algebra.

The work is positioned against theorem proving and program induction (e.g., [4, 5, 11, 18, 20]) but sidesteps undecidability by focusing on homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree. It contrasts with CAS systems like Maple/Mathematica [6, 27], which simplify but do not explicitly optimize complexity, and with prior attribute-grammar optimization uses [7, 19, 26] by targeting symbolic identity discovery. The learned representations connect to NLP embeddings and RNNs over trees [8, 15, 16, 17, 23], but apply them to symbolic computation trees.

Empirically, the method discovers efficient identities across several expression families, including cases where naive evaluation would be cubic or exponential but the discovered forms are quadratic or cubic, and demonstrates that learned search (especially n-grams) substantially enlarges the range of tractable targets. The RNN achieves low error on an expression-classification proxy task and offers a learned representation applicable to rule selection.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The grammar/search framework is well specified conceptually; the search scheduler and learned policies are clearly described. The use of randomized finite-field evaluation for equivalence checking and modular Gaussian elimination for linear combinations is standard in spirit (polynomial identity testing), but the paper lacks formal bounds on failure probability or guidance on choosing the number of evaluation points and modulus. A brief analysis using standard results (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel) would strengthen correctness claims. The decision to remove matrix-multiplication from the grammar for some families enforces an O(n^2) ceiling but also constrains the search space; this is logically consistent but should be discussed as a bias. Claims of “previously undiscovered” identities would benefit from formal proofs (beyond randomized checking) and literature cross-checks, especially for families like symmetric polynomials where rich identities exist [24].
- Empirics: The evaluation measures success rates under fixed time limits across increasing degree k, with multiple random seeds, and compares random, n-gram, and RNN strategies. This is appropriate for a search problem. Reporting wall-clock statistics and success fractions is useful; however, more ablations (e.g., descriptor size N, modulus p, training set size, scheduler variants) and comparisons to CAS simplification routines or simple algebraic heuristics would make the case stronger. The RNN underperforming n-grams on several families is reported honestly; some diagnostic analysis is included (dropout, identity initialization), but further insight into failure modes would help.
- Completeness: The paper describes all components needed to reproduce results and provides code. Some implementation details (exact grammar rules and complexity attributes in Table 1, dataset sizes per k, hyperparameters for the rule-selection softmax) could be expanded.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The motivation, constraints (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, single variable in most experiments), and pipeline are stated upfront. The RNN description includes architecture, training tricks, and curriculum. Suggested improvements:
- Include the full grammar table inline with explicit input/output shapes and cost attributes.
- Provide pseudocode for the scheduler-strategy interaction and the linear-combination solver.
- Add a brief theoretical justification for equivalence testing over finite fields and guidance on N and p.
- Clarify the exact stopping/termination rules when the tree depth equals degree and scalar requirement.

Originality
The combination of:
- an attribute grammar for algebraic expression generation with complexity attributes,
- learned search policies trained on solutions to simpler targets,
- and continuous RNN embeddings for symbolic expressions used to guide rule selection,
appears novel relative to prior work cited. While learning heuristics for theorem provers has precedent [4], and grammars have been used for optimization [7, 19, 26], applying learned policies to discover complexity-reducing algebraic identities with efficient randomized equivalence checking is, to my knowledge, new in this form (for the 2014–2015 timeframe). The RNN embedding of algebraic trees is an original application of techniques akin to [23].

Significance
The work addresses a core challenge—search in combinatorial spaces—on a symbolic domain with practical implications: automating algebraic optimizations that reduce computational complexity. Potential downstream use includes compiler optimization, fast evaluation of certain series, and possibly closed-form approximations relevant to ML (e.g., RBM partition function terms). However, current scope restrictions (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, mostly single variable) and the need to constrain the grammar to enforce subcubic complexity limit immediate applicability. The empirical gains over random search are substantial; the approach could seed further work on learned symbolic manipulation.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and modular framework (grammar, representation, search).
- Effective use of learning to significantly improve search, with reproducible code.
- RNN representation for expressions achieves high classification accuracy and is novel in this context.
- Discovery of efficient formulas for nontrivial families; transparent reporting of successes/failures.

Cons
- No formal error bounds for randomized equivalence testing; lacks symbolic verification of final identities.
- Limited baselines (no comparison to CAS simplification or other search heuristics).
- Scope restrictions (homogeneous degree, scalar outputs; some experiments remove matrix multiply).
- RNN guidance underperforms simple n-grams on key families; limited analysis of why.
- Claims of novelty of identities would benefit from stronger literature checks and proofs.

Questions for authors
1. Can you provide probabilistic bounds (in terms of degree, modulus size, and number of samples) for false positives in the descriptor-based equality check?
2. For the reported identities, do you provide symbolic proofs or machine-checkable certificates beyond randomized testing?
3. How sensitive are results to the choice of p and N? What are typical N/M ratios in the linear-combination step?
4. Can CAS systems (e.g., Mathematica’s FullSimplify with cost-based objectives) find any of the reported identities?
5. What are the main error modes for the RNN policy vs n-grams, and can hybrid policies improve results?

Recommendation
The paper presents a coherent, reproducible, and reasonably well-evaluated approach that is original and potentially impactful, with clear strengths in methodology and honest discussion of limitations. Addressing the correctness guarantees for equality testing and adding stronger baselines would further solidify the contribution. Overall, the work meets the NeurIPS criteria on quality, clarity, originality, and significance with some caveats.
