

# Review: "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The authors address the problem of finding expressions that compute the same result as a target expression but with lower computational complexity. They introduce an attribute grammar framework to represent symbolic expressions as sequences of grammar rules, and then use machine learning (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for efficient expressions. The key insight is that solutions to simpler expressions can be used to train models that improve the search strategy for more complex expressions. The authors demonstrate their approach on several families of mathematical expressions, showing significant complexity reductions (e.g., from O(n³) to O(n²) for matrix operations).

The work bridges several fields: attribute grammars from compiler design, symbolic computation, and machine learning techniques from natural language processing. It differs from related work in theorem proving and symbolic computation by using learning to automatically discover search strategies rather than relying on hand-designed heuristics.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with well-structured experiments that support its claims. The authors provide a clear problem definition, a well-motivated framework, and comprehensive experimental results. They carefully evaluate both the learned representation (Section 7.1) and the identity discovery (Section 7.2), with appropriate baselines (random search) and metrics (success rate within 10-minute time limit).

The experimental design is strong, with multiple expression families (RBM-1, RBM-2, Symk, etc.) across varying degrees of complexity (k=2 to k=15). The paper acknowledges limitations, noting that the RBM-2 family proved particularly challenging and that their approach is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree.

One minor weakness is the lack of deeper theoretical analysis of why n-gram models outperform RNNs for this specific task, despite the empirical results showing this trend. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between these learning approaches.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The structure follows a logical flow from problem statement to framework to experiments. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical formulations are clear. The examples provided (especially Example 1 and 2) effectively illustrate the key concepts.

The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand and potentially reproduce the results. The description of the integer-based descriptor (Section 4.1) and the RNN architecture (Section 4.2) are particularly clear. The supplementary material (mentioned in the paper) would likely contain additional details needed for full reproducibility.

## Originality
This paper makes a significant contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The core idea of using learning to guide the search for mathematical identities within an attribute grammar framework is novel. While attribute grammars have been used in other domains (e.g., compiler design, bioinformatics), the authors are the first to apply them to discover mathematical identities with machine learning.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work in theorem proving (which uses hand-designed heuristics), symbolic computation (which uses rule-based simplification), and program induction (which typically addresses different problem spaces). The application of n-gram models and RNNs to this domain, particularly the use of RNNs to learn continuous representations of symbolic expressions, represents a creative and novel combination of techniques.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. The discovered identities offer concrete computational speedups that could be immediately applicable in machine learning (e.g., for more efficient implementations of dropout or RBM partition function computation). The paper demonstrates that their approach can discover identities too complex for manual discovery or brute-force search, which is a significant advancement in the field.

The broader impact is in showing how machine learning can be applied to guide search in combinatorially complex spaces, which could have applications beyond mathematical identity discovery. The paper's approach of learning search strategies from simpler problems to tackle more complex ones could inspire similar techniques in other AI domains facing combinatorial search challenges.

## Strengths
- Clear problem definition and well-motivated approach
- Strong experimental results demonstrating significant complexity reductions
- Novel integration of attribute grammars with machine learning techniques
- Concrete examples of discovered identities (e.g., Example 2 for matrix expressions)
- Practical relevance to machine learning applications (RBM, dropout)
- Thorough comparison with random search baseline
- Appropriate discussion of limitations and scope

## Weaknesses
- The problem is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, limiting general applicability
- The RNN representation learning (Section 4.2) could be more deeply analyzed (e.g., why 5% error rate is sufficient for the search task)
- The paper doesn't fully explain why n-gram models outperform RNNs in this specific context
- The RBM-2 family results (k>5) are disappointing, but the paper doesn't explore why this approach fails here
- The supplementary material (where full identities are listed) is mentioned but not provided in the paper

## Recommendation
I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. The work is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of machine learning applied to symbolic computation. The paper demonstrates clear significance by showing that their approach can discover mathematical identities with substantial computational benefits that would be difficult to find through other means.

The paper's main limitation (restricted scope to homogeneous polynomials) is acknowledged by the authors and is a reasonable starting point for this novel approach. The fact that they can discover identities that are "too complex to be found manually or by exhaustive search" is a compelling strength.

The paper addresses the core challenge of combinatorial search in symbolic domains through a creative application of machine learning, which aligns well with NIPS' interest in novel machine learning approaches to challenging problems. The results are significant, and the framework has potential for broader application in mathematical discovery and symbolic computation.

**Overall score: Strong Accept**
