Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a framework to automatically discover computationally efficient identities equivalent to given target expressions. Expressions are generated via an attribute grammar whose rules encode algebraic operations along with complexity attributes. Candidate expressions are represented in two ways: (i) numerically, by evaluating expressions over randomly sampled inputs in a finite field Zp to obtain descriptors used for equality testing and for solving for linear combinations of trees via modular Gaussian elimination; and (ii) learned, by training a recursive neural network (RNN) to embed expression trees into continuous vectors, initially via a multi-class task that clusters equivalent expressions.

Because brute-force enumeration of grammar trees is exponential and quickly intractable, the paper learns search strategies from solutions to simpler targets. Two families of strategies are studied: n-gram counts of frequently occurring subtrees in past solutions, and an RNN-based policy that predicts the next grammar rule given embeddings of current partial branches. Experiments across five expression families—including matrix polynomial sums, elementwise variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, and two RBM-related exponential-sum families—show that learning-guided search significantly increases the rate of finding efficient identities compared to random exploration; n-grams often outperform the RNN policy on the studied families. The work is positioned relative to theorem proving, program induction, probabilistic programming, symbolic computation (Maple/Mathematica), attribute grammars for optimization, and NLP-style n-grams and recursive neural networks. The claimed novelty is in learning to guide the search for lower-complexity equivalents, and in continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation: given target T, search for S with S(V)=T(V) and lower complexity, with constraints ensuring finite search (homogeneous polynomials of degree k, scalar outputs).
- Well-specified framework unifying: attribute-grammar generation, fast equivalence checking via Zp descriptors, linear combinations across trees via modular linear algebra, and learned search policies.
- Thoughtful engineering for the RNN embedding (identity initialization, ReLU, curriculum, differential learning rates, input backprop, dropout), achieving low classification error on equivalence classes.
- Empirical evidence that learned strategies enable discovery for higher k where random search fails; concrete examples (e.g., reducing O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)); exploiting repetitive structure for powers).
- Open-source code and a catalog of discovered identities (supplementary), improving reproducibility and potential impact.

Weaknesses
- Scope limitations: single-variable, homogeneous polynomials and scalar outputs; a constrained grammar. The generality of the approach beyond these settings is not demonstrated.
- Correctness guarantees: equality is validated via evaluations over Zp on N random points; no formal bound on collision probability or failure probability is provided (e.g., via the Schwartz–Zippel lemma or multi-prime checks).
- Evaluation metrics: success is measured as the fraction of runs finding any identity within a fixed time, rather than wall-clock speedups in actual computation, the number of nodes explored, or guarantees of optimality/minimality of the discovered complexity.
- Baselines and comparisons: no direct comparison against CAS simplification plus cost-aware rewrites, compiler optimizations (e.g., common subexpression elimination, strength reduction), or algebraic complexity techniques; n-gram vs. RNN is mostly internal.
- RNN policy conditioning: it is not clearly stated how the target is incorporated into the decision of the next rule beyond training on solution trees from lower-degree instances. The policy appears to depend on the current partial branches but not explicitly on the target descriptor, which may limit target-specific guidance.
- Mixed performance on the hardest family ((RBM-2)k): no successes beyond k=5; learning does not outperform random in discovery rate, though it reduces runtime to first solution at k=5.
- Some design choices (e.g., removing matrix-multiply from the grammar to force O(n^2) solutions) may bias the search space and complicate claims about general efficiency discovery.

Quality
The components are technically sound and internally consistent. The linear-algebraic approach over Zp is standard and efficient; the RNN embedding methodology is plausible and achieves good classification performance. However, theoretical guarantees on correctness (collision/failure probability) and optimality are not provided. Empirical evaluation is careful in showing success rates across k and strategy types, but lacks head-to-head baselines against established systems and broader metrics (e.g., search cost, number of evaluated trees, real runtime savings for instantiated sizes).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized, with motivating examples (including explicit trees), clear descriptions of the grammar/search framework, and useful implementation details for training the RNN. Some clarifications would help: precisely how the target informs the policy at test time; the size of N and p and the resulting error bounds; and a more explicit listing of the grammar rules (Table 1 is referenced but not reproduced here).

Originality
The combination of attribute grammars with learned search (n-grams and an RNN) for discovering lower-complexity equivalent expressions appears novel, as does leveraging continuous embeddings for symbolic expression equivalence. Prior uses of grammars in optimization and RNNs in NLP are appropriately cited; related automated reasoning and CAS work are discussed but not directly comparable experimentally.

Significance
The ability to discover efficient identities automatically is potentially impactful for program synthesis, compiler optimization, and symbolic computation. The demonstrated identities and search improvements on the studied families are promising, though broader applicability and scalability remain to be established. The learned embedding of symbolic expressions may be useful beyond this task.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide formal error bounds for the Zp-based equivalence test, e.g., via Schwartz–Zippel, and consider multi-prime verification or occasional exact symbolic checks for confirmed solutions.
- Report additional metrics: number of expanded nodes/trees, time-to-first-solution distributions, and end-to-end runtime speedups on instantiated problem sizes.
- Compare against CAS with cost-aware rewrite strategies and compiler-style optimizations on the same families.
- Condition the policy explicitly on the target (e.g., concatenate target descriptor with branch embeddings) and explore stronger search (beam/MCTS) to handle non-repetitive structures.
- Include ablations for embedding dimension, N and p, curriculum, and dropout; and explore cross-family generalization.
- Discuss or demonstrate extension to multivariate, non-homogeneous, and non-polynomial operators.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated framework addressing a challenging combinatorial search problem with learning-guided strategies.
- Novel application of RNN embeddings to symbolic expressions with demonstrated utility.
- Empirical evidence of discovering nontrivial, efficiency-improving identities beyond brute-force capabilities.
- Relevance to ML practice (e.g., RBM-related sums) and potential integration with compiler/CAS tooling.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited scope and lack of theoretical guarantees on correctness and optimality.
- Evaluation lacks strong external baselines and broader metrics of search efficiency and practical runtime gains.
- Learning policy underperforms simple n-grams on key families and struggles on the hardest class.
- Target conditioning and generalization beyond studied families are insufficiently demonstrated.
