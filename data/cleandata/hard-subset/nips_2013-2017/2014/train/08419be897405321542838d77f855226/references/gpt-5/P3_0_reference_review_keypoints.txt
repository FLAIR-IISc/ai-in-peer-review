- Summary: Paper searches grammar trees of matrix/vector operations to find asymptotically cheaper expressions equivalent to a target, using learned search (n‑grams and an RNN) and randomized equivalence tests over Zp, with experiments on five expression families.

- Strength: Tackles a timely ML–symbolic computation problem with compiler and numerical-algebra relevance.

- Strength: Clear framework combining attribute grammars, mod‑p randomized equivalence descriptors, and learning-guided search.

- Strength: Practical mod‑p descriptor scales better than symbolic equality and enables fast equivalence testing.

- Strength: Novel RNN embeddings for expression trees with strong classification accuracy up to degree 6.

- Strength: Empirical evidence that learned policies expand tractable search space; n‑grams often capture repetitive algebraic motifs effectively.

- Strength: Public release of code and discovered identities improves reproducibility.

- Weakness: Correctness guarantees are underspecified; no quantified collision/failure bounds (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel) for randomized tests.

- Weakness: Unclear how modular solutions over Zp map to valid real/rational coefficients; no described rational reconstruction or symbolic verification.

- Weakness: Weak baselines — no comparisons to CAS (Maple/Mathematica/SymPy), equality saturation, superoptimization, or theorem provers/E‑graphs.

- Weakness: Removing matrix-multiplication rules for some families biases search and may exclude valid asymptotic improvements; justification and impact analysis missing.

- Weakness: RNN gives modest benefit; n‑grams outperform it in several families and failure modes/benefit conditions are not analyzed.

- Weakness: Claims of novel identities are not substantiated in the main text; need representative identities with proofs or mechanized verification.

- Weakness: Scalability limited on hardest (RBM‑2)k family; learning stalls beyond k=5 and lacks discussion or remedies.

- Quality: Methodologically sound but lacks theoretical correctness guarantees and bridging modular-to-real coefficient issues; complexity claims not fully rigorous.

- Clarity: Generally well written and organized, but key details (grammar, verification protocol, coefficient reconstruction, complexity accounting) are missing or in supplements.

- Originality: Novel combination of attribute grammars with learned guidance and RNN embeddings; mod‑p descriptor is a useful engineering contribution.

- Significance: Potentially moderate-to-high impact if correctness guarantees and scalability are addressed; current empirical gains are promising but limited.

- Suggestion: Provide probabilistic correctness analysis (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel) with concrete N and p choices to bound failure probability.

- Suggestion: Explain and implement lifting of modular coefficients to integer/rational coefficients and add symbolic/mechanized verification for reported identities.

- Suggestion: Include and compare to stronger baselines (CAS, equality saturation, superoptimization, E‑graphs).

- Suggestion: Explore stronger search strategies (beam search, MCTS) and combine policy with a value estimator.

- Suggestion: Analyze and ablate RNN vs n‑gram contributions; consider end-to-end policy training rather than classification pretraining.

- Suggestion: Remove or justify matrix-multiply restrictions and quantify their impact on discovered identities.

- Pros for acceptance: Addresses important problem, shows empirical improvements, code released, and introduces novel RNN embeddings.

- Cons for acceptance: Lacks formal verification and modular-to-real reconstruction, weak baselines, limited scalability, and unsubstantiated claims about new identities.

- Recommendation: Weak reject (borderline); would be strong with added correctness guarantees, stronger baselines, and clearer identity presentation.

- Confidence: Reviewer highly confident in assessment.
