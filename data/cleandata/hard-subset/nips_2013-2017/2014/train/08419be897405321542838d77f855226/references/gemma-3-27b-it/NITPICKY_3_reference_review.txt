This paper presents an interesting approach to discovering efficient mathematical identities using a grammar-based framework combined with machine learning. The core idea of leveraging learning to guide the search through the exponentially large space of possible expression trees is novel and potentially impactful. However, despite the promising premise, numerous shortcomings in the presentation, methodology, and experimental evaluation raise significant concerns.

The introduction sets a broad stage but lacks sufficient motivation for *why* machine learning is particularly well-suited to this problem compared to, for instance, symbolic manipulation techniques or more sophisticated theorem proving methods. The claim of discovering identities “too intricate to be discovered by human manipulation” feels unsupported without a more rigorous comparative analysis. 

The attribute grammar framework (Section 3) is presented without sufficient detail. Table 1, listing the grammar rules, is crucial, but lacks any discussion of their limitations or justification for their selection. Specifically, the choice to restrict the initial framework to homogeneous polynomials of degree *k* feels arbitrary and its consequences not fully explored. The explanation for the constraint limiting tree depth is weak and reads as a consequence of implementation rather than a principled design choice.

The representation of symbolic expressions (Section 4) is a key component, and both approaches (numerical and learned) suffer from issues. The numerical representation using integer-based descriptors modulo *p* is a sensible approach to avoid floating-point errors, but the choice of *p* and the size *N* of the descriptor are not justified.  The RNN-based approach (Section 4.2) is overly complex and suffers from a lack of clarity in its implementation details. The explanation of how the RNN evolves vectors along the grammar tree is convoluted and difficult to follow. The rationale for the specific activation functions, initialization schemes, and learning rate variations feels ad-hoc and lacks theoretical justification.  The statement that the dimensionality *l* was “selected by cross-validation” lacks specificity - what was cross-validated against?  Furthermore, dropout should be applied to weights and activations, not the input vector a, as described.

Section 5, describing the linear combinations of trees, is mathematically sound but again lacks sufficient detail regarding the computational complexity of solving the linear system, especially as the number of trees grows.

The search strategy section (Section 6) introduces n-gram and RNN-based approaches. The n-gram strategy is simplistic and its success is presented without a clear understanding of *why* it works – is it simply capturing common sub-expressions, or is something more nuanced happening? The RNN-based strategy suffers from the same lack of clarity as the RNN representation itself. The justification for using the softmax output as scores for the scheduler is weak.

The experimental setup (Section 7) is a major weakness. The chosen families of expressions feel contrived and lack real-world relevance. The evaluation metric (“fraction of successful runs”) is poorly defined and obscures the actual time required to find solutions.  Figure 3 is difficult to interpret and lacks error bars, making it impossible to assess the statistical significance of the results. The claim that the RNN consistently found solutions faster for the (RBM-2)k family is contradicted by the relatively poor performance overall. The conclusion that the 3-gram model outperforms the RNN feels misleading given the RNN’s complexity and the relatively simple nature of the 3-gram approach.  There is no comparison to baseline methods like exhaustive search or existing symbolic computation tools.  The claim that the identities discovered are “previously undiscovered” is unsubstantiated without a thorough literature review or comparison to established mathematical databases. 

Minor issues abound throughout the paper: inconsistent notation (e.g., use of both *n* and *k* to denote different parameters), grammatical errors, and a generally verbose writing style. Example 1 and 2 contain excessive formatting and poor typesetting.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty of applying machine learning to guide the search for mathematical identities.
*   Potential for discovering previously unknown identities.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Lack of theoretical justification for many methodological choices.
*   Poorly defined experimental setup and evaluation metrics.
*   Insufficiently detailed implementation descriptions.
*   Weak comparison to existing techniques.
*   Numerous minor flaws in presentation and writing.
*   Contrived experimental setup.
*   Unsubstantiated claims of novelty.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite a promising idea, the paper suffers from significant methodological and presentation flaws. The lack of rigor, detail, and clear evaluation makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the work. While the core concept is interesting, the execution is insufficient for publication in NIPS. I recommend **rejection**. Significant revisions addressing the points raised above would be necessary for reconsideration.
