This paper explores the application of machine learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities within a grammar-based framework. The core idea – leveraging learning to guide search within a symbolic space – is interesting and potentially impactful. However, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet cumulatively substantial, flaws in presentation, technical detail, and experimental rigor. While the contributions are listed as novel, many aspects feel incremental or lack sufficient justification.

The introduction establishes the motivation well, but the description of the attribute grammar framework feels rushed. The jump from brute-force enumeration to random search is acknowledged, but the limitations of the random search aren't adequately quantified before introducing the learning components. The claim that examples are “too complex to be found manually” requires stronger substantiation – what specific efforts were made to exhaust manual approaches?

The grammar itself (Table 1) is presented without sufficient explanation of design choices. Why these specific operations? Are there alternative grammars that might be more effective? The restriction to homogeneous polynomials, while acknowledged, feels somewhat arbitrary and its impact on generalizability isn't fully discussed.

Section 4, on representation of symbolic expressions, is particularly problematic. The numerical representation, while theoretically sound, feels overly complex. The justification for using a large prime number ‘p’ is weak – it merely avoids rounding errors, but introduces computational overhead. The learned representation using an RNN is more promising, but the details are excessively brief. The role of the initial weight initialization (“identity plus noise”) is mentioned as crucial, but lacks proper explanation or citation. The curriculum learning strategy is described, but the rationale behind the specific ordering of complexities isn't clear. The choice of dropout rate feels ad-hoc. The phrase "we backpropagate to the input also" is imprecise and should be reworded.

Section 5, on linear combinations of trees, is mathematically correct but lacks context. The connection between the integer descriptor and the original symbolic expressions feels tenuous and isn't clearly explained.

Section 6, detailing the search strategy, is a significant weakness. The descriptions of the n-gram and RNN strategies are superficial. The claim that n-grams outperform the RNN, despite the latter’s representational power, is surprising and requires deeper investigation. Why do simpler n-gram models (3-gram) perform better than the more sophisticated RNN? Is this due to overfitting, limitations in the RNN architecture, or something else? The hyperparameters for the n-gram models (n=1…5) are chosen without clear justification.

The experimental section (Section 7) is marred by a lack of detail. The choice of families of expressions feels arbitrary. What motivated these specific choices? The statement "we remove the matrix-multiply rule" to ensure efficiency feels like a constraint imposed *after* observing poor performance rather than a deliberate design choice. The cut-off time of 10 minutes is mentioned, but its impact on the results isn't adequately analyzed. Table 3, showing the search space size, is useful but lacks context – what is the practical implication of this exponential growth? The success rates reported in Fig. 3 are vague ("fraction of successful runs") and don’t provide enough information to draw meaningful conclusions. Reporting the *actual* number of successful runs and the standard deviation would be much more informative. The claim that the RNN finds the k=5 solution faster is mentioned but lacks statistical significance testing.

The discussion section reiterates the contributions but doesn't address the limitations of the work. The potential applications to machine learning (dropout, RBMs) are speculative and need stronger justification.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* **Lack of Rigor:** Numerous minor technical details are glossed over, and justifications for design choices are weak.
* **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** The experimental setup lacks crucial information, making it difficult to assess the validity of the results.
* **Superficial Analysis:** The analysis of the results is shallow and doesn't provide deep insights.
* **Incremental Contribution:** While the idea is interesting, the overall contribution feels incremental and doesn’t represent a significant advance in the field.
* **Presentation Issues:** The paper is poorly organized in places and lacks clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty of Approach:** The application of machine learning to symbolic identity discovery is a novel idea.
* **Potential Impact:** The approach could potentially lead to more efficient algorithms for certain mathematical computations.
* **Well-Defined Problem:** The problem is clearly defined and motivated.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the approach, the paper's numerous flaws outweigh its strengths. The lack of rigor, insufficient experimental detail, and superficial analysis undermine the credibility of the results. I recommend **rejection** at this time. The authors should address the comments above, provide more detailed explanations of their methods, and conduct a more thorough evaluation of their approach before resubmitting. A significantly revised version with more rigorous analysis and detailed experimental results might be reconsidered.
