- Proposes a framework combining attribute grammars and machine learning (n-gram, RNN) to discover equivalent expressions with lower computational complexity.  
- Uses an integer-based numerical descriptor (Zp) for efficient equivalence checking and a grammar to define the search space.  
- Empirically discovers identities that reduce complexity (e.g., O(n³) → O(n²)) for several polynomial expression families.  
- Methodology is clear and well-designed; RNN representation learning achieves ≤5% error on expression classification across degrees 3–6.  
- n-gram models reliably find repetitive patterns in simpler families; experiments are convincing for those domains.  
- Problem formulation is overly restrictive—assumes homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with single matrix/vector variable, limiting applicability.  
- Lacks theoretical analysis: no completeness or optimality guarantees and superficial explanation for why some models fail on complex patterns.  
- Related work discussion is weak and fails to clearly differentiate from prior learning-guided symbolic search (e.g., Bridge et al.).  
- Evaluation scope is limited to five expression families and provides insufficient evidence of practical utility (claims like a closed-form dropout are unsupported).  
- Overall: technically sound and a meaningful contribution within a narrow scope, but significance is constrained by scope, theory, and related-work gaps.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions; authors should address generalization paths, provide stronger theoretical analysis, clarify distinctions from prior work, and bolster practical applicability evidence.
