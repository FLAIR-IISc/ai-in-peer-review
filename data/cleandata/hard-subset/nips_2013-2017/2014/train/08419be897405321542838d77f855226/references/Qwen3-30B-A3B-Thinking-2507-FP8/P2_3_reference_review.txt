

# Review: "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The authors define a grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions and leverage learning techniques (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for efficient expression variants. The key insight is that training on solutions to simpler expressions enables discovery of more complex identities that would be intractable with brute-force search. The paper demonstrates success on several polynomial families, showing reductions in computational complexity from O(n³) to O(n²), with some identities previously undiscovered. The work connects attribute grammars (from compiler construction) with machine learning techniques from NLP, creating a novel approach to symbolic computation.

## Strengths

**Originality (High):** The combination of attribute grammars with machine learning for mathematical identity discovery is genuinely novel. While attribute grammars have been used in other domains (e.g., bioinformatics, music), this is the first application to mathematical identity discovery. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work in theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation engines. The application of recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of mathematical structures is particularly innovative and extends NLP techniques to a new domain.

**Quality (High):** The paper is technically sound with well-defined problem formulation and rigorous experimental validation. The grammar framework is clearly specified with rules that constrain the search space appropriately. The numerical representation using Zp avoids floating-point issues, and the learned representation with RNNs is well-justified and properly evaluated (Table 2 shows 95%+ accuracy). The experimental setup is thorough, with multiple search strategies tested across five expression families, and the authors properly acknowledge limitations (e.g., the RBM-2 family proves challenging).

**Significance (Moderate-High):** The discovered identities have clear potential applications in machine learning (e.g., for dropout or RBM partition function approximation). The framework could integrate with compilers or optimization tools to automatically replace inefficient computations. The paper demonstrates a promising approach to combinatorial search problems that could generalize to other domains with appropriate grammar extensions. While the scope is restricted to homogeneous polynomials, the core approach is generalizable.

## Weaknesses

**Scope Limitations (Moderate):** The paper focuses exclusively on homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with single matrix/vector inputs (Section 2). While the authors acknowledge this limitation (Section 8), the narrow scope reduces the perceived significance. The most complex family (RBM-2) isn't solved effectively, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how the approach would scale to more general mathematical domains (e.g., with trigonometric functions or recursion). The significance claims about applications to dropout and RBMs remain somewhat speculative.

**Experimental Coverage (Moderate):** While the paper shows results for five families, the most challenging family (RBM-2) is largely unsolved by their methods, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of why the learning approaches fail for this case. The paper also doesn't compare against alternative approaches beyond random search, such as heuristic-based methods or symbolic computation engines (e.g., Mathematica).

**Clarity (Moderate):** The paper is generally well-written, but some technical details could be clearer. The explanation of how the RNN learns the representation (Section 4.2) is somewhat dense, and the connection between the learned representation and the search strategy (Section 6.3) could be more explicit. The notation for the grammatical rules (e.g., "sum(A, 1)") is consistent but might require more explanation for readers unfamiliar with MATLAB-style syntax.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a genuinely novel contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The authors successfully demonstrate how learning can guide combinatorial search in a way that discovers previously unknown mathematical identities with significant computational benefits. While the scope is limited, the framework is general enough to potentially apply to broader mathematical domains with appropriate extensions.

The paper meets all NIPS quality standards: it's technically sound, well-validated, and clearly written. The originality is high, and the significance, while somewhat constrained by the problem scope, is meaningful for computational efficiency in machine learning. The authors have carefully positioned their work relative to related research and acknowledged limitations.

The main concern is the narrow scope of the problem, which limits the broader impact. However, the authors acknowledge this in Section 8 and suggest pathways for future work. The paper also demonstrates that their approach can solve problems that are "too complex to be found manually or by exhaustive search," which is a significant achievement.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance**. The paper presents a novel and well-executed approach that advances the state of the art in symbolic computation. While the scope is limited, the core methodology is generalizable, and the discovered identities have clear potential applications. The paper is technically strong, well-organized, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The minor weaknesses regarding scope and experimental coverage don't detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.

The paper's greatest strength is its successful integration of machine learning with symbolic computation to solve a problem that's intractable with traditional approaches. This approach could inspire similar work across other domains where combinatorial search is a bottleneck. The authors have made a solid contribution that advances the field in a meaningful way, and I believe it would be valuable to the NIPS community.
