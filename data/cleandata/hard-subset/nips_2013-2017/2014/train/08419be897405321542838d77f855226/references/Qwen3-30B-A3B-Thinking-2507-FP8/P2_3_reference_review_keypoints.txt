- Proposes a novel framework combining attribute grammars with ML (n-gram models and RNNs) to discover efficient mathematical identities.
- Represents symbolic expressions with a grammar and uses learned models to guide search for computationally cheaper variants.
- Key insight: training on solutions to simpler expressions enables discovery of complex identities infeasible for brute-force search.
- Demonstrates reductions in complexity for polynomial families (e.g., O(n^3) → O(n^2)) and finds some previously unknown identities.
- Connects compiler-style attribute grammars with NLP-inspired learning methods, extending RNNs to symbolic math structures.
- Technically sound formulation with clear grammar rules and careful treatment of numeric issues using Zp arithmetic.
- Learned RNN representation is effective (reported 95%+ accuracy) and is integrated into the search strategy.
- Thorough experimental setup across five expression families with multiple search strategies evaluated.
- Significance is moderate–high: clear potential applications in ML and compiler optimization, though scope is limited.
- Main scope limitation: focuses on homogeneous polynomials with single matrix/vector inputs, limiting broader impact.
- Experimental weakness: the hardest family (RBM-2) remains largely unsolved and lacks deep analysis of failure modes.
- Lacks comparisons to alternative heuristics or symbolic engines (e.g., Mathematica); current baselines are mainly random search.
- Clarity issues: RNN learning and its connection to search could be explained more clearly; some notation may confuse readers.
- Overall assessment: strong, novel, well-executed work deserving acceptance despite limited scope and some experimental gaps.
