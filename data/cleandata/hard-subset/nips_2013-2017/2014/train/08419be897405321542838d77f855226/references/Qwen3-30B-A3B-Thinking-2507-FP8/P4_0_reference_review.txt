

# Review: Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities

This paper introduces a framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars and machine learning. The authors propose representing symbolic expressions as trees derived from an attribute grammar, and then using machine learning techniques (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for efficient versions of target expressions. The key insight is that solutions to simpler expressions can train a search strategy for more complex ones, enabling discovery of identities that are too intricate for manual derivation or brute-force search.

The paper relates to several areas: theorem proving (e.g., [5,9,11]), program induction ([18,28]), and probabilistic programming ([12,20]). It distinguishes itself from prior work by using learning rather than hand-designed heuristics. The attribute grammar approach has been used in other domains ([7,10,19,26]), but the authors' application to discovering mathematical identities appears novel. The paper also draws connections to NLP techniques, particularly n-gram models and recursive neural networks ([8,15,16,22,23]), applying them to symbolic computation rather than natural language.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The problem statement is well-defined, with clear constraints on the restricted domain (homogeneous polynomials of degree k with a single variable). The authors provide a thorough methodology, including two representations for symbolic expressions (numerical and learned), a linear combination approach for complex expressions, and three search strategies. The experimental evaluation demonstrates clear improvements over random search, with the 3-gram model consistently outperforming random strategies for higher-degree polynomials. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the restricted domain and the fact that some families (e.g., (RBM-2)^k) remain challenging. The learned representation for symbolic expressions is validated through classification experiments (Table 2), showing low error rates (≤5%) across varying degrees.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The examples (Examples 1 and 2) effectively illustrate the approach, with concrete comparisons between naive and efficient expressions. The methodology is explained in sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The technical sections (e.g., 4.1 and 4.2) provide adequate explanation of the numerical and learned representations. The figures (particularly Figures 1 and 2) enhance understanding of the grammar trees and the RNN process. The paper makes good use of the supplementary material to provide additional identities and examples.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by combining attribute grammars with machine learning for symbolic identity discovery. While attribute grammars have been used in other domains ([7,10,19,26]), their application to discovering mathematical identities through learning is new. The use of recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of symbolic expressions is particularly innovative, extending the application of NLP techniques to a new domain. The authors claim to have discovered previously unknown identities (e.g., for the (sum(AA^T))^k family), which appears valid based on the examples provided.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a meaningful problem in computational efficiency, with potential applications to machine learning (e.g., optimizing dropout or RBM partition functions). The demonstrated reductions in computational complexity (e.g., from O(n^3) to O(n^2)) are significant for specific use cases. The framework could potentially be integrated with compilers or optimization tools. However, the restricted domain (single variable, homogeneous polynomials) limits the breadth of applications. While the authors mention potential extensions to broader mathematical domains, they do not provide evidence of the framework's applicability beyond their specific examples.

**Strengths:** 
- Novel integration of machine learning with attribute grammars for symbolic identity discovery
- Demonstrated success in discovering previously unknown identities with significant computational savings
- Well-executed experimental evaluation showing clear advantages of learning approaches over random search
- Clear explanation of the learned representation for symbolic expressions
- Practical potential for integration with optimization tools

**Weaknesses:**
- Restricted domain limits broader applicability; the paper doesn't sufficiently address how to extend to more general mathematical expressions
- Limited comparison with existing symbolic computation tools (Maple, Mathematica) in terms of efficiency or coverage
- The paper doesn't fully explore the limitations of their grammar rules and how they might prevent discovery of certain identities
- The significance claims for broader mathematical applications remain speculative without more evidence

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper presents a novel framework that successfully applies machine learning to a challenging symbolic computation problem. The technical approach is sound, the results are compelling, and the work has clear potential applications in computational efficiency for machine learning algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The restricted scope of the problem (single variable, homogeneous polynomials) limits the broader impact of the work. The paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how the approach would generalize to more complex mathematical domains or compare with existing symbolic computation tools.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with a novel approach to a meaningful problem. While the domain restriction is a limitation, the paper makes a clear contribution to the field by demonstrating how machine learning can be effectively integrated with symbolic computation. The results are significant within their scope, and the framework has potential for broader application with further development. The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, clarity, and technical soundness, though its significance is somewhat limited by the restricted domain.
