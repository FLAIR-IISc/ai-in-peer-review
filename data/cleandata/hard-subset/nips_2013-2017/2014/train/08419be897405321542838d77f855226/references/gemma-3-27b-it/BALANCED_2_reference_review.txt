This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities, leveraging attribute grammars and machine learning. The core idea is compelling: use learning to guide the search for simplified expressions within a formally defined grammar. This is a challenging problem, bridging symbolic manipulation with statistical learning, and the authors demonstrate promising results, uncovering identities that are non-trivial and previously unknown. The application of RNNs to learn representations of symbolic expressions is also a significant contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for identity discovery is a genuinely new approach.  Applying learning to *guide* the symbolic search, rather than attempting to directly learn the identity, is a clever strategy.
* **Technical Soundness:** The framework appears well-designed. The use of attribute grammars provides a formal basis for expression manipulation, and the proposed learning methods (n-grams and RNNs) are appropriately chosen and implemented. The use of modular arithmetic to address numerical issues in the descriptor-based matching is a good practical detail.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate that the learning-based strategies significantly outperform random search for moderately complex expressions. The discovery of previously unknown identities is a strong validation of the approach. Examples 1 & 2 are illustrative and convincing.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts. The examples help to understand the framework and its benefits. The notation is fairly standard.
* **Significance:** The potential impact is considerable.  Automated discovery of efficient mathematical identities could have applications in areas like compiler optimization, numerical computation, and even potentially within machine learning models themselves (e.g., simplifying expressions in neural networks).
* **Code Release:** The authors provide code and data, which is excellent and facilitates reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability:** While the results are good for the tested expressions, the scalability to *highly* complex identities remains unclear. The RBM-2 family results show the method struggles with very intricate expressions.  The exponential nature of the search space poses a fundamental challenge, and it’s not fully clear how the approach can be scaled to tackle even more complex problems. Further analysis of the computational cost of the learning and search phases would be beneficial.
* **Grammar Limitations:** The restricted setting (homogeneous polynomials of degree k with a single matrix variable) limits the applicability of the framework. The discussion of these restrictions in Section 2 is adequate, but exploring ways to relax them, even incrementally, would strengthen the work. What are the bottlenecks preventing generalization to more complex expression types?
* **RNN Performance:** While the RNN-based approach shows promise, particularly in terms of search speed in some cases, its overall performance appears to be overshadowed by the n-gram models. A deeper analysis of why the RNN isn’t performing as well as expected is warranted. Are the learned representations truly capturing relevant semantic information, or are the n-gram models simply exploiting local patterns more effectively?  The choice of dropout rate seems somewhat arbitrary; a more systematic exploration of hyperparameters might improve RNN performance.
* **Related Work Coverage:**  While the related work section covers relevant areas, it could be more exhaustive. For instance, the connection to automated theorem proving could be expanded, especially mentioning techniques like E-matching or superposition.  Also, recent work on neural symbolic machines (e.g., from DeepMind) could be relevant.
* **Evaluation Metrics:** The evaluation focuses on success/failure based on a time limit. While reasonable, reporting metrics like the average length of the discovered expression, or the reduction in the number of operations, could provide a more fine-grained assessment of the efficiency gains.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a novel and challenging problem.
* The proposed framework is technically sound and well-motivated.
* The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements over baseline methods.
* The discovered identities represent a genuine contribution to mathematical knowledge.
* The code release fosters reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scalability of the approach remains a concern.
* The RNN-based method doesn’t consistently outperform simpler approaches.
* The limited scope of the grammar restricts the applicability of the framework.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning is innovative, and the results are promising. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding scalability and the performance of the RNN, the contributions are substantial enough to warrant acceptance.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.

**Recommendations for Revision:**

1. **Scalability Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the framework as the complexity of the target expression increases. Explore potential strategies for addressing the exponential search space.
2. **RNN Analysis:** Investigate why the RNN-based approach isn't performing as well as the n-gram models. Experiment with different hyperparameters and network architectures.
3. **Grammar Generalization:** Discuss potential avenues for relaxing the restrictions on the grammar.
4. **Related Work:** Expand the related work section to include more recent and relevant publications, particularly in automated theorem proving and neural-symbolic computation.
5. **Evaluation Metrics:**  Consider adding metrics beyond success/failure to provide a more nuanced evaluation of the efficiency gains.
