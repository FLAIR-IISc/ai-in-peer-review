- Tackles discovering lower-cost equivalent expressions via attribute grammars and learned search.  
- Checks equivalence by randomized evaluations over Zp and forms identities as linear combinations solved mod p.  
- Learns n-gram subtree models and an RNN embedding policy from simpler targets to guide search.  
- Experiments on five families show learned policies (especially n-grams) outperform random search and find identities with large asymptotic gains.  
- Code and data are released.  
- Framework is coherent, but paper lacks quantified collision probabilities or formal guarantees for modular equivalence.  
- Unclear how modular coefficients are mapped to integer/rational coefficients and whether solutions hold over the intended domain.  
- RNN often underperforms n-grams; evaluation primarily measures success rate within a 10-minute cap.  
- Missing comparisons to CAS tools (Mathematica/Maple), no end-to-end runtime speedups on numeric instances, and no formal complexity proofs.  
- Claims of “previously undiscovered” identities are not substantiated or expert-verified.  
- Authors are transparent about limitations; more ablations (curriculum, dropout, descriptor length N, prime p) are needed.  
- Paper is generally clear and readable, though some crucial details (full grammar, coefficient interpretation) are deferred.  
- The framing and use of learned search policies are original; RNN embeddings for algebraic expressions are a novel contribution.  
- Potentially significant for automating algebraic optimization, but scope is limited (homogeneous single-variable polynomials, restricted grammar).  
- Pros: well-motivated framework, learned guidance improves search, useful RNN training insights, released code, and some substantial asymptotic improvements.  
- Cons: no formal equivalence guarantees, limited and possibly incomplete evaluation, RNN underperformance, restricted problem scope, and incomplete cost accounting for linear combinations/shared subexpressions.  
- Suggested improvements: provide Schwartz–Zippel-type bounds, detail mapping from Zp to rationals/integers and cost computation, add CAS and runtime baselines, perform more ablations, verify novelty, and explore richer grammars.  
- Recommendation: borderline, leaning weak accept—novel and promising but needs stronger guarantees and baselines.
