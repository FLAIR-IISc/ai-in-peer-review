**Summary**

This paper introduces a framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient, yet mathematically equivalent, identities for a given symbolic expression. The core idea is to define a search space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar, where each rule is a mathematical operation. Because this space is combinatorially large, brute-force or random search is infeasible for complex target expressions. The main contribution of the paper is to use machine learning to learn an effective search strategy. The authors train models (n-gram models and a recursive neural network) on the structure of solutions found for simpler expressions to guide the search for more complex ones. The equivalence between a candidate expression and the target is checked efficiently using a numerical representation based on evaluation over random integers modulo a prime. The authors demonstrate their framework on five families of expressions, including some related to the RBM partition function, successfully discovering novel identities that reduce computational complexity from exponential or high-order polynomial time to low-order polynomial time.

This work sits at an interesting intersection of symbolic computation, program synthesis, and machine learning. While symbolic engines like Mathematica can simplify expressions, they typically rely on fixed rule sets and do not learn to search for complexity reduction. This paper's novelty lies in applying modern ML techniques, particularly sequence/tree models borrowed from NLP, to the symbolic domain of mathematical reasoning to achieve a specific optimization goal.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The problem of learning to discover efficient mathematical identities is highly original and ambitious. Applying machine learning to guide symbolic search in this manner is a creative and promising direction. The potential impact is significant, with possible applications in optimizing scientific computing code, compiler design, and even discovering new, efficient ways to compute quantities in theoretical machine learning (as demonstrated by the RBM examples).

2.  **Novel Technical Contributions:** The paper presents several strong technical ideas:
    *   The overall framework combining an attribute grammar with a learned search strategy is elegant.
    *   The use of curriculum learning—solving simpler problems to generate training data for harder ones—is a very natural and effective fit for this problem.
    *   The proposal to learn a continuous, vector-space representation of symbolic expressions using a recursive neural network is a valuable contribution in its own right, potentially enabling a wide range of ML techniques to be applied to symbolic domains.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The framework is shown to discover non-trivial identities for several challenging expression families. The results in Figure 3 clearly demonstrate that the learned strategies (n-gram and RNN) significantly outperform a random search baseline, successfully finding solutions for high-degree polynomials where the baseline completely fails. The discovery of novel identities that reduce complexity from O(n^k) or O(2^n) to O(n^2) or O(n^3) is a powerful demonstration of the system's capabilities.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly motivated. The use of simple, illustrative examples in the introduction (Examples 1 & 2) is very effective at conveying the core problem and the structure of the solutions being sought. The overall organization is logical and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Analysis of Model Performance:** A surprising result is that the simpler n-gram models consistently outperform the more sophisticated Recursive Neural Network (RNN) model. The paper briefly suggests this is because solutions involve repetitive local patterns. This is a crucial finding that warrants deeper analysis. Why does the RNN, which is designed to capture compositional structure, fail to learn these patterns as effectively? Is it an issue with the architecture, the optimization procedure (e.g., vanishing/exploding gradients in deep trees), or the representational capacity? A more thorough discussion would strengthen the paper's conclusions about the suitability of different models for this task.

2.  **Reproducibility of the RNN Model:** While the overall idea of the RNN is clear, some implementation details are missing, which could hinder reproducibility. For example, how is the input to the final softmax layer constructed from the representations of the two branches, `phi(b1)` and `phi(b2)`? Is it a concatenation, a sum, or another learned combination? Clarifying details about the RNN's architecture and the training of the search policy would be beneficial.

3.  **Limited Scope and Discussion of Generalization:** The framework is evaluated on a restricted class of expressions: homogeneous polynomials of a single variable with a scalar output. The authors acknowledge this limitation. However, the paper would be stronger with a more detailed discussion of the key challenges involved in extending the framework to more general mathematics. For instance:
    *   How would the numerical equivalence check handle non-polynomial functions (e.g., trigonometric, exponential) or floating-point arithmetic where exact equality is problematic?
    *   How would the grammar and search need to be adapted to handle expressions with conditional logic, recursion, or non-scalar outputs (e.g., matrix identities)?

4.  **Tangibility of Discovered Identities:** The paper states that the discovered identities are listed in the supplementary material. While this is standard, including one or two of the most impressive and non-obvious *new* identities directly in the main paper (perhaps for the Symk or RBM-1 families) would make the significance of the results more tangible and impactful to the reader.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance**

*   **Pro:**
    *   Addresses a novel and important problem.
    *   The core idea of using ML to guide symbolic search is creative and well-executed.
    *   The paper demonstrates impressive empirical success, discovering new and useful mathematical identities.
    *   The learned continuous representation for symbolic expressions is a significant contribution on its own.

*   **Con:**
    *   The analysis of why simpler n-gram models outperform the RNN is underdeveloped.
    *   The framework's applicability is currently demonstrated on a relatively narrow class of mathematical expressions.
    *   Some implementation details for the RNN model are missing.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a high-quality, original, and thought-provoking paper that introduces a novel and promising research direction. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the problem and the impressive empirical demonstrations, far outweigh the weaknesses. The paper makes a significant contribution by showing how modern machine learning can be effectively leveraged to tackle complex symbolic reasoning and optimization problems. I strongly recommend acceptance. The suggestions above are intended to further strengthen what is already an excellent piece of work for a final version.
