Summary
The paper proposes a framework to automatically discover computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target expression. Expressions are generated via an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic operations; equality to the target is checked numerically using descriptors obtained by evaluating the expressions on random inputs modulo a large prime, and linear combinations of trees are found via modular Gaussian elimination. To guide search in the combinatorial space, the authors train n-gram models over successful subtrees and a recursive neural network (RNN) that embeds expression trees and predicts the next rule. Experiments on five families of expressions (including sums over matrix polynomials, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related series) suggest that learned strategies outperform random search and can recover identities with significantly reduced asymptotic cost. The paper also claims to learn continuous embeddings for symbolic math expressions.

Strengths
- Ambitious and interesting problem with potential impact at the intersection of program synthesis, algebraic simplification, and ML-guided search.
- Clear framing using attribute grammars; the linear-combination-of-trees trick is a useful extension beyond single-tree matches.
- Use of modular arithmetic descriptors for fast equivalence checking is practical and likely broadly useful.
- Learning-guided search (n-gram and RNN) is a reasonable and novel application of NLP-style models to symbolic math trees; curriculum training and identity initialization are well motivated.
- The idea of embedding symbolic expressions to reuse ML tools is compelling.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Double-blind violation: explicit GitHub URL and arXiv reference with author names. This alone warrants rejection under NeurIPS policy.
- Clarity and completeness: Several core elements are missing or underspecified.
  - Table 1 (the grammar and rule complexities) is referenced but absent; Fig. 1–3 are referenced but not provided; key experimental results are thus unverifiable.
  - Many notational/formatting errors: e.g., O(()n3), O(()n2), inconsistent use of k, k∗, φmaps (spacing), inconsistent punctuation/spaces around math (1 . . . C), footnote markers (†, ‡) but no consistent footnotes, etc.
  - Overloaded O to mean both output and big-O complexity; ambiguous in places.
- Example correctness and dimensional consistency:
  - Example 2 appears dimensionally inconsistent: the chain sum((((((sum(A,1)) * B) * A) * B) ... )',1) uses sum(A,1) (1×m) times B (m×p) → 1×p, then multiplies by A (n×m), etc., which does not type-check unless additional transposes are inserted. The paper as written has mismatched dimensions.
  - The family definition sum(sum((A*B)^k)) requires square products; with A∈Rn×m, B∈Rm×p, (A*B)^k is only defined when p=n; this constraint is not stated, and in Example 2 it seems violated.
- Evaluation methodology:
  - The primary metric is “fraction of successful runs within 10 minutes,” with the statement “any non-zero count is deemed a success.” This criterion is weak and does not quantify search efficiency, coverage, or robustness. No ablations on scheduler choice (greedy vs. beam), beam size, or time/solution trade-offs are reported.
  - Removing the matrix-multiply rule to force O(n^2) solutions biases the search space and success definition; it may exclude the true target and overstate “efficiency discovery” when a solution exists under this artificial restriction.
  - Lack of important baselines: no comparison to equality-saturation/e-graphs, superoptimization techniques (e.g., Massalin; STOKE), SMT/ILP formulations, or CAS systems (Maple/Mathematica) with user-specified cost models or rule search. Even a heuristic search (beam/MCTS/A*) baseline would strengthen claims.
  - For Symk, known dynamic programming evaluates in O(nk), much better than naive O(n^k). The paper does not acknowledge or compare to known algorithms (Stanley and many others), making “discoveries” on this family unclear.
  - For RBM sums, there are known combinatorial expansions for moments of sums of Bernoullis; no discussion of prior work or verification that identities are novel.
- Correctness guarantees:
  - The equivalence test uses random evaluations over Zp. No error bounds are provided on collision probability, choice of p, or N vs. polynomial degree and number of monomials. For linear combinations, the requirement “N must be greater than the number of linear equations” is vague; a reproducible specification and a high-probability correctness theorem are missing.
  - Claimed “discovery of many new identities” is not substantiated in the main paper; proofs are not given, and the supplementary material is referenced but not included for review.
- RNN modeling:
  - The architecture description is incomplete: precise parameter counts per operator, handling of arity/type (matrix vs. vector vs. scalar nodes), and how tensors interact with typed inputs are missing. The expression φ(S)=max((W3∗a)∗a,0) is dimensionally unclear.
  - The scheduler is greedy; no discussion of exploration vs. exploitation or backtracking beyond restarts. Overfitting is acknowledged; dropout over tree depth is only briefly sketched.
  - Reported classification accuracy (≤5% error) lacks table details, confidence intervals, or per-k breakdown; the dataset construction and split could induce leakage if structurally similar trees dominate both splits.
- Reproducibility:
  - Critical hyperparameters (p, N, learning rates, optimizer, batch sizes, curriculum schedule, M for linear combinations, restart policy) are not specified.
  - The main identities and complete experiments are deferred to the supplement; code link breaks anonymity.

Pros for acceptance
- Timely and creative attempt to bring ML-guided search to symbolic math optimization.
- Practical technique (mod-p descriptors + linear combinations) that others could adopt.
- The framing with grammars and learned strategies could inspire follow-up work.

Cons for acceptance
- Double-blind violation and substantial clarity/formatting issues.
- Dimensional inconsistencies and lack of formal correctness guarantees.
- Weak empirical evaluation (metrics, baselines, missing figures/tables) and reliance on supplements.
- Novelty relative to known algorithms (e.g., Symk) and related synthesis/superoptimization work not adequately addressed.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide a formal statement (with probability bounds) that your Zp descriptor equality implies true polynomial equivalence over R for your grammar, including linear combinations. Specify p and N as functions of k and n.
2. Clarify Example 2 with fully type-checked expressions and state required dimension constraints (e.g., p=n).
3. Provide Table 1 (grammar + complexity attributes) and all missing figures/tables. How is operation cost aggregated when combining trees?
4. Compare against baseline search methods (beam/MCTS/A*) and, where applicable, known algorithms (Symk dynamic programming), CAS with cost-guided rewriting, and superoptimization.
5. Detail the RNN architecture and training: operator embeddings, typing, optimizer/hyperparameters, beam size or backtracking, and ablations.
6. List the identities found in the main paper and provide symbolic proofs (or certified verification) for at least the representative cases.

Typos/format issues (non-exhaustive)
- O(()n3), O(()n2); “which help which the discovery”; φmaps; inconsistent spaces around math; missing/misplaced footnotes; inconsistent k/k∗; overloading O; many incomplete references (missing years/pages).

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is promising, but the submission has significant issues with anonymity, clarity, correctness, evaluation, and missing content. With careful revision, stronger baselines, formal guarantees, and complete, type-correct examples/proofs, this could become a strong paper. My decision is driven by the aggregate of many (fixable) issues rather than a single fatal technical flaw.
