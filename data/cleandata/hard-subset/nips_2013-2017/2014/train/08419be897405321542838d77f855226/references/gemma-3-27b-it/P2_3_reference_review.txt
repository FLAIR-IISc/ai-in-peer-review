## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

**Overall Assessment:** I believe this paper presents a novel and promising approach to a challenging problem – the automated discovery of mathematical identities. While the experimental results are somewhat limited in scope and the RNN approach doesn't outperform simpler methods consistently, the core idea of leveraging machine learning to guide the search within a grammar-based framework is strong and the demonstrated successes are significant. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions, contingent on addressing the clarity concerns detailed below.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of finding computationally efficient equivalent expressions for a given target mathematical expression. They achieve this by framing the problem within an attribute grammar, allowing exploration of possible transformations. Recognizing the exponential search space, they employ machine learning – n-gram models and a recursive neural network – to learn search strategies based on solutions to simpler problems.  The reported results include discovering identities that reduce the complexity of matrix operations (e.g., from O(n^3) to O(n^2)).

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, covering theorem proving, program induction, symbolic computation, and relevant NLP techniques (grammars, RNNs). The authors correctly position their work as distinct from existing rule-based symbolic computation systems by emphasizing the learning component.  

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for automated identity discovery is novel. Applying learning to *guide* the search within a symbolic manipulation framework is a clever idea.
* **Potential Significance:** The ability to automatically discover mathematical identities, especially those too complex for humans to find, is potentially very impactful. The examples provided demonstrate tangible improvements in computational complexity.
* **Technical Soundness:** The framework appears theoretically sound. The use of attribute grammars provides a well-defined search space. The numerical and learned representations of expressions are reasonable approaches to addressing the matching problem.
* **Code Release:** Providing the code and evaluation data is excellent and facilitates reproducibility and further research.
* **Clear Problem Statement:** The problem is clearly defined, and the limitations of the approach (e.g., restricting to homogeneous polynomials) are acknowledged.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Scope & Generalization:** While the examples are compelling, the experiments are limited to a few families of expressions. The RNN consistently *underperforms* the n-gram models, raising questions about its effectiveness in this domain. The reason for the n-gram model's superior performance, given the complexity of the search space, isn’t fully explored. It’s unclear how well these findings generalize to other types of mathematical expressions.
* **Clarity of RNN Implementation:** The description of the RNN implementation (Section 4.2) is dense and somewhat difficult to follow.  More detail on the network architecture (e.g., number of layers, hidden unit sizes) and training procedure would be helpful. The role of the dropout rate isn’t sufficiently justified.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While the authors acknowledge the exponential growth of the search space, a more thorough discussion of the limitations and potential scalability bottlenecks of the framework would be beneficial.  What prevents scaling this to even more complex expressions?
* **Lack of Comparison to Existing Tools:** The authors mention Mathematica and Maple but don't provide a direct comparison.  Demonstrating that their framework can find identities that these tools *cannot* would strengthen the claim of originality and significance.



**4. Evaluation of Criteria:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is generally technically sound, but the underwhelming RNN results and limited experimental scope slightly detract from the overall quality.
* **Clarity (6/10):** The core idea is clear, but the details of the RNN implementation and some of the experimental setup could be presented more clearly.
* **Originality (9/10):** The combination of grammars and ML for identity discovery is genuinely novel.
* **Significance (7/10):** The potential impact is significant, but the limited experimental results and the question of generalization temper the perceived significance.

**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel and promising approach to a challenging problem.
* Demonstrates successful discovery of efficient identities.
* Well-defined framework based on attribute grammars.
* Code and data are publicly available.

**Con:**

* Limited experimental scope and generalization.
* RNN underperforms n-gram models.
* Clarity issues in the description of the RNN.
* Lack of comparison to existing symbolic computation tools.

**6. Recommendations:**

* **Expand Experiments:** Conduct experiments on a wider range of mathematical expressions to assess the generalizability of the approach.
* **Analyze RNN Performance:** Investigate why the RNN underperforms the n-gram models.  Experiment with different RNN architectures and training parameters.
* **Improve Clarity:** Provide a more detailed and accessible explanation of the RNN implementation.
* **Benchmarking:**  Compare the performance of the framework to existing symbolic computation tools (Mathematica, Maple) on a set of benchmark problems.
* **Scalability Discussion:** Include a more thorough discussion of the limitations and scalability bottlenecks of the framework.



In conclusion, I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field.  Addressing the minor revisions outlined above will significantly enhance the clarity and impact of the work and justify a strong acceptance decision.
