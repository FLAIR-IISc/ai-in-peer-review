

# Peer Review: "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

## Summary and Context

This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars combined with machine learning. The authors address the problem of finding an expression S that computes the same result as a target expression T but with lower complexity. They introduce a grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions, develop a numerical descriptor for equivalence checking, and train n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) to guide the search for efficient expressions. The approach demonstrates success on specific families of polynomial expressions, discovering identities that reduce complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) or better.

The work connects to several areas: attribute grammars from compiler theory, symbolic computation (Maple/Mathematica), and NLP techniques applied to symbolic domains. While prior work has explored grammars for optimization [7, 19, 26] and machine learning for theorem proving [4], this paper makes a novel contribution by combining these approaches specifically for discovering mathematical identities with reduced computational complexity.

## Strengths

The paper's most significant strengths lie in its clear methodology and promising experimental results. The attribute grammar framework provides a well-defined search space, and the integer-based descriptor (Zp) for expression equivalence elegantly solves the symbolic equivalence problem without relying on potentially slow symbolic computation. The RNN representation learning (Section 4.2) is particularly impressive, achieving ≤5% error rates on expression classification across degrees 3-6 despite the combinatorial complexity.

The experimental results (Section 7.2) convincingly demonstrate the approach's effectiveness for specific problem families, particularly the first two families where n-gram models reliably discover patterns that repeat across degrees (as shown in Example 2). The discovery of new identities that reduce computational complexity from O(kn³) to O(kn²) represents a tangible contribution to computational mathematics.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

The paper's most significant limitation is its restrictive problem formulation. The assumption that targets are homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree k with a single matrix/vector variable (Section 2) severely limits applicability. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't sufficiently address how to extend the framework to more general mathematical expressions (e.g., involving trigonometric functions or recursion) or to multiple variables beyond the two-matrix example in Figure 1.

The paper also lacks sufficient theoretical analysis. The authors don't provide any guarantees about the completeness of their search or the optimality of discovered identities. The explanation for why n-gram models work well for repetitive patterns (first two families) but fail for more complex patterns (RBM-2 family) is superficial—this is a critical gap that needs addressing.

The related work section is problematic. The authors claim to be the first to apply learning to discover mathematical formulas using grammars, but they don't adequately distinguish their work from Bridge et al. [4] (Machine learning for first-order theorem proving). Both papers use learning to guide search in symbolic spaces, and the authors' approach is not clearly differentiated from Bridge's heuristic-based selection approach.

The evaluation is also limited in scope. The paper focuses on five specific families of expressions, but it doesn't demonstrate how widely applicable the discovered identities are to practical machine learning problems beyond the RBM examples mentioned in Section 8. The claim that these identities could "give a closed-form version of dropout" lacks supporting evidence.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a genuine contribution to the intersection of symbolic computation and machine learning. The framework is well-designed, the experiments are well-executed for the problem domain, and the discovered identities represent a meaningful advance for their specific scope.

However, the restrictive assumptions and limited scope significantly constrain the paper's significance. The approach is currently applicable to a narrow class of problems, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how to extend it to more general mathematical domains. The lack of theoretical grounding and insufficient differentiation from prior work on learning-guided search are also notable weaknesses.

While the work is novel within the specific domain it addresses, it doesn't represent a major leap forward in the broader field of machine learning or symbolic computation. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the limitations in scope, providing better theoretical analysis, and more clearly differentiating from related work.

## Recommendation

**Accept with minor revisions** (but borderline). The paper presents a technically sound and interesting approach to a specific problem in symbolic computation. The core methodology (grammar-based search guided by learning) is sound and well-executed. However, the authors need to:

1. Better address the limitations of their restrictive assumptions and discuss concrete paths for generalization
2. Provide more thorough theoretical analysis of the search process
3. Clarify how their approach differs from Bridge et al. [4] and other related work
4. Provide stronger evidence for the practical applicability of their discovered identities

The paper would be a stronger contribution to NIPS if it demonstrated broader applicability or provided more general insights about learning-guided search in combinatorial spaces, rather than focusing narrowly on a specific class of polynomial identities.
