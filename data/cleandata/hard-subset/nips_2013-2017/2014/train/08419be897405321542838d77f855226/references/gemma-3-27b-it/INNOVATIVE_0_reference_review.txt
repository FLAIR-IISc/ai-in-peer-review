This paper presents a novel and ambitious approach to discovering mathematical identities by framing the problem as a search within an attribute grammar, guided by machine learning. The core idea – leveraging learning to navigate a vast combinatorial space of symbolic expressions – is genuinely exciting and represents a significant departure from traditional methods. The reported successes, including finding identities that are non-trivial and previously unknown, are compelling. While the methodology isn't flawlessly polished, the sheer novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh its imperfections.

The paper is well-structured, and the problem statement is clear. The motivation – applying machine learning to symbolic reasoning – is strong. The choice of attribute grammars as the foundational framework is sensible, and the explanation is reasonably accessible. The exploration of both n-gram models and RNNs for guiding the search is commendable, demonstrating a willingness to experiment with different learning approaches. The experimental setup appears appropriate, and the inclusion of a code repository is excellent. The examples provided (e.g., reducing O(n³) to O(n²)) are concrete and persuasive.

However, the paper does have weaknesses. The explanation of the RNN architecture could be more detailed; the role of dropout and the initial weight initialization, while mentioned, feel a bit hand-wavy and could benefit from further justification. The comparison between the different search strategies (n-gram vs. RNN) isn’t entirely conclusive. While the n-gram models seem to perform better on some tasks, the RNN shows promise in more complex scenarios, and a more thorough analysis of *why* this is the case would be beneficial. The scope of the grammar is limited, which the authors acknowledge, but the potential for extending it to more general mathematical expressions isn’t fully explored. The experiments are largely focused on matrix operations, and it would be helpful to see how the framework performs on other types of symbolic expressions. The connection to related work, while present, could be strengthened – for instance, a deeper discussion of the relationship to automated theorem proving would be valuable. The choice of integer-based descriptors for numerical representation feels slightly ad-hoc, and a more rigorous justification of its advantages (beyond preventing floating-point issues) would be appreciated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High novelty:** The application of machine learning to automated identity discovery within a grammar framework is a genuinely new and promising direction.
*   **Significant results:** The discovery of previously unknown identities with computational benefits is a strong indicator of the approach’s potential.
*   **Bold approach:** The paper tackles a challenging problem with an innovative methodology.
*   **Open source code:** The availability of code facilitates reproducibility and further research.
*   **Potential impact:** The framework could have applications in compiler optimization, symbolic computation, and potentially machine learning itself.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Methodological imperfections:** The RNN architecture and the comparison of search strategies could be more thoroughly analyzed.
*   **Limited scope:** The current grammar restricts the types of expressions that can be handled.
*   **Lack of broader generalization:** The experiments are primarily focused on matrix operations.
*   **Somewhat ad-hoc choices:** The integer-based descriptors feel slightly arbitrary.



**Overall assessment:**

Despite its imperfections, this is a strong and exciting paper. The novelty of the approach, the significant results, and the potential impact outweigh the methodological weaknesses. While further refinement and generalization are needed, this work represents a substantial contribution to the field and deserves acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the RNN architecture and provide a more comprehensive comparison of the different search strategies. The authors should also briefly discuss potential avenues for extending the grammar to handle a wider range of mathematical expressions.



**Rating:** Accept (with minor revisions)
