This paper addresses the problem of discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The central idea is to represent expressions as trees derived from an attribute grammar and to search this space for an expression that is equivalent to a given target expression but has a lower computational complexity. The authors correctly identify that the search space of possible expression trees is combinatorially large, making brute-force or random search infeasible for non-trivial targets. The core contribution is the use of machine learning to guide this search. Specifically, the paper proposes training models on solutions found for simpler target expressions (e.g., those with a lower polynomial degree) to learn a search strategy for more complex ones.

Two main learning approaches are explored: an n-gram model that learns common sub-structures from successful expression trees, and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation for expressions. This learned representation is then used to predict the next grammar rule to apply in the search. To verify the equivalence of expressions, the paper uses a numerical representation where each expression is characterized by its output on a set of random inputs, with computations performed over a finite field to avoid numerical precision issues. The framework is demonstrated on five families of polynomial expressions, some of which are relevant to machine learning problems (e.g., related to RBM partition functions). The results show that the learning-guided search strategies significantly outperform random search and are able to discover complex, non-trivial identities that reduce computational complexity from exponential or O(n^3) to polynomial time (O(n^2) or O(n^3)). This work relates to prior efforts in automated theorem proving and program induction but distinguishes itself by its focus on computational efficiency and its novel application of statistical learning techniques, drawing parallels to methods used in natural language processing.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality:** The paper presents a highly original and creative approach to a challenging problem. Framing the discovery of mathematical identities as a learning-guided search over a grammar is a novel idea. The application of techniques from natural language processing, such as n-gram models and recursive neural networks, to the domain of symbolic mathematics is both insightful and effective. The development of a continuous, learned representation for mathematical expressions is a significant contribution in its own right.

2.  **Significance:** The potential impact of this work is substantial. The ability to automatically discover more efficient algorithms for mathematical computations could have broad applications in scientific computing, compiler optimization, and machine learning. The specific examples explored, such as terms related to the RBM partition function, suggest that this method could yield practical speedups for existing ML models. Beyond the specific identities discovered, the paper's core idea—using ML to navigate vast, discrete search spaces in formal systems—is a valuable contribution to the broader field of AI.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sound and well-executed. The use of numerical descriptors modulo a large prime for robust equality checking is a clever solution to a practical problem. The experimental design is solid, providing a clear comparison between the random baseline and the learning-based strategies across multiple problem families of increasing difficulty. The results convincingly demonstrate that learning from simpler instances enables the discovery of solutions for complex problems that are otherwise intractable. The incremental, curriculum-based training approach is well-motivated and sensible.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity:** While the paper is generally well-written, the connection between the two uses of the recursive neural network could be clarified. The RNN is first trained on a classification task to learn a general-purpose representation (Section 4.2), and then a new classification layer is trained on top of this frozen representation to guide the search (Section 6.3). A more explicit discussion of why a representation learned for equivalence classification is well-suited for guiding a search towards a *specific* target identity would strengthen the paper's narrative.

2.  **Scope of Experiments:** The experiments, while well-conducted, are confined to a specific class of problems: homogeneous polynomials of a single matrix or vector variable. The authors acknowledge this limitation but extending the evaluation to include expressions with multiple distinct variables (e.g., involving matrices A, B, and C) or non-homogeneous polynomials would provide stronger evidence for the framework's generality.

3.  **Analysis of Results:** The n-gram models consistently outperform the more sophisticated RNN model across most experiments. The paper offers a plausible explanation—that the solutions contain simple, repetitive local patterns. However, a more in-depth analysis of the RNN's failures would be beneficial. Is the performance gap due to the model architecture, the amount of training data, or a more fundamental mismatch between the RNN's modeling capabilities and the problem structure? Such an analysis would provide valuable insights for future work in this area. Furthermore, while the discovery of new identities is a key result, the paper relegates them to supplementary material. Including one or two of the more complex discovered identities in the main text, with a brief analysis, would make the achievement more concrete for the reader.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   Presents a novel and compelling synthesis of ideas from grammars, search, and machine learning applied to the domain of symbolic mathematics.
*   The problem of automated discovery of efficient computations is important and has high potential impact.
*   The experimental results are strong and clearly demonstrate the value of the proposed learning-based approach over a non-learning baseline.
*   The learned continuous representation for symbolic expressions is a valuable contribution that could enable further applications of ML in this area.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
*   The empirical evaluation is restricted to a relatively narrow class of expressions, which may limit the perceived generality of the approach.
*   The superior performance of simpler n-gram models over the RNN is not fully explored, leaving open questions about the best way to apply deep learning to this problem.
*   Claims of practical utility for the discovered identities remain speculative without a demonstration of their use in a real-world application.

Overall, the paper's strengths in originality, significance, and technical quality outweigh its weaknesses. It introduces a novel and promising research direction and provides a solid foundation for future work.
