This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars and machine learning. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions as grammar trees and then leverage learning to guide the search for more efficient equivalent expressions. The authors explore both n-gram models and recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to predict the next grammar rule to apply, effectively learning a search strategy. The results demonstrate the ability to discover identities that reduce computational complexity in several cases, often exceeding manual or exhaustive search capabilities.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a challenging problem at the intersection of symbolic computation and machine learning. The proposed approach is novel in its combination of attribute grammars with learned search strategies. The use of an RNN to learn a continuous representation of symbolic expressions is a significant contribution, potentially opening avenues for applying broader machine learning techniques to this domain. The empirical evaluation on several families of expressions demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach, particularly for expressions where simpler strategies fail. The clarity of the presentation is generally good, with clear explanations of the key concepts and techniques. The authors acknowledge limitations and discuss potential avenues for future work. The code and data availability is a strong positive aspect.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the experimental results are promising, the scope of the evaluation appears limited. The performance gains are most pronounced for certain expression families (e.g., those amenable to n-gram capture), while others (RBM-2) remain challenging. A more comprehensive evaluation across a wider range of expression types would strengthen the claims. The paper lacks a detailed analysis of *why* certain learning strategies succeed in specific cases and fail in others. A deeper understanding of the interplay between the grammar structure, the learning model, and the expression characteristics would be valuable. The ablation studies comparing different RNN configurations (dropout rates) are informative, but a more thorough exploration of hyperparameters and architectures would be beneficial. The choice of Matlab-style syntax, while clear to some, may limit accessibility for readers unfamiliar with the language. Although the authors mention restrictions on the scope of expressions (single matrix, homogeneous polynomials), the implications of these restrictions aren’t thoroughly discussed. How representative are these restrictions of broader mathematical problems?

**Strengths:**

*   Novel combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for identity discovery.
*   Effective use of RNNs to learn continuous representations of symbolic expressions.
*   Demonstrated ability to discover identities that reduce computational complexity.
*   Clear presentation and code availability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited scope of experimental evaluation.
*   Lack of detailed analysis of why certain strategies succeed or fail.
*   Insufficient hyperparameter exploration.
*   Restrictions on expression types are not fully discussed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*   The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods in certain cases.
*   The contribution of learning a continuous representation for symbolic expressions is noteworthy.
*   The code and data availability promote reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The scope of the evaluation is limited, and the generalizability of the results is uncertain.
*   The paper lacks a deeper analysis of the underlying mechanisms driving the success of the learned strategies.
*   The restrictions on the types of expressions considered may limit the practical impact of the work.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, the technical soundness, and the promising experimental results outweigh the identified weaknesses. While further research is needed to address the limitations and broaden the scope of the evaluation, the paper demonstrates a significant step forward in applying machine learning to symbolic computation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the comments regarding a broader evaluation and deeper analysis.
