

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars and machine learning techniques. The authors introduce a grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions and use n-gram models and recursive neural networks to guide the search for efficient expressions that compute the same result as the target but with lower complexity. The paper demonstrates their approach on several families of expressions, claiming significant complexity reductions.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an interesting problem at the intersection of symbolic computation and machine learning. The application of attribute grammars to mathematical identity discovery is novel, and the integration of learning techniques to guide the search space exploration is a promising direction. The experimental results for the simpler families (like the (∑AAT)^k and (∑(A.*A)AT)^k) are compelling, showing that their approach can discover identities that reduce complexity from O(kn^3) to O(kn^2). The paper also makes a reasonable effort to relate their work to related fields like theorem proving and program induction.

## Weaknesses

**Technical and Methodological Concerns:**
1. The paper lacks rigorous proof of identity equivalence. While they use integer-based descriptors to check equivalence, they don't provide sufficient justification for why this approach guarantees equivalence, especially given the complexity of the expressions. The paper claims "If the two match, then S(V) = T(V)," but this is only true if the numerical representation uniquely characterizes the symbolic expression, which the authors don't properly establish.

2. The paper claims to discover "previously undiscovered" identities but provides no proof of novelty. There's no comparison to existing mathematical literature or symbolic computation tools that might have already discovered these identities. For example, the symmetric polynomials (Symk) are well-studied [24], but the paper doesn't properly contextualize their findings relative to existing work.

3. The experimental evaluation lacks statistical rigor. The paper reports "fraction of successful runs" but doesn't provide standard deviations or confidence intervals for these results. The claim that "the 3-gram model reliably finds solutions" is unsubstantiated without proper statistical analysis.

4. The paper makes unsupported claims about the practical significance. For instance, they claim their approach could "give a closed-form version of dropout" but provide no evidence or analysis to support this claim. The computational savings in practice are not demonstrated through actual runtime measurements.

**Writing and Clarity Issues:**
1. The paper has numerous formatting inconsistencies. For example, "O ( n3 )" appears with a space before the parenthesis in some places but not others, and "O(n2)" is written without a space in others. These inconsistencies undermine the paper's professionalism.

2. The mathematical notation is inconsistent throughout. In Example 1, they write "sum(sum(A*B))" but in Example 2, they use "sum(sum((A*B)k))" with inconsistent parentheses and spacing.

3. The paper references "Fig. 1" multiple times but doesn't include the figure, making it impossible to properly assess some of their explanations. This is a significant clarity issue.

4. The paper states "the majority of these examples are too complex to be found manually or by exhaustive search" but provides no evidence or analysis to support this claim. They don't compare their search time to exhaustive search times or provide any complexity analysis of the search space.

**Originality and Significance:**
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from prior work. While they cite Bridge et al. [4] as using learning to select heuristics, they don't clearly explain how their approach differs in significant ways.

2. The claimed significance is overstated. The paper's framework is limited to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, which is a very restricted setting. The paper doesn't adequately discuss how this limitation affects the practical applicability of their approach to broader mathematical problems.

3. The paper claims to "discover many new mathematical identities," but the identities are only listed in supplementary material without proof of equivalence or novelty. The paper would be much stronger with a concise presentation of 2-3 key identities in the main text with formal proof of equivalence.

**Minor Issues:**
- The paper has numerous grammatical errors and typos (e.g., "eval-" in "naive eval-uation").
- The references are inconsistently formatted (some with page numbers, some without; some with conference names, some without).
- The RNN training details are insufficient (e.g., "we learned using a curriculum" but no details on the curriculum schedule).
- The paper claims "the framework is able to discover an efficient version" for Example 1 but doesn't provide the actual efficient expression in the main text.

## Recommendation

While the paper addresses an interesting problem and shows promise in a restricted setting, the methodological weaknesses, lack of rigorous proof of equivalence, and insufficient experimental validation significantly undermine the paper's contribution. The technical claims are not well-supported, the writing has numerous inconsistencies, and the significance of the work is overstated relative to the limited scope of the experiments.

The paper would be significantly improved with:
1. Rigorous proof of identity equivalence
2. Proper statistical analysis of experimental results
3. Clearer differentiation from prior work
4. More thorough discussion of limitations
5. Consistent formatting and notation

Given these substantial issues, I recommend rejection. The paper presents a potentially interesting idea but fails to provide sufficient technical rigor to support its claims. The minor flaws identified are not merely superficial; they affect the paper's core technical claims and the validity of its results.
