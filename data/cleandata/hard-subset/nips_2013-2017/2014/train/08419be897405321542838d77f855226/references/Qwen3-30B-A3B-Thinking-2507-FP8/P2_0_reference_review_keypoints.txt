- Novel framework: Uses attribute grammars plus ML to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities.  
- Learning-guided search: Employs n-gram models and recursive neural networks to guide expression search from simpler solutions.  
- Empirical success: Finds identities that reduce complexity (e.g., O(n³) to O(n²)) and outperforms random search across tested families.  
- RNN representation: Recursive neural networks achieve strong classification accuracy (≤5% error) for representing symbolic expressions.  
- Experimental design: Well-designed experiments across multiple expression families and degrees with statistical measures (10 runs).  
- Limitations acknowledged: Authors note difficulties with certain families (e.g., RBM-2) and discuss constraints.  
- Clarity and reproducibility: Paper is well-written, organized, and provides sufficient detail and supplementary material for reproduction.  
- Originality: Novel application of attribute grammars and RNNs to mathematical identity discovery, distinct from theorem proving/program induction.  
- Practical significance: Discovered identities have potential real-world impact for ML (e.g., dropout, RBM partition function) and broader combinatorial search tasks.  
- Strength — problem formulation: Identifies a meaningful, previously unaddressed problem for ML-guided identity discovery.  
- Strength — framework and results: Well-structured attribute grammar and strong empirical improvements over baseline searches.  
- Weakness — scope: Restricted to homogeneous polynomials with single matrix/vector input; limits broader applicability.  
- Weakness — theory gap: Lacks theoretical analysis explaining when/why the approach succeeds or fails (e.g., RBM-2).  
- Weakness — computational cost: Insufficient discussion of trade-offs between training cost and benefits of discovered identities.  
- Weakness — application integration: Underdeveloped discussion on how to practically integrate discovered identities into ML pipelines.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept for NIPS — technically sound, novel, and significant.  
- Suggested improvements: Expand practical application discussion, add theoretical analysis of learnability, clarify training vs. benefit trade-offs, and state limitations explicitly in the introduction.
