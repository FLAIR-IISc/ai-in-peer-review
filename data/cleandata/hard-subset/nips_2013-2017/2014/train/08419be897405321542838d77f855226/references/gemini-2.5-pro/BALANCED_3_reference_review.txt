This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar, where each rule is a mathematical operation and the attribute is its computational complexity. Finding an efficient identity is thus framed as a search problem over the trees generated by this grammar. As brute-force or random search is intractable for complex expressions, the authors' main contribution is to use machine learning to guide this search. Specifically, they train n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) on solutions found for simpler expressions (e.g., lower-degree polynomials) to predict promising search directions for more complex targets. The framework is demonstrated on several families of polynomial expressions, successfully discovering non-trivial and seemingly novel identities that offer significant speedups (e.g., reducing O(n^k) or O(2^n) complexity to low-order polynomial time).

The work connects to several fields. It is related to automated theorem proving and program induction, but it distinguishes itself by focusing specifically on optimizing for computational complexity rather than logical satisfiability or general program synthesis. The use of a grammar-based search is not new, but the application of modern machine learning techniques to guide this search in the domain of mathematical identities is a novel contribution. The methods themselves are inspired by natural language processing, particularly the use of n-gram models and recursive neural networks on tree structures (e.g., Socher et al., 2013), adapting them from linguistic to symbolic mathematical trees. The idea of learning a continuous embedding for symbolic expressions is a particularly interesting parallel to word embeddings in NLP.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a challenging and important problem: the automated discovery of efficient computational identities. This is a novel research direction that sits at a compelling intersection of machine learning, symbolic computation, and computer algebra. The potential impact is substantial, with possible applications in optimizing compilers, scientific computing libraries, and even accelerating computations within machine learning itself (as hinted by the RBM-related examples). The discovery of new, useful identities is a significant outcome on its own.

2.  **Elegant and Effective Approach:** The core idea of learning a search strategy from simpler, solved instances is both elegant and powerful. It is a practical application of curriculum learning to a complex combinatorial search problem. The adaptation of NLP techniques, particularly the recursive neural network for learning a continuous representation of symbolic expressions, is a clever and well-motivated choice that could open up symbolic mathematics to a wider range of machine learning tools.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough and convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the learned search strategies over a random baseline. The framework is shown to solve problems with a vast search space (Table 3) that would be impossible to explore exhaustively. The results on five different families of expressions, including those with exponential naive complexity, clearly validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The problem is clearly defined, the proposed framework is explained logically, and the experiments are easy to follow. The inclusion of concrete examples (Example 1 & 2) in the introduction is particularly effective at illustrating the problem and the nature of the discovered solutions.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Limited Scope of the Current Framework:** The experiments are restricted to homogeneous polynomials of a single variable with a scalar output. While this is a reasonable starting point for a novel research direction, the paper could be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the challenges and potential paths toward generalization. For instance, how would the framework handle heterogeneous polynomials, multiple variables, non-polynomial operations (trigonometric, exponential), or vector/matrix outputs? Expanding on the limitations mentioned in Section 8 would add valuable context for future work.

2.  **Analysis of Model Performance:** An interesting finding is that the simpler n-gram models often outperform the more complex RNN. The paper suggests this is because the solutions have repetitive local patterns. While plausible, a deeper analysis would be welcome. Could the authors provide more insight into why the RNN, which is designed to capture recursive structure, fails to model these patterns as effectively as n-grams? Perhaps visualizing the learned representations (e.g., with t-SNE) could reveal whether the model is learning a meaningful algebraic space. This would also strengthen the contribution regarding the learned continuous representation.

3.  **Baselines for Search Strategy:** The primary baseline is a random search strategy. While this effectively demonstrates that learning provides a benefit, the comparison could be more comprehensive. It would be interesting to discuss how the proposed method compares, at least conceptually, to other sophisticated search algorithms that do not involve learning, such as Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) or search guided by hand-crafted heuristics common in symbolic algebra systems. This would help to better situate the specific contribution of the *learning-based* guidance.

4.  **Clarification on the Linear Solver:** Section 5 describes using a linear solver to find combinations of trees that match the target. It is slightly unclear how the set of M trees used to form the matrix F is selected. Is it the set of all unique trees found so far in the search? If so, does the cost of the linear solve become a bottleneck as the search progresses and M grows large? A brief clarification on this process and its computational implications would be helpful.

**Decision:**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that is creative, technically sound, and opens up an exciting new avenue of research. The strengths—in particular, the novelty of the problem formulation and the elegance of the learning-based solution—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to the scope and potential for future extensions. The work is a perfect fit for NeurIPS, as it combines ideas from core machine learning with a significant new application domain. I strongly recommend acceptance.
