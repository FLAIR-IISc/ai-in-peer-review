

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper presents a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The authors frame the problem as finding an expression S that computes the same result as a target expression T but with lower computational complexity, using attribute grammars to define the search space. They introduce two key representations: a numerical descriptor for equivalence checking and a learned continuous representation via recursive neural networks. The main contribution is using learning (n-gram models and RNNs) to guide the search for efficient identities rather than relying on random or brute-force exploration.

**Strengths:**
1. The application of machine learning to discover mathematical identities is highly original and addresses an important gap in the literature. The examples demonstrating complexity reductions (e.g., O(n³) to O(n²)) are compelling and showcase the practical value of the approach.

2. The technical execution is strong, particularly the careful attention to training details for the RNN (curriculum learning, initialization, activation functions), resulting in excellent classification accuracy (≤5% error rate for k=3-6).

3. The paper is well-organized with clear problem definition, representation, and search strategy components. The examples (Examples 1 and 2) effectively illustrate the approach and its benefits.

4. The framework properly situates itself in the literature, distinguishing from related work in theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation. The discussion of the grammar's application to symbolic computation is particularly novel.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The problem scope is quite restricted (homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, single matrix/vector inputs), which limits the broader applicability of the framework. While acknowledged in Section 8, the paper doesn't sufficiently explore how to extend the approach to more general mathematical expressions.

2. The paper lacks theoretical analysis of why the learning approaches work, particularly the RNN. It would be valuable to provide insight into the inductive biases of the learned representations and how they relate to mathematical structure.

3. The comparison with other search strategies is limited to random search. A more comprehensive comparison with heuristic-based approaches (e.g., A* with carefully designed heuristics) would strengthen the case for the learning approach.

4. The computational complexity of the search strategies themselves is not well-analyzed. The paper mentions exponential search space growth but doesn't provide concrete analysis of how the learning approach mitigates this.

5. The paper doesn't sufficiently address the limitations of the grammar framework. For instance, it doesn't clearly identify what kinds of mathematical identities cannot be discovered with this approach, which is critical given the claim of "previously undiscovered" identities.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**
1. Expand the discussion of limitations and potential extensions. Specifically, address how the grammar could be extended to handle more general expressions (e.g., trigonometric functions, recursion) and what challenges this would introduce.

2. Provide more theoretical analysis of the learning approach. Why do n-gram models work well for repetitive structures (like Example 2) but fail for more complex patterns? What properties of the learned representations enable generalization?

3. Include comparisons with other search strategies beyond random search. For example, how does the approach compare to heuristic search methods used in symbolic computation or theorem proving?

4. Analyze the computational complexity of the search strategies more thoroughly. How does the learning phase scale with problem complexity? What is the trade-off between training time and search efficiency?

5. Provide a more detailed analysis of the discovered identities. How do the discovered identities compare to hand-crafted solutions for the same problems? Are there cases where the learned approach discovers identities that humans would not have found?

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a technically sound paper that makes a valuable contribution to the application of machine learning to symbolic computation. The framework is well-designed, the experimental results are convincing, and the approach shows clear promise for practical applications (e.g., efficient computation of dropout or RBM partition functions). While the restricted problem scope is a limitation, the core ideas are novel and well-executed.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the theoretical gaps, expanding the comparison with alternative approaches, and providing a more thorough analysis of the framework's limitations and potential extensions. With these improvements, the paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS.

Given the novelty of the approach, the technical soundness of the implementation, and the practical significance of the results, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper is at the right level of contribution for NIPS, though addressing the theoretical gaps would make it even stronger.
