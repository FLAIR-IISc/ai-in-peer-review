- Proposes a framework that uses attribute grammars and learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities.  
- Represents expressions as grammar trees and learns search strategies to find equivalent lower-complexity expressions.  
- Uses n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) trained on simpler expressions to guide search for complex identities.  
- Demonstrates large computational savings (e.g., O(n^3) → O(n^2)) across multiple expression families, finding many previously unknown identities.  
- Technically sound methodology with practical solutions like integer-based Zp descriptors to check equivalence robustly.  
- Thorough experimental design: multiple families, degrees k=2..15, and three search strategies tested under controlled 10-minute runs.  
- RNN representation is well-designed with curriculum learning, initialization, and dropout; achieves ≤5% error up to degree 6.  
- Authors transparently acknowledge limits (e.g., difficulty on (RBM-2)k family).  
- Empirical results show learning-based methods outperform random search for complex expressions (Figure 3).  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and reproducible; code and data are available on GitHub.  
- Effective figures (Fig.1–3) and clear presentation linking to related NLP techniques.  
- Novel application of ML to symbolic computation; attribute grammars and RNNs adapted innovatively for identity discovery.  
- Discovery of new identities that reduce complexity is a significant original contribution.  
- Work has substantial potential impact (compilers, optimization tools, ML algorithm efficiency).  
- Demonstrates broader promise of ML-guided combinatorial search in symbolic domains.  
- Strengths: novelty, careful methodology, strong empirical gains, clear writing, reproducibility, and practical potential.  
- Weaknesses: limited to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree k, reducing generality.  
- Weaknesses: lacks comparison to existing symbolic tools (Mathematica/Maple) for contextual benchmarking.  
- Weaknesses: limited theoretical analysis of why some patterns are easier to learn than others.  
- Weaknesses: real-world impact examples are somewhat hypothetical; more concrete use cases needed.  
- Weaknesses: (RBM-2)k family remains challenging without analysis of causes or fixes.  
- Recommendation: accept — technically sound, original, significant, and well-executed with minor addressable weaknesses.  
- Detailed suggestions: state scope limitations earlier, compare to symbolic tools, provide deeper learning dynamics analysis, give concrete practical examples, and analyze the (RBM-2)k difficulty.
