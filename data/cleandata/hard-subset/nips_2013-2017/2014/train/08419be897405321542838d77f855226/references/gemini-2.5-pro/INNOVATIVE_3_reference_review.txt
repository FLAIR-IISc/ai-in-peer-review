This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of mathematical identities. The core idea is to define a search space of symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar and then apply machine learning to guide the exploration of this space, which is intractable for brute-force or random search. The authors propose several learning-based search strategies, including n-gram models and, most notably, a recursive neural network (RNN). A key contribution is the method for learning a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions via an RNN, which is then used to predict the next grammar rule to apply in order to find a solution. The system is trained on solutions found for simpler expressions and is shown to successfully generalize to discover complex identities for higher-degree polynomials. Experiments are conducted on five families of expressions, including those related to the partition function of an RBM, demonstrating that learning-based search dramatically outperforms a random baseline and can uncover previously unknown, non-trivial identities.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Boldness:** The paper's central idea is highly original and ambitious. Applying machine learning, particularly deep learning models for structured data, to the problem of symbolic mathematical discovery is a bold step that pushes the boundaries of typical ML applications. This is not an incremental improvement on an existing method but rather the exploration of a fundamentally new direction. This is exactly the kind of high-risk, high-reward research that NIPS should champion.

2.  **Significant Conceptual Contribution:** The introduction of a learned, continuous representation for symbolic expressions is a powerful concept with potential impact far beyond this specific application. Much as word embeddings unlocked the application of deep learning to natural language, a robust method for embedding mathematical formulae could open up the vast and challenging domain of symbolic reasoning to a wide array of statistical learning techniques. The high classification accuracy achieved by the RNN on this task is a strong proof of concept for this idea.

3.  **Demonstrated Efficacy on a Hard Problem:** The authors don't just propose a framework; they show that it works on genuinely difficult problems. Discovering polynomial-time equivalents for expressions that are naively exponential to compute (e.g., the RBM families) is a very impressive result. The fact that the system discovers identities too complex for manual derivation or exhaustive search validates the entire approach.

4.  **Potential for High Impact:** The work has clear potential to be highly influential. The discovered identities themselves could be practically useful in speeding up computations in machine learning (e.g., approximations for partition functions). More broadly, the framework could be integrated into compilers for automatic code optimization or used as a tool for automated scientific discovery. It provides a compelling example of how machine learning can be used to navigate combinatorial search spaces, a core challenge in artificial intelligence.

**Weaknesses / Points for Consideration:**

1.  **Limited Scope:** The current framework is restricted to a relatively narrow class of expressions: homogeneous polynomials of a single matrix or vector variable. While this is a necessary simplification to make the problem tractable, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the hurdles involved in extending the grammar to include, for instance, trigonometric functions, conditional logic, or recursion, which would greatly expand its applicability.

2.  **Performance of RNN vs. n-grams:** It is interesting that the simpler n-gram models outperform the more sophisticated RNN on several of the tasks. This likely indicates that the solutions for those families possess a highly regular, repetitive structure that is easily captured by counting local subtrees. While this doesn't diminish the novelty of the RNN approach, it does suggest that the full power of its compositional representation may not have been realized on these specific problems. The RNN's stronger relative performance on the more complex RBM-2 family is encouraging, however.

3.  **Heuristic Nature of RNN Training:** The success of the RNN training appears to depend on a number of specific implementation choices (identity initialization, curriculum learning, differential learning rates). While these techniques are well-motivated, they highlight the fragility and difficulty of training such models on discrete, symbolic structures. This is not a critical flaw but an honest reflection of the problem's difficulty.

**Recommendation: Strong Accept**

This paper is an excellent example of creative, boundary-pushing research. It tackles a profound problem with a highly novel synthesis of ideas from formal grammars, symbolic computation, and deep learning. The central contributions—using learning to guide symbolic search and creating continuous representations for mathematical expressions—are significant and thought-provoking. While the methodology is not yet fully mature and the scope is currently limited, the work opens up an exciting new research direction with immense potential. The demonstrated success in discovering new, complex identities is a compelling proof of concept. The paper's novelty and potential impact far outweigh its current limitations. This is precisely the type of visionary work that should be showcased at NIPS.
