- Paper proposes a framework combining attribute grammars with learning (n-grams, RNNs) to discover lower-complexity symbolic identities for target expressions.  
- Key insight: training on simpler expressions helps guide search for complex identities and mitigates combinatorial explosion.  
- Novelty: first application of attribute grammars plus ML to discover mathematical identities and extension of continuous representations to symbolic math.  
- Technical contribution: recursive neural network learns meaningful continuous representations of symbolic expressions.  
- Concrete results: discovers identities that reduce complexity (e.g., from O(n^3) to O(n^2)) for several expression families.  
- Experimental validation: thorough experiments across families showing learning-based search (n-gram, RNN) outperforms random search on many tasks.  
- Presentation: paper is well-written, organized, and provides sufficient technical detail and illustrative figures for reproducibility.  
- Strength: framework is technically sound and opens possibilities for applying ML to symbolic computation.  
- Weakness: problem scope is narrow—focused on homogeneous polynomials of a single matrix/vector—limiting applicability.  
- Weakness: limited concrete comparison with existing symbolic computation systems (Maple, Mathematica).  
- Weakness: scalability issues—learning approaches fail to outperform random search on the hardest family ((RBM-2)^k) for k > 5.  
- Weakness: lacks theoretical analysis of complexity guarantees or conditions for method effectiveness.  
- Weakness: limited discussion/examples of broader practical applicability to ML problems (e.g., dropout, RBM partition functions).  
- Evaluation: quality, clarity, and originality are high; claims are well-supported by experiments despite acknowledged limitations.  
- Significance: contributions are meaningful for computational mathematics and ML, with potential broader impact via continuous symbolic representations.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel combination of techniques, demonstrated discovery of nontrivial identities, solid experiments, and promising continuous representations.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited scope, struggles on most complex cases, insufficient comparisons to symbolic tools, and missing theoretical analysis.  
- Recommendation: accept—paper is a strong, well-executed contribution that lays groundwork for future work.  
- Suggestions: add concrete comparisons with Maple/Mathematica, expand discussion on extending to general expressions, include theoretical analysis of when learning helps, and analyze why (RBM-2)^k is particularly challenging.
