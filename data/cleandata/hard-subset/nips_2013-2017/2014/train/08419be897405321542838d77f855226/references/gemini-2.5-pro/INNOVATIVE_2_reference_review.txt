This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to frame the discovery process as a search problem over a space of possible expressions, which is defined by an attribute grammar. Recognizing that this search space is combinatorially large and intractable for brute-force or random exploration, the authors' key contribution is to apply machine learning to learn an effective search strategy. The system is trained on solutions for simpler expressions to find identities for more complex targets. The authors propose two learning-based search strategies: an n-gram model over sub-expressions and, more ambitiously, a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions. This work builds on concepts from program induction and utilizes techniques inspired by natural language processing (n-grams, recursive networks, continuous embeddings) but applies them in the novel domain of symbolic mathematics, a significant departure from prior work in automated theorem proving or rule-based simplification engines like Mathematica.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its exceptional novelty and boldness. The proposal to use machine learning to guide the search for new mathematical knowledge is a truly exciting and ambitious direction. It tackles a fundamental AI problem—combinatorial search in a symbolic domain—with modern learning techniques, bridging a gap that has seen surprisingly little exploration. This is not an incremental advance but a foundational paper that could open up a new line of research at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation.

The conceptual contributions are significant:
1.  **A new paradigm for automated discovery:** The overall framework of grammar-based search guided by learned heuristics is powerful and elegant. It demonstrates that machine learning can be used not just for statistical pattern recognition but also for discovering exact, symbolic structures.
2.  **Learned representations for mathematics:** The idea of learning a continuous embedding for symbolic expressions using an RNN is a major contribution in its own right. Analogous to how word embeddings revolutionized NLP, this approach could unlock the application of a vast array of deep learning tools to symbolic domains, which have traditionally been resistant to them. The high accuracy achieved on the expression classification task provides strong evidence for the quality of this representation.
3.  **Concrete and useful results:** The authors don't just present a framework; they use it to discover new, non-trivial identities for complex expression families, including some related to the RBM partition function. This demonstrates the practical power of the approach and suggests its potential for real-world impact in speeding up scientific computations.

The paper provides a strong proof of concept. The experiments convincingly show that the learning-based strategies (both n-gram and RNN) dramatically outperform the random baseline, pushing the boundary of what can be discovered automatically. The success on expressions with exponential naive complexity is particularly impressive.

**Weaknesses**

While the core ideas are outstanding, the work is still in its early stages, and some aspects could be strengthened. These are not fatal flaws but rather opportunities for future work that do not detract from the paper's core contribution.

1.  **Limited Scope of the Grammar:** The framework is currently demonstrated on a relatively constrained problem class: homogeneous polynomials of a single variable that produce a scalar output. This was a necessary simplification to make the problem tractable, but it limits the immediate applicability of the discovered identities. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the path toward incorporating a richer set of operations (e.g., trigonometry, division, recursion) and handling more general expression types.
2.  **Mixed Performance of the RNN:** One of the most exciting technical contributions is the RNN for learning expression embeddings and guiding search. However, in the main identity-discovery experiments, the simpler n-gram models often outperform the RNN. The authors are transparent about this, which is commendable. This result does not diminish the value of the RNN as a representation learner, but it suggests that its full potential as a search heuristic has not yet been realized and that simpler models can be surprisingly effective for patterns with local, repetitive structure.
3.  **Generalization:** The system is evaluated on five specific families of expressions. While these are well-chosen to demonstrate the system's capabilities, it is unclear how well the learned strategies would generalize to novel expression types not seen during the curriculum training phase.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
*   The paper is exceptionally original and proposes a bold new research direction for machine learning. It is high-risk, high-reward research of the kind NIPS should actively encourage.
*   The potential impact is very high. This work could inspire a new subfield focused on ML for symbolic reasoning and automated scientific discovery. The learned symbolic representations could become a standard tool.
*   The paper provides a strong proof-of-concept with compelling empirical results, including the discovery of previously unknown mathematical identities.
*   It is well-written, clear, and does an excellent job of explaining a complex system.

**Arguments against Acceptance**
*   The methodological scope is somewhat narrow (restricted class of polynomials), which could be viewed as a limitation on the work's immediate impact.
*   The most sophisticated proposed model (RNN) is not consistently the best-performing one on the main task, which might raise questions about its practical utility for search compared to simpler n-gram models.

**Conclusion**

This is an outstanding paper that presents a highly novel and significant contribution. It tackles a difficult, important problem with a creative and powerful approach. While the methodology has limitations and is not yet perfected, the boldness of the vision and the strength of the initial results are more than sufficient compensation. This paper introduces a new way of thinking about the synergy between machine learning and symbolic mathematics and is highly likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
