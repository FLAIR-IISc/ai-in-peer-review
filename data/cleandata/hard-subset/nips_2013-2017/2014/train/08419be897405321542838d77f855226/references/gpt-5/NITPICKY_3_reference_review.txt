Summary
The paper proposes a framework to discover computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target expression. Expressions are generated via an attribute grammar; equality is checked using randomized numerical descriptors over Zp (mod prime), and more complex targets may require solving for linear combinations of trees via modular linear algebra. To mitigate combinatorial explosion, the authors learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets: (i) n-gram statistics over subtrees; (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees and, with a softmax head, predicts the next grammar rule. Experiments on five families of expressions (including sums of matrix polynomials, symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related expansions) show learned strategies outperform random search in many settings; n-grams generally outperform the RNN. The paper claims discovery of new identities and provides code.

Strengths
- Ambitious and interesting problem: learning to discover algebraic simplifications with potentially large computational savings.
- Clear articulation of the search bottleneck and a reasonable framework to inject learned guidance.
- The Zp-based evaluation avoids floating-point pitfalls; the idea to use an RNN to embed symbolic expressions is intriguing and of potential broader use.
- Public code and a dataset of enumerated expressions (if complete) improve reproducibility.
- Some examples (e.g., sum(sum(A*B))) are correct and practically relevant; reported savings from O(nm p) to O(n(m+p)).

Weaknesses
Quality (methodological soundness and empirical support)
- Equivalence checking is only probabilistically correct, yet claims like “uniquely characterizes T” are made without collision/error bounds. No derivation of failure probability as a function of N, p, and grammar size; no cross-modulus verification; no independent symbolic verification (e.g., CAS) is reported for discovered identities. For a paper whose core claim is correctness of identities, this is a major gap.
- Linear combinations of trees: solving Fw = t mod p yields coefficients in Zp, not necessarily small integers. The paper does not constrain w to an appropriate coefficient set (e.g., integers within a bounded range) nor does it address whether modular equality implies real-equality for the intended coefficient domain. Bit complexity and coefficient growth are ignored; complexity comparisons omit the cost of combining many trees with large coefficients.
- Inconsistent and sometimes incorrect dimensionality in Example 2: the provided chain sum(A,1)*B*A*B… is dimensionally invalid under A ∈ R^{n×m}, B ∈ R^{m×p}, unless additional constraints (e.g., p = n, m = n) are stated. The exponentiation (A*B)^k also implies square shapes but is not clarified.
- The attribute grammar is not fully specified in the main text (Table 1 and Fig. 1 are referenced but not described sufficiently here). Without the rule set and typing constraints, the approach is not reproducible. How are types enforced to prevent invalid trees during search?
- The learned search strategy uses a greedy scheduler; no beam search or exploration strategy is described. Given the combinatorial nature, a purely greedy policy is likely brittle; ablation showing beam size vs. performance is missing.
- The n-gram weighting 10^k is ad hoc; no smoothing/backoff details or justification are provided. Overfitting concerns are acknowledged but insufficiently analyzed.
- Experimental methodology: “success if any of 10 runs within 10 minutes” inflates success rates and obscures time-to-solution distributions. No wall-clock or sample-efficiency plots; no significance tests. The strongest learner (n-grams) often outperforms the RNN; yet the paper’s novelty hinges on the RNN.
- The failure of all methods on the most challenging family ((RBM-2)k beyond k=5) indicates limited scalability; conclusions about “dramatically widening complexity” feel overstated.

Clarity
- Numerous typographical/formatting issues impede readability and confidence: “O(()n3)”, errant spaces inside O(·), “which the discovery,” “1 . . . C,” “bk/2c,” “∑ n,pAB,” inconsistent use of primes and dimensions, and multiple dangling figure/table references. References are often incomplete or inconsistent (venues/years/pages missing).
- The constraints “A used exactly k times” and “output scalar” are stated, but not how the grammar enforces degree/typing during generation; the scheduler interface to the strategy is underspecified (e.g., handling of unary vs. binary ops, partial trees, and admissible next steps).
- The RNN section lacks critical details: exact parameter counts, optimizer settings, training set sizes per k, curriculum schedule, dropout schedule “toward the top of the tree,” and how negative examples are sampled for the next-rule predictor.

Originality
- Combining attribute grammars with learned guidance for algebraic identity discovery is novel in the ML community circa the cited timeframe. However, the empirically best-performing component (n-gram subtrees) is relatively simple and undercuts the claimed advantage of the RNN. Related work on program synthesis/induction is cited but comparisons are not attempted.

Significance
- Potentially impactful if correctness and scalability are addressed; could influence compiler algebraic optimization and symbolic ML. As presented, limits on scale, lack of formal validation, and modest empirical gains over naive baselines constrain significance.

Questions for authors
- Provide formal probabilistic guarantees for the Zp descriptor: bound the collision probability for two non-identical polynomials under your sampling scheme; justify choices of p and N; report verification across multiple primes.
- How are coefficients w constrained in linear combinations? Are the discovered identities accompanied by small integer coefficients in R? Do you post-validate with symbolic algebra?
- Specify the grammar completely, with types and complexity attributes, and clarify how invalid compositions are prevented during search.
- Resolve dimensional consistency in Example 2 and state the shape assumptions for powers (A*B)^k.
- Report time-to-discovery distributions, not only binary success. Include beam search and compare greedy vs. beam. Provide ablations for N, p, n-gram order/weighting, and embedding size l.
- Compare against a CAS-based rewrite+search baseline (e.g., using a limited set of rewrite rules); even if not learning-based, it is a relevant baseline.
- Provide some identities in the main paper and prove a subset formally.

Minor comments and formatting
- Numerous typos and spacing errors (examples above) require attention.
- References are incomplete; fix venues/years/pages and standardize style.
- Clarify notation for sum(A,1) vs. sum(A,2), transpose placement, and MATLAB semantics.
- Replace “uniquely characterizes” with a probabilistic statement and provide bounds.
- Avoid ambiguous symbols (e.g., using k both as degree and loop index).

Pros
- Interesting problem; clear potential impact.
- Creative application of learned embeddings to symbolic math.
- Public code; some compelling case studies.

Cons
- Lack of formal correctness guarantees and coefficient handling.
- Incomplete grammar/specification; reproducibility issues.
- Empirical methodology and baselines insufficient; RNN underperforms n-grams.
- Many clarity/formatting issues; dimensional inconsistencies.

Recommendation
Score: 4 (reject)
Confidence: 0.8

Rationale: Despite an appealing idea and some promising results, the submission contains enough methodological gaps (correctness, specification, evaluation rigor) and clarity issues to preclude acceptance at this stage. Strengthening formal validation, experimental methodology, and presentation would substantially improve the work.
