Summary
This paper tackles the automatic discovery of computationally efficient identities for symbolic expressions. The authors formulate the search space via an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic operations, and check semantic equivalence using compact integer-valued descriptors (evaluations mod a large prime) rather than full symbolic reasoning. To overcome the exponential search, they learn search policies from solutions to simpler targets: (i) n-gram statistics over operation subtrees and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees and, on top, a softmax policy to propose the next grammar rule. They also allow linear combinations of trees by solving a modular linear system to match a target descriptor. The system discovers identities that reduce asymptotic complexity (e.g., from O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)) and from O(n^3) to O(n^2)); they report families of expressions including matrix polynomials, elementwise products, symmetric polynomials, and two RBM-motivated sums. Learning-based search (especially higher-order n-grams) substantially outperforms random search as degree increases, and the RNN yields a low-error embedding for expression classification.

Strengths
- Novel problem framing: learning to guide search for algebraic identities that reduce computational complexity. This is an ambitious, bold direction connecting ML and symbolic mathematics.
- Clear, modular framework: grammar-based space, probabilistic guidance, fast equivalence check, and linear combination solver.
- Continuous embeddings of symbolic expressions via an RNN, with solid empirical classification accuracy; this representation is itself a compelling contribution enabling ML on symbolic structures.
- Demonstrated discoveries with nontrivial complexity reductions, some beyond brute force or manual derivation.
- Open-sourced code and data, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Correctness is not formally certified. Equivalence is tested via randomized modular evaluation; this is high-probability evidence but not a proof. For new identities, at least CAS verification or algebraic proofs would strengthen claims.
- Scope restrictions: homogeneous polynomials, primarily single-variable, fixed degree, and a relatively small rule set. Many interesting identities fall outside this space.
- The most challenging family (RBM-2) defeats all learned strategies beyond k=5, raising questions about generalization to genuinely hard combinatorial targets.
- The n-gram policy frequently outperforms the RNN policy; the paper does not fully analyze why, nor provide ablations that might improve neural guidance.
- Complexity gains are mostly argued in big-O terms; there is limited empirical benchmarking of wall-clock speedups, memory use, or constant factors versus standard libraries/compilers/CAS.
- Comparison to related search/optimization paradigms (e.g., superoptimization, rewrite-system-driven algebraic simplification) is limited; the paper emphasizes novelty but could better situate against these literatures.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a careful decomposition of the pipeline and well-explained training tricks for the RNN. The numerical descriptor approach is clever and practical, though it deserves a more explicit discussion of collision probability and how N and p are selected to make false positives vanishingly unlikely. The linear-combination step is neat but limited to linear combinations; discussion of when this is sufficient would help. Experimental methodology (time-budgeted runs, success rates over random seeds, curriculum across k) is reasonable, though additional baselines (e.g., guided tree search such as beam search/MCTS/A*) and runtime/accuracy tradeoff plots would be valuable.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The grammar and scheduler/strategy split are easy to follow. It would help to include the full grammar table and complexity attributes in the main text, provide more details on descriptor size and prime selection, and report actual discovered identities (at least a representative sample) in the main paper rather than largely in the supplement.

Originality
High. Learning to discover complexity-reducing identities through a learned search policy over an attribute grammar is new to my knowledge, as is the use of continuous expression embeddings in this context. While elements (grammars, n-grams, RNNs) are familiar, their integration for this purpose is fresh and interesting.

Significance
Promising. If extended, this line could influence compiler optimization, numerical code generation, and ML systems that currently rely on costly algebraic routines. The RNN-based embedding may spur further work on neural guidance for symbolic computation. The current empirical scope is limited, but the approach opens a direction rather than offering just an incremental tweak.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide bounds or empirical estimates on descriptor collision probability and guidance on choosing N, p relative to search size.
- Validate discovered identities with exact symbolic tools (e.g., CAS) in addition to modular checks, at least for representative cases claimed as novel.
- Analyze why n-grams outperform the RNN for many families; consider hybrid policies (e.g., mixture-of-experts, beam search with neural scores).
- Include runtime speedups on real numeric data and compare with CAS simplification and simple algebraic rewrite baselines.
- Discuss extending the grammar (multiple variables, non-homogeneous polynomials, traces, trigonometric/exponential functions) and termination criteria.

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, novel direction; bridges ML and symbolic math.
- Clear contributions: grammar framework, learned search, continuous embeddings.
- Demonstrated nontrivial identities and complexity reductions.
- Reproducible with released code/data.

Cons against acceptance
- Lack of formal verification of identities.
- Limited scope and mixed performance on the hardest family.
- Neural method not consistently superior to simpler n-grams.
- Limited empirical evaluation of practical speedups versus baselines.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning). The paper is not flawless, but the novelty and potential impact outweigh the imperfections, and the work opens an exciting research direction.

Confidence
High.
