- Proposal: framework that discovers computationally efficient symbolic identities by generating expressions with an attribute grammar, checking correctness via randomized evaluation in Zp, and assembling complex identities via a modular linear solver; learning (n-grams and RNN embeddings) guides search and improves success rates; code released.

- Novelty vs prior work: differs from theorem proving/CAS/program induction by focusing on fixed-degree homogeneous polynomials and learning-guided grammar search; RNN embeddings for algebraic trees and linear-combination framing are novel; should be compared to superoptimization and equality-saturation approaches.

- Soundness concern: pipeline is sensible but correctness is probabilistic — needs explicit collision probability bounds (Schwartz–Zippel style) and clarity on mapping modular coefficients back to exact identities.

- Methods quality: learning components (n-grams, RNN with curriculum, initialization, regularization) are appropriate and supported by ablations; RNN classification accuracy is strong for k ≤ 6.

- Evaluation limitations: main metric is fraction of successes within 10 minutes; paper needs explicit identity counts/complexity reductions in main text, stronger non-learning baselines, and empirical runtime measurements on concrete inputs, not just asymptotic claims.

- Scope limitations: restricted grammar, single-variable scalar outputs, removal of matrix-matrix multiply imposes a strong prior, and method relies on solved instances for training; struggles on hardest RBM cases.

- Clarity: generally well organized and readable with useful training details, but modular arithmetic/correctness discussion and several nontrivial identities are deferred to the supplement and should be moved/clarified.

- Originality: applying learned n-gram and RNN strategies to search algebraic identity space and framing identity discovery as linear combinations of grammar trees is original and connects symbolic math to representation learning.

- Significance: promising direction for ML-for-systems, compilers, and CAS if broadened and made robust; current scope is limited but results show meaningful expansion of the tractable frontier.

- Pros: clear, well-motivated framework; novel continuous embeddings of algebraic trees; learned strategies (even simple n-grams) substantially outperform random search; public code and identities.

- Cons: correctness is probabilistic with no formal guarantees; evaluation lacks head-to-head baselines and concrete speedup measurements; restrictive problem scope; limited analysis of search and sample complexity.

- Suggestions: provide formal collision bounds and final certification via multiple primes or exact arithmetic; compare to rewrite-based/CAS baselines with cost models; report concrete runtime speedups and include representative identities in the main paper; investigate sparsity/regularization in the linear solver and clarify modular-to-exact coefficient mapping.

- Overall assessment: creative and timely work that convincingly demonstrates learning-guided identity discovery in a constrained setting; leans toward acceptance pending clearer correctness guarantees and stronger comparisons.
