

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars and machine learning. The authors propose a grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions and demonstrate how machine learning techniques (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) can guide the search for efficient expressions that compute the same result as a target but with lower complexity. The approach learns search strategies from solutions to simpler expressions, enabling the discovery of complex identities that would be intractable for brute-force enumeration. The paper demonstrates significant improvements over random search strategies, showing reductions from O(n³) to O(n²) complexity for certain expressions, with examples provided for multiple expression families.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with well-defined problem formulation, clear methodology, and thorough experimental evaluation. The attribute grammar framework is appropriately designed for the restricted problem domain (homogeneous polynomials with single matrix/vector variables), and the numerical representation with integer-based descriptors effectively addresses the equivalence checking problem. The learned representation using recursive neural networks is carefully developed with attention to key training details (identity initialization, rectified linear units, curriculum learning) that lead to strong classification performance (≤5% error rate on expression classification). The experiments are well-designed, with clear metrics (success rate within 10-minute time limit) across multiple expression families and increasingly complex target expressions (k=2 to k=15). The paper demonstrates that learning-based search strategies significantly outperform random search for complex expressions, with the 3-gram, 4-gram, and 5-gram models showing particularly strong performance.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement is precise, the mathematical notation is consistent, and the examples (particularly Examples 1 and 2) effectively illustrate the approach. The technical details of the grammar, representations, and search strategies are explained clearly. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results, including the use of integer-based descriptors to avoid numerical issues, the RNN architecture with key training considerations, and the linear combination approach for handling complex expressions. The supplementary material (referenced but not included in the submission) appears to contain the discovered identities, which would be valuable for reproduction.

**Originality:** The paper makes several novel contributions: (1) a grammar framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities, (2) the application of machine learning (n-gram models and RNNs) to guide the search within this grammar, (3) a novel application of recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of mathematical structures, and (4) the discovery of new mathematical identities with significant computational complexity improvements. While attribute grammars have been used in other domains (e.g., compiler construction, bioinformatics), the application to discovering mathematical identities with learning-based search strategies is novel. The paper properly contextualizes this work against related areas including theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation engines (Maple, Mathematica), noting their limitations in using learning approaches for this specific problem.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a meaningful problem with practical implications for computational mathematics and machine learning. The discovered identities could potentially optimize computations in machine learning algorithms (e.g., for dropout or RBM partition functions), and the automatic nature of the system lends itself to integration with compilers. The continuous representation of symbolic expressions opens the door to applying many machine learning techniques to symbolic domains, which has broad potential. The demonstrated improvements over brute-force search for complex expressions represent a significant advancement in the domain of symbolic computation.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a well-motivated problem with clear technical contributions. The framework is technically sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the code/data are publicly available. The learned representation of symbolic expressions is particularly noteworthy as a potential bridge between symbolic and statistical approaches.

**Weaknesses:** The problem scope is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of a single matrix/vector, which limits the generalizability of the approach. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the learned search strategies generalize beyond the specific expression families studied. The theoretical justification for why the learning approaches work is limited to empirical results rather than formal analysis. While the paper acknowledges the restricted domain in Section 8, the significance claims could be tempered with more discussion of the limitations.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:** The paper presents a novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation with clear technical contributions. The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements over baseline methods. The discovered identities represent a tangible advance in computational efficiency for specific mathematical problems. The learned representation of symbolic expressions has broader potential applications beyond the specific problem studied.

**Con-acceptance arguments:** The restricted problem domain (single matrix/vector homogeneous polynomials) limits the immediate applicability of the approach. The theoretical justification for the learning approach is limited to empirical results. The paper could provide more context on prior work in the specific mathematical domains of the discovered identities to better establish novelty claims.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the application of machine learning in symbolic domains. The technical soundness, clear presentation, and meaningful results outweigh the limitations of the restricted problem scope. The paper advances the state of the art in discovering efficient mathematical identities and opens new possibilities for applying machine learning to symbolic computation.
