Summary
The paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target symbolic expression. Expressions are constructed via an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic operations; efficiency is measured via the grammar’s complexity attributes. Because exhaustive enumeration of grammar trees is infeasible for higher-degree polynomials, the authors learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets. They explore (i) n-gram statistics over subtrees and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions and a softmax policy predicting the next grammar rule. Expressions (and linear combinations thereof) are validated for equivalence via randomized evaluation over integers modulo a large prime (a form of numerical identity testing). Experiments show that learning-based strategies dramatically improve success rates and search times, discovering identities that reduce complexity from O(n3) to O(n2) (or from exponential to polynomial) on several expression families. The authors release code and data.

Relation to prior work: The setup relates to theorem proving, program induction, probabilistic programming, NLP grammar models, and recursive networks for structured inputs. Unlike symbolic algebra systems (e.g., Maple/Mathematica), the focus is on complexity reduction rather than algebraic simplification, and the search is guided by learned heuristics rather than hand-crafted rules. The work is also connected to randomized polynomial identity testing and to compiler optimization (algebraic rewrites), though these links could be deepened.

Strengths
- Novel problem framing: learning to guide discovery of efficient identities within a constrained grammar; clear potential impact for automatic algebraic optimization.
- Effective use of learning to combat combinatorial explosion; substantial empirical gains over random search; n-grams and RNN both help.
- Interesting and generally well-explained RNN representation for symbolic expressions; careful training details (identity init, curriculum, ReLU, dropout).
- The linear-combination-of-trees mechanism pragmatically expands expressivity.
- Public release of code/data enhances reproducibility and community utility.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical guarantees are underdeveloped. The paper relies on randomized finite-field evaluation to certify equivalence but does not analyze collision probability nor cite standard PIT guarantees (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel). Likewise, no guarantees are given that linear combinations over Zp correspond to identities over reals with small integer coefficients.
- Claims of “previously undiscovered” identities are not substantiated with proofs or literature checks; no symbolic verification is reported.
- Evaluation focuses on success rates vs time, with limited ablations and little end-to-end runtime speedups on actual numeric problems.
- The grammar is somewhat ad hoc and restricted; removing matrix-matrix multiply to force O(n2) solutions may bias outcomes. How well does this generalize beyond chosen families?
- The RNN often underperforms n-grams; the benefits of learned embeddings over simpler statistics are not convincingly demonstrated.
- Important implementation details are scattered (e.g., exact grammar rules, choice of prime p and descriptor length N, solver constraints on coefficients).

Quality
- Technically sound overall as an empirical framework, but key components need more rigor:
  - Identity testing: provide bounds on failure probability as a function of p and N; cite PIT. Clarify that integer weights returned by the linear solver yield valid real-field identities; constrain coefficients (e.g., small integers) or post-validate symbolically.
  - Complexity accounting: when using linear combinations, discuss cost of summations and scalar multiplications; report actual asymptotics and constants.
- Experiments are suggestive but not comprehensive. Please add:
  - Symbolic or formal verification on discovered identities (or an automated proof tactic).
  - End-to-end wall-clock speedups on representative numerical tasks for varying n.
  - Ablations: effect of curriculum, dropout rate, embedding size; comparison to alternative search methods (e.g., MCTS or beam search without learning).

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with instructive examples (e.g., Example 1–2). However:
  - Provide the full grammar (Table 1) and complexity attributes in the main text.
  - Precisely specify the scheduler, stopping criteria, and branching policy.
  - Detail the finite-field parameters (p, N), thresholding/tolerance, and how modular solutions are mapped to real coefficients.
  - Explicitly state when matrix-matrix multiply is disallowed and why that does not exclude valid efficient identities.

Originality
- The combination of attribute grammars, learned search heuristics, and continuous expression embeddings for complexity-driven identity discovery is novel. Related work in theorem proving/program synthesis is acknowledged; more discussion of randomized PIT and compiler algebraic optimizations would clarify distinctiveness.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for symbolic-numeric computation, compiler backends, and ML model efficiency (e.g., RBM partition functions, dropout-like expansions). The current scope is limited to specific families and degrees; stronger evidence of generality and practicality would raise significance.

Questions for the authors
1. What are the choices of p and N, and the resulting probability of false identity per Schwartz–Zippel? Do you perform any final symbolic verification?
2. How do you constrain or interpret coefficients from Fw = t mod p to ensure small-integer identities over reals?
3. What are the exact grammar rules and their attributed complexities? Does the grammar include addition/scalar multiplication explicitly?
4. Can you report wall-clock speedups for representative sizes n,m,p, and compare constant factors?
5. Why does the RNN underperform n-grams in several families? Any analysis of the learned embeddings?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a PIT analysis and/or symbolic verification step; cite Schwartz–Zippel.
- Report end-to-end runtime and memory improvements on concrete numerical tasks.
- Include full grammar and complexity definitions; discuss generalization to richer operators.
- Provide discovered identities and proofs in the main paper for at least a few nontrivial cases.
- Explore stronger search strategies (beam/MCTS), better smoothing for n-grams, and hybrid models leveraging both.

Pros
- Novel and timely problem; neat integration of learning and symbolic computation.
- Clear empirical gains on difficult search tasks; open-sourced artifacts.
- Insightful RNN treatment of symbolic expressions.

Cons
- Limited theoretical guarantees; potential for false positives.
- Evaluation lacks end-to-end performance and broader baselines.
- RNN benefits not consistently demonstrated over simple n-grams.

Recommendation: Weak accept (6/10). Innovative idea with promising results and useful tools; would benefit from stronger theoretical grounding and broader empirical validation.

Reviewer confidence: Moderate.
