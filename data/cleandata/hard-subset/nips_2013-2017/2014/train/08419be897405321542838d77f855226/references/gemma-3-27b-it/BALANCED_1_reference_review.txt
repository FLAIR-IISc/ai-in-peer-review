This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities, leveraging attribute grammars and machine learning to navigate the search space of possible expressions. The core idea – using learning to guide the exploration of a grammar-defined space of symbolic expressions – is interesting and potentially impactful, bridging the gap between traditional symbolic computation and modern machine learning techniques. The authors demonstrate the framework’s ability to find identities that are difficult or impossible to discover manually, achieving computational speedups in several examples.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for this specific problem is a genuinely novel approach. The application of RNNs to learn representations of symbolic expressions is also a noteworthy contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The framework appears to be well-defined, with a clear problem statement, a formally described grammar, and a detailed explanation of the learning methods. The use of integer-based descriptors to mitigate floating-point issues is a practical and sensible design choice.
*   **Empirical Results:** The authors provide compelling evidence of their framework’s effectiveness, demonstrating significant speedups in several example cases. The results clearly show that the learning-guided search outperforms a random strategy.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The examples are helpful for understanding the concepts, and the notation is reasonably clear.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors have made their code and evaluation data publicly available, which is a major strength and facilitates reproducibility.
*   **Significance:** The ability to automatically discover mathematical identities has potential applications in a variety of fields, including compiler optimization, scientific computing, and potentially even the automated discovery of algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability:** While the framework demonstrates success on the presented examples, its scalability to more complex expressions and larger problem domains is not fully established. The authors acknowledge limitations in the (RBM-2)k family. The exponential growth of the search space remains a fundamental challenge.
*   **Grammar Limitations:** The current grammar, while sufficient for the explored examples, may be too restrictive for broader applications. The assumption of homogeneous polynomials of degree *k* limits the types of identities that can be discovered. Expanding the grammar would increase complexity but could also unlock new possibilities.
*   **n-gram Performance:** The n-gram models consistently outperform the RNN, which is somewhat surprising given the RNN’s potential to capture more complex relationships. The authors speculate about this, but a deeper investigation into the reasons for this discrepancy would be valuable.  The limited dropout rates tested might be a factor here, as well as the RNN architecture itself.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper lacks detail regarding the hyperparameter tuning process, particularly for the RNN. Providing more information on the optimization process would strengthen the reproducibility and credibility of the results.
*   **Related Work Coverage:** While the related work section is reasonably comprehensive, it could benefit from a more thorough discussion of techniques for symbolic regression and automated theorem proving, particularly recent advances in neural-symbolic methods. The connection to techniques like Evolving Algorithms or Genetic Programming could also be explored.
*   **Presentation of Identities:** The discovered identities are largely presented in the supplementary material. Including a more substantial discussion of these identities within the main paper could enhance the impact of the work. Specifically, relating these identities to known properties or providing a broader context.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the framework in discovering efficient mathematical identities.
*   The code and data are publicly available, promoting reproducibility.
*   The work has the potential to advance the field of symbolic computation and inspire new research in related areas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The scalability of the framework remains a concern.
*   The reliance on a specific grammar limits the generality of the approach.
*   The unexpected performance of n-gram models compared to the RNN warrants further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach. While there are some weaknesses, they are not fundamental enough to warrant outright rejection. The strengths of the paper, particularly the novelty, technical soundness, and empirical results, outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions. 

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

*   Discuss the scalability limitations in more detail and outline potential strategies for addressing them.
*   Explore the possibility of extending the grammar to handle a wider range of expressions.
*   Conduct a more thorough analysis of the RNN’s performance and investigate potential improvements to the architecture or training procedure.
*   Provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process.
*   Expand the related work section to include a more comprehensive discussion of symbolic regression and neural-symbolic methods.
*   Include a more detailed discussion of the discovered identities within the main paper, providing context and relating them to existing knowledge.
