Summary
The paper proposes a learning-guided framework to discover computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target symbolic expression. Expressions are represented via an attribute grammar over linear algebra operations; candidate expressions (trees) are compared to the target using fast numerical descriptors computed modulo a large prime. Complex targets often require linear combinations of trees, handled by solving a linear system over descriptors. To scale the combinatorial search, the authors learn strategies from solutions to simpler targets: (i) n-gram statistics over subtrees and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions and predicts the next grammar rule. Experiments on several families of expressions (including sums of matrix polynomials and exponential sums related to RBMs) show substantial speedups over naive evaluation and that learned search significantly outperforms random exploration. The RNN also achieves low error on classifying expressions into equivalence classes.

Strengths
- Novel problem formulation: learning to discover more efficient equivalent mathematical expressions is bold and timely, bridging symbolic manipulation and ML.
- Creative integration: attribute grammars for expressiveness + learned search policies (n-grams, RNN) + fast equality testing via modular evaluation. This combination is original and well-motivated.
- Continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions: the RNN representation is a compelling idea that could enable broader ML over symbolic domains.
- Demonstrated impact: multiple examples with dramatic asymptotic improvements (e.g., O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)); polynomial-time surrogates for exponential sums for small k). Code release is valuable.
- Clear incremental curriculum: training on lower-degree solutions to guide higher-degree searches is intuitive and effective.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scope limitations: the grammar targets homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, and often a single variable. Claims about broader mathematical discovery are speculative; the empirical scope is narrow.
- Equivalence checking: equality is decided by random evaluation over Z_p at N points. This is essentially randomized polynomial identity testing but the paper does not quantify failure probability or use multiple primes to bound error; a short Schwartz–Zippel-style analysis would strengthen rigor.
- Validation/comparisons: no comparison to strong symbolic systems (e.g., Mathematica/Maple with rewrite rules, or equality saturation frameworks) on these benchmarks; it’s unclear whether some identities are already implied by standard algebraic transformations.
- Reporting: many “new identities” are deferred to the supplement; the main text would benefit from at least a taxonomy or a few more non-trivial examples with derivations and complexity analyses.
- Search setup: for some families, the matrix-multiply rule is removed to force O(n^2) solutions. This can bias the solution space and undercuts claims about automatic discovery of complexity reductions.
- RNN vs. n-gram: in most discovery tasks, n-grams outperform the RNN; the benefits of the continuous representation for search are therefore not conclusively established.

Quality
- Generally sound. The search framework, modular descriptors, and linear combination solver are coherent. However, the equivalence-testing step needs a probabilistic error bound and perhaps verification across multiple primes.
- Experimental methodology is reasonable (multiple runs with time caps; curriculum training) but lacks baselines from symbolic tools. Complexity analyses for discovered identities are mostly qualitative; more runtime plots would help.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly well written and easy to follow. The high-level system design, examples, and training details are clear. Some notation/formatting issues and minor typos remain. More detail on the grammar (Table 1) and the exact operation set would help replication.

Originality
- High. Applying ML to learn search policies for discovering efficient mathematical identities with an attribute grammar, and learning continuous embeddings of symbolic expressions, is novel.

Significance
- Potentially high. If generalized, this line could influence compiler optimizations, program synthesis, and symbolic algebra. Even in the restricted setting, the identities and the learned-search methodology are valuable and likely to stimulate follow-up work.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a formal error bound for modular identity testing (e.g., using Schwartz–Zippel) and report the number of primes/evaluation points used?
- Do the linear-combination coefficients correspond to meaningful integer/rational coefficients in the final identities? How are they recovered from solutions over Z_p?
- How do Mathematica/Maple or modern equality-saturation systems fare on your benchmarks?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of grammar? Could you add a few non-linear or trigonometric rules and show a proof-of-concept?
- Can you quantify real runtime speedups (wall-clock) on actual numeric inputs for several sizes n, not just asymptotics?

Minor comments and typos
- “help which the discovery” → “help with the discovery”
- Several O(()n3) formatting glitches; please fix.
- Please include a compact table of discovered identities and their complexities in the main text.

Pros
- Novel and ambitious direction; strong conceptual contribution.
- Effective framework blending grammars, ML, and randomized equality testing.
- Code release; compelling examples with large complexity reductions.

Cons
- Restricted scope; limited baselines; incomplete rigor on equivalence testing.
- Empirical advantage of the RNN search over simpler n-grams is unclear.

Recommendation
Accept (weak-to-moderate). Score: 7/10. The paper introduces a significant new idea and direction despite some methodological and evaluation gaps. With minor revisions (probabilistic guarantees, stronger baselines, a few more showcased identities), this would be a strong NeurIPS contribution.
