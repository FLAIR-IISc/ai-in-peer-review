Summary
The paper tackles the problem of discovering computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target expression. It casts expressions as trees over an attribute grammar (operators tagged with cost), and searches for expressions with the same semantics but lower cost. Equivalence checking is done numerically via descriptors over Zp (randomized input evaluation modulo a large prime), and more complex identities are assembled as linear combinations of trees by solving a linear system over Zp. To address the combinatorial explosion in grammar-tree search, the authors learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets: (i) n-gram models over subtrees, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions and predicts the next rule. Experiments on five expression families (including matrix polynomials, Hadamard variants, symmetric polynomials, and sums over RBM configurations) show that learned strategies successfully discover efficient forms far beyond random search for several families; many reported identities reduce complexity by orders (e.g., from O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)), and from exponential to polynomial in k for some families). Code and data are released.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The overall framework is coherent: an explicit search space (attribute grammar), a randomized equivalence test, and learned policies to guide search. The numerical descriptor over a finite field is a standard idea; however, the paper does not quantify collision probabilities or provide formal guarantees that equality under finitely many evaluations modulo p implies semantic equality over the intended domain. A brief reference to Schwartz–Zippel or a proof outline would strengthen correctness claims, especially when identities are declared “discovered.” Likewise, solving Fw = t mod p may yield coefficients in Zp; mapping those to integer/rational coefficients and ensuring the linear combination corresponds to an efficient real-valued expression (and not an artifact of modular arithmetic) needs more discussion.
- Experimental support: The experiments convincingly show that learned strategies (especially n-grams of depth 3–5) increase the success rate under a fixed time budget on several families up to k ≈ 15. The RNN achieves low classification error on equivalence classes and sometimes accelerates search, though it generally underperforms n-grams as a search policy. However, the evaluation is primarily “fraction of runs that find any solution within 10 minutes,” not end-to-end speed-ups on actual numeric instances or rigorous complexity proofs for the resulting expressions. Comparisons to CAS systems (Mathematica/Maple simplification, matrix identities, and compiler algebraic rewrites) are missing. Claims that many identities are “previously undiscovered” are not substantiated (citations or verification by domain experts would help).
- Completeness and honesty: The paper is candid about limitations (RBM-2 remains hard; RNN sometimes overfits; search space exponential; restricted grammar). More ablations would help (e.g., effect of curriculum, dropout, identity initialization, descriptor length N, prime size p).

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, well-structured, and readable. The grammar-based search and the learned components are explained at a level most NIPS readers can follow. Details such as identity initialization of RNN weights, curriculum, and dropout are appreciated. Some crucial elements are deferred or missing in the excerpt (e.g., Table 1 with the full grammar). The description of linear combinations and how coefficients are interpreted outside Zp could be clearer. It would help to include a small, fully worked example of the linear solver yielding an identity, with coefficients and final complexity.

Originality
The problem framing—learning to discover efficient symbolic identities via attribute grammars—and the use of learned search policies from simpler instances are original and timely. While n-grams are standard, their application to subtree patterns in mathematical identities is novel. Learning continuous embeddings of algebraic expressions with an RNN for guidance is an interesting contribution that connects symbolic reasoning and representation learning. Related work is reasonably discussed; the paper could expand comparisons to program synthesis/induction and superoptimization (e.g., STOKE) which also combine search with learned/prioritized heuristics.

Significance
Potentially significant: If robust, these methods could automate algebraic optimization beyond hand-crafted rules, benefiting ML and scientific computing (e.g., fast evaluation of polynomial-like expressions, RBM-related terms, or compiler passes). The RNN-based embedding may spur more work in learning over symbolic structures. However, current evidence is mixed: the strongest empirical wins are on families with repetitive local structure (where n-grams excel), the RNN underperforms n-grams for guidance, and the scope is limited (homogeneous polynomials, single variable, fixed degree, restricted grammar). The lack of baselines against CAS and missing formal guarantees weakens the practical impact claim.

Pros
- Clear problem and well-motivated framework combining grammar-based search with learning.
- Demonstrates that learned guidance dramatically improves search over random on several families.
- RNN learns useful embeddings of expressions; solid training insights (identity init, curriculum, ReLUs).
- Code/data released; approach is general and could be extended.
- Some identities produce substantial asymptotic improvements.

Cons
- No formal guarantees for equivalence checking or for lifting modular linear solutions to real/integer identities; potential for false positives not quantified.
- Limited evaluation: success rate under a time cap but no systematic end-to-end runtime benchmarks, no comparison to CAS/rewriting baselines, and no expert verification of “novel” identities.
- RNN policy generally underperforms simpler n-gram models; benefits are modest on the hardest family.
- Scope restricted to specific grammars and single-variable homogeneous polynomials; generalization unclear.
- Complexity accounting for linear combinations and shared subexpressions is not fully specified.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide probabilistic guarantees for equivalence (e.g., bounds via Schwartz–Zippel; choice of N and p).
- Show how modular coefficients are mapped to small integers/rationals and how the final expression’s cost is computed, including sharing.
- Add baselines: Mathematica/Maple simplification/FullSimplify, common-subexpression elimination, and rule-based algebraic optimizers; report actual runtime speed-ups on numeric instances.
- Include more ablations and a qualitative analysis of discovered patterns; verify novelty of identities.
- Explore richer grammars (multiple variables, transposes/traces) and discuss scheduler termination strategies.

Recommendation
Borderline, leaning weak accept. The paper opens an interesting direction—learning to guide symbolic identity discovery—with a concrete framework and nontrivial empirical evidence on several families. Despite evaluation gaps and limited guarantees, the ideas are novel and likely to stimulate follow-up work bridging ML and symbolic computation. Strengthening the guarantees and baselines would make this a clear accept.
