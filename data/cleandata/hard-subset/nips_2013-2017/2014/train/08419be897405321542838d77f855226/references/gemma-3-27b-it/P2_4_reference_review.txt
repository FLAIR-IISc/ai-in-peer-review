## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and interesting approach to discovering mathematical identities by combining attribute grammars with machine learning. The core idea of learning a search strategy within a grammar-defined space is compelling, and the results demonstrate a significant ability to find efficient expressions that are difficult to discover manually or through brute-force search. While there are areas for improvement in clarity and broader generalization, the paper is technically sound, original, and demonstrates a significant advancement in a challenging problem. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of automatically finding computationally efficient equivalent expressions for a given target expression. It proposes a framework leveraging attribute grammars to represent symbolic expressions and then employs machine learning (n-gram models and RNNs) to intelligently navigate the exponentially growing search space of possible expressions. The authors position their work within the broader contexts of theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation, appropriately highlighting its distinct contributions – specifically, the learning-guided search within a grammatical framework and the discovery of novel identities. The related work section is well-covered, referencing key papers in each relevant area.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars with machine learning for this task is a genuinely novel approach. This isn’t simply applying a standard ML technique to a new domain; it’s a clever integration of symbolic reasoning with learning-based search.
* **Technical Soundness:** The framework appears technically sound. The use of attribute grammars provides a well-defined search space, and the integer-based descriptor for numerical comparison addresses a critical practical issue (floating-point inaccuracies). The RNN architecture is well-explained and the training procedure seems reasonable.
* **Empirical Results:** The paper provides compelling evidence of success. Discovering identities like reducing O(n<sup>3</sup>) to O(n<sup>2</sup>) operations is a significant result, especially given the claim that these identities were previously unknown.  The experiments demonstrate that learning-based search strategies outperform random search, especially for more complex expressions.
* **Potential Impact:** The ability to automatically discover efficient mathematical identities has potential applications in compiler optimization, symbolic computation, and even within machine learning itself (as suggested by the authors’ discussion of RBMs and dropout).
* **Code Availability:**  The publicly available code and evaluation data (via GitHub) are a strong positive, promoting reproducibility and further research.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Accessibility:** While the overall structure is logical, certain sections (especially regarding the attribute grammar and the nuances of the RNN training) could benefit from further clarification. More intuitive examples and diagrams would be helpful for readers less familiar with these concepts. The notation is generally clear but could be streamlined.
* **Generalizability:** The current experiments are limited to a specific class of polynomials (homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree). While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the extent to which the approach can be generalized to broader classes of expressions remains an open question. The success with the RBM-2 family appears limited, raising concerns about scalability to more complex scenarios.
* **RNN Performance:** While the RNN shows some improvement over random search, it is consistently outperformed by the n-gram models. The authors suggest dropout as a mitigation for overfitting, but a more thorough analysis of why the RNN doesn't perform as well as expected would be beneficial.  Why do n-grams, capturing local structure, work so well compared to a potentially more powerful RNN?
* **Limited Scope of Grammar:**  The restricted grammar (Table 1) prevents exploration of a wider range of mathematical expressions. While this restriction simplifies the problem, it also limits the potential for discovery.



**4. Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:**  The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by experimental results, and the authors acknowledge limitations. It’s a complete piece of work within its defined scope.
* **Clarity:**  Moderate. The paper is generally understandable, but sections on attribute grammars and RNN training could be more accessible.
* **Originality:** High. The combination of techniques and the application to this specific problem are novel.
* **Significance:**  High. The ability to automatically discover efficient identities has the potential to be impactful in multiple fields.




**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Demonstrates a novel and effective approach to a challenging problem.
* Achieves significant results – discovering previously unknown mathematical identities.
* Provides a solid technical foundation and strong empirical validation.
* Has the potential for broader impact in related fields.
* Code and data are publicly available.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited generalizability to broader classes of expressions.
* RNN performance is not competitive with n-gram models.
* Some sections could benefit from increased clarity and accessibility.




**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  

I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically:

* **Improve Clarity:** Revise sections on attribute grammars and RNN training for better accessibility.  Include more illustrative examples and diagrams.
* **Discuss RNN Performance:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of why the RNN performs less effectively than n-gram models.  Explore potential improvements.
* **Acknowledge Limitations:** Clearly articulate the limitations of the current approach, particularly regarding generalizability, in the conclusion.
* **Consider expanding the discussion on the grammar:** Briefly discuss the challenges of expanding the grammar and the potential strategies for doing so.




This paper represents a significant contribution to the field, and the suggested revisions would further strengthen its impact and clarity.
