### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is equivalent to a given target expression but has a lower computational complexity. Recognizing that a brute-force or random search through the combinatorial space of grammar trees is intractable for complex targets, the authors propose a machine learning approach to guide the search. The key insight is to train search strategies (based on n-gram models and a recursive neural network) on solutions discovered for simpler expressions, and then use these learned models to find solutions for more complex problems. A significant secondary contribution is the use of a recursive neural network to learn a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions, a novel idea that could bridge the symbolic and connectionist domains for other tasks. The authors demonstrate their framework's effectiveness by discovering efficient identities for several families of expressions, some of which are claimed to be novel and relevant to machine learning computations.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

This is a highly ambitious and original paper that tackles a fundamental problem at the intersection of symbolic computation, program synthesis, and machine learning. Its primary strength lies in its boldness and novelty.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Exceptional Originality and Significance:** The central idea of using machine learning to *learn how to discover* mathematical identities is a significant departure from traditional, rule-based symbolic systems and a refreshing direction for the field. It reframes a problem of discrete, combinatorial search as a guided exploration problem where experience on simpler tasks accelerates the solution of harder ones. The potential impact is substantial, with applications ranging from compiler optimization to accelerating scientific computing and deriving new machine learning algorithms.

2.  **A Compelling New Tool: Learned Representations for Symbolic Mathematics:** The paper's demonstration of a recursive neural network that learns a continuous representation for symbolic expressions is a standout contribution. In the same way that word embeddings revolutionized NLP by making language amenable to deep learning, this work provides a proof-of-concept for doing the same with mathematical formulas. The high classification accuracy reported in Table 2 suggests this is a powerful and effective representation. This contribution alone is of significant interest to the community.

3.  **Elegant and Effective Framework:** The combination of a formal grammar to define the search space with a learned policy to navigate it is both elegant and demonstrably effective. The results in Figure 3 are convincing; the learned n-gram and RNN strategies dramatically outperform the random baseline, pushing the boundary of what is discoverable within a reasonable time budget. This success validates the core hypothesis that structure within mathematical identities can be learned and generalized.

4.  **Concrete and Impressive Results:** The paper doesn't just propose a framework; it uses it to discover new, non-trivial identities for complex expression families, such as those related to RBM partition functions. Discovering a polynomial-time equivalent for a problem with naive exponential complexity is a major achievement and provides tangible evidence of the system's power.

**Weaknesses (Points for Consideration):**

1.  **Limited Scope of the Current Framework:** The experiments are restricted to a fairly narrow class of expressions: homogeneous polynomials of a single variable with a scalar output. This is a reasonable and necessary simplification for a foundational paper, but it does leave open the question of scalability to more general mathematics involving trigonometry, logarithms, conditional logic, or recursion. A more thorough discussion of the conceptual hurdles to extending the grammar and search would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Relative Performance of Learning Models:** It is interesting that the simpler n-gram models often outperform the more sophisticated RNN-based search strategy. While the RNN's representational power is a key contribution, its direct application as a search policy appears to be suboptimal in some cases. The authors could provide more intuition as to why this might be the case. Perhaps the repetitive, local structure of the solutions found (e.g., Example 2) is particularly well-suited to n-gram statistics, while the RNN struggles to capitalize on this specific type of regularity. This is not a fatal flaw, but an interesting finding that deserves more exploration.

3.  **Comparison with Symbolic Computation Systems:** The paper mentions that systems like Maple and Mathematica do not explicitly search for lower-complexity forms. However, these systems contain powerful, highly-optimized simplification engines. A qualitative or even simple quantitative comparison, showing a case where a commercial system fails to find the efficient form that the proposed method discovers, would make the claims of novelty even more concrete.

### Recommendation

**Recommendation: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that I would strongly advocate for accepting. It is creative, ambitious, and opens up a genuinely new line of inquiry that is highly relevant to the NIPS community. The work tackles the grand challenge of bridging statistical machine learning with symbolic reasoning in a novel and practical way.

While the current implementation has limitations in its scope, this is to be expected from a paper charting new territory. The core contributions—the learning-guided search framework and the continuous representation for symbolic expressions—are significant and thought-provoking. The paper's willingness to prioritize a bold new concept over a methodologically perfect but incremental advance is precisely the kind of research that drives the field forward. The weaknesses identified are minor and represent exciting avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the presented research. This work is likely to inspire many follow-up projects and has the potential to be highly influential.
