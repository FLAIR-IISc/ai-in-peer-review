

# Peer Review: "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The authors introduce a grammar-based approach to represent symbolic expressions as sequences of operations, and then employ machine learning techniques (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for efficient expression variants. The key insight is that learning from simpler expressions can help discover more complex ones, with demonstrated improvements in computational complexity (e.g., reducing from O(n³) to O(n²)). The paper presents experiments on several families of expressions, showing that their learning-based search strategies significantly outperform random search for complex identities.

The work sits at the intersection of symbolic computation, machine learning, and compiler optimization. While attribute grammars have been used in other domains (e.g., compiler construction, bioinformatics), their application to discover mathematical identities with machine learning guidance represents a novel approach. The paper contrasts well with previous work in theorem proving and symbolic computation (Maple, Mathematica), which rely on rule-based systems without learning components.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with a well-defined problem statement and clear methodology. The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of their approach across multiple expression families, with appropriate baselines (random search) and metrics (success rate within a fixed time budget).

The numerical verification approach using integer-based descriptors (Zp) is a clever solution to the problem of symbolic equivalence checking. This avoids the numerical instability issues of floating-point comparisons. However, the paper could have provided more detail on the choice of prime p and the relationship between p, the number of random points N, and the likelihood of false positives. The authors mention that N must be greater than the number of linear equations, but don't specify how they determined this threshold.

The experimental design is sound, with multiple runs (10) per parameter setting and a fixed 10-minute time limit. The results in Figure 3 clearly show that learning-based strategies outperform random search for complex identities, with the n-gram models (3-gram, 4-gram, 5-gram) performing particularly well on repetitive structures like those in Example 2.

However, the paper lacks theoretical analysis of why the learning approaches work better than random search. While the empirical results are convincing, a theoretical justification would strengthen the paper. Additionally, the authors don't discuss the computational cost of training the learning models themselves, which is an important consideration for practical application.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and presents the key contributions. The problem statement, attribute grammar, and representation sections provide sufficient detail for a reader to understand the technical approach.

The examples (Examples 1 and 2) are particularly helpful in illustrating the core ideas. The figures (Figures 1-3) are well-designed and effectively communicate the main results.

The paper would benefit from more detail on the hyperparameters used for the RNN (e.g., learning rate, batch size, number of training epochs) and the specific architecture details (e.g., the exact size of the weight matrices). While the paper mentions "l = 30" as the embedding dimension, it doesn't explain how this was chosen or whether the results are sensitive to this parameter.

## Originality

This work makes a significant contribution to the intersection of symbolic AI and machine learning. The application of n-gram models and recursive neural networks to guide the search for mathematical identities is novel in the context of symbolic computation. While attribute grammars have been used before (e.g., in compiler construction and music composition), their use for discovering mathematical identities with learning guidance is new.

The paper clearly distinguishes itself from related work in theorem proving (which doesn't focus on computational complexity optimization), symbolic computation engines (which don't use learning), and NLP (where grammars are used for parsing, not identity discovery). The authors also cite relevant work in the area, though they might have included more recent work on neural approaches to symbolic reasoning (e.g., DeepMind's AlphaTensor, which discovered new matrix multiplication algorithms).

The most novel aspect is the use of learning to guide the search for efficient mathematical identities, rather than using hand-designed heuristics. This represents a significant shift from previous approaches in the field.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial, particularly for machine learning practitioners. The discovered identities could potentially lead to more efficient implementations of common operations (e.g., approximating dropout or computing RBM partition functions more efficiently). The authors rightly point out that their framework could be integrated with compilers or optimization tools.

The paper's demonstration of continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions has broader implications beyond mathematical identity discovery. This could enable machine learning approaches to be applied to symbolic computation problems, potentially opening up new research directions.

However, the restricted scope (homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix variable) limits the immediate applicability of the results. While the authors acknowledge this in Section 8, they could have more clearly articulated the path to generalizing the framework to more complex expressions.

## Critical Assessment

### Strengths
1. **Novel integration of machine learning with symbolic computation**: The core idea of using learning to guide the search for efficient mathematical identities is genuinely novel and well-executed.
2. **Strong experimental results**: The paper demonstrates clear improvements over random search for complex identities, with concrete examples showing significant complexity reductions.
3. **Practical implications**: The work has clear potential applications in machine learning (e.g., for efficient dropout approximation) and compiler optimization.
4. **Clear presentation**: The paper is well-organized and effectively communicates a complex technical approach.

### Weaknesses
1. **Restricted problem scope**: The focus on homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with a single matrix variable limits the generalizability of the approach. The authors acknowledge this but don't sufficiently explore the path to more general expressions.
2. **Lack of theoretical justification**: While the empirical results are strong, the paper doesn't provide a theoretical explanation for why the learning approaches work better than random search.
3. **Limited comparison to other methods**: The paper compares only to random search, but doesn't compare to other symbolic optimization approaches (if any exist).
4. **Insufficient detail on hyperparameters**: The paper lacks details on key hyperparameters for the RNN (e.g., learning rate, batch size, number of training epochs).
5. **Verification concerns**: The paper relies on numerical verification (using Zp), but doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of this approach (e.g., the probability of false positives).

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. Include a theoretical analysis of why the learning approaches work better than random search.
2. Expand the scope to include more general expressions (even if only as a discussion point).
3. Provide more detail on hyperparameters and the training process for the RNN.
4. Compare to at least one alternative symbolic optimization method (if available).
5. Discuss the computational cost of training the learning models themselves.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the intersection of symbolic AI and machine learning. The approach is novel, the experiments are well-designed, and the results are compelling. The paper would benefit from addressing the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly regarding the theoretical justification and problem scope.

The work is highly relevant to NIPS as it represents a novel application of machine learning techniques to a symbolic reasoning problem. It's a promising step toward combining the strengths of symbolic reasoning and machine learning.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The results are significant and the work has clear potential applications. While there are some limitations (particularly the restricted scope), these are acknowledged by the authors and represent natural directions for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the current approach.

The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, contributing to the growing area of neural-symbolic integration. I believe it would be of interest to a wide range of researchers in machine learning and AI.
