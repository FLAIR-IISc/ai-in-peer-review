This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and to search this space for an expression that is equivalent to a given target but has lower computational complexity. Recognizing that the search space is intractably large for brute-force or random exploration, the authors propose using machine learning to guide the search. The learning models, which include n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN), are trained on solutions found for simpler target expressions, allowing the system to generalize and solve progressively more complex problems. A key contribution is the novel use of an RNN to learn a continuous vector representation for symbolic expression trees, analogous to word embeddings in natural language processing. The authors demonstrate the framework's effectiveness by discovering efficient identities for five families of expressions, including non-trivial polynomial-time equivalents for expressions that are naively exponential to compute, some of which appear to be new discoveries.

This work sits at an interesting and timely intersection of machine learning, symbolic computation, and program induction. While symbolic engines like Mathematica can simplify expressions, they are typically rule-based and do not use statistical learning to discover novel, low-complexity identities. The application of techniques from natural language processing, such as n-gram models and tree-based RNNs (similar to Socher et al., 2013), to the domain of symbolic mathematics is highly original and opens up a promising new research direction.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength is its originality. The idea of using machine learning to guide a search for mathematical identities is novel and significant. It represents a solid step toward bridging the gap between statistical pattern recognition and symbolic reasoning. If this approach can be generalized, it could have a substantial impact on optimizing scientific code, compiler design, and even theoretical research. The specific application to expressions related to RBM partition functions suggests immediate relevance to the machine learning community.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sound and well-conceived. The combination of an attribute grammar for defining the search space, a clever numerical representation (using modular arithmetic) for efficiently checking expression equivalence, and the integration of learned search strategies is elegant. The experiments are thorough, testing the approach on a variety of challenging expression families and providing a clear comparison between baseline and learning-based strategies. The authors are also commendably transparent about the limitations of their method, for instance, in the case of the (RBM-2)k family where the learned models failed to outperform random search for k > 5.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivating examples in the introduction are excellent for conveying the core problem and the nature of the solutions sought. The logical flow from the problem statement to the final results is easy to follow. The learned representation via an RNN is explained well, including important implementation details that were crucial for success.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope:** The primary weakness is the current limited scope of the problem setting. The framework is demonstrated on homogeneous polynomials involving a single variable and producing a scalar output. While the authors state that the framework can handle more, the paper does not explore the significant challenges that would arise from a more general setting (e.g., expressions with mixed-degree terms, multiple variables of different types, or non-polynomial operators like trigonometric functions). The fixed-degree constraint is key to bounding the search tree depth; relaxing this would require a much more sophisticated search scheduler.
*   **RNN Performance and Analysis:** While the application of an RNN is a novel contribution, it is somewhat surprising that the simpler n-gram models consistently outperform it in the identity discovery task. The authors suggest this is due to the repetitive local structure of the solutions. While plausible, a more in-depth analysis would strengthen the paper. For instance, is the RNN's learned representation truly capturing properties relevant to computational complexity, or is it primarily focused on semantic equivalence (as it was trained to do in the classification pre-training task)? The search strategy needs to be biased towards *simpler* trees, and it is not explicitly clear how this bias is incorporated into the RNN-based strategy.
*   **Details on Linear Combinations:** Section 5, which describes how linear combinations of trees are used to form solutions, is quite brief. This appears to be a crucial component for finding more complex identities, and its explanation would benefit from more detail or a small illustrative example. For instance, it is unclear when the linear solver is invoked during the search process.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Highly original and creative application of machine learning to a fundamental problem in symbolic computation.
*   The approach is technically sound and the experimental results are strong, demonstrating the ability to find non-trivial and seemingly new identities.
*   The paper is well-written, clear, and opens up a promising new area of research.
*   The idea of learning continuous representations for symbolic mathematics could have a broad impact beyond this specific application.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental setting is somewhat restricted, and the paper could do more to discuss the challenges of scaling to more general mathematical expressions.
*   The more sophisticated RNN model is often outperformed by simpler n-gram models, which may temper the excitement around the deep learning contribution for this specific task.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Generalization Discussion:** In the discussion, it would be valuable to elaborate more concretely on the path toward generalizing the framework. What are the key hurdles in handling non-polynomials or expressions without a fixed degree, and what are some potential solutions (e.g., modifying the scheduler, incorporating new grammar rules with termination conditions)?
2.  **Analysis of Search Strategies:** A deeper analysis of the relative performance of the n-gram and RNN models would be insightful. Could the RNN architecture be modified to better capture the recursive patterns that n-grams seem to find easily? Perhaps incorporating the complexity attribute of the grammar rules directly into the RNN's training objective for the search strategy would help guide it toward more efficient solutions.
3.  **Clarification of Methods:** I would recommend expanding Section 5 slightly to clarify the process of solving for linear combinations. Additionally, providing more detail on the curriculum learning strategy mentioned in Section 4.2 would be helpful for reproducibility.
4.  **Discovered Identities:** The paper's impact relies on the discovered identities being genuinely useful. While they are listed in the supplement, including one or two of the more complex and interesting non-trivial identities (e.g., for Symk or RBM-1) in an appendix of the main paper would make the contribution more tangible.

Overall, this is an excellent and thought-provoking paper that presents a novel and promising direction for research. The strengths in originality and significance far outweigh the weaknesses related to the current scope. I strongly recommend acceptance.
