## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and ambitious approach to automating the discovery of mathematical identities, specifically focusing on computationally efficient equivalents of given expressions. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates promising results, particularly in finding identities not previously known. While the scope is restricted, the techniques employed – combining attribute grammars with machine learning – are interesting and potentially impactful. I believe this paper is a **strong accept** for NIPS, with minor revisions suggested.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the challenging problem of symbolic manipulation via machine learning. It proposes a framework built around attribute grammars to represent mathematical expressions and explores using both n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to guide the search for more efficient equivalent expressions. The authors position their work within the context of theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, but importantly highlight the restrictions they impose (polynomials of fixed degree, single or two variables) which allows for a tractable search space.  The paper does a good job of differentiating itself from existing symbolic computation engines (Mathematica, Maple) which rely on rule-based systems, and emphasizes the novelty of *learning* the search strategy. The connection to NLP techniques like n-gram models and RNNs applied to parse trees is clearly drawn.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for mathematical identity discovery is a fresh and innovative approach. The application of RNNs to learn representations of symbolic expressions is particularly noteworthy.
* **Technical Soundness:** The described framework is well-defined, and the experiments appear rigorous. The use of integer-based descriptors to address floating-point precision issues is a clever solution. The ablation study comparing different search strategies (random, n-gram, RNN) provides valuable insights.
* **Demonstrated Results:** The paper provides compelling examples (e.g., reducing O(n^3) to O(n^2) complexity) and claims to have discovered previously unknown identities. While a comprehensive verification of *all* discovered identities is beyond the scope of a review, the presented examples are convincing.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, and the technical details are presented in a reasonably accessible manner.
* **Code Availability:** Publicly releasing the code and evaluation data is a significant strength, enabling reproducibility and further research.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Scope Limitations:** The restrictions imposed on the problem (homogeneous polynomials, fixed degree, limited variables) are substantial. While necessary for tractability, they limit the broader applicability of the approach.  The discussion acknowledges this, but it’s a key consideration.
* **RNN Performance:** While the RNN provides a continuous representation, its performance doesn't consistently outperform the n-gram models, particularly for the simpler families. The authors suggest the RNN shines for more complex problems (RBM-2), but lack conclusive evidence given the current experiments.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments don't provide extensive details about hyperparameter tuning for the learning models. Understanding the sensitivity of the results to different learning rates, network architectures, etc., would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Limited Explanation of Discovered Identities:** While the paper demonstrates *that* identities are discovered, providing more intuition behind *why* the learned models find these specific identities would be beneficial.



**4. Evaluation of Criteria:**

* **Quality (Strong):** The technical approach is solid, the experimental design is reasonable, and the results are compelling. The integer-based descriptors and curriculum learning for the RNN are particularly well-considered.
* **Clarity (Good):** The paper is mostly clear, although the technical details of the attribute grammars could benefit from further simplification or visual aids.
* **Originality (Strong):** The combination of grammars and learning is genuinely novel, and the application to this specific problem domain appears unique.
* **Significance (Good):**  The potential for automating mathematical discovery and optimizing computations is significant. While the current scope is limited, the approach opens up avenues for tackling more complex symbolic problems.



**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Novel and promising approach to a challenging problem.
* Demonstrates ability to discover previously unknown identities.
* Technically sound and well-executed experiments.
* Code and data are publicly available.

**Against:**

* Limited scope of application due to restrictions on the problem domain.
* RNN performance isn’t consistently superior to simpler methods.




**5. Recommendation & Suggestions for Revision:**

I recommend **accepting** this paper for NIPS. 

Minor revisions would strengthen the paper:

* **Expand discussion of limitations:**  A more thorough discussion of the challenges involved in extending the framework to more general symbolic expressions (e.g., handling trigonometric functions, inequalities) would be valuable.
* **RNN analysis:** Provide more detailed analysis of the RNN’s performance.  Perhaps conduct a more thorough hyperparameter search or investigate the learned representations to gain insights into its strengths and weaknesses.
* **Intuition for Identities:** Briefly discuss the rationale behind some of the discovered identities, even if it’s just a high-level explanation.
* **Clarify Grammar Rules:** Perhaps a visual representation of the grammar rules could aid understanding.




In conclusion, this is a well-written, technically sound, and original paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of automated mathematical discovery. The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning is a promising direction, and the demonstrated results are encouraging.  I believe this work has the potential to inspire further research and development in this exciting area.
