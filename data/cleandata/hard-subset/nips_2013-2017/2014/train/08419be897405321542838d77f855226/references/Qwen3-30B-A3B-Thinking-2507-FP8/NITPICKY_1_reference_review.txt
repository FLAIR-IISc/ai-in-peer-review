

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars combined with machine learning techniques. The authors frame the problem as finding symbolic expressions equivalent to a target but with lower computational complexity, and they introduce a grammar-based search space with learning-based strategies to navigate it. The main contributions include a grammar framework for symbolic expression optimization, integration of n-gram models and recursive neural networks for search strategy learning, and the discovery of new mathematical identities with significant complexity reductions.

**Strengths:** The problem is well-motivated, and the approach represents a novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation. The experimental results demonstrate that learning-based search strategies outperform random search for complex targets, which is a meaningful contribution. The paper provides a clear technical framework and demonstrates practical results on several interesting mathematical families.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the promising results, the paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies and methodological shortcomings that undermine its scientific rigor:

1. **Formatting and Presentation Inconsistencies:** The paper exhibits inconsistent mathematical notation throughout (e.g., "O ( n3 )" vs "O(n3)"), which makes it difficult to follow the complexity claims. In Example 2, the expression formatting is inconsistent with parentheses and spacing, making the described algorithm ambiguous.

2. **Incomplete Technical Details:** The paper claims to use "integers modulo p" for numerical representation but fails to specify how p is chosen or its impact on the method's performance. The transition from floating-point to integer-based representation is not properly justified, and the paper doesn't explain how to determine the minimal N required for the descriptor to uniquely characterize expressions.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The claim that "N must be sufficiently large to ensure different expressions are not mapped to the same descriptor" lacks theoretical backing. The authors provide no analysis of the probability of collisions or the minimum N required for their specific application.

4. **Ambiguous Experimental Details:** The paper states that the RNN is trained with "a small amount of Gaussian noise" but doesn't specify the standard deviation. The weighting scheme for n-grams ("10k where k is the depth of the tree") is presented without justification or analysis of its impact on performance.

5. **Insufficient Experimental Reporting:** The paper claims "low error rate of ≤ 5%" for the RNN classification but doesn't provide exact error rates for different degrees (k=3 to k=6). The standard deviations in the timing results (100±12 vs 438±77 secs) are not properly explained (are these for the 10 runs?).

6. **Incomplete Related Work:** The paper mentions Bridge et al. [4] as a related work but doesn't sufficiently contrast their approach with the proposed method. The connection to Socher et al. [23] is acknowledged but not deeply explored in the context of their specific application.

7. **Missing Critical Details:** The paper refers to "Table 3" showing the search space growth but doesn't include it. The paper mentions "Figure 1" multiple times but doesn't provide the figure. The hardware specifications for experiments are buried in a footnote rather than being in the main text.

8. **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The statement that "the majority of these examples are too complex to be found manually or by exhaustive search and, as far as we are aware, are previously undiscovered" lacks evidence or comparison to exhaustive search times for the specific cases.

**Conclusion:** While the paper presents an interesting approach to a challenging problem, the numerous technical inconsistencies, lack of sufficient methodological detail, and unsubstantiated claims significantly undermine its scientific contribution. The paper would benefit from thorough revision to address these issues before publication. The current version does not provide sufficient detail for reproducibility or rigorous evaluation of the claims made. The minor methodological flaws and presentation inconsistencies are too numerous to be overlooked in a NIPS submission, which demands exceptional technical rigor.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial number of technical shortcomings that prevent proper evaluation of the method's contribution. The paper's potential is evident, but the execution lacks the precision expected at NIPS.
