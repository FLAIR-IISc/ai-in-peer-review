This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars and machine learning. The core idea – leveraging learning to guide the search within a grammar-defined space of expressions – is interesting and potentially impactful. However, despite the novelty, I find a substantial number of flaws, ranging from minor inconsistencies to questionable experimental choices and a lack of rigorous analysis, leading me to recommend rejection.

The introduction is reasonably well-written but suffers from hyperbole ("too intricate to be discovered by human manipulation"). While the proposed identities may be non-trivial, claiming they are beyond human discovery feels overstated. The contributions list is also too expansive; discovering mathematical identities *is* the contribution, not a separate bullet point.

The attribute grammar framework itself (Section 3) is presented adequately, but the justification for the specific grammar rules chosen feels arbitrary. Why these operations and not others? A more thorough exploration of alternative grammar definitions, and a justification for the chosen one, is needed. The limitations imposed – homogeneous polynomials, single matrix/vector – are acknowledged, but not convincingly justified as a necessary simplification.

The representations of symbolic expressions (Section 4) are a major concern. The numerical representation, while clever, relies on a large prime number and is vulnerable to numerical instability, even with integer-based descriptors. The claim of avoiding “rounding issues” is suspect; floating-point arithmetic is still involved in the initial evaluation, and modulo operations can introduce their own artifacts. The learned representation using an RNN is more promising, but the training details are glossed over. The choice of l=30 is justified by cross-validation, but the specific results aren’t presented. The dropout rate justification feels similarly hand-wavy. Crucially, the paper doesn’t demonstrate the quality of the learned representations *independent* of the identity discovery task. Is the RNN truly learning meaningful representations, or simply memorizing solutions? A visualization of the learned embeddings would be invaluable.

Section 5, on linear combinations of trees, is mathematically sound, but lacks discussion of potential ill-conditioning issues in solving the linear system, especially with a large number of trees.

The search strategy (Section 6) is where the paper truly falters. The n-gram approach is presented as effective, yet the authors acknowledge its sparsity issues and resort to weighted combinations. The justification for the specific weights (10k) is non-existent. The RNN-based strategy is similarly lacking in detail. The dropout settings are empirically determined, but the reasoning is vague. Furthermore, the comparison between the strategies (Fig. 3) is misleading. The y-axis represents the *fraction* of successful runs, but the total number of runs is not stated, making it difficult to assess statistical significance. The statement that the RNN is consistently faster for the (RBM-2)k family is quantified with imprecise "±" values.

The experimental results (Section 7) are presented with insufficient rigor. The choice of families is not well motivated. The performance metrics are not clearly defined (e.g., "success" is simply finding *a* solution, not necessarily the *most* efficient one). The comparison to existing symbolic computation engines (Maple, Mathematica) is dismissive, stating they "do not explicitly seek versions of lower complexity." This is incorrect; these engines perform significant simplification, albeit not necessarily guided by machine learning.

Minor issues abound. There are inconsistencies in notation (e.g., A ∈ Rn×n vs. A ∈ Rn×1). The example in the introduction (Example 1) is unnecessarily verbose and obscures the core idea. The reference list is missing some relevant works (e.g., on symbolic regression).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of machine learning to mathematical identity discovery.
*   Potentially useful framework for automating symbolic optimization.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Lack of rigorous experimental evaluation and analysis.
*   Insufficient justification for design choices (grammar rules, hyperparameters).
*   Questionable claims of novelty and impact.
*   Numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies.
*   Overstated significance of the contribution.



In summary, while the paper presents an interesting idea, its execution is flawed. The lack of rigor, the numerous minor issues, and the unsubstantiated claims significantly detract from its overall quality. I believe the paper, in its current form, is not suitable for publication at NIPS. A major revision, addressing the points outlined above, would be necessary for reconsideration.
