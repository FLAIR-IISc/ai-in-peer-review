Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the problem of discovering mathematically equivalent but computationally cheaper expressions for a given target. It formalizes the search space via an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic primitives, and explores trees whose leaves are variables and whose attributes encode operation costs. Exact equivalence is checked using descriptors computed by evaluating expressions on random inputs over a large finite field Zp; for complex expressions, the method also solves for linear combinations of trees via modular Gaussian elimination. To cope with the exponential tree space, the paper learns search strategies from solutions to simpler targets: (i) n-gram models over subtrees, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of trees and a softmax policy for the next rule.

Empirically, the approach discovers identities that reduce naive O(n3) matrix computations to O(n2), and in families involving exponential sums over binary vectors (RBM-like terms), finds polynomial-time forms for small k. The RNN achieves low classification error when grouping trees by equivalence classes up to degree k = 6. Across families, n-gram strategies generally outperform the RNN in discovery rate, though the RNN finds some solutions faster.

This sits at the intersection of theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation. Unlike CAS tools (Maple, Mathematica) that rely on hand-coded rewrite systems, and prior learned heuristics for theorem proving, the contribution here is a learned search policy within a restricted algebraic grammar. The idea of continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions parallels word/sentence embeddings in NLP, and RNNs over trees used in parsing/sentiment; here they are repurposed for algebraic structures.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and a practical search framework grounded in attribute grammars with cost attributes.
- Clever and efficient equivalence checking via random evaluations over Zp, coupled with linear-combination solving to assemble complex targets.
- Learning to guide search demonstrably broadens the discoverable set versus random exploration; n-grams and RNNs generalize across increasing degrees k.
- An interesting and potentially reusable result: a continuous embedding for algebraic expression trees that enables learned policies.
- Open-sourcing code/data increases reproducibility and potential impact.

Weaknesses
- Correctness guarantees for equivalence checking are informal: no quantitative bound on descriptor length N, choice of prime p, or collision probability (e.g., via Schwartz–Zippel). Solving Fw = t mod p can yield spurious matches without a clear argument they vanish with high probability under the stated assumptions.
- The discovered identities are not independently verified/proved; claims of novelty are not substantiated. Example 1 is a standard associativity/linearity rearrangement; many may be known as trace/linear-algebra identities.
- The scope is quite restricted: homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, primarily single-variable (despite a two-variable example), and a limited grammar. The results for the hardest family (RBM-2) stall beyond k = 5.
- Empirical evaluation focuses on “found/not found within 10 minutes” rather than measuring end-to-end runtime gains with realistic matrix sizes or constant-factor improvements, and lacks baselines against CAS simplification or algebraic compilers.
- The learned RNN policy underperforms simple n-gram models on most families; ablations and analysis of when/why the embedding helps are limited.

Quality
Technically sound overall, but key probabilistic-algebraic underpinnings are missing. Since all expressions are bounded-degree polynomials over entries of A with ring operations, it should be possible to provide formal high-probability guarantees for identity testing over finite fields (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel–DeMillo–Lipton) and to set N relative to the number of monomials. Similarly, mapping solutions of modular linear systems back to real-valued identities requires care (coefficients, ring homomorphisms). The scheduler/strategy set-up is reasonable; experiments are thorough for the chosen families, but broader baselines and runtime measurements would strengthen the case.

Clarity
Generally well written, with intuitive examples and sensible organization. Important missing details include: the exact grammar (Table 1) and how its attributes compose; explicit bounds/choices for N and p; how scalar coefficients in linear combinations correspond to allowed grammar operations; and a more explicit description of the scheduler’s termination/branching when building two-input operations. Some claims (e.g., “previously undiscovered”) should be toned down or supported.

Originality
Using learned policies to guide identity discovery within an attribute grammar, and learning continuous embeddings for algebraic trees, are novel in this combination. The n-gram idea is straightforward; the RNN application is interesting but not yet clearly superior.

Significance
If matured, this could impact compiler optimization, tensor algebra systems, and approximate inference (e.g., small-k expansions), by automating strength reduction. As-is, the scope and guarantees are too limited for strong impact at NeurIPS; however, the direction is promising and the released artifacts are valuable.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Provide formal probabilistic guarantees for the equivalence test over Zp: bounds on N and p as a function of degree and variable count; cite and adapt polynomial identity testing results.
- Show that modular solutions correspond to real-valued identities and that coefficients are realizable within the grammar (e.g., sums and scalar multiplies are permitted).
- Verify identities symbolically for a held-out set using a CAS, and/or provide human-readable proofs for key cases.
- Compare against CAS simplifiers and algebraic compilers on the same targets; report actual wall-clock speedups for realistic n, including constant factors.
- Analyze failure cases (e.g., RBM-2 beyond k = 5); consider richer models (tree-LSTM, attention), beam/MCTS search, and value functions estimating “distance” to target.
- Expand scope: multi-variable targets, non-homogeneous polynomials, additional operations; or clearly justify current restrictions.
- Provide more ablations: curriculum, dropout, identity init, and training data size; analyze when RNN beats n-grams.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
While the problem is interesting and the approach creative, the current submission lacks theoretical guarantees for its core equivalence machinery, offers limited empirical comparisons and runtime impact, and shows that simple n-grams often outperform the proposed RNN strategy. Strengthening the theoretical foundations, broadening baselines, and demonstrating robust gains on harder, diverse targets would make this a compelling NeurIPS paper.
