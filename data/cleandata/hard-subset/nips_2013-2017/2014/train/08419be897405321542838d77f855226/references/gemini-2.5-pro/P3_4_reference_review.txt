This paper presents a novel and ambitious framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities for a given symbolic expression. The core idea is to define a space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then learn an efficient search strategy to explore this space. The search is guided by models trained on solutions to simpler problems, embodying a form of curriculum learning. The authors propose two main learning-based search strategies: an n-gram model over sub-expressions and, more novelly, a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions. The framework is successfully applied to several families of complex polynomial expressions, discovering new, non-obvious identities that reduce computational complexity from exponential or high-order polynomial to low-order polynomial time.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength is its high degree of originality. The application of modern machine learning techniques, particularly deep learning, to the problem of symbolic identity discovery is a fresh and exciting direction. It bridges the gap between the symbolic reasoning community and the statistical pattern recognition focus of NIPS. The potential impact is significant; such a system could be integrated into compilers for automatic optimization, or used as a tool for mathematicians and scientists to discover new, efficient formulas, as demonstrated by the potential applications to RBM partition functions.

2.  **Strong Technical Contributions:** The paper introduces several clever technical ideas.
    *   The use of a numerical representation (evaluating expressions over a finite field Zp) to efficiently check for equality is a highly pragmatic solution to the bottleneck of symbolic manipulation.
    *   The development of a continuous, learned representation for symbolic expressions via an RNN is a standout contribution. This conceptual leap, analogous to word embeddings in NLP, could unlock the application of a wide array of machine learning tools to symbolic domains, a direction with vast potential.
    *   The overall framework, combining a grammar-based search with a learned search policy trained via curriculum learning, is elegant and well-motivated.

3.  **Compelling Empirical Results:** The experiments are well-designed and the results are impressive. The authors don't just show that their method is faster; they demonstrate the discovery of previously unknown identities for non-trivial expression families. The fact that the learning-based strategies (n-gram and RNN) significantly outperform random search on complex problems (high *k*) provides strong evidence for the central claim of the paper. The success on families like `Sym-k` and `RBM-k` is particularly compelling, as these have direct relevance to combinatorics and machine learning.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Scope of the Grammar:** The framework, as presented, is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of a single variable with a scalar output. While the authors acknowledge this, the limitations are significant and temper the claims of generality. The true challenge in automated formula discovery lies in handling a richer set of operations (e.g., trigonometric, exponential, conditional, recursive), which could introduce issues like termination and a much more complex search space. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the specific challenges in extending the grammar and how the learning framework might adapt.

2.  **Analysis of n-gram vs. RNN Performance:** It is somewhat surprising that the simpler n-gram models consistently outperform the more sophisticated RNN strategy. The paper notes this result but does not provide a deep analysis as to *why*. A likely explanation is that the solutions for the tested families exhibit strong, local, repetitive patterns (as seen in Example 2), which are perfectly suited for n-gram models. The RNN may struggle to capture this specific type of long-range periodic structure. A deeper investigation into the failure modes of the RNN and the structural properties of the discovered solutions would strengthen the paper and provide valuable insights for future work in this area.

3.  **Clarity on RNN Training:** The RNN is a core contribution, but its training details are presented as a list of "crucial" tricks (identity initialization, curriculum, high learning rate for the final layer, etc.). While this is helpful for reproducibility, it lacks a more principled explanation. For instance, why does the network overfit so catastrophically without identity initialization? This suggests that training these models on symbolic trees is highly non-trivial, and a more thorough discussion of the optimization landscape and the role of these specific techniques would be valuable.

4.  **Baselines:** The random search is a necessary but weak baseline. The field of program synthesis and symbolic regression has explored more sophisticated search techniques (e.g., genetic programming, stochastic search with heuristics). While a direct comparison might be difficult due to different problem formulations, acknowledging and briefly discussing how this work relates to non-learning-based guided search methods would better contextualize the contribution.

**Summary of Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound, the claims are well-supported by experiments, and the work is complete. The authors are honest about the performance of their different models.
*   **Clarity:** Good. The paper is generally well-written and organized. The core ideas are conveyed effectively. As noted, certain sections, particularly the RNN training, could benefit from more detailed explanations.
*   **Originality:** Excellent. This work is highly novel in its problem formulation and its application of deep learning to a symbolic domain. It clearly differs from prior work in symbolic computation (which is typically rule-based) and theorem proving.
*   **Significance:** High. The results are important and thought-provoking. The idea of learning continuous representations for symbolic math could be highly influential. The framework has clear potential for practical applications and for inspiring further research at the intersection of ML and symbolic reasoning.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong, innovative, and well-executed paper that opens up a promising new research direction. The weaknesses identified are primarily limitations in scope and opportunities for deeper analysis, rather than fundamental flaws. The work is a perfect fit for the NIPS community, as it showcases how core machine learning principles can be brought to bear on fundamental problems in computation and AI. I strongly recommend acceptance.
