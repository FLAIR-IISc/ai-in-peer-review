

# Peer Review: "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors address the problem of finding symbolic expressions that compute the same result as a target expression but with lower computational complexity. Their approach combines attribute grammars to represent symbolic expressions with machine learning methods (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for efficient identities. The key insight is that learning from simpler expressions can improve the search for more complex identities, overcoming the combinatorial explosion that plagues brute-force enumeration.

The paper relates to previous work by borrowing concepts from compiler construction (attribute grammars), natural language processing (n-gram models and RNNs), and symbolic computation (Maple, Mathematica). While attribute grammars have been used in other domains (e.g., knapsack problems, bioinformatics), the authors are the first to apply them to discover mathematical identities. The paper also builds on recent work in learning continuous representations for symbolic structures, extending techniques from NLP to mathematical domains.

## Strengths

1. **Novel application and framework**: The paper makes a significant contribution by applying machine learning to the problem of discovering efficient mathematical identities. This is a new application domain for attribute grammars and machine learning, and the framework is well-structured and technically sound.

2. **Technical contribution**: The continuous representation of symbolic expressions using RNNs is particularly noteworthy. The paper demonstrates that a recursive neural network can learn meaningful representations of mathematical expressions, which could potentially enable many other machine learning techniques to be applied to symbolic computation.

3. **Concrete results**: The paper provides specific examples of new mathematical identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n^3) to O(n^2) (e.g., for sum(sum(A*B)) and higher-degree polynomial expressions). These are significant improvements that are not easily discovered manually.

4. **Experimental validation**: The paper includes thorough experiments across multiple families of expressions, comparing random search with n-gram and RNN-based approaches. The results clearly demonstrate the advantages of learning-based search strategies for complex expressions.

5. **Clear presentation**: The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the framework, sufficient technical details for reproducibility, and helpful examples (Figures 1-3) that illustrate the approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Restricted problem scope**: The paper focuses on homogeneous polynomials of a single matrix or vector, which limits the applicability of the framework. While the authors acknowledge this in Section 8, the paper would be stronger with a more detailed discussion of how the framework could be extended to more general mathematical expressions.

2. **Limited comparison with existing symbolic computation tools**: The paper mentions symbolic computation engines like Maple and Mathematica in related work, but doesn't provide a concrete comparison of their approach with these tools. It would be valuable to understand how the authors' method differs from and improves upon existing symbolic simplification techniques.

3. **Scalability limitations**: The approach struggles with the most complex family of expressions ((RBM-2)^k), where none of the learning approaches outperform random search for k > 5. The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why this is the case or how to address it.

4. **Lack of theoretical analysis**: While the paper provides empirical results, it lacks theoretical analysis of the complexity guarantees or conditions under which the learning approaches will be effective.

5. **Limited discussion of broader applicability**: The paper mentions potential applications to machine learning (e.g., dropout, RBM partition functions), but doesn't elaborate on how these would be implemented or the practical impact of these discoveries.

## Evaluation Against Review Criteria

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The methodology is carefully explained, and the experimental results clearly demonstrate the advantages of the learning-based approach. The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding the (RBM-2)^k family. The paper is complete, with sufficient detail for reproduction.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The concepts are introduced logically, with helpful examples and figures. The notation is consistent throughout, and the paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results.

**Originality**: The paper makes a novel contribution by combining attribute grammars with machine learning for a new application domain. The continuous representation of symbolic expressions using RNNs is particularly original. The paper properly situates itself within related work, demonstrating how it differs from previous approaches.

**Significance**: The paper addresses an important problem in computational mathematics with practical implications for efficiency in machine learning and scientific computing. The discovered identities represent genuine improvements in computational complexity, and the framework could potentially be extended to broader mathematical domains. The continuous representation approach has broader implications for applying machine learning to symbolic computation.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- Novel combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for discovering mathematical identities
- Demonstrated success in finding previously undiscovered identities with significant computational improvements
- Technical soundness and clear presentation
- Contribution of continuous representations for symbolic expressions
- Potential to impact areas like machine learning (e.g., efficient computation of RBM partition functions)
- Well-executed experiments with clear comparison to baseline approaches

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- Limited scope to homogeneous polynomials of a single matrix/vector
- Struggles with the most complex family of expressions
- Limited comparison with existing symbolic computation tools
- Lack of theoretical analysis of the approach

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The contributions are significant, the methodology is sound, and the results are compelling. While the scope is limited, the paper provides a solid foundation for future work in applying machine learning to symbolic computation. The continuous representation of symbolic expressions is particularly promising and could have broader implications for the field.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation, a domain that has seen limited exploration in recent years. The authors have successfully demonstrated how learning can guide the search for efficient mathematical identities, opening up new possibilities for applying machine learning techniques to symbolic domains.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. In the related work section, provide a more detailed comparison with existing symbolic computation tools like Maple and Mathematica, explaining how the proposed approach differs from and improves upon these systems.

2. Expand the discussion in Section 8 about potential extensions to more general mathematical domains, including a discussion of the additional challenges that would need to be addressed.

3. Consider adding a brief theoretical analysis of the conditions under which the learning approaches are likely to be effective.

4. Include a more detailed analysis of why the (RBM-2)^k family is particularly challenging, to help guide future work in addressing this limitation.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The framework and techniques presented have the potential to be applied to a wide range of mathematical and computational problems, and the continuous representation approach could be particularly influential in future work.
