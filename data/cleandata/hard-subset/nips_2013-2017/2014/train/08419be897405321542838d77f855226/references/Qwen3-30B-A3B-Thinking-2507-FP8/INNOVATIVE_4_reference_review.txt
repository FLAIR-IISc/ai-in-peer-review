

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning to guide the search within a grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions. The authors propose a grammar framework based on attribute grammars, which allows symbolic expressions to be represented as sequences of grammar rules. They then introduce machine learning techniques—specifically n-gram models and a recursive neural network—to learn search strategies that selectively explore grammar trees likely to yield efficient expressions, rather than relying on brute-force or random search.

The core innovation lies in applying machine learning to guide the search for mathematical identities, rather than using hand-designed heuristics. The authors demonstrate that training on simpler expressions enables the discovery of complex identities that are intractable for manual derivation or exhaustive search. For instance, they discover identities that reduce computation from O(n^3) to O(n^2) for certain matrix operations (Example 1) and find patterns for higher-degree polynomial expressions (Example 2) that would be nearly impossible to discover through conventional means.

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) a grammar framework for efficient identity discovery, (2) integration of machine learning into this framework, (3) a novel application of recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of mathematical structures, and (4) the discovery of previously unknown mathematical identities with significant computational advantages.

The work is highly original in its application of machine learning techniques to a symbolic computation problem. While attribute grammars have been used in other domains (e.g., compiler construction, bioinformatics), their application to discovering mathematical identities with machine learning guidance is novel. The paper also makes an interesting connection to natural language processing, borrowing n-gram models and recursive neural networks from that domain to solve a problem in symbolic mathematics. This cross-domain transfer of ideas is particularly compelling.

The significance of this work is substantial. The discovered identities could have practical implications in machine learning (e.g., for efficient dropout implementation or RBM partition function computation) and broader applications in symbolic computation. The framework's potential for integration with compilers or optimization tools represents a meaningful step toward making symbolic computation more accessible to machine learning techniques.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, with results showing that the learning-based approaches significantly outperform random search for complex expressions. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of different learning strategies (n-grams, RNNs) across multiple families of mathematical expressions, including those with exponential naive complexity (like RBM-1 and RBM-2 families).

While the scope is limited to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree (a reasonable restriction for their proof-of-concept), this limitation is acknowledged and does not detract from the novelty of the approach. The paper also properly references related work in theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation, distinguishing their learning-based approach from previous rule-based systems like Mathematica or Maple.

The paper's main weakness is that it doesn't fully explore the broader implications of the continuous representations for symbolic expressions. While the RNN representation is demonstrated for classification, the paper could have more explicitly discussed how this enables other machine learning applications on symbolic data. However, as a proof-of-concept for their core problem, this limitation is minor.

From a novelty perspective, this paper offers a bold approach to a challenging problem in symbolic computation. The integration of machine learning to guide the search for mathematical identities is both creative and impactful, representing a significant departure from traditional approaches in the field. The paper's claim of discovering previously unknown identities with substantial complexity reductions is supported by their experiments.

The authors have clearly demonstrated how machine learning can be applied to guide combinatorial search in a symbolic domain—a problem that has been notoriously difficult for AI. This work opens up new possibilities for applying machine learning techniques to symbolic computation problems that have traditionally been approached with rule-based systems.

In conclusion, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field by introducing a novel framework that combines attribute grammars with machine learning to discover efficient mathematical identities. The work is original, significant, and demonstrates a creative application of machine learning techniques to a challenging symbolic problem. While the scope is somewhat limited, this is a reasonable starting point for what could become a broader framework for symbolic computation.

I recommend acceptance as a significant contribution that pushes the boundaries of what machine learning can do in symbolic domains. The paper's novelty and potential impact on both theoretical and applied aspects of symbolic computation outweigh the minor methodological limitations.
