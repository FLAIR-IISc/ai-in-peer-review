This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent expressions as trees constructed via an attribute grammar and then leverage machine learning to guide the search for more efficient equivalent expressions. The authors explore both n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to learn search strategies, demonstrating significant improvements in handling complex expressions compared to random search. The work includes several compelling examples where the discovered identities offer substantial computational savings, and the authors make code and data publicly available.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for automated mathematical identity discovery is a genuinely novel approach. This is a significant departure from traditional symbolic computation systems that rely on rule-based simplification.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results are convincing. The authors demonstrate the ability to discover efficient identities for expressions where naive evaluation is significantly more costly. The examples provided (O(n3) to O(n2), exponential to O(n2) or O(n3)) are impactful.
* **Technical Soundness:** The framework appears technically sound. The use of attribute grammars provides a well-defined search space, and the integration of machine learning offers a principled way to navigate it. The numerical representation and learned representation with RNNs are well-motivated and effectively employed.
* **Clarity (generally good):** The paper is generally well-written and the core ideas are clearly explained. The examples are helpful in illustrating the concepts.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors have made their code and data publicly available, which greatly enhances the reproducibility of their results.
* **Significance:** The potential impact is significant. Automating the discovery of mathematical identities could have applications in compiler optimization, symbolic computation, and potentially even in identifying efficient algorithms in other domains.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Restricted Scope:** The focus on homogeneous polynomials of degree *k* with a single matrix variable is a significant limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, it restricts the broader applicability of the method. Extending the framework to more general expressions (e.g., those involving trigonometric functions, arbitrary variables, or higher-order tensors) would be a major step forward.
* **n-gram Performance:** While the n-gram models perform well on some tasks, the authors note they struggle with expressions lacking simple repetitive structures. This suggests a limited generalization ability.  A more in-depth analysis of *why* n-grams succeed in certain cases and fail in others would be valuable.
* **RNN Performance:** The RNN performance is generally not better than the n-gram models. Although it performs slightly better on one particular problem, the authors themselves acknowledge this is a limited result. More exploration of RNN architectures and training techniques may be needed to unlock its full potential. The importance of dropout is well highlighted, though.
* **Integer Descriptor Limitations:** The integer-based descriptor, using modulo arithmetic, is a clever way to avoid numerical issues, but its effectiveness is dependent on choosing a sufficiently large prime number *p*.  The paper doesn't elaborate on how *p* is chosen or its impact on performance. A discussion of potential issues with descriptor collisions, even with a large *p*, would be beneficial.
* **Scalability:** While the authors demonstrate improvements over naive approaches, the scalability of the method to even higher degree polynomials or more complex expressions remains an open question. The exponential growth of the search space is a fundamental challenge.
* **Related Work Depth:** While the related work section is reasonably comprehensive, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of techniques from automated theorem proving and symbolic regression, especially recent advances beyond the cited papers.  A connection to program synthesis would also be relevant.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
* Demonstrably effective in discovering efficient mathematical identities.
* Publicly available code and data promote reproducibility.
* Potential for significant impact in various fields.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Restricted scope limits the generalizability of the method.
* Performance of RNNs is not compelling.
* Scalability concerns remain unaddressed.
* Could benefit from a more thorough exploration of related work.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel approach and promising results. While the restricted scope and limited RNN performance are weaknesses, the overall contribution is significant. The paper demonstrates a clear technical advance and opens up new avenues for research in automated mathematical identity discovery. I believe the strengths outweigh the weaknesses, but recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. 

**Suggested Revisions:**

1. **Discussion of Scope:** Expand the discussion of the limitations of the current scope (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix variable) and outline potential directions for generalization.
2. **RNN Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the RNN’s performance, including an investigation of different architectures, training techniques, and potential bottlenecks.
3. **Scalability Analysis:** Discuss the scalability of the method, even if qualitatively.  What are the key factors limiting scalability, and what potential strategies could be used to address them?
4. **Integer Descriptor:**  Elaborate on the selection of the prime number *p* used in the integer descriptor and discuss the potential for collisions.
5. **Related Work:** Expand the related work section to include more recent advances in automated theorem proving, symbolic regression, and program synthesis.
