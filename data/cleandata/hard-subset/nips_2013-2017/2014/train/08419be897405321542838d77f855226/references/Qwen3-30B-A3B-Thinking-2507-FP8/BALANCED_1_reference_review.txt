

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient versions of mathematical expressions using attribute grammars combined with machine learning. The authors address the problem of finding symbolic expressions equivalent to a target but with lower computational complexity. They frame this as a search problem over grammar trees, where the search is guided by learning from simpler expressions. The key innovation is integrating machine learning (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) into the grammar search process, enabling the discovery of identities that would be intractable with brute-force or random search.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) a grammar framework for symbolic expression discovery, (2) a demonstration that learning from simpler expressions improves search for complex ones, (3) a novel application of RNNs for continuous representation of symbolic structures, and (4) discovery of several new mathematical identities with significant complexity reductions.

**Strengths:**
The paper is technically sound with clear theoretical foundations and well-executed experiments. The problem statement is well-defined within a constrained but meaningful domain (homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree), which avoids undecidability issues while still being practically relevant. The authors demonstrate a clear advantage of their learning-based approach over random search for complex expressions (e.g., reducing search time from hundreds of seconds to tens of seconds for k=5 in the RBM-2 family). The framework's application to matrix operations (Examples 1 and 2) is particularly compelling, showing how the approach can discover non-intuitive identities that improve computational complexity from O(n³) to O(n²).

The paper's connection to NLP is well-motivated and executed, with appropriate citations to relevant work (e.g., Socher et al. for RNNs on symbolic structures). The learned representation of symbolic expressions (Section 4.2) is a significant contribution, demonstrating that continuous embeddings can capture mathematical equivalence—a potentially broad contribution to symbolic computation. The experimental design is thorough, with careful consideration of representation methods (numerical vs. learned), search strategies (random, n-gram, RNN), and multiple expression families with varying complexity.

**Weaknesses:**
While the framework is impressive within its constrained domain, the scope limitations are substantial. The focus on homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with a single matrix/vector input significantly restricts applicability to more general mathematical contexts. The paper acknowledges this limitation (Section 8) but doesn't sufficiently explore how to extend the framework to more complex mathematical structures (e.g., trigonometric functions, recursion), which would be necessary for broader impact.

The experimental results present a curious paradox: the n-gram models outperform the RNNs across most benchmarks, despite the RNN being a more sophisticated model. The paper doesn't adequately explain this discrepancy. The RNN's advantage in the RBM-2 case (where it found solutions faster than random search) is noted, but the authors don't explore why the simpler n-gram models generalize better for the discovered patterns. This is a missed opportunity to understand the relationship between the structure of the discovered identities and the learning approaches.

The significance section could be strengthened by more concrete examples of how these discovered identities would improve practical machine learning systems. While the paper mentions potential applications to dropout and RBM partition functions, it doesn't quantify the computational savings or provide implementation details. The connection between the mathematical identities and practical ML algorithms needs more elaboration to demonstrate real-world impact.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents an interesting and technically sound approach to a non-trivial problem at the intersection of symbolic computation and machine learning. The framework is novel, the experiments are well-designed, and the discovered identities are genuinely impressive within the constrained domain. However, the scope limitations and the unexplained performance gap between n-grams and RNNs prevent it from being a strong candidate for NIPS.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Address the n-gram vs. RNN performance discrepancy by analyzing the structural properties of the discovered identities that make them amenable to n-gram modeling but not to RNNs.
2. Expand the discussion on how the framework could be extended to more complex mathematical domains (beyond homogeneous polynomials), including potential challenges and modifications to the grammar.
3. Provide more concrete examples of how the discovered identities would improve practical machine learning algorithms (beyond the vague mention of RBM partition functions).
4. Include a more detailed comparison with alternative approaches to symbolic identity discovery (even if they're not directly comparable).
5. In the supplementary material, provide more details about the discovered identities, including their mathematical significance beyond just computational complexity.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the intersection of symbolic computation and machine learning, but to reach the standard of NIPS, it would benefit from addressing the scope limitations and providing clearer pathways to broader impact. With these improvements, it could become a strong candidate for acceptance. As it stands, it's an interesting technical contribution within a narrow domain, but the significance claims need stronger justification.
