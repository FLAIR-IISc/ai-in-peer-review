## Peer Review for "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient versions of mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions as trees built from an attribute grammar, and then use machine learning (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for efficient alternatives to a given target expression. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several families of expressions, achieving speedups (e.g., O(n<sup>2</sup>) vs. O(n<sup>3</sup>)) and, importantly, discovering identities that are non-trivial and, as far as they know, novel. The work bridges symbolic computation and machine learning, offering a promising approach to automated mathematical discovery.

**Related Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within related fields like theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, and highlight the key differences – primarily a focus on polynomial expressions of fixed degree, allowing for a more manageable search space. They appropriately acknowledge symbolic computation engines (Maple, Mathematica) but point out their lack of learning-based approaches. The related work section is comprehensive and covers relevant advancements in NLP (grammars, RNNs) and representation learning, demonstrating a good understanding of the broader landscape.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for symbolic identity discovery is a genuinely novel approach.  The results indicate the framework can uncover identities beyond manual derivation or brute-force search.
* **Empirical Results:** The paper provides compelling evidence of the framework's efficacy. Demonstrating speedups, particularly on families where naive evaluation is exponentially complex, is a significant achievement.  The results in Table 2 showcase the RNN's ability to learn a useful representation of symbolic expressions.
* **Well-Defined Framework:** The use of attribute grammars provides a solid, formal foundation for the search process. The problem definition is clearly stated, and the notation is consistent.
* **Practicality:** The publicly available code and data (https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning) promote reproducibility and further research.
* **Potential Impact:** The ability to automatically discover efficient identities has potential applications in compiler optimization, machine learning (efficient RBM calculations), and other areas.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The experiments systematically evaluate different search strategies (random, n-gram, RNN) across various expression families and degrees.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Grammar Limitations:** The restricted setting – homogeneous polynomials of degree *k* with a single matrix variable – limits the broader applicability of the framework. While the authors acknowledge this, a more in-depth discussion of potential extensions and challenges would be beneficial.
* **RNN Performance:** While the RNN shows promising results, the n-gram models consistently outperform it for some families (e.g., (∑ AAT)<sup>k</sup>). A deeper analysis of *why* the RNN struggles in these cases would be valuable. It’s not clear if the RNN’s ability to capture long-range dependencies is fully leveraged or hampered by the grammar structure.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper mentions cross-validation for selecting the RNN’s hidden layer size (l=30) but provides limited detail on other hyperparameters (e.g., learning rates, dropout rates, n-gram order). More transparency in this regard would strengthen the experimental reproducibility.
* **Scalability:** While the framework works for expressions up to *k*=15, the search space grows rapidly. The authors acknowledge this, but further discussion of scalability limitations and potential mitigation strategies (e.g., parallelization, more sophisticated search algorithms) would be helpful.
* **Descriptor Choice:** The use of integer-based descriptors modulo a prime *p* is a clever solution to avoid numerical issues. However, the choice of *p* and its impact on the accuracy of the matching process are not fully discussed.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical framework is well-defined, and the experimental results are convincingly presented. The authors demonstrate careful evaluation of both strengths and weaknesses.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, although some sections (e.g., the description of the RNN training process) could benefit from further clarification. The use of examples is helpful.
* **Originality:** The work is highly original, combining ideas from multiple fields in a novel way. The discovered identities demonstrate a non-trivial contribution to the field.
* **Significance:** The results are significant and have the potential to impact areas like compiler optimization and machine learning. The automated discovery of efficient mathematical identities is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound framework for automated mathematical identity discovery.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
* The work bridges symbolic computation and machine learning, offering a promising new direction for research.
* The publicly available code and data promote reproducibility.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The restricted setting of the grammar limits the broader applicability of the framework.
* The RNN performance is not consistently superior to n-gram models.
* A more thorough discussion of scalability limitations and hyperparameter tuning would strengthen the paper.




**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend **acceptance** of this paper.  Despite some minor weaknesses, the work is highly original, technically sound, and presents significant and potentially impactful results. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field, and the framework shows great promise for future research. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above (e.g., more detailed discussion of RNN performance and scalability) could further strengthen the paper, but the current version is already of high quality and worthy of inclusion in NIPS.
