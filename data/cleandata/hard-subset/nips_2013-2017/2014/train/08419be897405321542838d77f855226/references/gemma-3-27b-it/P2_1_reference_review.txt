## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient versions of mathematical identities. It leverages attribute grammars to represent symbolic expressions and employs machine learning (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for more efficient formulations. The core idea is to learn a search strategy from solutions to simpler problems to tackle more complex ones. The authors demonstrate their approach on several families of expressions, discovering identities that are, to their knowledge, previously unknown and offer significant computational improvements.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for symbolic identity discovery is a genuinely novel approach. This departs from traditional rule-based simplification methods and opens up new avenues for automating mathematical reasoning.
* **Significance:** The potential impact is substantial. Discovering more efficient mathematical identities directly translates to performance gains in various applications, particularly in machine learning where these identities could be used to optimize computations (e.g., RBM partition function). The examples provided (reducing O(n^3) to O(n^2)) are compelling.
* **Technical Soundness:** The framework appears well-designed and logically sound. The use of attribute grammars provides a structured way to explore the search space. The integer-based numerical representation addresses potential floating-point issues. The RNN-based representation is a clever application of recent advances in deep learning to a symbolic domain.
* **Experimental Validation:** The authors provide experimental results across multiple families of expressions, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.  The inclusion of a comparison against random search is valuable.
* **Code Release:** The public availability of code and data is a major strength, promoting reproducibility and facilitating further research.
* **Clarity:**  The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts. The examples, particularly Example 1 and 2, effectively illustrate the benefits of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability:** While the learning approaches improve performance compared to random search, the results in Figure 3 demonstrate that the approach struggles with increasingly complex expressions (RBM-2 family). The search space grows rapidly, and the gains from learning diminish. The authors acknowledge this, but a more in-depth discussion of the limitations of scalability would be beneficial.
* **Grammar Limitations:** The authors restrict the problem to homogeneous polynomials. While this simplifies the problem, it limits the applicability of the approach. It’s unclear how easily the framework could be extended to handle a wider range of mathematical expressions.
* **RNN Performance:** The n-gram models consistently outperform the RNN. The authors mention that the RNN's advantage is seen in finding *faster* solutions for a specific case, but the overall accuracy isn’t superior. A deeper analysis of why the RNN underperforms, despite its potential, would be valuable. Was the architecture sufficient? Was the training data adequate?
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper mentions cross-validation for selecting the RNN hidden layer size, but provides limited details about other hyperparameters used in the experiments (e.g., learning rates, n-gram order).
* **Related Work Depth:** While the related work section is adequate, a more detailed discussion of recent advances in symbolic regression and automated theorem proving (beyond those cited) would strengthen the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and promising approach to a challenging problem. The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning is innovative, and the experimental results demonstrate the potential of the framework. While scalability remains a concern and the RNN performance is somewhat disappointing, the overall contribution is significant. The authors have addressed the review criteria well, particularly regarding technical soundness, clarity, and originality.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to a long-standing problem in mathematics and computer science.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the potential for significant performance gains.
* The code and data are publicly available, promoting reproducibility.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Scalability limitations may hinder the applicability of the approach to very complex expressions.
* The RNN performance is not as strong as the n-gram models, suggesting that further research is needed to leverage deep learning effectively.

**Rating:**

**Strong Accept (7/10)** -  The paper demonstrates a significant advance in the field. The weaknesses are not critical and do not detract substantially from the overall contribution.  I believe this work will stimulate further research in the area of automated mathematical discovery.
