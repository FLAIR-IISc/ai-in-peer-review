

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper presents a genuinely novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The core innovation lies in applying attribute grammars to represent symbolic expressions and then using machine learning techniques (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for computationally efficient versions of target expressions. This represents a bold and significant departure from previous work in symbolic computation, which has largely relied on hand-designed heuristics or brute-force search.

The authors demonstrate that by training on solutions to simpler expressions, their framework can discover complex mathematical identities that would be intractable to find through random search or manual derivation. For instance, they successfully transform O(n³) expressions into O(n²) versions (Example 1) and discover identities for k=6 matrix expressions that would require exhaustive search over 9,785 possible grammar trees (Example 2). These discoveries are explicitly stated to be previously undiscovered, which is a significant contribution to mathematical knowledge.

The paper's most compelling aspect is its novel application of machine learning to a problem that hasn't been previously explored in the machine learning literature. While there is related work in theorem proving and program induction, the authors clearly differentiate their approach by focusing on computational complexity reduction through learning, rather than on proof verification or program synthesis. The adaptation of NLP techniques (n-grams, recursive neural networks) to symbolic computation is particularly insightful, and the authors' demonstration of continuous embeddings for mathematical structures opens up new possibilities for applying machine learning to symbolic domains.

The experimental results strongly support the main claims. The learning-based approaches consistently outperform random search for complex expressions (Figure 3), with n-gram models particularly effective at capturing repetitive patterns in the discovered identities (as seen in Example 2). The expression classification results (Table 2) demonstrate that the learned representation is effective (≤5% error rate), which is a promising foundation for the approach.

While the problem is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of degree k with a single matrix or vector variable, the authors thoughtfully address this limitation in Section 8, discussing how the framework could be extended to broader mathematical domains. The paper's focus on a manageable domain actually strengthens the contribution, as it allows the authors to thoroughly explore the core idea without getting lost in excessive complexity.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide thorough background on related work in theorem proving, program induction, and NLP, carefully distinguishing their approach. The examples are particularly effective at demonstrating the value of their method, with concrete computational complexity improvements that are easy to appreciate.

The significance of this work extends beyond the specific mathematical identities discovered. The authors rightly point out that their framework could potentially be applied to optimize machine learning algorithms (e.g., finding closed-form versions of dropout or efficient RBM partition function calculations). More broadly, the paper demonstrates a promising new direction for applying machine learning to symbolic computation, which could have implications for compiler design, mathematical software, and even automated theorem proving.

While the paper could have provided more thorough analysis of why n-grams outperformed the RNN (particularly for the repetitive patterns in the matrix examples), this is a minor limitation given the overall success of the approach. The authors' focus on novelty and significant results rather than methodological perfection aligns well with NIPS's emphasis on bold, innovative contributions.

This paper represents a genuinely novel application of machine learning to a domain where it has been underexplored. The ability to automatically discover mathematical identities with significant computational improvements is a significant contribution that opens up new research directions. The work is technically sound, well-justified, and demonstrates clear value in a domain with potential practical applications.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Strengths:**
- Truly novel application of machine learning to mathematical identity discovery
- Demonstrated discovery of previously undiscovered identities with significant computational improvements
- Effective integration of NLP techniques into symbolic computation
- Clear demonstration of the framework's ability to solve problems intractable to manual discovery or random search
- Strong connection to potential practical applications (compiler optimization, machine learning algorithms)

**Weaknesses:**
- Problem restricted to homogeneous polynomials with single matrix/vector variable (though the authors acknowledge this limitation)
- Limited exploration of why n-grams outperformed RNNs for the specific patterns discovered
- Some scope limitations for broader mathematical applications (though this is addressed in the discussion)

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by introducing a novel approach to symbolic computation that leverages machine learning in a way that's not just incremental but represents a new direction for the field. The discoveries themselves are impressive, and the framework has clear potential for broader application. Given the emphasis on novelty at NIPS, this paper's strong contribution makes it a clear accept.
