This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities for a given symbolic target expression. The core idea is to represent expressions using an attribute grammar and to search over the space of valid expression trees. As brute-force or random search is intractable for complex expressions, the authors propose using machine learning to guide the search. They train models (n-grams and a recursive neural network) on solutions discovered for simpler expressions to learn a search strategy for more complex targets. The paper also introduces a method for learning a continuous vector representation for symbolic expressions using a recursive neural network, which is pre-trained on an expression equivalence classification task. Experiments on five families of polynomial expressions demonstrate that the learning-based search strategies significantly outperform random search, discovering several non-trivial identities that reduce computational complexity from exponential or O(n^3) to O(n^2) or O(n^3).

### Strengths

*   **Novelty and Ambition:** The problem of automatically discovering efficient mathematical identities is both challenging and significant. Applying machine learning techniques to guide search in a symbolic domain is a novel and ambitious direction with potentially broad impact.
*   **Elegant Framework:** The combination of an attribute grammar with a learned search strategy is an elegant approach. The curriculum-based learning paradigm, where solutions to simpler problems provide training data for more complex ones, is well-motivated and effective.
*   **Learned Representation:** The application of a recursive neural network to learn a continuous embedding for symbolic expressions is a valuable contribution. The high classification accuracy reported in Table 2 suggests this is a promising representation that could be useful for other symbolic reasoning tasks.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments clearly demonstrate the superiority of the learned search strategies (n-grams and RNN) over a random baseline, especially as the complexity of the target expressions increases (Figure 3). The discovery of new identities is a concrete and impressive outcome.

### Weaknesses

The paper, while containing promising ideas, suffers from a significant number of flaws in clarity, methodological rigor, and presentation that detract from its quality.

**1. Clarity and Precision:** The manuscript lacks the precision expected for a NIPS publication.
*   **Ambiguous Notation:** The paper relies on "Matlab-style syntax" without defining the operators. The meaning of `sum(A, 1)`, the distinction between matrix `*` and element-wise `.*`, and the use of `’` for transpose should be explicitly defined in a table for clarity and reproducibility.
*   **Inconsistent Formatting:** There are numerous formatting inconsistencies, for instance, in the notation for computational complexity (e.g., `O ( n3 )`, `O(()n3)`, `O ( nk )` instead of the standard O(n^3)). This sloppiness pervades the paper.
*   **Erroneous Examples:** The expressions presented in Example 2 seem to be incorrect. The solutions for k=2 through k=6 appear to be cumulative copy-paste errors, which undermines confidence in the results presented. Similarly, the definitions of the expression families in Section 7.2 are confusing. For `( ∑ AAT)k`, the text refers to `bk/2c`, where `b` is undefined (presumably a typo for `k`). These definitions are critical to understanding the experiments and should be stated with mathematical precision.

**2. Methodological Gaps and Arbitrary Choices:** Several key methodological details are either poorly justified or seem arbitrary.
*   **Hard Constraints:** The constraint that a tree for a degree-`k` polynomial must use the input `A` exactly `k` times is a very strong heuristic that is not well-justified. Similarly, the ad-hoc removal of the matrix-multiplication rule for two of the experimental families feels like a way to force a particular outcome rather than allowing the search to discover it. A more principled approach would be to heavily penalize its complexity attribute.
*   **Probabilistic Equality Checking:** The numerical representation for checking expression equality is a probabilistic method. The authors should acknowledge this and cite relevant work like the Schwartz-Zippel lemma, which provides a theoretical foundation for such checks. The choice of N=1000 is stated without justification regarding the probability of collision.
*   **Arbitrary Hyperparameters:** Several model and training choices are presented without justification or ablation studies. For example, the n-gram weighting of `10k` is described as "effective" with no supporting evidence. In the RNN strategy, using a "fixed random vector" for unary operations is an unusual choice compared to alternatives like a zero vector or a separate parameter matrix. The `×100` larger learning rate for the final layer of the RNN is another red flag for delicate, possibly non-robust, hyperparameter tuning.

**3. Analysis and Discussion:**
*   **Overstated Claims:** The discussion makes several exaggerated claims. Suggesting the discovered identities "could potentially be used to give a closed-form version of dropout" is highly speculative and likely incorrect, as it misinterprets dropout's role as a stochastic regularizer. Similarly, implying that this method can "compute the RBM partition function efficiently" based on simplifying a few Taylor series terms is misleading, as computing the full partition function is known to be #P-hard.
*   **Lack of Deeper Analysis:** The experiments show that simpler n-gram models consistently outperform the more complex and computationally expensive RNN model. This is a significant finding, yet the paper offers no discussion or analysis as to why this might be the case. Is the structure of the solutions simply more amenable to local pattern matching? This is a missed opportunity for scientific insight.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Pro:**
*   Addresses a novel and important problem in symbolic mathematics with machine learning.
*   The overall framework of grammar-based search guided by curriculum learning is compelling.
*   The paper demonstrates empirically that learning dramatically improves the search process.
*   The work results in the discovery of new, useful mathematical identities.

**Con:**
*   The paper is marred by a lack of clarity, including ambiguous notation, inconsistent formatting, and erroneous examples.
*   Key methodological choices are poorly justified or arbitrary.
*   The discussion significantly overstates the potential implications of the work.
*   A key experimental outcome—the superiority of simple n-grams over a complex RNN—is not analyzed or discussed.

### Recommendation

While the core ideas in this paper are innovative and the high-level results are promising, the execution and presentation are not up to the standards of a top-tier conference. The large number of minor flaws—ranging from notational sloppiness to methodological gaps and unsubstantiated claims—collectively form a major barrier to accepting this work in its current form. The paper requires a thorough revision to address these issues of clarity, precision, and rigor. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**.
