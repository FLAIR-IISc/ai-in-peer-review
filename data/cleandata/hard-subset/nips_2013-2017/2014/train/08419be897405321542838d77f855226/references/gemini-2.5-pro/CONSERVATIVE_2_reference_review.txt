This paper proposes a framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is equivalent to a given target but has lower computational complexity. As the search space is intractably large, the authors propose using machine learning to guide the search. Specifically, they train n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) on solutions found for simpler expressions to learn a search strategy for more complex targets. The equivalence of expressions is checked by evaluating them on a set of random numerical inputs. The authors demonstrate their approach on five families of polynomial expressions, showing that the learning-based search strategies can find efficient identities for expressions of a higher degree than a random search baseline can.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses an important and challenging problem: the automatic optimization of symbolic computation. This has potential applications in areas like compiler design and speeding up scientific computing workloads.
*   The core idea of using machine learning, trained on simpler instances, to guide a combinatorial search in a symbolic domain is novel and interesting. It represents a creative synthesis of techniques from program induction, natural language processing, and AI search.
*   The proposed use of a recursive neural network to learn a continuous vector representation of symbolic expression trees is a novel contribution that could potentially enable the application of a wide range of continuous optimization and learning techniques to symbolic domains.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, while presenting an intriguing idea, suffers from several significant weaknesses in its methodology and evaluation that undermine the strength of its claims.

1.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** The primary baseline for the search strategy is a random search within the grammar. This is an exceptionally weak baseline. The field of symbolic computation has existed for decades, and systems like Mathematica and Maple employ sophisticated, albeit often heuristic, algorithms for expression simplification. While the authors correctly note that these systems do not use learning and may not explicitly optimize for computational complexity in the same way, a comparison is still essential. It is entirely possible that established simplification techniques could solve many of the presented problems. Similarly, the field of automated theorem proving has developed powerful search-guiding heuristics. By failing to compare against any established method, the paper leaves the reader unable to judge whether the proposed learning-based approach truly advances the state of the art or simply outperforms a naive random strategy.

2.  **Limited Scope and Questionable Generality:** The framework's effectiveness is demonstrated on only five specific families of homogeneous polynomials. The grammar itself is small and appears to be tailored to these problems. More concerningly, for two of the five families, the authors remove the matrix-matrix multiply rule from the grammar, which *guarantees* that any solution found will have a lower complexity class (O(n^2) vs. O(n^3)). This significantly constrains the problem, making it an artificial benchmark for the search algorithm rather than a genuine discovery task. The work provides little evidence that the approach would generalize to a broader class of mathematical expressions or that the learning strategies would be effective with a larger, more realistic grammar.

3.  **Methodological Soundness:**
    *   **Numerical Equivalence Checking:** The method for checking if two expressions are identical relies on evaluating them on N=1000 random inputs and checking for equality modulo a prime. This is a probabilistic, sampling-based approach. While practical, it lacks the formal guarantees required for symbolic manipulation. The paper provides no analysis of the probability of "collisions" (i.e., two functionally different expressions yielding the same numerical descriptor). In a domain where correctness is paramount, this heuristic approach is a significant concern.
    *   **RNN Training:** The success of the RNN model appears to depend on a specific set of training heuristics, such as identity matrix initialization and curriculum learning. The paper notes that without these, the model "overfits badly". This suggests that the learning process may be brittle and difficult to tune or apply to new problem domains, casting doubt on the robustness of this component of the framework.

4.  **Significance of Discovered Identities:** The paper claims the discovery of "many new mathematical identities" as a key contribution. However, the significance of these discoveries is not well-supported. The primary example shown (Example 2) is a highly regular and repetitive structure that is an ideal case for an n-gram model but may not represent a particularly deep or surprising mathematical result. The more complex identities are relegated to supplementary material, making it difficult for a reviewer to assess their novelty or importance.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-Acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel and creative application of machine learning to a difficult and important symbolic problem. The core idea of learning a search policy from simpler solved instances is compelling.
*   **Con-Acceptance:** The experimental evaluation is critically flawed due to the use of a weak random baseline and the lack of comparison to any established methods from symbolic computation. The problem scope is narrow and artificially constrained, raising doubts about the generality of the approach. The methodology relies on heuristics (e.g., numerical equivalence checking) without sufficient theoretical justification.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a promising research direction, it feels more like a preliminary proof-of-concept than a complete and rigorously validated piece of work. The claims of discovering novel identities and providing a general framework for symbolic optimization are not sufficiently supported by the limited and constrained experiments. The lack of comparison to relevant baselines from the mature field of symbolic computation is a major omission. For these reasons, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. The work would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough and realistic experimental evaluation against stronger baselines on a more diverse set of problems.
