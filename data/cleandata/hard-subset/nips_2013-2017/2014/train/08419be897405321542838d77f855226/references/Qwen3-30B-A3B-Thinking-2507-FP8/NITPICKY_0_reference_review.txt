

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars combined with machine learning techniques. The core idea is to use a grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions, with a search strategy learned from simpler expressions to discover more complex identities with reduced computational complexity.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds on attribute grammars [14] and applies machine learning techniques (n-grams and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for efficient expressions. It demonstrates success on several polynomial families, claiming significant complexity reductions from O(n^3) to O(n^2) or better. The work relates to recent NIPS papers on recursive neural networks [22, 23] and attribute grammars [7], but makes the novel contribution of applying these techniques to mathematical identity discovery.

## Strengths

The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by framing identity discovery as a search problem within an attribute grammar. The experimental results demonstrate that learning-based search strategies can discover identities that random search cannot, particularly for higher-degree expressions. The technical approach is well-motivated, and the results on the first two families (sum(AA^T)^k and sum((A.*A)A^T)^k) are promising.

## Weaknesses

Despite its interesting premise, the paper contains numerous methodological and technical flaws that undermine its scientific contribution:

1. **Inconsistent Mathematical Notation**: The paper uses inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., "O ( n3 )" vs "O(n^3)") and has multiple index errors in the examples (Example 1's target expression has inconsistent summation indices).

2. **Unjustified Technical Choices**: The paper claims "the weight matrix in the softmax classifier has much larger (×100) learning rate than the rest of the layers" but provides no theoretical justification or ablation study to support this claim.

3. **Questionable Numerical Representation**: The paper claims integer-based descriptors in Z_p "prevent both rounding issues as well as numerical overflow" but fails to prove that different expressions don't map to the same descriptor, nor does it explain how they choose p to avoid false positives.

4. **Inadequate Novelty Claims**: The paper states "the majority of these examples are too complex to be found manually or by exhaustive search and, as far as we are aware, are previously undiscovered" without providing evidence that these identities haven't been found in prior mathematical literature.

5. **Missing Experimental Details**: The paper doesn't provide standard deviations for the success rates in Figure 3, making it impossible to assess the statistical significance of the results. The claim that "the 3-gram model reliably finds solutions" lacks quantitative support.

6. **Unaddressed Limitations**: The paper fails to discuss why the RBM-2 family is fundamentally harder than the others, despite showing it's the most challenging. The restricted domain of homogeneous polynomials (only scalar outputs from single matrices) is acknowledged but not adequately justified.

7. **Formatting Issues**: Inconsistent parentheses in Example 2 (k=2 vs k=6 expressions), missing figure references (Fig. 2), and poor mathematical typography throughout undermine the paper's professionalism.

## Assessment

While the core idea of using machine learning to guide symbolic search is novel and potentially valuable, the paper's numerous technical flaws, inconsistent presentation, and lack of rigorous justification for key methodological choices prevent it from meeting NIPS's standards for a high-quality scientific contribution. The paper's claims about the novelty of discovered identities are unsubstantiated, and the numerical representation approach lacks theoretical grounding.

The paper's technical execution is particularly weak in its treatment of the integer-based descriptor method, where the paper claims benefits without providing evidence or proof of correctness. The experimental results, while promising for some families, are not presented with sufficient statistical rigor to support the authors' claims.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an interesting problem and makes a potentially valuable contribution, the numerous technical flaws, inconsistent presentation, and lack of rigorous justification for key methodological choices significantly undermine its scientific merit. The paper would require substantial revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication at NIPS. The authors would need to provide a formal analysis of their descriptor method, provide statistical evidence for their claims, and address the numerous notational and technical inconsistencies throughout the paper.
