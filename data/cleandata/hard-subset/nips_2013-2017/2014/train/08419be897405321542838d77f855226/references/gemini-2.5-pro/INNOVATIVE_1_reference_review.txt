This paper presents a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then to search this space for an expression that is equivalent to a given target but has lower computational complexity. Recognizing that a brute-force or random search is intractable for complex expressions, the authors propose a machine learning-based approach to guide the search. The system learns a search strategy by training on solutions discovered for simpler expressions, effectively a form of curriculum learning. The paper explores two main learning methods: n-gram models that capture common sub-structures in solution trees, and a more sophisticated recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous representation for symbolic expressions. This learned embedding is a key contribution, analogous to word embeddings in NLP, and allows for the application of continuous optimization techniques to this symbolic domain. The authors demonstrate the framework's effectiveness by discovering several new, non-trivial mathematical identities for families of expressions, including some related to machine learning models, which reduce complexity from exponential to polynomial time.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its exceptional novelty and ambition. It tackles a problem domain—automated symbolic mathematical discovery—that is largely unexplored by the mainstream machine learning community. This work represents a bold and exciting bridge between statistical machine learning and symbolic reasoning, opening up a promising new direction for research. This is not an incremental improvement on an existing method but rather the introduction of a new problem class and a compelling learning-based approach to solving it.

The technical contributions are significant. The idea of learning a search policy over a grammar of mathematical operations is powerful and well-executed. The curriculum learning strategy, where the system bootstraps its knowledge from simple problems to solve progressively harder ones, is both elegant and effective.

Furthermore, the development of a continuous, learned representation for symbolic expressions via a recursive neural network is a standout contribution. This technique for mapping discrete, structured mathematical expressions into a vector space has the potential for broad impact beyond the scope of this paper, enabling a wide range of ML techniques to be brought to bear on symbolic computation problems.

The empirical results are impressive and serve as strong validation for the proposed framework. The discovery of novel identities that provide exponential speedups (e.g., for the RBM-related families) is a concrete and significant outcome. It demonstrates that the approach is not merely a theoretical curiosity but a tool capable of producing genuinely useful mathematical results that are difficult to find by hand.

**Weaknesses**

While the vision and core ideas are outstanding, the current implementation has some limitations that could be addressed in future work.

1.  **Limited Scope of the Grammar:** The framework is currently demonstrated on a relatively restricted class of expressions: homogeneous polynomials involving a single variable and producing a scalar output. While this is a reasonable starting point for a proof-of-concept, the true potential of this approach lies in its generalization to a much richer grammar, including trigonometric functions, conditionals, and recursion. The authors acknowledge this, but the challenges in expanding the grammar and search space are non-trivial.

2.  **Relative Performance of Learning Models:** The experiments show that simpler n-gram models often outperform the more complex RNN-based search strategy. This might suggest that the chosen problem families have a highly repetitive local structure that is easily captured by counting subtrees. While the RNN's ability to perform reasonably well is still a success, and its improved speed on the most complex task is noted, its full potential on problems requiring more global, non-local reasoning is not yet fully demonstrated. This is a minor point, however, as the introduction of the RNN model and its learned representation is a key conceptual advance regardless of its performance on these specific tasks.

3.  **Equality Checking:** The reliance on numerical evaluation over finite fields to check for expression equivalence is a clever and practical solution. However, this could become a bottleneck as expressions become more complex, and it sidesteps the deeper challenges of symbolic equivalence checking.

**Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to the scope of the current implementation, which is understandable for a paper introducing such a novel research direction. The paper's strengths—its boldness, conceptual novelty, and impressive results—far outweigh these limitations. This work has the potential to inspire a great deal of follow-up research at the exciting intersection of machine learning and symbolic mathematics. It is a thought-provoking and significant contribution that is well-suited for the NIPS audience.
