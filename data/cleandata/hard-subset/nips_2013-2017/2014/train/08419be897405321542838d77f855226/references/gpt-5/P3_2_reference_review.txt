Summary
The paper proposes a framework to automatically discover algebraically equivalent but computationally cheaper expressions for a given target expression. The search space is defined by an attribute grammar over matrix/vector operations with cost annotations. Exact equivalence checking is replaced by randomized evaluation: each expression is mapped to an integer descriptor by evaluating on many random inputs modulo a large prime. Because many targets require sums of sub-expressions, the method solves a linear system over descriptors to find linear combinations of grammar trees that equal the target. To address the combinatorial explosion of grammar-tree enumeration, the authors learn search strategies from solutions to simpler problems: (i) n-gram models over subtrees, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees (pretrained via multi-class classification of equivalence classes) and then a softmax policy that predicts the next rule to apply. Experiments on several expression families (e.g., repeated sums of A A^T, elementwise-square variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, and sums arising in RBM partition functions) show that learned strategies discover efficient identities at higher degrees than random search; n-gram models often outperform the RNN, though the RNN finds some difficult cases faster. The paper includes illustrative examples such as reducing sum(sum(A*B)) from O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)).

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: using attribute grammars plus learned policies to guide symbolic search for low-complexity identities.
- Pragmatic correctness checking via randomized evaluation over Z_p; efficient and simple to integrate with learning.
- Nice use of linear combinations of trees to expand expressivity beyond single-tree matches.
- Learning signal reuse via curriculum (increasing degree k), which is natural for this domain.
- RNN-based continuous embeddings of algebraic expressions are interesting in their own right, potentially enabling broader ML-on-symbolic tasks.
- Empirically demonstrates substantial search efficiency gains over random for several families, discovering nontrivial reductions in computational complexity.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Correctness guarantees: The approach relies on randomized identity testing but provides no formal bound on collision probability or guidance on number/choice of primes and samples N. Without such bounds (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel style analysis, multiple primes), “discovered” identities could, in principle, be spurious. Similarly, solving F w = t mod p may yield coefficients that do not correspond to true integer/rational coefficients over reals. A robust verification phase (e.g., symbolic verification or evaluations over multiple primes with rigorous error bounds) is needed.
- Evaluation of the learned representation: The RNN classifier is trained and evaluated with train/test splits within the same equivalence classes. This measures memorization of class exemplars rather than generalization to unseen classes. Since the embedding is later used for policy learning, a more telling evaluation would hold out entire classes/families and measure transfer.
- Empirical baselines: The paper compares to random only. There is a sizable literature on search/optimization for programs/expressions that is not considered experimentally: equality-saturation-based rewriting with cost models, modern CAS rule systems with cost-aware strategies, superoptimization (e.g., Massalin; STOKE, Schkufza et al., 2013), Monte Carlo Tree Search or best-first/beam search with hand-crafted features. Even a simple beam search or A* with heuristic costs would be informative. Claiming novelty over CAS because they are “rule-based and do not use learning” is insufficient; a cost-aware rewrite system is a natural baseline.
- Search strategy details: The scheduler appears greedy (always pick highest-scored rule). Greedy selection risks myopic failures; beam search, lookahead, or backtracking budget could materially improve results. The paper does not report sensitivity to beam width or to exploration-exploitation trade-offs.
- Scope restrictions: Homogeneous polynomials in one variable with scalar output cover many of the reported families but limit broader impact. The introduction mentions multi-variable support, but experiments focus almost entirely on single-variable A. It is unclear how well the approach scales with more operators (e.g., trig, recursion) or multiple inputs where associativity/commutativity patterns explode.
- Reporting and clarity gaps:
  - The exact grammar (Table 1) and cost model are central but not detailed in the main text; reproducibility depends on them.
  - Descriptor size N, choice of prime(s), and probability-of-error analysis are not provided.
  - Linear-combination solver: constraints on coefficient sparsity/magnitude and how integer coefficients are recovered from mod-p solutions are not discussed.
  - For the discovered identities, only a few are shown; most are deferred to supplementary material. Including one or two “new” identities of higher complexity in the main text would strengthen the case.
- Practical significance: Some demonstrated identities (e.g., sum of entries of A B) are well known. For the harder families (RBM-2), the method does not scale beyond k=5, limiting immediate impact on those motivating applications.

Quality
- The methodology is sound at a high level, but missing theoretical assurances for identity testing and coefficient recovery. Experimental design lacks strong baselines and clearer ablations (e.g., effect of curriculum, identity initialization, dropout rates, beam width).

Clarity
- Generally readable, with useful examples and intuitions, but important implementation details are omitted. The RNN pretraining objective and curriculum schedule could be specified more precisely. Figures referenced (grammar table, examples) should be complete in the main text.

Originality
- Combining attribute grammars with learned search policies for algebraic identity discovery is novel and timely. The RNN embedding for symbolic expressions is a nice idea, although related NLP/tree-embedding lines are acknowledged. Closer connection to prior work on equality saturation, superoptimization, and program synthesis would clarify novelty boundaries.

Significance
- The idea of learning to guide symbolic search is promising and relevant to ML/PL/CA communities. However, the current evaluation does not yet demonstrate consistent advantages over strong search/rewriting baselines, and the scope restrictions limit immediate applicability. The work is a solid step toward learned symbolic optimization, with potential to be influential if strengthened.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide a formal error analysis for randomized identity testing; consider multiple primes and derive bounds on failure probability. Add a verification phase (e.g., symbolic proof) for reported identities.
- Compare to at least one cost-aware rewrite baseline (e.g., equality saturation with a simple cost model) and to non-greedy search (beam/A* or MCTS).
- Evaluate RNN transfer by holding out entire equivalence classes/families during pretraining; report whether embeddings improve policy learning over n-grams on those held-out families.
- Clarify grammar and cost model, descriptor parameters, and how mod-p solutions are translated to integer/rational coefficients.
- Explore invariance-aware modeling (e.g., encode commutativity/associativity via canonicalization or invariant architectures) to reduce sample complexity.
- Report beam width, search budget sensitivity, and include ablations for curriculum and identity initialization.

Recommendation
Borderline accept (6). The paper presents an interesting and novel direction—learning to guide symbolic identity discovery—with some compelling empirical evidence, but needs stronger validation, clearer methodology, and comparisons to established search/rewriting baselines to fully justify its impact.
