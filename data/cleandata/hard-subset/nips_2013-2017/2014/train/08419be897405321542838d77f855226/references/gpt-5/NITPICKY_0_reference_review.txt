Summary
The paper tackles the problem of discovering computationally efficient identities equivalent to a target symbolic expression. The authors define an attribute grammar over matrix/vector operations and search the space of grammar trees for expressions that match the target but have lower computational complexity. Matching is done via two representations: a numerical descriptor using evaluations modulo a large prime and a learned continuous embedding from a recursive neural network (RNN) trained to cluster expressions into semantic equivalence classes. To scale search, they learn strategies from solutions to simpler targets: n-gram counts over subtrees and an RNN-based policy that predicts the next rule. They report that learning-guided search finds substantially more complex identities than random exploration, with n-gram models often outperforming the RNN. They showcase examples, including reducing sum(sum(A*B)) and powers of (A*B), and explore five families (including symmetric polynomials and RBM-related sums). Code is released.

Strengths
- Interesting and timely problem at the intersection of ML and symbolic computation; clear potential impact if robust.
- Novel use of attribute grammars for identity discovery and integration with learning-based heuristics.
- Two representations are thoughtfully designed: a fast modular-evaluation descriptor and an RNN embedding; curriculum and identity-based initialization are sensible and practically useful.
- The framework reportedly discovers nontrivial identities beyond exhaustive search, with illustrative examples.
- Public release of code and data.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Correctness and verification
- The modular numerical descriptor is treated as “unique” without analysis. There is no bound on collision probability vs N and p, nor discussion of adversarial cancellations. Equality testing solely via a single modulus is unsafe; multiple primes or formal verification are needed.
- Linear combinations of trees: Fw = t mod p. It is unclear whether w is constrained to small integer coefficients (e.g., {−1,0,1}). Arbitrary solutions modulo p can be spurious and need not correspond to meaningful identities over reals/integers. There is no regularization or constraint to avoid large or mod-wrap coefficients, nor post-verification in exact arithmetic.
- The paper does not provide formal proofs or CAS verification (e.g., via Mathematica/SymPy) of discovered identities. Reliance on modular agreement risks false positives.

Methodology and reproducibility
- The grammar rules (Table 1) are referenced but not present in the paper; the exact operation set, types, and complexity attributes are essential.
- The scheduler is greedy; search breadth/beam size and exploration are not discussed. Without beam/MCTS/A*, the approach may prematurely commit; yet no ablation.
- The learned RNN policy underperforms n-grams on most families; limited analysis of failure modes or advantage regimes.
- Training details for the RNN (optimizer, epochs, batch sizes, dropout placement, curriculum schedule, number of restarts) are insufficient for reproducibility. The embedding size l is “chosen by cross-validation” but no protocol or splits are given.
- The dataset of expressions for RNN training relies on numerical-equivalence clustering; potential mislabeling from collisions is not addressed.
- The experimental metric is success within a fixed 10-minute cutoff. There is no reporting of number of nodes explored, time-to-first-solution distributions, or sensitivity to cutoff, p, N, or grammar size. The claim “changing the cut-off has little effect” needs quantitative support.

Clarity and technical accuracy
- Several inconsistencies and typos hinder clarity and precision:
  - Complexity expressions contain malformed parentheses, e.g., O(()n3).
  - “help which the discovery” (grammar).
  - F is stated as M by N, yet F = [f1,...,fM] with each fi length N implies F is N by M.
  - Example 2’s complexity claim “O(nm)” for k=6 appears optimistic; the proposed expression scales with repeated matrix-vector multiplies, typically O(k nm).
  - The RNN treats transpose and sum as learned linear layers (W2, W1) rather than structural operations; while acceptable for learned embeddings, this should be clearly framed as semantic embedding, not arithmetic simulation.
  - Footnote markers (†, ‡) are awkwardly placed; references inconsistent and incomplete (missing years, venues; duplicated Socher citations).
- Figures and table references (Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Table 1, Table 2, Table 3, Fig. 3) are crucial but not included here; without them, the manuscript feels incomplete.

Evaluation and baselines
- No comparison to stronger search methods (beam search, A*, MCTS with hand-crafted heuristics), nor to program synthesis/induction baselines beyond random.
- No comparison to modern CAS systems with cost-aware rewrites or equality saturation (e-graphs).
- The claim of discovering many “previously undiscovered” identities is not substantiated (e.g., literature search, proof sketches).
- The strongest application claims (closed-form dropout, efficient RBM partition function) are speculative and unsupported by experiments.

Originality and significance
- The combination of attribute grammars with learned heuristics for identity discovery is novel and aligns with recent interest in neuro-symbolic methods. The RNN embedding of expressions is original in this context. However, the strongest empirical results are delivered by n-grams; the RNN advantage is unclear.
- Potential impact is high if the framework can be made reliable and verifiable. As presented, the absence of rigorous correctness guarantees and limited baseline comparisons reduce confidence in significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide rigorous collision analysis for the modular descriptor; use multiple primes and verify discovered identities symbolically.
- Constrain linear-combination coefficients and add sparsity/size penalties; post-hoc integer recovery (e.g., via LLL) and exact verification.
- Include the full grammar, typing, and complexity attributes; add beam search and compare against A*/MCTS.
- Report node expansions and time-to-solution distributions; ablate N, p, beam width, curriculum schedule, and dropout.
- Compare to CAS with cost-aware rewrite systems (or e-graphs) and to equality saturation approaches.
- Temper application claims; include at least one concrete downstream speedup with verified identity.
- Fix notation, typos, and references.

Pros
- Interesting problem and novel framework.
- Practical engineering ideas (curriculum, identity init).
- Code released; some compelling examples.

Cons
- Weak correctness guarantees; risk of false positives.
- Linear-combination solver ill-specified; coefficients unconstrained.
- Missing grammar/table details; clarity issues and typos.
- Limited baselines; RNN underperforms n-grams; evaluation metric coarse.
- Speculative claims without evidence.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper addresses an exciting problem and presents promising ideas, but the current version has significant methodological and presentation issues, especially around correctness verification, evaluation rigor, and missing details. With stronger guarantees, clearer exposition, and more thorough comparisons, a future version could be impactful.
