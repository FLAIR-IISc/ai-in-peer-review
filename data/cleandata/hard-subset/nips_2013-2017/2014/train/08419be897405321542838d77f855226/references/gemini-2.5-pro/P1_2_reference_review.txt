This paper proposes a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to define a space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is equivalent to a given target expression but has a lower computational complexity. Recognizing that a brute-force or random search of the combinatorial space of grammar trees is intractable for complex targets, the authors' main contribution is to use machine learning to guide the search. Specifically, they train models (n-grams and a Recursive Neural Network) on solutions found for simpler expressions to predict which grammar rules are most likely to lead to a solution for a more complex target.

This work sits at an interesting intersection of symbolic computation, program induction, and machine learning. While symbolic math engines like Mathematica can simplify expressions, they are typically rule-based and do not use statistical learning to discover novel identities with lower asymptotic complexity. The application of techniques from Natural Language Processing, such as n-gram models and Recursive Neural Networks (RNNs) on tree structures, to the domain of symbolic mathematics is highly original. The overall approach of using solutions to simple problems to bootstrap the discovery of more complex ones (a form of curriculum learning) is a powerful and well-motivated concept for tackling combinatorial search problems.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a genuinely novel application of machine learning. The goal of automatically discovering new, efficient mathematical identities is ambitious and has significant potential impact. If successful and generalizable, such a system could be integrated into compilers or scientific computing libraries to optimize code automatically. The discovery of polynomial-time equivalents for expressions that are naively exponential (e.g., the RBM-related families) is particularly impressive and of direct interest to the machine learning community.

2.  **Sound and Clever Technical Approach:** The overall framework is well-designed. The use of an attribute grammar is a principled way to define the search space. The method for checking expression equivalence is particularly clever: instead of relying on slow symbolic manipulation, the authors use "numerical descriptors" by evaluating expressions on random inputs over a finite field (Zp). This is a pragmatic and efficient solution that is well-suited for integration with ML methods.

3.  **Novel Contribution in Representation Learning:** The development of a continuous vector representation for symbolic expressions using an RNN is a valuable contribution in its own right. Paralleling the success of word embeddings in NLP, such a representation could unlock the application of many other gradient-based machine learning methods to symbolic domains. The high classification accuracy reported in Table 2 demonstrates the effectiveness of this learned representation.

4.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors demonstrate the success of their framework on several non-trivial families of expressions. They show that the learning-guided search strategies (n-gram and RNN) significantly outperform a random search baseline, successfully finding identities for expressions of a degree (k) far beyond what the baseline can manage. The discovery of identities that reduce complexity from O(n^k) or O(2^n) to O(n^2) or O(n^3) is a powerful demonstration of the system's utility.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Scope of the Current Framework:** The authors are upfront about the current restrictions of their system (homogeneous polynomials of a single variable, scalar output), but this is a significant limitation. The real power of such a system would be in handling more general mathematics, including trigonometric functions, logarithms, conditional logic, and recursion. It is unclear how the current framework, especially the equivalence checking and the RNN representation, would scale to these more complex domains.

2.  **Superior Performance of Simpler Models:** A surprising result is that the simpler n-gram models (particularly 3-, 4-, and 5-grams) consistently outperform the more sophisticated RNN model in the search task. While the RNN excels at the classification task, its utility for guiding the search seems less pronounced. The paper notes this but does not offer a deep analysis as to why this might be the case. It could be that the specific solution structures have highly repetitive local patterns that are perfectly captured by n-grams, whereas the RNN struggles to generalize effectively in this search context. This finding warrants more discussion.

3.  **Scalability of the Learned Representation:** The RNN is trained as a classifier over C discrete equivalence classes of expressions. The paper notes that for k=6, C is already 1687. This number would likely grow explosively for higher k or a more expressive grammar. This approach seems to have a scalability bottleneck, as the model would need to be retrained with an ever-expanding output layer. This might limit the generality of the learned representation part of the work.

4.  **Clarity on Experimental Details:** While generally clear, the paper could benefit from more detail in a few areas. For two of the experimental families, the matrix-multiply rule was removed from the grammar. While justified as a way to guarantee an efficient solution is found, it feels like providing a very strong hint to the system. It would be interesting to know if the system could learn to *avoid* the O(n^3) operation on its own. Additionally, more details on the hyperparameter tuning for the search strategies would improve reproducibility.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Pros:**
*   Highly novel and creative application of ML to symbolic reasoning.
*   Addresses a significant and difficult problem with high potential impact.
*   The use of numerical descriptors and a learned RNN representation are strong technical contributions.
*   Demonstrably successful on non-trivial problems, discovering complex and useful identities.

**Cons:**
*   The framework is currently restricted to a narrow class of mathematical expressions.
*   Simpler n-gram models outperform the more complex RNN in the primary task of search, which requires more analysis.
*   The method for learning representations via classification may not scale to more complex grammars.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality, original, and thought-provoking paper that opens up a new and exciting research direction. The combination of ideas from compilers, program synthesis, and modern machine learning is creative and effective. Despite the current limitations in scope, the work represents a significant step forward in applying learning to symbolic domains and addresses a core AI problem of guided search in a combinatorial space. The results are impressive and the potential for future work is immense. This paper would be an excellent addition to the NIPS conference.
