Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles the novel problem of automatically discovering algebraically equivalent but computationally cheaper expressions for symbolic targets, focusing on homogeneous polynomials that yield scalar outputs. The core idea is to formulate the space of candidate expressions via an attribute grammar (with operation-cost annotations), and to learn a search policy that efficiently navigates grammar trees toward identities that match a target but reduce complexity. Two complementary representations are proposed: (i) a fast numerical descriptor based on evaluation over random inputs in Zp to test equivalence; and (ii) a learned continuous representation via a recursive neural network (RNN) trained to cluster equivalent expressions and to predict next grammar rules. Complex targets often require linear combinations of trees; the authors solve for coefficients via modular Gaussian elimination. Experiments on several families (matrix polynomials, elementwise/matrix mixtures, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related exponential sums) show that learned strategies (n-grams, RNN) substantially outperform random search, enabling discovery of identities with significant asymptotic improvements (e.g., O(nm) or O(n(m+p)) instead of O(nmp), and polynomial-time forms of sums with naive exponential complexity). The work intersects theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic algebra, but differs by learning search heuristics and focusing on computational efficiency rather than purely syntactic simplification.

Strengths
- High novelty: a clear, bold attempt to bring learning-guided search and continuous embeddings to symbolic identity discovery and complexity reduction.
- Practical framework with clear constraints (degree, scalar output) allowing tractable exploration without undecidability pitfalls.
- Clever combination of ingredients: attribute grammars with cost, numerical equivalence testing over finite fields, linear-combination matching, and learned policies (n-gram and RNN) trained on simpler instances.
- Demonstrates nontrivial, previously undocumented identities that reduce asymptotic cost; code and data are released.
- The learned embedding of expressions is itself a valuable contribution for applying ML to symbolic domains.

Weaknesses
- Correctness/verification: equivalence is assessed numerically over random inputs without formal error bounds or proof certificates; claims of “new” identities would benefit from certified verification.
- Limited baselines and analysis of search: comparisons are only to random and n-gram; no beam search, A*/MCTS, SAT/SMT, or other neural-guided enumeration baselines.
- Scope restrictions: single variable (mostly), homogeneous polynomials, and the need to remove some grammar rules to enforce desired complexity—unclear generalization beyond these settings.
- The RNN strategy underperforms n-grams on several families; analysis of failure modes and ablations is brief.
- Complexity accounting is sometimes informal; more rigorous operation counts and memory analyses would strengthen claims.

Quality
Technically solid overall, with a thoughtfully designed pipeline. The attribute grammar and cost annotations are sensible, and the linear system approach for tree combinations is elegant. However, the reliance on random testing for equality without theoretical guarantees is a gap; a short discussion citing Schwartz–Zippel and providing collision bounds over Zp would make the numerical method principled. The experiments convincingly show that learned strategies widen solvable regimes, but some claims (e.g., “previously undiscovered”) would benefit from stronger evidence and/or formal proofs.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The running examples help; the curriculum and initialization tricks for the RNN are appropriately detailed. A few suggestions:
- Include the full grammar (Table 1) with explicit operation costs and shapes to make reproduction easier.
- Provide a more explicit accounting of operation counts for representative identities, not just big-O.
- Summarize a few discovered identities in the main text (not only in the supplement) with their cost reductions.

Originality
High. Using learned policies to guide identity discovery in an attribute-grammar space is novel, as is training an RNN to embed algebraic expressions for equivalence clustering and rule prediction. The combination with modular linear solving to handle linear combinations is neat and, to my knowledge, not previously demonstrated in this context.

Significance
Promising direction with potential impact on symbolic algebra, compiler optimization, and probabilistic modeling (e.g., approximating RBM partition functions, closed-form dropout-like expectations). Even if current scope is limited, the approach opens a path to learning-guided program synthesis for algebraic optimization.

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, creative approach that advances learning in symbolic computation.
- Demonstrated discovery of meaningful, computationally impactful identities.
- Public code and data; clear potential for follow-on work.
- The learned expression embeddings are of independent interest.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of formal correctness guarantees or proof artifacts.
- Narrow baselines for search efficiency and limited analysis of why RNN underperforms n-grams.
- Scope restrictions and some hand-tuning (e.g., removing matrix-multiply) to ensure efficiency.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide probabilistic guarantees for numerical equivalence testing (Schwartz–Zippel style bounds) and discuss choice of p and N relative to tree counts; consider emitting proof certificates (e.g., via symbolic verification once a candidate is found).
- Compare against stronger search baselines (beam search, MCTS, A* with learned heuristics) and report success-time curves.
- Analyze why n-grams outperform the RNN on several families; can you incorporate local-context losses or contrastive objectives to improve the RNN policy?
- Demonstrate multi-variable, non-homogeneous targets and limited non-polynomial extensions (e.g., low-order Taylor expansions) to show extensibility.
- Report memory/time scaling for the modular linear solver as M grows; consider incremental or sparse solvers.
- Discuss numerical stability of the “efficient” expressions over floats, even if equality is over Zp.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a compelling and genuinely novel direction—learning-guided discovery of efficient mathematical identities—with concrete wins and open avenues. Despite some methodological gaps (formal verification, baselines), the conceptual contribution and demonstrated potential justify acceptance.

Confidence
High on novelty/significance assessment; moderate on completeness of related work in symbolic optimization.
