Summary
The paper proposes a framework to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities that are equivalent to a given target expression but have lower asymptotic cost. Expressions are generated via an attribute grammar over Matlab-like matrix/vector operations, with each rule annotated by cost. Because exhaustive enumeration is infeasible for higher-degree expressions, the authors train search policies from solutions to simpler targets. Two families of learned strategies are explored: n-gram models over grammar subtrees and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees. Equivalence between candidate and target expressions is checked numerically using integer arithmetic modulo a large prime, and for complex expressions the method solves for linear combinations of trees matching the target via a modular linear system. Experiments on several expression families (matrix polynomials, elementwise products, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related exponential sums) show that learned strategies discover efficient identities much more reliably than random search, including reductions from O(n^3) to O(n^2) and from exponential to polynomial for certain Taylor terms. The authors release code and data.

Relation to prior work
The work sits at the intersection of symbolic manipulation/program induction and statistical learning. It contrasts with rule-based CAS simplification (Maple/Mathematica), classic theorem proving, and probabilistic programming by focusing on a constrained space: homogeneous polynomials of bounded degree, with a cost-annotated grammar and no halting/undecidability issues. It relates to grammar-guided optimization and evolutionary program induction, and borrows ideas from NLP (n-grams, RNNs over trees). Notably, the paper presents a learned continuous representation for symbolic expressions, enabling learned search policies. Relevant adjacent strands not discussed include symbolic regression via genetic programming (e.g., Schmidt & Lipson, Science 2009), equality saturation/term-rewriting-based optimization (e.g., Tate et al., PLDI 2009), and program synthesis with SMT/Sketch; comparisons or discussion would strengthen positioning.

Strengths
- Novel problem framing: learning to guide discovery of efficient algebraic identities in a constrained yet nontrivial symbolic domain.
- Clear integration of grammars, a fast equivalence oracle (via modular evaluations), and learned search policies.
- The n-gram strategy is simple and effective; the RNN yields a reusable continuous embedding and shows decent classification accuracy across many equivalence classes.
- Demonstrated ability to find nontrivial identities impossible to discover by brute force at higher degrees, with evidence of substantial asymptotic reductions.
- Code and datasets are provided, improving reproducibility and potential impact.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Verification/equivalence: The integer-modulo descriptor provides a practical equivalence check, but the paper does not quantify collision probability or adopt standard safeguards (e.g., multiple independent primes/seeds) nor provide symbolic proofs of discovered identities. This weakens guarantees of correctness.
- Linear combinations of trees: The method solves Fw = t (mod p) and treats w as coefficients of a linear combination of trees. It is unclear how these coefficients map back to a single expression within the grammar (e.g., are addition and scalar multiplication rules present? Are coefficients restricted to small integers? How are negative/reals handled?). A constructive translation and constraints on w should be specified.
- Evaluation metrics: Main metric is “fraction of runs finding a solution within 10 minutes.” There is little reporting of actual computational savings on numerical inputs or end-to-end runtime comparisons against naïve evaluation. Complexity reductions are argued but not empirically validated with wall-clock benchmarks and cost models.
- Scalability: The approach relies on enumerating expressions up to degree k to create training data and clusters for the RNN, which may itself be exponential and limits scalability. The search strategy is greedy; exploration strategies (beam/MCTS) are not studied.
- Baselines: No comparisons to alternative search/induction methods (genetic programming/symbolic regression, A*, beam search, MCTS, ILP/SAT-based synthesis, equality saturation) are provided.
- Scope restrictions: Homogeneous polynomials in a single variable and a relatively small operation set limit generality. Some claims in the discussion (e.g., closed-form dropout or polynomial-time RBM partition function) are speculative given that only Taylor terms are addressed and solutions for RBM-2 stalled beyond k=5.
- RNN utility: Despite interesting embeddings and ≤5% classification error, n-grams often outperform the RNN for discovery—raising questions about practical added value of the RNN over simpler models. Ablations on representation size, pretraining, and curriculum effects are limited.

Quality
The work is technically interesting and the system design is coherent. However, rigor is weakened by the lack of formal guarantees or robust verification of identities, unclear handling of linear combinations, and absence of strong baselines. Experimental evidence supports that learning helps search, but the evaluation would benefit from stronger quantitative analyses of speedups and scalability.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized, but several crucial details are missing or deferred: full grammar specification and cost model (Table 1), verification parameters (N, prime sizes), mapping from modular solutions to real coefficients, and more algorithmic details of the scheduler. Figures/tables referenced are essential.

Originality
The combination of a cost-annotated grammar, learned policy over expression trees, and continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions is novel and timely. The task focus—efficient identity discovery—is distinct from standard CAS simplification or theorem proving.

Significance
If strengthened, this line could impact compiler optimization, scientific computing, and symbolic ML. The released identities and code are valuable. As-is, impact is somewhat limited by scope and verification concerns, but the direction is promising.

Questions for authors
- How do you map modular solutions w back to concrete coefficients in the original algebra (integers/reals)? Are addition and scalar multiplication explicit grammar rules? Are coefficients bounded?
- What is the probability of false positives in equivalence testing? Do you validate with multiple primes or symbolic checks post hoc?
- Can you report wall-clock runtime savings on numeric instances for selected identities?
- How large can k get before dataset creation (for RNN pretraining) becomes infeasible? Any strategy to avoid full enumeration?
- Would beam search or MCTS improve over greedy scheduling?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add robust verification: multiple primes, randomized test sets, and optionally symbolic proof postprocessing.
- Clarify the grammar (full rule list, inclusion of addition/coefficients), cost model, and the constructive synthesis of final expressions from w.
- Compare to stronger baselines (genetic programming/symbolic regression, beam/MCTS, equality saturation).
- Report empirical runtime/memory savings and constants, not just asymptotic claims.
- Provide more ablations and analysis of when RNN helps beyond n-grams; consider hybrid policies.
- Temper speculative claims about RBM/dropout and provide concrete demonstrations.

Pros
- Novel, interesting problem and approach; bridges learning and symbolic math.
- Clear evidence that learning improves search.
- Public code/data; potentially useful identities.

Cons
- Verification and correctness guarantees are weak.
- Limited scope and unclear handling of linear combinations.
- Evaluation lacks strong baselines and practical runtime measurements.
- RNN benefits over simpler models are modest.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper opens a compelling direction with concrete contributions and promising results, but would benefit from stronger verification, clearer synthesis of final expressions, and richer empirical evaluation.
