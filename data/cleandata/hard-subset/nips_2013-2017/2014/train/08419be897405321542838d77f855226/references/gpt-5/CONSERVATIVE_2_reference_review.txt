Summary
The paper aims to discover computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target expression by searching expression trees defined by an attribute grammar. Because exhaustive enumeration becomes infeasible, the authors propose learned search strategies trained on solutions to simpler targets: (i) n-gram models over subtrees and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees and a softmax to predict the next rule. Equivalence is checked via evaluation on many random integer inputs modulo a large prime, and complex targets are matched via linear combinations of trees solved mod p. Experiments on several expression families (matrix polynomials, elementwise/matrix mixes, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related exponential sums) show that learned strategies (especially n-grams) can find identities much more reliably than random search under time budgets, with some examples giving polynomial-time evaluation of targets that are naively exponential.

Strengths
- Interesting and relevant problem: optimizing algebraic expressions is central to ML systems, compilers, and symbolic computation.
- Clear articulation of a grammar-guided search framework and the need for guidance beyond brute force.
- Simple, effective n-gram heuristic that substantially improves search over random for several families.
- RNN-based embedding of symbolic expressions is novel and achieves low test error on a multi-class expression-equivalence proxy task.
- Code and data availability (per the paper) aid reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Correctness guarantees: The paper relies on random testing mod p to assert equivalence, but provides no formal error probability analysis (e.g., via Schwartz–Zippel). Without bounds on collision probability and on the linear combination solver’s robustness mod p, “discovered identities” lack rigorous validation. No proofs or certificate generation are provided.
- Baselines: There is no comparison against established systems and techniques:
  - Computer algebra systems (Maple/Mathematica) with simplification plus a cost model, common subexpression elimination, strength reduction, and reassociation.
  - Algebraic complexity/arithmetic circuit optimization or matrix-chain/tensor contraction optimization with known dynamic programming heuristics.
  - Superoptimization/equality saturation approaches that optimize expression DAGs via rewrite rules and cost models.
  The absence of such baselines makes it hard to assess significance beyond demonstrating that learned search beats random within this grammar.
- Evaluation metrics: Success is measured as “fraction of runs finding a solution within 10 minutes,” rather than wall-clock speedups for specific n, asymptotic complexity verification on real implementations, or number/quality of identities with proofs. Claims like O(nm) vs O(nmp) are asserted but not empirically verified over varying n with actual runtimes.
- Scope limitations conflated with success: For some families the matrix-multiply rule is removed to force any found solution to be “efficient.” This can bias the search and entangles the success criterion with the grammar restriction rather than with genuine discovery under the full operation set.
- Generalization: The RNN underperforms n-grams on discovery (despite good classification accuracy), calling into question the practical utility of the learned embedding for this task. For the most challenging RBM-2 family, no method finds solutions beyond k=5, and no convincing evidence is provided that the approach scales to genuinely hard symbolic domains.
- Novelty of some identities: Example 1 is a well-known identity (1^T A B 1 = (1^T A)(B 1)), and the “chain” examples (Example 2) reflect repeated associativity/linearity patterns. The paper asserts “previously undiscovered” identities but does not substantiate this claim with proofs or literature checks.
- Missing methodological details: 
  - No bound on N and p to ensure low false-positive rate in equivalence testing.
  - No discussion of coefficient growth or interpretability of the linear combination weights w.
  - Limited ablations on embedding size, curriculum schedule, and dropout; no beam search or alternative search strategies considered.

Quality
Technically, the framework is plausible but remains preliminary. The lack of formal guarantees for equivalence checking and the absence of rigorous baselines undermine the empirical claims. The experiments primarily show that learned heuristics can guide search better than random in a constrained grammar.

Clarity
Overall writing is clear and well-organized, though some notational inconsistencies and missing tables/figures in the submission make it hard to fully verify results. The paper would benefit from a precise statement of the probabilistic equivalence test with error bounds.

Originality
Applying an RNN to learn embeddings of symbolic expressions is novel in this context, and the combination of attribute grammars with learned search is interesting. However, the search guidance via n-grams is quite standard, and several discovered “identities” are classical.

Significance
Potentially significant if integrated with compilers/CAS and if correctness and baseline comparisons are strengthened. As presented, the contribution is more a promising direction than a mature, reliable tool.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide a formal equivalence-testing procedure with provable error bounds (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel style) and report parameters ensuring <10^-k failure probability. Ideally, emit machine-checkable proofs or use an SMT/CAS backend to certify identities post-discovery.
- Compare against CAS simplification plus a cost model, equality saturation/e-graphs, and matrix/tensor chain optimizers on identical targets and time budgets.
- Report actual runtime speedups over varying n with real implementations of both naive and discovered expressions.
- Clarify the linear combination solver: coefficient domains, sparsity, and whether the resulting identities are interpretable and portable back to re-usable formulas.
- Include a vetted list of identities with proofs and indications of novelty.
- Explore stronger search (beam search, A*, MCTS) and ablate RNN vs simpler models (Markov, bag-of-ops) more thoroughly.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem with an appealing learning-guided search idea.
- Public code/data; clear framework.
- n-gram guidance provides tangible search improvements.
- RNN embeddings of symbolic expressions are interesting and potentially reusable.

Cons
- No rigorous equivalence guarantees; claims of new identities not substantiated by proofs.
- Missing strong baselines; evaluation metric centered on “found within time” rather than true performance gains.
- Scope restrictions and grammar choices cloud the significance of results.
- Limited success on the hardest families; RNN underperforms simple heuristics in discovery.

Recommendation
Reject. Interesting direction but lacks rigorous validation, strong baselines, and reliable correctness guarantees necessary for NeurIPS acceptance. Confidence: high.
