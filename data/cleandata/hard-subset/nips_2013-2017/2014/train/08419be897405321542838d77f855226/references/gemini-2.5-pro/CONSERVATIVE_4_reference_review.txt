**Summary**

This paper introduces a framework for discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to define a space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is equivalent to a given target but has lower computational complexity. As brute-force or random search is intractable for complex targets, the authors propose using machine learning to guide the search. They train models—specifically n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN)—on solutions found for simpler expressions to predict promising search paths for more complex ones. The method is evaluated on five families of polynomial expressions, demonstrating the discovery of identities that reduce complexity from exponential or high-order polynomial to low-order polynomial time. The authors also propose a novel application of an RNN to learn a continuous vector representation for symbolic expressions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The problem of automatically discovering efficient computational identities is both challenging and highly relevant. Success in this area could have significant impact on fields like scientific computing, compiler design, and the optimization of machine learning algorithms.

2.  **Novel Framework:** The combination of an attribute grammar with a learned search strategy is a creative and well-motivated approach to this symbolic search problem. Framing the discovery process as a guided search is a sound conceptual contribution.

3.  **Empirical Demonstration:** The paper successfully demonstrates that the proposed learning-based strategies can discover non-trivial identities. The results on the five expression families, particularly the reduction from exponential to polynomial complexity for the RBM-related terms, are impressive and provide compelling evidence that the system works within its defined scope.

**Weaknesses**

My main reservations about this work stem from its evaluation methodology and the positioning of its contributions, particularly concerning the lack of comparison to established, non-learning-based methods and the performance of the proposed novel techniques.

1.  **Lack of Competitive Baselines:** The primary baseline for the search strategies is a random search within the grammar. While the learning-based methods clearly outperform this naive approach, this is a very low bar. The field of symbolic computation and computer algebra systems (CAS), such as Mathematica and Maple, has a long history of expression simplification and manipulation. While the authors correctly note that these systems are typically rule-based and do not use statistical learning, they are nonetheless the established state-of-the-art for this class of problem. A convincing demonstration of significance would require comparing the discovered identities (or the time taken to find them) against the output of such systems. It is not clear whether these established systems could find the same or similar efficiencies using their built-in simplification and series expansion routines. Without this comparison, the practical utility of the learning-based approach is difficult to assess.

2.  **Underperformance of the Novel Neural Model:** A significant contribution claimed by the paper is the novel application of a recursive neural network for this task. However, the experimental results in Figure 3 show that the RNN is consistently outperformed by simpler, well-established n-gram models (specifically 3-gram, 4-gram, and 5-gram). This result undermines the case for the more complex neural approach. If a standard counting-based method is more effective, the motivation for introducing the RNN becomes less clear. The paper does not offer a deep analysis as to *why* the RNN is less effective; one might speculate that the solutions have highly local, repetitive structures that are better captured by n-grams than by the distributed representations of the RNN, but this is not explored.

3.  **Limited Scope and Generalizability:** The framework is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of a single variable yielding a scalar output. While the authors acknowledge this, it is a very significant constraint that limits the immediate applicability of the findings. The claim of discovering "new mathematical identities" should be interpreted within this narrow context. The paper discusses extending the grammar to include more functions, but this would introduce substantial challenges (e.g., ensuring termination) that are not addressed.

4.  **Validation of Discovered Identities:** The paper claims the discovery of "previously undiscovered" identities. This is a very strong claim that requires careful validation. For the `Sym_k` family, which is well-studied in combinatorics, it would have been valuable to show whether the system rediscovered known efficient formulas or truly found new ones. For the other families, asserting that an identity is new requires a thorough search of relevant mathematical literature, which is not documented in the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel and important problem at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic reasoning.
*   The overall framework of grammar-based search guided by learning is sound and well-conceived.
*   The empirical results clearly show that learning from simpler problems accelerates discovery on more complex ones, outperforming a naive random search.
*   The identities discovered are non-trivial and demonstrate a significant reduction in computational complexity.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The evaluation lacks a crucial comparison to established baselines from the symbolic computation community (e.g., Mathematica, Maple), making it difficult to judge the practical significance of the results.
*   The paper's most novel technical contribution, the recursive neural network, is shown to be less effective than much simpler, traditional n-gram models, weakening the paper's claims about the value of this new approach.
*   The scope of the problem is very restricted, and claims of general applicability are speculative.
*   Strong claims about discovering "new" identities are not sufficiently substantiated with external validation or literature review.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper presents a fascinating and creative application of machine learning to a difficult symbolic problem, its methodological weaknesses prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. The lack of comparison to established computer algebra systems is a major omission that leaves the significance of the contribution in question. Furthermore, the underperformance of the novel RNN model relative to simpler n-gram methods calls into question the necessity of the more complex machinery. The work shows promise, but it requires more rigorous validation against stronger baselines and a more sober assessment of its contributions before it can be considered a clear advance for the field.
