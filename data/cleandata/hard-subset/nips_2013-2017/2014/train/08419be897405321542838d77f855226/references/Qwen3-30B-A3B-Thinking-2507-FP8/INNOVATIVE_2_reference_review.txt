

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning to guide the search through symbolic expression spaces defined by attribute grammars. The core insight is that learning from simpler identities can guide the discovery of more complex ones, moving beyond brute-force enumeration which becomes intractable for complex expressions. The authors introduce a grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions, develop numerical and learned continuous representations for comparing expressions, and employ n-gram models and recursive neural networks to guide the search for efficient identities.

The paper's most significant contribution is its bold application of machine learning techniques to a domain traditionally handled by symbolic computation systems. While attribute grammars have been used in compiler design and other domains, their application to discovering mathematical identities with machine learning guidance is novel. The authors demonstrate the approach on several families of expressions, including discovering identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) for matrix operations, and from exponential to polynomial time for certain sums. These discoveries are genuinely novel as the authors claim (and the supplementary material provides evidence), and they represent a significant improvement over naive evaluation.

The paper's novelty is particularly compelling because it bridges symbolic computation and machine learning in a non-trivial way. The use of recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of mathematical expressions is a creative extension of NLP techniques to a new domain, and the demonstration that such representations can effectively guide search is impressive. The approach is also practically significant, with potential applications in compiler optimization and machine learning algorithm design (e.g., for efficiently computing dropout or RBM partition functions).

The work stands out from related research in several ways. Unlike theorem provers or symbolic computation engines like Mathematica, this approach doesn't rely on hand-designed heuristics but learns them from previous solutions. While there's prior work on grammars in algorithm design (e.g., [7]), this paper is the first to combine grammars with learning for mathematical identity discovery. The connection to NLP techniques (n-grams, recursive neural networks) is well-motivated and appropriately contextualized.

The paper's strengths lie in its conceptual novelty and the demonstration of practical results. The authors successfully apply their framework to discover identities that would be difficult or impossible to find through manual manipulation or exhaustive search. The examples provided (particularly Example 2 with the k=6 case) are compelling evidence of the approach's power. The technical details of the framework are well-described, and the experimental results clearly show that learning-based strategies outperform random search for complex expressions.

Minor weaknesses include the restricted problem scope (homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree), which limits immediate applicability, and the lack of deeper theoretical justification for why the learning approach works. The paper also overstates the generality of the approach in some places (e.g., "broad applicability in allowing machine learning tools to be applied to symbolic computation" without sufficient evidence). However, these are minor concerns given the paper's strong novelty.

The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction. The experiments are thorough for the problem domain, and the results are compelling. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations in the discussion section.

This paper represents a significant step toward applying machine learning to symbolic domains in a novel and useful way. While the problem is somewhat restricted, the core idea—using learning to guide symbolic search—is bold and potentially transformative. The paper successfully demonstrates that learning can overcome combinatorial explosion in a domain where it would otherwise be intractable, opening up new possibilities for applying machine learning to mathematical discovery.

The significance of this work cannot be overstated. It addresses a fundamental problem in AI (combinatorial search) with a novel approach that could inspire similar techniques in other domains. The paper doesn't just present an incremental improvement—it establishes a new direction for research at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation.

For these reasons, I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper's novelty and potential impact outweigh the minor methodological limitations. It's exactly the kind of bold, novel work that NIPS should celebrate. The discovery of new mathematical identities with significant computational advantages is itself a major contribution, and the framework provides a promising new approach to a challenging problem.
