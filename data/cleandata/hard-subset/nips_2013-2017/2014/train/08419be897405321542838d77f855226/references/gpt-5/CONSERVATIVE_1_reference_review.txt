Summary
The paper proposes a grammar-based framework to search for algebraically equivalent but computationally cheaper expressions for a given target (restricted to homogeneous polynomials mapping matrix/vector inputs to a scalar). To combat the combinatorial explosion of grammar-tree enumeration, the authors learn search policies from simpler instances: (i) n-gram models over subtrees and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous representations of expressions and predicts next rules. Expression equivalence is checked numerically via randomized evaluations modulo a large prime (also used to solve for linear combinations of trees). Experiments report that learned search strategies (especially n-grams) discover identities at higher degrees than random search within a 10-minute budget, yielding, for some families, reductions from naive O(n^3) or even exponential to O(n^2)–O(n^3). Code is provided.

Relation to prior work
The approach combines attribute grammars (classical), simple statistical language models, and an RNN akin to those used in NLP parse-tree representations. The setup relates to program induction/superoptimization, term-rewriting/automated theorem proving, and CAS simplification. The authors emphasize that CAS typically simplifies but does not target lower complexity or use learning.

Strengths
- Clear, concrete problem statement within a constrained but nontrivial domain.
- A sensible grammar-guided search formulation with an explicit cost model.
- Empirical evidence that learning guides search better than random for several families; n-grams are strong, simple baselines.
- The RNN-based representation for expressions is an interesting idea; classification accuracy is reasonably high.
- Code availability aids reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/methodology
- Correctness/equivalence checking is only probabilistic. The approach relies on randomized integer descriptors and modulo-p linear solves. There is no formal error analysis (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel-style bounds) nor a final symbolic verification step. False positives modulo p or spurious linear combinations can occur; propagation of such errors into the training data is a serious risk.
- The claim of “discovering new identities” requires rigorous proof or CAS verification beyond randomized testing. This is not presented.
- Evaluation metric is “fraction of successful runs within 10 minutes,” which is a weak proxy. There is no systematic reporting of actual complexity reduction (FLOPs), runtime on real BLAS implementations, or quality of the found solutions versus known-optimal factorizations.
- Baselines are limited. The paper does not compare against:
  - Stronger search methods (beam search, A*, MCTS, constraint solving/SAT/SMT-backed enumeration).
  - CAS systems used with an explicit cost objective plus term-rewriting/factorization strategies.
  - Classical algebraic approaches for sum factorization or tensor contraction optimization.
- The main positive experimental result is that n-gram models outperform the proposed RNN on discovery rate; the RNN’s added value is unclear in the identity discovery task.
- The restriction “remove matrix-multiply from the grammar to ensure ≤ O(n^2)” biases the search to only yield efficient formulas and may limit the space in which identities are sought. This undercuts claims about discovering efficiency improvements.
- Scope limitations are substantial: single variable (mostly), homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs; the hardest family (RBM-2) fails beyond k=5 for all strategies.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but several technical details are missing for reproducibility at the level expected for NeurIPS:
  - Exact grammar (Table 1) not shown in the main text provided; termination criteria when more general rules are allowed; constraints on dimensions for well-typed trees.
  - Details of descriptor size N, prime p, and the statistical guarantees for collision rates.
  - Linear-combination solver specifics and how integer weights are interpreted over the reals.
- Some claims about downstream impact (closed-form dropout, RBM partition function) are speculative and not supported by experiments.

Originality
- The overall problem and the integration of simple statistical models/RNNs with grammar-guided search are interesting, but the learning components are fairly standard. The use of an RNN to embed symbolic expressions is novel in this domain, though similar in spirit to prior NLP tree models.

Significance
- Potentially impactful if correctness and search scalability can be guaranteed. As it stands, the empirical advances are modest: improvements over random on restricted families, with n-grams sufficing; limited success on the most challenging cases.
- The lack of formal verification and strong baselines limits confidence that the approach substantially advances the state of the art.

Questions for the authors
1. What is the formal probability of a false match under the integer descriptor scheme? Provide bounds in terms of N, p, and degree, and report empirical false-positive rates. Do you validate final identities symbolically (e.g., via CAS) to eliminate modulo-p artifacts?
2. For linear combinations solved modulo p, how do you ensure that the integer weights correspond to a valid identity over R or Z rather than only mod p?
3. Can you report FLOPs or wall-clock runtime reductions on instantiated numerical sizes, not only discovery rates?
4. Why does the RNN underperform n-gram models in discovery? What is the RNN’s demonstrated benefit beyond the classification experiment?
5. How do results compare to beam/A*/MCTS search, or to CAS simplification with a cost model?
6. How sensitive are results to descriptor size N and prime p? Ablate these choices.
7. Can you present at least one nontrivial identity with a formal proof in the main paper?

Pros
- Interesting problem; clear setup.
- Learning-guided search shows measurable gains on several families.
- Code available.
- RNN representation for expressions may be reusable.

Cons
- No formal correctness guarantees; risk of false positives and error propagation.
- Weak evaluation metrics; lack of baselines versus stronger search or CAS methods.
- RNN contributes little to the main identity-discovery results.
- Strong restrictions on grammar and problem scope; limited success on hardest tasks.
- Speculative claims about impact.

Recommendation
Score: 4 (reject)
Confidence: 0.7

Rationale: The paper addresses a compelling problem and presents some promising empirical results, but the lack of rigorous correctness guarantees and strong baselines, together with limited demonstrated impact of the proposed RNN and heavy restrictions on scope, make the contribution insufficiently validated for acceptance. Strengthening formal verification, adding competitive baselines and stronger evaluation, and providing symbolic proofs of key identities would substantially improve the work.
