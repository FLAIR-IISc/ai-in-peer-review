- Introduces a novel ML-guided framework using attribute grammars to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities.  
- Represents mathematical expressions as grammar trees with associated computational costs to guide optimization.  
- Uses learning methods (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to steer search toward efficient equivalent expressions.  
- Demonstrates substantial complexity reductions (e.g., O(n³) → O(n²) and exponential → polynomial) on families of homogeneous polynomials.  
- Proposes a continuous representation of symbolic expressions via RNNs, enabling ML techniques in symbolic domains.  
- Employs integer-based (Zp) numerical descriptors to reliably check expression equivalence without symbolic manipulation.  
- Methodology is technically sound, well-structured, and integrates learning components effectively into search.  
- Experimental validation is strong: learning-based search significantly outperforms random search and includes ablation studies.  
- Paper is clear and well-organized, with helpful examples and figures; minor hyperparameter detail omissions could be added.  
- Originality lies in applying learning to identity discovery, using RNNs for symbolic representations, and bootstrapping from simpler expressions.  
- Significance is high, with potential practical impact for compilers and optimization tools and concrete implications for ML problems (RBM, dropout).  
- Limitations: restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, practical ML application examples are theoretical, and the approach fails on the most complex family beyond k=5.  
- Would benefit from more discussion of experimental computational complexity and additional implementation details.  
- Recommendation: strong accept with minor revisions to address practical implementation and complexity discussion.
