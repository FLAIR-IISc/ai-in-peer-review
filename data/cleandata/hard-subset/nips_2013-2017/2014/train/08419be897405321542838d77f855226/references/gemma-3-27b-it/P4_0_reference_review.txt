This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent expressions as trees generated by an attribute grammar and then use machine learning to guide the search for more efficient equivalent expressions. The authors explore both n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to learn a search strategy, training on simpler expressions to improve performance on more complex ones. The contributions include the grammar framework, the integration of machine learning, the application of RNNs to symbolic representation, and the discovery of several new identities.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a challenging problem at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The approach is conceptually sound, leveraging the structure of attribute grammars and the learning capabilities of modern machine learning techniques. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, showcasing the discovery of identities with significant computational improvements. The inclusion of a code repository is a valuable asset.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for efficient identity discovery is a novel and promising approach. The application of RNNs to learning representations of symbolic expressions is also a noteworthy contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The framework is well-defined, and the experimental setup appears rigorous. The use of integer-based descriptors to address numerical precision issues is a practical consideration.
*   **Significance:** The potential applications of this work are significant. Discovering efficient mathematical identities can have a direct impact on performance in various computational domains, particularly machine learning, where many operations are expressed mathematically.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts. Examples are provided to illustrate the approach and the discovered identities.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scope & Generalizability:** The paper focuses on a relatively restricted setting – homogeneous polynomials of a single matrix. While the authors acknowledge this, the extent to which the approach can generalize to more complex expressions remains unclear. The experiments primarily concentrate on a few specific families of expressions.
*   **RNN Performance:** While the RNN demonstrates some improvement, the n-gram models consistently outperform it in the reported experiments. The reasons for this discrepancy are not fully explored, and the potential of the RNN approach may not be fully realized. The dropout rate tuning seems a bit ad-hoc.
*   **Linear Combination Complexity:** The paper doesn't delve into the computational cost of the linear combination step (solving the linear system). For very large expressions, this could become a bottleneck.
*   **Related Work Depth:** While related work is mentioned, some relevant areas, such as symbolic regression and automated theorem proving with machine learning components, could be discussed in more detail. For instance, the connection to Evolving Algorithms (EA) for discovering mathematical expressions could be discussed.
*  **Ablation studies:** Further ablation studies on the parameters of the RNN and n-gram models would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the framework in discovering useful identities.
*   The work has the potential to impact various areas of computation, including machine learning.
*   The code release promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The scope of the work is relatively limited, and the generalizability of the approach is uncertain.
*   The RNN performance is not as strong as the n-gram models.
*   The computational cost of the linear combination step could be a limitation for very complex expressions.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. While the scope is limited and the RNN performance could be improved, the novelty and technical soundness of the approach are compelling. The reported results are encouraging and demonstrate the feasibility of using machine learning to discover efficient mathematical identities. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses identified above, particularly regarding the discussion of generalizability, RNN performance, and related work. Specifically, the authors could benefit from exploring potential solutions to improve RNN performance and providing a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the linear combination step.
