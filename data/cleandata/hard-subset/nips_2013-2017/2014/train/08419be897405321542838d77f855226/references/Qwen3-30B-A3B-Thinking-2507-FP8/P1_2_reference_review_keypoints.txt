- Summary: Proposes a learning-guided attribute-grammar framework to discover lower-complexity mathematical identities, applying n-gram and RNN models to search symbolic expression space.

- Strength: Novel application of ML to symbolic computation, bridging NLP techniques and identity discovery.

- Strength: Demonstrated effectiveness on several expression families, finding identities that substantially reduce computational complexity.

- Strength: Technically sound experiments with controlled comparisons between random search, n-gram, and RNN strategies.

- Strength: Clear problem formulation limited to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, making the task tractable.

- Strength: Practical significance for ML tasks (e.g., efficient dropout or RBM computations).

- Weakness: Limited scope—restricted to homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix/vector variable, lacking exploration of broader function classes.

- Weakness: Lacks theoretical analysis explaining why learning methods work or how they scale with complexity.

- Weakness: Insufficient analysis of how training data quality/diversity affects performance, especially for complex expressions.

- Weakness: Could better differentiate from existing symbolic computation systems and program-induction work.

- Weakness: Reproducibility concerns—insufficient implementation details (hyperparameters, compute resources) despite GitHub link.

- Quality: Experiments are well-designed and support claims, but theoretical gaps remain.

- Clarity: Well-written and organized with helpful examples and figures, but more experimental/implementation details are needed.

- Originality: Novel use of attribute grammars and RNNs for symbolic identity discovery, distinct from prior theorem-proving and heuristic-selection work.

- Significance: Advances a new, learning-based approach to finding computationally efficient identities with practical ML applications.

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address limitations and reproducibility.

- Suggestion 1: Expand discussion on extending the framework to more general cases (e.g., trig functions, recursion).

- Suggestion 2: Add brief theoretical analysis for why learning approaches succeed in simpler cases.

- Suggestion 3: Provide detailed implementation/hyperparameter and compute-resource information for reproducibility.

- Suggestion 4: Include a more detailed comparison to symbolic systems like Maple/Mathematica.

- Suggestion 5: Clarify how RNN training data was generated and the amount per degree k.

- Final Assessment: High-quality, innovative work with strong empirical results and clear writing, deserving acceptance pending minor revisions.
