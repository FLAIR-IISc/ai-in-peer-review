

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

## Summary
This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars and machine learning techniques. The authors introduce a grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions and apply n-gram models and recursive neural networks to guide the search for efficient expressions. They demonstrate their approach on several polynomial families, claiming to discover new identities with reduced computational complexity.

## Strengths
The paper presents a novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation, which is an interesting direction. The framework is well-motivated for the specific problem domain of polynomial expressions, and the experimental results demonstrate that learning-based search strategies can outperform random search for complex targets. The use of integer-based descriptors to avoid numerical issues is a thoughtful technical detail.

## Weaknesses

### Methodological Issues
1. **Lack of Formal Novelty Proof**: The paper claims "previously undiscovered" identities but provides no formal proof of novelty. For Example 2, the pattern shown is a standard matrix multiplication pattern that could be derived from basic linear algebra properties. The paper fails to distinguish between "new to the authors" and "new to the field."

2. **Inconsistent Problem Formulation**: The problem statement limits to "homogeneous polynomials of degree k" with "a single matrix or vector," but Figure 1 shows expressions using two matrices (A and B), creating a contradiction that needs resolution.

3. **Grammar Specification**: The paper references "Table 1" for grammar rules but does not include the table. Without seeing the grammar rules, it's impossible to evaluate the completeness of the framework or the validity of the search space.

4. **Numerical Representation Justification**: The paper states "N must be sufficiently large to ensure that different expressions are not mapped to the same descriptor" but provides no theoretical bound or empirical evidence for this claim. The choice of N=1000 is arbitrary without justification.

5. **RNN Training Details**: The paper mentions "we drop activations as we propagate toward the top of the tree (the same fraction for each depth)" but fails to specify what fraction was used. This omission makes the method impossible to reproduce.

6. **Statistical Significance**: The paper reports "100±12 vs 438±77 secs" for RNN vs random strategy but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for this difference, which is essential for claiming a meaningful performance improvement.

### Technical and Formatting Issues
1. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses inconsistent spacing and formatting for complexity notation (e.g., "O ( n3 )" instead of "O(n³)"). This is unprofessional for a technical paper.

2. **Reference to Missing Material**: The paper states "All code and evaluation data can be found at https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning" but provides no specific commit hashes or versions, making reproducibility difficult.

3. **Mathematical Errors**: In Example 2, the expression for k=6 contains an extra closing parenthesis: "sum(((((((((((((sum(A, 1) * B) * A) * B) *A) * B) * A) * B)* A) * B) * A) * B)’), 1)" should be "sum((((((((((((sum(A, 1) * B) * A) * B) * A) * B) * A) * B) * A) * B) * A) * B)’, 1)".

4. **Misuse of Terminology**: The paper claims to "learn a continuous representation for symbolic expressions" but this is simply a standard embedding technique applied to tree structures, not a novel concept.

5. **Incomplete Experimental Details**: The paper states "For each run we impose a fixed cut-off time of 10 minutes" but doesn't explain why this specific time was chosen or how it affects the results.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents an interesting framework for a specific problem domain, it suffers from multiple methodological flaws, inconsistent presentation, and insufficient evidence to support its claims of novelty and effectiveness. The minor technical inconsistencies and methodological gaps are substantial enough to undermine the paper's credibility, despite the potentially interesting direction.

The paper fails to properly justify its claims of novelty, provides insufficient technical details for reproducibility, and contains multiple formatting and mathematical errors that indicate a lack of careful review. These issues collectively prevent the paper from meeting NIPS's standards for technical soundness and clarity.

I recommend rejection due to the significant methodological and presentation flaws that compromise the paper's contribution. The authors should address these issues thoroughly before resubmitting to a venue with a more appropriate scope.
