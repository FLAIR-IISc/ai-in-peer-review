- Proposes a framework to discover computationally efficient algebraic identities using a cost-annotated attribute grammar over matrix/vector operations.
- Generates expressions by grammar and trains search policies from solutions to simpler targets because exhaustive enumeration is infeasible.
- Explores two learned strategies: n-gram models over grammar subtrees and a recursive neural network (RNN) embedding of expression trees.
- Checks equivalence numerically via integer arithmetic modulo a large prime and solves modular linear systems to find linear combinations of candidate trees.
- Experiments on multiple expression families show learned strategies find efficient identities far more reliably than random search, achieving notable asymptotic reductions.
- Code and datasets are released, improving reproducibility.

- Positions work between symbolic CAS, theorem proving, and program synthesis, focusing on a constrained homogeneous-polynomial domain.
- Novelty includes a learned continuous representation for symbolic expressions to guide search.
- Paper omits discussion/comparison to related strands like symbolic regression/genetic programming, equality saturation, and SMT/Sketch synthesis.

- Strength: novel problem framing combining grammar, fast equivalence oracle, and learned search policies.
- Strength: simple n-gram strategy is effective; RNN provides reusable embeddings with decent classification accuracy.
- Strength: demonstrates discovery of nontrivial identities unreachable by brute force and provides public code/data.

- Weakness: equivalence checking via a single modular test lacks quantified collision probability and symbolic verification safeguards.
- Weakness: unclear how modular solution coefficients map to concrete grammar expressions—handling of additions, scalar multiplications, negatives, and reals is unspecified.
- Weakness: main metric is search success rate within 10 minutes; lacks wall-clock runtime benchmarks and end-to-end numerical savings.
- Weakness: scalability concerns—training data creation and clustering via full enumeration may be exponential and limits k.
- Weakness: greedy search strategy only; alternatives like beam search or MCTS are not evaluated.
- Weakness: lacks comparisons to stronger baselines (genetic programming, beam/MCTS, equality saturation, ILP/SAT).
- Weakness: scope limited to homogeneous polynomials and a small operation set; some claims (RBM/dropout) are speculative.
- Weakness: RNN often underperforms n-grams, raising questions about its practical benefit and limited ablation studies.

- Quality: technically interesting and coherent but weakened by lack of formal guarantees, unclear linear-combination synthesis, and weak baselines.
- Clarity: generally well-written but missing crucial details (full grammar, cost model, verification parameters, mapping from modular solutions to real coefficients).
- Originality: the combination of cost-annotated grammar, learned policy, and continuous symbolic embeddings is novel.
- Significance: potential impact on compiler optimizations and scientific computing, but current verification and scope limits reduce immediate impact.

- Key questions for authors: how are modular coefficients mapped to concrete expressions and coefficient domains?; what is false-positive probability and do you use multiple primes or symbolic checks?; can you report numeric wall-clock savings?; how large can k get before enumeration is infeasible?; would beam/MCTS improve results?

- Suggested improvements: add robust verification (multiple primes, randomized tests, symbolic proofs), clarify grammar and cost model, provide constructive synthesis of final expressions from modular solutions, compare to stronger baselines, and report empirical runtime/memory savings and more ablations.

- Pros: novel direction, empirical evidence learning aids search, public code/data.
- Cons: weak verification guarantees, unclear linear-combination handling, limited baselines and runtime evaluation, modest RNN gains.
- Overall recommendation: weak accept — promising direction but needs stronger verification, clearer synthesis, and richer empirical evaluation.
