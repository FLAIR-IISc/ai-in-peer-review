## NIPS Review: Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and intriguing approach to a challenging problem: automatically discovering mathematically equivalent, but computationally cheaper, expressions. The core idea of leveraging attribute grammars and machine learning to guide search is promising, and the results, while limited in scope, are compelling, demonstrating the discovery of identities not previously known. However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in presentation, particularly regarding the experimental setup and limitations, and the significance of the discovered identities needs to be more clearly articulated. I believe this paper is a **weak accept** contingent on significant revisions addressing the issues outlined below.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The authors aim to find computationally efficient versions of given mathematical expressions using a framework based on attribute grammars. Brute-force search quickly becomes intractable, so they employ machine learning (n-gram models and RNNs) to learn a search strategy based on solutions to simpler problems. They demonstrate successful identity discovery, reducing complexity in specific cases.

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, correctly identifying connections to theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation. The positioning of the work as distinct from existing systems (Maple, Mathematica) by focusing on *learning* search strategies is well-made. The connections to NLP (grammars, RNNs) are also appropriately drawn.  However, the discussion on [7] ("searching over the space of algorithms") feels superficial and the connection is not clearly explained.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars, machine learning-guided search, and the goal of automatically discovering mathematical identities is a truly novel contribution.
* **Promising Results:**  The demonstrated reductions in computational complexity (O(n^3) to O(n^2), exponential to O(n^2)/O(n^3)) are significant. The claim of discovering previously unknown identities is a strong point.
* **Well-Defined Framework:** The attribute grammar provides a structured approach to exploring the space of possible expressions.
* **Learned Representation:** The application of an RNN to learn a continuous representation of symbolic expressions is a clever idea, potentially opening doors for broader application of machine learning in this domain.
* **Code Availability:** Publicly available code and data are excellent for reproducibility and further research.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is often difficult to follow.  The notation is sometimes unclear (e.g., the definition of *k* keeps shifting slightly), and the description of the experimental setup is lacking in detail. It's hard to assess the scalability of the approach without a clearer understanding of the search space size at different *k* values beyond Table 3.
* **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental setup feels somewhat ad-hoc.  The choice of families of expressions feels driven by what’s tractable with the current system, rather than a systematic exploration of interesting mathematical problems.  The cut-off time of 10 minutes is arbitrary and makes comparing the performance of different strategies difficult. A more robust statistical comparison is needed.  What constitutes a “success” beyond finding *a* solution?  How sensitive are the results to the random initialization?
* **Significance of Identities:** While the authors claim to have discovered new identities, the paper doesn't sufficiently emphasize *why* these identities are important beyond the raw complexity reduction.  What practical problems could be solved more efficiently using these identities?  A more compelling demonstration of real-world impact would strengthen the paper considerably.
* **RNN Performance:** The RNN performance is consistently outperformed by n-gram models. The authors mention overfitting and dropout, but a deeper analysis of why the RNN isn't as effective would be valuable.  Is the learned representation truly capturing meaningful information, or is it simply memorizing patterns?
* **Grammar Limitations:** The reliance on a fixed grammar severely limits the scope of possible identities.  The authors acknowledge this, but the discussion could be more thorough, perhaps exploring potential extensions to the grammar.



**4. Specific Concerns & Questions:**

* **Table 3:** The numbers in Table 3 are difficult to interpret. What does "Search Space Size" actually represent?  Is it the total number of trees, or the number of valid rule combinations?
* **Section 6.3 (RNN):** The description of the RNN training process feels rushed.  More details on hyperparameter tuning (learning rate, dropout rate, dimensionality of the hidden layer) would be helpful.
* **Fig. 3:**  The error bars on the success rates are quite large. This suggests high variance in the results, which needs to be addressed.
* **Generalization:**  How well do the learned strategies generalize to expressions *outside* the families tested?  Could a strategy trained on (∑ AAT)k be effectively used to find identities in the RBM-2k family?




**5. Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The core idea is innovative and has the potential to significantly impact the field.
* The demonstrated identity discovery is a tangible result.
* The code and data availability promotes reproducibility.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The paper lacks clarity and rigor in its presentation and experimental evaluation.
* The significance of the discovered identities is not adequately emphasized.
* The RNN performance is underwhelming.




**Recommendation:** **Weak Accept** with major revisions. 

The authors should focus on:

1.  **Improving clarity and providing more details on the experimental setup.**
2.  **Conducting a more thorough statistical analysis of the results.**
3.  **Demonstrating the practical significance of the discovered identities.**
4.  **Providing a deeper analysis of the RNN performance and its limitations.**
5.  **Addressing the reviewer’s specific concerns.**

If these revisions are made, the paper could be a valuable contribution to NIPS. However, as it stands, the paper feels somewhat incomplete and the claims are not fully supported by the evidence presented.
