- Summary: Searches attribute-grammar-defined symbolic expressions to find lower-complexity equivalents for homogeneous polynomials (mainly matrix/vector), using enumeration, linear combinations, and Zp-based numeric equivalence checks; learns n-gram and RNN policies to guide search and shows learning outperforms random search on several families.

- Relation to prior work: Bridges theorem proving/program induction and NLP-style grammar learning; novel in applying learned grammars and continuous embeddings to symbolic algebra search focused on complexity reduction rather than mere simplification.

- Strength: Clear, practical problem formulation with potential compiler and ML applications.

- Strength: Elegant attribute grammar with complexity annotations and extension to linear combinations of trees for greater expressivity.

- Strength: Combines fast randomized Zp descriptors with learned RNN embeddings for equivalence checking and policy learning.

- Strength: Learned search (n-grams and RNN) finds identities unreachable by random search within a budget; n-grams excel on repetitive structure.

- Strength: Useful RNN training details and available code improve reproducibility.

- Weakness: Equivalence checking is probabilistic without formal Schwartz–Zippel-style bounds; collision/error probabilities are unquantified.

- Weakness: Discovered identities are not formally verified; reliance on randomized testing and supplemental material is insufficient.

- Weakness: Limited baselines—no comparisons to beam search, MCTS, A*, program synthesis, or CAS transformations targeting complexity.

- Weakness: Grammar scope is narrow (homogeneous polynomials, limited operators); removing matmul in some tests may bias results and exclude valid optimal solutions.

- Weakness: RNN underperforms n-grams on several families; failure modes and ablation studies (e.g., end-to-end fine-tuning) are lacking.

- Weakness: Evaluation metrics are limited (fraction of runs solving within 10 minutes); need time-to-solution, node expansions, diversity, and actual wall-clock speedups at scale.

- Weakness: Reproducibility gaps—full grammar, some hyperparameters, dataset sizes by k, and modular arithmetic handling are incompletely specified.

- Quality: Technically sound with solid engineering, but main scientific gap is lack of formal correctness guarantees and proofs for identities.

- Clarity: Paper is generally clear and well organized; examples are helpful.

- Clarity (suggestions): Include full grammar and complexity annotations, formal probability bounds for Zp descriptors, training protocol details across k, and representative identity proofs in the main paper.

- Originality: High—novel framing of identity discovery as learned search over attribute grammars with continuous symbolic embeddings.

- Significance: Moderate-to-high—advances learned guidance in combinatorial search and symbolic optimization; impact depends on stronger correctness guarantees and broader grammars.

- Questions for authors: What are theoretical collision probabilities for Zp descriptors and do you use multiple primes? Can discovered identities be post-verified by CAS/formal systems? How sensitive are results to grammar choices and matmul removal? Have you tried end-to-end or RL training for the RNN?

- Pros for acceptance: Novel problem/method, demonstrated nontrivial discoveries, convincing learned embeddings, and open-source implementation.

- Cons for acceptance: No formal correctness guarantees or proofs, limited baselines and failure analysis, narrow grammar with engineered constraints, and RNN not clearly superior to n-grams.

- Reproducibility: Code promised but paper must include full grammar, seeds, primes, and dataset details.

- Overall recommendation: Accept (7) — innovative and promising but needs stronger proof guarantees and comparisons.

- Reviewer confidence: High on ML aspects, moderate on symbolic algebra verification.
