

# Peer Review: "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions as grammar trees using attribute grammars and then employ learning methods to guide the search for efficient expressions (with lower computational complexity) that are equivalent to a target expression. The authors propose two learning-based search strategies—n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs)—that learn from solutions to simpler expressions to discover more complex identities.

The framework addresses a significant limitation of brute-force enumeration (exponential search space) by using learning to selectively explore promising branches of the grammar tree. The paper demonstrates the approach on several families of polynomial expressions, showing that it can discover identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) or from exponential to polynomial time. The authors provide a GitHub repository with code and evaluation data, and they claim to have discovered several previously unknown mathematical identities.

This work builds upon attribute grammars (originally from compiler construction), n-gram language models from NLP, and recursive neural networks for symbolic representation. It differs from previous work in symbolic computation (like Maple and Mathematica) by using learning rather than hand-designed heuristics, and it extends the application of NLP techniques to symbolic computation.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Application**: The paper makes a compelling case for applying machine learning techniques to a domain that has largely been approached with rule-based methods. The specific application to discovering mathematical identities with computational complexity improvements is genuinely novel.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The framework is well-structured with clear definitions of the attribute grammar, numerical and learned representations, and search strategies. The experiments are well-designed with appropriate baselines (random search) and multiple learning strategies compared.

3. **Significant Results**: The paper demonstrates substantial computational complexity improvements for several expression families. For example, the paper shows how to compute expressions that would take O(n³) time in O(n²) time, which represents a meaningful improvement for large n.

4. **Clarity and Reproducibility**: The paper is well-written with helpful examples (Examples 1 and 2) that clearly illustrate the approach. The inclusion of a GitHub repository with code and evaluation data significantly enhances reproducibility.

5. **Practical Implications**: The authors correctly identify potential applications in machine learning (e.g., for efficient computation of dropout or RBM partition functions), making the work relevant to the NIPS community.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overly Restrictive Scope**: The paper makes several significant assumptions that limit its applicability:
   - Only homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree k
   - Single matrix/vector variable
   - Scalar output
   While the authors acknowledge these limitations in Section 8, they don't adequately address how these constraints affect the generalizability of their approach. This is a major limitation, as most practical mathematical problems involve more complex expressions.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Analysis**: The paper provides strong empirical results but lacks theoretical analysis. There is no formal discussion of why the learning approaches work, how they relate to the structure of the search space, or guarantees about the completeness of the search. This is particularly surprising given the combinatorial nature of the problem.

3. **Limited Evaluation**: The evaluation is confined to five specific families of expressions. The paper doesn't compare to other symbolic computation methods (like Maple or Mathematica) to show how their approach differs and is better. Without this comparison, it's hard to assess the true value of their approach.

4. **Potential Overfitting Concerns**: The paper mentions using dropout to prevent overfitting, but doesn't provide evidence that the learned models aren't simply memorizing training examples. The success on k=2 to k=5 might be due to memorization rather than generalization, which would limit the approach's usefulness for larger k.

5. **Limited Discussion of Scalability**: The paper imposes a 10-minute time limit for experiments, but doesn't discuss how the approach scales to even larger problems. For k=15, the search space grows exponentially, and it's unclear whether the approach would remain practical.

6. **RNN Representation Details**: The paper's description of the RNN representation and how it's used for the search strategy is somewhat vague. The transition from the classification task to the search strategy could be explained more clearly.

## Overall Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound and well-executed, with clear experimental results. However, the lack of theoretical analysis and limited scope are notable weaknesses that prevent it from being a complete piece of work.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized. The examples effectively illustrate the approach. The GitHub repository enhances reproducibility.

**Originality**: The combination of attribute grammars with machine learning for mathematical identity discovery is genuinely novel. The application of NLP techniques to symbolic computation represents an interesting cross-pollination of ideas.

**Significance**: The work has potential significance for symbolic computation and compiler optimization, but the restricted scope limits its immediate impact. The paper doesn't fully demonstrate how these identities would be used in practice beyond the examples given.

## Pro and Con for Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel application of machine learning to discover mathematical identities
- Demonstrates significant computational complexity improvements for specific families of expressions
- Well-executed experiments with clear baselines
- Good reproducibility (code and data available)
- Addresses a challenging problem in symbolic computation

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Highly restricted scope (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix/vector, scalar output)
- Lack of theoretical analysis
- Limited evaluation to specific families of expressions
- No comparison to other symbolic computation methods
- Potential overfitting concerns without clear evidence

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Expand the scope**: Consider relaxing some of the assumptions (e.g., allowing multiple variables, non-scalar outputs) to demonstrate broader applicability. Discuss the limitations of the current framework and how they might be overcome.

2. **Add theoretical analysis**: Include a discussion of the search space properties, why the learning approaches work, and perhaps some formal guarantees about the completeness of the search.

3. **Include comparisons**: Compare the approach to other symbolic computation tools (Maple, Mathematica) to demonstrate the advantages of the learning-based approach.

4. **Address overfitting**: Provide evidence that the learned models generalize beyond the training examples, not just memorize them. Consider adding a validation set to measure generalization performance.

5. **Clarify the RNN representation**: Provide more details on how the RNN representation is used for the search strategy, particularly the transition from the classification task to the search strategy.

6. **Discuss scalability**: Provide more information about how the approach scales with larger k values, perhaps by including runtime data for larger k or discussing potential optimizations.

## Final Recommendation

This paper presents a novel and interesting approach to discovering mathematical identities using machine learning. While the framework is well-designed and the results are promising, the restricted scope and lack of theoretical analysis prevent it from being a complete contribution. I recommend acceptance with major revisions, particularly addressing the scope limitations, adding theoretical analysis, and including comparisons to other symbolic computation methods.

The paper has the potential to make a meaningful contribution to the field of symbolic computation and machine learning, but it needs to more thoroughly address the limitations of its approach and demonstrate broader applicability. With the suggested revisions, this paper could be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
