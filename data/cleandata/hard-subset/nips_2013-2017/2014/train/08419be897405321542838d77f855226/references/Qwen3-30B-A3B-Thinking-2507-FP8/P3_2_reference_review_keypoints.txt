- Proposes a framework that uses attribute grammars plus n-gram and RNN models to search for computationally efficient expressions equivalent to a target expression.  
- Strength: Novel application of ML (n-grams, RNNs) to guide symbolic computation search, showing learning from simpler expressions helps find solutions for harder ones.  
- Strength: Methodology is well-structured—grammar representation, numerical/learned expression comparison, ML-guided search, and experimental validation.  
- Strength: Strong experimental results showing improvements over random search and concrete complexity reductions (e.g., O(n^3)→O(n^2), exponential→polynomial).  
- Strength: Authors provide a candid analysis of limitations and failure cases (e.g., RBM-2 family).  
- Strength: Clear links to practical ML applications (e.g., efficient dropout computation, RBM partition function).  
- Weakness: Claims of novel identities are overstated; some discovered patterns are straightforward generalizations of known linear-algebraic optimizations.  
- Weakness: Problem scope is narrow (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix variable), limiting generality to real-world expressions with multiple variables or non-polynomial terms.  
- Weakness: Insufficient comparison to existing symbolic tools (Maple, Mathematica); no rigorous demonstration of outperforming them on the considered problems.  
- Weakness: RNN-based representation learning is presented as useful mainly for search guidance and lacks evidence of broader applicability.  
- Weakness: Overemphasis on learning—many examples could be found by simple pattern matching or known optimizations without ML.  
- Technical concern: Integer-based numerical descriptors modulo a prime may yield false positives; no analysis of required descriptor size N to keep collision probability low.  
- Technical concern: RNN training details are sparse (amount of training data per k, data generation), making it unclear whether models generalize or memorize.  
- Technical concern: Evaluation metric ("any non-zero count = success") is weak; time-to-solution and richer performance measures are missing for most cases.  
- Technical concern: Lacks theoretical analysis of search space, scaling, or guarantees for the learning-guided search methods.  
- Contribution: Demonstrates that learning from simpler expressions can guide search for complex identities, but novelty is incremental given prior grammar and ML-for-symbolic work.  
- Overall quality: Technically sound experiments and methodology but overstates novelty and lacks theoretical grounding.  
- Clarity: Well-written and clearly organized with helpful examples.  
- Originality: Moderately original in combining components, but core ideas have precedent in related fields.  
- Significance: Limited by narrow problem scope and insufficient evidence of broad applicability.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept—solid niche contribution but needs stronger novelty claims, scope expansion, and more rigorous comparisons/analysis.  
- Suggested improvements: clarify which identities are truly novel; discuss extensions to more general expressions; compare to pattern-matching baselines and symbolic tools; add theoretical analysis; give concrete ML system use-cases; analyze false-positive probability for numerical representation.
