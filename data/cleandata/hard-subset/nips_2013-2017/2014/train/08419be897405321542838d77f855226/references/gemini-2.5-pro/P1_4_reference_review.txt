This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent the space of possible mathematical expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is equivalent to a given target expression but has lower computational complexity. As the search space is intractably large, the main contribution is the use of machine learning techniques to guide the search, learning a policy from solutions found for simpler problems. The authors explore both n-gram models and a more sophisticated recursive neural network (RNN) to learn this search strategy.

The approach is creative and well-executed. It bridges the gap between symbolic computation and statistical machine learning, a direction of great interest and potential impact. The paper relates this work to several fields, including automated theorem proving, program induction, and natural language processing, correctly identifying its unique position. While symbolic engines like Mathematica can simplify expressions, they typically rely on pre-programmed rules and do not learn search strategies or explicitly optimize for computational complexity in this manner. The use of an RNN to learn a continuous embedding for symbolic expressions, analogous to word embeddings in NLP, is particularly novel and exciting.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses a challenging and important problem: the automated discovery of efficient algorithms. This is a fundamental task in computer science and mathematics. Applying machine learning to guide this symbolic search is a highly original approach. The potential discovery of new, useful mathematical identities (as claimed for several expression families) is a significant achievement that could have practical implications in scientific computing and machine learning itself (e.g., for partition function approximations).

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The overall framework is well-conceived. The use of an attribute grammar to define the search space is appropriate. The "numerical descriptor" method for checking expression equivalence is a clever and pragmatic solution to avoid the bottleneck of slow symbolic manipulation, making a large-scale search feasible. The learning-based search strategies are well-motivated, drawing sensible parallels from NLP.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments clearly demonstrate the value of the learning-based approach. For several families of complex expressions, the random search baseline fails completely as the problem complexity (`k`) increases, while the n-gram and RNN-guided strategies continue to find solutions. This provides compelling evidence that learning from simpler instances successfully transfers to more complex ones. The discovery of polynomial-time equivalents for expressions that are naively exponential to compute is particularly impressive.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The introductory examples are excellent for motivating the problem and providing intuition. The structure is logical, moving from the problem definition to the framework, the representation methods, the search strategies, and finally the experiments.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Analysis of Model Performance:** A surprising result is that the simpler n-gram models (particularly 3-, 4-, and 5-grams) often outperform the more sophisticated RNN model. While the authors report this honestly, the paper would be strengthened by a deeper analysis of *why* this might be the case. Is it because the solutions for the chosen expression families possess a highly regular, repetitive local structure that is perfectly captured by n-grams? It would be interesting to see if there exists a class of problems where the compositional generalization of the RNN would be superior. As it stands, the contribution of the RNN to the *search* part of the problem seems less pronounced than its contribution to the *representation* part.

2.  **Scope and Generalizability:** The framework is currently restricted to a relatively narrow class of expressions: homogeneous polynomials with a single variable and a scalar output. The authors acknowledge this, but a more thorough discussion of the challenges in extending the framework would be welcome. For instance, how would the numerical descriptor handle non-polynomial functions (e.g., trigonometric, exponential) or functions with discontinuities? How would the grammar and search need to be adapted to handle recursion or conditional logic? Acknowledging these hurdles would help frame the current work as a strong foundational step.

3.  **Clarity on RNN Training:** The paper lists several "crucial" details for successfully training the RNN (identity initialization, curriculum learning, different learning rates, etc.). While it is good that these are shared for reproducibility, they come across as a collection of implementation tricks. The paper could be improved by providing more intuition or theoretical justification for why these specific choices are so critical. For example, a brief explanation of how identity initialization prevents vanishing/exploding gradients in deep networks would be helpful for the reader.

**Summary of Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by strong experimental results. The authors are honest about the relative performance of their proposed models. It is a complete piece of work.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The core ideas are communicated effectively through good examples and logical flow.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. The application of guided search with learned policies to the discovery of mathematical identities is a novel concept. The use of an RNN to create continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions is a standout contribution.
*   **Significance:** The results are important and likely to be influential. The framework itself could be a valuable tool in scientific computing and compiler design. The broader idea of using ML to navigate vast symbolic search spaces has far-reaching implications for AI and automated reasoning. Other researchers are very likely to build on these ideas.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and challenging problem with a highly novel machine learning-based approach.
*   Demonstrates impressive results, discovering new, non-obvious identities and finding polynomial-time solutions for exponentially complex expressions.
*   The idea of learning continuous representations for symbolic expressions is a significant conceptual contribution with potential applications beyond this specific paper.
*   The work is well-executed, clearly presented, and opens up many interesting avenues for future research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The simpler n-gram models often work better than the more complex RNN, which could be seen as diminishing the impact of the more sophisticated model for the search task.
*   The scope of problems addressed is currently limited, and the path to generalization is non-trivial.

In conclusion, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, significant, and well-executed piece of research that sits at an exciting intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. It is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
