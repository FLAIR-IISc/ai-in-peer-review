- Overall assessment: Novel approach combining attribute grammars and learning to discover efficient mathematical identities; strong core contribution warranting acceptance despite some shortcomings.
- Summary: Formulates discovering cheaper evaluations of expressions as search over an attribute grammar guided by learned models (n-grams and RNNs), trained on simpler instances.
- Result highlight: Successfully reduces computational complexity for several expressions and discovers previously unknown identities; codebase provided for reproducibility.
- Novelty: Applying machine learning to symbolic expression simplification with attribute grammars and learning-guided search is a unique contribution.
- Significance: Demonstrated substantial complexity reductions (e.g., O(n^3)→O(n^2)) with potential compiler and ML library applications.
- Technical soundness: Framework is well-defined; numeric matching for expressions is a practical solution; RNN architecture is standard and appropriate.
- Clarity: Paper is generally well-written with helpful examples and available code/data.
- Representation learning: RNN-based continuous representations of symbolic expressions are an interesting direction for future work.
- RNN performance issue: RNN underperforms n-gram models; paper lacks deep analysis of reasons (e.g., overfitting, low embedding dimensionality).
- Limited experiments: Evaluation covers five expression families but needs broader scope, more k values, and hyperparameter tuning comparisons.
- Scaling concerns: Reliance on training from simpler expressions may not scale to highly complex solution spaces (e.g., RBM-2k family).
- Grammar limitations: Restricted setting (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix) and challenges in handling recursion are insufficiently analyzed.
- Statistical significance missing: Success rates lack formal statistical tests and comprehensive uncertainty reporting.
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, impactful idea with empirical success, clever integration of grammars and learning, and reproducible artifacts.
- Arguments against acceptance: RNN weaknesses, limited experimental scope, and unclear scalability to more complex identities.
- Overall recommendation: Accept, with suggested improvements including deeper RNN analysis and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation.
