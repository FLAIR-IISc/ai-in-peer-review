Summary
The paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient reformulations of target symbolic expressions. Expressions are generated by an attribute grammar whose rules carry complexity attributes. The space of grammar trees is explored by a scheduler guided by learned strategies. Equality to the target is checked via a numerical descriptor: evaluations over random inputs modulo a large prime (to avoid FP issues), with equivalence of expressions determined by matching descriptors. For hard targets that cannot be represented by a single tree, the method solves a linear system over Zp to find linear combinations of trees whose descriptor matches the target’s descriptor. To guide search, the authors train (i) simple n-gram models over subtrees from previously discovered solutions, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions by classifying them into equivalence classes and then, with a softmax head, predicting the next grammar rule. Experiments show substantial improvements over random search on several expression families, with many identities allegedly new and achieving significant complexity reductions (e.g., O(n^3) to O(n^2), or exponential to polynomial for certain RBM-inspired sums). Code and data are released.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself against theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, and borrows modeling ideas from NLP. Closely related but missing or underemphasized connections include: symbolic regression and grammar-guided genetic programming (Koza; grammar evolution), superoptimization (Massalin; STOKE), compiler algebraic simplification and strength reduction, and the Spiral system for automatically deriving fast linear transforms via algebraic identities (Püschel et al.). Also relevant are equality-saturation/e-graph approaches and term-rewriting/equational reasoning. Compared to these, the novelty here is to learn search heuristics from solved instances and to use randomized modular evaluations as an equivalence oracle at scale.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of a constrained but meaningful problem (homogeneous polynomials producing scalars) and a concrete grammar-based representation with complexity attributes.
- Practical equivalence-checking via modular evaluations enables scalable search and data generation.
- Learning to guide discrete search is effective: n-gram and RNN strategies improve discovery rates and enable higher-degree identities than random search.
- The RNN yields a continuous embedding for expressions that generalizes across k, with good classification accuracy on equivalence classes; the curriculum and initialization tricks are well-motivated.
- Code release and multiple families, including challenging RBM-inspired sums, increase credibility and potential impact.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Correctness/equality guarantees: The reliance on random evaluations over Zp is reasonable, but the paper lacks a formal collision analysis (e.g., via Schwartz–Zippel). Please quantify the failure probability given degree, number of variables, and choice of p, and mitigate via multiple primes.
- Linear combinations of trees: It is unclear what coefficient domain is permitted in w, and how a solution to Fw = t mod p maps to a realizable symbolic expression within the grammar. If coefficients can be arbitrary modulo p, do they correspond to operations present in the grammar (e.g., integer-weighted sums, subtraction)? Is the complexity model accounting for building such linear combinations? Some discussion of integrality constraints, coefficient size control, and potential spurious matches modulo p is needed.
- Cost model: The complexity accounting is informal and sometimes enforced by removing expensive rules (e.g., matrix–matrix multiply). Please specify the exact operation counts charged to each rule and include the cost of combining multiple trees. Empirical timing (microbenchmarks) on small sizes would substantiate claimed improvements.
- Evaluation: The primary metric is “fraction of runs that find a solution in 10 minutes.” While informative for search difficulty, it is not a direct measure of the quality or generality of identities. Include (a) the number and form of identities found per family and k (at least representative examples in the main text), (b) proof sketches or inductive derivations for the main recurring patterns, and (c) comparisons to known results (e.g., dynamic programming for symmetric polynomials).
- Learning ablations: The RNN underperforms n-grams on most families. Provide ablations on beam vs greedy scheduling, beam width, search depth, training set size, and alternative policies (e.g., MCTS/UCT or learned value functions). A simple beam search may materially change results.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, but several key details should be clarified: explicit grammar (table is referenced but not reproduced here); constraints on w; size and balance of the equivalence-class dataset; number of primes and N used; and how multi-variable examples are handled beyond A,B in Example 1.
- Move at least a few “new identities” into the main text; relying on supplementary only weakens the narrative.

Originality
- The integration of learned heuristics (n-gram/RNN) with a grammar-based symbolic search and modular-equivalence oracle is novel and timely. However, novelty is somewhat diluted by missing connections to prior work on grammar-guided program synthesis/optimization and fast transform search (e.g., Spiral). Position your contributions against these.

Significance
- If robust and generalizable, the approach could impact compiler algebraic optimization and symbolic computation, and the embedding of expressions is of independent interest. Currently, demonstrated scope is narrow (homogeneous scalar polynomials; frequent removal of expensive rules; limited multi-variable experiments). Strengthening generalization experiments and including stronger baselines would raise significance.

Questions for authors
1. What are concrete bounds on collision probability for your descriptor-based equality test? Do you use multiple primes?
2. What constraints are enforced on w to ensure a realizable expression in the grammar with controlled coefficient growth and cost?
3. How sensitive are results to greedy scheduling vs beam search? Did you try MCTS?
4. Can you report actual operation counts and/or empirical runtimes for representative identities?
5. For Symk, do you rediscover the standard O(nk) DP? How do your forms compare?

Pros
- Clear problem setup and practical system.
- Learning improves search substantially over random.
- Interesting, sometimes nontrivial identities; code released.
- Continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions with good classification accuracy.

Cons
- Lack of formal correctness guarantees and mapping from linear-system solutions to grammar expressions.
- Limited evaluation metrics and missing strong baselines.
- Scope restrictions and occasional removal of key grammar rules.
- Underdeveloped related work positioning.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying correctness/coefficients, adding representative identities and cost accounting in the main text, and strengthening comparisons to related program synthesis/optimization work.
