This paper explores the application of machine learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities within a grammar-based framework. The core idea—using learned strategies to navigate a search space of symbolic expressions—is interesting, but the paper suffers from a lack of convincing evidence and a tendency towards overstatement.

The authors establish a formal framework using attribute grammars, which is a reasonable approach for representing and manipulating symbolic expressions. However, the novelty of this framework *specifically* for the stated problem isn’t strongly articulated. Attribute grammars are a well-established technique; the innovation lies in the integration with learning to guide the search, not the grammar itself. The problem is well-defined, limiting the scope to polynomials, which is a sensible choice for initial exploration. 

The experimental evaluation presents mixed results. The RNN-based representation appears promising, achieving reasonable classification accuracy on expressions of varying degree. However, the key claim – the discovery of “many new mathematical identities” – is not thoroughly substantiated. The presented examples (k=2 and k=6) are interesting, but the scope of discovered identities feels limited, especially given the claims of significant complexity reduction. The experiments primarily focus on specific families of expressions, and the generalization ability of the learned strategies is not convincingly demonstrated. The performance gains from learning (particularly the RNN approach) are often marginal compared to simpler n-gram models, raising questions about the efficacy of the more complex model. The statement that most examples are "too complex to be found manually or by exhaustive search" feels unsubstantiated; more rigorous comparisons against optimized exhaustive searches would be beneficial. The dependency on a fixed cut-off time (10 minutes) also makes comparison between strategies difficult.

The paper's clarity is somewhat hindered by the dense mathematical notation and a lack of intuitive explanation for some of the design choices, particularly the dropout rate in the RNN. While the related work section is reasonably comprehensive, it downplays the connection to existing symbolic computation systems (e.g., Mathematica, Maple) which *already* perform simplification, albeit with rule-based rather than learning-based approaches. The discussion of limitations is also somewhat superficial.

**Strengths:**

*   Well-defined problem and formal framework using attribute grammars.
*   Demonstration of a learned representation for symbolic expressions (RNN).
*   Potential for discovering novel identities in a systematic manner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Overstated claims about the number and significance of discovered identities.
*   Limited experimental scope and lack of rigorous comparison to exhaustive search.
*   Marginal performance gains from the more complex RNN model.
*   Lack of clarity in some of the experimental details.
*   Limited discussion of the broader impact and scalability of the approach.
*   The assumption that the grammar fully captures the interesting identities within the scope is questionable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel integration of machine learning and symbolic computation.
*   The learned representation (RNN) shows potential for future research.
*   The framework provides a systematic approach to identity discovery.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The claims of significant contributions are not adequately supported by evidence.
*   The experimental results are not compelling enough to justify acceptance.
*   The paper lacks a thorough comparison to existing symbolic computation systems.
*   The limited scope and scalability of the approach raise concerns about its long-term impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the ideas presented are interesting, the paper falls short of demonstrating a significant contribution to the field. The claims are overstated, the experimental evaluation is limited, and the performance gains are marginal. The paper feels more like a proof-of-concept than a substantial advancement. The authors should consider expanding the experimental evaluation, providing more rigorous comparisons, and tempering their claims. Further work is needed to establish the true potential of this approach.
