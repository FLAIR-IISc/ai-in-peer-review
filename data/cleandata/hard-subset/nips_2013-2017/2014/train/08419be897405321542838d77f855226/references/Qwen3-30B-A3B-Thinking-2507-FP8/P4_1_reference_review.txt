

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The authors frame the problem as finding an expression S that computes the same result as a target expression T but with lower computational complexity. They introduce an attribute grammar-based representation for symbolic expressions and use machine learning techniques (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for efficient identities, rather than relying on brute-force enumeration. The approach trains on simpler expressions to discover solutions for more complex ones, demonstrating the discovery of previously unknown identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n^3) to O(n^2) or better.

The paper relates its work to several related domains: theorem proving (which deals with undecidability issues), program induction, probabilistic programming, and symbolic computation engines like Maple and Mathematica. While these systems simplify expressions, they don't explicitly seek versions of lower complexity. The authors note their work is the first to apply learning techniques to this specific problem, though they acknowledge some related work in attribute grammars (e.g., [7, 19, 26]) and recursive neural networks for symbolic tasks (e.g., [23]).

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-structured framework with clear mathematical definitions. The attribute grammar formalism is appropriately applied to the problem, and the experimental design (with controlled variables, multiple search strategies, and statistical evaluation) demonstrates good methodological rigor. The use of integer-based descriptors to avoid numerical issues in expression comparison is a thoughtful technical detail.

2. **Novelty:** The core contribution—applying machine learning to guide the search for efficient mathematical identities—appears genuinely novel. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from related work in theorem proving, symbolic computation, and program induction. The discovery of new identities (e.g., the pattern in Example 2) represents a concrete technical contribution.

3. **Significance:** The paper demonstrates practical improvements in computational complexity (e.g., from O(n^3) to O(n^2)) for several mathematical expressions relevant to machine learning (e.g., RBM partition functions). The framework's potential for integration with compilers or optimization tools could have meaningful practical implications.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with helpful examples (Examples 1 and 2) and figures (Fig. 1, Fig. 2) that illustrate key concepts. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the problem statement and technical approach are well-explained.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope:** The paper restricts the problem to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with a single matrix or vector variable. This significantly limits the applicability of the framework. The authors acknowledge this limitation (Section 8), but the paper doesn't adequately demonstrate how the approach would generalize to more complex mathematical expressions.

2. **Comparative Evaluation:** The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing symbolic computation approaches (e.g., Mathematica, Maple) or other methods for discovering mathematical identities. The claim that their discovered identities are "previously undiscovered" would be strengthened by a literature search showing they haven't been found previously.

3. **RNN Performance:** The RNN approach underperforms compared to n-gram models in the experiments (Fig. 3), yet the paper doesn't thoroughly explain this discrepancy. The authors note that "patterns that don't have a simple repetitive structure are much more difficult to generalize," but don't provide sufficient analysis of why the RNN fails in these cases.

4. **Reproducibility:** While the authors provide a GitHub link with code and data, the paper lacks sufficient detail on hyperparameters, training procedures, and computational resources to ensure full reproducibility of their results.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a well-defined problem.
- It demonstrates concrete improvements in computational complexity for specific mathematical expressions.
- The framework has potential applications in machine learning optimization and compiler design.
- The technical details and methodology are well-explained and rigorously evaluated.

*Con:*
- The restricted scope of the problem limits the generalizability of the approach.
- The lack of comparison with existing symbolic computation tools weakens the significance claim.
- The RNN approach underperforms compared to simpler n-gram models, raising questions about its value.
- The claim of "previously undiscovered" identities needs stronger supporting evidence.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a technically sound contribution to the intersection of symbolic computation and machine learning. While the scope is limited, the framework is novel and demonstrates significant improvements over brute-force search for specific problem classes. The paper is clearly written and well-structured, with appropriate experimental validation. The main limitations are the restricted problem scope and lack of comparison with existing symbolic computation approaches, but these do not undermine the core technical contribution.

The paper aligns with NIPS's focus on machine learning and its applications. The problem of combinatorial search with machine learning guidance is relevant to broader AI challenges, and the paper's approach has potential for wider application beyond mathematical identities.

Given the technical soundness, novelty of the approach, and demonstrated improvements in computational complexity, I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, and with minor revisions addressing the comparative evaluation and scope limitations, it could be even stronger.
