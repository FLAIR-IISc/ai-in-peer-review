Summary
This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target expression. The authors define an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic and elementwise operations and search the induced expression space. To test equivalence, they eschew symbolic manipulation in favor of a numerical descriptor: evaluating expressions on random inputs in Z_p and comparing results. Because many identities require sums of sub-expressions, they also solve for linear combinations of candidate trees by Gaussian elimination mod p. To scale beyond brute force/random search, they learn search policies from simpler targets: (i) n-gram statistics over subtrees, and (ii) a recursive neural network that embeds expression trees and predicts next rules. They show case studies (e.g., sum(sum(A*B)) and sum(sum((A*B)^k))) and report success rates (fraction of runs finding a solution within 10 minutes) on several expression families, where n-gram models often outperform the RNN. They also train the RNN to classify expressions into equivalence classes (derived by numerical equality), achieving ≤5% error for k up to 6.

Strengths
- Ambitious goal with a clear, modular framework (grammar, equivalence testing, linear combination solver, learned search strategy).
- The attribute-grammar formulation is natural and re-usable; the scheduler/strategy split is clean.
- Empirical evidence that simple learned heuristics (n-grams) can dramatically improve search over random baselines for larger k.
- The application of an RNN to learn continuous embeddings over algebraic expression trees is interesting and may enable other ML-for-symbolic tasks.
- The examples connect to linear-algebra identities, and the 1^T A trick underlying Example 1 is a sensible optimization target.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Verification/equality testing: The approach relies on random evaluation in Z_p at N points and solving Fw = t mod p. This can only provide probabilistic evidence of equivalence, and solving modulo p can introduce spurious solutions not valid over reals/integers. No error bounds (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel-style) are stated, no multi-prime cross-checking, and no independent symbolic verification is reported. Without formal guarantees or certified proofs for the “discovered” identities, the claims are not sufficiently substantiated.
- Complexity claims: The paper asserts O(n^2) or O(n(m+p)) runtimes without formal operation counts or explicit computational models. In some experiments, the matrix-multiply rule is removed to “ensure” O(n^2) solutions, which is an artificial constraint rather than a proof of improved complexity for the original target. There is no notion of optimality or lower bounds, only evidence that some expression with a lower upper bound was found.
- Novelty of identities: Example 1 and the chain for sum(sum((A B)^k)) are straightforward consequences of linearity and the use of the all-ones vector (1^T (A B)^k 1), known tricks in numerical linear algebra. The claim that “the majority” of identities are “previously undiscovered” is unconvincing without a survey, proofs, or comparison to well-known algebraic transformations (e.g., using associativity/commutativity and matrix identities).
- Evaluation methodology: The main metric is the fraction of random seeds that find a solution within a 10-minute cap on a 16-core machine. There is no comparison to established systems (CAS like Mathematica/Maple with FullSimplify; rewrite systems; Groebner bases for polynomial identities; classical algebraic complexity or dynamic programming for matrix-chain-style optimization). Reporting success fractions without wall-clock distributions, solution quality (cost), or reproducibility of particular identities is weak evidence.
- Scope limitations: The framework is restricted to homogeneous polynomials yielding scalars, a small set of operations, and degree-limited expressions. Claims about connections to dropout/RBM partition functions are speculative; no end-to-end application or concrete speedup in a practical ML pipeline is shown.
- RNN underperformance: In the core discovery task, n-gram heuristics generally outperform the RNN. This undermines the paper’s central learning contribution; the value of the continuous embedding is not demonstrated beyond the classification proxy.
- Ambiguity in linear combinations: Solving Fw = t mod p yields coefficients modulo p; there is no discussion of recovering small integer coefficients, constraints on signs, or verifying that the linear combination corresponds to a valid expression over the intended ring/field. This further weakens correctness guarantees.

Quality
- The work is technically interesting but lacks rigor where it matters most: correctness and complexity. Probabilistic identity testing is fine if accompanied by failure probability bounds and cross-prime validation; here, none are provided. Complexity improvements require formal counts, not just removal of certain grammar rules.
- Many details are missing or deferred to supplementary material (e.g., the exact grammar, the discovered identities, proofs/validation).

Clarity
- Generally readable and well organized. However, key definitions (Table 1 rules), the exact cost model, and the verification protocol should be explicit in the main text. The experimental section would benefit from concrete identities with proofs rather than success-rate plots alone.

Originality
- The combination of attribute grammars with learned search policies for algebraic identities is novel. Learning continuous embeddings of algebraic expressions was less common at the time. However, several components (n-grams, randomized evaluation for identity testing, linear algebra tricks) are standard.

Significance
- Potentially impactful if correctness and complexity claims were made rigorous and if demonstrated on practically relevant problems with end-to-end speedups. In its current form, the contribution is preliminary and largely heuristic, with limited evidence that the approach outperforms strong symbolic baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide formal probabilistic guarantees for identity testing (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel bounds), use multiple primes and random points, and include symbolic proofs (e.g., via CAS) for all reported identities. Make identities and proof scripts available.
- Define a precise cost model (operation counts) and report measured counts for target vs discovered expressions; provide proofs of asymptotic improvements when claimed.
- Compare against CAS simplification/rewrite baselines and classical algebraic techniques (Groebner bases for polynomial identities, dynamic programming for matrix chains). Discuss where your grammar exceeds these baselines’ capabilities.
- Clarify the linear-combination solver: constrain coefficients to small integers, recover them from mod p solutions, and verify over the intended field.
- Demonstrate a realistic application (e.g., a speedup in an ML pipeline or compiler pass) using discovered identities.
- Either show the RNN provides tangible benefits over n-grams (e.g., on non-local patterns) or reposition the RNN component as exploratory.

Pros
- Clear problem statement and modular framework
- Empirical evidence that simple learned heuristics can guide search
- Interesting application of RNNs to expression trees

Cons
- Lack of rigorous verification and complexity analysis
- Weak evaluation against established baselines
- Novelty of sample identities questionable
- RNN underperforms simpler n-grams in core task

Recommendation: Reject

Confidence: 4/5
