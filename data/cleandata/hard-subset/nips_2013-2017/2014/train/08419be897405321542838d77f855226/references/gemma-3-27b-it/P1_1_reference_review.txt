## Peer Review for "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and intriguing approach to discovering mathematical identities by combining attribute grammars with machine learning techniques. The idea of leveraging learning to guide the search for efficient symbolic representations is compelling, and the results demonstrate significant improvements over naive search strategies, even discovering previously unknown identities. While the scope is currently limited, the potential impact is substantial. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions, primarily focusing on clarifying certain experimental details and expanding the discussion of limitations.

**1. Summary and Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of mathematical expressions. It proposes a framework based on attribute grammars to represent symbolic expressions and explores using both n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to learn search strategies within the grammar tree. The learned strategies aim to prioritize branches likely to yield more efficient expressions. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several families of expressions, finding optimizations that are non-trivial and previously undiscovered. 

The related work section is well-structured and relevant. It correctly positions the work within the broader context of theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, clearly outlining the distinctions and limitations compared to existing approaches. The connection to NLP techniques (grammars, n-grams, RNNs) is also appropriately highlighted. The authors acknowledge the lack of prior work combining grammars and learning for formula discovery, strengthening the novelty of their contribution.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars with machine learning to discover mathematical identities is genuinely novel. It’s a creative application of techniques from different fields.
* **Significant Results:** The paper demonstrates substantial improvements in computational complexity for several expressions (O(n³) to O(n²), exponential to polynomial). The discovery of previously unknown identities is particularly impressive.
* **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental setup is well-defined, using a clear evaluation metric (computational complexity reduction). The experiments across multiple families of expressions provide good coverage. The ablation study comparing different learning strategies (n-gram, RNN) is valuable.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the core concepts (attribute grammars, search strategies, learned representations).
* **Code Availability:** The authors provide code and data, facilitating reproducibility and further research.
* **Learned Representation:** The use of an RNN to learn a continuous representation of symbolic expressions is a significant contribution, opening doors to applying further ML techniques in this domain.



**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope:** The current framework is restricted to homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix variable. While the authors acknowledge this and discuss potential extensions, it limits the immediate applicability of the approach.  
* **RNN Performance:** While the RNN is a valuable contribution for representing symbolic structures, its performance in guiding the search was consistently surpassed by the n-gram models. The reasons for this – potentially related to the complexity of capturing long-range dependencies in the grammar trees – could be discussed further.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of dropout rate in the RNN and the order of n-grams appear to be sensitive to the problem. A more systematic hyperparameter search and analysis would strengthen the results.
* **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates success on certain families of expressions, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of *why* the approach fails for others (e.g., the RBM-2 family). Understanding failure modes would be beneficial.
* **Descriptor Representation Details:** More detail regarding the choice of the prime number 'p' used in the integer-based descriptor and its impact on preventing numerical issues would be helpful.




**4. Detailed Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality (4/5):** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical foundation based on attribute grammars is solid, and the experimental results are well-supported. The use of a linear system to check for equivalence is a clever approach. While the scope is limited, within those limitations, the work is complete and well-executed. The honesty about limitations and acknowledgment of potential future improvements is commendable.
* **Clarity (4/5):** The paper is generally clear and well-organized.  The introduction provides a good overview of the problem and the proposed solution. However, some sections (e.g., the description of the RNN architecture) could benefit from further simplification or visual aids. 
* **Originality (5/5):** The core idea of combining attribute grammars with machine learning for identity discovery is highly original. While individual components (grammars, n-grams, RNNs) are well-established, their application to this specific problem is novel.
* **Significance (4/5):** The potential impact is significant. Discovering efficient mathematical identities can have practical benefits in various domains, especially those involving large-scale computations (e.g., machine learning, scientific simulations). The demonstration of previously unknown identities further underscores the significance of the work.  

**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Demonstrates a novel and effective approach to discovering mathematical identities.
* Achieves significant improvements in computational complexity.
* Discovers previously unknown identities.
* Provides a strong empirical evaluation.
* Offers a valuable contribution to the intersection of symbolic computation and machine learning.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited scope of the current framework.
* RNN performance is not consistently superior to simpler n-gram models.
* Lack of detailed error analysis.




**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. 

**Suggested Revisions:**

* **Expand Discussion of Limitations:**  Elaborate on the limitations of the current framework (single variable, homogeneous polynomials) and outline a roadmap for addressing these limitations in future work.
* **RNN Analysis:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of why the RNN’s performance trails behind the n-gram models. Explore potential architectural improvements or training strategies.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:**  Briefly discuss the hyperparameter tuning process (e.g., choice of dropout rate, n-gram order) and explain the rationale behind the chosen values.
* **Error Analysis:** Include a more detailed discussion of the failure cases, particularly for the RBM-2 family.
* **Descriptor Details:** Provide additional details regarding the choice of the prime number ‘p’ in the integer-based descriptor.




In conclusion, this is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach. Addressing the minor revisions suggested above will further strengthen the work and enhance its impact on the field.
