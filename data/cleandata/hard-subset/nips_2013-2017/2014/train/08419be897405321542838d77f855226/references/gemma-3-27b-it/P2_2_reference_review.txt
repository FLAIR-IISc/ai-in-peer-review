## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and interesting approach to discovering mathematical identities, leveraging machine learning to guide the search within a grammar-based framework. The results are promising, showcasing the ability to find identities that are non-trivial and, in some cases, previously unknown. While the technical details are solid and the experimental setup is reasonable, the scope is somewhat limited, and the performance gains aren't universally strong across all tested families. I recommend **accepting** the paper with minor revisions, primarily focusing on clarifying the limitations and expanding the discussion of future work.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the challenging problem of automating the discovery of computationally efficient mathematical identities. It introduces a framework based on attribute grammars to represent symbolic expressions and utilizes machine learning – specifically n-gram models and a recursive neural network – to intelligently explore the vast search space of possible rule combinations. The authors demonstrate that their approach can uncover optimizations like reducing the complexity of matrix computations from O(n^3) to O(n^2). The related work section is well-written, positioning the work within the context of theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation, while effectively highlighting the novelty of combining these areas with machine learning.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars with machine learning for identity discovery is a genuinely novel contribution. This is not a straightforward application of existing techniques.
* **Technical Soundness:** The methodology appears well-founded. The use of attribute grammars provides a structured search space, and the integer-based descriptor for numerical comparison is a clever workaround for floating-point issues. The RNN architecture is appropriately chosen and implemented.
* **Demonstrated Results:** The paper provides concrete examples of discovered identities that reduce computational complexity.  Finding identities that are *previously unknown* is a strong signal of impact.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The examples (especially Example 1 & 2) are helpful for understanding the core idea.
* **Code Availability:** Providing code and data promotes reproducibility and further research.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope:** The problem is significantly constrained by focusing on homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix variable. While the authors acknowledge this, it's a substantial limitation. The applicability to broader classes of mathematical expressions isn't immediately clear.
* **Variable Performance:** While successful for some families (e.g., ∑ AAT), the results on the RBM-based families are less impressive. The RNN doesn't consistently outperform the random strategy in these cases. This suggests the current learning approach isn't universally effective.
* **n-gram Dominance:** The n-gram models consistently outperform the RNN, which is somewhat surprising given the RNN's potential for capturing more complex patterns. The authors provide a potential explanation (repetitive patterns), but further investigation into *why* the RNN struggles would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of dropout rate and the RNN’s hidden layer size (l=30) are mentioned, but a more detailed discussion of how these hyperparameters were tuned and their impact on performance would strengthen the paper.
* **Scalability Concerns:** While the framework works for the considered expression degrees, the exponential growth of the search space raises questions about its scalability to significantly larger or more complex problems.



**4. Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** Technically sound, with appropriate analysis and experimentation. The limitations are acknowledged. A complete piece of work within its defined scope.
* **Clarity:**  Generally clear, though some sections (e.g., the RNN training details) could benefit from further elaboration.
* **Originality:** Highly original in its combination of techniques and problem framing.  The related work section demonstrates awareness of existing research.
* **Significance:** The potential impact is significant. Automating the discovery of mathematical identities could have applications in compiler optimization, machine learning algorithm design (as the authors suggest), and potentially other areas.

**5. Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Novel approach with promising results.
* Solid technical foundation and clear presentation.
* Discovering previously unknown identities is a significant achievement.
* Code availability facilitates reproducibility.

**Against:**

* Limited scope and scalability concerns.
* Inconsistent performance across different expression families.
* n-gram models unexpectedly outperform the RNN.



**6. Recommendation & Suggestions for Revision:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper because the core idea is novel, the technical execution is strong, and the demonstrated results are encouraging.  However, I suggest the following revisions:

* **Expand Discussion of Limitations:**  More explicitly discuss the limitations of the current framework (scope, scalability, performance variability).
* **Analyze RNN Performance:** Investigate *why* the RNN doesn’t consistently outperform the n-gram models. Perhaps the RNN is struggling to generalize to patterns outside of those seen during training.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Provide more details on the hyperparameter selection process.
* **Future Work:** Elaborate on potential extensions to address the limitations.  For example, exploring alternative learning architectures, incorporating more general grammar rules, or developing strategies for handling larger search spaces.
* **Minor Clarity Improvements:**  Consider adding a brief summary table listing the discovered identities and their complexity improvements.



In conclusion, this is a valuable contribution that pushes the boundaries of applying machine learning to symbolic computation. The revisions suggested above will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
