Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target symbolic expression. Expressions are generated from an attribute grammar whose rules carry computational cost. Because exhaustive enumeration of grammar trees is infeasible for complex targets, the authors learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets. Two learned strategies are explored: (1) n-gram frequency models over subtrees, and (2) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions and a softmax policy over grammar rules. Equivalence between a candidate and the target is tested via evaluation over multiple random instances in a finite field Zp; for complex targets, linear combinations of trees are found by solving a linear system modulo p.

Experiments consider five expression families, including sums of matrix polynomials, elementwise products, elementary symmetric polynomials, and sums over binary vectors (motivated by RBM partition functions). The learned strategies substantially outperform random search as degree k increases; n-gram models often outperform the RNN. The authors report discovery of numerous identities, some resulting in significant complexity reductions (e.g., O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)) and exponential to polynomial-time cases), with most identities provided in the supplement. The work connects to theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic algebra systems, but differs by using learned search policies rather than hand-designed heuristics, and by learning continuous representations for symbolic expressions.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation within a well-scoped grammar, with an explicit complexity attribute per rule.
- Practical, scalable equivalence testing via evaluation in Zp, and a linear-combination solver modulo p to combine trees.
- Learning-based search substantially enlarges the tractable search space; solutions for higher-degree instances become discoverable within fixed time budgets.
- RNN-based continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions are novel in this domain and achieve high classification accuracy on equivalence classes.
- Open-source code and explicit experimental protocol (time budget, repeated runs) support reproducibility.
- The identities discovered can yield significant reductions in computational complexity and may be relevant to ML computations (e.g., RBM-like sums, reductions akin to strength reduction).

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Equivalence testing: The paper relies on random evaluation in Zp and states that a large N “uniquely characterizes” an expression. This is a probabilistic identity test (akin to Schwartz–Zippel) and can produce false positives/negatives. The paper does not provide bounds on failure probability, nor uses multiple independent primes to drive this probability down. Using multiple primes and reporting aggregate failure probabilities would strengthen soundness. Similarly, validating final identities symbolically or with additional verification would increase confidence.
- Linear combinations modulo p: Solving Fw = t mod p can admit spurious solutions that do not correspond to true integer/rational linear combinations. Again, multiple primes or rational reconstruction techniques would mitigate this risk; this is not discussed.
- Scope restrictions: Experiments primarily handle homogeneous polynomials with a single variable and scalar outputs. While the framework can, in principle, handle multiple variables and other operators, most results and guarantees are demonstrated under a restricted setting. Claims about broader applicability would benefit from additional experiments with multivariate targets and richer grammars (e.g., trigonometric or recursive constructs) and a termination strategy.
- Empirical reporting: Many identities and their exact complexity reductions are deferred to the supplement. The main text would benefit from presenting several representative identities in full, including precise asymptotic cost derivations and, where relevant, lower bounds or references to known optimality.
- Baselines: Comparisons are limited to random search and learned strategies within the same grammar. There is no comparison to rule-based simplification/optimization (e.g., Maple/Mathematica with a cost metric, compiler algebraic strength reduction), or to other search methods (e.g., beam search, MCTS, A* with heuristics). The random baseline is weak; stronger non-learned heuristic baselines would make the case more compelling.
- RNN vs n-gram: The n-gram approach often outperforms the RNN on the reported families, suggesting that the learned continuous embedding does not yet translate into superior search guidance for this task. Analysis of failure modes for the RNN policy and ablations (e.g., beam size, curriculum details) would clarify where continuous representations help.
- Complexity accounting: The mapping from grammar rule counts to asymptotic complexity abstracts away constant factors and cache effects. For claims like “exponential to polynomial,” it would be helpful to include end-to-end cost models or empirical runtime measurements on large synthetic inputs to validate practical speedups.

Quality
Overall technically sound as a proof of concept, with caveats about probabilistic equivalence testing and modulo-p linear solving. Methodological choices are reasonable; adding formal probability bounds and multi-prime verification would improve rigor. The search/scheduling method is simple (greedy), and the learning components are clearly described.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The grammar, scheduler, and strategies are explained with illustrative examples. Important implementation details for the RNN are provided. The paper would benefit from: more identities in the main text; formal statements/bounds for the descriptor method; clearer specification of the grammar (full rule list and complexity attributes) in the main paper; and a more detailed runtime/compute budget analysis.

Originality
Good. Learning to guide symbolic identity discovery within an attribute-grammar framework is novel in ML venues. The use of RNNs to embed symbolic expressions and to drive search decisions is also novel in this context. Prior uses of grammars for optimization are acknowledged; the learning-guided search and continuous embeddings contribute new ideas.

Significance
Moderate. The approach opens a path for applying ML to symbolic discovery and can produce nontrivial complexity reductions. However, current scope limitations and dependence on probabilistic verification constrain immediate practical impact. With stronger verification, broader grammars, and comparisons to established optimizers, significance could be high.

Pros
- Innovative application of ML to symbolic identity discovery.
- Clear framework with demonstrated scaling beyond brute-force/random search.
- High-accuracy learned embeddings for expressions; reproducible codebase.
- Nontrivial discovered identities with substantial complexity reduction.

Cons
- Probabilistic equivalence testing and modulo-p solving lack formal failure bounds and multi-prime safeguards.
- Limited scope (mostly single-variable homogeneous polynomials) and reliance on removed rules to enforce efficiency.
- Main results depend on supplemental identities; limited comparison to stronger baselines; n-gram often beats RNN.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide formal probability bounds for the descriptor approach; use multiple primes and report empirical false positive rates; symbolically verify a subset of identities.
- Include representative identities with full derivations and complexity analyses in the main paper.
- Compare against stronger baselines (e.g., Maple/Mathematica with cost-aware simplification; heuristic-guided enumeration; beam/MCTS).
- Explore broader grammars and multivariate targets; add a termination policy for recursive rules.
- Analyze why n-grams outperform the RNN and whether beam search or alternative architectures improve the learned policy.

Overall assessment
A solid and original contribution showing that learned search can discover efficient algebraic identities beyond brute force, with clear potential but in a restricted setting and with verification methodology that needs stronger guarantees.
