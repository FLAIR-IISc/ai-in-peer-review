Summary
The paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient algebraic identities equivalent to a given target expression. Expressions are generated via an attribute grammar over linear algebraic and elementwise operators; equality is tested numerically using descriptors produced by evaluating expressions on random inputs over Zp. For complex targets, a linear combination of multiple grammar trees is solved via a modular linear system. To make the combinatorial search tractable, the authors train search strategies from solutions to simpler targets: (i) n-gram models over subtrees; and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees and a softmax policy for next-rule selection. Experiments on several families of expressions report that learned strategies find efficient forms at higher degrees than random search, with n-grams often outperforming the RNN. The paper claims discovery of previously unknown identities that reduce complexity from O(n3) to O(n2) and from exponential to polynomial time in some cases.

Strengths
- Interesting and timely problem: learning to discover symbolic identities with computational advantages is compelling and relevant to ML systems and compilers.
- Clear high-level framework combining grammars, randomized equivalence checking, and learned search policies.
- Use of RNNs to embed algebraic trees is novel in this context and potentially useful beyond this paper.
- Experimental protocol of bootstrapping from lower-degree solutions is sensible; reporting fraction of runs that succeed within a time budget provides some reproducibility signal.
- Open-source code is promised.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Correctness and soundness
- Equivalence via random evaluation in Zp: The claim that the descriptor “uniquely characterizes” an expression is incorrect in general. No collision bounds are provided. Use of Schwartz-Zippel or similar would allow an explicit failure probability; using multiple primes would further reduce risk. As written, both false positives and false negatives are possible, especially when solving linear systems modulo p.
- Linear combinations of trees: Solving Fw = t mod p may produce coefficients w in Zp. How are these interpreted over reals? Are negative/integer coefficients allowed in the grammar? The grammar appears to lack scalar constants; if coefficients are not constrained to {0,1,−1}, it is unclear that the resulting linear combination corresponds to a valid expression in the original grammar. This is a major gap.
- Validation of discovered identities: There is no formal proof or rational-field verification presented. Relying solely on modular testing is insufficient. At minimum, identities should be verified over multiple large primes or symbolically with a CAS.

Methodological clarity and completeness
- Grammar specification: Table 1 is referenced but not fully described in the main text. Types, dimensions, and attribute constraints (e.g., ensuring scalar outputs and correct shapes) should be spelled out. The handling of constants, scalar multiplications, and subtraction is unclear.
- Scheduler and strategy: The scheduler greedily picks the max-scoring rule. No beam search, sampling temperature, or exploration mechanism is discussed. This likely induces local optima; an ablation comparing greedy vs beam would be informative.
- RNN representation: The dataset of equivalence classes is created using the same potentially fallible numerical equality test, introducing label noise. Splitting each class 80/20 means test expressions are from classes seen in training; report generalization to unseen classes would be more compelling. Key training details are missing (optimizer, batch size, epochs, learning rates beyond “×100 in classifier”, early stopping).
- Dropout “toward the top of the tree” is described imprecisely. Which layers, what schedule, and how is it applied to variable-depth trees?
- Initialization “to identity” is underspecified for third-order tensors; what does “identity” mean for W3?
- The n-gram scoring uses a 10^k weighting by depth with no justification or smoothing; this seems numerically extreme and arbitrary.

Complexity claims and examples
- Example 1 complexity is misstated. Given A ∈ R^{n×m}, B ∈ R^{m×p}, the proposed computation costs O(nm) to compute sum(A,1) plus O(mp) for (1×m)·(m×p), for total O(nm + mp). The paper claims O(n(m+p)), which is dimensionally inconsistent (p cannot be multiplied by n in the second term).
- For the families where the matrix-matrix multiply rule is removed “to ensure O(n^2)”, this is an ad hoc restriction that may preclude valid solutions and biases the search. It should be framed as a constrained problem variant, with explicit implications for completeness.
- Claims of exponential-to-polynomial reductions are not supported in the main text (details deferred to the supplement). At least one explicit nontrivial identity should be shown and verified.

Evaluation and baselines
- Success metric is “fraction of runs that find a solution in 10 minutes.” Missing are: time-to-discovery distributions, number of trees explored, beam width, policy entropy, and solution complexity/size. Without these, it is difficult to assess efficiency improvements due to learning vs chance.
- The RNN underperforms n-grams in several cases; analysis of failure modes is lacking.
- No comparisons to symbolic systems (Mathematica/Maple) on the same families, even to confirm they do not find similar simplifications automatically. Also missing are comparisons to alternative search (e.g., beam search without learning, MCTS).
- The RBM-2 family results are inconclusive; the paper reports no solutions for k>5 and only speedups for k=5, undermining stronger claims in the abstract/intro.

Presentation and formatting
- Numerous typos and inconsistencies, e.g., “help which the discovery”, inconsistent spacing “AA T”, “O(()n3)”, inconsistent use of k vs k∗, inconsistent MATLAB dimension conventions, “mod Zp” usage. Reference list has many incomplete entries (missing years/pages, arXiv-only entries without proper formatting).
- Several figures/tables critical for understanding are not present in the main text (in the submission excerpt). Ensure all required materials are included and legible.

Questions for the authors
1. How do you ensure that linear-combination coefficients correspond to allowable constants in the grammar? Are coefficients restricted to {0,1,−1}? If not, how are larger integers or rationals represented?
2. What collision probability guarantees do you provide for descriptor-based equality? Do you verify identities across multiple primes?
3. Can you include at least one complete, human-readable derivation of an exponential-to-polynomial identity and a proof of correctness?
4. What is the beam size or exploration mechanism used by the scheduler? Have you tried non-greedy decoding?
5. How do you define “identity initialization” for third-order tensors in the RNN?

Pros
- Novel and interesting problem formulation.
- Creative use of learned policies to guide combinatorial search.
- Potentially impactful if correctness and evaluation are strengthened.

Cons
- Lack of rigorous correctness guarantees; possible invalid identities due to modular testing and unconstrained linear combinations.
- Methodological underspecification and weak baselines.
- Several technical inaccuracies and many minor clarity/formatting issues.

Recommendation
Reject. While the idea is promising and the direction exciting, the accumulation of methodological gaps, unclear correctness guarantees, and presentation issues undermines confidence. I encourage a revision that (i) provides rigorous equivalence guarantees, (ii) clarifies the grammar and linear-combination mechanism, (iii) strengthens evaluation with proofs and stronger baselines, and (iv) fixes numerous clarity and formatting issues.

Confidence
High.
