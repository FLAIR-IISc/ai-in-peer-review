

# Peer Review: "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors address the problem of finding an expression S that computes the same result as a target expression T, but with lower computational complexity (e.g., from O(n³) to O(n²)). The key innovation lies in applying machine learning to guide the search within an attribute grammar framework, which represents symbolic expressions as sequences of grammar rules.

The paper makes a creative connection between NLP techniques (n-gram models, recursive neural networks) and symbolic mathematics. The authors demonstrate their approach on several families of expressions (including homogeneous polynomials and symmetric polynomials), showing they can discover identities that reduce computational complexity significantly. For instance, they find a way to compute ∑∑(A*B)^6 in O(nm) time rather than the naive O(n³) time. The paper differs from previous work in theorem proving and symbolic computation by using learning to guide the search rather than relying on hand-designed heuristics.

## Strengths

1. **Novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation**: The paper makes a compelling connection between NLP techniques (n-gram models, RNNs) and mathematical identity discovery. This is a fresh perspective that bridges two seemingly disparate fields.

2. **Demonstrated effectiveness**: The authors provide concrete examples where their approach discovers identities that reduce computational complexity from exponential or O(n³) to O(n²) or better, which are too complex to find manually or through brute-force search. The examples (Examples 1 and 2) are particularly well-chosen and clearly illustrate the approach.

3. **Technical soundness**: The experimental design is strong, with controlled comparisons between random search, n-gram models, and RNN-based strategies. The paper carefully evaluates both strengths and weaknesses (e.g., noting that the RNN approach struggles with highly complex patterns).

4. **Clear problem formulation**: The paper precisely defines the problem of finding efficient mathematical identities within the restricted setting of homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, which makes the problem tractable while still being meaningful.

5. **Practical significance**: The discovered identities could have direct applications in machine learning (e.g., for efficient computation of dropout or RBM partition functions), making the work relevant to practitioners.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope**: The paper restricts itself to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with a single matrix or vector variable. While the authors acknowledge this limitation in Section 8, they don't sufficiently explore how to extend the framework to more general cases (e.g., involving trigonometric functions or recursion). This significantly limits the broader applicability of the approach.

2. **Lack of theoretical analysis**: The paper provides strong empirical results but lacks theoretical analysis of why the learning approaches work or how they scale with problem complexity. A theoretical foundation would strengthen the contribution.

3. **Training data limitations**: The approach relies on training data from simpler expressions, but the paper doesn't thoroughly analyze how the quality and diversity of training data affect performance on more complex expressions. This is particularly important since the authors note that the RNN approach doesn't work as well for the most complex families.

4. **Comparison to related work**: While the paper cites relevant work, it could better differentiate itself from existing symbolic computation systems (like Maple or Mathematica) and other approaches to program induction. The authors acknowledge that symbolic computation engines don't use learning approaches, but could provide a more detailed comparison.

5. **Reproducibility concerns**: The paper mentions code and data are available on GitHub, but doesn't provide sufficient details about the implementation (e.g., specific hyperparameters, computational resources) to ensure reproducibility.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments that support their claims. The authors carefully evaluate both strengths and weaknesses of their approach, acknowledging limitations like the restricted scope of their framework and the challenges with the most complex expression families. The experimental results are clearly presented and demonstrate significant improvements over random search for many cases. However, the lack of theoretical analysis is a notable gap in the technical contribution.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and well-organized. The problem statement is clear, and the methods are explained in sufficient detail. The examples (Examples 1 and 2) are particularly helpful for understanding the approach. The figures (Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Fig. 3) effectively illustrate key concepts. The paper could be improved by adding more details about the experimental setup and implementation to aid reproducibility, particularly regarding the integer-based descriptor and the training of the RNN.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant contribution by applying machine learning techniques to guide the search for mathematical identities within a grammar-based framework. While attribute grammars have been used in other domains (e.g., compiler construction, bioinformatics), their application to mathematical identity discovery is novel. The use of RNNs for learning continuous representations of symbolic expressions is also innovative. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work in theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation, and the approach is distinct from the recent work of Bridge et al. [4] who use learning to select between heuristics rather than learning the heuristics themselves.

## Significance

The results are significant because they demonstrate a new way to discover mathematical identities that reduce computational complexity. These identities could have practical applications in machine learning, as the authors note (e.g., for efficient computation of dropout or RBM partition functions). The approach also opens up new possibilities for using machine learning to solve symbolic problems, which could inspire further research in this area. The paper advances the state of the art by demonstrating that learning can be effectively applied to guide the search for mathematical identities in a domain where brute-force search is intractable.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field with a novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation. The results are impressive and well-supported. The limitations are acknowledged, and the paper provides a solid foundation for future work. The main areas for improvement are expanding the discussion of the limitations of the current framework and providing more details about the implementation to aid reproducibility.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In Section 8 (Discussion), expand on how to extend the framework to more general cases (e.g., involving trigonometric functions or recursion) and address the challenges in doing so.

2. Add a brief theoretical analysis of why the learning approaches work, at least for the simpler cases.

3. Provide more details about the implementation, including specific hyperparameters for the RNN (e.g., the exact learning rate, dropout rate, etc.) and computational resources used.

4. Consider adding a more detailed comparison to symbolic computation systems like Maple or Mathematica, emphasizing how the learning-based approach differs from their rule-based approaches.

5. In the experiments, provide more information about how the training data was generated for the RNN (e.g., how many examples were used for each degree k).

## Final Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field by applying machine learning techniques to a novel problem domain. The approach is innovative, the experiments are well-designed, and the results are impressive. While the scope is somewhat limited, the paper provides a strong foundation for future work in this area. The paper is well-written and clearly presents a new and valuable approach to mathematical identity discovery.

**Overall Recommendation: Accept**
