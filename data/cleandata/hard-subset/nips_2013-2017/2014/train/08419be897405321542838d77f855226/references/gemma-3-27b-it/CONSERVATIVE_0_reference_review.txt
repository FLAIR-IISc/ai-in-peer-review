This paper proposes a framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars and machine learning. The core idea – leveraging learned search strategies within a grammar-defined space of expressions – is interesting, but the paper suffers from a lack of rigorous evaluation and overstates its contributions. While the reported results are promising for specific cases, the overall approach feels exploratory rather than a fully-fledged, thoroughly validated system.

The technical soundness is questionable in several aspects. The reliance on a numerical representation with modulo arithmetic to compare expressions is a potential source of instability and may not reliably identify true identities, especially for more complex expressions. The justification for choosing specific RNN architectures and hyperparameters feels somewhat arbitrary. The claim of discovering “many new mathematical identities” requires much stronger evidence; the examples presented (k=2, k=6 in Example 2) are illustrative but lack broad generalization. The performance improvements cited (O(n3) to O(n2)) are beneficial but should be contrasted with existing symbolic computation tools like Mathematica or Maple, which were dismissed too lightly. Were these identities genuinely *unknown* to these systems, or simply not readily discovered using their existing algorithms? This is a critical distinction.

Clarity is decent, although the notation can be dense. The explanation of the attribute grammar is adequate, but the connection between the grammar rules, the search strategy, and the learning components isn't always immediately clear. More detailed explanations of the training process for the n-gram and RNN models are needed – what datasets were used, what were the training parameters, and what validation procedures were employed? The presentation of results is fragmented; the tables and figures are helpful, but the narrative lacks a cohesive assessment of the overall system’s performance across the different expression families.

The originality lies in the combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for this specific problem. However, the use of n-grams and RNNs is fairly standard, and their application here doesn't represent a significant methodological breakthrough. The related work section acknowledges relevant areas, but the discussion lacks depth. For example, the connection to program synthesis and automated reasoning is mentioned but not explored in detail. The claim of a “novel application” of RNNs to symbolic structures feels overstated; similar approaches have been used in NLP for parsing and semantic analysis.

The significance of the work is uncertain. While the potential to automate the discovery of mathematical identities is appealing, the current system’s limited scope and performance raise doubts about its practical impact. The restriction to homogeneous polynomials and single-variable matrices significantly reduces the applicability of the framework. The fact that the n-gram model often outperforms the more sophisticated RNN suggests that the learned representations aren’t capturing sufficiently complex patterns. The success is limited to a few specific expression families; the inability to find improvements for the (RBM-2)k family is particularly concerning.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The idea of combining grammars and learning for symbolic manipulation is novel and potentially promising.
*   The reported results show some ability to discover efficient identities for specific expression families.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The technical soundness is questionable, with potential issues related to numerical stability and model justification.
*   The claims of novelty and significance are overstated.
*   The evaluation is insufficient; a more thorough comparison with existing symbolic computation tools is needed.
*   The scope of the framework is limited, and its performance is uneven across different expression families.
*   The paper lacks a compelling demonstration that the discovered identities are genuinely new or previously inaccessible.

**Overall Assessment:**

Reject. While the paper presents an interesting idea, it falls short of the standards for NIPS publication. The methodological weaknesses, lack of rigorous evaluation, and overstated claims outweigh the potential novelty of the approach. The authors need to address the concerns raised above, provide more convincing evidence of the system’s capabilities, and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing techniques before this work could be considered for publication. A significantly revised version with a stronger empirical evaluation and more careful analysis would be required.
