Summary
The paper tackles the problem of discovering computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target expression. It formalizes a search space using an attribute grammar over matrix/vector operations with associated cost attributes, and searches for alternative expressions of lower asymptotic complexity. Because exhaustive enumeration is infeasible for higher-degree polynomials, the authors learn search strategies from solutions to simpler instances. Two families of learned strategies are considered: n-gram models over subtrees and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of symbolic expressions and, on top, a softmax policy that predicts the next rule to apply. Equivalence between expressions is checked numerically by evaluating over many random inputs in a finite field Zp; for complex targets, linear combinations of trees are allowed and solved via modular Gaussian elimination. The method discovers identities that reduce O(n^3) expressions to O(n^2), and polynomial-time surrogates for some exponentially large sums (e.g., RBM-related series) for small k. Code and data are released.

Relation to prior work
The problem overlaps with theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, but the paper focuses on fixed-degree homogeneous polynomials, avoiding halting/undecidability. Unlike CAS simplifiers (Maple/Mathematica), the goal is explicit complexity reduction, and learning is central. The attribute grammar angle has precedents in optimization and music composition; the RNN embedding is inspired by NLP tree models and embedding literature. The idea of learning heuristics from solved instances relates to prior learned guidance for theorem proving, but here heuristics are learned without hand-crafted rule scoring.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria

Quality
- Soundness: The framework for exploring grammar trees and the modular numerical check is sensible. However, the paper lacks formal guarantees for the descriptor-based equality check. A Schwartz–Zippel-style bound for polynomials over finite fields would clarify the probability of false matches (and how N and p control it). Similarly, the correctness of lifting a modular linear solution back to an actual symbolic sum with integer coefficients should be discussed (e.g., integrality, coefficient magnitudes, and how to reconstruct a valid expression tree).
- Experiments: The search experiments are well-motivated and show that learned strategies (especially 3–5-grams) dramatically increase the rate of discovery versus random. The RNN representation achieves low classification error on equivalence classes, though its downstream search performance is mixed—n-grams often outperform it except on one harder family where it finds k=5 faster than random. The time budget (10 minutes per run) and multiple random restarts are reasonable, but more detailed statistics (time to first solution, variance, number of trees explored) would strengthen claims.
- Missing baselines: There is no comparison against CAS simplification under a cost model, nor against hand-designed heuristics or MCTS-style guided search. For the identities discovered, providing symbolic verification (beyond numerical descriptors) for the final results would increase confidence.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with concrete examples (e.g., sum(sum(AB))). The RNN training details (identity init, curriculum, ReLU, dropout) are useful.
- Some necessary details are missing or deferred: the exact grammar (Table 1), RNN classification accuracies (Table 2), search space sizes (Table 3), and the identities themselves are in the supplement; at least a subset should be in the main text for self-containment.
- The linear-combination construction (Section 5) would benefit from a clear mapping between a solution vector w and a valid symbolic expression using grammar rules (sum and scalar-multiply). How are scalar coefficients represented? Are coefficients constrained (e.g., small integers)?

Originality
- Applying learned search over an attribute grammar to discover lower-cost symbolic identities is novel and timely. The learned continuous embedding of algebraic expressions via an RNN trained on equivalence classes is a nice contribution that could be reused in other symbolic reasoning tasks.
- The use of finite-field numerical descriptors for equality and modular linear solving in this setting is a pragmatic twist, although related ideas exist in randomized polynomial identity testing.

Significance
- If matured, the approach could influence automatic code optimization, compilation, and symbolic/numeric hybrid systems. The discovered identities (especially for repetitive polynomial families) illustrate potential.
- However, current scope is limited: a fairly small grammar, reliance on training from lower k, and difficulties on the most challenging family (RBM-2) beyond k=5. The strongest gains are in families with regular, repetitive solutions where n-grams excel. Demonstrating impact on real ML workloads or compiler-in-the-loop settings would enhance significance.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated problem with potential practical impact.
- Novel combination of attribute grammars, learned guidance (n-grams, RNN), and modular randomized equality checks.
- Release of code/data; reproducibility seems feasible.
- RNN embedding of expressions achieves good classification accuracy; interesting beyond this application.
- Substantial improvement in search effectiveness over random; several non-trivial identities found.

Cons
- Lack of formal probability/error bounds for the equality check and linear-combination correctness.
- Limited experimental baselines and ablations; RNN often underperforms n-grams for search.
- Expressivity restricted by the grammar; some “discoveries” (e.g., Example 1) are trivial and known.
- Claims of novelty of identities are not rigorously substantiated; symbolic proofs absent in the main text.
- Evaluation metric (fraction of successful runs within 10 minutes) could be supplemented with stronger statistics.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theoretical bounds (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel) on collision probability in Zp and guidance for choosing N and p.
- Describe explicitly how to reconstruct a valid symbolic expression (with integer coefficients) from linear solutions; discuss coefficient sizes and any normalization.
- Add baselines: CAS simplification under a cost model; heuristic search (e.g., MCTS or A* with admissible heuristics); symbolic equality checking for final solutions.
- Include a subset of the discovered identities in the main paper with human-readable justification.
- Analyze why n-grams outperform the RNN and explore stronger models (tree-LSTMs, graph neural networks, policy/value training, Monte Carlo Tree Search).
- Broaden families or demonstrate integration into an automatic code optimization task with measurable end-to-end speedups.

Recommendation: Weak Accept. The paper opens an interesting path at the ML–symbolic computation interface with credible empirical evidence and a reusable embedding component, despite limitations in theory and evaluation.

Confidence: Medium.
