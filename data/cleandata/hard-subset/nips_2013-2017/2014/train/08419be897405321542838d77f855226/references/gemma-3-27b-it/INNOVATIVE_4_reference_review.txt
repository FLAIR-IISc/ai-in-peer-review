This paper presents a fascinating and ambitious attempt to apply machine learning to the discovery of mathematical identities, specifically focusing on finding computationally efficient expressions. The core idea – framing identity discovery as a search problem within an attribute grammar and using learned strategies to guide that search – is highly novel and represents a significant departure from traditional approaches. While the methodology isn’t flawless and the validation is somewhat limited, the potential impact is substantial, and I believe the paper warrants acceptance despite some weaknesses.

The novelty is the strongest aspect of this work. The combination of attribute grammars with machine learning for symbolic manipulation is, to my knowledge, a genuinely new approach. Existing systems like Mathematica excel at simplification based on pre-defined rules, but lack the ability to *discover* fundamentally more efficient representations. The demonstration of finding O(n^2) computations for expressions naively requiring O(n^3) or even exponential time is compelling. The application of RNNs to learn representations of mathematical structures is also a valuable contribution, opening the door to applying a wider range of ML techniques to symbolic domains.

However, the paper isn't without its drawbacks. The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating success, feels somewhat limited in scope. The chosen families of expressions (while well-defined) aren’t necessarily the most representative of real-world mathematical challenges.  The performance comparisons between the different search strategies (random, n-gram, RNN) aren’t always conclusive, and the superior performance of n-grams over RNNs is somewhat surprising and not fully explained. The RNN’s performance is intriguing, particularly in accelerating the search for the k=5 case, but more detailed analysis of *why* it works (or doesn't work better) would be beneficial. The ablation study related to the RNN dropout rate is a good start, but more analysis is needed. 

The reliance on a fixed cut-off time of 10 minutes limits the assessment of scalability. While understandable, it prevents a comprehensive comparison of the approaches on truly intractable problems. The paper also acknowledges several restrictions (single matrix, homogeneous polynomials) that limit the generality of the framework. While these are justified by the need to constrain the search space, a more thorough discussion of how these limitations might be addressed in future work would strengthen the paper. The numerical representation using modular arithmetic is a clever workaround for floating-point issues, but the choice of the prime 'p' and its implications on the representation accuracy aren't fully discussed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **High Novelty:** The core idea is genuinely innovative and hasn't been explored before.
* **Significant Potential Impact:**  The ability to automatically discover more efficient mathematical identities could have broad implications for various fields.
* **Compelling Demonstrations:** The examples of finding faster computations are convincing, even if limited in number.
* **Interesting Application of RNNs:** The use of RNNs for learning representations of symbolic structures is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The choice of expression families feels restrictive.
* **Inconclusive Strategy Comparison:** The performance differences between search strategies aren't always clear-cut.
* **Scalability Concerns:**  The fixed cut-off time limits the evaluation on truly challenging problems.
* **Methodological imperfections**: The choice of prime number for the modular representation is not well discussed.



**Overall:**

Despite its limitations, the originality and potential impact of this work are compelling. The authors have tackled a difficult problem with a creative and promising approach. I believe the benefits of showcasing this novel research direction outweigh the drawbacks. The paper demonstrates a significant shift in how we might approach symbolic computation, and I recommend acceptance. Future work building on this foundation will likely be of great interest to the NIPS community. I encourage the authors to address the points raised in this review in a revision, particularly focusing on expanding the experimental evaluation and providing a more detailed analysis of the RNN’s performance.
