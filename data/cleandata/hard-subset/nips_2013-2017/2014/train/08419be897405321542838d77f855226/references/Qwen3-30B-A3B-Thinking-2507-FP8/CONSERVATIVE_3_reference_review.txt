

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars combined with machine learning techniques. The authors frame the problem as finding an alternate expression S(V) = T(V) with lower computational complexity than a target expression T(V), where T is a homogeneous polynomial of fixed degree k.

The paper's main contributions include a grammar framework for symbolic expressions, integration of n-gram models and RNNs for guiding the search strategy, and demonstration of the framework discovering identities that reduce computational complexity (e.g., from O(n³) to O(n²)). The authors claim these identities are previously undiscovered and too complex for manual discovery or brute-force search.

**Strengths:**
The problem is well-defined within a restricted but mathematically interesting domain. The technical approach is methodologically sound, with clear presentation of the attribute grammar framework and the numerical representation of symbolic expressions. The experimental results (Table 2) show the RNN-based representation achieves reasonable classification accuracy (≤5% error) for symbolic expressions, which is a useful contribution to the field of symbolic computation. The paper demonstrates how machine learning can guide search in combinatorially large spaces, which is a valuable insight.

**Weaknesses:**
The most significant limitation is the highly restrictive problem formulation. The authors consider only homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree k with a single matrix or vector variable, which severely limits the applicability of their approach. The paper acknowledges these restrictions in Section 8 but fails to convincingly argue why these specific families of expressions are meaningful in broader mathematical or machine learning contexts. The claimed "previously undiscovered" identities (supplementary material) appear to be narrow mathematical curiosities with no clear connection to practical problems.

The experimental evaluation is insufficient. The authors compare their method only against a random search strategy, which is a weak baseline for a problem of this nature. There is no comparison against established symbolic computation tools like Maple or Mathematica, which could potentially solve similar problems without learning components. The paper's claim that their approach "dramatically widens the complexity and scope" is not supported by evidence—Figure 3 shows only marginal improvements over random search for some cases, and the RNN performs worse than n-gram models for most families.

The paper's "novel application" of RNNs to symbolic expressions is not particularly novel. The authors cite Socher et al.'s work on recursive neural networks for sentiment analysis and Bowman's work on learning logical predicates, but fail to clearly differentiate their approach. The representation learning approach is essentially a reapplication of NLP techniques to a new domain without demonstrating significant improvements over simpler methods like n-grams.

Most critically, the paper fails to establish the significance of its contribution. The discovered identities (e.g., for the (RBM-1)^k family) are claimed to have potential applications in machine learning (dropout approximation, RBM partition functions), but the paper provides no evidence that these identities would actually be useful in practice. The computational complexity improvements are theoretically interesting but lack demonstration of practical impact.

The framework's reliance on learning from simpler expressions creates a circular dependency: the method can only discover identities more complex than those already solved by simpler instances of the method. This severely limits the scalability of the approach. The paper's claim that the method "naturally lends itself to integration with compilers" is unsubstantiated and speculative.

**Conclusion:**
While the paper presents a technically sound approach to a well-defined problem within a restricted domain, it fails to demonstrate the significance of its contribution to the broader field of machine learning or symbolic computation. The problem is too narrow, the experimental evaluation is insufficient, and the claimed advantages over simpler methods are marginal at best. The paper's approach essentially applies established NLP techniques to a symbolic domain without providing convincing evidence that these techniques offer substantial advantages over more straightforward approaches.

The work is methodologically sound but lacks sufficient novelty or significance to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The authors would need to substantially broaden the scope of their problem, provide more convincing evidence of practical utility, and conduct more rigorous comparisons against established approaches to strengthen their contribution.

**Recommendation: Reject**
