Summary
The paper tackles the discovery of computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given symbolic target expression. It defines an attribute grammar over linear/matrix operations with complexity annotations and searches the space of grammar trees for expressions that match the target but have lower cost. Because exhaustive search is intractable, the authors learn search strategies from solutions of simpler targets: (a) n-gram statistics over subtrees, and (b) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions and predicts the next grammar rule to apply. Equivalence checking is done numerically using descriptors produced by evaluating expressions on random integer inputs modulo a large prime, and linear combinations of trees are found by solving Fw = t mod p. Experiments on five families (including sums over powers of AAT, elementwise variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related exponential sums) show that learning dramatically improves the chance of finding efficient formulas, sometimes reducing naive O(n3) or exponential costs to O(n2) or O(nm). An RNN classifies expression equivalence classes with ≤5% error up to degree 6 and provides a representation for guided search, though n-grams often outperform it in identity discovery.

Relation to prior work
The problem bridges theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, but is constrained to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree and scalar outputs. The use of attribute grammars for optimization echoes earlier AG-based search/optimization work; applying them to mathematical identity discovery appears novel. The RNN over expression trees parallels work in NLP on tree-structured models (Socher et al., Bowman; word embeddings). Compared to CAS systems (Maple/Mathematica), which simplify but don’t target computational cost, the learning-guided search for low-complexity equivalents is a differentiator. That said, comparisons to GP/evolutionary program induction or modern search (MCTS) are absent.

Strengths
- Novel and interesting problem formulation: learning to discover cost-reducing identities within a grammar.
- Methodological blend of grammars, numerical equivalence checking modulo primes, linear combination solving, and learned search strategies.
- Practical evidence that learning enables identities unreachable by random/brute-force under time constraints; code/data promised.
- Clear demonstration that local structural regularities can be learned (n-grams) and that a neural embedding achieves low error in equivalence classification.
- Potentially impactful idea for compiler-level algebraic optimization and for bridging ML and symbolic computation.

Weaknesses
- Correctness guarantees are weak: equivalence is validated via random testing mod p; no formal proof, no multi-prime reconstruction, and no CAS verification. Risk of false positives remains.
- Scope limitations are strong: single variable A, homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs; many operations excluded. Some successes require removing matrix-multiply from the grammar by hand to force O(n2) forms.
- Limited empirical rigor on identity utility: unclear if discovered identities are truly novel or practically important beyond toy families; minimal analysis of complexity optimality.
- Baselines are thin: no comparison to CAS with custom rule sets, GP, MCTS/beam search, or constraint solvers; scheduler is greedy with little ablation (e.g., beam size, breadth-first vs best-first).
- The RNN, a key novelty, underperforms simple n-grams in the main discovery task; analysis of when/why the RNN helps is limited.
- Cost model is informal; complexity counts ignore constants/memory and assume simple n-based asymptotics. Dimensional assumptions and operator costs could be specified more precisely.

Quality
Technically sound overall, but several components would benefit from stronger theoretical and empirical validation:
- Equivalence checking should use Schwartz–Zippel bounds, multiple primes, and rational reconstruction to produce integer identities provably valid over ℚ/ℝ, or CAS verification.
- The linear system over Zp for coefficients needs justification that recovered integer coefficients correspond to true real identities (and how negatives/large magnitudes are handled).
- The scheduler and training protocols are described, but ablations on search hyperparameters and time-to-solution distributions are limited.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. The grammar, representation, and scheduler are explained clearly, with motivating examples. However:
- Precisely list the grammar rules and their complexity model in the main text (not only in a table).
- Clarify the coefficient domain for linear combinations and how mod p solutions map to integer/rational coefficients.
- Expand on dataset construction for the RNN, avoiding possible leakage, and clarify the training/test split across equivalence classes.

Originality
High on problem framing and combination of tools; applying RNNs to expression trees for identity discovery is novel. Using n-grams in this domain is simple but effective. Prior related areas are cited; comparisons to GP and CAS-guided optimization could be deeper.

Significance
Moderate. The approach is promising and could catalyze ML-for-symbolic advances. Demonstrated wins on some families are compelling, but broader impact hinges on stronger guarantees, scalability, and applicability beyond narrowly defined grammars. As presented, it is an intriguing step rather than a definitive advance.

Reproducibility
Appears reasonable with released code/data; key training details for the RNN are provided. Still, precise search hyperparameters, seeds, and full lists of discovered identities in the main text would help.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, exciting direction connecting ML and symbolic identity discovery.
- Clear empirical signal that learning improves search in combinatorial spaces.
- Useful technical ideas (descriptor mod p, curriculum training, tree RNN embedding) and public code.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient correctness guarantees and verification of identities.
- Narrow scope and hand-tailored grammar constraints for some wins.
- Sparse baselines and limited analysis of when the RNN helps vs n-grams.
- Unclear practical significance and novelty of the reported identities.

Questions for the authors
- How do you ensure identities hold over ℚ/ℝ? Have you verified with multiple primes and exact reconstruction or CAS proofs?
- What coefficients emerge in linear combinations (integers/rationals)? Any sparsity/regularization to avoid spurious mod p fits?
- Can you provide ablations with beam search, MCTS, or GP baselines?
- Any evidence of utility in real compiler/ML pipelines (e.g., speeding up real workloads)?

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents a compelling idea and promising initial results, but lacks strong correctness guarantees, broader baselines, and evidence of practical impact. Strengthening verification, expanding baselines, and demonstrating utility beyond the current scope would make this a strong NeurIPS contribution.
