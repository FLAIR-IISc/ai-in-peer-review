

# Peer Review: "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The authors propose a grammar-based approach where symbolic expressions are represented as trees of grammar rules, with attribute grammars defining the computational complexity of each operation. The key innovation is integrating machine learning techniques (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for efficient expression versions, training on solutions to simpler expressions to tackle more complex targets. The approach successfully discovers new identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n^3) to O(n^2) for several expression families, including matrix polynomial identities and symmetric polynomials. This work bridges symbolic computation with machine learning, applying techniques from NLP (like n-grams and RNNs) to a domain traditionally dominated by rule-based systems.

The paper relates to several areas: attribute grammars (originally for compiler construction), symbolic computation systems (Maple, Mathematica), and machine learning for program induction. Unlike previous symbolic systems that rely on hand-crafted rules, this work learns search strategies from examples, making it distinct from Bridge et al.'s heuristic-based approach and more general than the restricted cases in [7].

## Strengths

**Originality (High):** The integration of machine learning into grammar-based search for mathematical identities is genuinely novel. The paper demonstrates how RNNs can learn continuous representations of symbolic expressions—a significant contribution to making symbolic domains accessible to machine learning techniques. The discovery of new identities (e.g., the pattern for Example 2) that are too complex to find manually is a compelling application.

**Significance (High):** The results have potential practical impact in machine learning, as the discovered identities could optimize computationally expensive operations like dropout or RBM partition function computation. The framework could be integrated into compilers or optimization tools, making it more than just theoretical.

**Quality (High):** The technical approach is sound and well-executed. The numerical representation using integer modulo p elegantly solves the equivalence-checking problem. The RNN implementation details (identity initialization, rectified linear units, curriculum learning) are carefully chosen and well-justified. The experimental results clearly demonstrate the superiority of learning-based strategies over random search for complex expressions.

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-organized with clear examples (Examples 1 and 2) that effectively illustrate the approach. The problem statement, framework, and experiments are logically presented. The supplementary material (referenced but not detailed in the main text) appears to contain the discovered identities, which would be valuable for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Justification (Moderate):** While the experiments show clear empirical success, the paper lacks theoretical analysis of *why* the learning approaches work well. For instance, why do n-gram models outperform RNNs for repetitive patterns? A brief discussion of the underlying principles would strengthen the contribution.

**Scope Limitations (Moderate):** The restricted domain (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix/vector variables) limits the general applicability of the framework. The paper acknowledges this in Section 8, but it would be helpful to more explicitly discuss how the framework could be extended to broader mathematical domains (e.g., with trigonometric functions or recursion).

**Comparison with Related Work (Moderate):** While the related work section is comprehensive, it could better distinguish the paper's approach from recent work in program synthesis (e.g., [18, 28]) and symbolic regression. The paper notes that their work differs from Bridge et al. [4] by learning heuristics automatically, but a more detailed comparison would strengthen the novelty claim.

**Evaluation Metrics (Moderate):** The evaluation focuses on whether an identity is discovered within 10 minutes, but doesn't measure the *quality* of the discovered identities (e.g., whether they're the most efficient possible). The paper states that "any non-zero count is deemed a success," but a more nuanced metric would better demonstrate the approach's value.

## Specific Suggestions

1. **Expand the theoretical discussion** on why n-grams work well for repetitive patterns but fail for more complex structures (like RBM-2). This would help readers understand the limitations and potential of the approach.

2. **Clarify the relationship** between this work and recent advances in program synthesis (e.g., Neural Program Synthesis [18]) and symbolic regression [28]. A brief comparison would better position the contribution.

3. **Include more visualizations** of the grammar trees, especially for the more complex examples. This would help readers better understand the structure of the discovered identities.

4. **Discuss the scalability** of the approach. The paper mentions that the search space grows exponentially, but it doesn't address how the framework would handle even larger expressions or more complex grammars.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong, well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The framework is novel, the experiments are thorough, and the discovered identities are genuinely interesting. While the scope is somewhat restricted, the approach opens up exciting possibilities for applying machine learning to symbolic domains. The paper's practical implications for optimizing machine learning computations make it particularly relevant to the NIPS community.

The main weaknesses are the limited theoretical justification and the restricted domain, but these don't significantly undermine the paper's core contribution. The empirical results are convincing, and the approach is clearly superior to random search for complex expressions.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field with novel technical ideas, strong empirical results, and clear potential applications. It addresses a non-trivial problem in a creative way, and the results are well-supported by experiments. The paper is well-written and clearly presented, with appropriate comparisons to related work. While not all limitations are fully addressed, they don't detract from the paper's overall quality and impact. The work would be of interest to a broad audience at NIPS, particularly those working on machine learning for symbolic computation or optimization.
