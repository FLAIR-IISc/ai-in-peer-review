

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars combined with machine learning techniques. The authors present a grammar-based approach to represent symbolic expressions, with the goal of finding equivalent expressions with lower computational complexity. They introduce three search strategies: random, n-gram, and recursive neural network (RNN) based approaches, trained on solutions for simpler expressions to discover identities for more complex ones.

**Strengths:**
The paper addresses an interesting problem at the intersection of symbolic computation and machine learning. The framework is well-motivated, and the results for specific families of expressions (particularly the first two) demonstrate the approach's potential. The use of integer-based descriptors to avoid numerical issues is a clever technical solution, and the RNN representation for symbolic expressions shows promise for broader applications in symbolic computation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Formatting and Technical Errors:** The paper contains numerous inconsistent mathematical notations. Expressions like "O ( n3 )" should be "O(n³)" with proper superscripts. In Example 2, the expression for k=6 has mismatched parentheses (12 opening vs. 11 closing parentheses), which is a serious technical error that undermines the paper's credibility. The paper claims "Naive evaluation is O(kn³)" for the first family, but for fixed k, this should be O(n³), not O(kn³) - a significant technical error that appears multiple times.

2. **Lack of Methodological Detail:** The paper states "For k = 6, the dataset consists of C = 1687 classes" without explaining how this number was derived or verified. The dimensionality l = 30 for the RNN representation is mentioned as "selected by cross-validation" but without details on the validation procedure or why 30 was chosen over other values. The claim that "the number of solutions can vary" in Section 5 doesn't provide sufficient mathematical justification for the linear system solution.

3. **Inconsistent Terminology:** The paper uses k for both the degree of the polynomial and in the weighting factor "10^k" for the n-gram model, creating significant confusion. The paper also claims "the search space quickly grows with k" but fails to provide Table 3 as referenced, making it impossible to verify this claim.

4. **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The paper claims "the majority of these examples are too complex to be found manually or by exhaustive search" without providing evidence for this claim. The time comparison for k=5 (100±12 vs 438±77 seconds) lacks context - are these total search times, per successful run, or something else? The paper doesn't provide enough detail about the experimental setup to reproduce results.

5. **Underdeveloped Theoretical Justification:** The paper states that using integers modulo p prevents numerical issues, but fails to explain why this would be superior to standard floating-point comparisons with tolerance thresholds. The paper claims the framework "can easily handle multiple variables" but provides no examples or details about this extension.

6. **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims "the discovery of many new mathematical identities" but only provides examples for specific cases (k=2 through k=6 for one family). The supplementary material is referenced but not described, making it impossible to verify the claimed novelty.

**Recommendation:**
This paper has promising ideas but is undermined by numerous technical errors, inconsistent notation, insufficient methodological detail, and overstatements. The formatting issues alone (mismatched parentheses, inconsistent mathematical notation) are unacceptable for a NIPS submission. The technical error regarding the complexity notation (O(kn³) vs O(n³)) is particularly troubling as it suggests a fundamental misunderstanding of computational complexity.

While the problem is interesting and the framework potentially valuable, the paper's current form does not meet NIPS's standards for technical rigor. The authors need to address these issues thoroughly before the paper can be considered for publication. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to correct the technical errors, provide more methodological detail, and properly justify claims.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial number of minor but critical flaws that undermine the paper's credibility and scientific contribution. The authors should address these issues before resubmitting.
