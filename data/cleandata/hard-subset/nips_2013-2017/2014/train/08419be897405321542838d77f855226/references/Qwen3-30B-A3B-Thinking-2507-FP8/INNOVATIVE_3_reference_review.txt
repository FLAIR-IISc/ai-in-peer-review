

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper presents a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The authors propose representing symbolic expressions as grammar trees and using learning techniques to guide the search for efficient versions of target expressions, rather than relying on brute-force enumeration. The key innovation is applying machine learning (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to learn search strategies from solutions to simpler expressions, enabling discovery of identities too complex for human or brute-force methods.

The paper makes several significant contributions: a grammar framework for symbolic expression optimization, integration of machine learning into this framework, a novel application of recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of symbolic structures, and the discovery of new mathematical identities that reduce computational complexity (e.g., from O(n³) to O(n²)). The authors demonstrate their approach on five families of expressions, showing that learning-based search strategies substantially outperform random search for complex targets.

The work is highly original in its application of machine learning to guide symbolic search. While related to theorem proving, program induction, and attribute grammars, it makes a clear distinction by using learning rather than hand-designed heuristics. The paper properly situates itself against related work in Section 1.1, acknowledging prior work on attribute grammars while highlighting the novelty of their learning-based approach to identity discovery.

The significance of this work is substantial. The discovered identities could potentially be applied to improve computational efficiency in machine learning (e.g., for dropout or RBM partition functions). More broadly, the framework opens a new direction for applying machine learning to symbolic computation, with potential applications in compiler optimization and mathematical discovery.

The paper is technically sound, with clear problem formulation, well-structured methodology, and thorough experimental evaluation. The authors demonstrate that their learning-based approaches consistently outperform random search for complex targets, with the 3-gram model particularly effective for repetitive patterns. The learned representation for symbolic expressions (Section 4.2) is well-validated with classification accuracy exceeding 95% for expressions up to degree 6.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with effective examples (like Example 2 showing the pattern for k=6) that illustrate the approach. The supplementary material provides additional identities, enhancing reproducibility.

The main limitations are the restrictive assumptions about the problem space (homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, single matrix/vector inputs), and the limited scope of the experimental evaluation to five specific families of expressions. While the authors acknowledge these in Section 8, the paper could more thoroughly discuss how the framework might be extended to more general mathematical domains.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper highly compelling. It introduces a genuinely new direction for applying machine learning to symbolic computation, with the potential to impact both theoretical mathematics and practical computational efficiency. The paper's core contribution—using learning to guide symbolic search—is significant and not incremental. The minor limitations in scope and experimental coverage do not detract from the paper's conceptual novelty and potential impact.

The paper's strength lies in its bold conceptual approach rather than methodological perfection. The authors successfully demonstrate that machine learning can guide symbolic search to discover identities previously inaccessible to humans or brute-force methods. This represents a meaningful advance in the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation.

I believe this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field and would be a valuable addition to NIPS. It opens up a new research direction that other researchers can build upon, and the practical implications of the discovered identities are potentially significant for computational efficiency in machine learning.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it combines theoretical novelty with practical applications, addressing a fundamental problem in AI (combinatorial search) with a creative machine learning approach. The work is both theoretically interesting and potentially impactful for practitioners.

In summary, this paper presents a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning, demonstrates significant improvements over random search, and opens up a promising new research direction. While the scope is somewhat limited, the conceptual contribution is substantial and aligns well with NIPS' interest in innovative machine learning approaches to challenging problems.

**Recommendation: Accept**
