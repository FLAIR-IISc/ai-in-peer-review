Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a framework to discover computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target symbolic expression. The search space of candidate expressions is defined by an attribute grammar whose rules encode primitive matrix/vector operations and their computational attributes. Because exhaustive search quickly becomes infeasible, the authors learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets.

Key components:
- Representation and matching: Two approaches are used to test equivalence between a candidate expression S and target T. (i) A numerical descriptor obtained by evaluating both expressions on many random inputs over a finite field Zp, enabling fast probabilistic identity testing and linear-combination solving; and (ii) a learned continuous embedding φ(S) via a recursive neural network (RNN) trained to classify expressions into equivalence classes formed by the numerical descriptors.
- Linear combinations of trees: Complex targets often require linear combinations of trees; the authors assemble candidate descriptors into a linear system Fw = t mod p and solve via Gaussian elimination over Zp to find weights for a matching linear combination.
- Learned search strategies: They compare random search, n-gram models counting frequent subtrees from previously found solutions, and an RNN-based strategy using φ(S) to predict the next grammar rule. A curriculum over degree k is used to scale to more complex targets.

Experiments consider five families: summations over powers of AA^T or (A.*A)A^T (with matrix-multiply removed to enforce O(n^2) candidates), elementary symmetric polynomials Sym_k, and two RBM-related exponential sums over binary vectors. They report that learning greatly increases the chance of finding efficient identities within a 10-minute budget, with n-grams typically outperforming the RNN for larger k; some targets with naive O(n^3) or exponential complexity are reduced to O(n^2) or low-degree polynomial time. The RNN achieves ≤5% error on expression-classification across up to 1687 classes. The paper situates itself relative to theorem proving, program induction, probabilistic programming, NLP use of grammars and RNNs, and CAS systems like Maple/Mathematica, arguing that existing CASs focus on algebraic simplification rather than explicit computational efficiency.

Strengths
- Clear, modular framework: attribute grammar, probabilistic identity testing, linear-combination solver, and learned strategies.
- Novel use of learning to guide combinatorial search for symbolic identities, with curriculum training that transfers from simpler to harder expressions.
- Demonstrates new identities (with code/data) and clear complexity reductions on nontrivial families, including patterns that are hard to find via random search.
- RNN-based continuous embedding for symbolic expressions is well motivated and achieves strong classification accuracy; training details are carefully reported (initialization, ReLU, curriculum, classifier LR, input backprop).
- Open-source release improves reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Verification guarantees: Equivalence relies on randomized testing over Zp; there is no formal bound on the probability of false positives/negatives (e.g., via Schwartz–Zippel bounds with multiple primes or input sizes). A deterministic verification step (e.g., exact symbolic check or rational function identity testing) for the final discovered identities is not reported.
- Linear combinations: The semantics and constraints on coefficients w found modulo p are underspecified. It is unclear how solutions mod p translate to integer/rational coefficients over reals, whether negative coefficients and scaling are allowed, and how this impacts the claimed complexity reductions. A post-hoc exact solution reconstruction (e.g., via Chinese remainder or exact linear algebra over Q) is not described.
- Scope and grammar limitations: The domain is restricted to homogeneous polynomials with scalar outputs and a specific rule set. While intentionally constrained, the practical significance is limited without broader operators (e.g., recursion, trigonometric functions) or multi-variable targets; termination criteria with richer grammars would need addressing.
- Evaluation breadth: No direct comparison to strong baselines in computer algebra (e.g., Mathematica/Maple simplification and factorization, known DP algorithms for Sym_k, or algebraic complexity methods). For example, efficient O(nk) computation of elementary symmetric polynomials is classical; it is unclear whether the framework rediscovers known DP formulas or provides novel ones.
- Metrics and scalability: Reporting focuses on fraction of successful runs within a fixed time budget, without detailed time-to-solution distributions or resource scaling. The search space growth is acknowledged, but success beyond modest k and families (e.g., RBM-2 beyond k=5) remains limited. RNN underperforms n-grams for search despite good classification accuracy, suggesting a mismatch between the learned embedding and decision boundary needed for rule prediction.
- Complexity accounting: Operation cost models and the claim of O(n^2) or O(n^3) reductions would benefit from a precise operation count and asymptotic analysis per discovered identity.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The overall methodology is reasonable and components are correctly applied. However, the lack of formal probability bounds for identity testing and the ambiguities in linear-combination reconstruction reduce rigor. More careful post-verification would strengthen claims.
- Completeness: The work is a substantial system effort with multiple components and nontrivial experiments, but comparisons to standard baselines and formal guarantees are missing.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written. Some details are absent (full grammar table in-text, precise scheduler/tie-breaking policy, selection of N and p, bounds on collision probability, constraints on w).
- Reproducibility: Code and data availability are positives.

Originality
- The framing—learning to guide search over an attribute grammar to discover efficient algebraic identities—is novel in ML, distinct from rule-based CAS and prior theorem proving/program induction approaches. The learned continuous embedding for symbolic expressions is also original for this setting. The n-gram strategy is simple but effective; the RNN application is innovative though not yet superior.

Significance
- The potential impact is promising for compiler optimization and automated discovery of efficient formulas. Demonstrated identities show meaningful asymptotic gains on selected families. However, current scope and scalability limits, plus lack of comparison to existing CAS/algorithms, temper immediate impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and timely integration of learning with symbolic reasoning/search; clear positive results on challenging families.
- Introduces practical tools (finite-field testing, linear combination solver) into a coherent learning-guided discovery pipeline.
- Opens a path toward ML-assisted algebraic optimization; code/data provided.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient formal verification and probabilistic guarantees for discovered identities; unclear reconstruction of coefficients.
- Limited evaluation against strong baselines and known formulas; unclear novelty of some identities (e.g., symmetric polynomials).
- RNN-based strategy does not surpass simpler n-grams; scalability to harder families is limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add formal bounds for the identity test (multiple primes/inputs) and include a final exact verification step (symbolic or exact arithmetic) for discovered identities and coefficients.
- Clarify the allowed coefficient set, reconstruction from mod p to integers/rationals, and how this affects complexity and correctness.
- Provide baselines from CAS systems and known algorithmic identities (e.g., DP for Sym_k), and report exact operation counts.
- Analyze why the RNN underperforms n-grams for search and explore architectures/training that better align embedding with rule prediction.
- Discuss generalization to richer grammars and termination strategies, and report time-to-solution statistics and scaling with resources.

Overall, this is a creative and potentially impactful direction with solid empirical evidence within its scope, but it would benefit from stronger verification and comparative evaluation to meet NeurIPS standards.
