This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities, leveraging attribute grammars and machine learning to navigate the search space of possible expression transformations. The core idea – using ML to guide the exploration of a grammar-defined space of symbolic expressions – is intriguing and addresses a challenging problem. The authors demonstrate success in finding more efficient versions of expressions, some of which appear to be previously undiscovered, and provide a solid empirical evaluation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars with machine learning for symbolic manipulation is a fresh approach. While attribute grammars themselves are established, their application to *discovering* efficient identities, coupled with learning-based search, is a significant contribution.
* **Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates concrete improvements in computational complexity for several example expressions. Discovering O(n<sup>2</sup>) solutions for expressions initially requiring O(n<sup>3</sup>) or exponential time is compelling. The ablation studies comparing different search strategies (n-gram, RNN, random) are well-executed and insightful.
* **Technical Soundness:** The framework appears technically well-grounded. The use of integer-based descriptors to address floating-point precision issues is a clever detail. The explanation of the RNN architecture and training process is clear.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors have made their code and evaluation data publicly available, which greatly enhances the reproducibility and impact of their work.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the core concepts and experimental setup.  The included examples (particularly Example 1 & 2) help illustrate the core ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scope & Generalizability:** The current framework is limited to homogeneous polynomials of degree *k* with a single matrix variable. While the authors acknowledge these restrictions, the practical impact of the work is somewhat constrained by them. The results on the more complex RBM-2 family are particularly disappointing, suggesting the approach struggles with highly complex expressions.  It's not immediately clear how easily this framework can be extended to more general symbolic expressions (e.g., those involving trigonometric functions, integrals, or multiple variables in a more complex way).
* **RNN Performance:** While the RNN shows promise, its performance is generally inferior to the n-gram models in the experiments. This raises questions about the effectiveness of the continuous representation and the suitability of the RNN architecture for this task. The authors attribute the RNN's underperformance to overfitting, but the chosen dropout rate seems somewhat arbitrary.  A more thorough investigation of different RNN architectures or training strategies could be beneficial.
* **Search Strategy Limitations:** The current search strategy, even with learning, can be slow for complex expressions. The 10-minute cut-off time implies that some potentially efficient identities may have been missed.
* **Related Work Discussion:** While the related work section covers relevant areas, it could benefit from a more detailed discussion of existing symbolic computation techniques (beyond Maple/Mathematica) and automated theorem proving methods. The connection to program synthesis could also be explored further.
* **Descriptor Size:** The choice of the prime number *p* and descriptor size *N* is somewhat ad-hoc. A more principled approach to determining these parameters might improve robustness.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a challenging problem in symbolic computation.
* The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements in computational complexity for specific expressions.
* The publicly available code and data promote reproducibility and facilitate further research.
* The work opens up new avenues for applying machine learning techniques to symbolic reasoning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The limited scope of the current framework restricts its practical applicability.
* The RNN performance is underwhelming compared to simpler n-gram models.
* The search strategy can be computationally expensive for complex expressions.
* The related work discussion could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and innovative paper with the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of symbolic computation. While the current framework has limitations, the authors have demonstrated a promising proof-of-concept and have provided a strong foundation for future research.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The paper is strong, but the limitations in scope and RNN performance, coupled with the relatively modest gains on the more challenging examples, prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form.  

**Suggestions for Revision:**

* **Expand the scope:** Explore extending the framework to handle more general symbolic expressions (e.g., involving trigonometric functions or multiple variables).
* **Improve RNN Performance:** Investigate alternative RNN architectures, training strategies, or regularization techniques to enhance the performance of the continuous representation.
* **Optimize Search Strategy:** Explore more efficient search algorithms or heuristics to accelerate the discovery of efficient identities.
* **Strengthen Related Work:** Provide a more thorough and nuanced discussion of related work in symbolic computation and automated theorem proving.
* **Descriptor Parameter Selection:** Investigate a more principled approach to selecting the parameters for the integer-based descriptor.
* **Additional Experiments:** Include experiments on a wider range of expressions and datasets to demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of the framework.
