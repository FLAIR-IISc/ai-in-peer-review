This paper proposes a framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars and machine learning. The core idea – leveraging learning to guide the search for optimized expressions – is interesting and potentially impactful. However, the paper suffers from a multitude of small flaws, inconsistencies, and a lack of rigorous evaluation that, cumulatively, lead me to recommend rejection.

The introduction adequately motivates the problem, but overstates the novelty. While applying ML to *symbolic* manipulation is relatively unexplored, the paper doesn't sufficiently contextualize its contribution within the broader areas of symbolic computation and automated theorem proving. The claim of discovering “many new mathematical identities” is bold and requires significantly more justification than presented. 

The attribute grammar framework (Section 3) is clearly explained, but feels somewhat contrived. The restriction to homogeneous polynomials feels arbitrary, and the justification provided (“undecidability and halting problem are not present”) is weak; these are not practical concerns for the problem scale addressed. The handling of multiple variables (mentioned briefly) feels underdeveloped. The decision to remove the matrix-matrix multiply rule for certain families is a crucial detail, and its impact on the generality of the solutions isn’t fully discussed.  Table 1 is also awkwardly formatted; rule descriptions should be aligned consistently for better readability.

Section 4, on representing symbolic expressions, is problematic. The numerical representation, while a clever workaround for equivalence checking, introduces potential numerical instability issues that are acknowledged but not adequately addressed. The use of a large prime `p` is a good start, but the choice of `p` isn’t justified. The learned representation using RNNs is more promising, but the training details are vague. Why was `l=30` chosen? What is the architecture of the RNN?  The statement that “the weight matrix should be initialized to the identity, plus a small amount of Gaussian noise” feels ad-hoc and lacks theoretical backing. The dropout implementation, while effective, is described in an imprecise manner.  Fig. 2 is helpful but lacks sufficient explanation of the matrix/tensor operations within the RNN.

Section 5, on linear combinations of trees, is concise but complete. However, the assertion that the number of solutions can vary is a trivial observation, and doesn’t add value. 

Section 6, detailing the search strategies, suffers from significant issues. The description of the n-gram approach is superficial.  Why were specific n-gram orders (1-5) chosen? The lack of ablation studies comparing these orders is concerning. The RNN strategy relies heavily on the pre-trained RNN from Section 4. The connection between classification accuracy (Table 2) and search performance isn’t clear. Table 2 is also poorly presented; it needs a clear caption explaining what “Degree” refers to.  The RNN performance description ("100± 12 vs 438± 77 secs") is confusing – is this the total search time, or the time to find the first solution?  The claim that the RNN *consistently* finds solutions faster than random is not statistically supported.

Section 7, the experimental results, is the weakest part of the paper. The choice of expression families is arbitrary and lacks justification. The evaluation metric ("fraction of successful runs") is unusual and obscures the actual performance improvement (or lack thereof).  The claim that the 3-gram model "reliably finds solutions" is not quantified; “reliably” is subjective. The discussion of results is overly optimistic, downplaying the failure of most approaches on the (RBM-2)k family.  Referencing the supplementary material for the discovered identities is unacceptable; these should be included directly in the paper. Table 3, showing the search space size, is helpful but arrives too late in the paper.

The discussion (Section 8) reiterates the paper’s contributions, but doesn’t adequately address the limitations. The acknowledgement section is standard.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The idea of using ML to guide symbolic manipulation is novel.
*   The attribute grammar framework provides a structured approach to the problem.
*   The learned representation using RNNs shows promise.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies throughout the paper.
*   Lack of rigorous evaluation and statistical analysis.
*   Overstated claims of novelty and significance.
*   Vague descriptions of key experimental details.
*   Poor presentation of results.
*   Unjustified design choices and ad-hoc parameters.
*   Limited scope and generality of the approach.



Overall, while the core idea is intriguing, the paper is marred by a lack of polish and rigor. The numerous minor issues detract from the overall quality and credibility of the work.  A major revision, addressing the concerns outlined above, would be necessary for reconsideration. I recommend rejection in its current form.
