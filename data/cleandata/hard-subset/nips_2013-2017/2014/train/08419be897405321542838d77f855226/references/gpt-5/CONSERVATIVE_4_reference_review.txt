Summary
The paper proposes using an attribute-grammar framework to enumerate symbolic expressions and learning to guide the search toward algebraically equivalent but computationally cheaper identities. Two representations are introduced for equivalence checking: (i) numerical descriptors computed over Z_p at multiple random instantiations to avoid floating-point issues, and (ii) a learned continuous embedding via a recursive neural network (RNN) trained to classify expressions into equivalence classes. To scale search, the authors propose n-gram models over grammar subtrees and an RNN-based policy to select the next grammar rule. For more complex targets, linear combinations of trees are solved via Gaussian elimination modulo p. Experiments on five families of expressions show that learned strategies (especially n-grams) increase the fraction of runs that discover efficient identities within a fixed time budget, compared to random search.

Strengths
- An interesting framing of identity discovery as grammar-guided search with learned heuristics that is reminiscent of program synthesis but specialized to a tractable algebraic subdomain.
- Clear articulation of two representations and how they are employed in the pipeline; the integer-based descriptor is practical for fast equality testing.
- The empirical finding that simple n-gram statistics can substantially improve over random search for some families is useful and reproducible.
- The RNN classification accuracy on expression equivalence classes suggests structure can be captured by learned embeddings, which could be of independent interest.

Weaknesses
- Validation is largely “fraction of successful runs within 10 minutes,” not end-to-end computational savings on concrete instances. There is no timing comparison against optimized baselines (e.g., CAS simplifiers, factorization/trace identities, matrix-chain optimization, standard compiler algebraic rewrites, or heuristic search such as beam search/MCTS/ILP/SMT). The practical impact is therefore unclear.
- Correctness is not rigorously established. Equivalence is checked via random evaluation in Z_p; the paper does not quantify collision probabilities, number of primes tried, or provide symbolic verification of the reported identities. Linear combinations solved modulo p risk producing coefficients not valid over the reals/integers; constraints on coefficients (e.g., small integers) are not stated.
- Claims of discovering “previously undiscovered” identities are not substantiated with proofs or bibliographic evidence. Example 1 is a straightforward application of linearity of summation/multiplication and seems standard.
- The grammar is heavily restricted (homogeneous polynomials in a single variable with scalar output), and the strongest positive results are on families with simple local repetition, where n-grams outperform the RNN. This undercuts the central claim that learned continuous representations are key to scaling discovery.
- Important design choices are ad hoc and insufficiently ablated: number of descriptor samples N and choice of prime p; the decision to remove matrix-matrix multiply from the grammar to force O(n^2) solutions; softmax learning rate ×100; dropout schedule; effect of curriculum; and the scheduler’s search policy (e.g., breadth vs depth).
- The use of linear combinations of trees is under-specified. What is the allowed coefficient set? Are weights constrained to small integers/rationals? How are multiple solutions disambiguated? How are resulting expressions rendered back in the original grammar?
- Reported “complexity” improvements are asymptotic only; no analysis of constant factors or numerical stability, and no real-world runtime benchmarks on varying n, m, p. 
- The RNN equivalence classification task is interesting but orthogonal: high test accuracy does not directly translate into better search quality; the empirical results show the RNN policy is often inferior to n-grams.

Quality
Technically ambitious but empirically under-supported. The search and verification components lack formal guarantees or systematic ablations. The absence of comparisons to established symbolic tools and search heuristics weakens the case that the approach advances the state of the art.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-organized. However, several critical details are missing or ambiguous: coefficient constraints in the linear solver; collision analysis for Z_p descriptors; specifics of the scheduler; and precise definitions of the grammar rules (Table 1 is referenced but not present here). Claims of novelty for identities need clearer justification and, ideally, formal proofs.

Originality
Framing identity discovery with attribute grammars and learning-guided search is novel within ML venues, though related to program synthesis, theorem proving, and algebraic simplification. The RNN embedding for expressions is a reasonable adaptation of prior NLP work. However, the empirical novelty is limited by the finding that simple n-grams outperform the RNN on most tasks.

Significance
Potentially significant if it could robustly discover nontrivial identities that translate into real speedups in practical systems (CAS, compilers, ML models). As presented, the scope is narrow and the evidence limited; without rigorous correctness and stronger baselines, it is hard to judge the broader impact.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and a plausible end-to-end pipeline.
- Practical integer-based descriptor for fast equivalence testing.
- Demonstrates that simple learned heuristics can guide search effectively on some families.
- Opens a path for ML-guided symbolic reasoning.

Cons
- Weak experimental validation; lacks strong baselines and runtime benchmarks.
- Correctness verification is probabilistic and under-analyzed; no formal proofs.
- Grammar and problem scope are narrow; many results rely on removing costly operations a priori.
- RNN does not clearly outperform simpler models; limited ablations.
- Novelty claims about identities are not substantiated.

Questions for authors
1. How do you ensure coefficients from the linear combination are valid over reals/integers and map back to the grammar? Do you restrict to small integer weights?
2. What is the probability of false equivalence under your Z_p scheme? How many primes and samples N are used, and do you validate with multiple primes?
3. Can you provide formal proofs (or CAS verification) for a subset of “new” identities?
4. How does your method compare to Mathematica/Maple simplification, equality saturation/term rewriting, or beam/MCTS search?
5. What is the end-to-end wall-clock speedup on realistic sizes versus naive evaluation and versus hand-optimized rewrites?
6. How sensitive are results to the choice of grammar (e.g., including/excluding matmul) and to curriculum design?

Recommendation: Weak reject
The idea is intriguing, but the empirical evidence and rigor are insufficient for NeurIPS at this stage. Strengthening correctness guarantees, adding strong baselines and real runtime evaluations, and clarifying the linear combination step would significantly improve the paper.
