

**Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"**

This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars combined with machine learning. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions as grammar trees and use ML models (n-grams, RNNs) to guide the search for efficient versions of target expressions. The authors claim to discover new identities that reduce computational complexity (e.g., from O(n³) to O(n²) for certain matrix operations).

**Strengths:**  
The problem of finding efficient mathematical identities is well-motivated for computational applications. The use of attribute grammars to formalize the search space is a reasonable approach, and the paper demonstrates the feasibility of the approach for several constrained expression families. The numerical representation using integer-based descriptors is a practical solution to the equivalence checking problem.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Lack of meaningful baselines:** The paper only compares against a random search strategy, which is a poor baseline for this problem. The authors fail to compare against established symbolic computation tools (Mathematica, Maple) that already perform simplifications and optimizations. The cited work on symbolic computation engines explicitly states they "do not explicitly seek versions of lower complexity," yet the paper doesn't demonstrate how their approach improves upon these systems for the specific problem of computational efficiency.

2. **Overstated novelty:** The approach appears to be primarily a combination of existing techniques applied to a highly constrained problem domain. The claim that the RNN learns a "novel continuous representation" for mathematical structures isn't sufficiently differentiated from prior work (e.g., Bowman et al. on logical predicates, Socher et al. on sentiment analysis). The paper doesn't clearly articulate what new insights the representation provides beyond what could be achieved with simpler methods.

3. **Questionable significance of "discoveries":** The paper claims to discover "previously undiscovered" identities, but the examples provided (like the matrix examples) appear to be straightforward generalizations of known algebraic properties (e.g., associative properties of matrix multiplication). The paper doesn't provide evidence that these identities are genuinely novel or useful beyond the specific constrained problem they address.

4. **Limited scope and applicability:** The problem is severely constrained (only homogeneous polynomials of degree k, single matrix/vector, scalar output), which limits the practical relevance of the approach. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't adequately address whether the method can be generalized to more practical mathematical problems.

5. **Insufficient evidence for ML advantage:** While the paper shows ML approaches outperform random search for some simple cases (k=2-6), this is expected given the constrained search space. For the most challenging family (RBM-2), the ML approaches don't outperform random search at all, and no solutions were found for k>5. The paper lacks a clear analysis of when the ML approach is beneficial versus when it's not.

6. **Theoretical underpinnings missing:** The paper lacks any theoretical analysis of why the ML approach should work better than brute-force search. The empirical results are limited to a narrow range of problems without demonstrating scalability to more complex scenarios.

**Recommendation:**  
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a technically sound framework for a constrained problem, it fails to provide sufficient evidence that the ML approach offers significant advantages over simpler, established methods. The claims about "discovering new mathematical identities" are overstated, and the paper doesn't adequately address how this approach compares to existing symbolic computation tools. The problem is too narrowly defined to be of broad significance to the machine learning community, and the approach doesn't demonstrate clear advantages over simpler alternatives for the problems it addresses.

The paper would benefit from: (1) comparing against established symbolic computation tools, (2) providing a clearer justification for why ML is necessary, (3) expanding the problem scope to more general mathematical expressions, and (4) including a thorough analysis of limitations and failure cases. Without these improvements, the contribution is not sufficiently novel or significant for publication at NIPS.
