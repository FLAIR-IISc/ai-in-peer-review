- Proposes a novel grammar-based, learning-guided framework to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities.  
- Represents symbolic expressions as sequences of operations and uses n-gram models and recursive neural networks to guide search.  
- Key insight: learning from simpler expressions helps discover more complex, lower-complexity identities (e.g., O(n^3) → O(n^2)).  
- Experiments on several expression families show learning-based search significantly outperforms random search for complex identities.  
- Uses integer-based verification in Zp to check symbolic equivalence, avoiding floating-point instability.  
- Paper is technically sound with a clear problem statement, methodology, and appropriate experimental baselines and metrics.  
- Experimental design includes multiple runs and fixed time budgets; figures clearly demonstrate performance gains (n-grams excel on repetitive structures).  
- Lacks theoretical analysis explaining why learning approaches outperform random search.  
- Does not discuss computational cost of training the learning models, an important practical consideration.  
- Clarity is strong: well-organized, good motivation, clear representation and examples, and effective figures.  
- Missing details on RNN hyperparameters (learning rate, batch size, epochs) and justification/sensitivity of embedding dimension l=30.  
- Original contribution: novel application of n-gram and recursive neural models to guide discovery of mathematical identities.  
- Distinguishes itself from theorem provers and symbolic engines by using learning rather than rule-based heuristics.  
- Significance: potential impact on ML practice and compiler optimization; continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions open broader avenues.  
- Limited scope: focused on homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix variable, restricting immediate applicability.  
- Limited comparison: only compared against random search, lacking comparisons to other symbolic optimization methods.  
- Verification concerns: Zp approach is clever but needs more discussion on choice of prime p, number of points N, and false-positive risk.  
- Suggestions: provide theoretical justification, expand scope to more general expressions, detail RNN training/hyperparameters, compare to alternative methods, and discuss training costs.  
- Overall assessment: strong, novel, and well-executed work with important results; recommended for acceptance.
