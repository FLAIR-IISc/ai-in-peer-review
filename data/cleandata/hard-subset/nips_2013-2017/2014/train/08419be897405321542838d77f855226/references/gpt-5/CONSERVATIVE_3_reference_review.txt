Summary
The paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient identities equivalent to given target symbolic expressions. Expressions are generated via an attribute grammar over matrix/vector operations; complexity is associated to rules. Because exhaustive enumeration is infeasible, the authors learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets. Two strategies are explored: n-gram counts of subtrees and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees and a softmax policy for next-rule prediction. Equivalence to the target is checked numerically by evaluating expressions over random inputs modulo a large prime, and linear combinations of trees are allowed by solving a modular linear system. Experiments on several expression families (e.g., sums over AAT powers, elementwise variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, RBM-related sums) show that learning-guided search finds identities at higher degrees than random search within a 10-minute budget; n-grams often outperform the RNN. The paper claims discovery of new identities and substantial complexity reductions (e.g., O(nm) vs O(nmp), O(n2) vs O(n3)).

Relation to prior work
The work sits at the intersection of program induction, compiler optimization, theorem proving, and symbolic computation. It references attribute grammars, RNNs for trees, and some ML-for-theorem-proving efforts. However, it omits highly relevant baselines and theory: equality saturation approaches for algebraic optimization (e.g., equality graphs/rewriting; Tate et al., 2009), superoptimization and stochastic search (e.g., STOKE, Schkufza et al., 2013; Massalin, 1987), program synthesis systems like Sketch (Solar-Lezama, 2008), and polynomial identity testing (Schwartz–Zippel) for correctness guarantees. Comparison to CAS/rewriting systems (Mathematica/Maple with simplification plus cost models) is also lacking.

Quality
- Correctness/verification: The use of finite-field numerical evaluation to certify equality is reasonable in spirit (indeed standard in PIT), but the paper gives no failure probability bounds, no choice of p, and no analysis of N needed to bound error. Claims of “previously undiscovered” identities are not supported by formal proofs or references and rely on probabilistic checks; no symbolic verification with CAS is reported. For identities used across linear combinations of trees, a PIT-style bound is especially important.
- Methodology: The search objective is weakly formulated. In key experiments the authors remove matrix-matrix multiplication to force solutions to be O(n2); this constrains the hypothesis space rather than optimizing a cost under a principled objective. A more rigorous approach would assign rule costs and search for minimum-cost equivalent expressions. The scheduler/strategy interface is underspecified (e.g., beam size, backtracking, tie-breaking, pruning).
- Empirics: The primary metric is “fraction of runs that find a solution within 10 minutes” over 10 trials. There are no time-to-solution distributions with confidence intervals, no ablations on training size, no evaluation of solution optimality, and limited scaling. The RNN is largely underperforming n-grams; the representation’s classification accuracy (≤5% error up to k=6) is shown, but its relevance to search quality is unclear. No comparison is made to established systems (CAS simplification/rewriting with cost, equality saturation, SAT/ILP-guided synthesis, MCTS/A*).
- Reproducibility: Many training details are provided for the RNN, but key parameters for the equivalence test (prime p, N, collision handling), grammar completeness, and search hyperparameters are not fully specified in the main text. Identities are deferred to the supplement.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable with concrete examples and a clear high-level pipeline. However, important elements (full grammar, complexity attributes, verification parameters) are only summarized, and the presentation of the linear-combination solver and its implications for identity verification would benefit from more formalism and bounds.

Originality
Applying learned search policies (n-grams, RNNs) over an attribute grammar to discover efficient symbolic identities is a creative angle. That said, the idea of exploring a large equivalence space under a cost model is well-trodden in compiler optimization and synthesis; the paper would benefit from clearer differentiation and principled integration of established techniques.

Significance
If validated and benchmarked against strong baselines with formal correctness guarantees, the approach could be impactful for automatic algebraic optimization. As presented, the practical significance is limited by (i) lack of formal verification, (ii) constrained grammars, (iii) absence of comparisons to established optimizers, and (iv) modest RNN gains over simple n-grams.

Pros
- Interesting problem formulation and potential practical utility.
- Clear grammar-based search setup; linear combinations broaden expressivity.
- Modular arithmetic for numerical checks is more robust than floating point.
- Demonstrates that simple learned heuristics (n-grams) can substantially help search.

Cons
- No formal correctness guarantees (no PIT bounds, no symbolic proofs), yet claims “new identities.”
- No comparison to strong baselines (CAS simplification with cost, equality saturation, STOKE/Sketch, A*/MCTS).
- Cost/complexity optimization is enforced by ad hoc rule removal rather than principled cost-aware search.
- Empirical evaluation is limited (small number of trials, no CIs, no optimality assessment).
- RNN adds complexity but underperforms n-grams; unclear added value.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide formal verification: state and use Schwartz–Zippel-style bounds to choose p and N; report failure probabilities; optionally validate discovered identities symbolically (CAS) for small n and symbolically via PIT for generality.
- Replace rule removal with a cost-aware objective; consider dynamic programming over e-graphs/equality saturation with learned cost models.
- Add strong baselines: Mathematica/Maple simplification + cost; equality saturation; STOKE-like stochastic search; Sketch-based synthesis; search with A*/beam/MCTS guided by handcrafted heuristics.
- Report time-to-solution distributions, success probabilities with CIs, and quality/optimality of solutions (measured cost vs known lower bounds).
- Clarify the grammar and its completeness for each family; detail search parameters; provide the list of identities in the main text or as a concise table.
- Discuss generalization across families (train on one, test on another) and ablations on training size/degree.

Questions for authors
1. How do you bound the probability of false positives in the modular numerical equivalence test? What p and N are used?
2. Are the “new” identities formally proved or checked with CAS for general n? Can you provide proofs or references?
3. Why not use a cost-aware search over the full grammar instead of removing rules?
4. How do your methods compare to equality saturation and CAS-based simplification with a cost model?

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem is valuable and the approach is intriguing, but the paper lacks formal verification, principled cost optimization, and comparisons to established baselines. Strengthening these aspects could make this a solid contribution.
