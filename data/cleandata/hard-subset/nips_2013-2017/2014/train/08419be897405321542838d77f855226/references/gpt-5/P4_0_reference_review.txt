Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a framework to discover computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target symbolic expression. Expressions are built from an attribute grammar whose rules carry complexity annotations. Because exhaustive enumeration of valid expression trees is infeasible for higher degrees, the authors introduce learned search strategies to guide the exploration: (i) n-gram models over subtrees observed in solutions to simpler targets, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees and, atop that, a softmax policy that predicts the next grammar rule during search. To check equivalence efficiently, they avoid full symbolic manipulation and instead use descriptors: evaluations over randomly sampled inputs computed in Zp (integers mod a large prime). For more complex targets, the method allows linear combinations of multiple trees by solving a linear system Fw = t over Zp to match the target descriptor. Experiments on five families of expressions (matrix polynomials, elementwise-multiplied variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, and two RBM-related exponential-sum families) show that learned strategies substantially outperform random search, with n-gram strategies often beating the RNN policy. The authors claim discovery of identities reducing complexity from O(n3) to O(n2) and from exponential to polynomial time for certain families.

The work connects to theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming. It also relates to algebraic optimization in compilers and superoptimization (e.g., stochastic superoptimization) and to domain-specific search for algorithmic identities (e.g., fast transforms). While the paper cites attribute grammars, RNNs in NLP, and CAS systems, it omits relevant prior work in algebraic/rewriting-based optimization and code search (e.g., superoptimization, equality saturation, and the SPIRAL system for fast DSP transforms), which would situate the contribution more precisely.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and constrained scope (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs) that makes the search tractable while remaining nontrivial.
- A practical equivalence-checking scheme using modular arithmetic that enables learning loops and large-scale search.
- A novel application of RNNs to learn continuous embeddings of symbolic expressions that support downstream prediction (rule selection).
- Solid empirical study across multiple expression families with meaningful complexity reductions; open-source code claimed.
- The learned search markedly expands the feasible degree/complexity compared to random search; n-gram strategies are simple and effective.

Weaknesses and detailed assessment

Quality (technical soundness)
- Equivalence verification: Descriptors in Zp provide probabilistic, not formal, guarantees of equality. The paper does not quantify collision probability, choose N and p with provable bounds, or use multiple primes to reduce risk. Because these descriptors also label training data for the RNN, any rare collisions could propagate. A formal verification step (symbolic proof or multiple independent primes with bounds) for reported identities would strengthen claims.
- Linear combinations of trees: The method solves Fw = t over Zp and interprets a solution as a linear combination of trees. It is unclear whether the grammar permits arbitrary scalar coefficients and whether coefficients in Zp correspond to admissible real/integer coefficients in the underlying expression language. If coefficients beyond ±1 are used, the paper should explain how these are realized in the grammar and whether the semantic field mismatch (mod p vs reals) is handled. Otherwise, the solver could yield solutions not representable by the grammar over the intended domain.
- Complexity claims rely partly on excluding certain rules (e.g., matrix-multiply) to bound the search to O(n2). This ensures any found identity is “efficient” but does not necessarily establish best-known complexity or quantify operation counts. More rigorous operation counts and comparisons would help.
- The scheduler is greedy (highest-scoring rule at each step). No analysis of search completeness or exploration is provided. Beam search, MCTS, or learned exploration could reduce myopic failures (noted on RBM-2).
- Experimental baselines are limited. Comparisons against CAS simplification/factorization (e.g., Mathematica’s FullSimplify/TensorReduce) or rewrite/equality-saturation systems, as well as dynamic-programming baselines for special cases, would contextualize gains.
- The RNN policy often underperforms n-gram models; there is limited analysis of why and of data/architecture choices (e.g., effect of representation dimension l, training set size, curriculum stages).

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear and well organized. However:
  - The exact grammar (Table 1) and coefficient rules should be fully specified, including allowed scalar multiplications and additions.
  - The descriptor construction needs explicit N and p, collision bounds, and the criterion for declaring equality (e.g., exact match vs threshold).
  - Details on F’s construction (number of trees M, sampling strategy) and how many trees are combined per solution would improve reproducibility.
  - Some families (RBM-2) would benefit from concrete example trees.
- The use of the RNN for both representation learning and policy learning is described clearly, with sensible training heuristics.

Originality
- Applying learned policies (including RNN embeddings) to navigate an attribute-grammar search for algebraic identities is novel and interesting. The probabilistic equivalence-checking pipeline enabling large-scale learning over symbolic structures is also a noteworthy design.
- However, the paper would benefit from engaging with prior art in search-based optimization of algebraic/program expressions (e.g., SPIRAL for transform identities; stochastic superoptimizers; equality saturation), clarifying differences (focus on polynomials, learned search vs hand-designed rewrites).

Significance
- The approach shows potential to automate discovery of useful algebraic reductions, with prospective applications in ML (e.g., sums over binary masks, partition functions) and compiler optimization. The ability to learn from simpler instances to solve harder ones is promising.
- Current performance is mixed on the hardest family (RBM-2), and n-grams outperform RNNs in several cases, tempering the impact of the learned representation. Stronger empirical comparisons and formal verification would enhance significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a formal verification stage (e.g., multiple large primes with quantified error bounds, and/or symbolic proof using a CAS) for all reported identities; report any counterexamples found.
- Clarify grammar expressivity regarding scalar coefficients and how solutions from Fw = t are translated into valid expressions over reals.
- Provide operation counts and wall-clock comparisons for discovered identities vs naive and CAS-optimized baselines.
- Compare against established systems (e.g., Mathematica simplifications, equality-saturation frameworks) and discuss SPIRAL-like systems and superoptimizers in related work.
- Explore stronger search (beam/MCTS) and analyze why the RNN policy underperforms n-grams; consider data augmentation and better smoothing (e.g., Kneser–Ney) for n-grams.
- Quantify descriptor parameters (N, p) and collision probabilities; consider multiple primes.
- Discuss scalability to multiple variables and richer grammars; include ablations on l and curriculum.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated framework that integrates learning with symbolic search; promising results on nontrivial families.
- Novel use of RNN embeddings for symbolic expressions; practical descriptor trick enables learning.
- Potentially impactful direction bridging ML and symbolic algebra.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of formal verification and ambiguity around linear combinations over Zp vs real expressions.
- Missing comparisons to strong baselines and related work in program/algebraic optimization.
- Limited success on the most challenging family; n-grams often surpass the RNN policy without deeper insight.

Overall, the work is intriguing and technically competent, but would benefit from stronger validation, clearer handling of coefficients/equality, and broader contextualization against existing optimization frameworks.
