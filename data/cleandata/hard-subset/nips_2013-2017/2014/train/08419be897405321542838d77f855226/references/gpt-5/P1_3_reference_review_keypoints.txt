- Proposes a framework to discover computationally efficient identities for symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar and learned search strategies.
- Measures efficiency via grammar complexity attributes and validates equivalence with randomized finite-field evaluation.
- Learns search policies from simpler targets using n-gram subtree statistics and an RNN that embeds expressions and predicts next grammar rules.
- Demonstrates large empirical gains over random search, discovering identities reducing complexity (e.g., O(n^3) → O(n^2) or exponential → polynomial).
- Public release of code and data to aid reproducibility.

- Relates to theorem proving, program induction, probabilistic grammars, and recursive networks; differs from CAS by focusing on complexity reduction and learned heuristics.
- Connected to randomized polynomial identity testing and compiler algebraic optimizations, but these links could be expanded.

- Novel framing: learning-guided discovery of efficient identities within a constrained grammar with clear potential impact.
- Effective empirical use of learning to manage combinatorial search; both n-grams and RNN improve performance.
- Well-explained RNN for symbolic expressions with careful training choices (identity init, curriculum, ReLU, dropout).
- Linear-combination-of-trees mechanism pragmatically expands expressivity.
- Open-source code/data boosts reproducibility and community value.

- Theoretical guarantees are underdeveloped: no analysis of collision probability for finite-field testing nor citation of Schwartz–Zippel/PIT.
- No symbolic verification or literature checks for claimed “previously undiscovered” identities.
- Evaluation lacks end-to-end runtime speedup metrics on real numerical tasks and limited ablations.
- Grammar is ad hoc/restricted (e.g., disallowing matrix-matrix multiply may bias results); generalization is unclear.
- RNN often underperforms n-grams; advantage of embeddings over simple statistics not convincingly shown.
- Important implementation details (exact grammar, prime p, descriptor length N, solver constraints) are scattered.

- Technically sound empirically, but requires more rigor on identity testing (failure bounds, citation of PIT) and mapping modular solutions to real identities.
- Need to account for costs of linear combinations (sums and scalars) when reporting complexity.
- Experiments are suggestive but incomplete; request symbolic verification, wall-clock speedups, and ablations (curriculum, dropout, embedding size, alternative searches).

- Generally well written with instructive examples, but missing full grammar and complexity attributes in main text.
- Lack of precise specification for scheduler, stopping criteria, and branching policy.
- Missing finite-field parameters (p, N), thresholding rules, and mapping from modular coefficients to real coefficients.
- Unclear when/why matrix-matrix multiply is disallowed and how that affects validity of efficient identities.

- The combination of attribute grammars, learned heuristics, and continuous embeddings for complexity-driven identity discovery is novel.
- More discussion needed comparing to randomized PIT and compiler algebraic optimizations to clarify distinctiveness.

- Potentially impactful for symbolic-numeric computation, compiler backends, and ML model efficiency, but current scope is limited to certain families and degrees.

- Q1: What are p and N, and what is the resulting false-identity probability per Schwartz–Zippel; is final symbolic verification performed?
- Q2: How are coefficients from Fw = t mod p constrained or interpreted to ensure small-integer real identities?
- Q3: What are the exact grammar rules and attributed complexities; does grammar include addition/scalar multiplication explicitly?
- Q4: Can you report wall-clock speedups for representative sizes and compare constant factors?
- Q5: Why does the RNN underperform n-grams in several families; any analysis of learned embeddings?

- Add PIT analysis and cite Schwartz–Zippel; include symbolic verification step where possible.
- Report end-to-end runtime and memory improvements on concrete numerical tasks across varying n.
- Include full grammar and complexity definitions in the main paper; discuss extension to richer operators.
- Provide discovered identities and formal proofs for several nontrivial cases in the paper.
- Explore stronger search strategies (beam search, MCTS), improved smoothing for n-grams, and hybrid models combining methods.

- Pros: novel timely problem, good integration of learning and symbolic methods, clear empirical gains, and open-source artifacts.
- Pros: insightful RNN treatment and pragmatic linear-combination mechanism.
- Cons: limited theoretical guarantees and potential false positives from probabilistic testing.
- Cons: evaluation lacks end-to-end performance metrics and broader baseline comparisons; RNN benefits not consistently demonstrated.

- Recommendation: Weak accept (6/10) — promising and innovative but needs stronger theoretical grounding and broader empirical validation.
