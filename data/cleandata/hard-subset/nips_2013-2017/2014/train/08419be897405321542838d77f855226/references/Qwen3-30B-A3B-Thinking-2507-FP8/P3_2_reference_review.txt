

# Peer Review: "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors frame the problem as finding an expression S that computes the same result as a target expression T, but with lower computational complexity. Their approach combines attribute grammars for representing symbolic expressions with machine learning techniques (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for efficient expressions. The key insight is that learning from solutions to simpler expressions can improve the search strategy for more complex ones.

The work builds on several strands of previous research: attribute grammars (Knuth, 1968), symbolic computation systems (Maple, Mathematica), and machine learning techniques from NLP (n-grams, recursive neural networks). The authors position their work as filling a gap in applying statistical machine learning to symbolic computation, contrasting with rule-based symbolic systems that do not use learning.

## Strengths

### 1. Novel Application of Machine Learning to Symbolic Computation
The paper makes a compelling case for applying machine learning to a domain traditionally dominated by rule-based symbolic computation. The integration of NLP techniques (n-grams, recursive neural networks) to a symbolic computation problem is innovative and well-motivated. The authors demonstrate that learning from simpler expressions significantly improves the search for efficient identities for more complex ones.

### 2. Well-Structured Methodology
The paper presents a clear and logical progression of ideas:
- Attribute grammar framework for representing expressions
- Numerical and learned representations for expression comparison
- Search strategies guided by machine learning
- Experimental validation on several families of expressions

The methodology is well-organized and logically developed, with each component building on the previous one.

### 3. Strong Experimental Results
The experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over random search, particularly for higher-degree expressions. The paper successfully discovers identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n^3) to O(n^2) for matrix operations, and from exponential to polynomial time for certain expressions. The authors provide specific examples (like Example 2) that clearly illustrate the efficiency gains.

### 4. Thorough Analysis of Limitations
The authors honestly discuss the limitations of their approach, particularly for the most challenging cases (e.g., the RBM-2 family). They acknowledge that their framework cannot solve all problems and provide specific cases where their approach fails, demonstrating scientific rigor.

### 5. Clear Connection to Practical Applications
The authors effectively connect their work to practical machine learning problems, such as efficiently computing dropout or the RBM partition function. This demonstrates the potential real-world impact of their approach.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Overstated Claims About "New" Identities
The paper claims that "all of these examples are too complex to be found manually or by exhaustive search and, as far as we are aware, are previously undiscovered." However, for the specific examples provided (like Example 2), the efficient identities are straightforward generalizations of patterns observed for lower k values. The authors do not provide sufficient evidence that these identities are truly novel to the mathematical community. For instance, the pattern in Example 2 is essentially recognizing that repeated matrix multiplications can be optimized by accumulating intermediate results, a technique that is well-known in linear algebra.

### 2. Limited Scope of the Problem
The paper restricts the problem to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree k with a single matrix variable. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it significantly reduces the generality of their approach. Many practical mathematical expressions involve multiple matrix variables, non-polynomial functions, or non-homogeneous terms. The paper does not convincingly argue why these limitations are necessary or how they could be overcome.

### 3. Insufficient Comparison to Existing Symbolic Computation Tools
The paper mentions that systems like Maple and Mathematica don't seek lower-complexity versions of expressions, but it doesn't provide a rigorous comparison of how their approach would outperform these tools for the specific problems they address. For example, Maple's simplify function can often find equivalent expressions with reduced complexity, and the paper doesn't demonstrate that their approach would be better for the specific cases they consider.

### 4. Questionable Representation Learning
The learned representation using RNNs for symbolic expressions seems to be primarily a tool to enable the search strategy, rather than a fundamental contribution to representation learning. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this representation has broader applicability beyond the specific task of guiding the search for efficient identities. The claim that "this could have broad applicability in allowing machine learning tools to be applied to symbolic computation" is somewhat overstated given the limited scope of the experiments.

### 5. Overemphasis on "Learning" When Simple Pattern Matching Would Suffice
For many of the examples (particularly Examples 1 and 2), the efficient identities follow simple repetitive patterns that could be discovered by straightforward pattern matching or by leveraging known linear algebra optimizations, rather than requiring machine learning. The paper's use of n-gram models and RNNs appears to be an over-engineered solution for problems that could be addressed with simpler techniques.

## Specific Technical Concerns

1. **Numerical Representation**: The use of integer-based descriptors modulo a large prime is a clever solution to the numerical stability problem, but the paper doesn't sufficiently address the potential for false positives. With a finite prime p, there's always a small probability that two different expressions could produce the same descriptor vector modulo p. The paper claims "N must be sufficiently large," but doesn't provide any analysis of how large N needs to be to ensure uniqueness with high probability.

2. **RNN Training Details**: The paper mentions using curriculum learning and specific initialization strategies to avoid overfitting, but doesn't provide sufficient detail about how the training was conducted. For instance, how many training examples were used for each degree k, and how the training data was generated? This makes it difficult to assess whether the RNN learning was truly effective or just memorizing the training examples.

3. **Evaluation Metrics**: The paper evaluates success as "any non-zero count is deemed a success," which is problematic. The paper should report not just whether a solution was found, but how quickly it was found (the time to solution), as this is the key metric for evaluating the effectiveness of the search strategies. The paper does report average times for k=5 in the RBM-2 case, but doesn't provide this for the other cases.

4. **Lack of Theoretical Analysis**: The paper is almost entirely empirical, with no theoretical analysis of the search space, the guarantees of the learning approaches, or the complexity of the proposed methods. For instance, it's unclear how the learning approaches scale with the size of the grammar or the complexity of the target expression.

## Contribution to the Field

The paper makes a modest contribution to the field of symbolic computation by demonstrating how machine learning can be used to guide the search for efficient identities. However, the novelty is somewhat limited because:
- The attribute grammar approach is not new (it's been used in compiler construction and other domains)
- The application of n-grams and RNNs to symbolic computation has been explored before (e.g., in the context of program synthesis)
- The specific problem of finding efficient identities for homogeneous polynomials has been studied in linear algebra

The paper's main contribution is the demonstration that learning from simpler expressions can guide the search for more complex ones, which is valuable but not revolutionary.

## Overall Assessment

### Quality
The paper is technically sound in its methodology and experiments, but the claims about novelty and the significance of the discovered identities are overstated. The experimental results are well-presented but lack sufficient theoretical grounding.

### Clarity
The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with good explanations of the key concepts. The examples (like Example 1 and Example 2) are particularly helpful for understanding the approach.

### Originality
The paper is not highly original. While the specific combination of attribute grammars with machine learning for this problem is novel, the core ideas (using grammars for symbolic computation, applying NLP techniques to symbolic domains) have been explored before. The novelty is more incremental than transformative.

### Significance
The significance of the work is limited by the restricted scope of the problem. While the approach could potentially be useful for specific applications in machine learning (as the authors suggest), the paper doesn't provide enough evidence that it would be widely applicable beyond the specific cases they consider.

## Recommendation

**Weak Accept**

The paper presents a well-executed study of a specific problem in symbolic computation, with clear methodology and interesting results. However, the claims of novelty and significance are overstated, and the scope of the problem is too limited to have broad impact. The paper would be significantly stronger with:
1. More rigorous evidence that the discovered identities are truly novel
2. A more compelling argument for why the learning approach is necessary rather than just convenient
3. A discussion of how the approach could be extended to more general symbolic computation problems
4. A more thorough comparison to existing symbolic computation tools

The paper is a solid technical contribution to a niche problem, but it doesn't advance the field in a way that would make it a major contribution to NeurIPS. It would be more appropriate for a conference focused on symbolic computation or programming languages.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Revise the claims about novelty**: Explicitly state which identities are truly novel (if any), and which are straightforward generalizations of known techniques.

2. **Expand the scope of the problem**: Discuss how the approach could be extended to handle more general expressions (multiple matrix variables, non-polynomial functions).

3. **Provide more detailed comparisons**: Compare the performance of the learning approaches to simpler pattern-matching techniques that might be more efficient for the specific cases considered.

4. **Include theoretical analysis**: Provide some analysis of the search space complexity and the theoretical guarantees of the learning approaches.

5. **Clarify the practical utility**: Provide concrete examples of how the discovered identities could be used in real machine learning systems, rather than just stating they could be used.

6. **Address the false positive concern**: Provide a more detailed analysis of the probability of false positives with the numerical representation method.

This paper demonstrates a promising direction for applying machine learning to symbolic computation, but it needs to be more careful about the claims it makes and the scope of its contributions.
