

# Peer Review: Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The authors propose representing symbolic expressions as grammar trees and using learning techniques (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for efficient versions of target expressions. The core innovation lies in applying learning to the grammar-based search process, enabling the discovery of identities too complex for manual or brute-force approaches. The framework demonstrates significant complexity reductions (e.g., from O(n³) to O(n²)) for various polynomial expressions, with the authors claiming these identities are previously undiscovered.

The work builds on attribute grammars (Knuth, 1968) and borrows techniques from NLP (n-grams, RNNs) to tackle symbolic computation. The authors develop a numerical representation for symbolic expressions to efficiently check equivalence and train an RNN to learn continuous representations of mathematical structures, making symbolic domains accessible to machine learning approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Originality**: The application of learning techniques to guide grammar search for mathematical identities is highly novel. While attribute grammars have been used in other domains, their combination with learning for mathematical identity discovery represents a significant advance. The paper effectively differentiates itself from related work in theorem proving (Bridge et al., 2014) and symbolic computation engines (Maple, Mathematica).

2. **Technical Soundness**: The framework is well-designed with clear methodology. The authors provide thorough experimental evaluation across five families of expressions, demonstrating that learning-based strategies significantly outperform random search for complex expressions. The 3-gram model consistently outperforms the RNN for repetitive patterns (evident in Example 2), which aligns with the paper's core insight about pattern repetition.

3. **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized with clear examples (Examples 1 & 2) that effectively illustrate the approach. The figures (Fig. 1-3) are particularly helpful for understanding the grammar trees and experimental results. The authors clearly explain the numerical representation and RNN training details, making the technical content accessible.

4. **Significance**: The discovered identities have practical relevance to machine learning (e.g., efficient dropout approximation, RBM partition function computation). The framework could be integrated with compilers or optimization tools, suggesting tangible applications beyond theoretical interest. The paper demonstrates a meaningful step toward making symbolic computation more accessible to learning approaches.

## Weaknesses

1. **Scope Limitations**: The paper makes restrictive assumptions (homogeneous polynomials, fixed degree, single matrix/vector inputs) that limit broader applicability. While acknowledged in Section 8, these constraints significantly reduce the generalizability of the approach. A stronger paper would address how the framework could be extended to handle more complex mathematical expressions.

2. **Limited Comparison**: The paper lacks meaningful comparison with existing symbolic computation tools like Mathematica or Maple, which could simplify expressions. While the authors note these systems don't explicitly seek lower-complexity versions, a direct comparison would strengthen their claim of novelty and impact.

3. **RNN Performance**: The RNN approach underperforms compared to n-grams for the majority of cases, with the authors attributing this to the repetitive nature of solutions. The paper could better explain why the RNN didn't generalize as well as expected, especially given the success of RNNs in other symbolic tasks (Socher et al., 2013).

4. **Theoretical Weakness**: There's no theoretical analysis of the search space or guarantees about the completeness of the approach. While empirical results are strong, a brief theoretical discussion would add depth and help readers understand the limitations of the method.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The framework is novel, the experiments are well-designed, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over baseline approaches. The limitations are acknowledged by the authors, but they do somewhat constrain the broader impact of the work.

The paper's most significant contribution is demonstrating how learning can guide symbolic search in a combinatorially challenging space. This approach has potential applications beyond mathematics, such as in compiler optimization or program synthesis. The continuous representation of symbolic expressions via RNNs is particularly noteworthy and could inspire further work in applying machine learning to symbolic domains.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper is technically sound, original, and addresses an important problem with clear potential applications. The limitations are acknowledged and don't detract from the core contribution. The work represents a significant advance in using learning to solve symbolic problems, which aligns well with NIPS's focus on the intersection of machine learning and scientific computation.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a brief comparison with symbolic computation tools (Maple, Mathematica) in the related work section to better establish novelty.

2. In Section 8, expand on how the framework could be extended to handle more general mathematical expressions, acknowledging the challenges.

3. Include a short theoretical discussion about the properties of the search space or the learned representations.

4. Better explain the performance gap between n-grams and the RNN, especially since the paper claims the RNN is a "novel application."

5. Clarify the relationship between this work and the closest related paper [7] mentioned in the related work section.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, and with these minor improvements, it would be an excellent addition to NIPS proceedings.
