## NIPS Review: Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient versions of mathematical identities. The authors leverage attribute grammars to represent symbolic expressions and propose using machine learning – specifically n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) – to guide the search for efficient rewrites. The core idea is to learn from solutions found for simpler expressions to accelerate the discovery process for more complex ones. The authors demonstrate promising results on a set of polynomial expressions, finding identities that are not easily discovered manually and offer significant computational savings.

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the intersection of theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming. It appropriately highlights the limitations of existing approaches (rule-based simplification in systems like Maple/Mathematica) and emphasizes the novelty of applying machine learning to *discover* identities rather than simply selecting among pre-defined heuristics (as in Bridge et al.). The connection to attribute grammars is well-explained, and the authors accurately identify related work in areas like algorithmic optimization using grammars and natural language processing. The references are comprehensive and relevant.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** This work tackles a challenging problem – automated discovery of mathematical identities – with a fresh approach. Combining attribute grammars with machine learning is a significant contribution.
* **Technical Soundness:** The methodology appears logically sound. The use of attribute grammars provides a structured search space, and the learning components (n-grams and RNNs) are well-motivated and implemented. The numerical representation for comparison and the integer-based descriptor to avoid floating-point errors are clever solutions.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling. The demonstrated speedups (O(n3) to O(n2) or better) are substantial.  The examples provided (particularly Example 2) illustrate the power of the approach to find complex identities.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and the contributions.  The inclusion of examples and figures (like the trees in Figure 1) enhances understanding.
* **Code Availability:**  The release of code and data is excellent and crucial for reproducibility and further research.
* **Application Potential:** The potential applications of discovering such identities within compiler optimization and in areas like machine learning (e.g., efficient RBM computations) are valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Grammar Limitations:** The restriction to homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix variable, while justified for tractability, limits the broader applicability of the framework.  The discussion in Section 2 acknowledges this, but further exploration of relaxing these constraints (even if only conceptually) would be beneficial.
* **RNN Performance:** While the RNN demonstrates a continuous representation, its performance appears to be surpassed by the n-gram models in the experiments.  A deeper analysis of *why* the RNN doesn’t perform as well as expected would be valuable.  Is it due to the specific RNN architecture chosen, the training data, or the nature of the problem?
* **Scalability:** Although the learning strategies improve upon random search, the scalability to truly high-degree polynomials (k >> 6) remains a concern. The exponential growth of the search space is mentioned, but a more detailed discussion of the computational limitations and potential strategies to mitigate them would be useful.  Specifically, the search cut-off time of 10 minutes seems arbitrary and its effect on the results isn’t adequately discussed.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The details regarding hyperparameter tuning (e.g., learning rates, dropout rates, n-gram order) are somewhat limited.  More thorough reporting on these aspects would improve reproducibility.
* **RBM-2 Family:** The failure to find solutions for the RBM-2 family beyond k=5 suggests a limitation of the current approach when dealing with highly complex dependencies. Addressing this weakness or elaborating on the challenges posed by this specific family would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* **Highly novel and innovative approach to a challenging problem.**
* **Demonstrated ability to discover non-trivial and useful mathematical identities.**
* **Solid technical foundation and thorough experimental evaluation.**
* **Clear presentation and open-source code promote reproducibility and further research.**
* **Potential for significant impact in areas like compiler optimization and machine learning.**

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Limitations in scalability to very high-degree polynomials.**
* **RNN performance is not competitive with n-gram models.**
* **Restrictions on the grammar limit the broader applicability of the approach.**




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The authors address a fascinating problem with a creative and well-executed approach. While there are some limitations, the novelty, technical soundness, and promising results outweigh these concerns.  I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance to NIPS, **with minor revisions**. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept (with minor revisions)**

**Suggested Revisions:**

*   Provide a more in-depth analysis of the RNN's performance compared to n-grams.
*   Discuss scalability limitations and potential future work to address them.
*   Provide more details on hyperparameter tuning.
*   Elaborate on the challenges posed by the RBM-2 family and potential strategies to overcome them.
*   Clarify the rationale behind the 10-minute search cut-off time and its impact on the reported results.
