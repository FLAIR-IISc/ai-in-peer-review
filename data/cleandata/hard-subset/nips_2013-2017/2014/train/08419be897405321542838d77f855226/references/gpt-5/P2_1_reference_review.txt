Summary
The paper proposes a framework to automatically discover computationally efficient identities for symbolic expressions. Expressions are generated via an attribute grammar; correctness is checked via numerical descriptors computed over random inputs in Zp; and more complex identities are assembled as linear combinations of trees by solving a modular linear system. To avoid exponential blow-up in grammar enumeration, the authors learn search strategies from solutions to simpler targets: (a) n-gram models over subtrees and (b) a recursive neural network that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees and, on top, a softmax that scores next-rule choices. On several families of targets (matrix polynomials, elementwise-augmented polynomials, symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related exponential sums) the learned strategies drastically improve the success rate and speed of finding identities within a fixed time budget, sometimes yielding large asymptotic improvements (e.g., O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)); O(2^n) to O(n^2)/O(n^3) in some cases). The code and identities are made available.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned against theorem proving, program induction, probabilistic programming, and CAS simplification. It differs by focusing on fixed-degree homogeneous polynomials and by using learning-guided search rather than hand-designed heuristics. The use of attribute grammars in search/optimization has precedents; applying them to discover math identities with learned guidance appears novel. The RNN-based representation of symbolic expressions echoes NLP tree models and word embeddings; here the objects are algebraic trees. It would be helpful to position against superoptimization and equality-saturation style rewrite search (which also navigate huge equivalence classes guided by cost), but the general contrast—learning from solved instances instead of manual heuristics—is clear.

Quality
- Soundness: The overall pipeline is sensible: constrained grammar, probabilistic equality checking via randomized evaluation in a finite field, and a linear solver to assemble composite identities. The use of PIT-like random evaluation is standard in spirit, but the paper should more explicitly quantify collision probabilities (e.g., via Schwartz–Zippel style arguments, multiple primes) and clarify how modular coefficients translate back to exact integer/rational identities. As stated, correctness is high-probability rather than formally certified.
- Methods: The learning components (n-grams, RNN embeddings with curriculum, identity initialization, ReLUs, dropout) are appropriate and justified by ablations/anecdotal observations. The RNN classification accuracy on equivalence classes is strong for k up to 6.
- Evaluation: The main metric is “fraction of successful runs within 10 minutes,” plotted versus degree k, with comparisons to a random strategy. While informative, the evaluation would be stronger with: (i) explicit counts and complexity reductions for the discovered identities in the main paper (not only the supplement); (ii) comparisons to stronger non-learning baselines (e.g., grammar-guided search with hand-tuned heuristics, greedy rewrite systems, or CAS plus cost-aware rewrites); (iii) measurements of actual runtime reduction on synthetic inputs for discovered expressions, not only asymptotic claims.
- Limitations are acknowledged: restricted grammar, single variable, scalar outputs, difficulty on RBM-2 for larger k, and reliance on previously solved instances.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The grammar/search framework and the RNN embedding are described clearly, with useful training details (curriculum, initialization, learning rates). The linear-combination solver and the descriptor construction are understandable, though the discussion of correctness and modular arithmetic should be more explicit. Some crucial identities and figures are deferred to the supplement; including at least a few nontrivial examples in the main text would aid comprehension.

Originality
- Applying learned strategies (n-gram over symbolic subtrees; RNN embeddings) to guide search for algebraic identities appears novel.
- Using RNNs to learn continuous representations of algebraic expressions and to predict next grammar rules is original in this domain and connects symbolic math to representation learning.
- The framing of identity discovery as linear combinations of grammar trees is a neat, pragmatic twist.

Significance
- The work addresses a challenging, combinatorial search problem and shows that learning from simpler instances can materially expand the tractable frontier. This is a compelling message for ML-for-systems and ML-for-symbolic domains.
- If robust and broadened, the approach could impact compilers, automatic differentiation, fast ML inference (e.g., closed-form dropout approximations), and CAS systems.
- Current scope is limited: single-variable homogeneous polynomials, modest k, and mixed success on the hardest families. Still, the direction is promising and the code release is valuable for follow-up.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated framework that bridges symbolic computation and learned search.
- Novel continuous embedding of algebraic trees with strong classification accuracy.
- Empirical evidence that learned strategies dramatically outperform random search; simple n-grams already powerful, RNN helps in some hard cases.
- Public code and identities.

Cons
- Correctness is probabilistic; no formal guarantees or certified proofs are provided. The modular linear-combination step needs more rigor on soundness.
- Evaluation relies on a time-budget success metric with limited head-to-head comparisons to stronger baselines.
- Scope restrictions (grammar, single variable, scalar outputs); removal of matrix-matrix multiply to enforce “efficiency” encodes a strong prior that may bias results.
- Limited analysis of search complexity, sample complexity (descriptor size), or generalization beyond the trained families.

Suggestions
- Provide formal bounds on collision probability and certify identities using multiple primes or exact rational arithmetic for final verification.
- Compare to rewrite-based search with cost-driven heuristics and to CAS equipped with cost models.
- Report concrete speedups on instantiated sizes, not only asymptotics, and include representative nontrivial identities in the main text.
- Explore sparsity/regularization in the linear-combination solver and clarify how modular coefficients map to exact expressions.

Overall assessment
A creative and timely contribution that convincingly demonstrates learning-guided discovery of efficient identities within a constrained but meaningful setting. Despite limitations in guarantees and evaluation breadth, the paper meets the NeurIPS criteria reasonably well: technically sound in practice, clearly presented, original in approach, and potentially impactful. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying correctness and strengthening comparisons.
