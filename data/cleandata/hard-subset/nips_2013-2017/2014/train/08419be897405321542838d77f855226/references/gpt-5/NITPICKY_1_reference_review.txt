Summary
The paper proposes a framework to discover computationally efficient identities equivalent to a target symbolic expression. Expressions are generated by an attribute grammar; equality is tested via randomized numerical descriptors over Z_p; and for harder targets, a learned search strategy guides tree construction. Two learning strategies are explored: (i) n-gram models over grammar subtrees, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions and a softmax predictor for next-rule selection. Experiments on several expression families (e.g., sums of powers of A A^T, elementwise-squared variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-inspired exponential sums) report that learned strategies greatly outperform random search for larger degrees k, with n-grams generally beating the RNN. The paper claims discovery of many new identities (supplement).

Strengths
- Ambitious and appealing goal: automated discovery of efficient algebraic rewrites; fits NeurIPS’s interests at the ML-systems/symbolic reasoning interface.
- Clear high-level framework: attribute grammars + learned search; reuses ideas from NLP and program induction.
- The randomized equality test over Z_p and linear-combination approach enable scalable checking and composition.
- Interesting case studies (Examples 1–2), with non-trivial reductions in complexity; plausible applicability to compiler-level optimization or to approximate probabilistic models.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and methodology
- Equality via randomized evaluation: The paper asserts descriptors “uniquely characterize” expressions but provides no probabilistic guarantees. This is essentially polynomial identity testing; please quantify error bounds (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel) for matrices/tensors under your operation set, specify the distribution, modulus size p, and N (number of points), and report collision checks. Without this, both class labels for the RNN and identity verification risk silent errors.
- Linear combinations of trees: You solve F w = t over Z_p and then claim to realize a linear combination of trees. However:
  - Are addition and scalar multiplication by arbitrary integer coefficients part of the grammar? If not, how do you map a solution w back into a valid expression S? If yes, what is the cost model for scalar coefficients and addition? Are negative/rational coefficients allowed?
  - A solution mod p does not necessarily correspond to an integer-weighted combination over reals. How do you lift w from Z_p to Z (or Q) and ensure semantic equivalence over R?
  - If the intent is to find sparse 0/1 combinations, this should be enforced and justified.
- Example 2 dimensionality: The target is sum(sum((A*B)^k)) with A ∈ R^{n×m}, B ∈ R^{m×p}. (A*B)^k requires AB to be square; this is not addressed. The reported complexity O(nm) also seems to ignore k (should be O(k nm) for chained vector–matrix multiplies) unless k is treated as a constant; please clarify assumptions and costs.
- Scheduler and search: The scheduler greedily selects the highest-scoring rule. Is there any backtracking, beam search, or stochasticity to escape local optima? Comparing to simple baselines like beam/MCTS would strengthen claims.
- n-gram strategy: The “10^k” weighting (k used as depth, confusingly overloaded with polynomial degree elsewhere) is ad hoc, with no smoothing/backoff details. Sparse statistics at higher n need principled handling.
- RNN representation and training:
  - The equivalence classes are built using the same randomized descriptor; any label noise propagates through training. Please quantify label noise and its impact.
  - Architecture details are underspecified: optimizer, batch size, epochs, learning-rate schedules for all layers (only the softmax is mentioned), dropout placement and rates during training/inference, and cross-validation protocol for l=30 selection.
  - Generalization assessment: The reported RNN underperforms n-grams for discovery. Provide ablations (with/without pretraining, different l, alternative initializations), and analysis of failure cases.
- Experimental reporting:
  - Success metric: You state that any non-zero count across 10 runs is deemed success, yet also plot the fraction of successful runs. Report time-to-solution distributions (median/variance), and include sensitivity to the 10-minute cutoff, N of sampled points, and random seeds for both the scheduler and descriptors.
  - Missing or unclear baselines: No comparison to CAS heuristics (e.g., Mathematica’s FullSimplify with complexity cost), program synthesis (Sketch/STOKE), or generic search enhancements (beam/MCTS).
  - Removing matrix-multiply from the grammar for some families biases the search and guarantees any found identity is ≤O(n^2); but it also excludes potential identities that temporarily use matmul to enable cancellations. Please justify and discuss missed solutions.

Clarity and presentation
- Several notational and formatting issues impede readability:
  - Inconsistent big-O formatting (e.g., O(()n3)), stray punctuation (i.e. :), inconsistent spacing, and re-use of k for degree and depth.
  - Floor notation b k/2 c is hard to parse; define clearly.
  - Example 2 lacks dimensions ensuring (AB)^k is defined.
  - Footnotes (†, ‡) are awkwardly placed; define Z_p and l selection clearly in-text.
  - Figures/Tables are heavily referenced but insufficiently described in the main text; ensure the paper is self-contained.
- Claims of “previously undiscovered” identities are unsubstantiated; provide verification (e.g., via CAS searches, literature checks), or frame cautiously.

Originality and significance
- The combination of attribute grammars with learned search is interesting, but related search/induction paradigms (e.g., stochastic super-optimization, MCTS for program synthesis) are not compared.
- The RNN embedding for symbolic expressions is a nice idea, but as presented it does not yet outperform simple n-grams; its practical significance is unclear without stronger results or broader tasks.
- Potential application to RBM/dropout is intriguing but remains speculative; no concrete end-to-end speedups or use in real systems are demonstrated.

Minor issues and typos (non-exhaustive)
- “help which the discovery” → “help with the discovery”
- “We start with k = 2 and increase up to k = 15” — specify per-family maxima; Table 3 referenced but not shown.
- Reference list is incomplete/inconsistent (missing years/venues; e.g., Collobert & Weston).

Questions for authors
1. How do you guarantee that solutions to F w = t over Z_p translate to valid real-domain identities with realizable coefficients in the grammar?
2. What are the bounds on descriptor collision probability, and how were p and N selected? Any empirical collision checks?
3. Can you provide beam/MCTS baselines and CAS comparisons?
4. Please clarify the true computational complexity of Example 2 and dimensional constraints.

Recommendation: Weak reject
While the problem and approach are compelling and the framework has promise, there are multiple methodological gaps (probabilistic equality guarantees, mapping mod p solutions back to executable expressions, dimensional/cost inconsistencies), insufficient baselines, and clarity/formatting issues. Addressing these would significantly strengthen the paper.
