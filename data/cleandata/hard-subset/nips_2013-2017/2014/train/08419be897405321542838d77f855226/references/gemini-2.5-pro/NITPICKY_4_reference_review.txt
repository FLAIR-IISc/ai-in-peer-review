This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and to search this space for an identity equivalent to a given target expression but with lower computational complexity. As exhaustive search is intractable, the authors propose using machine learning to guide the search. Specifically, they train n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) on solutions found for simpler expressions to learn a search strategy for more complex targets. A key technical contribution is the use of an RNN to learn a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions, which is validated in a classification task. The authors demonstrate their framework by discovering efficient identities for several families of expressions, some of which are claimed to be novel.

**Strengths**

*   The problem of automatically discovering efficient mathematical identities is both challenging and significant. A successful system could have applications in areas like compiler optimization and machine learning model acceleration.
*   The approach of learning a search policy over a grammar is a strong, contemporary idea, drawing sensible parallels to work in natural language processing and program synthesis.
*   The use of a recursive neural network to learn a continuous embedding for symbolic expressions is novel and well-executed. The high accuracy reported on the expression classification task (Section 7.1) provides compelling evidence that the learned representation captures meaningful semantic properties of the expressions.
*   The overall framework, which combines a grammar, numerical checking via modular arithmetic, and a learnable search strategy, is well-conceived.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising direction, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws, ranging from critical clarity issues to minor but frequent inconsistencies.

1.  **Severe Lack of Clarity in Key Examples and Experiments:** The paper is exceptionally difficult to understand in its most crucial sections, making the main results unverifiable.
    *   **Example 2:** The example is incomprehensible. It states the target is `sum(sum((A*B)k))` for `k=6`, but then provides solutions for `k=2, 3, 4, 5, 6`. The expression for `k=2` involves four matrices (`sum(A,1)`, `B`, `A`, `B`), which does not seem to match a degree-2 target. The expressions for `k=3, 4, ...` appear to be generated by simply appending `* A) * B)` to the previous expression. This seems incorrect and is presented without any explanation, making it impossible to understand what problem is actually being solved.
    *   **Section 7.2:** The descriptions of the five families of expressions are similarly opaque. The notation `bk/2c` is presumably `floor(k/2)`, but this is non-standard. The notation `AAT` is used, whereas matrix transpose was previously denoted with a prime (e.g., `B'`). It is unclear if `sum i,j` refers to the sum of all elements of the resulting matrix. These descriptions are ambiguous to the point that a reader cannot reproduce the target expressions, which is a fatal flaw for an experimental paper.

2.  **Methodological and Analytical Gaps:**
    *   The removal of the matrix-matrix multiply rule for two of the experimental families (Section 7.2) is a strong heuristic that biases the search towards the desired complexity class. While a valid experimental choice, its implications should be discussed more thoroughly. It feels less like a "discovery" and more like a search within a constrained space where efficient solutions are guaranteed to exist if a solution is found at all.
    *   The paper reports that n-gram models outperform the more sophisticated RNN model on several tasks. This is a significant finding that warrants discussion and analysis, but none is provided. Why might a simple count-based model outperform a deep learning architecture designed to capture compositional structure? Without this analysis, the results are merely presented, not understood.
    *   Several hyperparameters are presented without justification. For example, the n-gram model uses a weighting of `10k` (presumably `10^k`). This "magic number" should be justified or ablated.

3.  **Overstated or Unsubstantiated Claims:**
    *   The paper claims the discovery of "many new mathematical identities." However, Example 1 (`sum(sum(A*B))`) is a basic and widely known identity. Without access to the supplementary material, it is difficult to judge the novelty of the other discoveries.
    *   In the discussion, the authors suggest their identities "could potentially be used to ... compute the RBM partition function efficiently (i.e. in polynomial time)." This is an extraordinary claim. The RBM partition function is a #P-hard problem. The paper deals with finding polynomial-time identities for fixed-degree Taylor series terms of the partition function, which is a fundamentally different and much easier problem. This statement is highly misleading and should be corrected to be far more precise.

4.  **Sloppy Presentation and Formatting:** The manuscript is replete with minor errors that collectively undermine its credibility.
    *   **Typos:** E.g., "...can help *which* the discovery..." (Abstract), `O(()n3)` (Section 3).
    *   **Inconsistent Notation:** `B'` vs. `AAT` for transpose; `k*` is used once to denote degree and never explained; `Zp†` contains an unexplained dagger symbol. Spacing around big-O notation is inconsistent (e.g., `O ( n3 )` vs. `O(nmp)`).
    *   **References:** The bibliography is poorly formatted. Several entries have extraneous commas (e.g., `ICML,, ,`), formatting is inconsistent across entries, and details like page numbers or DOIs are often missing.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **For Acceptance:** The paper addresses a novel and important problem. The core idea of using an RNN to learn embeddings for symbolic math expressions is a significant and well-validated contribution.
*   **Against Acceptance:** The paper suffers from critical clarity issues in its experimental description, making the primary results impossible to interpret or verify. It contains overstated claims, lacks important analysis of its own results, and is plagued by numerous presentation errors.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the research direction is promising and some of the technical ideas are strong, the paper in its current form falls far short of the standard for NIPS. The severe clarity issues surrounding the core experiments are a disqualifying flaw. I cannot recommend acceptance until the paper is fundamentally rewritten to clearly and unambiguously describe its experimental setup and results, to temper its claims, and to fix the pervasive formatting and typographical errors. The authors have a strong foundation for an interesting paper, but a major revision is necessary.
