## NIPS Paper Review: Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent mathematical expressions as trees built from an attribute grammar and then use machine learning to guide the search for more efficient equivalent expressions. The authors explore both n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to learn a search strategy, trained on solutions to simpler problems to accelerate discovery in more complex cases. The paper demonstrates success in finding identities that reduce computational complexity, sometimes significantly, and claims many of these identities are novel and difficult to find manually.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work bridges several fields: theorem proving, program induction, symbolic computation, and machine learning. The authors correctly position their work as distinct from traditional theorem provers and symbolic computation engines (like Maple/Mathematica) which rely on rule-based systems rather than learning. They acknowledge relevant work in probabilistic programming and heuristic search (Bridge et al.), but emphasize the novelty of *learning* the search strategy rather than designing it by hand. The paper also draws connections to NLP techniques like n-gram models and recursive neural networks, adapting them for the symbolic domain. The use of attribute grammars, while established in compiler construction, is a novel application to the problem of mathematical identity discovery.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning to discover mathematical identities is a genuinely novel approach. The problem itself – automating the discovery of such identities – is interesting and under-explored.
* **Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling empirical results demonstrating the framework’s ability to find efficient expressions. The examples (specifically Example 1 and Example 2) clearly illustrate the computational benefits achieved. The success on complex expressions (reducing O(n<sup>3</sup>) to O(n<sup>2</sup>), or exponential to polynomial time) is significant.
* **Learned Representation:** The application of a recursive neural network to learn a continuous representation of symbolic expressions is a strong contribution. This representation could potentially unlock further applications of machine learning to symbolic computation.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors explore different learning strategies (n-gram and RNN) and provide a systematic evaluation of their performance across a range of problem families.
* **Code Release:**  The release of code and evaluation data (https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning) is a significant positive aspect, promoting reproducibility and further research.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and methodology.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Grammar Limitations:** The restricted setting (homogeneous polynomials of degree k with a single matrix/vector variable) limits the generality of the approach. While the authors acknowledge this, it’s a substantial limitation.  The scope of expressions is restricted by the grammar rules defined in Table 1. Expanding the grammar could lead to a significant increase in complexity, but also broaden applicability.
* **Scalability:** While the learning strategies improve performance, the scalability to even more complex expressions (higher degree polynomials, multiple variables) is not fully demonstrated. The (RBM-2)k family highlights a case where the learning approaches fail to outperform random search.
* **N-gram Superiority:** The surprising result that n-gram models consistently outperform the RNN in many cases deserves further investigation.  The authors speculate about the repetitive patterns in some families, but a deeper understanding of why the RNN struggles would be valuable.
* **Numerical Stability:** The reliance on numerical representation with modular arithmetic to compare expressions raises concerns about robustness and potential for false positives due to rounding errors or choosing an inappropriate prime number. While the authors address this to some extent, a more detailed analysis of the impact of these choices would be beneficial.
* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper provides empirical evidence, it lacks a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the search space, the convergence properties of the learning algorithms, or the computational complexity of the overall framework.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and potentially impactful approach to the problem of discovering mathematical identities.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements in computational efficiency.
* The learned representation of symbolic expressions is a valuable contribution.
* The code release promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The limited scope of the grammar restricts the generality of the approach.
* The scalability to very complex expressions remains unproven.
* The unexpected performance of n-grams compared to the RNN warrants further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The core idea is novel, the experimental results are promising, and the work is well-presented. While the limitations regarding scope and scalability are noteworthy, they do not overshadow the contributions of the paper.  I believe this work represents a valuable step forward in applying machine learning to symbolic computation and is worthy of acceptance to NIPS. 

**Rating:** Accept (Strongly Consider)

**Confidence:** 8/10
