### Summary

This paper presents a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is equivalent to a given target but has a lower computational complexity. As exhaustive search is intractable, the main contribution is the application of machine learning techniques to guide the search. The authors propose training models on solutions found for simpler expressions to discover identities for more complex ones.

Two learning-based search strategies are explored: an n-gram model that captures common local patterns in successful expression trees, and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation for symbolic expressions. This learned representation is a key contribution in itself, analogous to word embeddings in NLP, and is used to predict the next grammar rule to apply during the search. To verify the equivalence of expressions, the authors use a practical numerical representation, evaluating expressions on random inputs over a finite field. The framework is demonstrated on five families of polynomial expressions, including some relevant to machine learning (e.g., related to Restricted Boltzmann Machines). The results show that the learning-based strategies, particularly the n-gram models, can discover complex identities that are inaccessible to a random search strategy.

This work relates to several fields, including automated theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation. It differentiates itself from traditional symbolic engines like Mathematica or Maple by employing statistical learning to guide the discovery process, rather than relying solely on predefined rewrite rules. The application of NLP techniques, such as n-gram models and RNNs on tree structures (similar to Socher et al., 2013), to the domain of symbolic mathematics is a novel and creative synthesis of ideas.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed framework, combining an attribute grammar with a learning-based search strategy, is well-conceived. The use of a numerical descriptor over a finite field to check for expression equivalence is a clever and pragmatic solution that avoids the complexities of symbolic manipulation. The experimental evaluation is solid, demonstrating a clear advantage of the proposed learning methods over a random baseline across several non-trivial problem families. The authors are transparent about their methodology and provide code, which supports reproducibility.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction provides motivating examples that effectively communicate the problem and the goals of the work. The different components of the system—the grammar, the expression representations, and the search strategies—are explained logically and in sufficient detail. The figures and tables are informative and support the main claims of the paper.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The primary contribution—using machine learning to discover efficient mathematical identities—represents a novel research direction. While attribute grammars and ML techniques like n-grams and RNNs are not new, their application and integration in this symbolic domain are creative and insightful. The development of a continuous, learned representation for mathematical expressions is a significant and novel contribution on its own, potentially opening the door for other ML applications in symbolic reasoning.

**Significance:** The potential significance of this work is high. On a practical level, the ability to automatically find more efficient computations could be integrated into compilers or scientific computing libraries, leading to performance improvements. The discovery of efficient forms for expressions related to RBM partition functions, for instance, could have a direct impact on the machine learning community. More broadly, this work addresses a core AI challenge: navigating combinatorially large search spaces. The successful application of learning to guide this search could inspire similar approaches in other domains like program synthesis or theorem proving. The learned symbolic representation is a valuable contribution that other researchers are likely to build upon.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Scope of Demonstration:** The framework is evaluated on a restricted class of expressions: homogeneous polynomials of a single variable resulting in a scalar. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper would be stronger if it demonstrated or discussed in more detail the path to handling more general expressions, such as those involving multiple variables, non-polynomial functions (e.g., trigonometric, exponential), or conditional logic. This limitation currently narrows the immediate applicability of the discovered identities.

2.  **Relative Performance of Learning Models:** In the main identity discovery task, the simpler n-gram models consistently outperform the more complex and computationally intensive RNN model. While the RNN's success on the expression classification task validates its ability to create a meaningful representation, its weaker performance on the search task is a notable weakness. The paper would benefit from a deeper analysis of why this is the case. It may be that the solutions for the chosen expression families possess a highly repetitive, local structure that is exceptionally well-suited to n-gram models, and the RNN struggles to generalize this specific type of pattern as effectively.

3.  **Comparison to Existing Baselines:** For the family of elementary symmetric polynomials (Symk), the paper notes that they are well-studied. However, it does not compare the complexity of the discovered solutions (e.g., O(n^2) or O(n^3)) to known efficient algorithms, such as those based on Newton's sums, which can compute all elementary symmetric polynomials in O(n^2) or faster. Such a comparison would be crucial for contextualizing the "efficiency" of the discovered identities and for benchmarking the system against established methods in computer algebra.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **High Novelty and Creativity:** The paper introduces a novel problem setting and a creative solution, bridging symbolic computation and modern machine learning.
*   **Significant Potential Impact:** The work could inspire a new line of research in symbolic mathematics and has the potential for practical applications in compilers and scientific computing. The learned representation for symbolic expressions is a standalone contribution of high value.
*   **Strong Technical Execution:** The framework is well-designed, and the experiments convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the core idea. The paper is clear and well-written.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   **Limited Scope:** The experiments are confined to a narrow class of polynomials, which may limit the perceived generality and immediate impact of the method.
*   **Underperformance of the RNN:** One of the main technical contributions, the RNN-based search strategy, is shown to be less effective than simpler n-gram models, which could be seen as weakening the paper's claims about the utility of this more sophisticated approach for this task.
*   **Lack of Comparison to Computer Algebra Methods:** The paper misses an opportunity to compare its results against established algorithms for well-known problems like symmetric polynomials, making it difficult to fully assess the state-of-the-art advancement.

**Conclusion:**
Despite the weaknesses regarding the scope and the relative performance of the RNN, the strengths of this paper in terms of originality, potential significance, and technical quality are overwhelming. It introduces a genuinely new and exciting research direction. This work is likely to be highly influential and to stimulate significant follow-up research. Therefore, I strongly recommend acceptance.
