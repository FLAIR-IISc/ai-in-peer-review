Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the problem of discovering computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given symbolic expression. The authors formalize a search space via an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic and elementwise operations, and seek lower-complexity realizations of a fixed-degree target polynomial. Because exhaustive enumeration is infeasible, they (i) represent expressions either by randomized numerical descriptors over Z_p (enabling fast equivalence checks and linear combination solving) or via a learned continuous embedding from a recursive neural network (RNN); and (ii) learn search strategies—n-gram models over subtrees and an RNN-based policy—to guide a scheduler through the grammar. They demonstrate discovery of identities that reduce naive O(n^3) computations to O(n^2) in matrix settings (e.g., sum(sum(A*B))) and polynomial-time reductions for some exponentially large sums related to RBM partition-function terms. The RNN learns an embedding that classifies expression-equivalence classes with low error and is used to score rule choices during search. Experiments compare random, n-gram, and RNN-guided search on five expression families; n-gram models typically outperform the RNN, and both beat random on harder instances. Code and data are released.

This sits at the intersection of program induction, automated theorem proving, and symbolic algebra, but departs from rule-based CAS heuristics by learning the search policy from solutions to simpler instances. The randomized equivalence check is closely related to polynomial identity testing (PIT) via Schwartz–Zippel-style evaluations. Prior use of attribute grammars for optimization exists (e.g., media processing, knapsack), and RNNs for tree structures are widely explored in NLP; here they are adapted to algebraic expression trees.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The overall framework is coherent: a constrained grammar defines the search space; randomized equivalence checks allow fast acceptance; linear combinations of trees solve more complex targets; and learned policies reduce search. However, several aspects would benefit from stronger foundations:
  - Equivalence verification uses random evaluations mod p without an explicit collision bound or failure probability; the connection to PIT is implicit but not formalized.
  - There is no proof or certification that the found expressions are minimally complex; the complexity model is coarse (e.g., removal of matrix-matrix multiply to force O(n^2)).
  - Empirical evaluation uses success rates under a 10-minute cutoff without reporting confidence bounds on identity correctness beyond the randomized test, nor comparisons to symbolic baselines.
- Empirical support: The paper reports multiple nontrivial identities, scalability trends with k, and an informative negative result on RBM-2 where learning helps moderately but does not crack k > 5. The RNN embedding achieves ≤5% classification error on equivalence classes up to k=6.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with clear motivation and examples (especially Example 1–2). Useful engineering details for RNN training are provided. Some clarity gaps remain:
- The grammar (Table 1) and its complexity model are critical; more explicit listing and justification would help.
- The descriptor size N, choice of prime p, and bounds for false positives in the Z_p test should be stated.
- The linear-combination solver section could more clearly explain when a scalar target requires combinations and how M and N are chosen.
- Many identities are deferred to the supplement; include at least a few representative ones in the main text with a brief complexity analysis.

Originality
Framing identity discovery as a learned search over an attribute grammar and using RNN embeddings of algebraic expressions is novel and intriguing. The work combines ideas from NLP (n-grams, RNNs) with symbolic computation in a new way. Discovering previously undocumented efficiency identities is a notable contribution. That said, some techniques (randomized equivalence testing, grammar-based program search) have precursors, and the best-performing learned strategy is a relatively simple n-gram model.

Significance
The problem is important: neural-guided search in combinatorial symbolic spaces is broadly relevant. The ability to automatically derive faster equivalents of common ML-related expressions (matrix sums, symmetric polynomials, RBM terms) could matter for compilers and systems. The current scope is limited (homogeneous polynomials, single variable, scalar outputs), and the evaluation leaves open how often such identities arise in practice and how far the approach scales. Still, this is a promising direction that could catalyze further work.

Pros
- Clear and timely problem; compelling bridge between learning and symbolic search.
- Well-designed framework with both randomized and learned representations.
- Discovery of nontrivial, efficiency-improving identities; code released.
- RNN embedding of expressions yields strong equivalence classification accuracy.
- Learning substantially improves search over random on several families.

Cons
- Restrictive setting (homogeneous polynomials, single variable, scalar target); limited evidence of generality.
- No formal PIT-based collision/failure probability; no symbolic certification of reported identities.
- Weak baselines: no comparison to CAS simplification/factorization, common-subexpression elimination, or modern rewriting/equality-saturation methods.
- RNN guidance underperforms n-grams; limited analysis of why and of architecture alternatives.
- Evaluation metric (fraction of runs under a time cap) is coarse; no analysis of search efficiency (nodes expanded, branching factor) or minimality of found solutions.

Suggestions for improvement
- Formalize the equivalence check with PIT bounds (Schwartz–Zippel) and report the failure probability given N and p. For reported identities, provide a symbolic verification (or a certificate) in the supplement.
- Add baselines: CAS simplification/factorization (Maple/Mathematica), heuristic algebraic rewriting, and simple compiler-style optimizations; report runtime and complexity of their outputs.
- Provide search diagnostics (expanded nodes, beam width, branching factors) and ablations (curriculum, identity initialization, dropout).
- Evaluate generalization: train policies on one family and test on another; report success vs k and vs time.
- Explore stronger search policies (beam search, MCTS, heuristic cost-to-go estimates), and richer architectures (tree transformers, gated RNNs) with explicit structure sharing.
- Expand the grammar incrementally (multiple variables, non-homogeneous terms) and show at least one multi-variable case beyond A,B examples.
- Report constant-factor speedups and memory costs for the discovered identities on actual numerical kernels.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a novel and promising approach to learned discovery of efficient mathematical identities, with credible empirical evidence and open-source release. While the scope and evaluation have limitations and the theoretical underpinnings of equivalence checking should be strengthened, the contribution is sufficiently original and potentially impactful for NeurIPS. Strengthening the baselines, formal guarantees, and analysis would further solidify the case.
