### **Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to define a space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then use machine learning to guide the search through this combinatorially large space. The authors frame the problem as finding an expression S that is equivalent to a target expression T but has a lower computational complexity.

The framework has two key technical components. First, it requires a method to represent and check the equivalence of expressions. The authors propose two: a robust numerical "descriptor" based on evaluating expressions on random inputs in a finite field (Zp) to avoid floating-point issues, and a learned continuous representation using a recursive neural network (RNN). Second, to overcome the intractability of brute-force search, they propose learning a search strategy. Simple solutions found for low-degree polynomials are used as training data to learn policies (based on n-gram models or the RNN) that can then discover solutions for more complex, higher-degree targets.

This work sits at a creative intersection of symbolic computation, program induction, and machine learning, drawing heavily on techniques from Natural Language Processing. While symbolic engines like Mathematica use rule-based simplification, this paper's main contribution is the *learning* of a search strategy from data, rather than relying on hand-crafted heuristics. It relates to program induction but constrains the problem to a tractable (though still exponential) space of fixed-degree polynomials, avoiding issues like the halting problem. The application of n-gram models and particularly the RNN to parse trees of mathematical expressions is a clever adaptation of methods from NLP (e.g., Socher et al., 2013), and the idea of a continuous embedding for symbolic expressions is analogous to word embeddings (e.g., Mikolov et al., 2013).

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the work appears complete.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The methodology is well-conceived. The use of an attribute grammar provides a principled way to define the search space.
    *   The numerical representation using integer arithmetic modulo a prime is a clever and robust solution to the expression equivalence problem, avoiding the pitfalls of symbolic manipulation speed and floating-point inaccuracies.
    *   The experimental evaluation is thorough. The authors test their method on five non-trivial families of expressions, demonstrating that the learning-based strategies consistently and significantly outperform a random search baseline as problem complexity grows (Fig. 3).
    *   The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach, noting where it failed (e.g., for the `(RBM-2)k` family for k > 5) and which models performed best.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The success of the RNN seems highly dependent on a specific set of training tricks (identity initialization, curriculum learning, differential learning rates). This may suggest that the learned representation is somewhat brittle and could be difficult to apply to new grammars or problem domains without significant expert tuning.
    *   It is interesting that the simpler n-gram models outperform the more complex RNN on most tasks. While the authors present this result clearly, the paper would benefit from a deeper discussion as to *why* this might be the case. Perhaps the solutions possess highly local, repetitive structures that are captured well by n-grams, while the RNN struggles to generalize or overfits.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The introduction effectively motivates the problem with concrete, easy-to-understand examples (Example 1 & 2).
    *   The paper is structured logically, moving from the problem definition to the grammar, representation, search strategies, and finally experiments. This makes the entire framework easy to follow.
    *   The description of the numerical representation and the search strategies is particularly clear.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The description of the RNN architecture in Section 4.2 could be slightly more detailed. For instance, a small diagram illustrating how the weight tensors/matrices combine the vector representations for a single operation would be helpful for reader comprehension.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The central idea of *learning to discover mathematical identities* is novel and ambitious. While its components are drawn from different fields, their synthesis into this framework for this specific purpose is new.
    *   The application of an RNN to learn a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions is a significant and novel contribution. This opens the door to applying a wide range of continuous-space machine learning models to symbolic domains.
    *   The paper delivers on its promise by discovering what appear to be new, non-trivial mathematical identities, which is a concrete and impressive result.

**Significance:** The paper has the potential for significant impact.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The work provides a powerful proof-of-concept for how machine learning can guide search in vast, symbolic, combinatorial spaces—a core challenge in AI. The success here could inspire similar approaches in automated theorem proving, program synthesis, and other reasoning tasks.
    *   The discovery of efficient polynomial-time identities for expressions with naive exponential complexity (e.g., the RBM-related families) could have direct practical applications in machine learning and other scientific fields.
    *   The idea of automatically finding efficient computations could be integrated into compilers or scientific computing libraries, potentially leading to significant performance optimizations without human intervention.
    *   The learned representation for symbolic math is a contribution that other researchers can build upon for a variety of tasks beyond identity discovery.

### **Final Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that presents a highly novel and creative fusion of ideas from symbolic computation, grammars, and machine learning. It tackles a difficult and important problem with a well-designed framework and provides compelling empirical evidence of its success. The discovery of new, useful mathematical identities is a standout result. While the current scope is restricted to polynomials and the RNN training seems delicate, these are minor limitations in what is otherwise a significant, high-quality, and thought-provoking contribution. The work is likely to be influential and inspire follow-up research. This paper is a clear accept for NIPS.
