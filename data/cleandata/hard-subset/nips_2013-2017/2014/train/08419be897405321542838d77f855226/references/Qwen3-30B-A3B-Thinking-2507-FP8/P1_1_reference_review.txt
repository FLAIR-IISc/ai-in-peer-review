

# Peer Review: "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors represent mathematical expressions as trees using an attribute grammar, where each grammar rule has an associated computational complexity attribute. They then use machine learning (specifically n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for expressions that compute the same result as a target expression but with lower computational complexity. The framework is applied to discover new identities for several families of mathematical expressions, including cases where the naive computation would be O(n³) but can be reduced to O(n²), and cases where exponential-time computation can be reduced to polynomial time.

The paper makes several key contributions: (1) a grammar framework for finding efficient versions of symbolic expressions, (2) the integration of machine learning techniques into this framework, (3) a novel application of recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of mathematical structures, and (4) the discovery of new mathematical identities with significant computational benefits.

The work is positioned at the intersection of machine learning, symbolic computation, and compiler design, with clear connections to prior work in attribute grammars (Knuth, 1968), program synthesis, and natural language processing.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors have developed a clear framework with well-defined components (grammar, representations, search strategies) and thoroughly evaluated their approach across multiple mathematical families. The experimental results (Table 2 and Figure 3) provide strong empirical evidence that learning-based search strategies significantly outperform random search for complex targets.

The paper acknowledges its limitations, particularly the restriction to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, and discusses how this constraint could be relaxed in future work. The authors also provide a thoughtful comparison to related work, distinguishing their approach from theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming.

One area for improvement would be deeper theoretical analysis of why certain learning approaches work better than others for specific expression families. The paper demonstrates that n-grams work well for repetitive patterns (like Example 2) while RNNs perform better on more complex patterns, but doesn't provide deeper theoretical justification for these observations.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and well-organized. The problem statement is clear, and the technical details of the grammar framework and representation learning are explained thoroughly. The examples (Examples 1 and 2) are particularly effective at illustrating the core concepts. The figures (Figures 1-3) are well-designed and help clarify the methodology and results.

The paper could be improved by providing more detail on the numerical representation with integer modulo p. Specifically, it would be helpful to know how the choice of prime p affects the results, and why a larger p is needed for higher-degree expressions. The paper mentions using "a large prime number" but doesn't elaborate on the criteria for choosing p or how this choice impacts the computational complexity of the approach.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by applying machine learning techniques to a problem that has traditionally been approached with rule-based methods. The specific integration of attribute grammars with machine learning for discovering efficient mathematical identities is novel. The authors' approach of learning a continuous representation for symbolic expressions is particularly innovative and has broader potential applications beyond the specific problem of identity discovery.

The paper is clearly differentiated from related work. While there has been work on theorem proving and program induction, this paper focuses specifically on discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques, which is a new direction. The connection to natural language processing (n-grams, RNNs) is well-justified and represents a meaningful transfer of techniques from one domain to another.

## Significance

The significance of this work is twofold: first, the discovery of new mathematical identities with reduced computational complexity has potential practical applications in areas like machine learning (e.g., for efficient computation of dropout or RBM partition functions). The authors note that some of the families considered are close to expressions encountered in machine learning, which suggests potential for real-world impact.

Second, the paper demonstrates a promising approach for using machine learning to guide combinatorial search, which has broader implications for AI. The authors note that "the problem addressed in this paper involves discrete search within a combinatorially large space – a core problem with AI. Our successful use of machine learning to guide the search gives hope that similar techniques might be effective in other AI tasks where combinatorial explosions are encountered."

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Well-defined problem with clear technical contributions
- Novel integration of attribute grammars with machine learning
- Thorough experimental evaluation across multiple families of expressions
- Discovery of new mathematical identities with significant computational benefits
- Clear and well-organized writing
- Good comparison to related work with clear differentiation

**Weaknesses:**
- Restrictive problem formulation (homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree) limits the broader applicability
- Limited discussion of practical applications beyond the specific mathematical identities
- Insufficient detail on the numerical representation with integer modulo p
- The 10-minute time limit for each run might be too short for some complex cases, and the paper doesn't fully discuss how this affects results
- Limited discussion of how the discovered identities could be integrated into real-world systems

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NeurIPS. It makes a significant technical contribution to the field of machine learning and symbolic computation, with well-designed experiments and clear results. The paper is well-written and addresses a challenging problem with a novel approach. While the problem formulation is restrictive, the authors acknowledge this limitation and the results are still highly valuable for the specific problem they address.

The paper would benefit from a bit more discussion of the practical implications of the discovered identities, but this is a minor issue. Overall, this is a strong submission that advances the state of the art in a meaningful way.

## Additional Comments for Authors

1. Consider providing more detail on the choice of prime p for the numerical representation. How does the size of p affect the accuracy of the representation? Is there a theoretical basis for choosing a particular size of p?

2. The paper mentions that the RNN-based representation can be used to enable many symbolic tasks to be posed as machine learning problems. It would be helpful to provide a specific example of another symbolic task that could benefit from this representation.

3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly with respect to the size of the grammar and how it scales with more complex expressions.

4. The authors note that the (RBM-2)^k family is the most challenging, with no solutions found for k > 5. It would be interesting to discuss why this family is particularly challenging and whether there are any theoretical reasons for this difficulty.

5. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the discovered identities could be integrated into existing machine learning frameworks or compilers, beyond the brief mention of RBM partition functions.

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The authors have successfully demonstrated that machine learning can be effectively used to guide combinatorial search in symbolic domains, opening up new possibilities for future research.
