## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper tackles the interesting and challenging problem of automatically discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The core idea – leveraging learned patterns from simpler expressions to guide the search for optimizations in more complex ones – is novel and potentially impactful. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and explore both n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to improve the search process. 

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing symbolic computation systems and the potential benefits of a learning-based approach. The experimental setup is sound, demonstrating the ability to discover identities for expressions where naive evaluation is computationally expensive. The examples provided (e.g., reducing O(n³) to O(n²)) are compelling and showcase the potential of the approach. The inclusion of code and data is a significant positive, promoting reproducibility. 

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the RNN is introduced, its performance is consistently outperformed by simpler n-gram models. The authors acknowledge this but don't offer a thorough analysis of *why* the RNN isn't more effective. A deeper dive into the learned representations and potential architectural improvements would be valuable. The scope of the grammar is also somewhat restrictive, limiting the types of identities that can be discovered. While acknowledged, a more detailed discussion of how to expand the grammar and address the limitations would strengthen the work. The experimental evaluation, while solid, focuses on a limited set of expression families. Expanding this to include more diverse examples would increase confidence in the generalizability of the approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of machine learning to symbolic identity discovery.
*   Demonstrated ability to find identities beyond the reach of brute-force search.
*   Clear presentation and well-defined experimental setup.
*   Publicly available code and data.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   RNN performance is consistently lower than n-gram models.
*   Limited scope of the grammar and expression families tested.
*   Lack of in-depth analysis of RNN performance and potential improvements.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a promising approach to a difficult problem. While the RNN component doesn’t fully deliver on its potential, the overall framework and the success of the n-gram models are significant contributions. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more thorough analysis of the RNN results and a discussion of future work expanding the grammar and exploring more diverse expression families. The potential impact of this work warrants its inclusion in the conference.
