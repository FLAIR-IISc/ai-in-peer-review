This paper explores the application of machine learning to discover efficient mathematical identities. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and propose using n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to guide a search for computationally cheaper, yet equivalent, expressions. The core idea of learning from simpler cases to tackle more complex ones is compelling, and the demonstrated results – finding identities not easily obtainable manually or through exhaustive search – are significant.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars with machine learning for discovering mathematical identities appears genuinely novel. While attribute grammars have been used in various contexts (compiler construction, bioinformatics), their application to this specific problem, coupled with learning techniques, is a strong contribution.
* **Significance:** The successful discovery of identities that reduce computational complexity is important. The examples provided (O(n3) to O(n2)) are concrete and demonstrate the potential practical impact. The claim that these identities are previously undiscovered adds further weight.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed framework appears well-defined. The use of integer-based descriptors to address potential floating-point inaccuracies is a thoughtful design choice. The explanation of the RNN architecture and training process is clear. The experiments are reasonably well-designed, exploring different search strategies and varying the complexity of the target expressions.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly states the problem and contributions. The examples (though potentially needing further elaboration, see weaknesses) help illustrate the core concept. The notation is reasonably clear.
* **Code Availability:** Providing the code and evaluation data (https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning) is a major plus, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Example Elaboration:** While Examples 1 & 2 are presented, they feel somewhat hand-wavy. A more detailed explanation of *how* the framework discovers the efficient identity for each example would be beneficial. Showing the grammar trees (beyond the final efficient form) would illuminate the search process. For Example 2, the repeated structure is evident, but the explanation doesn't clearly link this to the n-gram model’s success.
* **Limited Scope:** The problem is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of degree k with a single matrix variable. While acknowledged by the authors, the limitations are significant. The discussion of generalizing to more complex cases (recursion, trigonometric functions) feels brief. Addressing the challenge of terminating the search for more general grammars is crucial.
* **RNN Performance:** The RNN approach doesn't consistently outperform the n-gram models. The explanation attributes this to the repetitive structure of some expressions being better captured by n-grams. However, a deeper analysis of *why* the RNN struggles in certain cases would be valuable. Investigating different RNN architectures or training strategies might improve performance. The impact of dropout rate appears significant, further investigation is warranted.
* **Related Work:** The related work section could be more comprehensive. The connection to symbolic regression, though not explicitly mentioned, seems relevant.  Exploring recent advances in neural symbolic methods might strengthen the positioning of the work.  Specifically, the connection to differentiable programming and automatic differentiation techniques could be discussed, as these also aim to manipulate symbolic expressions.
* **Linear Combination Solver:** The efficiency of the linear system solver (Gaussian elimination) isn’t discussed. For very high-degree polynomials, the system could become computationally expensive to solve. Exploring alternative solvers or approximation techniques might be necessary.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a novel and challenging problem with a technically sound and well-motivated approach.
* The demonstrated results are significant, showcasing the discovery of new mathematical identities with practical benefits.
* The code and data availability promote reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The limited scope of the problem and the relatively modest performance of the RNN approach raise concerns about the generalizability of the framework.
* The examples, while illustrative, lack sufficient detail to fully understand the search process.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The idea of using machine learning to discover mathematical identities is innovative and has the potential to impact various areas. While the limitations are acknowledged, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding example elaboration, RNN performance analysis, and related work discussion.  Specifically, the authors should consider expanding the examples with visual representations of the search tree exploration and a deeper analysis of the RNN's shortcomings.
