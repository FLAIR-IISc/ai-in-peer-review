- Proposes a novel framework using an attribute grammar to search for mathematically equivalent expressions with lower computational complexity.  
- Uses machine learning (n-gram models and a recursive neural network) trained on simpler identities to guide combinatorial search.  
- Bridges symbolic computation, program induction, and statistical ML, learning search heuristics rather than relying on hand-coded rules like Mathematica.  
- Original and significant: RNN provides a continuous representation of symbolic expressions and the approach is highly novel.  
- Strong empirical results: discovers new non-trivial identities that in some cases reduce exponential-time to polynomial-time computations.  
- Technical quality is high: sound methods and well-designed experiments; learning-based strategies outperform random search.  
- Paper is clear and well-written, with useful motivating examples and model/grammar descriptions.  
- Limitation: scope restricted to homogeneous polynomials; extension to other function classes (trigonometric, recursive) needs discussion.  
- Limitation: simpler n-gram models often outperform the RNN; deeper analysis on why RNN fails to capture necessary long-range dependencies is needed.  
- Overall recommendation: significant contribution likely to inspire follow-up work; strongly recommended for acceptance.
