

This paper proposes an interesting application of machine learning to the problem of discovering efficient mathematical identities. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and develop learning-based strategies to guide the search for computationally efficient expressions that are analytically equivalent to a target expression. The key insight is that brute-force search becomes intractable for complex expressions, but learning from simpler expressions can guide the search toward promising regions of the space.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) a grammar framework for symbolic expressions with computational complexity attributes; (2) two learning approaches (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search strategy; (3) a novel application of RNNs to learn continuous representations of mathematical structures; and (4) several previously undiscovered mathematical identities with significant computational complexity improvements.

The quality of the paper is strong. The technical approach is sound, with clear theoretical foundations in attribute grammars and well-motivated learning strategies. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with careful ablation studies comparing the random, n-gram, and RNN strategies across multiple families of expressions. The results demonstrate that the learning approaches significantly outperform random search for complex expressions, with the 3-gram model particularly effective for repetitive patterns. The paper also provides a clear explanation of the numerical representation technique using integer descriptors modulo a large prime to avoid floating-point issues.

Clarity is excellent. The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the problem formulation, grammar definition, and learning approaches. The examples (particularly Examples 1 and 2) effectively illustrate the core concepts. The writing is precise and accessible to readers familiar with machine learning concepts.

Originality is strong. While attribute grammars have been used in other domains (e.g., compiler construction, bioinformatics), the authors demonstrate a novel application to discovering mathematical identities. The use of machine learning to guide the search within the grammar space represents a fresh perspective on a classical symbolic computation problem. The paper also properly contextualizes its work relative to related fields like theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation engines, while clearly differentiating its approach.

Significance is moderate. The paper demonstrates concrete computational improvements for specific classes of expressions, which could be valuable for optimization in mathematical software. The discovery of previously unknown identities with complexity reductions (e.g., from O(n³) to O(n²)) is noteworthy. However, the scope is limited to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with a single matrix variable, which may restrict broader applicability. The connection to machine learning applications (e.g., dropout, RBM partition function) is promising but not fully developed.

The paper could be strengthened by: (1) providing more concrete examples of how these discovered identities could be integrated into existing mathematical software or machine learning frameworks; (2) discussing the limitations of the current grammar framework more thoroughly and suggesting potential extensions to handle more complex mathematical structures; (3) including a more detailed analysis of why the RNN performed worse than n-grams for the repetitive patterns in Examples 1 and 2, despite the authors' claim that RNNs are better suited for capturing structural patterns.

The most significant weakness is the limited scope of the problem domain. While the authors acknowledge this in Section 8, the paper doesn't sufficiently explore how the framework could be extended to handle more general mathematical expressions. The RBM-2 family (which involves double exponential sums) proved particularly challenging, with no solutions found for k > 5, suggesting the current framework may not scale to the most complex expressions.

The paper is well-positioned to contribute to the field of symbolic computation by bridging machine learning and mathematical discovery. It demonstrates that machine learning can effectively guide combinatorial search in symbolic domains, which has broader implications for AI. The authors have made their code and data publicly available, which is highly valuable for reproducibility.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper with a novel approach to an interesting problem. The technical contributions are sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results are significant for the specific problem domain. While the scope is limited, the framework provides a promising foundation for future work in machine learning for symbolic mathematics.

Pro: Novel application of machine learning to symbolic mathematics; effective demonstration of complexity improvements; clear framework and implementation; well-written with good examples.

Con: Limited scope of problem domain; some results (RBM-2) show limited success; connection to broader machine learning applications could be strengthened.

I recommend acceptance, as this is a valuable contribution to the field of symbolic computation that demonstrates the potential of machine learning to guide combinatorial search in mathematical discovery.
