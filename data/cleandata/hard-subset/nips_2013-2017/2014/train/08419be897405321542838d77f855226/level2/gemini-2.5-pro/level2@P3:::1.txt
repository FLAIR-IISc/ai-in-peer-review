### NIPS 2014 Review

**Summary**
This paper presents a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient, yet analytically equivalent, mathematical identities for a given target expression. The core approach is to define a space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar of mathematical operations. The primary challenge is the combinatorial explosion in the number of possible expression trees. To address this, the work proposes guiding the search through this space using machine learning models. These models, including n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN), are trained on solutions found for simpler expressions to learn heuristics for discovering more complex ones.

The framework uses two representations for symbolic expressions: a numerical representation based on evaluation over random integers modulo a prime (used to efficiently check for equivalence between expressions), and a learned continuous representation generated by an RNN. The work demonstrates that this learning-guided search can discover non-trivial identities for several families of expressions, including some related to machine learning models like RBMs, significantly reducing their computational complexity from exponential or high-order polynomial time to low-order polynomial time. The work relates its contributions to the fields of theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation, while drawing methodological inspiration from natural language processing (n-gram models, RNNs for parsing, and learned embeddings).

**Quality**
The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and well-executed piece of research. The proposed framework, which integrates a generative grammar, a verification module (numerical representation and linear solver), and a learned search strategy, is coherent and well-motivated. The experimental design is solid: the learning models are trained in a curriculum-like fashion on simpler problems to solve harder ones, which is a sensible approach. The results are well-supported by the experiments, which clearly demonstrate that the learning-based strategies significantly outperform a random search baseline, particularly as the complexity of the target expressions grows. The inclusion of implementation details crucial for training the RNN, such as identity initialization and curriculum learning, adds to the work's reproducibility and value.

**Clarity**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem is introduced effectively with compelling examples that make the motivation and potential impact immediately apparent. The different components of the system—the attribute grammar, the numerical and learned representations, and the search strategies—are explained logically and in sufficient detail for an expert to understand the methodology. The figures and tables are informative and support the main text well. In particular, Figure 1 helps visualize the expression trees, and Figure 3 provides a clear comparison of the different search strategies.

**Originality**
The work is highly original. While areas like automated theorem proving and program synthesis have a long history, the application of modern statistical machine learning techniques, especially deep learning models like RNNs, to discover new mathematical identities appears to be a novel contribution. The idea of learning search heuristics from simpler problems to solve more complex symbolic ones is powerful. The development of a continuous vector representation for symbolic mathematical expressions using an RNN is itself a creative and valuable idea, drawing a clever parallel to word embeddings in NLP and opening the door for other learning-based approaches in symbolic domains. The paper does a good job of positioning itself relative to related work in symbolic computation and NLP, clearly articulating its unique contributions.

**Significance**
The results presented are important and likely to be influential. The framework demonstrates a practical path toward automating the discovery of optimizations that are often beyond the reach of human intuition or existing rule-based symbolic manipulation systems. The discovered identities, particularly those related to RBM partition functions, could have direct practical applications in the machine learning community. More broadly, this work represents a successful bridge between the domains of symbolic reasoning and statistical machine learning. It suggests that learning-based methods can be a powerful tool for navigating the vast combinatorial search spaces inherent in many fundamental AI problems. Other researchers are likely to build upon these ideas, both by extending this framework and by applying similar principles to other symbolic search and reasoning tasks.

**Suggestions for Improvement**
While the work is strong, a few points could be clarified or expanded upon to further improve the manuscript.

1.  **Scope and Generalization:** The framework is currently demonstrated on a specific class of expressions (homogeneous polynomials of a single variable). A more detailed discussion of the primary obstacles and potential solutions for extending the approach to a broader set of mathematical operations (e.g., trigonometric, exponential, recursive functions) and data structures would strengthen the paper's outlook.
2.  **Analysis of Model Performance:** An interesting and somewhat surprising result is that n-gram models outperform the more sophisticated RNN model on several of the tasks. The paper would benefit from a deeper analysis of why this might be the case. It could be that the structure of the discovered solutions is highly repetitive and local, a pattern that n-grams are perfectly suited to capture. Understanding the relative strengths and weaknesses of these models could provide valuable insights for future work in this area.
3.  **Claims of Novelty for Identities:** The paper claims the discovery of "previously undiscovered" identities. This is a strong claim. It might be more precise to state that these identities are not present in standard symbolic algebra packages or are not widely known in the literature, as a comprehensive proof of non-existence is very difficult. This is a minor point of phrasing but would make the claims more robust.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Presents a highly novel and creative application of machine learning to the domain of symbolic mathematics.
*   **Pro:** The proposed framework is technically sound, well-designed, and successfully discovers complex, non-trivial mathematical identities.
*   **Pro:** The experimental results are convincing and clearly demonstrate the value of learning-guided search over random exploration.
*   **Pro:** The work has high potential significance, both for its specific discoveries and for pioneering a new direction at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic reasoning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The evaluation is confined to a relatively narrow class of mathematical expressions.
*   **Con:** The paper could provide a more detailed analysis of the comparative performance of the different learning models.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It addresses a fascinating problem with a novel and effective approach, and the results are significant. This work is a strong candidate for acceptance and is likely to be of great interest to the NIPS community.
