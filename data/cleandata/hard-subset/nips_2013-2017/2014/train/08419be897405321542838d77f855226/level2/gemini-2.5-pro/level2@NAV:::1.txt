This paper presents a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is analytically equivalent to a target expression but has lower computational complexity. The main contribution lies in demonstrating that this combinatorial search, which is intractable for complex expressions, can be effectively guided by machine learning models. Specifically, the authors train n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) on solutions to simpler identities to find patterns that help solve more complex ones. This work cleverly bridges the domains of symbolic computation, program induction, and statistical machine learning, distinguishing itself from prior rule-based systems like Mathematica by learning search heuristics automatically.

The paper has several significant strengths.
**Originality and Significance:** The application of modern ML techniques, particularly the RNN for learning a continuous representation of symbolic expressions, to this classic symbolic problem is highly original. The results are impressive; the framework discovers new, non-trivial identities for several families of expressions, some of which reduce exponential-time computations to polynomial time. This represents a demonstrable advance in automated symbolic reasoning.
**Quality:** The technical approach is sound, and the experiments are well-designed. The comparison of the learning-based strategies against a random search baseline clearly validates the central claim that learning is crucial for tackling complex problems. The paper is a complete piece of work, moving from theory to strong empirical validation.
**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The inclusion of motivating examples and clear descriptions of the grammar and learning models makes a complex topic accessible.

The paper also has minor weaknesses that could be addressed. The scope is currently limited to homogeneous polynomials, and while the authors acknowledge this, a brief discussion on the challenges of extending the grammar to other functions (e.g., trigonometric, recursive) would be welcome. Furthermore, it is interesting that the simpler n-gram models often outperform the more complex RNN. A deeper analysis into why the RNN did not capture the necessary long-range dependencies more effectively would strengthen the paper's conclusions about the relative merits of these models.

Despite these minor points, the paper's novel approach and strong results make it a significant contribution. It opens up a promising new direction for applying machine learning to symbolic mathematics. The work is likely to inspire further research at the intersection of these fields. I strongly recommend acceptance.
