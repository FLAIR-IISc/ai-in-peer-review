

This paper proposes an interesting approach to discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques, specifically through the integration of attribute grammars with n-gram models and recursive neural networks. The authors present a framework that searches for symbolic expressions equivalent to a target but with lower computational complexity, addressing a problem that has been largely unexplored in the machine learning literature.

The main strengths of this paper are its novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation and the demonstration of discovering new mathematical identities with significant computational benefits. The paper clearly articulates a well-defined problem (finding equivalent expressions with lower complexity), presents a reasonable framework using attribute grammars, and demonstrates practical results on several challenging polynomial families. The use of integer-based descriptors to avoid numerical issues is a thoughtful technical detail. The RNN-based continuous representation for symbolic expressions is particularly interesting and could have broader implications for applying machine learning to symbolic domains. The experiments show that their learning approaches outperform random search for complex expressions (e.g., k=6 in Example 2), which is impressive given the combinatorial explosion of possible expressions.

The paper makes a reasonable effort to situate its work in the context of related fields, mentioning theorem proving, program induction, and NLP. However, the related work section could be strengthened by more thoroughly distinguishing this work from prior approaches to symbolic computation. For instance, the paper mentions Maple and Mathematica but doesn't adequately explain why their rule-based approaches aren't sufficient for the authors' problem. The connection to recent work on neural symbolic reasoning (e.g., by Socher, Bowman, and others) is noted but could be expanded to better highlight the novelty.

Regarding quality, the paper presents a technically sound framework with reasonable experimental validation. The use of numerical representation with modulo arithmetic to avoid floating point errors is well-justified. The RNN training details (curriculum learning, initialization, activation functions) demonstrate careful attention to practical implementation. However, the paper lacks a thorough theoretical analysis of the search space complexity and the conditions under which the learning approach will succeed or fail. The claim that their approach "dramatically widens the complexity and scope of expressions that can be handled" is supported by examples but not rigorously proven.

The paper's significance is somewhat limited by the restrictive problem formulation (homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, single matrix/vector input). While the authors acknowledge these limitations in Section 8, they don't sufficiently address how these constraints impact the broader applicability of their framework. The examples provided (e.g., for RBM partition function approximation) are promising but remain speculative without concrete implementations or performance comparisons.

A major weakness is the lack of comparison to alternative approaches. The paper compares its learning strategies to random search, but doesn't benchmark against other symbolic computation methods or state-of-the-art theorem provers. The claim that their identities are "previously undiscovered" is difficult to verify without a more thorough literature review of mathematical identities in the specific domains they explore.

The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful examples. The technical details about the RNN architecture, training procedures, and experimental setup are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. However, the paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of the limitations of their approach and a clearer roadmap for future work beyond the current restricted setting.

In terms of originality, the paper does present a novel combination of techniques (attribute grammars + learning for symbolic search) that hasn't been previously explored. The application of RNNs to learn representations of symbolic expressions is particularly innovative. However, the paper could more clearly articulate how this differs from existing work in program synthesis and grammar-based search.

Overall, this is an interesting and potentially valuable contribution that bridges symbolic computation and machine learning. The framework has clear potential applications in compiler optimization and mathematical software. The discovery of new mathematical identities with reduced computational complexity is significant, though the scope of the problem is somewhat limited. The paper would benefit from stronger theoretical grounding, more comprehensive comparisons to alternative approaches, and a clearer discussion of the limitations of the current framework.

The paper's strengths (novel approach, practical results, well-executed experiments) outweigh its weaknesses (limited scope, lack of theoretical analysis, insufficient comparison to alternatives). I believe this work represents a meaningful contribution to the field of symbolic computation and machine learning, with potential for broader impact in optimization and mathematical software. The paper meets NIPS standards for quality and significance, though it could be strengthened by addressing the limitations noted above. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses in the camera-ready version.
