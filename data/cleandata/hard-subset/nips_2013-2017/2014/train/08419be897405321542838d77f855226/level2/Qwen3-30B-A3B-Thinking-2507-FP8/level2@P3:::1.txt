

This paper introduces a framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors propose representing symbolic expressions through an attribute grammar and employ learning approaches to guide the search for computationally efficient expressions that are analytically equivalent to a target. The work builds on attribute grammars originally developed by Knuth for compiler construction, adapting them to a search and optimization problem. The authors introduce two learning strategies: n-gram models that capture common patterns in solutions to simpler expressions, and a recursive neural network that learns a continuous representation of symbolic expressions to guide the search.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with clear experimental validation. The use of integer-based descriptors to avoid numerical matching issues is a clever technical detail that enhances the reliability of the approach. The learned representation via recursive neural networks is well-executed, with appropriate attention to training details such as initialization, activation functions, and curriculum learning. The experiments demonstrate clear improvements over random search for the first two families of expressions, with the n-gram models particularly effective for expressions with repetitive structures. The discovery of new mathematical identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) for certain matrix operations is a significant contribution.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with effective examples illustrating the approach. The problem statement is precise, and the relationship to related work in theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation is well-articulated. The authors correctly acknowledge that existing symbolic computation engines like Maple and Mathematica do not explicitly seek lower-complexity versions of expressions, and that their approach differs from previous work by using learning rather than hand-designed heuristics.

The originality of the work is strong, as the authors present a novel application of machine learning to the discovery of mathematical identities. The framework represents a significant departure from traditional symbolic computation approaches. The paper clearly differentiates its contribution from related work in attribute grammars (e.g., [7, 19, 26, 10]) and from NLP techniques applied to symbolic domains (e.g., [2, 3, 8, 16, 17, 22, 23, 25]).

The significance of the work is demonstrated through potential applications in machine learning, particularly for efficiently computing expressions related to dropout and restricted Boltzmann machines. The ability to automatically discover identities beyond human reach has clear practical value, and the framework could potentially be integrated with compilers or optimization tools. The paper also makes a broader contribution by showing how machine learning can be applied to discrete search problems, which has implications beyond mathematical identity discovery.

Several weaknesses should be addressed. The problem domain is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of degree k with a single matrix or vector variable, which limits the applicability of the framework. The paper acknowledges this limitation but does not sufficiently explore how these restrictions could be relaxed for more general mathematical expressions. Additionally, while the paper claims the discovered identities are "previously undiscovered," it provides limited evidence for this claim beyond stating "as far as we are aware." A more thorough comparison with mathematical literature would strengthen this assertion.

The experiments focus on five families of expressions, but it's unclear how representative these are of real-world symbolic computation problems. The results for the most challenging family (RBM-2)k are weak, with no solutions found for k > 5, which limits the perceived robustness of the approach. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of training the learning models, particularly for large-scale applications.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The framework demonstrates how learning can guide combinatorial search in a domain with exponential complexity, which has broader implications for AI. The technical execution is sound, and the results are well-supported by experiments. The work represents a meaningful step toward applying machine learning to symbolic tasks, with clear potential for practical applications in machine learning and computational mathematics.

The paper meets the quality criteria with a technically sound approach, clear experimental validation, and appropriate attention to both strengths and limitations. The clarity is excellent, with well-organized exposition and effective examples. The originality is strong, with a novel application of learning techniques to mathematical identity discovery. The significance is demonstrated through both theoretical contributions and potential practical applications. While the scope is somewhat restricted, the approach could be extended to more general settings with appropriate modifications to the grammar and scheduler. The work represents a compelling contribution to the field, advancing the state of the art in symbolic computation through machine learning.
