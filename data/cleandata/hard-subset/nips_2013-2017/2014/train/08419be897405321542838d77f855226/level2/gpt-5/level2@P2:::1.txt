Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the discovery of computationally efficient algebraic identities for given target expressions by searching over a space defined by an attribute grammar. Expressions are represented as trees composed from a small set of linear algebraic and elementwise operations, with rule attributes indicating asymptotic cost. Because exhaustive search is infeasible, the authors learn to guide search from solutions to simpler targets using two strategies: (1) an n-gram model over subtrees, and (2) a recursive neural network (RNN) trained to learn continuous embeddings of expression trees and, on top, a softmax that predicts the next rule to apply. Equivalence checking is done by randomized evaluation over Zp to create integer “descriptors”; for more complex targets, linear combinations of trees are solved for via a modular linear system.

The work sits at the intersection of theorem proving/program synthesis, grammar-based optimization, and representation learning. It usefully distinguishes itself from rule-based CAS simplification (e.g., Maple, Mathematica) by optimizing for computational cost and by learning heuristics. Related program-search efforts in systems/PL (e.g., superoptimization, STOKE (Schkufza et al., PLDI’13), and Spiral for fast transforms (Püschel et al.)) and equality-saturation based optimizers (e.g., Tate et al., 2009) are not cited but are relevant comparators in spirit. For randomized equivalence checking, connections to polynomial identity testing and the Schwartz–Zippel lemma are also pertinent. On the ML side, the RNN approach is in line with contemporaneous work in NLP on tree-structured models.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated problem: producing lower-complexity equivalents to a target expression has practical and scientific value.
- Elegant framework: attribute grammars for expression space, modular search strategies, and an efficient equivalence-checking surrogate enable a tractable pipeline.
- Learning to search: training on simpler instances to generalize to harder ones is compelling; the n-gram model’s strong performance on repetitive structures is insightful.
- RNN representation: shows that continuous embeddings can capture nontrivial algebraic structure with low classification error and can guide search better than random in some cases.
- Empirical demonstrations: nontrivial identities found for matrix sums and exponential-sum families, with code and data released for reproducibility.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
Major
- Correctness guarantees: The use of randomized evaluation in Zp to decide equivalence and to solve for linear combinations is only probabilistically sound. The paper does not provide bounds on collision probability, choice of p, number of samples N, or a protocol (e.g., multiple independent primes) to make the probability of a false positive negligible. Without this, the claim of discovering “previously undiscovered identities” is vulnerable. Suggest grounding in PIT (Schwartz–Zippel) and providing rigorous success probabilities and validation with multiple primes and seeds.
- Linear combinations of trees: Section 5 lacks crucial constraints on coefficients. Are w constrained to small integers (e.g., {−1,0,1}) or arbitrary elements of Zp? If the latter, identities that hold modulo p may not correspond to valid real/integer identities, and large/inexact coefficients could defeat computational gains. Please specify the coefficient domain, how results are realized as actual symbolic expressions, and whether solutions are postprocessed (e.g., via CRT/rational reconstruction) to exact integer coefficients.
- Evaluation breadth and baselines: Beyond “random,” the paper omits comparisons to strong search/optimization baselines (beam search, MCTS, A* with simple cost heuristics, equality-saturation/term rewriting with cost-aware extraction), or to CAS systems configured to minimize operation counts/BLAS calls. Even if CASs do not directly optimize complexity, reporting what Mathematica/Maple/Spiral produce on select targets would contextualize gains.
- Significance of RNN: The RNN underperforms n-grams on the main families (except a modest speedup for RBM-2 at k=5). More analysis is needed on when the learned embeddings help (e.g., nonrepetitive motifs), and whether stronger architectures (tensor RNNs, attention, or merely a beam over the softmax) would improve search. As is, the main empirical advancement appears to be the n-gram strategy.

Minor
- Grammar scope and cost model: The grammar is tailored to homogeneous polynomials and scalar outputs, with manual removal of matrix-matrix multiply for some families. While reasonable for a first study, the restrictions and their implications on generality and optimality should be discussed more candidly. Also clarify the cost model (e.g., flop counts vs. asymptotic surrogates) and whether discovered identities actually reduce wall-clock time in a numerical environment.
- Scheduler: The greedy selection of the highest-scoring rule seems brittle. A small beam or stochastic selection proportional to scores might improve robustness; an ablation would be informative.
- Claims of novelty: “Previously undiscovered” identities should be stated cautiously unless accompanied by proof and literature vetting. Consider rephrasing to “to our knowledge” and include formal proofs for highlighted identities in the supplement.
- Dataset labeling for RNN: Since class labels are induced from numerical descriptors, the same concerns about collisions apply. Please quantify the probability of mislabeling and whether you verified clusters symbolically for a subset.
- Clarity: Include the full grammar (Table 1) and exact coefficient constraints for linear combinations in the main text or a prominent appendix.

Quality
Technically sound overall but missing theoretical guarantees for the randomized equivalence test and coefficient recovery; evaluation omits strong baselines and runtime/flop validation. Methods are described with helpful training details, code is provided.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. Some critical definitions (grammar rules, coefficient domain, cost model) should be made more explicit.

Originality
Applying learned search policies and RNN embeddings to algebraic identity discovery is novel within ML venues. Relation to prior program search/optimization work should be expanded.

Significance
Promising direction; the ability to automate discovery of complexity-reducing identities could impact compilers and ML models. Current empirical scope is limited; stronger guarantees and baselines would elevate impact.

Suggestions
- Add PIT-based guarantees: choose multiple primes and independent samples; provide theoretical bounds and an error budget.
- Constrain/recover coefficients: restrict to small integers during solve or reconstruct exact integers via CRT; present formal proofs for key identities.
- Add baselines: beam search, MCTS, equality-saturation with cost-aware extraction; CAS/Spiral comparisons on representative tasks; report flop counts and wall-clock improvements.
- Strengthen search: combine RNN/n-gram with a beam; report ablations.
- Expand related work to superoptimization (Massalin; STOKE), Spiral, and equality saturation.

Arguments for acceptance
- Interesting, underexplored problem with practical upside.
- Conceptually clean integration of grammars, learning, and randomized equivalence.
- Empirical success on identities beyond brute-force/random, with open-source code.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of rigorous correctness guarantees for discovered identities and coefficient recovery.
- Missing strong baselines and runtime validation.
- RNN’s contribution to search is modest; main gains from simple n-grams.
- Narrow grammar and manual constraints limit generality.
