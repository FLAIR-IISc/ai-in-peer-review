- Problem: learn search policies to discover algebraically equivalent but cheaper expressions using an attribute grammar over linear-algebra operations.
- Methods: two learned strategies—an n-gram model over frequent subtrees and an RNN embedding with a softmax policy—plus modular evaluation over Zp and modular Gaussian elimination for linear combinations.
- Empirical claim: learned policies expand discoverable identities under a time budget; n-gram often outperforms RNN at higher complexity.

- Strength: well-motivated goal with practical applications in compilers, autodiff, and ML.
- Strength: principled grammar with attributed costs and a scalable modular evaluation/equivalence-checking pipeline.
- Strength: novel application of RNNs to embed algebraic expressions with low classification error up to k=6.
- Strength: compelling empirical results showing learned strategies beat brute-force/random search on several target families.
- Strength: careful RNN training engineering and released code/data.

- Weakness: equivalence checking via Zp is probabilistic with no quantified collision bounds or multiple-prime safeguards.
- Weakness: scope limited to homogeneous polynomials in a single matrix/vector, reducing applicability to multi-variable or non-polynomial cases.
- Weakness: baseline comparisons are narrow—missing beam search, MCTS, A*/best-first, genetic programming, and equality-saturation style baselines.
- Weakness: search scheduler is underspecified (greedy vs beam, restarts, exploration), risking sensitivity to early mistakes.
- Weakness: novelty claims for some identities are weak—some are standard linear-algebra rewrites and need stronger literature cross-checks.
- Weakness: RNN often underperforms n-gram on harder tasks; analysis of why is insufficient.
- Weakness: complexity accounting sometimes enforced by disallowing ops (e.g., matrix-matrix multiply) instead of a systematic cost model.

- Quality: technically sound but needs stronger rigor in probabilistic equivalence testing and verification.
- Clarity: generally clear, but the scheduler, complexity accounting, and verification pipeline need more explicit description.
- Originality: novel combination of grammars with learned search and RNN embeddings for algebraic expressions; n-gram baseline is simple but effective.
- Significance: promising direction with potential real-world impact, though current scope and baselines limit immediate applicability.

- Question: how are discovered identities ultimately certified—multiple primes, counts, or symbolic verification?
- Question: what is the precise scheduler algorithm (greedy vs beam size, restarts, temperature/exploration)?
- Question: how are matrix dimensions and non-square cases handled in complexity estimates?
- Question: can the RNN policy be improved by joint training of the embedding and policy head?

- Essential revision: add a verification protocol (multiple primes and collision bounds or symbolic checks) for discovered identities.
- Essential revision: fully specify the search procedure and include a beam-search baseline, reporting its impact on results.
- Essential revision: substantiate novelty claims for representative identities or soften claims where literature precedent exists.

- Optional revision: add broader search baselines (MCTS, genetic programming) to contextualize learning gains.
- Optional revision: analyze why n-grams outperform RNNs with ablations on embedding size, data size, and curriculum.
- Optional revision: extend experiments to multi-variable targets to demonstrate broader applicability.
- Optional revision: report end-to-end computational savings including constants for practical implementations.

- Argument for acceptance: interesting, timely problem with a coherent framework, practical contributions, and released code/data.
- Argument for acceptance: evidence that learned guidance enables discovery of nontrivial identities beyond brute-force search.
- Argument for rejection: probabilistic equivalence checks lack rigorous guarantees and verification protocol.
- Argument for rejection: limited comparative evaluation and narrow experimental scope weaken claims about general effectiveness.
- Argument for rejection: RNN policy underperforms simpler baselines on harder instances with limited analysis of failure modes.

- Overall: solid and thought-provoking work that would benefit from stronger verification, clearer search methodology, and broader baselines before being a strong submission.
