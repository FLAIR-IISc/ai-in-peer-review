- Presents a framework that uses an attribute grammar to represent expression space and searches for analytically equivalent, computationally cheaper expressions.  
- Key challenge: exponential search space; authors use machine learning to guide search rather than brute force.  
- Two learned search strategies: an n-gram model over expression subtrees and a recursive neural network (RNN).  
- RNN is used to learn continuous vector representations of symbolic expressions (analogous to word embeddings).  
- Equivalence checking is done via efficient numerical evaluation over a finite field, avoiding heavy symbolic manipulation.  
- Empirical evaluation on five expression families shows learned strategies (n-gram and RNN) significantly outperform random search.  
- Experiments demonstrate discovery of complex, previously unknown identities; claims are well-supported by results.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-executed, and convincingly evaluated.  
- Writing and organization are excellent; examples, figures, and exposition are clear and accessible.  
- Highly original: combining grammars, ML-guided search, and learned continuous representations for symbolic math is novel.  
- High potential significance for symbolic mathematics, scientific computing, compilers, and ML.  
- Strengths: strong novelty, learned representations via RNN, solid empirical gains, and clear presentation.  
- Weaknesses: grammar currently limited to homogeneous polynomials with single-variable scalar outputs — needs discussion on extending to richer operations and data types.  
- Weaknesses: simpler n-gram often outperforms RNN; paper should analyze why and whether alternate RNN architectures help.  
- Weaknesses: scalability concerns about the linear-combination solver step — clarify how required tree count M and solver cost scale with problem complexity.
