This paper proposes learning-guided discovery of computationally efficient mathematical identities. The authors define an attribute grammar over matrix/vector operations, represent candidate expressions as trees, and search for expressions that are equivalent to a target but cheaper to evaluate. Equivalence is checked numerically via descriptors over Zp; more complex targets are matched via linear combinations of tree descriptors. To scale search, they learn strategies from solutions to simpler targets: (i) n-gram models over subtrees, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions and a softmax policy for next-rule selection. Experiments on five expression families (including sums over AAT, symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related exponential sums) show reliable complexity reductions (e.g., O(n3)→O(n2), sometimes exponential→polynomial for small k). The work connects to theorem proving, program induction, grammar-based optimization, and RNNs for structured data previously seen at NIPS.

Quality: The main claims—learned guidance expands the tractable search frontier and discovers nontrivial identities—are supported by success-rate curves across k, time-to-solution, and a separate expression classification task with ≤5% error. However, the evaluation lacks comparisons to strong alternative search baselines (e.g., beam/MCTS, A*/cost-aware rewrite systems, superoptimization), and to symbolic tools (Mathematica/Maple) configured with cost models. The numerical equivalence check needs collision-probability bounds and, ideally, post hoc symbolic verification; the claim of “previously undiscovered” identities would benefit from proofs and a literature audit. Still, the released code is a plus.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written with helpful examples (e.g., k-chains). Some details are underspecified: the full grammar table, choice of N and prime p, ablations on curriculum/dropout, and the scheduler’s tie-breaking/beam width.

Originality: Using learned policies (n-grams/RNN embeddings) to guide identity discovery in an attribute-grammar space is novel and distinct from rule-based CAS simplifications and prior RNN-on-tree NLP work.

Significance: The approach is promising for cost-aware algebraic optimization and could inform compiler passes. Current scope is narrow (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, mostly single-variable), and scaling to the hardest family (RBM-2) is limited.

Constructive suggestions:
- Provide formal bounds for Zp collision probability and perform symbolic verification of discovered identities.
- Add baselines (beam search, MCTS, heuristic cost-aware rewrites) and a comparison to CAS with custom rewrite rules.
- Report wall-clock/compute vs. success, and analyze policy/sample efficiency.
- Explore stronger tree models (TreeLSTM/GNN) and reinforcement learning for the search policy.
- Extend to multi-variable targets and richer grammars; include proofs or references for “new” identities.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, original formulation; compelling idea of learning-guided symbolic search.
- Demonstrated complexity reductions with open-source code.
- Bridges representation learning and symbolic computation.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited baselines and formal verification.
- Restricted problem setting and mixed scaling on hardest tasks.
- Some methodological details and ablations missing.
