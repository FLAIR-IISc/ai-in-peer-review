## Review of “Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities”

This paper explores the application of machine learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea – using learned strategies to navigate the exponential search space of symbolic expressions – is intriguing and addresses a challenging problem at the intersection of symbolic computation and machine learning. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework and propose two learning approaches: n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN), to guide the search for more efficient expressions.

The paper’s strengths lie in its novel approach and promising results. The framework for representing symbolic expressions using attribute grammars provides a solid foundation, and the integration of learning techniques to improve search efficiency is a significant contribution. The experimental results, particularly the discovery of identities reducing complexity from O(n<sup>3</sup>) to O(n<sup>2</sup>), demonstrate the potential of the approach. The inclusion of code and data availability enhances reproducibility. The learned representations of symbolic expressions using the RNN are a noteworthy addition, potentially opening doors for further research.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. The experimental evaluation, while promising, feels somewhat limited. The focus on a specific set of polynomial expressions, though justified by scope limitations, restricts the generalizability assessment. The RNN performance is consistently outperformed by n-gram models, raising questions about its effectiveness in this context and requiring further investigation. The discussion regarding limitations feels brief. While acknowledging restrictions (single variable, polynomial degree), a deeper dive into potential failure modes and scalability concerns would be valuable. The choice of hyperparameters for both learning methods could benefit from more detailed justification.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of ML to symbolic identity discovery.
*   Demonstrated ability to find identities beyond manual derivation or brute-force search.
*   Sound technical framework based on attribute grammars.
*   Publicly available code and data.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of experimental evaluation.
*   RNN performance lags behind simpler n-gram models.
*   Lack of in-depth discussion of limitations.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field, offering a novel approach to a challenging problem. While further research is needed to address the limitations, the core ideas and initial results are compelling. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, specifically focusing on expanding the experimental evaluation and providing a more detailed analysis of the RNN’s performance. The authors should also expand on the discussion of limitations and potential future work.
