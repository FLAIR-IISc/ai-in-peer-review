This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of mathematical expressions. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is analytically equivalent to a given target expression but has a lower computational cost. The main contribution is addressing the combinatorial explosion inherent in this search. Instead of brute-force or random search, the authors propose using machine learning models—specifically n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN)—to learn a search strategy. These models are trained on solutions found for simpler expressions, and this knowledge is then transferred to guide the search for more complex identities. The paper also introduces a novel application of an RNN to learn a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions, which is a significant contribution in its own right.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength is its novelty. Applying modern machine learning techniques, particularly deep representation learning, to the classical domain of symbolic mathematics and identity discovery is a creative and promising direction. It bridges the gap between statistical pattern recognition and symbolic reasoning. The potential impact is high; this work could influence areas like compiler optimization, scientific computing, and theoretical machine learning by automating the discovery of efficient computations. The discovery of several new, non-trivial identities for expressions relevant to machine learning (e.g., related to RBMs) demonstrates the practical utility of the approach.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sound and well-executed. The use of an attribute grammar to structure the search space is elegant. The two methods for verifying expression equivalence are clever solutions to avoid slow symbolic manipulation: the numerical descriptor method using modular arithmetic is robust, and the learned RNN representation is a highly novel contribution. The experimental setup is thorough, comparing the learning-based strategies against a random baseline on several challenging families of expressions. The results clearly demonstrate that learning from simpler problems drastically improves the ability to find complex identities.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear, and the motivation is compelling. The introductory examples (Example 1 & 2) are excellent for building intuition. The authors do a good job of explaining the different components of their system, from the grammar and expression representations to the search strategies. The inclusion of crucial training details for the RNN (e.g., identity initialization, curriculum learning) is valuable for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Scope and Generalizability:** The current framework is limited to homogeneous polynomials of a single variable that produce a scalar output. While this is a reasonable starting point and still allows for a rich problem space, the paper would be stronger with a more detailed discussion on the challenges of extending the framework. For instance, how would the numerical descriptor method work for non-polynomial expressions or functions with specific domains? Extending the grammar to include trigonometric functions, recursion, or control flow would likely require a much more sophisticated scheduler than the one presented. A brief discussion of these future challenges would help contextualize the current work.

2.  **Analysis of Model Performance:** The n-gram models, which are conceptually simpler, outperform the more complex RNN model on several tasks. The paper attributes this to the repetitive structure of the solutions found. This is a plausible explanation, but a more in-depth analysis would be beneficial. It would be interesting to understand why the RNN struggles to capture this compositional, repetitive structure. Perhaps an analysis of the learned representations or an ablation study on the RNN's architecture could provide more insight. This could also lead to suggestions for future architectures (e.g., with attention mechanisms) that might better capture the nature of symbolic expressions.

3.  **Clarification on Linear Combinations:** The method of finding linear combinations of expression trees is critical for solving more complex targets. However, the process of generating the set of `M` candidate trees for the linear system is not fully detailed. Are these trees generated randomly, or using the learned strategy? Is there a principled way to select a diverse and useful set of basis expressions? Furthermore, the computational cost of solving the `M x N` linear system via Gaussian elimination could become a bottleneck, and a brief discussion of this would be welcome.

**Summary of Pro/Con Arguments:**

*   **Pro:**
    *   Highly original and significant application of ML to symbolic computation.
    *   Novel RNN-based method for learning continuous representations of symbolic expressions.
    *   Strong empirical evidence showing the learning-based search outperforms random search.
    *   Successfully discovers new and complex mathematical identities.
    *   The paper is well-written and clear.

*   **Con:**
    *   The framework is currently restricted to a narrow class of mathematical expressions.
    *   The simpler n-gram model often outperforms the more sophisticated RNN, and the reasons for this could be analyzed more deeply.
    *   Some details about the generation of trees for the linear combination solver could be clarified.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that introduces a creative and powerful new methodology. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws. The work is likely to be influential and inspire further research at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic reasoning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
