- Paper proposes learning-guided discovery of computationally efficient mathematical identities via attribute grammars over matrix/vector operations.
- Candidate expressions are represented as trees; search seeks expressions equivalent to a target but cheaper to evaluate.
- Equivalence is checked numerically using descriptors over Zp, with linear-combination matching for complex targets.
- To scale search, authors learn strategies from simpler targets: n-gram models over subtrees and an RNN that embeds expressions and predicts next-rule via softmax.
- Experiments on five expression families (e.g., AAT sums, symmetric polynomials, RBM exponentials) show consistent complexity reductions, sometimes dramatic for small k.
- Evaluation presents success-rate curves, time-to-solution, and an expression classification task with ≤5% error to support claims.
- Paper situates work relative to theorem proving, program induction, grammar-based optimization, and RNNs for structured data.
- Main evaluation weakness: lacks comparisons to strong alternative search baselines (beam search, MCTS, A*, superoptimization).
- Missing comparison to symbolic CAS (Mathematica/Maple) configured with cost models and rewrite rules.
- Numerical equivalence check needs formal collision-probability bounds for Zp and ideally post hoc symbolic verification.
- Claim of “previously undiscovered” identities requires proofs or a literature audit to validate novelty.
- Released code is a positive aspect that aids reproducibility.
- Writing is generally clear with helpful examples, but some methodological details are underspecified (full grammar, choice of N and prime p).
- Additional underspecified items: ablations on curriculum/dropout and scheduler tie-breaking/beam-width settings.
- Originality: novel use of learned policies (n-grams/RNN embeddings) to guide identity discovery in an attribute-grammar search space.
- Significance: promising for cost-aware algebraic optimization and potential compiler applications, but current scope is narrow (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, mostly single-variable).
- Scalability concern: limited scaling on the hardest family (RBM-2).
- Constructive suggestions: provide Zp collision bounds and symbolic verification, add strong baselines and CAS comparisons, report wall-clock/compute vs. success, analyze policy/sample efficiency.
- Further suggestions: explore stronger tree models (TreeLSTM/GNN) and reinforcement learning for search, extend to multivariable targets and richer grammars, and include proofs/references for claimed new identities.
- Pros for acceptance: clear and original formulation, demonstrated complexity reductions, open-source code, and a bridge between representation learning and symbolic computation.
- Cons against acceptance: limited baselines and formal verification, restricted problem setting, mixed scaling on hardest tasks, and missing methodological ablations.
