This paper explores the application of machine learning techniques to discover efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions as trees built from an attribute grammar and then learn a strategy to search this tree space for expressions analytically equivalent to a target, but with lower computational complexity. Two learning approaches are investigated: an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN). The paper demonstrates the ability to derive complex identities beyond manual derivation or brute-force search.

The paper is well-motivated, tackling a challenging problem at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The use of attribute grammars provides a structured framework for representing and manipulating mathematical expressions. The proposed learning approaches—n-grams and RNNs—are relevant and intuitively suited to capturing patterns in successful identity derivations. The experimental results demonstrate that the learning-based search strategies outperform random search, especially for higher-degree polynomials. The discovery of new mathematical identities is a significant contribution, and the availability of code and evaluation data promotes reproducibility and further research. The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, acknowledging connections to theorem proving, program induction, and NLP techniques.

A key strength is the clear problem statement and the systematic approach to addressing it. The paper’s structure is logical, progressing from the problem definition and grammar framework to the learning strategies and experimental evaluation. The inclusion of specific examples (e.g., reducing O(n^3) to O(n^2) matrix multiplication) effectively illustrates the benefits of the approach.  The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the restricted scope (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix variable), and discuss potential avenues for generalization.

However, the paper has several areas for improvement. While the experimental results are positive, the evaluation is somewhat limited. The choice of families of expressions appears somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic evaluation across a wider range of mathematical problems would strengthen the claims of generality.  Furthermore, the comparison between the n-gram and RNN approaches lacks sufficient detail. A more thorough ablation study, examining the influence of hyperparameters (e.g., n in n-grams, dropout rate in RNN) would be valuable. The RNN performance seems surpassed by the n-gram models, and this disparity isn't fully explained.  Why does a simple n-gram model outperform a more sophisticated RNN in certain cases? Is the RNN representation truly capturing meaningful semantic information, or is it merely memorizing patterns?

The numerical representation of expressions, while necessary for comparison, introduces potential issues with numerical stability (addressed with modular arithmetic). However, the implications of using a finite field (Zp) for the numerical representation are not fully discussed. Could this lead to spurious matches or limit the precision of the search?

Clarity could be improved in several sections. The explanation of the attribute grammar (Section 3) could benefit from a more intuitive explanation. The notation, while generally clear, can be dense in places.  For example, the explanation of the RNN in Section 4.2 is dense and could be easier to follow with diagrams or a more step-by-step breakdown.

The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational cost of training the learning models and the search process itself.  What are the practical limitations in terms of the size of the target expression or the complexity of the grammar? 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of machine learning to discover mathematical identities.
*   Demonstrated ability to find efficient identities beyond manual or brute-force methods.
*   Well-defined problem statement and systematic approach.
*   Availability of code and data for reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of evaluation and somewhat arbitrary choice of expression families.
*   Insufficient analysis of the n-gram vs. RNN performance disparity.
*   Lack of detailed discussion of the implications of the numerical representation.
*   Clarity could be improved in several sections.



Overall, this is a promising and innovative work. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. A major revision is recommended. Specifically, expanding the evaluation, providing a more detailed analysis of the RNN performance, and clarifying the technical details would be essential.
