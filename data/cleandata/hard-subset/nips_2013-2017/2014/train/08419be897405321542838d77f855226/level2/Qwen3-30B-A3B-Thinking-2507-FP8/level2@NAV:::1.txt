

This paper presents an intriguing application of machine learning to the discovery of efficient mathematical identities, using attribute grammars to represent symbolic expressions and learning approaches to guide the search for computationally optimal forms. The authors' main claims are: (1) a grammar framework enables systematic exploration of symbolic expression space, (2) machine learning techniques (n-gram models and RNNs) can learn from simpler expressions to guide the search for complex identities, and (3) their approach discovers novel identities that significantly reduce computational complexity beyond brute-force or human capabilities.

The paper demonstrates solid technical quality with experiments showing their learning approaches (particularly 3-gram models) outperform random search for many expression families. The RNN-based representation learning is well-executed, with appropriate attention to training details like curriculum learning and weight initialization. The authors provide clear examples (e.g., reducing O(n³) to O(n²) complexity) and demonstrate the framework's ability to discover previously unknown identities.

The work shows good originality in applying NLP techniques (n-grams, recursive neural networks) to symbolic computation, though the attribute grammar approach has precedents in other domains. The significance is strong for theoretical computer science and potentially for machine learning practitioners who could use these identities in optimization (e.g., for RBM partition functions or dropout approximations). The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately, noting the restricted scope to homogeneous polynomials and single matrices.

Strengths include clear problem formulation, thorough experimental evaluation across multiple expression families, and well-motivated connections to related work in theorem proving and NLP. The paper's clarity is good, with effective use of examples and figures to illustrate concepts.

A few weaknesses merit mention: the restricted problem scope (single matrix/vector, homogeneous polynomials) limits immediate applicability to broader mathematical contexts, and the paper could better explain why n-gram models outperformed RNNs for certain expression patterns. The supplementary material listing identities is valuable but could be integrated more directly into the main text.

Overall, this is a technically sound, well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to applying machine learning to symbolic domains. The framework is novel in its integration of learning with grammar-based search, and the discovered identities represent genuine advances in computational efficiency. The work demonstrates how machine learning can effectively guide combinatorial search in mathematical domains, with potential implications for optimization in AI systems. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the discussion of limitations and practical implications.
