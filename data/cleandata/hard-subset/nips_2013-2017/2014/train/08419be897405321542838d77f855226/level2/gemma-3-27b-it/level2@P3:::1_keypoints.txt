- Paper applies machine learning to discover efficient mathematical identities by searching expression trees built from an attribute grammar.  
- Two learning approaches are evaluated: an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN).  
- Method can find complex identities that are hard to derive manually or by brute-force search.  
- Use of attribute grammars provides a structured framework for representing and manipulating symbolic expressions.  
- N-gram and RNN approaches are relevant and intuitive for capturing patterns in successful derivations.  
- Experimental results show learning-based search outperforms random search, especially for higher-degree polynomials.  
- Discovery of new mathematical identities and provision of code/data are strong contributions for reproducibility.  
- Related work coverage is reasonable, linking to theorem proving, program induction, and NLP techniques.  
- Paper is well-motivated with a clear problem statement and logical structure from framework to experiments.  
- Good illustrative examples (e.g., reducing O(n^3) to O(n^2) matrix multiplication) demonstrate practical benefits.  
- Authors acknowledge scope limitations (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix variable) and possible generalizations.  
- Evaluation is limited and uses somewhat arbitrary families of expressions, weakening claims of generality.  
- Comparison between n-gram and RNN lacks sufficient detail and ablation (e.g., hyperparameter effects).  
- Unexplained phenomenon: simple n-gram outperforms RNN in some cases, raising questions about RNN capturing semantics vs memorization.  
- Numerical representation via finite field (Zp) and modular arithmetic is used but implications (spurious matches, precision limits) are under-discussed.  
- Clarity issues: attribute grammar and RNN explanations are dense and would benefit from more intuitive explanations or diagrams.  
- Computational cost and practical limitations of training models and search (target size, grammar complexity) are not analyzed.  
- Recommendation: promising work deserving acceptance after major revision—expand evaluation, analyze RNN vs n-gram performance, and clarify technical details.
