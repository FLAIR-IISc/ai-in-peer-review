This paper explores the application of machine learning to discover efficient mathematical identities, specifically focusing on finding computationally cheaper ways to calculate the same result. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework to represent symbolic expressions and leverage n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to guide a tree search for these identities, overcoming the exponential search space. The core idea is to learn from simpler expressions to efficiently find solutions for more complex ones.

The paper is well-motivated. Finding efficient mathematical identities has practical benefits in various fields, and the limitations of traditional symbolic computation systems are rightly highlighted. The introduction provides a clear overview of the problem and the proposed approach. The use of attribute grammars is a sensible choice for representing symbolic expressions, and the combination with machine learning techniques is novel. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning (specifically n-grams and RNNs) for discovering mathematical identities is a novel and interesting approach.
*   **Practicality:** The potential to discover identities that are difficult or impossible to find manually or through exhaustive search is significant. The examples provided (O(n^3) to O(n^2) improvements) demonstrate the practical value.
*   **Thoroughness:** The authors explore multiple learning approaches (n-grams and RNNs) and perform experiments on several expression families.  The ablation study with dropout rates is a good example of careful experimentation.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the concepts. The inclusion of examples helps to illustrate the approach.
*   **Availability of Code:** The code and evaluation data being publicly available is excellent and promotes reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope:** The problem is restricted to homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix/vector, which limits the applicability of the approach. While acknowledged, the impact of these restrictions isn't thoroughly discussed, particularly regarding the potential for generalization.
*   **RNN Performance:** The RNN approach does not consistently outperform the n-gram models, and the best n-gram model (3-gram) is quite simple. More analysis could be provided to understand why the RNN, despite its representational power, isn't performing as well.  The effect of different RNN architectures (e.g., LSTMs) wasn't explored.
*   **Descriptor Limitations:** The reliance on numerical descriptors with modulo arithmetic for comparison could be a potential source of error or instability. Though the authors address the rounding issues with integer-based descriptors, a more robust comparison method might be beneficial. The sensitivity to the prime number 'p' is not discussed.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't extensively discuss the sensitivity of results to hyperparameters, such as the size of the n-grams or the RNN’s hidden layer size. A brief sensitivity analysis could strengthen the results.
*    **Missing Baselines:** While the paper compares against random search, it lacks a comparison against other symbolic manipulation tools (e.g., Mathematica, Maple) applied to the same problem. Including such a comparison would strengthen the claims of novelty and efficiency.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a challenging problem.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the potential of the proposed method to discover efficient mathematical identities.
*   The code and data availability promotes reproducibility and further research.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The limited scope of the problem and the lack of generalization discussion.
*   The relatively modest performance of the RNN compared to the simpler n-gram models.
*   The potential limitations of the numerical descriptor comparison method.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While the scope is somewhat limited, the novelty of the approach and the promising experimental results make it worthy of acceptance. The weaknesses are not major and could be addressed in a future iteration of the work.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should address the limitations of scope more explicitly, provide a more in-depth analysis of the RNN's performance (and consider alternative architectures), and discuss the sensitivity of the results to key hyperparameters. A comparison against existing symbolic computation tools would further strengthen the paper.



**Quality:** 8/10 - Technically sound, good experimental setup.
**Clarity:** 9/10 - Very well written and organized.
**Originality:** 9/10 - Novel combination of techniques.
**Significance:** 7/10 - Potentially significant, especially if the approach can be generalized.
