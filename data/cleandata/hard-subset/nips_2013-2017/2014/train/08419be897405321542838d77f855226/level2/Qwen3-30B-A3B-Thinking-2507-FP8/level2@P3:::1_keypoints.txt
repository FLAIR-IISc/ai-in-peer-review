- Introduces a framework that uses machine learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities.  
- Represents symbolic expressions with an attribute grammar and searches for analytically equivalent, lower-cost expressions.  
- Adapts Knuth-style attribute grammars from compiler construction to a search and optimization setting.  
- Proposes two learning strategies: n-gram models capturing common solution patterns and a recursive neural network (RNN) learning continuous expression representations.  
- Uses integer-based descriptors to avoid numerical matching issues, improving matching reliability.  
- The RNN representation and training (initialization, activations, curriculum learning) are well-executed.  
- Experiments show clear improvements over random search for the first two expression families.  
- n-gram models are particularly effective for expressions with repetitive structures.  
- Discovers new identities that reduce complexity from O(n^3) to O(n^2) for certain matrix operations—a significant contribution.  
- Paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides effective illustrative examples.  
- Precisely states the problem and situates work relative to theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation.  
- Correctly distinguishes the approach from symbolic engines (Maple/Mathematica) by emphasizing learning over hand-designed heuristics.  
- Originality is strong: novel application of ML to mathematical identity discovery and departure from traditional symbolic methods.  
- Demonstrates potential significance for machine learning applications (e.g., dropout, RBMs) and compiler/optimization tool integration.  
- Shows broader implications by applying learning to combinatorial discrete search problems.  
- Major limitation: restricted to homogeneous polynomials of degree k with a single matrix/vector variable, limiting applicability.  
- Does not sufficiently explore how to relax domain restrictions for more general expressions.  
- Claim that identities are “previously undiscovered” is weakly supported and needs deeper literature comparison.  
- Experiments cover only five families, raising questions about representativeness for real-world symbolic computation.  
- Performance is weak on the hardest family (RBM-2)^k, with no solutions for k > 5, limiting perceived robustness.  
- Lacks detailed discussion of computational cost to train models, especially for large-scale applications.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, well-validated, and significant, with clear potential for extension despite scope limitations.
