- Proposes an attribute grammar-based framework using n-gram and RNN models to discover efficient mathematical identities, outperforming brute-force and random search.

- Novelty: innovative application of machine learning (especially RNNs) to symbolic computation and the combination of attribute grammars with learning-based search.

- Contributions: discovers previously unknown identities and introduces a learned continuous representation for symbolic expressions with potential applications in compiler optimization and ML.

- Evaluation: thorough experiments across multiple expression families and a comparative analysis of n-gram vs. RNN strategies.

- Reproducibility: code and evaluation data are provided.

- Scope limitation: restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree and scalar outputs, limiting broader applicability.

- RNN performance: RNNs are often outperformed by simpler n-gram models, especially on repetitive patterns, questioning scalability and generalization.

- Related work: discussion of prior work (theorem proving, program induction, probabilistic programming) is sparse and could be expanded.

- Grammar complexity: current grammar rules are simple and do not handle recursion or complex functions (e.g., trigonometric), limiting discovery potential.

- Suggestion: extend the framework to handle non-homogeneous polynomials, recursion, and trigonometric functions.

- Suggestion: analyze why RNNs underperform and investigate alternative neural architectures better suited for symbolic structure.

- Suggestion: expand the related-work discussion to include recent advances in symbolic computation and AI-guided theorem proving.

- Suggestion: include a more detailed analysis of discovered identities and their potential real-world impact.

- Recommendation: recommend acceptance, provided the authors address limitations and expand the discussion in the final version.
