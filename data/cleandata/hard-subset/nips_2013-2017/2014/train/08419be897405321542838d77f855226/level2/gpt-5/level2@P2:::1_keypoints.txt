- Summary: Learns to discover computationally cheaper algebraic equivalents by searching an attribute-grammar space using n-gram and RNN-guided policies and randomized equivalence checking over Zp.
- Relation to prior work: Connects to theorem proving/program synthesis, superoptimization, Spiral, and equality-saturation; missing citations to several relevant systems (STOKE, Spiral, equality-saturation literature).
- Strength: Well-motivated problem with practical value in reducing computational complexity of expressions.
- Strength: Elegant pipeline combining attribute grammars, learned search heuristics, and an efficient randomized equivalence-checking surrogate.
- Strength: Learning-from-simpler-instances approach is compelling; the n-gram model performs strongly on repetitive structures.
- Strength: RNN embeddings capture algebraic structure with low classification error and can guide search better than random in some cases.
- Strength: Empirical demonstrations find nontrivial identities for matrix sums and exponential-sum families with code/data released.
- Major weakness: Randomized equivalence testing lacks theoretical bounds, choice of primes, sample counts, and a protocol to make false-positive risk negligible.
- Major weakness: Linear-combination solver omits constraints on coefficients; unclear if coefficients are small integers or arbitrary Zp elements and whether recovered solutions map to exact real/integer identities.
- Major weakness: Evaluation omits strong baselines (beam search, MCTS, A*, equality-saturation, CAS/Spiral) and lacks flop/wall-clock comparisons to contextualize gains.
- Major weakness: RNN contribution is limited—underperforms n-grams on main families—and requires more analysis and exploration of stronger architectures or decoding strategies.
- Minor weakness: Grammar restricts to homogeneous polynomials/scalar outputs and sometimes removes matrix-matrix multiply; implications for generality and optimality need discussion.
- Minor weakness: Greedy scheduler may be brittle; small beam or stochastic selection and ablations should be evaluated.
- Minor weakness: Claims of “previously undiscovered” identities should be hedged and supported by formal proof/literature checks.
- Minor weakness: RNN dataset labels derived from numerical descriptors may suffer collisions; mislabeling probability should be quantified and symbolically verified on samples.
- Minor weakness: Key definitions (full grammar, coefficient constraints, cost model) should be explicitly included in main text or prominent appendix.
- Quality: Technically sound overall but missing theoretical guarantees for equivalence tests and coefficient recovery and lacking stronger baselines and runtime validation.
- Clarity: Generally clear and organized, but critical definitions and constraints need to be made explicit.
- Originality: Novel application of learned search policies and RNN embeddings to algebraic identity discovery within ML venues, but should expand relations to program-search/optimization literature.
- Significance: Promising direction with potential impact on compilers and ML, but limited empirical scope and guarantees reduce current impact.
- Suggestion: Add PIT/Schwartz–Zippel-based guarantees using multiple primes and independent samples and provide theoretical error bounds.
- Suggestion: Constrain or reconstruct coefficients (small integers, CRT/rational reconstruction) and provide formal proofs for key identities.
- Suggestion: Add and compare to strong search baselines (beam, MCTS, equality-saturation) and CAS/Spiral, and report flop counts and wall-clock speedups.
- Suggestion: Improve search by combining RNN/n-gram with beam or alternative architectures (attention/tensor RNNs) and include ablations.
- Suggestion: Expand related work discussion to include superoptimization (Massalin, STOKE), Spiral, and equality-saturation approaches.
- Arguments for acceptance: Interesting, underexplored problem; clean integration of grammars, learning, and randomized checking; empirical success with open-source code.
- Arguments against acceptance: Lacks rigorous correctness guarantees, strong baselines, runtime validation, and the RNN adds limited empirical value; grammar restrictions limit generality.
