Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles the problem of automatically discovering algebraically equivalent but computationally cheaper expressions for a given target. Expressions are generated by an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic operations. To avoid exponential blow-up in brute-force enumeration, the work learns search policies from solutions to simpler targets. Two strategies are explored: (1) an n-gram model over frequently occurring subtrees; and (2) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous embedding for symbolic expressions and, on top of that, a softmax policy for choosing the next grammar rule. Expression equivalence is tested primarily via evaluation at random inputs over Zp (large prime), and linear combinations of trees are found via modular Gaussian elimination on descriptor vectors. Experiments on several families of targets (e.g., repeated sums of AA^T, elementwise variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-inspired exponential sums) demonstrate that learned strategies expand the scope and depth of identities discoverable within a fixed time budget, with n-gram baselines often outperforming the RNN policy for higher k. The work connects to symbolic computation (Maple/Mathematica), program induction, theorem proving, and NLP-inspired models (n-grams, recursive nets), and differs by explicitly targeting reductions in computational complexity and learning search heuristics from data rather than relying on hand-crafted rewrite rules.

Strengths:
- A clear and well-motivated goal—automated discovery of lower-complexity algebraic forms—with potential utility in compilers, autodiff frameworks, and ML models.
- A principled grammar-based formulation with attributed operations (costs), combined with a practical equivalence-checking pipeline using modular evaluation. Handling linear combinations of trees via modular linear algebra is an effective, scalable idea.
- A novel application of recursive neural networks to learn continuous embeddings of algebraic expressions, validated by low classification error on equivalence classes up to k=6.
- Compelling empirical results: learned strategies enable discovery of identities beyond brute-force/random search; concrete speed-up patterns for families with repetitive local structure; code/data released.
- Careful engineering choices for RNN training (identity initialization, curriculum learning, dropout) that improve stability.

Weaknesses and detailed comments:
- Soundness and verification: Equivalence checking via evaluation in Zp is probabilistic. While practical, the paper does not quantify collision probabilities or describe safeguards (e.g., multiple independent primes or Schwartz–Zippel-style bounds). Similarly, the modular linear solver may admit spurious solutions if descriptors collide. A post-verification step (e.g., symbolic verification or multiple-prime confirmation) would strengthen correctness claims, especially for “previously undiscovered” identities.
- Scope limitations: The focus on homogeneous polynomials in a single matrix/vector with scalar outputs narrows applicability. Practical systems frequently involve multiple variables and non-polynomial operators; although the paper discusses generalization, the experiments remain in a constrained regime.
- Evaluation breadth: Comparisons are restricted to random, n-gram, and RNN strategies. Related search paradigms such as beam search, Monte Carlo Tree Search, A*/best-first with cost-aware heuristics, genetic programming with grammar constraints, or equality-saturation style rewriting could provide informative baselines. Even simple beams on the n-gram/RNN scores might change outcomes substantially.
- Search characterization: The scheduler appears to greedily pick the highest-scoring rule at each step. This could bias exploration and make the approach sensitive to early mistakes. The exploration strategy (any stochasticity, restarts, or beams) is not fully specified; time-to-solution distributions are shown, but search efficiency would benefit from clearer algorithms and ablations (e.g., greedy vs beam, with/without dropout noise at test time).
- Claims of novelty of discovered identities: Some examples (e.g., sum(sum(A*B)) re-expression) are standard linear algebra; for the more complex families, stronger evidence that discovered identities are new would be valuable (e.g., cross-checks against known identities in symmetric polynomials or linear algebra literature).
- RNN efficacy: Despite an appealing learned embedding with strong classification accuracy, the RNN policy often underperforms n-grams on harder families. A deeper analysis (e.g., error breakdown, impact of representation vs policy head, training data size effects, compositional generalization tests) would clarify where RNNs help and how to close the performance gap.
- Complexity accounting: The paper occasionally enforces efficiency by disallowing matrix-matrix multiply in the grammar. This ensures O(n^2) solutions but may hide interesting trade-offs. A more systematic complexity model (including dimensions and costs of vector-matrix vs matrix-matrix products, and constants) would improve interpretability.

Quality:
Overall technically sound, with a coherent framework and multiple practical components. The main area where rigor can be improved is the probabilistic equivalence test and the verification strategy for final identities. The experimental methodology is otherwise reasonable and reproducible (code provided), though baseline coverage could be broader.

Clarity:
Generally clear and well-organized. The grammar and search setup are understandable, and RNN training details are helpful. A more explicit description of the scheduler (greedy vs beam, restarts), complexity accounting, and the verification pipeline would improve clarity.

Originality:
Combining attribute grammars with learned search policies for algebraic identity discovery, and learning continuous embeddings of algebraic expressions via an RNN, is novel in this specific context. The n-gram idea is simple but effective; the RNN contribution is conceptually interesting even if not the top performer.

Significance:
The direction is promising: learned guidance can make combinatorial symbolic search feasible, with potential downstream impact on compiler optimizations and ML systems. While the current scope is constrained and baselines limited, the work opens a path for integrating learning with symbolic optimization.

Questions/requests for clarification:
- How is equivalence ultimately certified? Are multiple primes used, and if so, how many and with what bounds? Is there any symbolic verification for final candidates?
- What is the exact scheduler algorithm (greedy vs beam size k, restarts, temperature/exploration)?
- How are matrix dimensions handled in complexity estimates when shapes vary (e.g., non-square cases)?
- Can the RNN policy be improved with joint training (representation + policy), rather than freezing φ and only training the softmax?

Suggested revisions:
Essential:
- Add a verification protocol for discovered identities (e.g., re-check with multiple independent primes and provide collision probability bounds; optionally validate finalists symbolically).
- Describe the search procedure precisely (scheduler type, exploration parameters) and include a simple beam-search baseline; report its effect on success rates and time-to-solution.
- Provide stronger evidence for novelty of representative identities (e.g., literature checks for symmetric polynomials and standard linear algebra identities), or soften claims accordingly.

Optional (nice-to-have):
- Include additional search baselines (e.g., MCTS or genetic programming with the same grammar) to contextualize the learning gains.
- Analyze why n-grams outperform the RNN policy on some families; provide ablations on embedding dimensionality, policy training data size, and curriculum settings.
- Extend experiments to multi-variable targets (A, B, …) to demonstrate broader applicability, even at modest degrees.
- Report end-to-end computational savings, including constants, for a few identities in actual implementations.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Interesting and timely problem; clear framework; compelling use of learning to guide symbolic search.
- Practical contributions (descriptor approach, modular linear solver) and released code/data.
- Evidence that nontrivial identities can be discovered beyond brute-force.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Probabilistic equivalence checking lacks rigorous guarantees or a stated verification protocol.
- Limited comparative evaluation and narrow scope of targets.
- RNN policy underperforms simpler n-gram baselines on harder instances; limited analysis of failure modes.

Overall assessment:
A solid and thought-provoking contribution at the ML–symbolic computation interface. With stronger verification, clearer search methodology, and slightly broader baselines, this work would be a strong fit. As is, it presents meaningful ideas and results that merit discussion and could seed follow-on research.
