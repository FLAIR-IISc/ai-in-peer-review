- Proposes using an attribute grammar and learning-guided search to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities.
- Introduces a grammar framework for symbolic expressions annotated with computational complexity attributes.
- Develops two learning strategies to guide search: n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs).
- Applies RNNs to learn continuous representations of mathematical structures.
- Demonstrates discovery of several previously unknown identities with significant complexity reductions.
- Technical approach is sound with clear theoretical foundations in attribute grammars.
- Experimental evaluation is thorough, including ablation studies comparing random, n-gram, and RNN strategies.
- Results show learning approaches outperform random search for complex expressions; 3-gram is especially effective for repetitive patterns.
- Uses integer descriptors modulo a large prime to avoid floating-point issues in numerical representation.
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, and examples effectively illustrate core concepts.
- Originality is strong: novel application of ML to symbolic mathematics and clear differentiation from related fields.
- Significance is moderate due to scope limits (homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with a single matrix variable).
- Suggested improvements: show integration into math/software frameworks, discuss grammar limitations and extensions, and analyze why RNN underperforms n-grams on repetitive patterns.
- Main weakness: limited problem scope and scalability issues (e.g., RBM-2 family unsolved for k > 5).
- Broader impact: promising bridge between ML and symbolic computation; authors provide code and data for reproducibility.
- Recommendation: accept — valuable contribution demonstrating ML can guide combinatorial search in symbolic domains.
- Pros: novel approach, effective complexity improvements, clear framework and implementation, strong writing and examples.
- Cons: limited domain scope, some failures on hard families (RBM-2), and weaker connection to broader ML applications.
