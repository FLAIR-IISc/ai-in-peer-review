Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper tackles the discovery of computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning to guide search in a space of symbolic expressions defined by an attribute grammar. Given a target expression T (restricted to homogeneous polynomials, scalar output, typically one matrix or vector variable), the system searches for an equivalent expression S with lower computational complexity. The core components are: (i) a grammar of matrix/vector operators with associated complexity attributes; (ii) representations to test equivalence—either “numerical descriptors” created by evaluating expressions at many random integer inputs modulo a large prime or a learned continuous embedding from a recursive neural network (RNN); (iii) a scheduler/strategy search framework where the strategy is learned from identities found at smaller degrees to guide exploration for larger degrees; and (iv) solving linear combinations of trees via a linear system over Zp. Two learned strategies are investigated: n-gram statistics over successful subtrees and an RNN that embeds partial trees and predicts the next rule. Experiments on several expression families (matrix sums, elementwise-matrix interactions, elementary symmetric polynomials, and exponential sums related to RBM partition functions) show that learned strategies discover identities beyond random/brute force search, with n-grams often outperforming the RNN.

The approach connects to theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming but deliberately restricts the domain to avoid undecidability issues. It leverages ideas from NLP (n-grams; recursive networks for tree-structured inputs). Compared to CAS systems (Maple/Mathematica), which simplify via rule-based transformations, the focus here is on complexity reduction and learning search heuristics.

Strengths:
- Clear and timely problem: using learning to guide combinatorial search in symbolic domains, with potential impact for compiler-like optimizations and ML-relevant sums (e.g., RBM partition functions).
- Well-defined grammar framework and careful engineering of equivalence testing. The Zp-based descriptor addresses floating point issues and scales to large enumeration batches.
- Learning from simpler cases to harder ones (curriculum) is natural and effective; the n-gram strategy provides a surprisingly strong baseline; the RNN embedding for symbolic expressions is itself interesting and potentially reusable.
- The discovery of identities that reduce asymptotic complexity (e.g., reducing O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)), and O(n^3) to O(n^2) for certain families) is compelling. Code release increases reproducibility.
- Experiments include multiple families of increasing difficulty, and report success rates and runtimes under a fixed budget.

Weaknesses:
- Scope restrictions are strong (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, mostly single variable), and the paper does not quantify how easily the approach extends (e.g., multiple variables, non-polynomial operators, recursion).
- Equivalence via random evaluation modulo a prime is probabilistic. The paper lacks formal error bounds or safeguards (e.g., multiple primes, provable Schwartz–Zippel-style guarantees) and does not discuss potential false positives/negatives in the linear-combination solver over Zp.
- The evaluation metric (fraction of runs succeeding within 10 minutes) is coarse. There is limited analysis of search efficiency (e.g., nodes expanded, beam vs greedy), and speedups are not demonstrated on actual numerical runtimes beyond asymptotics.
- Baselines are limited. Comparisons to CAS simplifiers, hand-engineered heuristics, enumerative search with canonicalization/equivalence hashing, or grammar-guided genetic programming (GE) would strengthen claims. Related work in program synthesis/search (e.g., heuristic/A* search, MCTS, or SAT/SMT-based enumerators) is scarcely discussed.
- The RNN underperforms n-grams on the hardest families; the paper does not probe why (e.g., ablations for representation size, beam search over predicted rules, better regularization, or joint training of the embedding and action predictor).
- “Discovery of new identities” is asserted; the paper would benefit from (i) making key identities explicit in the main text, (ii) third-party verification or formal proofs, or (iii) pointers to how novelty was assessed.

Quality:
Technically sound overall and carefully implemented. The main methodological gaps are the lack of formal guarantees for the modulo-prime equivalence testing and limited baselines. Claims about novelty of identities and generality would benefit from more rigorous support.

Clarity:
Generally well organized, with motivating examples and training details for the RNN (initialization, curriculum, dropout). Some details are missing or could be clarified: the exact grammar (Table 1) and attribute costs, the scheduler (greedy vs beam), the linear solver’s coefficient domain and its interpretation over reals/integers, and dataset sizes for enumeration at each degree.

Originality:
Applying learned strategies (n-grams, RNNs) to guide identity discovery in a grammar-based symbolic space is novel for NIPS at the time and distinct from rule-based CAS or heuristic search. The continuous embedding for algebraic trees is a notable contribution.

Significance:
Promising direction with potential to influence symbolic computation, compiler optimization, and ML practice. The demonstrated identities and methodology are likely to inspire follow-up work. That said, current empirical gains rely heavily on n-grams, and the scope limitations temper the immediate impact.

Detailed comments and suggestions:
- Provide probabilistic guarantees for equivalence testing (e.g., use multiple random evaluation sets and primes; cite and apply Schwartz–Zippel-type bounds; discuss failure probability).
- Report actual runtime speedups on concrete matrix sizes for several identities and include the most impactful identities in the main paper, not only the supplement.
- Describe the coefficient domain in the linear combination step (are coefficients restricted to small integers? how do modulo-p solutions translate to valid integer identities?), and ensure validation over the integers/reals beyond Zp.
- Expand baselines: CAS with aggressive simplify/collect; grammar-guided GP; equivalence hashing with canonical forms; best-first or MCTS search with hand-crafted costs.
- Explore more powerful search policies: beam search using the learned scores; value functions that estimate distance-to-solution; or reinforcement learning over the grammar actions.
- Analyze why the RNN underperforms n-grams at higher k and whether larger embeddings, better negative sampling, or joint end-to-end training can help.
- Discuss scalability of the enumeration used to create RNN training labels and the limits of k where this remains feasible.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, well-motivated problem and framework with learning guiding combinatorial search.
- Interesting and reusable contribution: continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions via RNNs.
- Demonstrated discovery of nontrivial identities and significant asymptotic improvements.
- Code release and thoughtful engineering details (curriculum, initialization).

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited scope and generality; no formal guarantees for equivalence checking; claims of novelty not rigorously validated.
- Evaluation lacks strong baselines and more granular search efficiency metrics; practical runtime benefits not quantified.
- The learned RNN strategy underperforms simpler n-grams on harder tasks, with limited analysis.

Recommendation:
Borderline accept. The paper opens an exciting direction—learning to guide symbolic identity discovery—with substantive results and clear potential, despite evaluation and rigor gaps that should be addressed in a revision.
