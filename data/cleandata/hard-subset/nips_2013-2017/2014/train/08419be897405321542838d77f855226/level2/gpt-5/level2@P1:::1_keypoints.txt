- Paper tackles learning-guided search to discover computationally cheaper symbolic identities for homogeneous polynomial expressions (matrix/vector grammar).  
- Core system components: grammar with cost attributes, equivalence testing via Zp numerical descriptors or RNN embeddings, learned search scheduler from small-to-large degrees, and solving linear combinations over Zp.  
- Two learned strategies evaluated: n-gram statistics over successful subtrees and an RNN embedding that predicts next grammar rule.  
- Experiments on multiple expression families show learned strategies outperform random/brute-force search; n-grams often beat the RNN.  
- Relates to theorem proving, program induction, probabilistic programming, and NLP techniques, distinct from CAS rule-based simplifiers by focusing on complexity reduction.  

Strengths:
- Clear, timely problem with potential impact for compiler-style optimizations and ML sums (e.g., RBM partition functions).  
- Solid grammar framework and practical equivalence testing; Zp-based descriptors avoid floating-point issues and scale to batch enumeration.  
- Curriculum learning (train on simpler cases) is natural and effective; n-gram baseline is strong and RNN embeddings are a reusable contribution.  
- Demonstrated discovery of asymptotic complexity-reducing identities and released code for reproducibility.  
- Experiments cover multiple families with reported success rates and runtime budgets.  

Weaknesses:
- Strong scope restrictions (homogeneous polynomials, scalar output, mostly single variable); extensibility to broader domains is not quantified.  
- Equivalence testing via single-prime random evaluations is probabilistic with no formal error bounds or multi-prime safeguards discussed.  
- Coarse evaluation metric (fraction of runs within 10 minutes); limited analysis of search efficiency and no empirical runtime speedups reported.  
- Baselines are limited; missing comparisons to CAS simplifiers, enumerative canonicalization, GP, A*/MCTS, or SMT-based methods.  
- RNN underperforms n-grams on harder tasks with insufficient ablations to explain or fix this.  
- Claims of “new identities” lack formal proofs, explicit presentation in main text, or third-party verification.  

Quality:
- Technically sound and carefully implemented but lacks formal guarantees for Zp testing and stronger baselines to support claims.  

Clarity:
- Generally well organized, but missing exact grammar/costs, scheduler details (greedy vs beam), coefficient-domain interpretation, and enumeration dataset sizes.  

Originality:
- Novel application of learned strategies (n-grams, RNNs) to guide symbolic identity discovery; continuous embeddings for algebraic trees are notable.  

Significance:
- Promising direction likely to inspire follow-up work, though immediate impact tempered by scope limits and heavy reliance on n-grams.  

Key detailed suggestions:
- Provide probabilistic guarantees (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel bounds), use multiple primes/evaluation sets, and discuss failure probabilities.  
- Report concrete runtime speedups on realistic matrix sizes and include major identities in the main paper.  
- Clarify coefficient domain in linear-combination solving and validate Zp solutions over integers/reals.  
- Expand baselines to include CAS simplifiers, grammar-guided GP, canonicalization-based enumeration, and heuristic search methods.  
- Explore stronger search policies (beam search, value functions, RL) and investigate why RNN underperforms (ablation on embedding size, training, regularization).  
- Discuss scalability limits of enumeration used for RNN training and where curriculum breaks down.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, well-motivated framework with reusable RNN embeddings and evidence of nontrivial identity discovery; good engineering and code release.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited scope, no formal equivalence guarantees, weak baselines, coarse evaluation metrics, and RNN shortcomings with limited analysis.  

Recommendation:
- Borderline accept: promising direction with substantive results but requires revisions addressing rigor, baselines, and empirical validation.
