## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper explores a novel idea: leveraging machine learning, specifically DNNs, to discover mathematically equivalent but computationally cheaper expressions. The authors frame this as a search problem within an attribute grammar, and introduce both n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to guide this search, overcoming the limitations of brute-force approaches for complex expressions. This is a potentially interesting direction, as the automation of mathematical simplification has traditionally relied on symbolic manipulation rules rather than learning. The connection to related work in theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation is adequately addressed, highlighting the novelty of applying learning-based approaches to this specific problem.

However, despite the appealing premise, the paper currently feels preliminary and falls short of the standards typically expected for NIPS publication. The reported performance gains, while demonstrated on a few examples, are not particularly impressive. The improvement from O(n³) to O(n²) for a specific case (Example 1) is noteworthy, but the generalizability of this success seems limited. Furthermore, the experiments are conducted on a very constrained set of input expressions – primarily polynomial manipulations with a single matrix. This restricted domain raises the concern that hand-derived rules, or a simpler optimization strategy, might achieve similar results with less computational overhead (“sledgehammer to crack a nut”). 

The scalability of the approach is also unclear. While the authors mention the exponential growth of the search space, it’s not convincingly demonstrated that their learned strategies provide a substantial advantage beyond what could be achieved with more sophisticated heuristic search techniques. The RNN, in particular, doesn’t consistently outperform the simpler n-gram models. The discussion acknowledges the limitations, but the paper lacks a robust analysis of the computational cost of learning the models versus the benefits gained in simplification. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of DNNs to mathematical identity discovery.
*   Potentially interesting approach for automating mathematical simplification.
*   Clear presentation of the attribute grammar framework.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental validation and modest performance improvements.
*   Restricted scope of the experiments and potential for simpler alternative solutions.
*   Unclear scalability and justification for the complexity of the proposed methods.



Overall, the paper presents an interesting idea but requires significant further development and more comprehensive evaluation to meet NIPS standards. I recommend rejection at this time, but encourage the authors to continue exploring this promising research direction with a broader scope and a more rigorous assessment of performance and scalability.
