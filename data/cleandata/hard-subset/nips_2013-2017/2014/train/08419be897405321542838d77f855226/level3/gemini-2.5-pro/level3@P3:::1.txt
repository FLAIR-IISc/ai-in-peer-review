This paper proposes a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then use machine learning to guide the search through this combinatorially large space. The authors explore two main learning approaches: a simple n-gram model over grammar rules and a more sophisticated recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous representation of symbolic expressions. These models are trained on solutions found for simpler problems to help discover identities for more complex target expressions. The method is evaluated on five families of polynomial expressions, demonstrating that the learning-based strategies can find efficient identities where a random search fails.

This work sits at an interesting intersection of machine learning, symbolic computation, and automated reasoning. While symbolic computation engines like Mathematica and Maple can simplify expressions, they typically rely on predefined rule-based systems rather than statistical learning. The application of modern machine learning, particularly deep learning models like RNNs, to this symbolic domain is highly novel. The approach is conceptually related to program induction, but the authors constrain the problem to a more manageable space of fixed-degree polynomials. The use of an RNN to learn embeddings for symbolic trees echoes similar successful applications in natural language processing for parsing and sentiment analysis (e.g., Socher et al., 2013), but its application to formal mathematics is new and interesting.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novelty. Applying deep learning to the problem of discovering mathematical identities is a creative and thought-provoking idea. If successful and scalable, such an approach could have a significant impact on fields ranging from compiler design to theoretical physics, by automating the discovery of efficient computational shortcuts. The problem itself is fundamental and important.

2.  **Learned Representation:** The demonstration that an RNN can learn a meaningful continuous representation for symbolic expressions is a valuable contribution in its own right. This could pave the way for applying a wide range of standard machine learning tools to problems in symbolic domains, which have traditionally been difficult to tackle with statistical methods.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The authors do a good job of explaining their grammar-based framework, the numerical and learned representations, and the different search strategies. The inclusion of concrete examples (e.g., Example 1 & 2) helps build intuition.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Preliminary Nature of the Work:** While the idea is exciting, the overall work feels more like a proof-of-concept than a fully-fledged research paper. The evaluation is limited, and the results, while positive relative to a weak baseline, are not overwhelmingly strong. This gives the impression that the work is in its early stages and may not yet meet the high standards for publication at NIPS.

2.  **Modest Performance:** The performance of the proposed methods in discovering identities is not particularly impressive. For the most complex family of expressions (RBM-2), the learning methods fail to find solutions beyond k=5, performing no better than the random baseline. Furthermore, across several tasks, the simpler n-gram models outperform the more complex and computationally intensive RNN. This raises questions about the necessity and effectiveness of the deep learning component, which is presented as a key contribution.

3.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments are conducted on only five specific families of expressions. These expressions are quite restricted: they involve a single matrix or vector and produce a scalar output. This narrow domain makes it difficult to assess the generality of the proposed framework. The structure of the solutions for some families appears highly repetitive (as shown in Example 2), which naturally favors the n-gram model.

4.  **Scalability and Justification:** It is unclear whether this approach would scale to more general or complex mathematical domains. The introduction of more grammar rules (e.g., trigonometric functions, division, recursion) would lead to a much larger search space and potentially intractable branching factors. Given the modest results on a very constrained problem, the justification for scaling this complex machinery is not yet well established. For the limited problems explored, it feels like a "sledgehammer to crack a nut." It is plausible that hand-derived mathematical rules or simpler pattern-matching heuristics could have solved these specific problems more efficiently without the overhead of a machine learning framework.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper presents a highly original and interesting application of machine learning to the discovery of mathematical identities. The core idea is innovative and the framework is sensible. However, the execution feels preliminary. The experiments are conducted on a very limited set of expressions, and the performance of the proposed methods is modest, with simpler n-gram models often outperforming the more sophisticated RNN. The paper does not make a compelling case that the approach can scale to more general and complex mathematical problems, which is where its true value would lie.

While I find the direction of research fascinating and commend the authors for their creativity, the paper in its current form feels slightly below the threshold for acceptance at NIPS. It reads more like an intriguing workshop paper or an initial exploration of a promising idea. A stronger version would require a more comprehensive evaluation on a broader class of expressions, a more thorough analysis of the model's limitations, and a more convincing argument for its advantages over simpler, non-learning-based approaches.

**Recommendation: Reject (Borderline)**
