

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars combined with machine learning techniques. The authors propose representing symbolic expressions as trees built from a grammar of mathematical operators, and then using n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to guide the search for efficient versions of target expressions. The key insight is that learning from simpler expressions can help discover more complex identities beyond brute-force search capabilities.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution by applying machine learning to a symbolic problem domain, which is relatively unexplored in the NIPS literature. The attribute grammar framework is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how they represent symbolic expressions numerically and through learned continuous embeddings. The RNN approach to learning a continuous representation for symbolic expressions parallels work in NLP (e.g., Socher et al. 2013), which is appropriately cited.

The paper demonstrates the approach on five families of expressions, showing that learning-based search strategies can find efficient identities for certain cases that random search fails to discover. For example, the paper claims to discover identities that reduce computation from O(n³) to O(n²) for specific polynomial expressions. The results in Figure 3 show that n-gram models outperform random search for some expression families, though the improvement is modest for larger values of k.

**Strengths:**
- The core idea of applying machine learning to guide symbolic search is novel and well-motivated.
- The framework for learning continuous representations of symbolic expressions is technically interesting and has potential broader applications.
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with appropriate citations to related work in NLP and symbolic computation.
- The approach demonstrates a concrete application of machine learning to a problem domain where it's not commonly applied.

**Weaknesses:**
- The scope of the problem is extremely limited (single matrix/vector inputs, homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree), making the results less generalizable. The claimed "novel identities" are largely confined to specific, artificial cases that would likely be derivable by hand for the researchers.
- The experiments use an extremely narrow range of expressions, with no evidence that the approach would scale to more complex mathematical problems or real-world applications.
- The claimed significance is overstated. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the discovered identities have practical value in machine learning or other domains beyond the specific examples.
- The performance gains are modest at best, with the paper showing only small improvements in success rates for k > 5 (Figure 3), and no solution for the most challenging family (RBM-2) for k > 5.
- The approach feels like "sledgehammer to crack a nut" - the complexity of the machine learning approach seems disproportionate to the problem size and the limited scope of the identities discovered.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation
- Technical soundness of the framework
- Demonstrated proof of concept for a specific problem

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Problem scope is far too narrow to justify publication at NIPS
- Results are modest and lack clear significance for the field
- Approach doesn't scale to more meaningful mathematical problems
- The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the method offers advantages over simpler, hand-derived approaches for the limited domain considered

While the paper introduces an interesting technical idea, I believe it falls short of NIPS publication standards due to its limited scope, modest results, and lack of clear significance. The problem addressed is very specific and artificial, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the machine learning approach offers meaningful advantages over simpler, hand-derived solutions for the cases considered. The "discovered" identities are mostly confined to the narrow domain the authors define, and the paper doesn't provide evidence that the approach would be useful for more general mathematical problems or real-world applications.

The authors should either significantly broaden the scope of their experiments to demonstrate more general applicability, or more clearly justify why the limited domain they've chosen is sufficiently important to warrant publication. As it stands, the paper feels preliminary and doesn't make a strong enough contribution to the field of machine learning to justify publication at NIPS. I recommend rejection.
