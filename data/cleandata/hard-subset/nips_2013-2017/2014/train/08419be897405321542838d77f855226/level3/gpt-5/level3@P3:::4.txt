Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes learning-guided search to discover algebraically equivalent but computationally cheaper identities for symbolic expressions. The core idea is to represent expressions with an attribute grammar and search over expression trees for identities that match a target while (ideally) lowering computational complexity. Because brute-force enumeration is exponential, the authors train (i) n-gram models over subtrees and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of symbolic expressions and a softmax policy to guide tree construction. Matching to a target is done numerically via descriptors in Zp to avoid floating-point issues; more complex identities are formed as linear combinations of trees by solving a modular linear system.

The problem connects to automated theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, but focuses on a constrained polynomial domain to avoid undecidability. The grammar-driven approach is related to prior work on attribute grammars and to grammar-based search in NLP. The RNN use parallels work on tree-structured neural models in NLP and logical reasoning; here it is repurposed to embed symbolic math structures. Compared to rule-based CAS systems like Mathematica/Maple, the novelty is the data-driven search policy and learned expression embeddings.

Strengths
- Novelty: A compelling, well-motivated application of machine learning to symbolic algebra. The learned continuous representations for expressions and their use to guide search are original and interesting.
- Potential impact: If robust, the approach could augment CAS/compilers by automatically replacing high-cost patterns with cheaper identities and could assist ML algorithms (e.g., RBM partition function approximations, dropout-like sums).
- Empirical promise: On several expression families, n-gram/RNN policies find identities beyond the reach of random/brute-force; the learned embedding achieves low classification error over equivalence classes.
- Reproducibility: Code and data are promised.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited scope: The problem setting is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with a single variable and scalar outputs. The paper does not establish how methods extend to more general expressions (multiple variables, non-polynomial ops, recursion, non-scalar outputs).
- Complexity mismatch: The problem statement seeks lower complexity identities, but the search procedure generally does not enforce this constraint. In some experiments, complexity is bounded only by removing certain rules (e.g., matrix-matrix multiply). There is no general mechanism to bias/guarantee low-complexity solutions.
- Clarity/organization: The pipeline (grammar, numeric/learned representation, scheduler vs strategy, linear-combination solver) is difficult to follow on a first read. Key assumptions and constraints are scattered; Section 2 leaves several restrictions insufficiently justified.
- Technical specification gaps: Dimensionality/type constraints for grammar rules are not fully formalized. For example, the RBM-1 formulation in the supplement appears invalid or ill-defined for k=1, n=1. It’s unclear how shapes are enforced during search and in the RNN encoding.
- Evaluation limitations: No comparison against strong baselines (e.g., CAS simplification/FullSimplify with complexity-aware heuristics; hand-engineered theorem-prover heuristics). Correctness relies on randomized modular descriptors without a collision analysis or multiple primes. Impact on actual computational cost (wall-time/operation counts) is not consistently reported for discovered identities.

Quality
- Methodologically sound at a proof-of-concept level. However, claims about discovering lower-complexity identities would be stronger with an objective that encodes computational cost (e.g., attribute-cost-aware scoring, A*-like search on estimated complexity), formal or empirical guarantees on collision probability in the descriptor matching (e.g., use of multiple large primes), and explicit reporting of complexity reductions vs naive/CAS baselines.
- The RNN training details are helpful; still, ablations (e.g., effect of curriculum, dropout schedule, identity initialization) and generalization to higher k or across families would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- The presentation would benefit from a clearer separation of components and a running example throughout:
  - A formal typed grammar with dimensions and complexity attributes.
  - Pseudocode/flow diagram for the scheduler/strategy loop and for forming linear combinations.
  - A consolidated complexity model (time/space, op-cost assumptions) and how it influences search.
- Section 2 restrictions: It is unclear whether the scheduler is the primary limiting factor or whether the grammar/representation is. Provide formal justification (e.g., complexity of search with and without certain rules) or empirical studies showing how each restriction affects tractability and solution quality.

Originality
- The combination of attribute grammars, learned embeddings for symbolic expressions, and learning-guided search to discover computational identities is novel and well differentiated from prior NIPS work in NLP RNNs and from rule-based CAS simplification.

Significance
- Potentially significant if extended beyond the current scope and integrated with production symbolic systems. Immediate impact is more limited due to constraints and lack of baselines, but the direction is promising.

Specific suggestions
- Enforce low-complexity explicitly:
  - Incorporate an attribute-cost into the policy score (e.g., learned value function that predicts eventual complexity; regularize/punish costly ops).
  - Use A*-like search with admissible heuristics on complexity; prune branches whose minimal possible cost exceeds the current best.
  - Add grammar-side constraints (typed rules with per-op complexity budgets) rather than ad hoc rule removal.
- Formalize types and dimensions: Provide a typing system for vectors/matrices and operator preconditions; ensure the RNN respects types; clarify constraints so cases like RBM-1 at k=1, n=1 are well-defined or excluded.
- Correctness and robustness: Use multiple primes for descriptors and report collision bounds; for final identities, verify with a CAS or symbolic proof when feasible.
- Baselines and metrics: Compare to Mathematica/Maple (with complexity-aware options), and report operation counts and empirical runtimes. Include an ablation comparing random, n-gram, RNN, and hybrids.
- Organization: Rework the exposition to present (1) problem and complexity model, (2) typed attribute grammar, (3) equality testing (numeric vs learned), (4) search (scheduler/strategies), (5) linear combinations, (6) experiments with clear per-family complexity results.

- Please clarify vector/matrix sizes throughout; ensure RBM-1 and RBM-2 definitions are valid for all k, n considered.

Pros for acceptance
- Innovative application of learned representations to symbolic math.
- Demonstrated ability to find nontrivial identities; promising empirical results on several families.
- Potentially impactful direction for ML-guided program synthesis and CAS integration.

Cons for acceptance
- Restricted problem scope with unclear extensibility.
- Lack of explicit complexity control; occasional ad hoc constraints.
- Organizational and specification clarity issues; missing baselines and stronger correctness guarantees.

Overall
A creative and promising paper that opens a valuable direction. With clearer specification, stronger complexity-aware search, and comparative evaluation, this could be a strong NIPS contribution. In its current form, I am positive about the idea but would like to see the above issues addressed.
