

This paper introduces an innovative framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques applied to attribute grammars. The authors propose a novel approach where symbolic expressions are represented as trees, and learning methods (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) guide the search for expressions with equivalent results but lower computational complexity. The work builds on attribute grammars from compiler construction and borrows techniques from NLP, particularly n-gram language models and recursive neural networks for parsing trees, as seen in Socher et al. (2013) and Bowman (2013).

The paper's strengths are significant. It demonstrates high technical quality with a well-structured framework, clear problem definition, and thorough experimental validation. The originality is high, as the application of learning to symbolic identity discovery represents a novel intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The experiments successfully show the framework can discover complex identities beyond brute-force search, with n-gram models outperforming RNNs on two of three tasks (Figure 3).

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. Clarity is limited, particularly in Section 7.2, where the experimental setup and interpretation of results are difficult to follow. The significance is rated low, as the problem appears somewhat contrived—the restricted domain of homogeneous polynomials with fixed degree k limits practical applicability. The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate why these discovered identities would be adopted in real-world computational settings, and the practical usefulness of the proposed methods remains unclear. While the framework is technically sound, the problem's relevance to broader computational challenges is not adequately justified.

Arguments for acceptance: High technical quality, strong originality, and promising experimental results. Arguments against: Low significance, unclear practical utility, and limited clarity in key sections. The paper makes an interesting contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation, but its narrow focus and unclear practical impact prevent it from being highly significant for the broader AI community. The work would benefit from a stronger justification of its practical relevance and improved clarity in the methodology and results sections.
