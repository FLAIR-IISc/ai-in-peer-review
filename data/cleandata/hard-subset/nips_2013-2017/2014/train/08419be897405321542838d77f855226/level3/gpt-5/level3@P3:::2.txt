Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the problem of automatically discovering algebraically equivalent but computationally more efficient forms of given symbolic expressions. It introduces an attribute-grammar framework for constructing expression trees and searching the space of compositions under complexity-aware constraints. Equivalence checking is performed numerically via modular arithmetic descriptors, and complex targets may be expressed as linear combinations of multiple trees by solving a linear system over Zp.

To make the exponentially large search tractable, the authors propose two learned search strategies trained on solutions to simpler instances: (1) n-gram models over sequences of subtrees (sub-expression composition rules), and (2) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of subtrees and a softmax policy to predict the next combination rule. They demonstrate applications to several families of expressions (matrix polynomials, elementwise products, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related sums), reporting that higher-order n-gram models generally outperform the RNN on two of three plotted families (Figure 3), while both approaches beat a random baseline on many instances.

The work connects to program induction, automated theorem proving, and probabilistic programming, but restricts to homogeneous polynomials with scalar outputs to avoid undecidability. Compared to CAS systems (Maple, Mathematica), which simplify but do not learn search heuristics for lower-complexity equivalents, the paper’s contribution is the data-driven search over a grammar. It also relates to NLP applications of recursive neural nets and to grammar-guided optimization, while differing from classical symbolic regression by focusing on provable equivalence and complexity reduction rather than fitting.

Strengths
- Clear problem statement and a principled grammar-based search space with explicit complexity attributes.
- Two distinct, well-motivated learning strategies for guiding search; the n-gram approach is simple and surprisingly effective.
- A clever equivalence-checking setup via integer descriptors and modular linear algebra to support combining multiple trees.
- Evidence that the learned strategies discover identities out of reach of random/brute-force search and sometimes beyond manual derivation.
- RNN achieves low error in expression-class classification, suggesting promising continuous embeddings for symbolic structures.
- Code and data are provided, supporting reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Significance and practical impact are uncertain. The target families feel somewhat contrived, and it is unclear if discovered identities translate to meaningful speedups in real workloads or compilers.
- The evaluation reports “fraction of successful runs within 10 minutes” rather than wall-clock speedups on numerical benchmarks or end-to-end applications. No runtime or memory usage measurements are shown.
- The n-gram method outperforms the RNN on two of three plotted families, suggesting limited advantage of the more complex model for this search problem; analysis of failure modes is thin.
- Clarity and organization are uneven. Section 7.2, in particular, is hard to follow; several key design choices (e.g., removing matrix-matrix multiply to force O(n^2) solutions) are buried and can bias conclusions.
- The numerical-equality approach lacks discussion of collision probability, choice of prime p and N, and whether solutions found modulo p always lift to valid integer coefficients with reasonable magnitude.
- Limited baselines. No comparisons to modern compiler algebraic optimizations, equality-saturation/e-rewriting style methods, or heuristic tree search (beam/A*/MCTS). CAS comparisons are qualitative rather than empirical.

Quality
- High-level technical soundness is good. The grammar, descriptor scheme, and learning strategies are coherent. However, the support for the core claim—computational efficiency—relies on asymptotic argument and success counts rather than concrete runtime improvements. The RNN representation results are promising but only indirectly tied to identity discovery performance.

Clarity
- Moderate to low. The paper is ambitious but sometimes difficult to follow. Section 7.2 interleaves families, rules disabled, and reporting choices in a way that obscures the setup. The scheduler-strategy interface and training curricula could use clearer pseudocode and flow diagrams.

Originality
- High. Learning-guided search for efficient symbolic identities in an attribute-grammar setting, with modular arithmetic descriptors and continuous subtree embeddings, is novel and interesting.

Significance
- Low to moderate. Without demonstrations on realistic computational kernels or compiler pipelines and without runtime evaluations, it is hard to judge real-world impact. The task design (e.g., forbidding matrix-matrix multiply) raises concerns about generalizability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide end-to-end runtime benchmarks on numeric workloads (varying n, k) comparing naive, discovered identities, and CAS/compiler baselines.
- Clarify the equality-testing pipeline: bounds on collision probability, sensitivity to p and N, and how integer coefficients are recovered and validated.
- Expand baselines: beam search with hand-crafted heuristics, A* with admissible cost, MCTS, and comparisons to CAS simplification plus strength reduction, or to grammar-based superoptimization.
- Analyze why n-grams outperform the RNN: ablations on representation dimensionality, curriculum, dropout, and whether the RNN learns mostly local patterns anyway.
- Improve exposition of Section 7.2 with a consistent template per family: grammar subset used, complexity target, search space size, training data carried over, success metric, and representative discovered identities.
- Report the magnitude and sparsity of linear-combination coefficients, and whether they lead to numerically stable, implementable formulas.

Questions for the authors
- What is the probability that two distinct expressions collide under the Zp descriptor, and how was p and N chosen in practice?
- Do the linear-combination coefficients ever grow large or dense? Are there constraints to prefer sparse/low-magnitude solutions?
- How sensitive are results to the curriculum schedule and to the specific families used for pretraining?
- Can the approach handle non-homogeneous expressions or multiple inputs without blowing up the search?
- Have you attempted to integrate with a compiler or CAS to realize actual speedups on real code?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel learning formulation bridging symbolic math and ML.
- Solid technical framework with released code.
- Empirical evidence that learning-guided search can beat brute force and random, and can discover nontrivial identities.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited evidence of practical impact; contrived benchmarks.
- Clarity issues, especially in the experimental section.
- Simpler n-grams outperform the RNN on most plotted tasks, undermining the need for the more complex modeling.
- Missing runtime evaluations and stronger baselines.

Recommendation
- Weak reject. The paper is original and technically competent, but its practical significance is unclear, and the exposition—especially of the evaluation—needs strengthening. With clearer experiments demonstrating real speedups and stronger baselines, this could be a compelling contribution.
