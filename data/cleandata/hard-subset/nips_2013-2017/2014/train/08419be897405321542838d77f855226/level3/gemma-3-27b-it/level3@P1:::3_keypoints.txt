- Paper applies machine learning (n-gram models and a recursive neural network) within an attribute grammar to discover computationally cheaper symbolic expressions equivalent to a target expression.
- Core idea: learn from simple expressions to guide search for more complex mathematical identities.
- Novel contribution: combining attribute grammars with learned search strategies and reporting discovery of previously unknown identities (e.g., O(n^2) vs O(n^3) improvements).
- Problem is clearly defined in a formal setting focused on finding efficient symbolic expressions.
- Empirical evaluation shows learning approaches—especially n-gram models—accelerate identity discovery as expression complexity grows.
- Learned expression representations succeed at classification tasks, supporting feasibility of the approach.
- Authors provide open-source code and data, aiding reproducibility.
- Potential practical impact: discovered identities could optimize ML computations (e.g., dropout, RBMs).
- Major limitation: problem domain is narrowly restricted to homogeneous polynomials of degree k with a single matrix variable, limiting broader applicability.
- Unclear model description: ambiguity over the weight tensor W3 and whether the model is a standard RNN or a neural tensor network (NTN).
- Missing justification for choosing an RNN over alternatives (e.g., tensor networks) that may better capture structural relationships.
- Experimental details are insufficient: unclear integration of the RNN into the search procedure and how strategies are compared.
- Presentation issues: multiple typos/grammatical errors and mislabeled figure (Figure 3 labels RNN as TNN) reduce readability.
- Scalability concerns: RNN does not show significant improvement on the hardest task (RBM-2k), leaving larger-scale effectiveness open.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, discovery of useful identities, supporting experiments (notably n-grams), and public release of resources.
- Arguments against acceptance: narrow domain, unclear architecture and experimental methodology, limited RNN gains over simpler methods, and need for substantial manuscript polishing.
- Overall assessment: interesting and potentially valuable work; reviewer leans weak accept contingent on clarifying RNN architecture, expanding experimental detail, and improving presentation.
