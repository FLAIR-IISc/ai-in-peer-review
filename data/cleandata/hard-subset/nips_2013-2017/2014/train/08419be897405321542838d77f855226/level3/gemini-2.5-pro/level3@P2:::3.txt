This paper presents a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions within an attribute grammar and search through the space of possible expression trees to find alternatives to a target expression that are analytically equivalent but have lower computational complexity. Recognizing that a brute-force search of this combinatorial space is intractable, the main contribution is the application of machine learning to guide the search. The authors propose two learning-based search strategies: a simple n-gram model and a more sophisticated recursive neural network (RNN). These models are trained on solutions to simpler expressions and then used to prune the search space for more complex targets. A key element of this work is the novel use of an RNN to learn a continuous, distributed representation for symbolic expressions, analogous to word embeddings in natural language processing. The authors demonstrate their framework by discovering new, non-trivial identities for several families of complex expressions, some of which are relevant to machine learning.

This work relates to several fields, including automated theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation. However, it distinguishes itself by its focus on computational efficiency rather than just logical equivalence and, most notably, by its application of modern statistical learning techniques (n-grams, RNNs) to a traditionally rule-based symbolic domain. The use of RNNs for learning representations of structured, symbolic data connects this work to recent advances in natural language processing and knowledge representation (e.g., Socher et al., 2013; Bowman, 2013).

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The application of modern machine learning techniques to the symbolic problem of discovering mathematical identities is a highly novel and significant research direction. It bridges the gap between statistical learning and symbolic reasoning, and the successful results suggest this is a very promising avenue for future work. The potential to automatically discover efficient identities for computations in compilers or scientific computing is of high practical importance.

2.  **Learned Symbolic Representations:** The concept of learning a continuous vector representation for symbolic expressions is a powerful contribution. Much as word and sentence embeddings have enabled deep learning to be applied to a wide range of NLP tasks, these symbolic embeddings could unlock new ways of applying machine learning to mathematics and program optimization. The high accuracy on the expression classification task provides strong evidence that the learned representations are meaningful.

3.  **Strong Empirical Demonstration:** The paper successfully demonstrates the discovery of complex and apparently novel identities for non-trivial expression families (e.g., symmetric polynomials and terms related to RBM partition functions). The fact that the learning-guided search methods significantly outperform a random baseline on difficult problems highlights the effectiveness of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in several key areas, which makes it difficult to fully understand the method and reproduce the results.
    *   The architecture of the recursive neural network is ambiguously described. The text mentions a "weight tensor W3 (of size lxlxl)," which suggests a neural tensor network, but the model is consistently referred to as an RNN. A precise mathematical definition of the composition function is needed.
    *   The search procedure is explained at a very high level. The paper should provide more detail on how new expressions are proposed and how the scheduler precisely uses the scores from the n-gram or RNN models to guide the construction of the tree.
    *   The connection between the RNN used for classification (Section 7.1) and the RNN used for the search strategy (Section 7.2) is not well-explained. It is unclear if the same model is used or how it is adapted for the search task.

2.  **Model Justification and Related Work:** The choice of an RNN is not sufficiently justified in the context of related work. For instance, Bowman (2013) investigates similar recursive models for logical reasoning and finds that tensor networks can outperform standard RNNs. The paper would be strengthened by citing this work and discussing why an RNN was chosen over a tensor network, or by providing an empirical comparison.

3.  **Restricted Problem Scope:** The problem is currently defined for a very restricted class of expressions: homogeneous polynomials of a single variable resulting in a scalar. While this is a reasonable starting point, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the challenges involved in extending this framework to a more general class of mathematical expressions.

4.  **Quality of Presentation:** The manuscript contains several typographical and grammatical errors, as well as some repeated sentences, which suggests a lack of careful proofreading. For example, the opening sentence of the introduction appears twice. Additionally, Figure 3 incorrectly labels the model as "TNN" when the text describes it as an RNN.

In summary, this paper introduces a fascinating and promising research direction with strong initial results. The core idea of using learned heuristics to navigate a symbolic search space is highly compelling. However, the paper is currently held back by significant clarity issues regarding the central components of the method (the RNN architecture and the search procedure) and a lack of engagement with closely related work on model choices. If the authors can substantially revise the paper to address these clarity and justification issues, it would represent a strong contribution to the conference.
