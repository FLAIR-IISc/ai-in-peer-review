- Proposes a novel framework to discover efficient mathematically equivalent identities using attribute grammars and learning-guided search.
- Frames the task as finding alternative symbolic expressions with lower computational complexity that are analytically equivalent to a target.
- Introduces two learning approaches to guide combinatorial search: an n-gram model over sub-expressions and a recursive neural network (RNN) embedding to predict grammar rules.
- Builds on attribute grammars and borrows NLP ideas (n-grams, RNNs), applying them to a new symbolic-computation problem.
- Technical quality is strong with a clear formal problem statement, solid experimental design, and well-executed representation learning (curriculum learning, weight init).
- Experiments show both learners outperform random search on complex targets; n-gram outperforms RNN on two of three expression families.
- Learned representation achieves good classification accuracy (≤5% error for k=3–6), validating continuous embeddings of symbolic expressions.
- Originality is high — first work (to reviewer’s knowledge) applying learning to grammar-based discovery of mathematical identities.
- Clarity issues: Section 7.2 and the description of expression families/complexities are confusing and need reorganization and clearer explanations.
- Significance is questionable: claimed practical uses (e.g., closed-form dropout, RBM partition computation) lack concrete evidence or demonstrations.
- Practical utility limited by restrictive problem scope (single matrix variable, scalar output) and lack of improvement on the hardest family (RBM-2 for k>5).
- Recommendation: technically sound and novel but needs stronger motivation, clearer experimental presentation, and concrete real-world applications to justify acceptance.
