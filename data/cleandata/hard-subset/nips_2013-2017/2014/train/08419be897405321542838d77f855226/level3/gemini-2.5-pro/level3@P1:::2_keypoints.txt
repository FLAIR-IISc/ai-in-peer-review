- Introduces a framework to automatically find computationally efficient equivalents of symbolic mathematical expressions using an attribute grammar of operations.  
- Represents expressions as trees and learns a search policy because brute-force search over trees is intractable.  
- Trains the search strategy on solutions to simpler expressions so it can generalize to more complex targets.  
- Proposes two learning approaches to guide search: an n-gram model capturing common subtrees and a recursive neural network (RNN) learning continuous tree embeddings.  
- RNN is used to predict the next grammar rule to apply when constructing an expression equivalent to the target.  
- Evaluates on five families of polynomial expressions and compares learning-based strategies to random search.  
- Finds learning-based strategies, especially n-gram models, can discover complex identities unreachable by random search.  
- Notes that simpler n-gram models often outperform the more sophisticated RNN on several tasks.  
- All methods fail on the most complex family (RBM-2) for k > 5, indicating scalability limits.  
- Technical quality is high: well-conceived framework, complete system, and systematic experiments.  
- Clarity is lacking: experimental setup (Section 7.2), dense family descriptions, and RNN training mechanics need clearer, more explicit exposition.  
- Originality is high: novel application of NLP techniques to symbolic identity discovery and development of learned continuous representations for expressions.  
- Significance is questionable: problem families seem contrived, practical utility and integration into real-world tools are unclear.  
- Practical overhead concerns: grammar setup, data generation, and model training may not justify gains versus existing optimized rule-based systems.  
- Arguments for acceptance: high originality, technical soundness, and a promising new research direction bridging ML and symbolic computation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited practical impact, mixed experimental evidence (n-gram beating RNN), clarity issues, and scalability failures on hardest tasks.
