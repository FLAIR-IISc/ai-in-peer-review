## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper tackles the intriguing problem of automatically discovering mathematically equivalent, but computationally cheaper, expressions. The authors propose a novel framework based on attribute grammars, coupled with two learning approaches – n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) – to guide the search for these more efficient identities. The core idea of learning from simpler expressions to tackle more complex ones is compelling and represents a unique application of machine learning to symbolic manipulation. This work builds upon existing research in symbolic computation (e.g., Maple, Mathematica) by introducing a learning component absent in traditional rule-based systems, and draws inspiration from NLP techniques like n-gram modeling and recursive neural networks.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The originality is high; the combination of attribute grammars with machine learning for this specific problem is a novel contribution. The quality is also high, with a well-defined problem, a clear methodology, and experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approaches. The learned representation using the RNN is particularly interesting and shows promise for broader applications in symbolic AI. 

However, the paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly in Section 7.2, where the experimental setup and results are difficult to follow. The significance of the work is currently low. While the technical achievement is notable, the problem itself feels somewhat contrived. The focus on polynomial simplification, while a good starting point, doesn’t immediately translate to practical, real-world computational efficiency gains. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate how these discovered identities would be applied to accelerate common algorithms or solve practical problems. The experimental evaluation, while thorough, focuses on a limited set of families of expressions. 

Furthermore, the n-gram method consistently outperforms the RNN, raising questions about the effectiveness of the more complex neural network approach in this context. While the authors acknowledge this, a deeper analysis of *why* the n-gram model performs better would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of machine learning to symbolic expression manipulation.
*   High technical quality and well-defined methodology.
*   Demonstrated ability to discover identities beyond brute-force search.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Limited clarity, especially in the experimental section.
*   Low significance – the problem feels contrived and lacks clear practical applications.
*   RNN performance is inferior to the simpler n-gram model.



Overall, this is a technically sound and original paper with a clear potential. However, addressing the clarity issues and better articulating the practical significance of the work would significantly strengthen the contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on improved clarity and a more compelling discussion of potential real-world applications.
