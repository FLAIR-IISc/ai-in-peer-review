- Novel approach: uses attribute grammars plus n-gram and recursive neural models to guide search for efficient equivalent expressions.  
- Originality: applying machine learning to automated discovery of mathematical identities is a genuinely novel contribution.  
- Framework: provides a structured pipeline for searching compositional expressions using learned priors.  
- Empirical promise: demonstrates discovery of identities beyond manual derivation and exhaustive search.  
- Potential impact: could significantly improve symbolic computation libraries by automating efficient computation paths.  
- Limited scope: experiments are restricted to relatively simple polynomial expressions and do not show extensibility to harder domains.  
- Complexity mismatch: problem statement targets lower computational complexity but the algorithm does not enforce complexity constraints during search.  
- Search limitation concern: authors should restrict or bias search toward low-complexity solutions; current method may not ensure that.  
- Clarity and organization: paper flow is disjoint and needs a clearer end-to-end explanation of grammar definition, search strategy, and verification.  
- Unsupported claims: Section 2’s restrictions (e.g., scheduler as bottleneck) are asserted without empirical evidence.  
- Technical issue: RBM-1 expression in the supplement appears invalid for k=1, n=1 (vector/matrix size constraints).  
- Minor errors: several typos present (lines 332, 148, 289, 338) that need correction.  
- Recommendation: interesting and original but insufficient scope, justification, and clarity to strongly recommend acceptance without revisions.
