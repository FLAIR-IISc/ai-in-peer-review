- Summary: Learns search policies over an attribute-grammar tree space to discover efficient algebraic identities using n-gram and RNN strategies, with numeric Zp descriptors for equivalence testing.
- Scope restriction: Experiments focus on homogeneous single-variable scalar polynomials; broader applicability to multivariate/non-polynomial/non-scalar cases is not demonstrated or justified.
- Two strategies: simple subtree n-gram model (strong baseline) and a recursive neural network that learns continuous embeddings to guide search.
- Representation/equivalence: Uses evaluations modulo a large prime (Zp) to test expression equivalence and learned embeddings for classification and search guidance.
- Empirical gains: Finds O(n2) algorithms from naive O(n3) in some matrix families and polynomial-time approximations for otherwise exponential expressions.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on attribute-grammar optimization and recursive neural networks from NLP/logical reasoning; contrasts with rule-based CAS systems.
- Strength: Interesting, timely problem of learning to guide combinatorial symbolic search with a clear high-level framework.
- Strength: n-gram baseline is simple, effective, and often outperforms random search and the RNN.
- Strength: RNN achieves low classification error, indicating useful learned structure; code and data are available.
- Major ambiguity: RNN composition function is underspecified—unclear if W3 denotes an NTN/RNTN or a simpler multiplicative tensor, and whether linear terms/biases are used or parameters are shared.
- Missing NTN comparison: Paper does not justify using a simpler RNN over NTNs despite prior work showing NTNs often perform better on logical tasks.
- Training/usage ambiguity: Unclear whether RNN trained as classifier is reused or fine-tuned as the next-rule policy, and which objectives guide the search model.
- Search procedure underspecified: Scheduler details missing—greedy vs beam/backtracking, tie-breaking, cycle/duplicate prevention, canonicalization/memoization, and shape/type constraint enforcement.
- Proposal-generation gap: Unclear handling when binary branches are incomplete (b2 set to random vector)—design rationale and effects not justified.
- Linear solver selection: Criteria for selecting multiple trees for linear-combination solver (M and N selection, pruning) are unspecified.
- Evaluation clarity: Need to state whether RNN is used only for next-rule or also to rank completed trees, provide wall-clock budgets, tree expansion counts, and variance across runs.
- Zp collision risk: No reported collision/certification rate for Zp descriptors or justification of chosen p and N per problem family.
- Results interpretation lacking: n-grams outperform RNN on several families—discussion missing on why and how RNN could be improved (NTN, curriculum, regularization).
- Presentation errors: Figure labeling (“TNN” vs RNN/NTN), repeated sentences, typos, inconsistent notation and spacing—these hinder readability.
- Quality assessment: Technically promising but underspecified in key methodological parts; stronger baselines and ablations needed.
- Clarity assessment: Generally readable but with crucial ambiguities and several editorial errors to fix.
- Originality: Novel application of learned representations and policies to discover algebraic identities via attribute grammars.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for compiler and symbolic ML optimization, but current narrow scope limits immediate significance.
- Key questions for authors: Precisely define the binary composition function and NTN usage; report search control strategies tried and sensitivity; provide Zp collision rates and chosen p/N values; clarify parameter sharing and regularization.
- Pros for acceptance: Novel formulation, evidence that learning accelerates discovery, and public code/data.
- Cons for acceptance: Methodological ambiguities, missing NTN comparison, narrow problem setting, and presentation issues.
- Recommendation: Weak reject pending clarifications, NTN baseline or justification, detailed search procedure (possibly beam-search ablations), and polished writing/figures.
