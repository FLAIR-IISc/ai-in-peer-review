This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and develop two learning approaches, an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN), to guide the search for efficient identities. The framework is demonstrated on several families of expressions, including those related to machine learning, and shows promising results in discovering complex identities that random or brute-force strategies cannot find.

The paper relates to previous work in theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, but differs in its focus on discovering efficient identities for polynomials with fixed degree. The use of machine learning techniques to guide the search is a significant contribution, as it allows the framework to explore a vast space of possible expressions efficiently.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the framework, the learning approaches, and the experimental results. The writing is generally clear, although there are some minor typos and unclear sentences.

The strengths of the paper include:

* Novel contribution: The paper introduces a new approach to discovering mathematical identities using machine learning techniques.
* Potential impact: The approach could benefit symbolic software libraries and potentially lead to more efficient computations.
* Experimental results: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their framework on several families of expressions.

However, there are also some weaknesses and concerns:

* Limited scope: The studied expressions are very restricted, and it is unclear whether the approach can be extended to more complex expressions.
* Complexity mismatch: The problem statement seeks lower computational complexity, but the algorithm does not enforce this constraint. The authors should consider ways to limit the search to low-complexity solutions.
* Section 2 restrictions: The justification for the restrictions on the expressions is unclear, and more formal justification or empirical studies are needed.
* Organization: The paper structure and stages are hard to follow and should be explained more clearly.
* Technical concern: The constraints on vector/matrix sizes are unclear, and the RBM-1 expression in the supplement seems invalid for k=1, n=1.

To improve the paper, the authors could:

* Provide more formal justification or empirical studies for the restrictions on the expressions.
* Consider ways to limit the search to low-complexity solutions.
* Clarify the constraints on vector/matrix sizes.
* Improve the organization and clarity of the paper.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper introduces a novel approach to discovering mathematical identities using machine learning techniques.
* The approach has the potential to benefit symbolic software libraries and lead to more efficient computations.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the framework on several families of expressions.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The scope of the studied expressions is very restricted, and it is unclear whether the approach can be extended to more complex expressions.
* The algorithm does not enforce the constraint of lower computational complexity, which is a key aspect of the problem statement.
* The paper has some minor typos and unclear sentences that need to be addressed.

Overall, I recommend acceptance of the paper, but with revisions to address the concerns and weaknesses mentioned above.
