- Proposes an attribute grammar-based framework to discover computationally equivalent mathematical identities with lower complexity.
- Uses learning-based search strategies (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) trained on simpler expressions to tame exponential search growth.
- Demonstrates examples where discovered identities reduce complexity from O(n^3) to O(n^2) for certain matrix operations.
- Builds on prior work in attribute grammars (Knuth) and continuous symbolic representations (Mnih & Hinton, Mikolov et al.), and relates to RNN work in NLP.
- Technically sound framework with appropriate numerical and learned expression comparison and reasonable experimental validation showing learning > random search.
- Problem setting is overly restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, limiting applicability to broader mathematical expressions.
- Explanation and justification of the weight tensor W3 (l×l×l) is unclear; it appears to be a tensor network without comparison to simpler linear models.
- Clarity issues: confusing description of the recursive neural network operation and unclear proposal generation process that hinders reproducibility.
- Presentation problems: Figure 3 mislabels RNN as "TNN," multiple duplicate sentences, grammatical errors throughout.
- Section 7.2 is unclear about how the RNN is used for classification versus guiding search, and evaluation of search strategies lacks detail.
- Original and well-motivated idea to use learning to guide symbolic search; attribute grammar application is a significant contribution.
- Novel use of continuous/RNN representations for symbolic expressions with potential broader impact in symbolic computation.
- Potential significance for ML tasks (e.g., dropout approximations, RBM partition functions) and integration with compilers/optimization tools, but real-world use is not demonstrated.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel ML application to symbolic identity discovery, demonstrated complexity gains, defined framework with experiments, and continuous symbolic representations.
- Arguments against acceptance: restricted problem domain, critical clarity and presentation issues, insufficient justification for tensor networks, and limited demonstrated real-world impact.
- Recommendation: promising work but requires substantial revisions to clarify methodology, justify model choices, and strengthen significance before acceptance.
