- Summary: Proposes an attribute-grammar framework to discover algebraically equivalent but lower-complexity symbolic expressions, using learned search and modular-arithmetic-based equivalence checking.
- Search methods: Two learned strategies—n-gram models over subtree composition rules and an RNN learning continuous subtree embeddings with a softmax policy.
- Equivalence checking: Uses integer descriptors modulo p and solves linear systems over Zp to express targets as linear combinations of trees.
- Results: Evaluated on several expression families; n-gram models often outperform the RNN and both beat random baselines on many instances.
- Relation to prior work: Connects to program induction, theorem proving, probabilistic programming, and differs from CAS and symbolic regression by learning search heuristics for provably equivalent, lower-complexity forms.
- Strength: Clear problem formulation and principled, complexity-aware grammar search space.
- Strength: Two well-motivated learning strategies, with n-grams simple and surprisingly effective.
- Strength: Clever modular-arithmetic equivalence-checking enabling combination of multiple trees.
- Strength: Demonstrates discovery of nontrivial identities beyond brute-force/random search and sometimes manual derivation.
- Strength: RNN yields promising low-error expression-class classification and continuous embeddings.
- Strength: Code and data released for reproducibility.
- Weakness: Practical significance unclear; target families seem contrived and real-world speedups are not demonstrated.
- Weakness: Evaluation reports success counts within 10 minutes instead of concrete wall-clock speedups, runtime, or memory usage.
- Weakness: N-gram outperforming RNN questions the need for the complex model; failure-mode analysis is thin.
- Weakness: Presentation and organization uneven, especially Section 7.2 which buries key design choices that can bias conclusions.
- Weakness: Equivalence-testing lacks discussion of collision probability, choice of p and N, and lifting modular solutions to integer coefficients.
- Weakness: Limited baselines—no comparisons to compiler algebraic optimizations, equality saturation/e-rewriting, or heuristic tree search (beam/A*/MCTS); CAS comparisons are qualitative.
- Quality: Technically coherent and sound, but core claim of computational efficiency is supported by asymptotic arguments and success counts rather than concrete runtime improvements.
- Clarity: Moderate to low; needs clearer pseudocode, flow diagrams, and more consistent experimental reporting.
- Originality: High—novel combination of grammar-guided search, learned heuristics, modular descriptors, and continuous subtree embeddings.
- Significance: Low to moderate until real kernels, compiler integration, or runtime evaluations are shown; some task design choices limit generalizability.
- Suggestions: Add end-to-end runtime benchmarks, clarify equality-testing/collision bounds and coefficient recovery, expand baselines (beam/A*/MCTS, CAS/compiler comparisons), analyze why n-grams beat RNN, and improve exposition of experiments.
- Questions for authors: What is collision probability under Zp and how were p and N chosen? Do coefficients grow large/dense and are sparse/low-magnitude constraints used? How sensitive are results to curriculum and family choices? Can the method handle non-homogeneous/multi-input expressions? Any attempts to integrate with compilers/CAS?
- Pros for acceptance: Novel learning formulation, solid technical framework, released code, evidence learning-guided search outperforms brute force.
- Cons against acceptance: Limited practical impact, clarity and experimental reporting issues, simpler models often outperform the RNN, missing runtime evaluations and stronger baselines.
- Recommendation: Weak reject; paper is original and competent but needs clearer experiments demonstrating real speedups and stronger baselines to be compelling.
