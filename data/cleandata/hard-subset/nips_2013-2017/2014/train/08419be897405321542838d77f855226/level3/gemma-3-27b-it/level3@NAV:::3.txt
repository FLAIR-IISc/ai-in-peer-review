## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper tackles the interesting problem of automatically discovering efficient mathematical identities. The authors propose a novel framework using attribute grammars, combined with learning-based search strategies—specifically n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN)—to navigate the exponentially growing space of possible expressions. The core idea of learning from simpler expressions to guide the search for more complex ones is compelling and represents a unique approach to symbolic computation. The work builds upon ideas from areas like compiler construction (attribute grammars) and natural language processing (RNNs, n-grams) applying them to a distinctly mathematical domain.

The paper’s strength lies in its innovative combination of techniques. The framework allows for the exploration of computational complexity reduction, a goal rarely addressed directly by machine learning. The experimental results, while limited in scope, demonstrate the potential of the approach to find identities beyond manual derivation or brute-force search.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The problem definition feels overly restrictive—limiting the scope to homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix variable reduces the broad applicability of the approach. The explanation of the RNN architecture, specifically the “weight tensor W3 (of size lxlxl...)”, is unclear. It's not immediately apparent whether a neural tensor network is being used, or simply a linear layer with a reshaped weight matrix. This needs clarification. Furthermore, the authors should address the rationale for choosing an RNN over other potentially more suitable architectures like tensor networks, especially given Bowman’s (2013) work highlighting their effectiveness in similar reasoning tasks.

Section 7.2, discussing the search strategies, is unclear. It’s not evident whether the RNN is still utilized for classification during the search, or solely for representation learning. The proposal generation aspect of the search procedure is underdeveloped; more detail on how the learned models influence rule selection is needed. The paper also contains numerous typos and grammatical errors throughout, which detract from clarity. Notably, Figure 3 incorrectly labels the RNN as a TNN. There are also instances of repeated sentences. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and interesting application of machine learning to symbolic mathematical discovery.
* Demonstrates the potential for finding identities beyond human reach.
* Well-motivated problem with a clear potential impact.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Overly restrictive problem definition limits generalizability.
* Lack of clarity in key architectural details (RNN structure).
* Failure to address relevant prior work (Bowman, 2013) justifying model choice.
* Insufficient explanation of search strategy and proposal generation.
* Numerous minor errors detract from readability.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend **rejection** in its current form. While the ideas presented are promising, the lack of clarity, restrictive scope, and missing justification for architectural choices prevent it from meeting the standards for a NIPS publication. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper.
