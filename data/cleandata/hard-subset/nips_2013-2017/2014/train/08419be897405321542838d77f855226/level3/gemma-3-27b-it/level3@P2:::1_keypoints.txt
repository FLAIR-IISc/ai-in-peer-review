- Paper applies machine learning to discover efficient mathematical identities using an attribute grammar representation.
- Uses n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) to guide search, trained on simpler expressions before harder ones.
- Domain restricted to homogeneous polynomials, mitigating undecidability and halting issues.
- Methodology borrows attribute grammars and RNN ideas from established compiler and NLP work.
- Strength: novel bridge between symbolic and statistical learning with a structured grammar-based framework.
- Strength: demonstrates discovery of nontrivial identities beyond manual derivation.
- Strength: authors release code and evaluation data, aiding reproducibility.
- Experimental results are limited in scope and not overwhelmingly impressive.
- Demonstrated complexity gains (e.g., O(n^3) to O(n^2)) are valuable but few and narrow.
- Scalability to more complex or general expressions is unclear and a major concern.
- Comparison to symbolic engines (Maple, Mathematica) is brief and needs deeper justification.
- RNN underperforms relative to simpler n-gram models, questioning the benefit of continuous representations.
- Ablation (dropout) is provided but insufficient to fully address RNN performance issues.
- The 10-minute search-time cutoff appears arbitrary and may bias comparisons.
- Suggested applications to ML (e.g., dropout, RBMs) are mentioned but not fully developed.
- Key open question: could a simpler hand-engineered approach suffice given the restricted domain?
- Quality: technically sound and well-defined, but limited impact as presented.
- Clarity: generally well-written and organized; intro could be tighter and more grammar-tree visuals would help.
- Originality: novel combination of ML and attribute grammars for identity discovery; related work coverage could better address symbolic tools.
- Significance: moderate potential contingent on scaling and practical application evidence.
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, clear framework, discovered identities, public code/data.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited experiments, modest gains, RNN weakness, unclear scalability, possible simpler alternatives.
- Overall assessment: interesting and promising but slightly below acceptance threshold; recommended to expand experiments and improve learning approaches (score ~6/10).
