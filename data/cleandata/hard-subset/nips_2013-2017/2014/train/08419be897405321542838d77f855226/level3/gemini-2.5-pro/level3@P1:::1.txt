### Summary

This paper proposes a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of mathematical expressions. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar and to search through the space of possible expression trees for one that is analytically equivalent to a target expression but has lower computational complexity. Recognizing that a brute-force or random search of this combinatorial space is intractable for non-trivial expressions, the authors introduce machine learning techniques to guide the search. Specifically, they learn a search strategy from solutions found for simpler expressions. Two main learning approaches are explored: an n-gram model over sub-trees of expressions and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation for symbolic expressions. The authors demonstrate their framework on several families of polynomial expressions, including some related to common machine learning computations, and show that the learning-based search can find efficient identities beyond the reach of a random baseline.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

The primary strength of this paper is its originality. Applying modern machine learning techniques, particularly deep learning models like RNNs, to the symbolic domain of mathematical identity discovery is a creative and ambitious direction. It bridges the gap between statistical pattern recognition and symbolic reasoning, a long-standing challenge in AI. The idea of learning a continuous embedding for mathematical expressions is particularly intriguing and could have applications beyond the immediate scope of this paper. The general methodology of using solutions to simpler problems as training data to tackle more complex ones (a form of curriculum learning) is well-motivated and sensible for this problem domain.

However, the work in its current form feels preliminary and has several weaknesses that temper its impact.

1.  **Limited Scope and Generality:** The experiments are restricted to a very narrow class of expressions: homogeneous polynomials involving a single matrix or vector, producing a scalar output. While the authors state the framework could be extended, the paper provides little evidence that it can scale to more general mathematical forms involving different operators (e.g., trigonometric, exponential), multiple variables in complex arrangements, or non-polynomial structures. The current results feel more like a proof-of-concept on a carefully selected set of problems rather than a demonstration of a broadly applicable tool.

2.  **Modest Performance:** While the learning-based strategies outperform the random baseline, their performance is not overwhelmingly strong. As shown in Figure 3, the success rate drops off significantly as the complexity (`k`) increases for most problem families. For the most challenging family (RBM-2), none of the learning methods perform better than random search for `k > 5`. This suggests that while the learning helps, it does not fundamentally overcome the combinatorial explosion of the search space, and the generalization from simpler to more complex expressions is limited.

3.  **Potential for Simpler Solutions:** For the specific expression families where the method is successful (e.g., `(AAT)^k`), the discovered efficient identities often possess a highly regular, repetitive structure (as shown in Example 2). This raises the question of whether the sophisticated machinery of an RNN or even n-grams is necessary. It seems plausible that simpler, hand-derived search heuristics or pattern-matching rules could exploit this regularity and find these specific solutions with less overhead. The paper presents a "sledgehammer to crack a nut" scenario, where the complexity of the solution method may not be justified by the complexity of the discovered identities in the tested domains.

4.  **Clarity of Contribution:** The paper claims the "discovery of many new mathematical identities." While technically true within the confines of the search, the significance of these identities is not well-established. Are they genuinely novel mathematical insights, or are they clever re-factorings that are artifacts of the specific grammar and operators provided? More discussion on the nature and potential utility of the discovered expressions would strengthen the paper's claims of significance.

### Arguments For/Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Originality:** The core idea of using learned search strategies, especially with RNNs, for symbolic mathematics is highly novel and thought-provoking. It opens up a new potential research direction.
*   **Interesting Problem:** The goal of automating the discovery of efficient algorithms/formulas is a fundamental and important problem in computer science and mathematics.
*   **Learned Representations:** The successful training of an RNN to classify and represent symbolic expressions is a valuable contribution in its own right.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Preliminary Results:** The work feels like an early exploration. The experimental validation is on a narrow set of problems, and the performance is not strong enough to suggest the method is robust or ready for wider application.
*   **Questionable Significance/Impact:** Given the limited domain and the regular structure of the discovered solutions, it's unclear if this approach advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The paper does not convince that the discovered identities are of broad interest or that the method is superior to more traditional symbolic manipulation or compiler optimization techniques for these types of problems.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The paper does not adequately address how the approach would scale to more general and complex mathematical domains, which is where a tool like this would be most valuable.

### Conclusion

This paper presents an interesting and original application of machine learning to the problem of discovering efficient mathematical identities. The approach is creative, and the idea of learning continuous representations for symbolic expressions is compelling. However, the experimental results are confined to a narrow domain and are not strong enough to demonstrate a significant breakthrough. The work feels more like an initial exploration of a promising idea than a complete piece of research with demonstrated impact. While the direction is laudable, the paper in its current form falls slightly below the high bar for acceptance at NIPS. It might be better suited for a workshop focused on new or unconventional applications of machine learning.
