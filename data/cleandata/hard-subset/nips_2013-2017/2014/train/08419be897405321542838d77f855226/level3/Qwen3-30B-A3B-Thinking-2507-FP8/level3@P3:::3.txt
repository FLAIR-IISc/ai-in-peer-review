

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars combined with machine learning techniques. The authors present a grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions and propose two learning approaches (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to guide the search for expressions with lower computational complexity that are analytically equivalent to a target expression. The work builds on attribute grammars originally developed for compiler construction and applies them to mathematical identity discovery, with a particular focus on polynomial expressions.

The paper's main contributions include a grammar framework for symbolic expressions, integration of machine learning to guide search strategies, a novel application of recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of mathematical structures, and the discovery of new mathematical identities with reduced computational complexity. The work connects to previous NIPS research through its use of recursive neural networks (e.g., Socher et al. [22, 23]) and draws parallels to natural language processing techniques like n-gram models. It also relates to Bowman's work on learning logical predicates using recursive networks [3], though the paper would benefit from more explicit comparison to this line of research.

**Strengths:**
- The core idea of using learning to guide symbolic search is novel and well-motivated.
- The paper demonstrates the discovery of new mathematical identities with significant computational complexity improvements (e.g., reducing O(n³) to O(n²) for certain expressions).
- The experimental results show that learning approaches outperform random search for complex expressions.
- The technical approach of using numerical descriptors to represent symbolic expressions is clever and practical.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Problem Restriction:** The problem definition is overly restrictive. The authors limit their domain to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree k with a single matrix/vector variable, which significantly limits the applicability of the approach. As noted in Section 2, while the authors claim this permits "a rich family of expressions," the restriction to homogeneous polynomials and single-variable expressions severely limits the scope compared to real mathematical problems.

2. **Model Justification:** The paper fails to justify why a standard RNN was chosen over a neural tensor network (NTN), which Bowman [3] has shown often outperforms RNNs in logical reasoning tasks. The paper claims to use a "weight tensor W3 (of size l×l×l)" but doesn't clarify whether this constitutes a neural tensor network or if they're using a linear network with a tensor as a weight. This is a critical omission that weakens the technical contribution.

3. **Clarity Issues:** Several sections lack clarity. Section 7.2 is confusing about whether the RNN is still used for classification during the search procedure. The proposal generation in the search procedure (Section 6.3) is not clearly explained, making it difficult to understand how the RNN generates new expression proposals. Figure 3 incorrectly labels the TNN as RNN, and the manuscript contains multiple typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "the framework is able to discover an efficient version of the formula" appears twice in the examples).

4. **Evaluation Ambiguity:** The evaluation of different search strategies in Section 7.2 is unclear. It's not specified how the "fraction of successful runs" (Figure 3) was calculated or whether the RNN's classification ability was maintained during the search process.

**Technical Concerns:**
The paper's claim that "distributed, recursive neural representations can classify algebraic identities" is partially supported by the classification results in Table 2 (95%+ accuracy for degree 3-6), but the paper doesn't clearly distinguish between the representation learning (Section 4.2) and the search strategy (Section 6.3). The RNN's role in the search procedure is not well-explained, and the paper doesn't demonstrate how the learned representation directly enables the search process beyond the classification task.

**Recommendation:**
The paper presents an interesting application of machine learning to symbolic computation, but the overly restricted problem domain, lack of justification for model choice, and several clarity issues significantly weaken its contribution. The work would be substantially improved by:
1. Broadening the problem definition to include more general mathematical expressions
2. Explicitly comparing neural tensor networks to RNNs (citing Bowman [3]) and justifying their model choice
3. Clarifying the technical details of the RNN's role in the search procedure
4. Correcting the numerous typos and grammatical errors

While the discovered identities are novel and interesting, their limited scope means they won't directly impact the broader machine learning community. The paper's primary contribution is the framework for guiding symbolic search with learning, but this is not sufficiently novel compared to existing work in program induction and theorem proving.

**Summary of Pro/Con Acceptance:**
*Pro:* Interesting application of learning to symbolic search, novel discovery of mathematical identities, strong empirical results for the restricted problem.
*Con:* Overly restricted problem domain, inadequate model justification, unclear technical explanations, numerous grammatical errors, limited significance for the broader ML community.

Given these concerns, I recommend rejection. The paper's core idea has potential, but the current version fails to sufficiently justify its approach or demonstrate its broader significance. The authors should address the methodological and clarity issues before resubmission.
