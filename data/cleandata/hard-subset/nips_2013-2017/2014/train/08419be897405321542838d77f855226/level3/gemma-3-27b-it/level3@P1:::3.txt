This paper explores the application of machine learning to discover efficient mathematical identities, specifically focusing on finding computationally cheaper expressions equivalent to a target expression. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and, recognizing the exponential growth of the search space, employ n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) to guide the search process. The core idea is to learn from simpler expressions to accelerate the discovery of more complex identities. This work builds on the established field of symbolic computation, attempting to inject learning-based approaches where traditional methods rely on rule-based systems.  Recent NIPS work has touched on related areas, such as learning for theorem proving (Bridge et al., 2013) and probabilistic programming (Goodman et al., 2012), but this paper focuses on a more constrained, polynomial-based domain. The introduction of a learned search strategy within an attribute grammar framework and the claim of discovering previously unknown mathematical identities represent a novel contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars with machine learning, specifically RNNs, for discovering mathematical identities is a novel approach. The reported discovery of previously unknown identities, particularly those offering significant computational improvements (O(n2) vs. O(n3)), is a significant result.
*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly defines the problem of finding computationally efficient mathematical expressions and establishes a formal setting for exploration.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments, though limited in scope, demonstrate the effectiveness of the learning approaches, particularly n-gram models, in accelerating the search for identities, especially as the expression complexity increases. The successful application of the learned representation for expression classification (Table 2) supports the feasibility of the approach.
*   **Open Source Code:** The publicly available code and data (https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning) promote reproducibility and further research.
*   **Potential Impact:** The potential applications of discovered identities in optimizing computations within machine learning algorithms (e.g., dropout, RBMs) offer practical benefits.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Restricted Problem Domain:** The limitations imposed by the problem definition—homogeneous polynomials of degree *k* with a single matrix variable—significantly narrow the applicability of the framework. While acknowledged by the authors, this restriction limits the broader impact of the work.
*   **Clarity of Representation:** The description of the weight tensor *W3* (size *lxlxl*) within the RNN section (Section 4.2) is unclear. It’s crucial to explicitly state whether a standard RNN or a neural tensor network (NTN) is being used.
*   **Model Justification:** The paper should address why an RNN was chosen over alternative models, such as tensor networks. Bowman (2013) shows that tensor networks often outperform RNNs in tasks involving structural relationships, which seem relevant here. A clear justification for this architectural choice is missing.
*   **Experimental Detail:** The explanation of the search procedure and how the different strategies are evaluated is lacking detail (Section 7.2). It's unclear how the RNN is integrated into the search and how the comparison between strategies is performed. Is the RNN used for classification during the search, or is it only utilized for pre-training?
*   **Presentation & Polish:** The manuscript contains several typos and grammatical errors, which detract from its readability. Examples include: [Provide reviewer's specific examples here, e.g. “repeated sentences in the introduction”]. Addressing these issues will significantly improve clarity. Figure 3 incorrectly labels the model as TNN when it is an RNN.
*   **Scalability:** The experiments do not demonstrate significant improvement using the RNN for the most challenging task (RBM-2k). The scalability of the proposed approach to even more complex expressions remains an open question.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel approach to a challenging problem—discovering mathematical identities—by combining attribute grammars with machine learning.
*   The reported discovery of new, computationally efficient identities is a significant contribution.
*   The experimental results, while limited, support the feasibility and effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly the n-gram approach.
*   The public release of code and data enhances reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The restricted problem domain limits the broader impact of the work.
*   The lack of clarity in the description of the RNN architecture and experimental details hinders understanding and reproducibility.
*   The relatively modest improvement offered by the RNN compared to simpler approaches (n-grams) raises concerns about its effectiveness.
*   The manuscript requires significant polishing to address the numerous typos and grammatical errors.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of automated mathematical discovery. While the restricted problem domain and areas of clarity limit its immediate impact, the novelty of the approach and the promising experimental results warrant consideration. The authors should address the weaknesses highlighted above – specifically clarifying the RNN architecture, providing more detailed experimental explanations, and improving the manuscript's presentation – to strengthen the paper's contribution. I lean towards **weak accept**, contingent on the authors addressing the clarity and justification issues raised in this review.
