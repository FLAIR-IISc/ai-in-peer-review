- Novel idea: applying ML to discover efficient mathematical identities by representing expressions as attribute grammars and guiding search with learned models.
- Clear problem formulation: finding computationally cheaper equivalent expressions is well-defined and valuable.
- Integration of techniques: reasonable combination of attribute grammars, n-gram models, and RNNs.
- Initial demonstration: experiments show potential by discovering some identities on polynomial examples.
- Code release: authors provide code and evaluation data, aiding reproducibility.

- Preliminary results: work feels early-stage and results are not very impressive relative to method complexity.
- Limited scope: experiments are restricted to a small set of polynomial expressions, limiting generalizability.
- Scalability concerns: unclear if approach scales to more complex expressions or other domains due to exponential search growth.
- “Sledgehammer” critique: simpler hand-crafted symbolic methods might achieve similar results with less overhead in this restricted domain.
- RNN underwhelming: RNN does not consistently outperform simpler n-gram models; gains are minimal for some families.
- Lack of comparison: missing thorough benchmarks against symbolic engines (e.g., Mathematica, Maple) and other optimization techniques.

- Quality: technically sound and clearly described, but analysis is incomplete and claims about many new identities are overstated.
- Clarity: generally well-written and organized, though some sections (e.g., RNN training details) could be more concise.
- Originality: novel combination/application, though constituent methods are established.
- Significance: potential interest is limited by current results and uncertain practical impact.

- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, potential to automate identity discovery, and integration of multiple ML techniques.
- Arguments against acceptance: preliminary scope and scalability issues, lack of clear advantage over simpler methods, and limited practical significance.
- Overall assessment: interesting and promising but below acceptance threshold; recommend rejection with suggestions to broaden domains, improve comparisons, and address scalability.
