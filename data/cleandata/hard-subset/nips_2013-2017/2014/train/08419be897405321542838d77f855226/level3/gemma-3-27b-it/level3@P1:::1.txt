This paper explores the application of machine learning techniques, specifically n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs), to the discovery of efficient mathematical identities. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework to represent symbolic expressions and aim to find expressions analytically equivalent to a target but with lower computational complexity. While the core idea of leveraging learning to guide the search within a grammar tree is interesting, the current implementation and experimental results raise concerns regarding its significance and overall impact.

The work builds upon existing research in several areas, including theorem proving [5, 9, 11], program induction [18, 28], and probabilistic programming [12, 20]. However, the authors appropriately narrow the scope to polynomials of a fixed degree, sidestepping issues of undecidability. They also acknowledge connections to symbolic computation engines like Maple [6] and Mathematica [27], differentiating their approach through the use of learning. The application of attribute grammars [14], while established in compiler construction, is novel in the context of formula discovery, as is the use of RNNs for learning representations of mathematical structures [2, 3, 22, 23]. The authors provide a thorough review of related work, demonstrating an understanding of the existing literature.

A key strength of the paper lies in its innovative problem formulation and the attempt to bridge the gap between symbolic computation and machine learning. The introduction of an attribute grammar provides a structured environment for exploring possible identities. The numerical and learned representations of symbolic expressions, particularly the use of a recursive neural network, are promising directions. The authors also make their code and data publicly available, which is commendable for reproducibility and further research [29].

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The experimental results, while demonstrating some success in finding more efficient expressions, are not overwhelmingly convincing. The performance gains often appear modest, and the improvement achieved using learning over a random search is not consistently substantial (Fig. 3).  The limited scope of the experiments – focusing on a restricted set of expression families – raises questions about the generalizability of the approach. While the authors acknowledge these limitations, the narrow focus diminishes the overall impact. 

Furthermore, the problem itself could be argued to be somewhat artificially constrained. Given the limited domain (polynomials of a fixed degree), it's plausible that more straightforward, hand-designed heuristics or even simple algebraic manipulations could achieve comparable or superior results. The authors do not provide a strong argument demonstrating why a machine learning approach is *necessary* to tackle this problem, or why it offers a significant advantage over traditional symbolic computation techniques. The claim of discovering “many new mathematical identities” needs further qualification – their significance within the broader mathematical landscape is not clearly established. 

The clarity of the presentation could also be improved. The notation and definitions, while generally sound, are occasionally dense and could benefit from more illustrative examples. The descriptions of the experimental setup and the performance metrics are relatively brief. A more detailed analysis of the discovered identities and their practical implications would further strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of machine learning to symbolic mathematical identity discovery.
*   Well-defined problem formulation within an attribute grammar framework.
*   Promising use of RNNs to learn representations of mathematical structures.
*   Publicly available code and data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental scope and modest performance gains.
*   Questionable necessity of a machine learning approach given the restricted domain.
*   Unclear significance of the discovered identities.
*   Potential for simpler, hand-designed solutions.
*   Presentation could be clearer and more detailed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and novel idea, but its current implementation and experimental results are not strong enough to warrant acceptance to NIPS. The work feels somewhat preliminary and requires further development and more convincing evidence of its effectiveness and significance. While the authors demonstrate technical competence and a good understanding of related work, the limited scope and modest gains raise doubts about its overall impact on the field. I would recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit to a future conference.
