This paper proposes a novel approach to discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and develop two learning approaches to find simpler symbolic expression trees: n-gram models over sub-expression sequences and a recursive neural network (RNN) producing continuous subtree representations to predict combination rules.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and the related work. The use of n-gram models and RNNs is well-motivated, and the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of the limitations of their approach and potential future directions.

However, the clarity of the paper is limited in some sections, particularly Section 7.2, which could benefit from more detailed explanations and examples. Additionally, the significance of the paper is rated low, as the problem addressed appears contrived and unlikely to be adopted for efficient computations. The practical usefulness of the proposed search methods for producing computationally efficient symbolic expressions is also unclear.

The originality of the paper is high, as the authors propose a novel application of machine learning techniques to the discovery of mathematical identities. The use of RNNs to learn continuous representations of symbolic expressions is particularly innovative.

Overall, I would rate the quality of the paper as high, but with some limitations in terms of clarity and significance. The paper is a good contribution to the field of machine learning and symbolic computation, but its impact may be limited to a specific niche.
