

This paper proposes a novel approach to discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques applied to an attribute grammar framework. The authors introduce a grammar-based representation for symbolic expressions and demonstrate how learning from simpler expressions can guide the search for more complex identities with lower computational complexity. The work builds on attribute grammars [14] and applies techniques from NLP (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to symbolic computation, making it a novel application of machine learning to a domain traditionally dominated by rule-based symbolic computation.

The paper's main strengths lie in its innovative application of machine learning to symbolic discovery, the clear demonstration of computational efficiency gains (e.g., reducing O(n³) to O(n²) complexity), and the effective learning of search strategies that enable discovery beyond brute-force capabilities. The framework successfully discovers previously unknown identities (as shown in the supplementary material), demonstrating significant practical value. The use of integer-based descriptors for numerical comparison and the learned continuous representation for symbolic expressions are particularly clever solutions to the problem of equivalence checking.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that need addressing. The problem definition is overly restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree k, which limits the applicability of the framework. The authors should explicitly discuss how their approach could be extended to more general mathematical expressions, including those involving trigonometric functions or recursion, which would greatly enhance the paper's significance.

The explanation of the recursive neural network architecture is unclear, particularly regarding the "weight tensor W3 (of size l×l×l)" mentioned in Section 4.2. This suggests the authors may be implementing a neural tensor network rather than a standard RNN, but they fail to clarify this distinction or justify why they chose this approach over simpler alternatives. The paper should cite Bowman's work [3] which demonstrated that tensor networks often outperform standard RNNs for logical reasoning tasks, and provide a clear justification for their architectural choice.

Section 7.2 lacks clarity about the evaluation of different search strategies. It's unclear whether the RNN is still used for classification during the identity discovery process or if the learned representations are solely used for the search strategy. The paper also fails to adequately explain the proposal generation mechanism in the search procedure, making it difficult to understand how the learning approaches actually guide the tree exploration.

Several technical issues undermine the paper's quality. Figure 3 incorrectly labels the RNN as "TNN" (likely a typo), and the manuscript contains multiple grammatical errors and duplicate sentences (e.g., the opening sentence appears twice). These issues reduce the paper's professionalism and readability.

The paper's originality is somewhat limited by the restricted problem scope, though the application of learning techniques to this specific symbolic discovery problem is novel. The work builds on attribute grammars [14] and NLP techniques [23], but the specific integration of these approaches for mathematical identity discovery appears to be new. The related work section is adequate but could better position the paper against recent work in theorem proving and program induction.

The significance of the paper is somewhat constrained by the problem restrictions. While the discovered identities could potentially be applied to machine learning tasks like dropout approximation or RBM partition function computation, the paper doesn't clearly demonstrate this practical impact. The most significant contribution appears to be the demonstration that machine learning can effectively guide search in combinatorially complex spaces, which has broader implications for AI.

To improve the paper, the authors should: (1) clearly define the scope limitations and discuss potential extensions; (2) clarify the neural network architecture and justify their choice; (3) provide a more detailed explanation of the search strategy and proposal generation; (4) correct the technical errors and typos; and (5) better connect the theoretical contributions to practical applications.

Overall, this paper presents an interesting and novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation with promising results, but the restricted problem formulation, unclear technical explanations, and several technical errors prevent it from being a strong contribution to NIPS. The core idea has merit, but the current presentation requires substantial improvement to be considered for acceptance. The learning approach for guiding search is potentially valuable, but the paper fails to fully demonstrate its advantages over simpler alternatives due to the unclear methodology.
