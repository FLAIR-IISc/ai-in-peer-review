- Introduces a grammar framework plus n-gram and recursive neural network (RNN) approaches to discover efficient mathematical identities.
- Problem is well-motivated and clearly stated but overly restricted to homogeneous polynomials of degree k* and single-variable expressions.
- Unclear how easily the method extends to more general, multi-variable or non-homogeneous cases.
- Using an RNN to learn continuous representations of symbolic expressions is a significant contribution.
- Explanation of the weight tensor W3 is unclear; unclear whether a neural tensor network or a linear network is used.
- Related work coverage is insufficient, especially regarding theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming.
- The authors should justify their model choice, e.g., in light of work showing tensor networks can outperform RNNs.
- Experimental results are impressive and support the effectiveness of the approach.
- Section 7.2 is unclear about whether the RNN is still used for classification and how different search strategies are compared.
- Proposal generation in the search procedure is not clearly explained and needs more detail.
- Manuscript contains several typos, grammatical errors, mislabeling in Figure 3 (TNN should be RNN), and duplicated sentences.
- Contributions are significant: novel grammar framework, machine learning application to symbolic identities, and discovery of new mathematical identities.
- Quality score: 7/10 — technically sound with supported claims, but writing and explanations need improvement.
- Clarity score: 6/10 — generally organized but some sections are unclear and the writing could be more accessible.
- Originality score: 8/10 — novel approach and notable use of RNN representations.
- Significance score: 8/10 — addresses an important problem with potential impact on compiler optimization, probabilistic programming, and automated theorem proving.
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty of approach, RNN-based representation, and strong experimental validation.
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive problem definition, unclear description of W3, manuscript errors, and insufficient explanation of certain methods.
