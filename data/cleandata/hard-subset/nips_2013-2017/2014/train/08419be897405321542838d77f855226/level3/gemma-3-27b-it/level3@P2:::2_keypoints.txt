- Introduces a framework that uses attribute grammars plus learning (n-gram and RNN) to search for mathematically equivalent expressions with lower computational complexity.
- Represents symbolic expressions as grammar-based trees and guides search with NLP-inspired models, focused on a restricted homogeneous polynomial setting.
- Novel and technically ambitious application of machine learning to guide grammar-defined symbolic search.
- Demonstrates feasibility by discovering identities (some claimed novel) and achieving computational speedups (e.g., O(n^2) vs O(n^3)).
- Experiments are well-designed with benchmarks, comparisons of search strategies, and publicly available code and data.
- Clever implementation detail: uses integer-based descriptors to avoid floating-point issues.
- Exposition is frequently dense and unclear; Section 7.2 (removing matrix-multiply rule) lacks clear motivation and explanation.
- Broader context and practical implications of discovered identities are not well explained; usefulness beyond lower complexity is opaque.
- Significance is questionable: problem feels contrived and the homogeneous-polynomial restriction limits real-world applicability and generalization.
- Empirically, simpler n-gram models often outperform the RNN, casting doubt on the benefit of the more complex neural approach.
- Learned representations are interesting but their practical impact on identity discovery is not convincingly demonstrated.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, successful discoveries, solid experiments, public code, and sound technical grounding.
- Arguments against acceptance: clarity problems, limited significance/generalizability, weak RNN advantage, and contrived problem setting.
- Overall assessment: Reject; major revision needed to improve clarity and demonstrate stronger real-world impact for reconsideration.
