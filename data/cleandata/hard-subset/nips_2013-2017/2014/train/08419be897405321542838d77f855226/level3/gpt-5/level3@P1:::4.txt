This paper proposes learning-guided discovery of algebraic identities that reduce computational complexity. The core idea is to express candidate expressions via an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic and elementwise operators; build trees that are provably equivalent to a target; and search the combinatorial space for lower-cost alternatives. Equivalence is checked quickly via integer-valued descriptors (evaluations mod a large prime over many random instantiations), and complex targets may be represented as linear combinations of multiple trees by solving a modular linear system. To scale the search beyond brute force, two learned strategies are introduced: (i) n-gram models over operator subtrees and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expressions and, on top, a softmax to propose the next grammar rule. Trained on solutions to simpler targets (curriculum), these strategies guide a scheduler to promising branches. Empirically, the approach discovers identities that reduce e.g., O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)) and O(n3) to O(n2), and yields polynomial-time formulas for certain sums otherwise requiring exponential evaluation, with n-grams often outperforming the RNN on the tested families. The work connects to theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic algebra, and draws on RNNs from NLP (e.g., Socher et al.) and curriculum learning (Bengio et al.).

Strengths
- Novelty: A compelling integration of attribute grammars, learned search policies, and continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions. Using modular evaluation as a fast, practical equality oracle is clever and effective.
- Empirical promise: Demonstrations include nontrivial reductions (e.g., repeated AB patterns; symmetric polynomials; RBM-related series). The approach reveals identities seemingly beyond reach of naive search within time limits.
- Potential impact: If matured, these techniques could augment CAS systems and compilers by automatically replacing high-cost expressions with equivalent, cheaper ones.
- Reproducibility: Public code and datasets are a strong plus.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope and generality: The setting is restricted to homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, and (mostly) single-variable inputs. It is unclear whether limitations arise from the equality oracle, grammar design, or scheduler. Please justify these constraints more formally and/or empirically. For example, show failures or degradation when allowing non-homogeneous terms, multiple variables, or limited non-polynomial ops, and articulate which components break (typing, oracle reliability, search explosion).
- Complexity objective mismatch: The problem statement seeks lower complexity, yet the search does not consistently enforce a complexity budget—beyond ad hoc rule removals (e.g., disabling matrix-matrix multiply). Since the grammar carries complexity attributes, consider:
  - A cost-aware scheduler (A*, beam search with cumulative operation cost, or soft penalties).
  - Learning to predict both next-rule probability and expected cost-to-go.
  - Explicit budgets and pruning by worst-case or measured cost models.
  - Reporting time-to-solution as a function of cost budget.
- Typing and dimensionality: The paper needs a precise type system for operators, including matrix/vector shapes, broadcasting rules, and scalarization (sum). Some examples (e.g., RBM-1 at k=1, n=1 in the supplement) appear ill-typed or ambiguous. Please add:
  - A table of operator signatures and output shapes.
  - Well-typedness checking in the scheduler to prevent invalid trees.
  - Edge-case handling and guarantees that linear combinations of trees preserve typing.
- Correctness guarantees of the equality oracle: The modular-evaluation equality test is probabilistic. Provide a bound on collision probability in terms of N (number of samples) and p (prime size), and discuss adversarial/correlated inputs. Consider cross-checking discovered identities with a CAS to verify exact equality.
- Evaluation clarity and baselines: The experiments convincingly show speedups over random search, but the comparison set is narrow. Please add:
  - CAS-based baselines (e.g., Mathematica/Maple simplification plus common-factor extraction) and hand-designed heuristics.
  - Search-efficiency plots (e.g., success probability vs. time, number of trees evaluated).
  - Ablations: curriculum vs. no curriculum; RNN capacity (embedding size), with/without identity initialization and dropout; effect of descriptor size N and prime p.
  - A clearer, consistent cost model (which operations are counted and with what weights) and runtime measurements on concrete sizes (n, m, p) complementing asymptotics.
- Organization and presentation: The current flow makes it hard to follow the end-to-end pipeline. Consider:
  - A single figure and pseudocode summarizing the full loop (grammar → scheduler/strategy → tree generation → linear combination solver → equality check → training signal).
  - Earlier, tighter notation for shapes and degree k; clearer separation between representation (modular descriptors vs. RNN embeddings) and strategy (n-gram vs. RNN+softmax).
  - Consolidating examples and referencing them consistently in the experiments.

Quality
- Sound ideas and careful engineering (identity initialization, ReLU, curriculum). However, the absence of formal guarantees for the equality oracle and the weak coupling between the search objective and computational cost detract from rigor. Addressing typing, cost-aware search, and oracle guarantees would strengthen technical soundness.

Clarity
- Generally readable but could be substantially improved by restructuring, adding a type system, defining the cost model early, and tightening notation. Minor typos listed below.

Originality
- The combination of attribute grammars, learned operator n-grams/RNN embeddings for symbolic expressions, and modular equality testing is novel. Related work is discussed; please also situate against program superoptimization/grammar-based synthesis approaches that optimize via equivalences.

Significance
- Promising direction with potential to influence symbolic computation and automated optimization. The empirical scope is still narrow; broader coverage and stronger guarantees would increase impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Innovative methodology bridging ML and symbolic algebra.
- Demonstrated ability to discover nontrivial, efficiency-improving identities.
- Open-source implementation.

Cons against acceptance
- Restricted problem scope with unclear extensibility.
- Incomplete treatment of complexity optimization (no hard constraints or explicit budgets).
- Ambiguities in typing and correctness guarantees.
- Presentation could be clearer; baselines are limited.

Minor comments
- Typos: line 148 “has contains”; 289 “is is”; 332 “show for”; 338 “by be”.
- Please clarify dimensional constraints throughout (A, B shapes; outputs of sum/transpose; broadcasting).
- Report prime p and N, and provide a failure-probability bound for the equality oracle.

Overall recommendation: Borderline/weak accept contingent on clarifying the type system and cost-aware search, and strengthening evaluation with baselines and oracle guarantees. The ideas are fresh and potentially impactful, and the empirical results, while limited in scope, are encouraging.
