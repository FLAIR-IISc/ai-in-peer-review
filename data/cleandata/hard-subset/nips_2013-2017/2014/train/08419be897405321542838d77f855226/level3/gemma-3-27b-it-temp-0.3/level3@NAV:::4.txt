## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper introduces an interesting approach to discovering mathematical identities by framing the problem as a search within a space of symbolic expressions defined by an attribute grammar, guided by machine learning. The core idea of learning to navigate this search space, initially from simpler expressions, is novel and potentially impactful. The authors leverage both n-gram models and recursive neural networks to improve search efficiency, demonstrating success in finding more efficient formulations of certain expressions than naive evaluation would allow. This work connects to areas like theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation, all of which have seen limited application of machine learning techniques – a gap this paper attempts to address.

The main strength of the paper lies in its novelty. The application of learning techniques to optimize symbolic expression search is a promising direction. The experimental results, while limited in scope, demonstrate the potential of the approach, particularly for expressions exhibiting repetitive structures where n-gram models excel. The learned representation using RNNs is also a valuable contribution, potentially opening doors for applying other machine learning tools to symbolic manipulation.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The scope is significantly restricted – the focus on homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix variable limits the generalizability of the findings. The authors acknowledge this, but the lack of discussion regarding extending the framework to more complex expressions is concerning. Furthermore, the claim of lower computational complexity isn’t consistently enforced; the algorithm doesn’t inherently prioritize solutions with lower complexity, relying instead on finding *a* more efficient solution. A mechanism to actively constrain the search towards lower complexity solutions is crucial. 

The justification for the restrictions imposed in Section 2 (single variable, polynomial degree) feels somewhat arbitrary. While the authors mention these simplify the problem, a more formal analysis or empirical study demonstrating the impact of these restrictions would strengthen the argument. The paper’s organization is also unclear; the flow between sections feels disjointed, making it difficult to follow the overall methodology. Finally, there are some technical concerns, including the unclear constraints on vector/matrix sizes and the invalid RBM-1 expression mentioned in the supplement. Several minor typos also detract from the polish of the manuscript.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a challenging problem.
*   Demonstrated ability to discover identities beyond brute-force search.
*   Potential impact on symbolic software libraries.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited scope and lack of discussion on extensibility.
*   Complexity mismatch – algorithm doesn’t enforce complexity constraints.
*   Unclear justification for restrictions and organizational issues.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The core idea is promising, but the limitations and lack of clarity prevent it from being a strong contribution at this time. Addressing the scope, complexity constraints, and organizational issues would significantly improve the paper’s quality.
