- Paper applies machine learning to find computationally cheaper symbolic identities using an attribute grammar representation.  
- Introduces two learning-guided search methods: an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN).  
- Learns from solutions to simpler expressions to guide search over exponentially large expression trees.  
- Focuses experimentally on polynomial simplification, avoiding undecidability issues of broader theorem proving.  
- Draws connections to NLP techniques (n-grams, RNNs) and adapts them to symbolic computation.  
- Core strength: novel combination of attribute grammars and ML for identity discovery.  
- Demonstrates that learning from simpler instances can guide discovery of more complex identities beyond brute-force.  
- Experimental results are promising for specific expression families, with n-gram models performing particularly well.  
- Provides reproducible code and datasets, and uses integer descriptors to avoid floating-point issues.  
- Clarity problems: dense framework and experiment descriptions, poor explanation of Section 7.2, and Matlab-style notation limiting readability.  
- RNN architecture and training details are under-explained.  
- Significance concerns: problem feels contrived and narrowly scoped (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix variable).  
- Unclear how methods generalize to realistic symbolic-simplification problems or attract broader adoption.  
- Technical work is sound with careful evaluation and comparisons to brute-force search, but limited practical impact.  
- Overall recommendation: weak accept — original and technically solid but needs improved clarity and broader applicability.
