

This paper proposes an interesting framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars and machine learning. The authors introduce a grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions and demonstrate how learning from simpler expressions can guide the search for complex identities that would be intractable with brute-force methods. The core contributions include a novel application of recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of symbolic expressions, and the discovery of new identities with significantly reduced computational complexity (e.g., reducing O(n³) to O(n²) operations).

The main strengths of the paper include the novel application of neural networks to symbolic computation, the demonstration of real mathematical discoveries, and the clear connection to natural language processing techniques. The experimental results show that learning-based approaches significantly outperform random search for complex identities, particularly for repetitive patterns.

However, several critical weaknesses undermine the paper's quality. The problem definition is overly restricted (homogeneous polynomials of single matrix/vector inputs), which limits the applicability of the framework. The explanation of the weight tensor W3 (size l×l×l) is unclear - it's uncertain whether a tensor network is being used or if this is simply a linear network with a tensor parameterization. The paper fails to justify why a recursive neural network was chosen over tensor networks, which Bowman (2013) demonstrated often outperform RNNs for logical tasks. Section 7.2 is confusing regarding whether the RNN is still used for classification during search, and the evaluation of search strategies lacks clarity. The proposal generation in the search procedure is not well-explained, and the paper contains multiple typos, grammatical errors, and a Figure 3 labeling error (TNN should be RNN). Additionally, the opening sentence appears twice in the manuscript.

While the core idea is promising, the technical execution and clarity issues prevent this from being a strong contribution to NIPS. The restricted problem scope and insufficient justification for model choices significantly limit the significance of the work. The paper would benefit substantially from addressing these weaknesses and expanding the problem definition to demonstrate broader applicability.
