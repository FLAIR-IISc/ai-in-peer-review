- Proposes a framework using attribute grammars and learned search (n-gram and RNN) to find computationally cheaper expressions equivalent to a target expression.  
- Demonstrates the approach on families of homogeneous polynomial expressions, showing learning-guided search outperforms random baselines on some cases.  
- Strength: highly original application of ML, especially RNNs, to symbolic mathematical identity discovery.  
- Strength: learning continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions is an intriguing contribution with potential broader uses.  
- Strength: using solutions to simpler problems as training data (curriculum learning) is sensible and well-motivated.  
- Weakness: experiments are narrowly scoped to single-matrix/vector homogeneous polynomials, with little evidence of generality to other operators or structures.  
- Weakness: performance is modest — success rates drop quickly as problem complexity grows, and some hard families show no improvement over random search.  
- Weakness: discovered solutions often have regular repetitive structure, raising doubt that complex models (RNNs) are necessary versus simpler heuristics or pattern matching.  
- Weakness: unclear significance of the "new" identities — may be artifacts of the grammar/operators rather than broadly useful mathematical results.  
- For acceptance: originality, importance of automating efficient formula discovery, and the learned representation result are compelling.  
- Against acceptance: results feel preliminary, evaluation domain is limited, and impact/significance are questionable.  
- Against acceptance: scalability to more general and complex mathematical domains is not addressed.  
- Conclusion: interesting and promising direction but currently preliminary and not strong enough for top-tier acceptance; better suited for a workshop or follow-up work.
