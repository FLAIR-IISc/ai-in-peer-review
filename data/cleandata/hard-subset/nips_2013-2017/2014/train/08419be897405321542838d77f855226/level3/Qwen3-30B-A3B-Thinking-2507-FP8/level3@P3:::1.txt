

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors introduce an attribute grammar approach to represent symbolic expressions, where the goal is to find expressions equivalent to a target but with lower computational complexity. To address the exponential growth of the search space, they propose two learning-based strategies: n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns to guide the grammar tree search. The paper demonstrates the approach on several families of mathematical expressions, claiming to discover identities that are too complex for brute-force search or human derivation. The work relates to attribute grammars in compiler design, symbolic computation systems like Maple and Mathematica, and natural language processing techniques applied to mathematical structures.

## Strengths
The paper's core idea of applying machine learning to discover mathematical identities is novel and interesting. The use of attribute grammars to formalize the search space is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear framework for representing symbolic expressions. The introduction of continuous representations for symbolic expressions using RNNs is a valuable contribution that could potentially enable other machine learning approaches to symbolic computation. The paper also provides a thoughtful discussion of related work, particularly connecting to NLP techniques for language modeling and recursive neural networks.

## Weaknesses
The paper suffers from several significant weaknesses that prevent it from meeting NIPS publication standards:

1. **Limited Scope and Domain**: The experiments are confined to a very narrow domain of homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix variable, which significantly limits the applicability of the results. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't sufficiently justify why this restricted domain is meaningful for broader mathematical discovery.

2. **Questionable Performance**: The method's performance is modest at best. The paper shows that their approach can discover identities for specific families of expressions, but these identities appear to follow highly repetitive patterns that could likely be derived by hand with minimal effort (e.g., the pattern in Example 2). The claim that these are "previously undiscovered" identities is questionable given the limited scope of the experiments.

3. **Inadequate Experimental Validation**: The experiments use a very limited set of input expressions (only 5 families, with most being highly repetitive patterns), and the paper doesn't demonstrate any meaningful scaling beyond small values of k (most results stop at k=5 or k=6). The "efficient" identities discovered often involve patterns that could be derived through simple pattern recognition rather than machine learning.

4. **Overstated Contribution**: The paper claims the method can discover "complex identities, beyond reach of brute-force search, or human derivation," but the examples provided (like Example 2) don't convincingly demonstrate this. For instance, the pattern in Example 2 could be derived by recognizing the repeating structure in simpler cases (k=2,3,4,5) without any machine learning.

5. **Lack of Generalization**: The paper doesn't provide any evidence that the approach would scale to more general mathematical expressions. The limited domain and repetitive patterns used in the experiments suggest the method is highly specialized rather than broadly applicable.

## Evaluation Against Review Criteria

**Quality**: The technical approach is sound, but the experimental validation is insufficient. The paper doesn't adequately address the weaknesses of the approach (e.g., the limited scope, the repetitive nature of the discovered identities), and the claims about the method's capabilities are overstated.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a good explanation of the grammar framework and learning approaches. However, the limited scope of the experiments is not sufficiently emphasized, leading to potentially misleading conclusions.

**Originality**: The application of machine learning to discover mathematical identities is novel, but the specific implementation appears to be a straightforward adaptation of existing NLP techniques to a narrow mathematical domain. The core contribution (using n-grams and RNNs to guide grammar search) doesn't represent a significant methodological advance beyond existing approaches.

**Significance**: The significance of the work is limited by the restricted domain of the experiments. The identities discovered don't appear to have substantial practical impact in machine learning or mathematics, and the approach doesn't convincingly demonstrate broader applicability beyond the narrow examples provided.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro**:
- Novel application of machine learning to mathematical identity discovery
- Well-constructed attribute grammar framework for symbolic expressions
- Introduction of continuous representations for symbolic structures using RNNs
- Interesting connection to NLP techniques

**Con**:
- Experiments confined to a very narrow mathematical domain
- Performance gains over simple pattern recognition are marginal
- Claims about "previously undiscovered" identities are overstated
- No evidence of scalability to more general mathematical expressions
- Method appears to be "sledgehammer to crack a nut" for the limited domain

## Recommendation
The paper presents an interesting but preliminary exploration of an application of machine learning to mathematical discovery. While the core idea is novel, the experimental validation is insufficient to demonstrate the method's value, and the results don't convincingly show that the approach offers advantages over simpler, hand-derived methods for the limited domain studied. The paper falls just below the threshold for acceptance at NIPS, as it doesn't provide sufficient evidence of significant scientific contribution to the field. The work would benefit from broader experimental validation, more convincing examples of non-trivial mathematical discovery, and a more rigorous justification for why the machine learning approach is necessary rather than a more direct mathematical derivation.
