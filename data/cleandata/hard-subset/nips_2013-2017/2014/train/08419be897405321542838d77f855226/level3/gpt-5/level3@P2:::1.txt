Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes using learning to discover computationally efficient identities equivalent to a target symbolic expression. The authors define an attribute grammar over linear algebra and elementwise operators, and search the resulting tree space for expressions that are provably equivalent to a target but cheaper to evaluate. Because exhaustive search is exponential in degree k, they learn search policies from solutions of simpler targets. Two strategies are studied: (i) n-gram statistics over subtrees; (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees and a softmax over next-rule choices. Equivalence is checked numerically using integer evaluations modulo a large prime, and (for more complex targets) by solving for linear combinations of candidate trees via modular Gaussian elimination. Experiments on five families (polynomials in AAT and in (A.*A)AT, elementary symmetric polynomials, and two RBM-inspired exponential sums) suggest learning-guided search finds some identities that random search misses; n-grams typically outperform the RNN.

The work connects to theorem proving and program induction [4,5,11,18,28], but differs by restricting to homogeneous polynomials with fixed degree and by focusing on computational cost rather than logical validity. It also relates to symbolic algebra systems (Maple, Mathematica) [6,27], but replaces hand-written rewrite systems with learned guidance. The use of RNNs over expression trees is inspired by NLP (e.g., Socher et al. [22,23], Luong et al. [15]) and learns embeddings for symbolic structures.

Evaluation

Quality
- The framework is clearly defined and the search/verification pipeline is coherent. The attribute grammar and constraints (degree, scalar outputs) make the search tractable.
- The modular arithmetic descriptor is a pragmatic proxy for equivalence; however, the paper does not quantify collision probabilities or detail safeguards (e.g., multiple primes, Schwartz–Zippel-style bounds) to guarantee correctness beyond numerical evidence.
- The experimental protocol reports discovery rates under a time cap and shows learning improves search efficiency in several cases, but the empirical performance appears modest: n-grams outperform the RNN in most families, and several challenging families (notably RBM-2 for k>5) remain out of reach.
- Baselines are limited. There is no comparison to cost-aware rule-based simplification with CAS, to established program synthesis/search (e.g., MCTS, ILP), or to human-crafted heuristics within the same grammar.
- The claim of “many new identities” would benefit from formal statements and proofs or rigorous cross-validation with multiple primes and algebraic verification.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and organized, with helpful examples (e.g., Example 1 and the k-step pattern in Example 2), and implementation details for the RNN (initialization, curriculum, dropout).
- Some details that would aid reproducibility and assessment are missing or brief: the exact grammar (Table 1 not shown here), descriptor size choices versus collision risk, how many primes used, and how linear-combination solutions map back to interpretable identities.

Originality
- Applying learned tree embeddings and n-gram statistics to guide identity discovery within an attribute grammar is a novel combination for the time of submission. Learning continuous representations of symbolic expressions is interesting and potentially impactful.
- The problem framing (cost-reducing equivalences) is less common in theorem proving/ CAS literature, which typically targets canonical forms, not computational complexity.

Significance
- While conceptually appealing, the demonstrated scope is fairly narrow: homogeneous polynomials in a single variable with scalar outputs, with matrix-matrix multiply sometimes removed from the grammar to bias toward O(n^2) solutions. It is unclear whether the approach scales to richer operators, multiple variables, or non-polynomial expressions.
- The practical impact is not yet established. The paper hints at applications (e.g., dropout, RBM partition functions), but does not deliver end-to-end improvements in an ML system or a compiler backend.
- Given that simple n-grams outperform the RNN in most cases, the benefit of deep models for guiding search is not convincingly demonstrated.

Strengths
- Creative and timely idea: learning to guide search for symbolic identities.
- Clear framework integrating grammar-based generation, learned guidance, and efficient (probabilistic) equivalence testing.
- Public code/data and concrete examples; curriculum learning and training details are thoughtfully presented.
- Some identities beyond brute-force/random search are found.

Weaknesses
- Narrow problem domain and grammar; scaling to more general expressions is unproven.
- Empirical gains are modest; the RNN underperforms simple n-grams in most families.
- Limited baselines; no comparison to CAS with cost-aware rewrites or other search methods.
- Equivalence verification relies on modular evaluations without quantified guarantees; “new identity” claims lack formal proof.
- The approach can feel like a sledgehammer relative to the restricted expressions tested; hand-crafted rules might suffice.

Questions for the authors
- How do you bound the probability of incorrect equivalence due to descriptor collisions? Do you use multiple primes or randomized trials?
- Can you compare against CAS (Maple/Mathematica) augmented with a simple cost metric and heuristic rewrites?
- How sensitive are results to grammar design (e.g., including/excluding matrix-matrix multiply) and to descriptor size N?
- Can the learned policies generalize across operators/variables not seen in training?

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen verification: use multiple independent primes and/or symbolic checking to certify reported identities; provide formal statements of key identities.
- Broaden baselines: include CAS with cost-driven simplification, MCTS/UCT, A* with learned cost, or RL-guided search; report search efficiency (nodes explored) and wall-clock time.
- Expand scope: multiple variables, additional operators (transpose, trace, Hadamard, Kronecker), and at least one application-driven benchmark (e.g., replacing a bottleneck in a real ML workload).
- Analyze failure modes and success patterns; provide ablations (with/without curriculum, dropout, identity init; n-gram depth).
- Improve the RNN: consider tree-LSTMs or graph neural networks that encode algebraic symmetries (associativity/commutativity), or hybrid models combining n-grams with neural scoring.

Recommendation
- Slightly below threshold. The paper is interesting and novel in spirit but feels preliminary: the domain is restricted, the empirical wins are limited, and stronger baselines and verification are needed to support publication at NIPS.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
