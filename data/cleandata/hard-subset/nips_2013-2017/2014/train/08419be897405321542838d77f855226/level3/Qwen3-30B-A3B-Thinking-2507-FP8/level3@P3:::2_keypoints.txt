- Introduces a framework using attribute grammars to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities via learning.  
- Addresses combinatorial explosion in brute-force search for equivalent symbolic expressions.  
- Proposes two learning methods: an n-gram model over sub-expression sequences and a recursive neural network (RNN) for continuous expression representations.  
- Demonstrates discovery of identities that reduce polynomial computation from O(n³) to O(n²) or lower.  
- Connects ideas from attribute grammars, symbolic computation, and NLP (n-grams, RNNs).  
- Novelty: first application of attribute grammars to identity discovery and novel use of RNNs for symbolic expressions.  
- Strong technical quality with clear problem formalization and comprehensive experiments across five expression families.  
- Learning approaches outperform random search on complex expressions; n-gram wins on two of three tasks.  
- RNN achieves ≤5% error on expression classification across varying degrees.  
- Paper suffers from clarity issues, especially Section 7.2 and dense mathematical notation needing more intuitive explanations.  
- Limited significance: problem is contrived and restricted (single matrix/vector variable, scalar output), limiting real-world applicability.  
- Practical usefulness unclear: no integration with existing computational frameworks or evidence of real-world impact.  
- Methods fail on the hardest family (RBM-2)^k beyond k=5; random search sometimes matches learning approaches.  
- Recommendation: reject — technically meritorious but lacks broad significance; authors should clarify exposition and demonstrate wider practical applications.
