

This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning to guide symbolic search within an attribute grammar. The core idea leverages attribute grammars [14] to represent symbolic expressions as trees and applies n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to learn search strategies from simpler identities. The authors demonstrate success in reducing computational complexity for specific polynomial families (e.g., from O(n³) to O(n²)), with examples like the identity for ∑∑(A*B)ᵏ. The work bridges symbolic computation with machine learning, drawing inspiration from NLP techniques (e.g., n-grams [8,16] and RNNs [22,23]) to tackle a problem previously addressed only via rule-based systems [6,27] or theorem proving [5,9].

**Strengths:**  
The paper’s most compelling contribution is its effective application of learning to guide combinatorial search in symbolic domains. The RNN-based continuous representation for symbolic expressions (Section 4.2) achieves high classification accuracy (≤5% error for k=3–6, Table 2), demonstrating that symbolic structures can be embedded for machine learning. The experimental results (Section 7) convincingly show that learning-based strategies (n-grams, RNNs) outperform random search for complex identities (e.g., k=6 in Example 2), enabling discoveries beyond brute-force reach. The framework’s potential to integrate with symbolic libraries (e.g., for efficient RBM partition function computation) is a notable practical significance. The technical execution is sound, with careful handling of numerical equivalence via integer descriptors (Section 4.1) and robust RNN training details (e.g., curriculum learning, identity initialization).

**Weaknesses:**  
The work’s scope is severely restricted, undermining its broader significance. Section 2 imposes artificial constraints: homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree k, single matrix inputs, and scalar outputs. This excludes common cases (e.g., mixed-degree polynomials or vector outputs) and lacks justification for why these restrictions are necessary beyond tractability. Crucially, the paper fails to enforce computational complexity constraints during search (Problem Statement vs. Section 6). The framework searches for *any* equivalent expression, not specifically low-complexity ones—e.g., a solution with O(n³) complexity would be accepted as "efficient" if it matched the target, contradicting the stated goal. The authors should explicitly integrate complexity as a search objective (e.g., via penalty terms or grammar constraints) to align with their problem definition.  

Section 2’s restrictions are further unconvincing: the paper claims the scheduler is the only limiting factor, but provides no formal analysis or empirical evidence (e.g., "What happens if we relax the single-matrix constraint?"). The RBM-1 family example (Supplement) contains an invalid case for k=1, n=1 (summing over v∈{0,1}ⁿ with n=1 yields a scalar, not a vector), revealing insufficient validation. The paper’s organization is also problematic: the grammar definition (Section 3), representation (Section 4), and search strategy (Section 6) are disjointed, making the workflow hard to follow without revisiting multiple sections. Minor typos (e.g., "has contains" in Section 2.1) and ambiguous phrasing (e.g., "O(n³) target expressions" in Example 1) further reduce clarity.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel integration of learning with symbolic search; strong empirical results on restricted but well-defined identities; effective RNN representation for symbolic expressions.  
*Con:* Scope too narrow to generalize; critical flaw in not enforcing computational complexity; inadequate justification for restrictions; organizational and technical inconsistencies.  

**Recommendation:**  
This paper presents a technically interesting approach with a clear innovation in applying machine learning to symbolic discovery. However, the failure to enforce computational complexity constraints—a core requirement of the problem—undermines the central claim. The restricted scope, while necessary for tractability, is not justified as a fundamental limitation of the method, and the lack of broader applicability diminishes its significance for NIPS. The authors should address these gaps by (1) modifying the search to prioritize low-complexity solutions, (2) rigorously justifying scope restrictions, and (3) clarifying the framework’s limitations. Without these, the work remains a promising niche contribution rather than a significant advancement for the field. **Weak Reject**.
