This paper proposes learning-guided search to discover mathematically equivalent but computationally cheaper identities. The core idea is to encode expressions with an attribute grammar, represent expressions either numerically via descriptors over Zp or through a learned recursive neural network (RNN) embedding, and guide tree search with learned strategies (n-gram or RNN). The numerical descriptor enables fast equivalence testing, and linear combinations of trees allow more complex identities. This bridges statistical ML with symbolic reasoning, relating to work in theorem proving, program induction, and NLP-style learned representations (e.g., Socher et al., Bowman), while differing from CAS systems (Maple/Mathematica) by explicitly targeting low-complexity equivalents and learning heuristics rather than hand-coded rules.

Quality: The framework is well-motivated and the empirical section demonstrates nontrivial identities unreachable by random search. However, there is a complexity-objective mismatch: the problem statement emphasizes lower asymptotic complexity, yet the algorithm only sometimes enforces this (e.g., by removing matrix-multiply) rather than optimizing or constraining complexity systematically. The reliance on Zp descriptors is practical, but a formal bound on collision probability and the required N relative to the linear system would strengthen correctness claims. Constraints on dimensions are under-specified; the RBM-1 construction in the supplement appears invalid for k=1, n=1. The evidence for extensibility beyond the narrow family (homogeneous polynomials in one variable with scalar outputs) is limited.

Clarity: The overall pipeline (grammar → representation → scheduler/strategy → linear solver) is difficult to follow; Section 6 should clearly separate the fixed scheduler from learned strategies, and Section 2’s restrictions need formal justification or ablations (e.g., are they fundamental or artifacts of the scheduler?). Please clarify admissible vector/matrix sizes and rule preconditions.

Originality: Introducing learned continuous representations for symbolic expressions and using them to guide identity discovery is novel and timely. The n-gram baseline outperforming RNN on several families is interesting.

Significance: If made robust and generalized, the approach could impact symbolic libraries, compilers, and ML systems (e.g., dropout/RBM approximations). Current scope limits broader impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel application of learned representations to symbolic identity discovery
- Practical wins on challenging identities and open-source release
- Clear connections to prior ML work and potential for integration with tools

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited scope with no strong evidence of extensibility
- Complexity objective not rigorously enforced or optimized
- Missing formal guarantees and dimensional constraints; unclear RBM-1 edge cases
- Organization/clarity issues
