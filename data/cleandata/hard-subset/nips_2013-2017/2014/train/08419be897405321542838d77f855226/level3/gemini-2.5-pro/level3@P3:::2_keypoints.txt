- Proposes a framework to discover computationally efficient symbolic expressions by representing expression space with an attribute grammar and searching for analytically equivalent, lower-complexity forms.
- Uses machine learning to guide search over the combinatorially large grammar space, training on simpler solutions to find harder ones.
- Presents two learning-based search strategies: an n-gram model that learns common operation sequences and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous representations of expression trees.
- Verifies expression equivalence via numerical descriptors computed on random inputs modulo a large prime.
- Evaluates methods on five families of complex polynomial expressions, showing learning-guided search finds efficient identities that random search cannot.
- High technical quality: well-conceived integration of grammar-based search with ML and thorough experiments comparing methods.
- Strong originality: framing identity discovery as a learning problem and applying NLP techniques (n-grams, tree-RNNs) to symbolic math is novel.
- Clarity is a notable weakness: key sections (RNN training, experimental task definitions) are dense and under-explained.
- Specific clarity issues: unclear RNN architecture/training objective, cryptic definitions for expression families (e.g., RBM-2)k), and insufficient justification for removing matrix-multiplication rules.
- Reproducibility concern: more detail needed on the search scheduler and interaction with learning strategies.
- Mixed significance: interesting proof-of-concept and potential for inspiring future work, but practical impact and general applicability are unclear.
- Concerns about scope: problem families seem contrived, applications (e.g., RBMs, dropout) are speculative, and the approach relies on a manual grammar and time-limited search.
- Positive empirical outcome: learning-based methods outperform random search and discover non-trivial identities.
- Ambiguous value of RNN: the simpler n-gram model often outperforms the RNN, which undermines the contribution of learned continuous representations.
