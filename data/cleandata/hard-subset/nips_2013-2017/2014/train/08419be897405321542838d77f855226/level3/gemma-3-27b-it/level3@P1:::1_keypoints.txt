- Paper applies ML (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities.  
- Introduces an attribute grammar framework to represent symbolic expressions and guide search for lower-complexity equivalents.  
- Scope is narrowly focused on polynomials of fixed degree, avoiding undecidability but limiting generality.  
- Builds on prior work in theorem proving, program induction, probabilistic programming, and symbolic computation, with appropriate citations.  
- Novelty lies in using attribute grammars for formula discovery and RNNs to learn representations of mathematical structures.  
- Authors release code and data, supporting reproducibility.  
- Strength: innovative problem formulation bridging symbolic computation and machine learning.  
- Strength: promising numerical and learned representations, notably the recursive neural network approach.  
- Experimental results show some success but performance gains are often modest and not consistently better than random search.  
- Limited experimental families raise concerns about the method’s generalizability and practical impact.  
- The problem setup may be artificially constrained; simpler heuristics or algebraic methods might match or outperform the proposed approach.  
- The paper does not convincingly argue why a machine learning solution is necessary or superior to traditional symbolic techniques.  
- The claim of discovering “many new mathematical identities” lacks context on their broader mathematical significance.  
- Presentation is occasionally dense, with notation and experimental descriptions needing clearer examples and more detailed analysis.  
- Overall assessment: interesting and novel but preliminary; current implementation and results are insufficient for acceptance, with recommendation to revise and strengthen experiments and presentation.
