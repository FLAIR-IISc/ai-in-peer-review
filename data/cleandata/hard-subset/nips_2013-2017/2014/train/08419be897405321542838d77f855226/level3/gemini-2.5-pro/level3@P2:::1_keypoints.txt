- Proposes a framework to discover computationally efficient symbolic expressions by searching an attribute-grammar-defined space for analytically equivalent, lower-cost expressions.
- Uses learned search strategies (n-gram models and an RNN) trained on simpler solutions to guide search for complex targets.
- Introduces a method to learn continuous vector representations of symbolic expressions via an RNN, analogous to word embeddings.
- Novelty: applying modern ML, especially deep nets, to discover mathematical identities is creative and original.
- Learned representation: RNN-based embeddings show high classification accuracy and appear to capture structural properties of expressions.
- Clarity: paper is well-written and explains grammar, representations, and search strategies clearly.
- Preliminary scope: experiments are limited to five families of single-variable homogeneous polynomials, restricting assessment of generality.
- Unconvincing performance: learned methods improve over random search only modestly; for complex cases the RNN fails to outperform random and n-grams often do better.
- Simpler methods: n-gram models frequently outperform the more complex and costly RNN, questioning the necessity of deep learning here.
- Missing baseline: lack of comparison to hand-crafted rewrite rules or traditional symbolic algebra makes practical utility unclear.
- Potential over-engineering: the learning framework may be excessive for the narrow polynomial domain tested.
- Scalability concerns: search space still grows exponentially and the paper lacks strong evidence the heuristic scales to richer grammars or operators.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel ML application to symbolic computation, promising learned continuous representations, and fit for the ML/AI community.
- Arguments against acceptance: narrow experiments, modest gains over baselines, n-grams outperform RNNs, and no comparison to non-learning symbolic methods.
- Overall: interesting and original idea but preliminary execution and limited experimental validation leave questions about robustness, scalability, and practical impact.
