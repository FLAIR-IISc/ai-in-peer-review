- Introduces a novel combination of attribute grammars and machine learning (n-grams and RNNs) to discover efficient mathematical identities.  
- Represents symbolic expressions as grammar-derived trees and uses learned models to guide search for computationally cheaper equivalents.  
- Demonstrates promising empirical results, finding non-trivial identities beyond manual derivation or exhaustive search in some cases.  
- RNN-based continuous representations of symbolic expressions are a valuable and novel contribution.  
- Potentially high impact for symbolic software libraries and optimization tools.  
- Current implementation is narrowly scoped to homogeneous polynomials of degree k with a single matrix/vector variable.  
- Justification for the problem restrictions is weak and it’s unclear if scheduler limits or fundamental approach limitations cause the scope constraints.  
- The search does not inherently enforce lower computational complexity and can produce higher-complexity solutions.  
- Authors partially mitigate complexity issues by removing costly rules in some experiments, but a general complexity-prioritization mechanism is needed.  
- Paper organization and exposition are convoluted; the interplay of grammar, search, and linear combinations is unclear.  
- More diagrams, a clear flowchart, and detailed explanations would improve readability.  
- Technical correctness concerns arise from questionable matrix/vector size constraints in a supplementary RBM-1 example.  
- Minor typographical errors are present and should be fixed.  
- Overall technically sound foundation, but limited scope and design-justification gaps reduce the contribution’s strength.  
- Originality is strong in integrating learning with attribute grammars and applying RNNs to symbolic structures.  
- Significance is promising but currently limited by scope and lack of demonstrated scalability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, promising limited results, and useful learned representations.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited scope, unclear justification for restrictions, clarity and organization issues, and implementation concerns.  
- Recommendation: rejection for now, with encouragement to broaden the grammar, rigorously justify restrictions, improve clarity, and validate implementation to strengthen the contribution.
