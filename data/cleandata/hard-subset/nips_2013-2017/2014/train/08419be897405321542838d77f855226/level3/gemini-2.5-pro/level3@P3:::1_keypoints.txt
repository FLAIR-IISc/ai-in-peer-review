- Core contribution: proposes an attribute-grammar framework and learning-guided search to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities.  
- Methods: compares an n-gram model over grammar rules and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of symbolic expressions.  
- Training strategy: models are trained on solutions to simpler problems to guide search for more complex expressions.  
- Evaluation: tested on five families of polynomial expressions; learning methods find efficient identities where random search fails.  
- Originality/significance: novel application of deep learning to symbolic mathematics with potential impact across multiple fields.  
- Learned representation: RNN successfully learns continuous embeddings for symbolic trees, enabling ML tools in symbolic domains.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written with clear explanations and helpful examples.  
- Preliminary nature: work feels like a proof-of-concept with limited evaluation and early-stage results.  
- Modest performance: methods struggle on the hardest tasks (RBM-2 beyond k=5); n-gram often outperforms the RNN, questioning the need for deep learning.  
- Limited scope: experiments are narrow (single matrix/vector, scalar output) so generality is uncertain; some solution structures favor n-gram models.  
- Scalability concerns: unclear how approach scales to richer grammars (trigonometry, division, recursion) due to combinatorial blowup; may be overkill for these problems.  
- Recommendation: interesting and creative direction but currently borderline rejection — needs broader experiments, deeper analysis of limitations, and stronger justification versus simpler methods.
