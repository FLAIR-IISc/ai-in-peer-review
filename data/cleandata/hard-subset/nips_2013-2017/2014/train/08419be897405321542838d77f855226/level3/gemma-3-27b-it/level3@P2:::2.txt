This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars and machine learning. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions as trees built from grammar rules and then employ learning techniques – specifically n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) – to guide the search for expressions equivalent to a target but with lower computational complexity. This work builds upon existing concepts in attribute grammars [14] and attempts to bridge the gap between symbolic computation and machine learning, drawing inspiration from natural language processing techniques like n-gram modeling and recursive neural networks [23]. Related work in theorem proving [5, 9, 11], program induction [18, 28] and probabilistic programming [12, 20] are acknowledged, but the authors correctly position their work as distinct by focusing on a restricted polynomial setting.

The strengths of this paper lie in its originality and technical ambition. The idea of applying machine learning to guide the search within a grammar-defined space of symbolic expressions is novel and potentially impactful. The authors demonstrate the feasibility of this approach by successfully discovering identities, some of which they claim are previously unknown, and achieving computational speedups (e.g., O(n<sup>2</sup>) vs O(n<sup>3</sup>)). The experimental setup appears well-designed, with a clear benchmark of families of expressions and a comparison of different search strategies. The code and evaluation data being publicly available ([https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning](https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning)) is also a significant positive. The use of integer-based descriptors to avoid floating-point issues is a clever detail.

However, the paper suffers from weaknesses, primarily in clarity and significance. The exposition is often dense and difficult to follow, particularly Section 7.2 which discusses removing the matrix-multiply rule. The motivation for this decision isn't immediately clear and requires more detailed explanation. While the authors provide examples (Example 1 & 2), the broader context and implications of the discovered identities remain somewhat opaque. The paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of *why* these identities are useful beyond merely stating their lower computational complexity.

More fundamentally, the significance of this work is questionable. While the technical achievements are noteworthy, the problem itself feels somewhat contrived. The restriction to homogeneous polynomials of a fixed degree, while simplifying the search space, significantly limits the real-world applicability. It’s unclear how these techniques would generalize to more complex mathematical expressions or other domains. The authors acknowledge this in their discussion section, but the overall impression is that the benefits are currently limited. The fact that n-gram models outperformed the RNN in several experiments also raises questions about the true benefit of the more complex neural network approach. While the learned representations are interesting (Section 4.2, Table 2), their practical impact on the identity discovery process is not fully demonstrated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to symbolic computation using machine learning.
*   Successful discovery of previously unknown mathematical identities.
*   Well-designed experiments and publicly available code.
*   Technically sound and demonstrates a solid understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited clarity and potentially dense exposition.
*   Questionable significance – the problem feels contrived and lacks clear real-world applications.
*   The benefit of the RNN over the n-gram model is not compelling.
*   The restricted setting (homogeneous polynomials) limits generalizability.



**Overall assessment:** Reject.

While the paper presents a technically interesting and original contribution, its limited significance and clarity issues outweigh its strengths. The contrived nature of the problem and the lack of clear practical applications make it difficult to justify acceptance to NIPS. A major revision addressing the clarity concerns and demonstrating a more compelling case for the real-world impact of this work would be necessary for reconsideration.
