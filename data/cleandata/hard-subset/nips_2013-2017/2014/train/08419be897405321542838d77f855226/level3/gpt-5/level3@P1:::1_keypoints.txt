- Proposes a learning-guided framework to discover computationally efficient algebraic identities using attribute grammars annotated with computational complexity.  
- Expressions are enumerated as trees and equivalence is checked via integer descriptors computed by evaluating expressions on random inputs modulo a large prime.  
- Trains search policies from simpler targets using (i) n-gram statistics over subtrees and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that embeds trees and produces a softmax rule-selection policy.  
- Demonstrates nontrivial complexity reductions on several families (e.g., matrix sums, symmetric polynomials, RBM-inspired sums).  
- N-gram models often outperform the RNN policy; the hardest family (RBM-2) does not scale beyond k=5 under a 10-minute budget.  
- Framework is technically coherent with careful choices for descriptors and RNN implementation details (identity init, ReLU, curriculum, dropout).  
- Learning-to-search setup (scheduler/strategy separation) and progressive curriculum are reasonable and well described.  
- Empirical protocol is sensible but evaluation is narrow and overall performance is modest.  
- Success metric is fraction of runs finding a solution within a time budget; lacks reporting of search effort (nodes expanded) and concrete wall-clock speedups for real sizes.  
- Missing comparisons to stronger non-learning baselines (heuristic search, MCTS, A*, SMT/CAS-guided rewrites).  
- Two experiments remove matrix-matrix multiply from the grammar to force O(n^2) solutions, which may bias results by altering the search space.  
- Claims of “previously undiscovered” identities are not substantiated with CAS checks or literature citations.  
- Equivalence check via random evaluation modulo p is practical but collision risk is not quantified and no formal verification step is provided.  
- Main grammar and complexity attributes are not fully detailed in the main text, hindering reproducibility without the supplement.  
- More explicit hyperparameter reporting (descriptor length, prime p, beam width) and resource usage would improve clarity.  
- Novelty: applying learned policies (n-gram and RNN) to discover complexity-reducing identities is original relative to theorem proving and CAS work.  
- However, the RNN’s limited benefit suggests many solutions rely on local repetitive patterns that n-grams capture.  
- Potential significance for compiler optimization and symbolic computation exists, but current domain is constrained (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, single variable).  
- Strengths: principled framework, novel RNN embeddings for expressions, concrete complexity-reduction examples, and released code/data.  
- Weaknesses: limited grammar scope, modest empirical gains, hardest families not solved, and narrow evaluation metrics.  
- Suggested improvements: broaden grammar, report search metrics, add stronger baselines and ablations, quantify practical speedups, and provide formal verification/literature audit.  
- Arguments for acceptance: interesting problem, promising learned guidance, and an ML–symbolic computation bridge with reusable representation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical evidence, deep model underperforms simple baselines, missing verification/baselines, and unclear scalability.  
- Overall assessment: creative and interesting direction but preliminary and slightly below acceptance threshold due to limited scope and empirical support.
