- Paper studies automatic discovery of computationally efficient mathematical identities by searching expression trees from an attribute grammar with cost attributes.
- Proposes learned search strategies trained on simpler targets: an n-gram subtree frequency estimator and an RNN-based recursive embedding with softmax policy.
- Uses two expression equality representations: numerical descriptors from random evaluations over Zp and a learned RNN embedding/classifier over equivalence classes.
- Experiments on synthetic families (matrix sums, elementary symmetric polynomials, RBM-motivated sums); learning from smaller k improves performance at larger k.
- N-gram generally outperforms the RNN on two of three families; RNN classification error ≤5% suggests embeddings capture useful structure.
- Technical construction is broadly sound: attribute grammar constrains search and modular descriptor enables practical equality testing and linear-combination solving.
- Need quantification of collision probability for Zp-based equality tests and description of any final exact symbolic verification.
- Unclear how modular integer weights returned by the solver are converted to valid real/integer coefficients and whether resulting expressions remain realizable within the grammar.
- Complexity accounting is imprecise: mix of big-O and operator classes; requests a precise operation-count model and empirical wall-clock or flop comparisons.
- Scope limitation: experiments focus on homogeneous polynomials with scalar outputs; paper should state failure modes and how to extend the grammar safely.
- Empirical protocol needs more informative metrics: time-to-first-solution curves, beam-size sensitivity, and success rates vs. number of trees expanded instead of only a fixed cutoff.
- Clarity issues, especially Section 7.2; full grammar and cost model should be moved into the main text.
- Request a small worked example showing descriptor vectors, the solved linear system, and the recovered symbolic identity.
- For k=6 example, ask to explicitly derive the claimed O(nm) complexity and give a brief correctness argument.
- RNN details missing: list hyperparameters, embedding dimensions, training-set sizes per k, and clarify the curriculum used.
- Suggest summarizing key discovered identities in the main paper rather than leaving them in the supplement.
- Originality rated high: novel combination of attribute grammars with learned search policies and continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions.
- Significance rated mixed/low: problem feels contrived, unclear practical speedups over standard optimizers, and largest task family remains largely out of reach.
- Pros: clear formulation, two learned strategies with careful comparison, effective n-gram baseline, and useful RNN embeddings.
- Cons: practical utility unclear, reliance on synthetic tasks and grammar restrictions, insufficient correctness guarantees for modular descriptors, and reproducibility/detail gaps.
- Key questions for authors: false-equivalence probability under Zp and exact checks; how modular weights map to real coefficients; runtime/flop comparisons to baselines; why n-gram beats RNN and whether RNN was conditioned or used with beam search; sensitivity to grammar; handling multiple inputs and non-homogeneous targets.
- Recommendation: technically solid and original but borderline accept — needs clearer exposition, stronger correctness justification, and demonstrations of practical performance gains.
