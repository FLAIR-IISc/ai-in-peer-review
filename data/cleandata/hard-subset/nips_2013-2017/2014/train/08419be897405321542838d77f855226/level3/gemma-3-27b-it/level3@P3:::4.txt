This paper introduces an attribute grammar framework combined with machine learning techniques to discover efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions as trees constructed from grammar rules, and then utilize n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to guide the search for computationally cheaper, yet equivalent, expressions. While the approach demonstrates promise, particularly in identifying identities not easily found manually, it suffers from limitations in scope, clarity, and a lack of rigorous justification for certain design choices.

The paper builds upon previous work in symbolic computation (e.g., Maple, Mathematica [6, 27]), theorem proving [5, 9, 11], and program induction [18, 28]. However, it differentiates itself by explicitly integrating learning into the process of identity discovery, rather than relying on rule-based systems or hand-designed heuristics [4]. The use of attribute grammars [14] provides a structured approach to representing and manipulating symbolic expressions, drawing parallels with work in compiler construction and other areas like bioinformatics [26]. The application of RNNs to learn continuous representations of symbolic structures is novel and inspired by advances in natural language processing [2, 3, 15, 22, 23]. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for discovering mathematical identities is a genuinely novel contribution.
* **Potential Impact:** The ability to automatically find more efficient expressions could be beneficial for symbolic software libraries and optimization tools.
* **Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates the framework's capability to find identities that are non-trivial and, as claimed, beyond manual derivation or exhaustive search in some cases. The examples provided (e.g., Example 1 & 2) are illustrative.
* **Learned Representation:** The RNN-based learned representation of symbolic expressions is a potentially valuable contribution, opening avenues for applying machine learning techniques to symbolic manipulation tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope:** The current implementation is heavily restricted to homogeneous polynomials of degree *k* with a single matrix/vector variable. The authors acknowledge this but the justification for these restrictions (Section 2) feels weak. It is unclear whether the scheduler’s limitations *are* the sole reason for these restrictions, or whether the underlying approach fundamentally struggles with broader expression classes. More empirical investigation, attempting to relax these constraints incrementally, is needed to demonstrate scalability.
* **Complexity Mismatch:** The problem statement explicitly seeks lower computational complexity, but the algorithm does not inherently enforce this constraint.  The search process could potentially generate solutions with *higher* complexity. While the authors mitigate this by removing the matrix-matrix multiply rule in some experiments, a more general mechanism to prioritize solutions based on complexity would be beneficial.
* **Lack of Clarity:** The paper's organization is somewhat convoluted. The interplay between the attribute grammar, the search strategies, and the linear combination of trees is not immediately clear. A more structured presentation, potentially with a clear flowchart outlining the stages of the process, would improve readability.
* **Technical Concern:** The constraints on vector/matrix sizes in the RBM-1 expression example in the supplementary material are questionable. The claim that it is valid for k=1, n=1 is incorrect.  This raises concerns about the thoroughness of the implementation and validation.
* **Minor Errors:** The paper contains minor typos (332: "show for," 148: "has contains," 289: "is is," 338: "by be").

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear mathematical foundation. However, the limited scope and lack of rigorous justification for certain design choices diminish its overall quality. The experimental evaluation provides some support for the claims, but further investigation is needed to assess the generalizability of the approach.

**Clarity:** The paper suffers from clarity issues, primarily due to its organizational structure and the complexity of the underlying concepts. More detailed explanations and illustrative diagrams would significantly improve readability.

**Originality:** The core idea of combining attribute grammars and machine learning for identity discovery is original. The application of RNNs to learn representations of symbolic expressions also contributes to the originality of the work. However, the authors appropriately acknowledge related work and position their contribution within the existing literature.

**Significance:** The results are potentially significant, particularly for applications involving symbolic computation and optimization. The ability to automatically discover efficient mathematical identities could have a tangible impact on performance in various domains. However, the limited scope of the current implementation restricts its immediate practical applicability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and interesting approach to a challenging problem.
* Demonstrates promising results on a limited set of expressions.
* Potential for significant impact in symbolic computation and optimization.
* RNN learned representations are a potentially useful contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Limited scope and lack of scalability.
* Unclear justification for restrictions on the problem domain.
* Weaknesses in clarity and organization.
* Technical concerns regarding implementation details.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising but incomplete exploration of using machine learning to discover efficient mathematical identities. While the core idea is novel and the results are encouraging, the limited scope and clarity issues prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the identified weaknesses – specifically, broaden the scope of the grammar, provide a more rigorous justification for the current restrictions, improve the clarity of the presentation, and thoroughly validate the implementation. Addressing these concerns would significantly enhance the paper's contribution and potential impact.
