- Introduces a novel approach to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities by representing symbolic expressions with an attribute grammar and searching for equivalent, lower-complexity expressions.  
- Search space grows exponentially, so authors learn search strategies from simpler problems to guide exploration.  
- Two learning methods explored: an n-gram model over grammar rules and a recursive neural network (RNN) that embeds symbolic expressions.  
- Connects symbolic computation, program induction, and NLP; using statistical learning to guide identity discovery is largely unexplored.  
- RNN-based continuous representations for expressions are a notable, novel contribution analogous to NLP embeddings.  
- Experimental results demonstrate discovery of non-trivial identities for complex polynomial families beyond brute-force reach.  
- Primary strength: originality and potential impact for integrating learning-guided identity discovery into symbolic tools or compilers.  
- Limitation: problem scope is very restricted to single-variable homogeneous polynomials with scalar outputs, limiting generality.  
- Authors fail to convincingly argue or show a path to extend the method to broader expression classes (e.g., trig functions, inequalities, multiple variables).  
- Mismatch between stated objective (find low-complexity expressions) and training objective (find any equivalent expression); complexity is not directly optimized.  
- Current workaround of manually removing high-complexity rules is ad hoc; complexity should be incorporated into the search objective (penalties or budgets).  
- Presentation/clarity issues: pipeline components and data flow are hard to follow and would benefit from a diagram and clearer organization.  
- Restrictions in Section 2 lack formal justification, making it unclear whether they are fundamental or experimental simplifications.  
- Technical clarity needed on domain-of-validity for discovered identities (e.g., dimensional constraints), as some reported identities may not hold in all cases.  
- Overall assessment: promising, original proof-of-concept with strong technical ideas but limited scope, clarity issues, and a key disconnect between search strategy and complexity goal.
