This paper, "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities," presents a novel approach to discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and propose two learning approaches, an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN), to learn a search strategy for the grammar trees.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of a learning-based approach to discovering efficient mathematical identities, which is a significant departure from traditional rule-based methods. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by discovering complex identities that are beyond the reach of brute-force search or human derivation.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, including the attribute grammar framework, the learning approaches, and the experimental results. The experiments are thorough, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the results.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The scope of the studied expressions is very restricted, with no proof of extensibility to more complex expressions. The authors acknowledge this limitation and discuss potential ways to extend their approach to more general settings.

Another limitation is the complexity mismatch between the problem statement and the algorithm. The problem statement seeks lower computational complexity, but the algorithm does not enforce this constraint. The authors should consider ways to limit the search to low-complexity solutions.

Additionally, the paper could benefit from more formal justification or empirical studies for the restrictions imposed in Section 2. The organization of the paper could also be improved, as the structure and stages are hard to follow at times.

From a technical perspective, the paper raises some concerns. For example, the constraints on vector/matrix sizes are unclear, and the RBM-1 expression in the supplement seems invalid for k=1, n=1.

In terms of significance, the paper has the potential to benefit symbolic software libraries, and the approach could be applied to more general settings to discover novel formulae in broader areas of mathematics.

Overall, I would rate this paper as follows:

* Novelty: 8/10 (the paper introduces a novel approach to discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques)
* Technical soundness: 7/10 (the paper raises some technical concerns, such as the complexity mismatch and unclear constraints on vector/matrix sizes)
* Clarity: 8/10 (the paper is well-written, but the organization could be improved)
* Significance: 8/10 (the paper has the potential to benefit symbolic software libraries and could be applied to more general settings)

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
