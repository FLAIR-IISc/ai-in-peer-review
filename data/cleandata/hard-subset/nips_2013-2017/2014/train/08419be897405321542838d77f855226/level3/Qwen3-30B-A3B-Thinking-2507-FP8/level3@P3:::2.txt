

# Review: Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities

This paper introduces an innovative approach to discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors propose a framework based on attribute grammars to represent symbolic expressions, addressing the combinatorial explosion problem inherent in brute-force search for equivalent but more efficient expressions. The key contributions include two novel learning approaches: an n-gram model over sub-expression sequences and a recursive neural network that learns continuous representations of symbolic expressions to predict combination rules. The authors demonstrate their approach on polynomial expressions, showing it can discover identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) or lower.

The work builds upon several related areas: attribute grammars from compiler construction (Knuth, 1968), symbolic computation engines (Maple, Mathematica), and natural language processing techniques (n-gram models, recursive neural networks). The paper effectively connects these domains, drawing inspiration from NLP approaches to apply them to mathematical expression discovery. While symbolic computation has been explored in theorem proving and program induction (e.g., Bridge et al., 2013), this paper is distinctive in its focus on learning from simpler expressions to discover more complex identities, rather than relying on hand-designed heuristics.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The framework is well-defined, with clear problem statement, appropriate mathematical formalism, and comprehensive experimental evaluation across five different expression families. The authors show that their learning approaches substantially outperform random search for complex expressions, with the n-gram model achieving superior results on two out of three tested tasks (Figure 3). The RNN-based representation learning (Section 4.2) is particularly impressive, achieving ≤5% error rates on expression classification tasks across varying degrees (Table 2).

The originality of the work is high. While attribute grammars have been used in other domains (e.g., knapsack packing, bioinformatics), this is the first application to discovering mathematical identities. The authors also make a novel contribution by applying recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of symbolic expressions, enabling machine learning techniques to operate on symbolic domains. This parallels work in NLP on word embeddings (Mikolov et al., 2013), but extends the concept to mathematical structures.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses in clarity and significance. The paper is somewhat difficult to follow, particularly Section 7.2, which explains the experimental setup and results. The mathematical notation can be dense without sufficient explanation, making it challenging for readers to fully grasp the methodology. The authors could improve clarity by providing more intuitive explanations of the attribute grammar framework and the relationship between the learned representations and the discovered identities.

The significance of the work is rated low. While the paper demonstrates an interesting technical approach, the problem appears contrived and unlikely to be adopted for practical computational efficiency. The paper's focus on restricted polynomial expressions with specific constraints (single matrix/vector variable, scalar output) limits its applicability to real-world mathematical problems. The discovered identities, while mathematically interesting, seem to address a narrow problem space that practitioners are unlikely to encounter frequently. The practical usefulness of the proposed search methods for producing computationally efficient symbolic expressions remains unclear, as the paper does not demonstrate integration with existing computational frameworks or provide evidence of real-world impact.

The paper's limitations are further underscored by the fact that the most challenging family of expressions (RBM-2)k could not be solved beyond k=5 with any of the proposed methods, while the random strategy performed comparably to the learning approaches for these complex cases. The authors acknowledge this limitation in Section 8 but do not sufficiently address how their framework could overcome these challenges in more general settings.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- High technical quality with sound methodology and thorough experiments
- Significant originality in applying machine learning to symbolic expression discovery
- Novel use of recursive neural networks to learn continuous representations of mathematical structures
- Demonstrates concrete examples of previously undiscovered mathematical identities

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Problem appears contrived with limited practical relevance to real-world computational problems
- Significance is low as the framework's applicability to broader mathematical domains remains uncertain
- Clarity issues, particularly in Section 7.2, hinder understanding of the methodology
- Practical usefulness for efficient computation is unclear, with no evidence of real-world adoption or integration

While the paper presents an interesting technical contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation, its limited significance and unclear practical utility make it less compelling for NIPS. The work is a valuable exploration of a novel technical approach, but the problem domain appears too narrow to have broad impact on the field. The authors should address the clarity issues and provide stronger justification for the practical importance of the problem they're solving.

I recommend rejection with the understanding that the work has technical merit but lacks sufficient significance for NIPS. The authors might consider expanding their framework to more general mathematical domains and demonstrating concrete applications to real computational problems to strengthen their contribution for future work.
