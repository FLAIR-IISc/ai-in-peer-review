- Novel idea: using DNNs to discover mathematically equivalent but computationally cheaper expressions.  
- Methodological framing: casts the problem as a search in an attribute grammar.  
- Models introduced: uses n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to guide the search.  
- Relation to literature: adequately connects to theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation.  
- Presentation: clear exposition of the attribute grammar framework.  
- Preliminary work: paper feels early-stage and below typical NIPS standards.  
- Limited empirical gains: reported performance improvements are modest overall.  
- Notable instance: one example achieves O(n^3) → O(n^2) improvement, but this may not generalize.  
- Narrow experimental scope: experiments restricted to polynomial manipulations with a single matrix.  
- Simpler baselines concern: hand-derived rules or simpler optimization strategies might achieve similar results.  
- Scalability unclear: exponential search growth is noted but learned strategies’ advantage over better heuristics is not convincingly shown.  
- RNN performance issues: RNN does not consistently outperform the simpler n-gram models.  
- Missing analysis: lacks robust assessment of computational cost of learning versus simplification benefits.  
- Acceptance positives: novel DNN application, interesting automation direction, and clear grammar presentation.  
- Acceptance negatives: limited validation, restricted scope, and unclear justification for method complexity.  
- Recommendation: recommend rejection now but encourage further development, broader evaluation, and stronger scalability analysis.
