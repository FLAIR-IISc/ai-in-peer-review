- Proposes a learning-guided framework to discover computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given symbolic target expression.
- Uses an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic operators to represent expressions and constrain search by degree and output type.
- Tests equivalence with two representations: modular integer evaluations for fast identity checks and modular Gaussian elimination for linear combinations.
- Also uses a learned continuous embedding from a recursive neural network (RNN) trained to classify expressions into equivalence classes.
- Trains strategies on simpler instances and transfers them to guide search on harder targets (curriculum learning).
- Evaluates two search strategies: an n-gram model counting frequent successful subtrees and an RNN policy predicting next grammar rule from subtree embeddings.
- Experiments on multiple families (AAT powers sums, elementwise variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, RBM exponentials) show learned strategies outperform random search.
- N-gram strategy often outperforms the RNN, and the most difficult family (RBM-2) remains largely unsolved.
- Situates work between automated theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic algebra, focusing on complexity reduction rather than general simplification.
- Novelty: applying learning-guided search and RNNs to symbolic identity discovery within an attribute-grammar space is original.
- The integer-descriptor plus linear-combination mechanism is a clever and practical engineering choice.
- Empirical study is substantial across five families and degrees, and the RNN achieves low classification error (<5%).
- Main evaluation metric is fraction of runs finding a match within 10 minutes; no end-to-end demonstration of real computational speedups.
- Missing quantification linking RNN embedding/classification accuracy to actual search performance; lacks ablations correlating representation quality and downstream success.
- Correctness checks rely on randomized modular testing without detailed discussion of failure probability or chosen parameters (N, p).
- Claims of “previously undiscovered” identities lack constructive symbolic proofs or independent verification.
- Baselines are limited to random search; no comparisons to greedy/beam/A*/MCTS, genetic programming, or CAS with complexity-aware objectives.
- Grammar restrictions (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, often single variable) limit generality and empirical breadth.
- Clarity issues: experimental section organization, lack of pseudocode for scheduler/strategy loop, and need for concrete worked examples and moved training details.
- Practical significance is uncertain; experimental scope feels contrived and no integration into realistic pipelines is shown.
- Pros: clear, original framing; solid engineering (descriptors, solver, curriculum learning); empirical gains over random; public code/data.
- Cons: unclear practical impact; limited baselines; RNN underperforms n-grams in many tasks; restrictive grammar; underspecified correctness guarantees and clarity problems.
- Suggestions: report wall-clock speedups and microbenchmarks; add stronger deterministic and evolutionary baselines and CAS comparisons; provide statistical guarantees for identity testing and symbolic proofs for some identities.
- Additional suggestions: analyze when/how the RNN helps with ablations correlating embedding accuracy and search success, consider RL for rule selection, and broaden grammar to multi-variable/non-homogeneous cases or a real system integration.
- Recommendation: technically interesting and original but currently limited in demonstrated significance and clarity; reviewer leans toward borderline reject pending stronger empirical validation and clearer exposition.
