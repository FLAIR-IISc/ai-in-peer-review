- Proposes discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning.
- Introduces an attribute grammar framework to represent symbolic expressions.
- Develops two learning-guided search methods: n-gram models over sub-expressions and an RNN producing continuous subtree representations to predict combination rules.
- Positions work relative to theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, but focuses on fixed-degree polynomials to avoid undecidability.
- Adapts NLP techniques (n-grams, recursive neural nets) to the symbolic computation domain.
- Main contributions: novel grammar, ML-guided search, and discovery of new identities that reduce computational complexity.
- Experiments show the methods find efficient identities across several expression families, including ones beyond brute-force or human derivation.
- Paper is generally well-written, though Section 7.2 (experimental setup/results) lacks clarity.
- Originality rated high (9/10).
- Overall quality rated high (8/10).
- Clarity rated moderate (6/10).
- Significance rated low (4/10) because the problem seems contrived and unlikely to be widely adopted.
- Limitation: focus on homogeneous polynomials of degree k and use of a custom grammar may limit generalizability and practical usefulness.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, demonstrated effectiveness, and promising adaptation of ML to symbolic computation.
- Arguments against acceptance: contrived problem, unclear practical usefulness, and limited applicability due to narrow focus and custom grammar.
