This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions within an attribute grammar and use machine learning models—specifically n-grams and a recursive neural network (RNN)—to guide the search for equivalent, more efficient expressions. This work connects to prior research in program induction and the application of recursive models to symbolic data, similar to work by Socher et al. in NLP and Bowman et al. in logical reasoning.

The primary strength lies in its originality and potential significance. The proposal to use learned, distributed representations to guide symbolic search is a compelling direction for a field traditionally dominated by rule-based systems. The demonstration of discovering new, non-trivial identities is a strong proof-of-concept, suggesting this approach has merit.

However, the paper has several weaknesses, primarily concerning clarity and methodological justification, which undermine its quality. The description of the RNN model is ambiguous, particularly the "weight tensor W3 (of size lxlxl...)." The authors should clarify if this constitutes a neural tensor network, which is a more standard architecture for such tasks. Relatedly, the paper should cite and discuss the work of Bowman, who showed that tensor networks often outperform standard RNNs on similar logical reasoning tasks, and justify their architectural choice.

Furthermore, the manuscript's clarity hinders reproducibility. The proposal generation mechanism within the search procedure is not explained in sufficient detail. Section 7.2 is also unclear on how the different search strategies are evaluated and the precise role of the RNN during the search. The paper is also marred by several typos, a duplicated opening sentence in the introduction, and an incorrect label in Figure 3 (TNN should be RNN). Finally, the problem definition is acknowledged as being restricted, which limits the immediate significance of the discovered identities, though the framework itself is promising.

In summary, the paper presents a highly original idea with promising initial results. The core contribution is significant. However, it is let down by a lack of clarity in key methodological descriptions and several presentation errors. A thorough revision to clarify the model architecture, justify the model choice against alternatives, and correct the textual errors would significantly strengthen this work.
