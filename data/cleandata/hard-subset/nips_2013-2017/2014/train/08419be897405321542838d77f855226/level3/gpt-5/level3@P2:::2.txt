Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies the automatic discovery of computationally efficient mathematical identities for a given target expression. The core idea is to search over expression trees generated by an attribute grammar, where each grammar rule carries an operation and an associated cost attribute. The goal is to find an expression equivalent to the target but with lower computational complexity. Because exhaustive enumeration is infeasible for nontrivial targets, the paper proposes learned search strategies trained on solutions to simpler targets:

- n-gram strategy: estimates the utility of subtrees by counting the frequency of depth-n substructures in previously discovered solutions.
- RNN strategy: learns a continuous representation φ(S) of sub-expressions using a recursive neural network defined over the grammar, then trains a softmax on top to predict which rule to apply next to grow a tree toward a solution.

Two representations of expressions are used for matching: (i) a numerical descriptor given by evaluations over random inputs in Zp, enabling fast equality checks and linear-combination solving via modular Gaussian elimination; and (ii) a learned embedding via RNN trained as a multiclass classifier over equivalence classes of expressions.

Experiments span synthetic families including sums over matrix expressions (with matrix multiply optionally disabled to ensure O(n^2) upper bounds), elementary symmetric polynomials, and exponentially large sums motivated by RBMs. Learning from simpler k improves performance at larger k; n-gram generally outperforms the RNN on two of three task families. The paper relates to theorem proving and program induction (e.g., Bridge et al., Knuth’s attribute grammars), symbolic computation systems (Maple/Mathematica), and neural methods in NLP (Socher et al.; recursive models).

Quality
- Positives: The technical construction is sound at a high level: the attribute-grammar setup constrains the search space; the integer-modulo descriptor is a practical device for equality testing and linear combinations; the learned strategies are trained incrementally and evaluated across increasing degrees k. The RNN classification results (≤5% error) suggest φ(S) captures useful structure. The n-gram vs. RNN comparison is honest: n-grams outperform the RNN on two families, and the paper provides intuition as to why.
- Concerns and clarifications needed:
  - Correctness guarantees: Equality over Zp with random inputs is a probabilistic test; please quantify the collision probability as a function of p, dimensionality, and number of samples N, and describe any final symbolic verification used.
  - Linear combinations of trees: The solver returns integer weights modulo p. How are these converted into valid symbolic identities over reals/integers (e.g., handling negative or large coefficients)? Are resulting expressions constrained to the grammar’s operations (sum, product, transpose, etc.) so that the identity corresponds to a realizable formula?
  - Complexity accounting: “Lower complexity” is discussed in big-O, but the cost model mixes algebraic degree and operator classes (e.g., disabling matrix-multiply). Please provide a precise operation-count model and validate actual wall-clock or flop reductions for representative sizes, compared to standard algebraic simplifiers (e.g., Mathematica’s FullSimplify) and known fast identities.
  - Scope limitations: Most experiments are on homogeneous polynomials in one variable (matrix or vector) with scalar outputs. The paper should clearly delineate where the approach fails (e.g., non-homogeneous targets, multiple variables, nonpolynomial ops) and how the grammar could be extended while retaining a sensible scheduler.
  - Empirical protocol: A fixed 10-minute cutoff is reasonable, but results would be more interpretable with curves over time-to-first-solution and beam size; also include success rates vs. number of trees expanded.

Clarity
The paper is ambitious but sometimes difficult to follow, especially Section 7.2. Specific suggestions:
- Move the full grammar and cost model into the main text, not just a table reference.
- Provide a small, self-contained example with its exact descriptor vectors, the solved linear system, and the recovered symbolic identity.
- For Example 2 (k=6), explicitly derive the O(nm) complexity and show a brief correctness argument.
- For the RNN, list all hyperparameters, dimensions, and training set sizes per k; clarify the training curriculum.
- Summarize key discovered identities in the main paper (perhaps one per family), not only in the supplement.

Originality
High. Applying attribute grammars with learned search policies to identity discovery is novel in this form. The learned continuous embedding φ(S) over symbolic expressions is an interesting contribution with potential reuse. The framing sits between classic rewrite/simplification systems and program synthesis, and the use of statistical learning to guide symbolic search is timely.

Significance
Mixed to low. While the technical ideas are interesting, the problem setting is fairly contrived, with hand-tailored grammars and targets. It is unclear whether the discovered identities offer practical speedups over standard algebraic optimizations or well-known linear algebra rearrangements, and whether practitioners will adopt such a system. The most ambitious family (RBM-2) remains largely out of reach beyond small k. Demonstrating practical wins in realistic pipelines (e.g., compiler passes, automatic differentiation systems, or probabilistic model evaluation) would strengthen impact.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and novel use of attribute grammars for identity discovery.
- Two concrete learned search strategies; careful comparison showing n-grams can be strong.
- RNN-based embeddings for symbolic expressions with good classification accuracy.
- Evidence that learning expands the feasible search horizon over brute force.

Cons
- Practical usefulness remains unclear; limited demonstration of end-to-end runtime gains.
- Heavy reliance on synthetic families and grammar restrictions (e.g., disabling matrix multiply).
- Insufficient discussion of correctness guarantees for modular descriptors and linear combinations.
- Clarity issues in experiments and missing details needed for full reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1. What is the probability of false equivalence under your Zp descriptor (as a function of p and N)? Do you perform a final exact symbolic check?
2. How do you translate modular weights into valid real-coefficient expressions? Are coefficients restricted (e.g., small integers), and are they represented within the grammar?
3. Can you report actual runtime or flop reductions for representative n,m,p, compared to baseline evaluation and symbolic simplifiers?
4. Why does the n-gram strategy outperform the RNN on two families? Did you try conditioning the RNN on k or using beam search with the softmax scores?
5. How sensitive are the results to the choice of grammar (operators included/excluded)?
6. Can the method handle multiple input matrices/vectors robustly, and non-homogeneous targets?

Recommendation
Technically solid and original, but with limited demonstrated significance. With clearer exposition in Section 7.2, stronger correctness justification for the descriptor/linear system, and a demonstration of real performance wins in practical settings, this would be much more compelling. As it stands, I am borderline due to concerns about practical impact, while recognizing the originality and potential of the learned-embedding approach to symbolic search.
