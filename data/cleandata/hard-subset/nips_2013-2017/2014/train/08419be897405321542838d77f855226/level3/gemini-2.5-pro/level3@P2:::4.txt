This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent the space of symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is analytically equivalent to a given target expression but has a lower computational cost. Recognizing that the search space grows exponentially, the authors propose using machine learning to guide the search. They explore two learning-based search strategies: an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN). These models are trained on solutions discovered for simpler expressions to learn patterns and structures that are common in efficient identities. A key contribution is the use of an RNN to learn a continuous vector representation for symbolic expressions, which allows symbolic manipulation to be cast as a machine learning problem. The paper demonstrates that this learning-based approach can discover complex, non-obvious identities that are intractable for brute-force or random search methods. This work connects ideas from program induction and symbolic computation with modern machine learning techniques from natural language processing, such as n-gram models and recursive networks for learning structured representations.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Originality and Significance:**
The primary strength of this paper is its novelty. The application of statistical machine learning, particularly deep learning models like RNNs, to the symbolic problem of discovering mathematical identities is highly original. The concept of learning a continuous representation for symbolic expressions, analogous to word embeddings in NLP, is a powerful idea that could open up this domain to a wide range of machine learning techniques.

The work has significant potential impact. If generalizable, this approach could be integrated into symbolic computation packages (e.g., Maple, Mathematica) or optimizing compilers to automatically find more efficient ways to compute complex expressions. The authors demonstrate this potential by discovering new, efficient identities for several families of expressions, some of which are relevant to machine learning (e.g., related to RBM partition functions). This demonstrates a clear advance over existing rule-based systems, which do not learn from experience.

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound in its implementation of the proposed learning models. The experimental results convincingly show that the learning-based search strategies (especially the n-gram models) significantly outperform the random search baseline, demonstrating the efficacy of learning from simpler problems to solve more complex ones.

However, there are a few concerns regarding the technical execution and claims:
1.  **Complexity Constraint:** The problem is defined as finding an expression with *lower* computational complexity. However, the search algorithm itself does not seem to explicitly enforce or prioritize this. For two of the experimental families, the authors achieve this by manually removing the expensive matrix-multiply rule from the grammar. This feels ad-hoc. A more principled approach would be to incorporate the complexity attribute directly into the search strategy's objective function, guiding the search towards low-cost solutions.
2.  **Scope Restrictions:** The framework is evaluated on a very restricted class of expressions: homogeneous polynomials of a single variable resulting in a scalar. The paper does not provide a clear justification for these restrictions or discuss the challenges in extending the framework to more general mathematical forms (e.g., involving different operators, multiple variables, or non-polynomial structures). Without this, the claims about the general applicability of the framework are not fully supported.
3.  **Technical Clarity:** Some details are unclear. For instance, there appear to be unstated constraints on the dimensions of vectors and matrices. The identity for the RBM-1 family provided in the supplement seems invalid for the simple case of k=1, n=1. Clarifying such details is important for reproducibility and assessing the work's correctness.

**Clarity:**
While the core ideas are exciting, the paper's organization makes it difficult to follow. The overall system has several stages (grammar definition, numerical representation, learned representation, tree generation with a scheduler/strategy, linear combination solver), but the relationship and flow between these components are not clearly articulated. A flowchart or high-level diagram of the entire discovery process would greatly improve readability. It is also not immediately clear when each of the two representations (numerical vs. learned) is used and for what purpose.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a highly original and promising research direction at the intersection of symbolic reasoning and machine learning.
*   The idea of learning continuous representations for mathematical expressions is a significant conceptual contribution.
*   The framework is shown to be effective, discovering new and non-trivial mathematical identities that are beyond the reach of naive search methods.
*   The work has strong potential for real-world impact in scientific computing and automated optimization.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper's clarity and organization are a major weakness, making the methodology difficult to understand.
*   The scope of the experiments is very limited, and the paper fails to convincingly argue for the method's generalizability.
*   The handling of the "lower complexity" constraint is ad-hoc, which weakens the problem formulation.
*   Certain technical details and justifications for design choices are missing.

**Conclusion**
Overall, this is a highly creative and thought-provoking paper that presents a genuinely new approach to a classic AI problem. The strengths in originality and potential impact are significant. While the paper suffers from issues with clarity and a limited experimental scope, these are addressable weaknesses. The core contribution is strong enough to warrant acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to revise the paper to improve its clarity and better contextualize the limitations and future challenges of their framework.
