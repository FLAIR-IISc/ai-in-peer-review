- Proposes ML-guided discovery of computationally efficient algebraic identities using an attribute grammar, Zp/randomized descriptors or RNN embeddings, and linear combinations of grammar trees.
- Learns search heuristics from simpler targets via an n-gram model over subtrees and an RNN that predicts the next grammar rule.
- Experiments on five expression families show some asymptotic cost reductions, with n-grams often outperforming the RNN.
- Novelty: applying neural guidance and combining attribute grammars with learned search for identity discovery is a fresh, original synthesis.
- Strength: clean formalization, clear presentation, useful examples, and released code.
- Strength: learned continuous embedding attains high accuracy on expression-equivalence classification.
- Weakness: empirical gains are modest and the method fails to outperform random search on some challenging families (e.g., RBM-2); scalability stalls at small k.
- Weakness: lacks strong baselines — no comparisons to modern CAS (Mathematica/Maple), compiler optimizations (CSE/Horner), or Gröbner-basis techniques.
- Weakness: verification relies on randomized Zp testing without quantified collision bounds or deterministic symbolic checks.
- Weakness: domain is narrowly constrained (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, often single-variable) where task-specific rules may suffice.
- Weakness: no wall-clock/runtime validation to confirm real-world speedups or account for search overhead.
- Clarity: paper is mostly clear but needs grammar and cost model in the main text, explicit expressivity of the linear-combination model, and more detail on the scheduler/pruning policy.
- Originality: individual components are standard, but their application to neural-guided identity discovery is reasonable and novel.
- Significance: limited by narrow domain, modest empirical wins, and the fact that simple statistical heuristics often suffice.
- Argument for acceptance: interesting, timely idea with a clean framework, code release, and some nontrivial discovered identities.
- Argument against acceptance: preliminary work with limited scope, modest results, weak validation, and heavy machinery that adds little over n-grams.
- Suggested improvement: add rigorous baselines using CAS and compiler-style optimizations with a consistent cost model.
- Suggested improvement: provide sound verification via symbolic equality checks or multi-prime certification with collision bounds.
- Suggested improvement: report real runtime speedups on BLAS-backed implementations including precomputation and memory costs.
- Suggested improvement: analyze failure modes, characterize when learning helps, expand the grammar, and ablate the training pipeline.
- Question: how often do false positives occur under the Zp descriptor and were identities validated with exact arithmetic?
- Question: how would this integrate with CAS or compilers and can you show end-to-end speedups on ML workloads (e.g., RBMs)?
- Question: can you provide comparisons to Mathematica/Maple on the target families under a cost proxy?
- Recommendation: interesting and creative but slightly below the acceptance bar due to limited scope, modest gains, lack of strong baselines, and unclear scalability.
