## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper introduces an interesting approach to discovering efficient mathematical identities by combining attribute grammars with machine learning. The core idea – using learned search strategies to navigate the exponentially growing space of possible expression trees – is novel and potentially impactful. The authors demonstrate this with examples of identities offering computational improvements, such as reducing the complexity of matrix operations from O(n³) to O(n²). This work builds upon existing research in symbolic computation, program induction, and the application of neural networks to structured data, drawing connections to areas like natural language processing (specifically recursive neural networks) and tensor networks.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The problem definition is overly restrictive, limiting the scope to homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix variable. While the authors acknowledge this, the justification for these constraints feels insufficient. More importantly, the description of the neural network architecture is unclear. The mention of “weight tensor W3 (of size lxlxl...)” is ambiguous; it’s unclear if a neural tensor network is being used or if this is simply a standard linear layer with a potentially misleading name. The authors should clarify this point. Furthermore, they fail to justify their choice of an RNN over tensor networks, especially given Bowman’s (2013) work demonstrating the superior performance of tensor networks on similar tasks involving structured data. 

Section 7.2, detailing the evaluation of different search strategies, is also unclear. It’s not immediately obvious whether the RNN is still used for classification *within* the search process, or solely for pre-training representations. The proposal generation step within the search procedure lacks sufficient detail – how are candidate rules selected and scored? 

Finally, the manuscript contains numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., repeated sentences, incorrect figure labels – Figure 3 incorrectly labels the RNN as a TNN). These detract from the paper’s readability and professionalism.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of machine learning to symbolic identity discovery.
*   Demonstrated ability to find identities beyond manual derivation or brute-force search.
*   Potential for practical applications in compiler optimization and algorithm simplification.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Overly restrictive problem definition.
*   Lack of clarity in the neural network architecture description.
*   Failure to address relevant work on tensor networks.
*   Unclear explanation of the search strategy evaluation.
*   Numerous grammatical errors and typos.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents an interesting idea with promising results. However, the clarity issues, lack of justification for key design choices, and grammatical errors prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. I suggest a **major revision** addressing the points raised above. A clearer explanation of the model, a justification for the RNN choice, and a more detailed description of the search strategy are crucial for improving the paper’s quality.
