Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles the novel problem of discovering computationally efficient algebraic identities equivalent to a given target expression. The authors formalize expressions via an attribute grammar whose production rules correspond to algebraic operations with associated computational costs. Because exhaustive search over grammar trees is exponential in degree, they propose learning-guided search strategies trained on identities discovered for simpler targets. Two representations are introduced: (i) a fast, integer-valued numerical descriptor modulo a large prime to test equivalence (and to assemble linear combinations of trees via Gaussian elimination mod p), and (ii) a learned continuous representation of expressions via a recursive neural network (RNN) trained to classify grammar trees into equivalence classes. Search is then guided either by n-gram statistics over subtrees or by an RNN-based policy predicting the next rule to apply. Experiments over several expression families (e.g., sums of powers of AAT, elementwise-polynomial variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related exponential sums) show that learning-guided search can discover identities of provably lower asymptotic complexity than the naive evaluation and extends the range of degrees beyond random search; n-gram strategies often outperform the RNN in these settings.

The work connects to automated theorem proving and program induction (e.g., Bridge et al. on learning to select heuristics; Church/probabilistic programming), symbolic computation systems (Maple/Mathematica), grammar-based optimization, and RNNs in NLP (Socher et al.) for learned structural representations. It also relates to symbolic regression/program synthesis and polynomial identity testing (e.g., DeMillo–Lipton/Schwartz–Zippel) as a way to reason about collision probabilities for the numerical descriptor.

Strengths
- Clear and interesting problem formulation with practical relevance: finding complexity-reducing rewrites of algebraic computations.
- Novel use of attribute grammars combined with learned search strategies; the n-gram vs RNN comparison is informative.
- A practical, fast equivalence test using modular arithmetic and a linear-combination solver enables scaling beyond fully symbolic methods.
- Evidence that learning from simpler identities improves the discovery rate for more complex ones; several identities appear nontrivial and hard to find by hand.
- Code and data are available, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Scope is quite restricted (homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree; primarily single variable; scalar outputs; a limited operator set). The paper stops short of demonstrating extensibility to richer operators or multi-variable settings.
- Complexity objective is not enforced during search. Beyond ad hoc constraints (e.g., removing matrix–matrix multiply in some families), the scheduler can generate high-cost trees; the selection criterion is primarily “matching the target,” not “minimizing cost.”
- Organization/flow is hard to follow; the roles of grammar, descriptor(s), linear combination step, and learned strategies emerge piecemeal.
- Some technical details are underspecified or potentially inconsistent: dimension constraints for certain families (e.g., RBM-1), choice of N and p for descriptors and collision guarantees, complexity accounting (which operations count, reuse of subexpressions, memory costs).
- Empirically, the RNN underperforms simple n-grams on several families; limited analysis of failure modes and when each approach should be expected to work.
- Limited baselines: no comparison to rule-based simplifiers (CAS), equality saturation/term-rewriting systems, or program synthesis/grammar-guided search with cost-aware heuristics.

Quality
- Soundness: The modular descriptor and linear solver are compelling; however, there is no probabilistic bound on false positives/negatives due to hash collisions. A simple analysis via Schwartz–Zippel would strengthen claims.
- Completeness: The work demonstrates end-to-end identity discovery but lacks guarantees on optimality (or even on returning a lower-cost identity when one exists). The evaluation focuses on success rates under time caps, not on achieved cost vs best-known cost.
- Evaluation: The families are reasonable and show benefits of learned search; yet comparisons are mostly to random search. Including a cost-aware heuristic (e.g., best-first with cumulative operator cost) or a CAS baseline would contextualize the gains.

Clarity
- The narrative would benefit from a clearer pipeline overview early on: (1) grammar and cost model, (2) equality checking via descriptors, (3) linear combinations of trees, (4) search scheduler and strategies, (5) training protocol across degrees. Pseudocode for the scheduler/strategy interface would help.
- Explicitly define the operation set and their cost model, including whether costs reflect asymptotics or flop counts, and how subexpression reuse is handled.
- Specify matrix/vector dimensions at each rule, and validate dimensionality for all families (see question below).
- Minor typos: line 332 “show for” → “show”; 148 “has contains” → “contains”; 289 “is is” → “is”; 338 “by be” → “be.”

Originality
- The combination of attribute grammars with learned, tree-structural representations for identity discovery is novel. The RNN embedding of symbolic expressions is an interesting contribution. Related areas (symbolic regression/program synthesis, equality saturation, and identity testing) could be discussed more fully.

Significance
- If extended to richer operators and multi-variable settings, this line of work could impact compiler backends, automatic code optimization, and CAS systems. As presented, the scope limits immediate applicability, though the demonstrated identities and methodology are promising.

Specific suggestions
- Enforce complexity during search: incorporate operator-cost-aware best-first/beam search; use A* with an admissible heuristic (e.g., lower bounds on remaining degree given cheapest operations); add a cost penalty to the RNN/n-gram scoring; prune partial trees whose minimum achievable cost exceeds a target budget.
- Provide formal bounds on descriptor collisions (choice of p, N) leveraging polynomial identity testing; report empirical collision rates.
- Clarify the linear-combination step: how trees are selected into F, whether coefficients are constrained (e.g., small integers), and how identities are rendered back into compact, human-readable formulas.
- Expand ablations: effect of training curriculum; varying l; dropout depth schedule; descriptor size; impact of multi-variable targets. Include a cost-aware non-learning baseline.
- Compare against simple rule-based CAS simplifications or equality-saturation engines on overlapping subsets.
- Improve organization with an early figure summarizing the pipeline and a table of all operators, input/output shapes, and costs.

Questions for the authors
- Dimensions: In RBM-1, A is stated as a vector in R^n and v ∈ {0,1}^n; for k=1, n=1 the supplemental expression appears dimensionally ambiguous. Please clarify shapes and how (v^T A)^k is formed for all n,k.
- Descriptor parameters: What are typical choices for p and N per family/degree, and how sensitive are results? Any observed or bounded collision rates?
- Complexity metric: Do you count sums, elementwise ops, and transposes equally? Are asymptotics derived from operator arities only, or do you account for reuse/common subexpression elimination?
- What prevents the RNN policy from preferring high-cost but “easier-to-match” expressions? Have you tried training with a cost-regularized loss or reward?

Arguments for acceptance
- Interesting, underexplored problem with concrete results and publicly released code/data.
- Novel and potentially influential combination of grammar-guided search with learned representations; some identities likely new.
- Clean modular descriptor enabling scalable equivalence checks.

Arguments against acceptance
- Scope is quite narrow, and extensibility is not demonstrated.
- The optimization objective (lower complexity) is not integrated into the search in a principled way.
- Empirical evaluation lacks stronger baselines and formal guarantees; RNN underperforms simple n-grams without much analysis.
- Clarity/organization issues and some technical ambiguities.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The paper presents a promising idea with solid components and some compelling results, but the restricted scope, lack of cost-aware search, and clarity/validation gaps limit its current impact. Addressing the above issues would make it much stronger.
