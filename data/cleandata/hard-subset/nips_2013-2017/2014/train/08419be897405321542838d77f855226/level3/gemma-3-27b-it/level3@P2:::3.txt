This paper explores the application of machine learning to the discovery of efficient mathematical identities. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and develop learning approaches – n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN) – to guide the search for computationally simpler, equivalent expressions. The core idea of learning from simpler expressions to tackle more complex ones is novel and potentially impactful.

The work builds upon existing research in theorem proving [5, 9, 11], program induction [18, 28], and probabilistic programming [12, 20], but appropriately restricts the domain to polynomials of fixed degree, mitigating issues of undecidability. The use of attribute grammars [14] as a search space is a sound foundation, drawing connections to compiler construction and previous applications in algorithmic problems [19, 26] and music [10]. The paper also acknowledges connections to natural language processing techniques like n-gram models and recursive neural networks [2, 3, 15, 22, 23], adapting them to the symbolic computation domain.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of machine learning, particularly deep learning with RNNs, to the problem of mathematical identity discovery is a significant contribution. The framework offers a potentially automated method for finding optimizations beyond manual derivation.
* **Technical Soundness:** The described attribute grammar and the numerical/learned representation methods appear logically sound. The experimental results, while limited in scope, demonstrate the effectiveness of the learning approaches in certain cases. The consistent improvement over random search, especially with n-gram models, is encouraging.
* **Potential Impact:** Discovering efficient mathematical identities can have practical implications for various fields, including scientific computing and machine learning itself (as hinted at in the discussion section).
* **Availability of Code:** The publicly available code and evaluation data ([https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning](https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning)) promote reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Problem Restriction:** The restriction to homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix variable is quite limiting. While acknowledged by the authors, the impact on the generalizability of the approach should be emphasized. The justification for these restrictions feels weak.
* **Clarity:** The explanation of the RNN architecture, specifically the “weight tensor W3 (of size lxlxl…)”, is unclear and requires further elaboration. Is this a neural tensor network, or something else? The paper should clarify this point.
* **Model Justification:** The choice of an RNN over, for example, a tensor network (as highlighted by Bowman [3]) is not justified. Tensor networks have demonstrated superior performance on similar tasks involving structured data and relational reasoning. The authors should address this.
* **Experimental Setup Clarity:** Section 7.2 lacks clarity regarding how the different search strategies are evaluated and whether the RNN is still employed for classification during the search process. A more detailed explanation of the experimental protocol is needed.
* **Proposal Generation:** The method for generating proposals within the search procedure isn't detailed enough. How are the initial trees constructed, and how does the strategy influence this process beyond scoring grammar rules? More explanation is needed to understand the entire search process.
* **Presentation & Polish:** The paper suffers from several minor issues, including typos and grammatical errors. For instance, the opening sentence of the introduction appears twice. Figure 3 incorrectly labels the TNN as RNN.  These errors detract from the paper's overall quality.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel approach to a challenging problem.
* The experimental results demonstrate the feasibility of using machine learning to discover useful mathematical identities.
* The code and data are publicly available.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The strong restriction on the problem domain limits the practical applicability of the proposed method.
* Clarity issues in the description of the RNN architecture and experimental setup.
* Lack of justification for the model choice and insufficient comparison to related work.
* Numerous minor presentation issues detract from the overall quality of the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution, but suffers from clarity issues, limitations in scope, and a lack of thorough justification for key design choices. While the core idea is promising, the current presentation requires significant improvement.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should address the clarity issues, broaden the scope of the problem (or provide stronger justification for the restrictions), and provide a more thorough comparison to related work, especially tensor networks. A careful proofreading to eliminate typos and grammatical errors is also essential. Addressing these concerns would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
