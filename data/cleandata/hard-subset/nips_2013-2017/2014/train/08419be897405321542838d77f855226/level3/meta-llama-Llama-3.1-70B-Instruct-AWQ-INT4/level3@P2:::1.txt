This paper explores the application of machine learning techniques to the discovery of efficient mathematical identities. The authors propose a novel attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and introduce two learning approaches to guide the search for efficient identities. The first approach uses n-gram models, while the second employs a recursive neural network (RNN) to learn a continuous representation of mathematical structures.

The authors relate their work to previous research in theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming. However, they argue that their approach is distinct in its use of machine learning techniques to learn heuristics automatically, rather than relying on hand-designed heuristics.

The paper's main contributions include the introduction of a novel grammar framework for finding efficient versions of symbolic expressions, the application of machine learning techniques to guide the search for these expressions, and the discovery of new mathematical identities that offer significant reductions in computational complexity.

The authors evaluate their approach using five families of expressions and compare the performance of their learning-based methods to a random search strategy. The results show that the learning-based methods outperform the random strategy for large values of k, with the n-gram models performing better than the RNN for certain families of expressions.

The paper's strengths include its novel application of machine learning techniques to the discovery of mathematical identities and its potential to scale to more general expressions. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of their approach and discuss its limitations.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The method's performance at discovering identities is not particularly impressive, and the experiments used a very limited set of input expressions. Additionally, it is unclear whether the approach would scale to more general expressions or whether scaling is justified given the modest results. The reviewer also notes that hand-derived mathematical rules might have been simpler, given the limited domain.

Overall, while the paper is interesting and presents a novel approach to the discovery of mathematical identities, its preliminary nature and limited results may not meet NIPS publication standards.
