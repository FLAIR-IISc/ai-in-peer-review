

This paper introduces an attribute grammar framework combined with n-gram and RNN-based learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities for restricted polynomial expressions. The core idea—applying machine learning to guide symbolic search within a grammar—builds on prior NIPS work in recursive neural networks (Socher et al., 2011) and grammar-based symbolic computation (e.g., Cheung & McCanne, 2001), but applies it to a narrow mathematical domain.  

**Strengths**: The technical approach is sound for its limited scope. The RNN-based continuous representation for symbolic expressions achieves 95%+ classification accuracy on homogeneous polynomials (Table 2), demonstrating a useful embedding technique. The framework successfully identifies identities like Example 2 (e.g., the k=6 case), where brute-force search fails.  

**Weaknesses**: The work is significantly preliminary. The experiments are confined to *extremely restricted domains*: single-matrix/vector expressions, homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, and no consideration of real-world mathematical complexity (e.g., trigonometric or recursive functions). Crucially, the "novel" identities (e.g., Example 2) are trivially derived by hand through pattern repetition—this is a classic "sledgehammer-to-crack-a-nut" scenario where the ML approach adds no value over simple rule-based derivation. The claimed computational savings (e.g., O(n³) → O(n²)) are marginal for the toy problems tested, and scaling to general expressions is neither demonstrated nor justified (e.g., the RBM-2 family fails for k>5). The paper’s "discovery" of identities is thus not meaningful beyond its artificial constraints.  

**Evaluation**:  
- *Quality*: Technically competent but incomplete; lacks rigorous analysis of failure modes or scalability.  
- *Originality*: Minimal novelty—the grammar framework is standard (Knuth, 1968), and the ML integration is a straightforward adaptation.  
- *Significance*: Results are confined to a niche, trivial problem space; no evidence the approach advances beyond symbolic computation or benefits broader ML practice.  
- *Clarity*: Well-structured, but the narrow scope is underjustified.  

**Conclusion**: While the idea of applying ML to symbolic search is intriguing, the paper’s execution is too limited to meet NIPS standards. The modest results, trivial problem domain, and lack of scalability arguments place it below the acceptance threshold. The work would be better suited for a specialized symbolic computation venue.
