This paper proposes an attribute-grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and a learned search procedure to discover algebraic identities with lower computational complexity than a given target. The search is guided by two learned strategies: (i) n-gram statistics over operator subtrees and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that embeds partial trees and predicts the next rule. The authors demonstrate that distributed, recursive neural representations can classify algebraic identities and that learned guidance enables discovery of identities beyond brute-force reach, including reductions of O(n3) expressions to O(n2) and speedups on RBM-like sums. This connects to prior work on grammars in optimization, program induction, theorem proving, and tree-structured neural models in NLP and logic (e.g., Socher et al.; Bowman).

Quality: The empirical results are intriguing and the release of code is valuable. However, several technical aspects are underspecified. The RNN architecture in Sec. 4.2 is unclear: the “weight tensor W3 (of size l×l×l)” suggests a tensor (RNTN-style) bilinear composition, but the text alternates between linear and tensor operations. Prior work (e.g., Bowman, 2013) often finds tensor composition outperforms vanilla RNNs on tree-structured logical tasks; the paper should justify its specific choice (linear vs tensor) and report ablations. In Sec. 7.2, it is unclear whether the RNN continues to serve as a classifier or only as a proposal model for search, and how search strategies are evaluated and compared (greedy vs beam, branching factor, tie-breaking, proposal generation, stopping criteria). The proposal-generation mechanism itself needs more detail.

Clarity: Organization is generally good, but the manuscript contains numerous typos and labeling issues that hinder comprehension. Examples: duplicated opening sentence in the Introduction; “which the discovery” → “which aid the discovery”; “O(()n3)” (spurious parentheses); “The output of this stage is this max((W3∗a)∗a,0)” (duplicated “this”); inconsistent notation around tensor contractions; Figure 3 labels “TNN” where it should be “RNN.” Please also clarify whether descriptors in Zp are provably collision-resistant for the explored grammar sizes.

Originality: The combination of attribute grammars with learned guidance for identity discovery and learned continuous embeddings for algebraic expressions is novel and timely relative to NIPS topics.

Significance: Potential impact is high, but currently limited by the overly restrictive problem setting (homogeneous polynomials, single variable, scalar outputs). A clearer path to relaxing these constraints or demonstrating breadth would strengthen the contribution.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel framing of identity discovery with learned search; nontrivial identities found.
- Interesting evidence that recursive neural representations classify algebraic structures.
- Public code/data; relevance to ML systems and compilers.

Cons for acceptance:
- Ambiguity about the RNN/RNTN architecture and missing comparisons to tensor networks.
- Insufficient detail on proposal generation and search evaluation.
- Restricted problem definition; several clarity and typographical errors.

Overall, promising work that would benefit from architectural clarification, stronger baselines/ablations (incl. tensor networks), clearer search-details, and a thorough proofreading.
