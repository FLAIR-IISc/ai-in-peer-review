- Presents a novel framework that searches an attribute-grammar space for analytically equivalent expressions with lower computational complexity.  
- Uses machine learning to guide an otherwise intractable combinatorial search, with two strategies: an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN).  
- Trains models on solutions to simpler expressions to prune the search space for more complex targets.  
- Introduces a learned continuous (RNN-based) vector representation for symbolic expressions, analogous to NLP embeddings.  
- Demonstrates discovery of non-trivial, novel identities for families of complex expressions, some relevant to ML.  
- Positions work at the intersection of automated theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation, focusing on computational efficiency.  
- Strength: highly novel and significant direction bridging statistical learning and symbolic reasoning with practical compiler/scientific computing potential.  
- Strength: learned symbolic embeddings are meaningful (high classification accuracy) and could enable new ML applications to mathematics/program optimization.  
- Strength: empirical results show learning-guided search outperforms random baselines on difficult problems.  
- Weakness: RNN architecture is ambiguously described (mention of a W3 tensor vs. calling it an RNN); needs precise mathematical definition.  
- Weakness: search procedure lacks detail—how expressions are proposed and how model scores drive the scheduler is under-specified.  
- Weakness: unclear relationship between the RNN used for classification and the RNN used for search (same model or adapted?).  
- Weakness: insufficient justification/comparison of model choice—prior work (e.g., Bowman 2013) suggests tensor networks may outperform RNNs; empirical or conceptual comparison needed.  
- Weakness: problem scope is restricted to homogeneous single-variable scalar polynomials; paper should discuss challenges for broader expression classes.  
- Weakness: presentation issues—typographical/grammatical errors, repeated sentences, and mislabeling in figures reduce clarity.  
- Overall: promising and compelling contribution but currently held back by clarity, model-justification, and presentation issues that should be addressed in revision.
