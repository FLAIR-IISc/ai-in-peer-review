

This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques applied to attribute grammars. The authors introduce a grammar-based representation of symbolic expressions and demonstrate how learning approaches (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) can guide the search for computationally efficient expressions that are analytically equivalent to target expressions. The key insight is that training models on solutions to simpler expressions enables discovery of more complex identities beyond brute-force reach.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with clear experimental validation. The learned representations for symbolic expressions (Section 4.2) demonstrate strong classification accuracy (≤5% error rate for degree 3-6 expressions), and the framework successfully discovers novel identities with significant computational complexity reductions (e.g., from O(n³) to O(n²) complexity). The approach represents a meaningful extension of NLP techniques to symbolic computation, with clear connections to previous work in recursive neural networks (Socher et al., 2013) and attribute grammars (Knuth, 1968).

However, the paper has several significant limitations. The scope is severely restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with a single matrix variable (Section 2), with no justification for why this limitation is fundamental rather than a technical convenience. The authors claim to seek lower computational complexity but fail to enforce this constraint in their algorithm, leaving open the possibility that learned search strategies could find high-complexity solutions. The technical justification for the grammar restrictions (e.g., removal of matrix-multiply rule) is insufficiently formalized, and the numerical examples (like RBM-1 in the supplement) appear invalid for small parameter values (k=1, n=1).

The paper's organization is challenging to follow, particularly the explanation of the scheduler and search strategy. While the approach has potential impact for symbolic computation libraries, the lack of proof for extensibility to more complex expressions weakens the significance claim. Several minor typos further undermine readability.

Strengths: Novel integration of learning with symbolic expression discovery, strong empirical results on restricted domain, clear connections to prior work in NLP and grammars.

Weaknesses: Overly restrictive problem formulation, insufficient justification for limitations, failure to enforce computational complexity constraints, poor organization, technical inconsistencies in examples.

The paper makes an interesting contribution to a niche area, but the limited scope and unaddressed technical concerns prevent it from being a significant advancement of the field. The work would be substantially strengthened by addressing the scope limitations and providing formal justification for the restrictions.
