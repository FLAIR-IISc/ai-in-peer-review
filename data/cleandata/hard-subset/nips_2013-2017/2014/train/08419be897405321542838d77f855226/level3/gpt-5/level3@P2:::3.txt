Summary
This paper proposes learning-guided search for discovering computationally more efficient identities that are analytically equivalent to a given target expression. Expressions are represented by an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic and elementwise operators; candidate expressions are composed as trees whose costs are the sum of operator costs. Equivalence checking is performed numerically via integer descriptors using random evaluations modulo a large prime (akin to randomized polynomial identity testing). For difficult targets, linear combinations of trees are solved via modular Gaussian elimination to match a target descriptor. Because exhaustive search is infeasible, the paper trains strategies from solutions to simpler targets: (i) n-gram models over frequently occurring subtrees; (ii) a recursive neural network that embeds partial trees and predicts the next rule. Experiments report identities that reduce asymptotic cost (e.g., O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)) in matrix sums; reducing O(kn^3) to O(kn^2) under a restricted grammar; limited progress on exponential-sum families). The paper also shows that distributed, recursive representations can classify algebraic expression equivalence classes with low error.

Relation to prior work: The work connects to theorem proving and program induction (e.g., Bridge et al.; Goodman et al.), compiler/grammar optimization (Knuth attribute grammars; Cheung & McCanne), and compositional representation learning with recursive networks (Socher et al., Luong et al.). The use of random modular evaluation relates to randomized identity testing (e.g., Schwartz–Zippel-style ideas, though not cited). Compared to symbolic CAS simplifiers (Maple/Mathematica), the novelty is learning to guide search for lower-cost equivalent programs.

Strengths
- Clear, timely problem: automating discovery of cheaper equivalent computations is impactful for ML/vision/numerics.
- Elegant integration of attribute grammars with learned policies; modular arithmetic descriptors make equality checking fast and learning-friendly.
- Empirically, n-gram and RNN strategies substantially enlarge the search horizon vs random; several nontrivial identities are reported.
- The learned recursive representation achieves low classification error on equivalence classes, suggesting a promising bridge between continuous embeddings and symbolic algebra.
- Code availability and curriculum training aid reproducibility and stability.

Weaknesses
- Scope restrictions are strong: homogeneous polynomials, single variable (mostly), scalar output, and in places removal of matrix-multiplication from the grammar. It is unclear how often the reported gains translate to general multivariate or nonhomogeneous settings.
- The RNN architecture and training objective are under-specified. The text alternates between linear RNN and tensor interactions; W3 is described as an l×l×l “weight tensor,” suggesting a neural tensor network (RNTN). The paper should clearly state the exact composition function(s) used, whether bilinear/tensor layers are employed, and justify the choice relative to RNTN baselines (cf. Socher et al., Bowman).
- Search procedure details are insufficient: proposal generation, branching, and termination criteria need precise pseudocode. Section 7.2 leaves unclear whether the RNN is used purely as an embedding for a softmax policy or also as a classifier at evaluation time; how many candidates per step are explored; and how the scheduler interleaves tree construction with the linear-combination solver.
- Evaluation framing focuses on “fraction of runs within 10 minutes” without reporting absolute search effort, solution diversity, or effect sizes in wall-clock speedups on actual numerical workloads. Comparisons to CAS systems or hand-crafted heuristics are missing.
- No probabilistic guarantee for the integer-descriptor equivalence check (collision probability as a function of prime size, number of evaluations, and degree) is provided.
- Presentation issues: several typos/grammar errors; duplicate/repeated sentences (opening line appears twice); Figure 3 labels “TNN” instead of “RNN”; section cross-references and notation are occasionally inconsistent.

Quality
- Methodologically sound at a high level, but with important missing details (exact composition function, proposal policy, and equivalence-check guarantees). Experimental results are promising yet limited by the narrow grammar and time-based success metric. Ablations (e.g., n-gram depth weighting, dropout rates, composition function variants) are sparse.

Clarity
- Overall narrative is understandable, but key components require clarification:
  - Precisely define the composition function(s): linear, bilinear, or tensor; parameter counts; sharing across operators; and why.
  - Provide the scheduler/strategy interface, proposal generation, and stopping criteria in pseudocode.
  - Explicitly separate the RNN’s roles: pretraining for equivalence classification vs policy learning; what is kept/frozen; how the softmax over rules is trained and regularized.
  - Specify prime sizes, number of evaluation points, and a bound on collision probability in the modular descriptor scheme.
  - Define the operator cost model and how complexity is measured when rules are removed (e.g., matrix-multiply).

Originality
- The combination of attribute grammars, modular equality testing, and learned search strategies for algebraic identity discovery appears novel for NIPS. The learned recursive embedding of symbolic expressions is an interesting contribution, though related to prior RNTN work.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for compiler optimization, automatic differentiation systems, and ML models with heavy linear algebra. However, current restrictions and the stronger performance of simple n-grams relative to the RNN cast some doubt on the added value of the neural approach as presented. Demonstrating end-to-end speedups and broader applicability would elevate significance.

Specific suggestions
- Clarify the architecture: if using an RNTN (tensor layer), say so explicitly; otherwise replace W3 with a bilinear or affine composition and justify. Compare RNN vs RNTN baselines (cf. Socher et al.; Bowman’s observations on logical tasks).
- Provide pseudocode of the scheduler and training data generation. Detail how candidate branches are selected, beam size (if any), and when the linear solver is invoked.
- Quantify descriptor correctness: prime p, number of samples N, and a bound on Pr[collision]. Cite randomized identity testing.
- Add baselines with CAS simplification and simple algebraic rewrite heuristics. Report actual runtime speedups on synthetic/real matrices.
- Expand to small multivariate cases or nonhomogeneous targets to demonstrate generality.
- Fix presentation issues: Figure 3 label (TNN→RNN); remove duplicate sentence in the introduction; proofread for typos/grammar; ensure consistent notation and references.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel framing and promising early results on an important problem.
- Demonstrates that learned policies can guide combinatorial symbolic search.
- Reusable idea: continuous embeddings for algebraic structures.

Arguments against acceptance
- Overly restricted problem setting and limited empirical scope.
- Key methodological details are unclear; RNN rationale vs alternatives not well justified.
- Evaluation lacks strong baselines and end-to-end performance evidence.
- Presentation issues (typos, mislabeled figure, repeated sentences).

Recommendation
Leaning reject. The paper tackles an exciting direction and contains good ideas, but clarity and evaluation need strengthening. With architectural clarification, stronger baselines, and broader evidence of impact, this could be a strong submission. Reviewer confidence: medium.
