This paper introduces a novel approach to discovering efficient mathematical identities by framing the problem within an attribute grammar and leveraging machine learning to guide the search for optimal expressions. The core idea – using learned models to navigate the exponentially growing space of symbolic expressions – is intriguing and represents a potentially valuable contribution to automated mathematical reasoning. The authors propose two methods: an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN) for predicting successful combinations of grammar rules.

The work builds upon existing research in several areas. It intersects with theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, all of which grapple with similar challenges of search and undecidability. However, the authors rightly point out that their setting – restricted to homogeneous polynomials – circumvents some of these inherent difficulties.  The use of attribute grammars, borrowed from compiler construction, is a clever way to represent symbolic expressions, and the adaptation of techniques from natural language processing (NLP), such as n-gram models and RNNs, to the symbolic domain is innovative.  Relevant prior work in symbolic computation engines (Maple, Mathematica) is acknowledged, noting their rule-based nature and lack of learning. The connection to recent work using learning for heuristics in reasoning (Bridge et al.) is also appropriately highlighted.

The quality of the paper is generally high. The technical approach appears sound, and the experimental results, as presented in Figure 3, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The authors provide examples (Example 1 & 2) illustrating the potential for discovering identities that reduce computational complexity. The provision of code and evaluation data is commendable and facilitates reproducibility. However, clarity is a significant weakness. While the overall concept is clear, the details, particularly in Section 7.2 concerning the removal of the matrix-multiply rule and its impact, are difficult to follow. The descriptions of the RNN training process could also benefit from more clarity; the specific details of dropout implementation and the handling of the input vector 'a' are not immediately obvious.

The originality of the work is high. The combination of attribute grammars, machine learning-guided search, and the application of RNNs to symbolic expressions is a novel contribution. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of related work and effectively position their approach within the broader research landscape. The reported discovery of new mathematical identities further strengthens the claim of originality.

However, the significance of the work is relatively low. The problem addressed – finding computationally efficient mathematical identities within the specified constraints – feels somewhat contrived. While the potential applications in areas like RBM optimization are mentioned, the likelihood of widespread adoption for general-purpose efficient computation seems limited. The restrictions imposed on the problem domain (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix/vector variable) significantly limit the applicability of the results. It's unclear how well these methods would generalize to more complex mathematical expressions or real-world optimization problems. The practical usefulness of the proposed search methods remains an open question. Even when successful, the improvement in complexity (e.g., O(n3) to O(n2)) may not be substantial enough to warrant the complexity of the learning-based search approach, particularly given the overhead of training the models.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and innovative approach combining attribute grammars and machine learning.
*   Demonstrated ability to discover new mathematical identities.
*   Sound technical approach and well-supported experimental results.
*   Availability of code and data for reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited clarity, particularly in Section 7.2 and the RNN training details.
*   The problem domain feels contrived and lacks broad applicability.
*   The practical significance of the complexity reductions achieved is questionable.
*   The RNN consistently underperforms the n-gram approach, questioning its added value.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a technically sound and original contribution, but the authors need to address the clarity issues and better articulate the potential impact of their work. A more thorough discussion of the limitations and potential future directions would also be beneficial. Specifically, elaborating on the reasons the n-gram model outperforms the RNN (and exploring potential remedies) is crucial.
