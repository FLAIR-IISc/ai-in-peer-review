

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering mathematical identities with lower computational complexity using machine learning. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and employ learning techniques to guide the search for efficient expressions. The core idea is to learn from simpler expressions to discover more complex identities, using n-gram models and recursive neural networks to improve search efficiency beyond brute-force enumeration.

The work builds upon attribute grammars (Knuth, 1968) and draws inspiration from natural language processing techniques (n-gram models, recursive neural networks). It differs from previous symbolic computation systems (Maple, Mathematica) by using learning approaches rather than hand-designed heuristics, and from theorem proving work by focusing on polynomial expressions with manageable search spaces. The paper demonstrates successful discovery of several new identities, including matrix polynomial identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n³) to O(n²).

**Strengths:**
1. The paper presents a novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation, demonstrating how learning can guide combinatorial search in a domain where brute-force approaches fail.
2. The framework is technically sound, with clear experimental results showing the effectiveness of learning approaches compared to random search.
3. The paper makes a compelling case for the potential impact on symbolic software libraries, as evidenced by the specific examples of matrix identities with practical computational benefits.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited scope:** The paper restricts its study to homogeneous polynomials of degree k with a single matrix/vector input and scalar output. The authors provide no justification for why this restricted scope is sufficient to address the broader problem of finding efficient mathematical identities. The paper fails to demonstrate how the approach could be extended to more complex expressions, such as those involving multiple variables, trigonometric functions, or non-polynomial operations. Without evidence of extensibility, the claimed potential impact on symbolic software libraries remains speculative.

2. **Complexity mismatch:** The problem statement seeks lower computational complexity, but the algorithm does not enforce this constraint. The authors should explicitly consider ways to limit the search to low-complexity solutions rather than simply searching for any equivalent expression.

3. **Section 2 restrictions:** The restrictions in Section 2 (homogeneous polynomial, single matrix/vector, scalar output) are not adequately justified. The paper does not clarify whether the scheduler is the only limiting factor or if these restrictions are necessary for the approach to work. More formal justification or empirical studies for these restrictions are needed.

4. **Organization and clarity:** The paper structure and stages are hard to follow. The transition from problem statement to grammar definition to representation learning lacks clear explanation, making it difficult for readers to understand how these components work together.

5. **Technical concern:** The RBM-1 expression in the supplement appears invalid for k=1, n=1 (as the sum over binary vectors would be empty), suggesting potential issues with the formalization that need to be addressed.

6. **Minor issues:** The paper contains several typos (332 "show for," 148 "has contains," 289 "is is," 338 "by be") that affect readability.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

*For acceptance:*
- Novel framework combining attribute grammars with machine learning for symbolic computation
- Demonstrates successful discovery of previously unknown mathematical identities with practical computational benefits
- Well-structured experiments showing clear advantages of learning approaches over random search
- Potential to impact symbolic computation software libraries

*Against acceptance:*
- Highly restricted scope with no evidence of extensibility to more complex expressions
- Problem statement claims to address computational complexity but algorithm doesn't enforce this constraint
- Key restrictions in Section 2 lack proper justification
- Paper structure is confusing and poorly organized
- Technical issues in the formalization (RBM-1 expression)

**Overall assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting application of machine learning to symbolic computation with promising results on a limited set of problems. The core idea of using learning to guide search in combinatorial spaces is compelling, and the specific examples of discovered identities are valuable. However, the paper's limited scope and lack of justification for restricting the problem to homogeneous polynomials with single matrix inputs significantly undermine its claimed significance. The authors fail to address how their approach could be extended to more practical mathematical domains, which is a critical limitation for a paper claiming potential impact on symbolic software libraries.

While the technical approach is sound within the restricted scope, the paper would benefit from more thorough analysis of the limitations and clear pathways to addressing them. Without addressing the scope limitations and providing evidence of extensibility, the paper's contribution is too narrow to justify acceptance at NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Weak reject. The paper presents an interesting idea with limited scope, but the lack of justification for the restrictions and failure to demonstrate extensibility to more complex problems prevent it from making a significant contribution to the field.
