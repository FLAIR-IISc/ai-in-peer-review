This paper proposes a learning-guided framework to discover computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given symbolic target expression. Expressions are represented via an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic operators; the search space consists of grammar trees constrained to match the target’s degree and output type. Two representations are used to test equivalence: (i) a numerical descriptor based on evaluations modulo a large prime, which enables fast identity checks and linear combinations of trees via modular Gaussian elimination; and (ii) a learned continuous representation from a recursive neural network (RNN) trained to classify expressions into equivalence classes. To make search tractable, the authors learn strategies from simpler instances and use them to guide exploration for harder targets. Two strategies are evaluated: an n-gram model counting frequently successful subtrees, and an RNN-based policy predicting the next rule given subtree embeddings. Experiments on several families (including sums over AAT powers, elementwise variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-related exponential sums) show that learned strategies recover identities far beyond random search. Notably, n-grams often outperform the RNN, while the hardest family (RBM-2) remains challenging.

Relation to prior work: The paper sits between automated theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic algebra, but focuses specifically on reducing computational complexity rather than general simplification. Unlike rule-based CAS (e.g., Maple/Mathematica) or hand-designed heuristics in theorem proving, the proposed approach learns heuristics from data. The use of attribute grammars for search is established in other domains; applying learning-guided search and RNNs to symbolic identities is original. The learned embeddings echo word/sentence embeddings in NLP and recent RNNs over trees.

Quality: The framework is technically sound overall. The combination of (i) a constrained grammar; (ii) integer descriptors to robustly test equivalence and assemble linear combinations; and (iii) data-driven search is well motivated. The empirical study is substantial across five families and varying degrees, with clear evidence that learning improves search over random baselines, and that simple n-grams can be surprisingly strong. The RNN achieves low classification error on expression equivalence (<5%), which is notable given the number of classes.

However, several issues limit the strength of the claims:
- The main evaluation metric is the fraction of runs finding a match within a 10-minute time budget; there is no end-to-end demonstration of actual computational savings on realistic inputs (e.g., wall-clock speedups on numerical kernels), even though asymptotic gains are discussed.
- The link between the learned embedding’s classification accuracy and search performance is not quantified; ablations that correlate representation quality and downstream success would strengthen the case for the RNN.
- The correctness check relies on randomized evaluation modulo a prime. While standard in polynomial identity testing, a clear discussion of failure probability and parameter choices (N, p) would improve rigor. For identities claimed as “previously undiscovered,” a constructive symbolic proof or independent verification would be desirable.
- Baselines are limited. Beyond random, comparisons to simple deterministic heuristics (greedy/beam/A*-like search), genetic programming with the same grammar, or CAS simplification with a complexity-aware objective would contextualize gains.
- The grammar assumptions (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, often single variable) are restrictive; the paper acknowledges this but the empirical section does not show breadth beyond the chosen families.

Clarity: The paper is generally readable but sometimes hard to follow. Section 7.2, where the families, constraints (e.g., removing matrix-matrix multiply), and success metrics are introduced, would benefit from tighter organization and a clearer statement of the experimental protocol. More explicit pseudocode for the scheduler/strategy loop, and a concrete worked example of building a linear combination (including coefficient interpretation) would help. The RNN training description is detailed but could be streamlined; moving some training heuristics to an appendix would free space for clearer high-level intuition and examples of discovered identities in the main text.

Originality: High. Learning to guide search for algebraic identities within an attribute-grammar space, including an RNN that embeds symbolic expressions for prediction, is novel within the NIPS literature at the time. The numerical-equivalence-plus-linear-combination mechanism is also a clever engineering choice.

Significance: Mixed to low. While intellectually appealing and potentially impactful in the long term, the current experimental scope feels contrived, and the practical relevance remains unproven. The strongest empirical gains are on families where repetitive local patterns favor n-grams; on more challenging instances (RBM-2), no approach scales beyond small k. Without demonstrations in realistic pipelines (e.g., compiler passes, tensor algebra optimization, or ML model inference), it is hard to judge whether others will adopt the techniques.

Pros:
- Clear, original framing of identity discovery as learning-guided grammar search.
- Solid engineering: integer descriptors, linear combination solver, curriculum learning.
- Empirical evidence that learned strategies beat random search; simple n-grams are strong.
- Public code/data and numerous discovered identities.

Cons:
- Practical impact unclear; no end-to-end speedups or integration into real systems.
- Limited baselines; lack of comparisons to heuristic search/CAS tools.
- The RNN underperforms n-grams on two of three tasks; benefits are not well diagnosed.
- Restrictions on grammar and targets reduce generality.
- Clarity issues in experimental section; some details (correctness guarantees) underspecified.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Report wall-clock speedups for representative numerical sizes; include microbenchmarks comparing the target vs discovered identities.
- Add stronger baselines (beam/A*/MCTS, evolutionary search) and CAS comparisons with a complexity objective.
- Provide statistical guarantees for polynomial identity testing (parameters N,p, failure probability), and symbolic proofs for a subset of “new” identities.
- Analyze when and why the RNN helps; include ablations correlating embedding accuracy with search success, and consider reinforcement learning for rule selection.
- Broaden the grammar or demonstrate multi-variable, non-homogeneous cases; include a case study integrating the method into a compiler/auto-diff/tensor graph optimizer.

Recommendation: Technically interesting and original, but with limited demonstrated significance and clarity issues. I lean toward borderline reject in its current form; with stronger empirical validation of practical benefits and clearer exposition, it could become a compelling contribution.
