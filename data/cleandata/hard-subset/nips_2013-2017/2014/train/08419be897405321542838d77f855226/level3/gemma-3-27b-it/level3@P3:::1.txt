This paper explores the application of machine learning techniques to discover efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is novel: representing symbolic expressions as attribute grammars and then using learned models – n-grams and recursive neural networks (RNNs) – to guide the search for more computationally efficient equivalent expressions. The authors demonstrate this approach on a limited set of polynomial expressions, aiming to reduce computational complexity.

The work builds upon established concepts from several areas. Attribute grammars, originally developed by Knuth, are repurposed for optimization rather than compiler construction. The use of n-grams draws from natural language processing, applying sequence modeling to mathematical operations. The recursive neural network leverages recent advances in learning continuous representations of structured data, similar to work in NLP on parsing trees. Related work in automated theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation is acknowledged, but the authors position their work as distinct due to its focus on learning search strategies and reducing computational complexity directly, rather than formal proof or general-purpose simplification.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of applying machine learning to systematically search for efficient mathematical identities is genuinely novel. Combining attribute grammars with learning-based search strategies is an interesting direction.
* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The problem of finding computationally cheaper equivalent expressions is well-defined and potentially valuable.
* **Integration of Techniques:** The paper demonstrates a reasonable integration of attribute grammars, n-gram models, and RNNs.
* **Initial Demonstration:** The experiments, while limited, do showcase the potential of the approach to discover some identities.
* **Code Release:** The availability of code and evaluation data is a positive aspect, promoting reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Preliminary Nature:** The work feels significantly preliminary. The results, while demonstrating *some* success, are not particularly impressive given the complexity of the approach.
* **Limited Scope:** The experiments are restricted to a very small set of polynomial expressions. This severely limits the generalizability of the findings. The choice of expressions seems driven by what’s tractable rather than representing a broad exploration of possible identities.
* **Scalability Concerns:** It’s unclear whether the method would scale to more complex expressions or different mathematical domains. The exponential growth of the search space remains a significant hurdle, and it’s not clear if the learning component effectively mitigates this.
* **“Sledgehammer” Argument:** Given the limited domain, it's plausible that hand-derived mathematical rules or more targeted symbolic manipulation techniques (“sledgehammer to crack a nut”) could achieve similar results with less computational overhead. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the learning approach provides a substantial advantage over simpler methods *within* this restricted domain.
* **RNN Performance:** The RNN, despite its novelty, doesn’t consistently outperform the simpler n-gram models.  The improvements observed for the RBM-2 family are minimal.
* **Lack of Comparison:** The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to existing symbolic computation engines (e.g., Mathematica, Maple) or other optimization techniques.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, presenting a clear description of the framework and experimental setup. However, the analysis feels incomplete. The claims of discovering "many new mathematical identities" are overstated given the limited scope. The experimental evaluation lacks the rigor needed to support strong conclusions.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly outlines the problem and contributions. The use of examples and figures is helpful. However, some sections (e.g., the detailed description of the RNN training) could be more concise.

**Originality:** The combination of techniques is original, but the individual components are well-established. The novelty lies in the application to the specific problem of identity discovery.

**Significance:** The potential significance of this work is limited by the current results. While the idea is interesting, the practical impact is uncertain. It’s unclear if the approach will be useful for solving real-world problems beyond the contrived examples presented.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel approach to a challenging problem.
* Potential to automate the discovery of mathematical identities.
* Integration of multiple machine learning techniques.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Preliminary results with limited scope and scalability.
* Lack of convincing demonstration of superiority over simpler methods.
* Limited practical significance.
* The paper feels more like a proof-of-concept than a fully developed research contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

I find the paper interesting and the idea promising. However, the work is currently below the threshold for acceptance to NIPS. The results are not substantial enough to justify the complexity of the approach, and the limited scope raises concerns about generalizability. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to further develop this line of research by exploring larger and more diverse problem domains, comparing against existing techniques, and addressing the scalability concerns. A stronger emphasis on the practical implications and potential applications would also be beneficial.
