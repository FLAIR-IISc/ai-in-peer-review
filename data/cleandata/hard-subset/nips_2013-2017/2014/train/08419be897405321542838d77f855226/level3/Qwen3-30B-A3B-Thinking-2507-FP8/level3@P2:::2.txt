

# Review for "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors introduce an attribute grammar-based approach to represent symbolic expressions, where the goal is to find equivalent expressions with lower computational complexity. To navigate the exponentially large search space, they propose two learning-based strategies: n-gram models that capture common sub-expression patterns from simpler solutions, and a recursive neural network that learns a continuous representation of symbolic expressions to predict the next grammar rule to apply.

The work draws connections to related areas including theorem proving [5,9,11], program induction [18,28], and probabilistic programming [12,20], while noting the key distinction that their restricted domain (polynomials with fixed degree k) avoids undecidability issues present in broader symbolic computation. The paper also makes interesting connections to NLP techniques, borrowing n-gram models and recursive neural networks for symbolic computation, with parallels to work on word embeddings [8,16] and recursive neural networks for parsing [22,23].

**Strengths:**

*Quality*: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors provide a clear framework, including both theoretical (attribute grammar) and practical (numerical descriptor, learned representations) components. The experimental design is appropriate for the problem, with careful comparison of the random, n-gram, and RNN approaches across multiple families of expressions. The RNN representation learning achieves good classification accuracy (≤5% error rate) as shown in Table 2, demonstrating the feasibility of their continuous representation approach.

*Originality*: The paper makes a significant contribution by applying machine learning techniques to the problem of discovering mathematical identities, a domain previously dominated by rule-based symbolic computation systems like Maple and Mathematica. The attribute grammar framework combined with learning-based search strategies represents a novel approach that differs substantially from previous work in theorem proving and program induction. The discovery of new mathematical identities (e.g., the pattern in Example 2) is particularly impressive.

**Weaknesses:**

*Clarity*: While the paper is technically strong, the presentation suffers from significant clarity issues. The description of the grammar rules, search strategy, and linear combinations of trees (Section 5) is particularly confusing. Section 7.2, which describes the experimental setup and results, is especially difficult to follow, making it challenging for readers to understand the scope of the problem and the significance of the results. The paper would benefit from clearer explanations, more intuitive examples, and better organization.

*Significance*: The most significant concern is the paper's limited practical significance. The problem addressed—finding efficient versions of specific polynomial expressions—is highly contrived and appears unlikely to be adopted in practice. The discovered identities (e.g., for (ΣAAT)^k) do not correspond to standard problems in machine learning or mathematics that would benefit from such optimizations. The authors suggest potential applications to dropout and RBM partition functions, but these connections are tenuous and not well-supported by the results. The computational complexity reductions described (e.g., from O(kn^3) to O(kn^2)) are meaningful only within the narrow scope of their restricted problem, and the practical impact on real-world applications is unclear.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The framework represents a novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation.
- The technical approach is sound and well-executed, with good experimental validation.
- The discovered identities are genuinely new (to the best of the authors' knowledge) and demonstrate the power of their approach.
- The learned representation for symbolic expressions has potential broader applications in machine learning.

*Con:*
- The problem domain is highly specialized and lacks clear practical significance.
- The paper is difficult to follow, particularly in the experimental section.
- The claimed practical benefits (e.g., for dropout or RBM partition functions) are speculative and not demonstrated.
- The n-gram approach outperforms the more complex RNN in most cases, raising questions about the necessity of the neural network component.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically strong paper that makes an interesting contribution to the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The framework and learning approaches are novel and well-executed. However, the limited practical significance of the problem domain and the unclear connection to real-world applications significantly weaken the paper's impact. While the technical contribution is valuable, the narrow scope and lack of clear practical motivation make this work less compelling for a general machine learning audience.

I recommend rejection, as the paper's significance is too low to warrant acceptance at NIPS, despite its technical quality and originality. The problem, while mathematically interesting, does not address a meaningful challenge in machine learning or computational mathematics that would benefit from the proposed approach. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the motivation and demonstrate more concrete practical applications to justify acceptance.
