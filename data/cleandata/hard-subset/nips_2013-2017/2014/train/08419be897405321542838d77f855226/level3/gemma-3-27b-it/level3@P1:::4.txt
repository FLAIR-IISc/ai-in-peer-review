This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities by leveraging machine learning techniques within an attribute grammar. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions as trees built from grammar rules and then employ learned strategies (n-gram models and a recursive neural network) to guide the search for expressions equivalent to a target but with lower computational complexity. This is a potentially valuable contribution, particularly given the limited application of machine learning to symbolic reasoning.

The work builds on existing techniques in symbolic computation (e.g., [6, 27]), theorem proving ([5, 9, 11]) and program induction ([18, 28]), but distinguishes itself by explicitly integrating learning to overcome the inherent combinatorial explosion in the search space. It also draws inspiration from natural language processing ([8, 16, 22, 23]) in its use of n-gram models and recursive neural networks for representing and manipulating symbolic structures. The use of attribute grammars ([14]) provides a formal foundation for representing symbolic expressions, building on prior work in compiler construction and various algorithmic domains ([19, 26, 10]). The authors correctly position their work as distinct from existing symbolic computation engines like Maple and Mathematica, which rely on rule-based systems rather than learning.

The strengths of the paper lie in its novelty and potential impact. The demonstration of automatically discovering identities that are difficult or impossible to derive manually, or through exhaustive search, is compelling.  The experiments show promising results, particularly for expressions where the learned strategies outperform random search. The provision of code and evaluation data ([29]) promotes reproducibility and further research. The application of RNNs to learning continuous representations of symbolic expressions is a particularly interesting and potentially broadly applicable contribution.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The scope of the problem is significantly restricted, limiting the generalizability of the approach. The focus on homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix variable, while simplifying the search space, raises concerns about whether the techniques can be extended to more complex and realistic scenarios. A stronger justification for these restrictions (Section 2) is needed, potentially accompanied by empirical studies to demonstrate the limitations of the approach when these restrictions are relaxed.

A significant concern is that while the problem statement aims for lower computational complexity, the algorithm itself doesn't inherently enforce this. The linear combination of trees (Section 5) can find equivalent expressions, but doesn’t guarantee they are *more efficient*. The authors acknowledge limiting the grammar in some cases, but a more robust mechanism for prioritizing lower-complexity solutions during the search is crucial.

The paper’s organization could be improved. The flow of ideas is sometimes unclear, making it difficult to follow the different stages of the framework. A clearer, more step-by-step explanation of the overall process – from grammar definition to identity discovery – would be beneficial. Furthermore, the technical details regarding the experimental setup and the size of vectors/matrices involved are sometimes lacking. For example, the RBM-1 expression in the supplementary material appears invalid for k=1 and n=1, raising concerns about the thoroughness of the evaluation.

Finally, there are minor typos throughout the paper (e.g., 332, 148, 289, 338) that detract from its polish.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of machine learning to symbolic identity discovery.
*   Demonstration of finding complex identities beyond human derivation or exhaustive search.
*   Interesting use of RNNs to learn continuous representations of symbolic expressions.
*   Availability of code and data for reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope and restrictions on the problem domain.
*   Lack of guarantees for finding lower-complexity solutions.
*   Organization and clarity could be improved.
*   Minor technical concerns and typos.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising and novel approach to a challenging problem. While the current scope is limited, the initial results are encouraging, and the ideas presented have the potential to contribute significantly to the field.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should: 1) Strengthen the justification for the problem restrictions and explore their impact on generalizability; 2) Incorporate a mechanism for explicitly prioritizing lower-complexity solutions during the search process; and 3) Improve the clarity of the paper’s organization and address the minor technical issues and typos.
