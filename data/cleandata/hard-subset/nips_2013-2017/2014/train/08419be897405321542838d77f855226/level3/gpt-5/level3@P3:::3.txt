Summary
This paper proposes to discover computationally efficient algebraic identities for a given target expression by searching over trees defined by an attribute grammar. Because exhaustive enumeration is infeasible, the authors learn search policies from solutions to simpler targets. Two strategies are studied: (i) an n-gram model over subtrees, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions and is then used to score next-rule choices during search. Expressions are represented either numerically (by evaluating on many random inputs modulo a large prime) to test equivalence, or by learned continuous embeddings for classification and guiding search. The approach finds identities that reduce naive O(n3) computations to O(n2) in some matrix-expression families and discovers low-degree polynomial-time approximations for expressions whose naive evaluation is exponential.

Relation to prior work
The problem connects to theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, but the paper focuses on a tractable subdomain (homogeneous polynomials in one variable with scalar outputs). The grammar-based search ties to prior attribute-grammar optimization. The learned representation builds on recursive networks from NLP (e.g., Socher et al.) and logical reasoning (Bowman), but applied here to symbolic math trees. Symbolic computation systems (Maple/Mathematica) are contrasted as rule-based rather than learned.

Strengths
- Interesting and timely problem: learning to guide discrete, combinatorial search for symbolic identities.
- Clear high-level framework: attribute grammar + scheduler + learned strategy.
- The n-gram baseline is simple, effective, and often outperforms random search and the RNN.
- The learned RNN representation achieves low error on expression-classification tasks, suggesting nontrivial structure is captured.
- Code and data availability improves reproducibility.

Weaknesses and key points needing clarification
- Scope seems overly restricted. The core experiments focus on homogeneous polynomials in a single variable with scalar outputs. While this enables tractable experimentation, the paper should better justify the restriction and present concrete paths (and challenges) toward multiple variables, nonhomogeneity, non-polynomial ops, and non-scalar outputs.
- RNN architecture unclear. Section 4.2 describes a weight tensor W3 “of size l×l×l” for binary ops, but it is not explicit whether this is a neural tensor network (NTN/RNTN) in the sense of Socher/Bowman (bilinear tensor terms plus linear terms), or a simpler multiplicative tensor layer without bilinear cross-terms or affine skips. Please precisely define the composition function for unary and binary rules, including parameterization, sharing (per rule type vs. global), and whether linear terms and biases are included. As written, “W3*a)*a” is ambiguous.
- Model choice not justified relative to tensor networks. Prior work (e.g., Bowman 2013) reports that recursive neural tensor networks outperform plain recursive networks on logical tasks. Given the symbolic/logical nature of the present domain, the paper should either adopt an NTN-style composition or justify why a simpler RNN suffices, and ideally report a comparison.
- Section 7.2 lacks clarity on how the learned RNN is used during discovery versus classification. The paper first trains the RNN as a classifier over equivalence classes, and later trains a softmax head to predict the next rule. Please make explicit which training objective(s) are used for the model that guides search, whether the classifier is discarded, and whether weights are fine-tuned jointly for the search task.
- Proposal generation and search procedure underspecified. The scheduler is described as “pick highest-scoring valid rule,” but details are missing:
  - Do you use greedy selection only, or beam search/top-k sampling? Any backtracking?
  - How are ties broken, and how do you prevent cycling/duplicates? Is there canonicalization or memoization of partial trees?
  - How are shape/type constraints enforced during rule application?
  - How are proposals generated when two branches are needed but only one exists? (b2 is set to a random vector; justify this design and its effect.)
  - How are multiple trees selected for the linear-combination solver (M and N selection, pruning criteria)?
- Evaluation clarity. In Fig. 3, different search strategies are compared, but more detail is needed:
  - Is the RNN used solely as a next-rule policy, or also to prioritize which completed trees enter the linear solver?
  - Provide the wall-clock budget per value of k and the number of tree expansions attempted; report variance across runs.
  - Report collision/certification rate for the Zp descriptor (probability of false matches), and how N and p were chosen.
- Results interpretation. n-grams outperform the RNN on several families; discuss why (e.g., local repetitive patterns) and whether the RNN could be improved with NTN composition, curriculum variants, or better regularization.
- Errors and presentation issues (examples)
  - Figure 3 labeling: “TNN” appears to label the neural baseline; it should be “RNN” (or “NTN” if that was intended).
  - Repeated sentence: the opening “In this paper we explore how machine learning techniques can be applied to the discovery of efficient mathematical identities.” appears twice verbatim (Abstract and Introduction); consider varying the phrasing or consolidating.
  - Typos/grammar:
    - “help which the discovery of more complex ones” → “help with the discovery…”
    - Extra spaces before punctuation, e.g., “N , must…”
    - “O(()n3)” stray parentheses in Sec. 3.
    - “i.e we backpropagate” → “i.e., we backpropagate”
    - In Example 2, inconsistent spacing around operators (e.g., “*A) * B” vs “* A) * B”) and mismatched parentheses hinder readability.
    - “This prevents both rounding issues as well as numerical overflow.” Prefer “prevents both rounding issues and numerical overflow.”
  - Notation consistency: floors bk/2c vs. text; unify.

Quality
- Technically promising but underspecified in critical parts (composition function, proposal generation). The equivalence-checking via Zp is reasonable but needs a discussion of collision risk. Empirical results support the claim that learning speeds search, but ablations and stronger baselines (e.g., NTN, beam search) would strengthen claims.

Clarity
- Generally readable, but important ambiguities remain (RNN vs. NTN, search details). Several typos and figure/labeling inconsistencies should be fixed.

Originality
- Applying learned representations and policies to discover efficient algebraic identities via attribute grammars is novel and interesting. The use of an expression-classifying RNN and transfer to search is a creative combination of known components.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for compiler algebraic optimization and symbolic ML, but present scope is narrow. Demonstrations on broader grammars and stronger baselines would increase significance.

Questions for the authors
1. Precisely define the binary composition function and whether it is an NTN. Did you try NTN/RNTN or bilinear terms as in Socher/Bowman?
2. What search control beyond greedy did you try (beam size, stochastic sampling)? How sensitive are results?
3. How often do Zp descriptor collisions occur in practice? What p and N were used per family?
4. Are parameters shared per operator type across tree positions? Any regularization other than dropout?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and appealing problem formulation.
- Clear evidence that learning can accelerate discovery beyond random search.
- Public code/data.

Cons for acceptance
- Methodological ambiguities and missing comparisons (NTN vs. RNN).
- Narrow problem setting; unclear generalization.
- Several clarity and presentation errors (including a mislabeled figure).

Recommendation
Weak reject. With clarifications, an NTN baseline or justification, more precise search details (and possibly a beam-search ablation), and polished writing/figures, this work could be strong.
