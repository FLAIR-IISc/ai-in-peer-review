- Proposes learning-guided search to find mathematically equivalent but computationally cheaper identities using an attribute grammar and tree search.  
- Represents expressions numerically via Zp descriptors or with learned recursive neural network (RNN) embeddings.  
- Uses learned schedulers/strategies (n-gram or RNN) to guide tree search and a linear solver to combine trees for more complex identities.  
- Numerical descriptors enable fast equivalence testing; linear combinations of trees allow representing more complex identities.  
- Positions work between statistical ML and symbolic reasoning, relating to theorem proving, program induction, and NLP-style learned representations, and differing from CAS by targeting low-complexity equivalents and learned heuristics.  
- Well-motivated framework with empirical demonstrations of nontrivial identities that random search misses.  
- Major concern: complexity objective mismatch — emphasis on lower asymptotic complexity is not consistently enforced or systematically optimized.  
- Reliance on Zp descriptors is practical but lacks formal bounds on collision probability and required sample size N relative to the linear system.  
- Constraints on vector/matrix dimensions are under-specified; supplement’s RBM-1 construction may be invalid for k=1, n=1.  
- Limited evidence for extensibility beyond the narrow tested family (homogeneous polynomials in one variable with scalar outputs).  
- Clarity issues: the overall pipeline is hard to follow and sections need reorganization and clearer separation of fixed vs. learned schedulers.  
- Section 2’s restrictions require formal justification or ablation studies to show whether they are fundamental or scheduler artifacts.  
- Needs clarification of admissible vector/matrix sizes and rule preconditions.  
- Originality: introducing learned continuous representations to guide symbolic identity discovery is novel and timely.  
- Empirical note: n-gram baseline outperforming RNN on several families is an interesting finding.  
- Significance: approach could impact symbolic libraries, compilers, and ML systems if made robust and generalized, but current scope limits impact.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel application of learned representations, practical wins on challenging identities, open-source release, and clear ML connections.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited scope and extensibility, complexity objective not rigorously enforced, missing formal guarantees/dimensional constraints, and organization/clarity problems.
