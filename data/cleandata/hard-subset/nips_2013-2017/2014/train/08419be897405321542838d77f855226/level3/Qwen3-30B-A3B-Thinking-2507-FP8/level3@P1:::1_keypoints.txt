- Proposes using attribute grammars plus learned n-gram and RNN models to search grammar trees for computationally cheaper symbolic expressions.
- Frames identity discovery as a search problem and uses learning from simpler expressions to guide search for more complex identities.
- Clever technical contribution: integer-based numerical descriptors to test symbolic equivalence without heavy symbolic computation.
- RNN-based continuous representations for symbolic expressions are promising; classification accuracy reported >95% (Table 2).
- Originality: thoughtful application of NLP techniques (n-grams, RNNs) to a novel symbolic computation domain.
- Experimental domain is narrowly scoped to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with one or few variables.
- Discovered identities appear to be simple repetitive patterns that could likely be derived by hand, undermining novelty claims.
- No demonstrated practical computational advantage; results are trivial or only applicable to constrained mathematical problems.
- The most challenging test family (RBM-2) shows no meaningful improvement over random search.
- Grammar rules used are extremely limited and the paper does not explain extension to more general mathematical operations.
- Claims that many examples are “too complex to be found manually” are unsubstantiated given simple patterns in examples.
- Authors claim applicability to compilers/optimizers but provide no evidence or integration experiments.
- Paper is generally well-written and experimental setup is described clearly, despite limited scope.
- Contribution to advancing machine learning is minimal due to the restricted problem domain and replaceability by domain-specific heuristics.
- Recommended improvements: expand scope to more complex expressions, demonstrate clear computational advantages, and provide evidence of broader applicability.
- Overall recommendation: interesting exploratory work but too preliminary and narrow for NIPS; better suited to a specialized symbolic computation venue.
