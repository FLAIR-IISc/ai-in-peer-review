This paper introduces a framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to represent expressions as trees derived from an attribute grammar of mathematical operations. Since a brute-force search over all possible trees is intractable for non-trivial expressions, the authors propose learning a search strategy. The training data for this strategy is derived from solutions to simpler expressions, allowing the system to generalize to more complex targets.

The paper explores two main learning approaches to guide the search. The first is an n-gram model, which learns common sequences of operations (subtrees) from previously discovered efficient identities. The second, more sophisticated approach, is a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation for symbolic expression trees. This learned representation is then used to predict the next grammar rule to apply in order to form an expression equivalent to the target. The authors test their framework on five families of polynomial expressions, showing that the learning-based strategies, particularly the n-gram models, can discover complex identities that are out of reach for a random search strategy. This work relates to areas like automated theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation, but distinguishes itself by its novel application of statistical machine learning techniques to guide the search for identities based on computational complexity rather than just analytical equivalence.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed framework, from the attribute grammar to the numerical and learned representations of expressions, is well-conceived and presented as a complete system. The experiments are systematic, comparing the proposed learning methods against a random search baseline across several problem families of increasing difficulty. The authors are also commendably honest about the limitations of their approach, for instance, by showing the failure of all methods on the most complex (RBM-2) family for k > 5 and noting that the simpler n-gram models often outperform the more complex RNN.

**Clarity:** The paper's clarity could be improved. While the high-level idea is understandable, the details of the experimental setup in Section 7.2 are difficult to follow. The descriptions of the five families of expressions are very dense and assume familiarity with the authors' notation (e.g., `(∑ AAT)k`). The manuscript would benefit significantly from more explicit mathematical definitions for each family and perhaps a clearer, step-by-step example for one of the more complex cases. Similarly, the mechanics of how the RNN is trained to predict the next rule (Section 6.3) could be explained in more detail.

**Originality:** The originality of this work is very high. The primary contribution is the novel application of machine learning, particularly techniques borrowed from natural language processing (n-grams, recursive neural networks), to the symbolic domain of discovering mathematical identities. The concept of learning a search policy over a grammar from solutions to simpler problems is an elegant and powerful idea. Furthermore, the development of a continuous, learned representation for symbolic expressions is a creative contribution that could have applications beyond the specific problem addressed in this paper.

**Significance:** The significance of the results is the paper's weakest point. While the technical approach is novel, the problem itself feels somewhat contrived. The families of expressions chosen for the experiments appear to be crafted to demonstrate the capabilities of the system rather than representing common computational bottlenecks that practitioners face. The paper suggests potential applications, such as finding a closed-form version of dropout or efficiently computing the RBM partition function, but these connections are speculative and not demonstrated. It is unclear whether the discovered identities are of broad interest or if the proposed framework would be practical for integration into compilers or symbolic math packages, where highly optimized, rule-based systems already exist. The overhead of setting up the grammar, generating data from simpler problems, and training the models may not justify the potential gains for many real-world problems.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **High Originality:** The paper proposes a novel and interesting problem: using ML to discover efficient mathematical identities. The application of NLP techniques like n-grams and RNNs to this symbolic domain is creative and thought-provoking.
*   **Technically Sound:** The proposed framework is coherent and the experiments, while on specific problem families, are well-designed and support the central claims.
*   **New Direction:** The work opens up a potentially fruitful research direction at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The idea of learned representations for mathematical expressions is particularly promising.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Low Significance/Impact:** The practical utility of the work is questionable. The problems feel artificial, and it is not clear that the discovered identities or the method itself would be widely used by researchers or practitioners.
*   **Limited Clarity:** The paper is difficult to read in key sections (especially the experimental setup), which hinders a full appreciation of the results and would make reproduction challenging.
*   **Mixed Experimental Results:** The simpler n-gram models outperform the more sophisticated RNN on several tasks, which somewhat weakens the contribution of the RNN-based approach. All methods fail on the most challenging task, suggesting scalability issues.

In summary, this paper presents a highly original and technically sound piece of work on a novel problem. However, its limited practical significance and issues with clarity are significant drawbacks.
