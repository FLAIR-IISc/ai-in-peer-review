

# Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors introduce an attribute grammar-based approach to represent symbolic expressions, with the goal of finding computationally equivalent expressions of lower complexity. To address the exponential growth of the search space, they employ two learning-based strategies: n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs), trained on solutions to simpler expressions. The paper demonstrates the framework's ability to discover identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n³) to O(n²) for certain matrix operations, with examples provided for specific cases.

The work relates to previous NIPS research in several ways. The attribute grammar framework builds on Knuth's 1968 work for compiler construction, while the use of RNNs for symbolic representations draws from NLP research (e.g., Socher et al. 2011, 2013). The paper also connects to recent NIPS work on continuous representations for symbolic structures (Mnih & Hinton, 2010; Mikolov et al., 2013), though it extends these ideas to a novel application domain. The authors correctly note that this is one of the few attempts to apply statistical learning to symbolic computation, contrasting with rule-based systems like Maple or Mathematica.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with reasonable experimental validation. The grammar framework is well-defined, and the use of numerical and learned representations for expression comparison is appropriate for the problem. The experimental results (Table 2, Figure 3) demonstrate that learning-based search strategies significantly outperform random search for complex expressions. However, the problem definition is overly restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree, which limits the applicability of the framework. The paper does not sufficiently address how these restrictions might be relaxed for more general mathematical expressions. Additionally, the explanation of the weight tensor W3 (size l×l×l) is unclear—this appears to be a neural tensor network rather than a standard RNN, yet the paper does not justify this choice or compare it to simpler linear models. The authors should cite Bowman (2013), which shows tensor networks often outperform RNNs for logical reasoning tasks, to better justify their model choice.

**Clarity:** The paper suffers from several clarity issues that significantly hinder understanding. The explanation of the recursive neural network's operation (Section 4.2) is confusing, particularly regarding how the weight tensor works. The proposal generation process in Section 6 is not clearly explained, making it difficult to replicate the search strategy. Figure 3 incorrectly labels the RNN as "TNN," and there are multiple instances of duplicate sentences and grammatical errors throughout the manuscript. Section 7.2 is unclear about how the RNN is used for classification versus search strategy, and the evaluation of different search strategies lacks sufficient detail.

**Originality:** The core idea of using learning to guide search in symbolic spaces is novel and well-motivated. The application of attribute grammars to mathematical identity discovery represents a significant contribution to the field. The paper also introduces a novel use of RNNs for continuous representation of symbolic expressions, which could have broader applications in symbolic computation. However, the authors could better distinguish their work from related approaches in theorem proving and program induction, particularly by clarifying how their restricted problem setting differs from previous work.

**Significance:** The discovered identities could potentially be useful in machine learning contexts, such as for approximating dropout or computing RBM partition functions more efficiently. The framework's potential integration with compilers or optimization tools represents a promising direction. However, the restricted problem domain limits the broader significance of the work. The paper does not sufficiently demonstrate how these specific identities would be used in real-world applications beyond the examples provided.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel application of machine learning to symbolic mathematical identity discovery
- Demonstrates significant computational complexity improvements for specific cases
- Provides a well-defined framework with experimental validation
- Introduces continuous representations for symbolic expressions
- Potential for integration with optimization tools

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Problem definition is overly restricted, limiting broader applicability
- Several critical clarity issues (explanation of weight tensor, search procedure)
- Insufficient justification for using tensor networks over simpler RNNs
- Figure 3 mislabels the RNN as TNN
- Grammatical errors and duplicate sentences throughout
- Limited demonstration of real-world significance beyond the specific examples

In conclusion, this paper presents an interesting approach to a challenging problem with some promising results. However, the overly restricted problem definition, unclear explanations of key technical elements, and several clarity issues significantly weaken the contribution. The authors should address these concerns before the paper can be considered for acceptance. With substantial revisions to clarify the methodology, justify model choices, and better articulate the significance beyond the limited problem domain, this paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to NIPS.
