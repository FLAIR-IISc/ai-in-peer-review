- Proposes a framework to automatically find computationally efficient, analytically equivalent versions of symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar and search.  
- Introduces two learned search strategies (n-gram model and recursive neural network) trained via curriculum on simpler expressions.  
- Represents expression trees with an RNN to predict next grammar rules and guides search in an exponentially large space.  
- Demonstrates the approach on five families of polynomial expressions, discovering several new identities.  
- Learning-based strategies significantly outperform a random search baseline, especially as target complexity grows.  
- Technical quality is high: well-conceived framework, appropriate methods, and thorough experimental evaluation with supportive figures/tables.  
- Clarity could be improved: experimental setup and description of the five families are dense; needs step-by-step examples and a system diagram.  
- Highly original: novel problem formulation and creative application of NLP-inspired models to symbolic mathematics.  
- Learning continuous representations of mathematical expressions is an interesting contribution enabling ML tools in symbolic domains.  
- Main weakness: unclear practical significance and limited demonstrated generalization beyond constructed single-variable polynomial families.  
- Broader utility and adoption for real-world computational optimization or compilers is not established.  
- Authors are transparent about limitations (e.g., failure on RBM-2 for k>5) and system complexity.  
- Arguments for acceptance: originality, strong technical contribution, and potential to inspire future research.  
- Arguments against acceptance: perceived niche practical impact and clarity issues that hinder comprehension.  
- Overall assessment: high-quality, original, and thought-provoking work whose research value outweighs current doubts about practical impact.
