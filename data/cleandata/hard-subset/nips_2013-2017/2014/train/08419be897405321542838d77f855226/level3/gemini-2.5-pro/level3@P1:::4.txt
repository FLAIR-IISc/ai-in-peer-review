This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar and then search through the space of possible expression trees to find one that is analytically equivalent to a target expression but has a lower computational cost. Recognizing that a brute-force or random search is intractable for all but the simplest expressions due to combinatorial explosion, the authors' main contribution is to apply machine learning techniques to guide the search. Specifically, they propose an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN) that are trained on solutions to simpler expressions to learn search heuristics for more complex ones. The work draws interesting parallels with natural language processing, adapting n-gram language models and RNNs (used for sentiment analysis and parsing) to the symbolic mathematics domain. It stands in contrast to traditional symbolic computation engines like Maple or Mathematica, which are typically rule-based and do not employ statistical learning to discover new identities.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and ambitious goal: applying modern machine learning to the discovery of symbolic mathematical identities. The idea of training a model on simple problems to solve more complex ones in a symbolic domain is highly original. The introduction of a learned continuous representation for symbolic expressions using an RNN is particularly innovative and could have a significant impact, potentially opening up the field of symbolic computation to a wide range of ML techniques, much as word embeddings did for natural language processing.

2.  **Potential Impact:** The application is well-motivated and potentially very useful. The automatic discovery of efficient identities could be integrated into compilers or symbolic software libraries to optimize computations. The chosen experimental families, such as those related to the RBM partition function, are of direct interest to the machine learning community and demonstrate the practical relevance of the work.

3.  **Technical Approach:** The use of an attribute grammar provides a structured framework for the search problem. The learning-based search strategies, especially the RNN, are a sophisticated way to tackle the combinatorial search space, moving beyond hand-designed heuristics common in related fields like automated theorem proving.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Scope and Generalizability:** A major weakness is the highly restricted domain in which the framework is evaluated. The experiments are confined to homogeneous polynomials of a single variable that produce a scalar output. While the authors suggest the framework could be extended, there is no evidence or substantive discussion of how it would handle more general mathematical expressions involving, for example, non-polynomial functions (trigonometric, exponential), recursion, or more complex data structures. This limitation makes it difficult to assess the true power and generalizability of the proposed method.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is difficult to follow. The overall architecture, which involves several distinct stages (grammar definition, numerical/learned representation, solving linear combinations of trees, and the search strategy), is not presented clearly. The connections between these components are often obscure, leaving the reader to piece together the full pipeline. A high-level diagram or a more structured "Methods" section that walks through the entire process from a target expression to a discovered identity would greatly improve the paper's clarity.

3.  **Mismatch between Objective and Method:** The stated problem is to find an expression S with *lower* computational complexity than a target T. However, the search algorithm does not seem to directly optimize for or enforce this constraint. For two of the experimental families, the authors manually remove the high-complexity matrix-multiply rule from the grammar. This feels like an ad-hoc solution. A more principled approach would be to incorporate computational complexity directly into the search strategy's objective function, guiding the search towards inherently efficient solutions.

4.  **Insufficient Justification for Restrictions:** The restrictions described in Section 2 (homogeneous polynomials, single variable, scalar output) are not sufficiently justified. It is unclear whether these are fundamental limitations of the framework (e.g., required by the numerical representation or the RNN structure) or merely simplifying assumptions for the experiments. A more formal discussion of why these restrictions are in place and the challenges in relaxing them is needed.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

*   **On the Search for Low-Complexity Solutions:** The authors should consider modifying their search scheduler to explicitly favor trees with lower computational complexity, which is available as an attribute in the grammar. This would align the method more closely with the problem statement and make the manual removal of grammar rules unnecessary.
*   **On the RBM-1 Identity:** There appears to be a lack of clarity or a potential error regarding the constraints on vector/matrix sizes. The reviewer's key points note that the RBM-1 expression provided in the supplement seems invalid for the case of k=1, n=1. The authors should double-check their supplementary material and clarify any ambiguities regarding dimensionality in the main text.
*   **Clarity of the Overall System:** I strongly recommend the authors add a figure or detailed algorithm box that illustrates the complete workflow:
    1.  A target expression T is given.
    2.  The scheduler proposes a new partial tree.
    3.  The ML strategy (n-gram or RNN) scores the next possible grammar rules.
    4.  The scheduler picks the highest-scoring rule, extends the tree, and repeats until a full tree S_i is formed.
    5.  The representation of S_i is computed and added to a matrix F.
    6.  The system Fw = t is solved to check if a linear combination of discovered trees matches the target.
    This would make the paper much easier to understand.

**Minor Typos:**
The manuscript contains several minor typos that should be corrected, for example: "has contains" (Section 2), "is is" (Section 6), "show for" (Figure 3 caption), and "by be" (Section 8).

In summary, this paper presents a creative and highly original approach to a challenging problem. The core ideas are promising and could inspire significant follow-up work. However, the paper is hampered by a lack of clarity in its presentation and a narrow experimental scope that leaves the generalizability of the method in question. If the authors can significantly improve the clarity and better address the limitations of their framework, this could be a strong contribution.
