- Tackles the problem of automatically finding mathematically equivalent but computationally cheaper expressions.  
- Represents expressions with an attribute grammar and uses ML (n-grams and RNNs) to guide the search.  
- Novel application of learning techniques to a symbolic domain and to navigating exponential search spaces.  
- Related work is well-covered, situating the paper within theorem proving, program induction, and NLP.  
- Technically sound approach with a well-defined attribute-grammar framework.  
- Experimental setup is rigorous, though limited in scope.  
- RNN-based representation of symbolic expressions is a noteworthy contribution with high classification accuracy (Table 2).  
- Reports discovery of novel identities for the tested expression families, showing feasibility.  
- Clarity issues (notably Section 7.2) and unclear motivations for some design choices (e.g., removing matrix-multiply).  
- Practical significance is questionable because the problem setup is somewhat contrived and uses limited variable types.  
- RNN often performs comparably or worse than n-grams (Figure 3), so the advantage of the complex model is not fully justified.  
- Experiments are narrow, focusing on only a few families of expressions.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, technical soundness, and demonstration that learned search strategies can work.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited real-world impact, clarity problems, and inconsistent RNN gains.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions—address clarity, better justify significance, and expand experimental scope.
