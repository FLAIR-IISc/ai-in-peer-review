- Learns-guided search for cheaper symbolic identities via attribute grammars and search over expression trees.
- Two learned strategies: n-gram models over subexpression sequences and an RNN that learns continuous subtree embeddings to predict rules.
- Equivalence checking via integer descriptors modulo a large prime plus a linear-combination solver.
- Empirically, learned guidance improves search success; n-gram outperforms the RNN on two of three task families.
- Technical framework is sound and complete, with released code.
- Collision risk of the modulo-p integer descriptor is not quantified.
- Claimed efficiency gains are confounded by removing matrix-multiply from the grammar (Section 7.2).
- Baselines are limited—no beam search, A*, MCTS with heuristics, or superoptimization-style comparisons.
- Paper is often hard to follow; Section 7.2, the scheduler/strategy interface, and training protocol need clearer, systematic description and figure cross-references.
- Originality is high: novel casting of identity discovery as learning over attribute grammars and continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions.
- Significance is low-to-moderate: benchmarks feel contrived and no end-to-end wall-clock speedups on real workloads are shown.
- Pros: clear framing, elegant grammar-based search, innovative learning guidance, open-source release, and evidence that learning expands feasible search.
- Cons: practical impact unclear, constrained tasks/grammar, n-gram beats RNN on most tasks, limited baselines, clarity issues, and missing analyses (descriptor collisions, runtime gains).
- Recommendation: leaning reject; suggests adding stronger baselines, quantifying end-to-end speedups, analyzing descriptor reliability, and improving exposition.
