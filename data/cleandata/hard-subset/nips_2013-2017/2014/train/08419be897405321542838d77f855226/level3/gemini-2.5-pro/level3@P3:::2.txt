### Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is analytically equivalent to a given target but has lower computational complexity. Recognizing that the search space is combinatorially large, the authors propose using machine learning to guide the search, training models on solutions found for simpler expressions to help discover more complex ones.

Two learning-based search strategies are presented. The first is an n-gram model which learns common sequences of operations (subtrees) from previously discovered solutions. The second is a more sophisticated recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation for symbolic expression trees. This learned representation is then used to train a classifier that predicts the next grammar rule to apply in the search process. To verify the equivalence of expressions, the authors use a numerical "descriptor" approach, evaluating expressions on random inputs modulo a large prime. The methods are evaluated on five families of complex polynomial expressions, demonstrating that the learning-based strategies can discover efficient identities that are intractable for random search or manual derivation.

This work creatively bridges the domains of symbolic computation and statistical machine learning. While symbolic computation engines like Mathematica and automated theorem provers exist, they are typically rule-based. This paper's contribution is to frame the discovery of identities as a learning problem, applying techniques from natural language processing (n-grams, tree-based RNNs) to a mathematical domain in a novel way, reminiscent of how RNNs have been used for parsing and semantic representation in NLP (e.g., Socher et al., 2013).

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed framework is well-conceived, connecting a grammar-based representation with modern machine learning techniques for search. The experiments are thorough, testing the methods on several challenging families of expressions and demonstrating a clear advantage over a random search baseline. The inclusion of two distinct learning methods (n-gram and RNN) and a comparison between them adds depth to the evaluation. The claims are well-supported by the experimental results presented.

**Originality:** The originality of this paper is its greatest strength. The central idea of using machine learning to discover efficient mathematical identities is highly novel. While program induction and synthesis are established fields, the specific focus on finding computationally cheaper, equivalent expressions is fresh. Furthermore, the application of n-gram models and, particularly, recursive neural networks to learn representations of and guide search over symbolic math expressions is a creative and powerful combination of techniques from different fields. The work is clearly differentiated from existing symbolic manipulation systems and theorem provers.

**Clarity:** The paper's clarity is a significant weakness. While the high-level ideas are communicated effectively, the details are often dense and difficult to follow.
*   Section 4.2's description of the RNN training for representation learning could be more explicit about the architecture and training objectives.
*   Section 7.2, which defines the experimental tasks, is particularly challenging to parse. The definitions of the k-th term for each expression family are compact but cryptic. A clearer, step-by-step explanation or a simple example for one of the more complex families (e.g., (RBM-2)k) would have greatly improved readability.
*   The motivation for removing the matrix-multiplication rule for two of the families feels somewhat ad-hoc. While the text states this is to ensure any found solution is efficient, it also constrains the search space in a significant way that warrants more discussion. A more detailed description of the search scheduler and its interaction with the strategy would also benefit readers attempting to reproduce the work.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is debatable. On one hand, it is a fascinating proof-of-concept demonstrating that ML can effectively guide search in a complex, symbolic domain. The learned continuous representations for mathematical formulas could inspire future work in applying ML to automated reasoning.

On the other hand, the practical impact seems low. The problem families, while challenging, appear somewhat contrived and it is not demonstrated that they are of broad interest to practitioners. The paper mentions potential applications to RBMs and dropout, but these connections are speculative and not substantiated with a concrete use case. It is unclear whether the proposed framework can scale to a general-purpose symbolic optimizer that could be integrated into a compiler or numerical computing library. The fact that the search is constrained by a manually defined grammar and a 10-minute timeout further limits its perceived general applicability. Therefore, while the ideas are novel, it is uncertain whether other researchers or practitioners will build directly upon these specific methods or use the discovered identities.

---

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **High Originality:** Proposes a novel and interesting problem at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic mathematics.
*   **Novel Methodology:** The application of NLP techniques, especially the recursive neural network for learning continuous representations of symbolic expressions, is a creative and valuable contribution.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The learning-based methods are shown to dramatically outperform a random search baseline, successfully discovering highly non-trivial identities.
*   **Inspiring Future Work:** The paper could stimulate further research into applying statistical learning methods to symbolic reasoning, program synthesis, and automated theorem proving.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Limited Significance and Practicality:** The problem appears niche, and the practical utility of both the framework and the discovered identities is unclear. The connection to real-world problems in machine learning is tenuous.
*   **Poor Clarity:** The paper is difficult to read and understand in key sections, which hinders a full appreciation of the technical details and would make reproduction difficult.
*   **Ambiguous Performance of RNN:** The simpler n-gram model outperforms the more complex and conceptually novel RNN on most of the reported tasks, which slightly undermines the contribution of the learned continuous representation for this specific search application.
