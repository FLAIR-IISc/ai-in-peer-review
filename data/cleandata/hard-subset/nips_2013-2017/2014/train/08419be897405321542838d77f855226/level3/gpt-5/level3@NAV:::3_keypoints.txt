- Paper proposes an attribute-grammar framework and learned search to discover algebraic identities with lower computational complexity than a target expression.  
- Search is guided by two learned strategies: n-gram statistics over operator subtrees and a recursive neural network (RNN) embedding partial trees to predict next rules.  
- Authors show recursive neural representations can classify algebraic identities and that learned guidance finds nontrivial identities beyond brute force, including O(n3)→O(n2) reductions and RBM-like speedups.  
- Work connects to prior grammars in optimization, program induction, theorem proving, and tree-structured neural models (e.g., Socher, Bowman).  
- Empirical results are intriguing and public code release is valuable.  
- RNN architecture description is underspecified and ambiguous about whether it uses linear vs tensor (RNTN-style) composition; ablation and justification are missing.  
- Unclear whether the RNN is used as a classifier, proposal model, or both during search, and how different search strategies are compared (greedy vs beam, branching, tie-breaking, stopping criteria).  
- The proposal-generation mechanism and search evaluation details require much more explanation.  
- Manuscript contains numerous typos, labeling issues, and inconsistent notation that hinder comprehension (duplicated sentences, wrong figure labels, spurious parentheses, duplicated words).  
- Request to clarify whether descriptors in Zp are provably collision-resistant for the grammar sizes explored.  
- Novelty is strong: combining attribute grammars with learned search and continuous embeddings for algebraic expressions is timely and original.  
- Significance is potentially high but currently limited by restrictive problem setting (homogeneous polynomials, single variable, scalar outputs); need a clearer path to broader settings.  
- Pros: novel framing and nontrivial identities found, evidence for recursive neural classification of algebraic structures, and public code/data.  
- Cons: ambiguous RNN/RNTN architecture and missing tensor-network comparisons, insufficient proposal/search details, restricted problem definition, and multiple clarity/typographical errors.  
- Overall: promising work that needs architectural clarification, stronger baselines/ablations (including tensor networks), clearer search-detailing, and thorough proofreading.
