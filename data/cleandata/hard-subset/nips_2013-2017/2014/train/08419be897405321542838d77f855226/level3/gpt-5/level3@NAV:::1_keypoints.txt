- Proposes a grammar-based framework to discover computationally efficient identities equivalent to a target expression.
- Generates expressions via an attribute grammar and tests equivalence with randomized descriptors over Zp; uses modular Gaussian elimination for complex targets.
- Makes search tractable by learning strategies from simpler instances: an n-gram model over solution subtrees and an RNN embedding partial trees to predict next rules.
- Experiments on restricted families (matrix polynomials, symmetric polynomials, RBM-like sums) show learned strategies sometimes outperform random search, but gains are modest and inconsistent.
- The n-gram baseline often outperforms the RNN in harder cases.
- Work connects to theorem proving, program induction, symbolic algebra, symbolic regression, and superoptimization.
- Technical implementation and learning components are sound and carefully executed.
- Empirical evidence is preliminary and the evaluation domain is very narrow (homogeneous polynomials, scalar/single-variable cases).
- Success rates on harder families are low and scaling to richer operators/multivariate settings is unclear.
- Equivalence testing via randomized Zp descriptors is pragmatic but lacks formal guarantees.
- Missing comparisons to strong baselines (Maple/Mathematica, algebraic rewriting, superoptimization, GP-based symbolic regression).
- No reported end-to-end runtime speedups on real computations.
- Paper is generally clear and organized but needs more pseudocode, an explicit cost model for “complexity,” and fuller dataset/RNN training details for reproducibility.
- Applying learned models, especially RNN embeddings, to guide identity discovery is novel and interesting, but positioning against related work needs improvement.
- Potentially high impact if the approach scales, but current results are limited and may be overkill for the constrained grammar used.
- Pros: novel framing, clean grammar setup, interesting learning-guided search, code/data availability, and some nontrivial identities found.
- Cons: narrow domain, modest empirical gains, weak scaling, RNN underperforms n-grams, missing strong baselines, no real speedups, and equivalence test lacks guarantees.
- Recommendation: Weak reject; suggested improvements include broadening domains/operators, adding rigorous baselines, demonstrating runtime speedups, strengthening equivalence guarantees, and deeper failure-mode and scalability analysis.
