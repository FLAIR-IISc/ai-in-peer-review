This paper introduces an interesting approach to discovering efficient mathematical identities by combining attribute grammars with machine learning, specifically n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN). The core idea of learning to guide the search for computationally cheaper equivalent expressions is novel and potentially impactful. The authors correctly identify the limitations of brute-force search in this space and propose learning-based strategies to overcome them.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses related to clarity, justification, and experimental detail. The problem definition, while clearly stated, feels overly restrictive. Focusing on homogeneous polynomials of degree *k* with a single matrix variable limits the broader applicability and impact of the work. While the authors acknowledge this in Section 8, a stronger justification for these choices upfront would be beneficial.

A key concern lies in the description of the RNN architecture. The explanation of the “weight tensor W3 (of size lxlxl...)” is unclear. Is this a standard neural tensor network, or is a different mechanism being employed? The paper needs to explicitly state the type of tensor operation used. Furthermore, the authors fail to adequately justify their choice of an RNN over other, potentially more suitable models. Bowman (2013) demonstrates that tensor networks often outperform RNNs on tasks involving structured data and logical reasoning – a relevant observation given the inherent structure of mathematical expressions. A discussion of why the authors opted for an RNN despite this prior work is crucial.

Section 7.2, detailing the removal of the matrix-matrix multiply rule, is also unclear. It’s not immediately obvious whether the RNN is still used for classification *after* this rule removal, or if it solely guides the search within the reduced grammar. Moreover, the evaluation of different search strategies isn't clearly articulated. How are the success rates compared, and what statistical significance can be attributed to the observed differences?

The proposal generation procedure within the search process lacks sufficient detail. It’s stated that the strategy assigns scores to grammar rules, but the specific mechanics of how these scores translate into a selection process aren’t fully explained. More information on the scheduler’s behavior would be valuable.

The manuscript contains several typos and grammatical errors. For instance, "O(()n3)" should be O(n^3). (The reviewer provided further examples, which should be addressed.) The repeated opening sentence is a minor issue but points to a lack of thorough editing. Additionally, Figure 3 is incorrectly labeled – it depicts an RNN, not a Tensor Neural Network (TNN) as indicated.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of attribute grammars and machine learning for identity discovery is a novel approach.
*   **Potential Impact:** The ability to automatically discover efficient mathematical identities has practical applications in compiler optimization and potentially other areas.
*   **Demonstrated Success:** The experiments show that the learning-based approaches can outperform random search, particularly for certain problem families.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Restricted Problem Setting:** The limited scope of the problem (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix variable) weakens the broader impact.
*   **Lack of Clarity:** The description of the RNN architecture and search procedure is unclear.
*   **Insufficient Justification:** The choice of an RNN over other models isn't adequately justified.
*   **Technical Weaknesses:** Typos, grammatical errors, and a mislabeled figure detract from the paper's quality.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting and potentially promising approach, but it requires significant revisions to address the clarity and justification issues. The restricted problem setting and lack of a comparative analysis against stronger baselines (e.g., tensor networks) are also concerns. As it stands, the paper does not meet the high standards for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend **rejection** with the encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the points raised in this review. The core idea is strong, but the presentation and technical details need substantial improvement.
