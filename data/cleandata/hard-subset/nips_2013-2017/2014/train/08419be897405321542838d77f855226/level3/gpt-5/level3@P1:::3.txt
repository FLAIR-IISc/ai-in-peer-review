This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient reformulations of symbolic expressions. The key idea is to define an attribute grammar over matrix/vector operations and search over expression trees whose leaves are variables and whose internal nodes are grammar rules, constrained to produce scalar outputs and fixed degree. Because exhaustive search is infeasible, the authors introduce two learned search strategies trained on solutions to simpler targets: an n-gram model over subtrees and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of partial trees and a softmax policy to choose the next rule. Equivalence between a candidate and the target is checked numerically via randomized evaluation over Zp, and complex identities may require solving a linear system over descriptors to find linear combinations of trees. Experiments across several expression families (matrix polynomials, elementwise variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-inspired exponential sums) show that learned guidance substantially enlarges the class of solvable targets compared to random search; in practice, n-grams outperform the RNN on most families. The authors also report high multiclass accuracy when using the RNN as an expression classifier, indicating it learns a distributed representation of symbolic expressions.

Relation to prior work: The problem connects to theorem proving and program induction, but the paper restricts itself to fixed-degree polynomials, avoiding undecidability. It also relates to compiler optimization via strength reduction and algebraic rewrites (cf. CAS systems like Maple/Mathematica) but differs by learning heuristics rather than relying on hand-coded rules. The use of attribute grammars in search has precedent, and the neural components borrow from NLP: n-gram models and recursive nets for tree-structured inputs, as in Socher et al. The continuous embedding of symbolic expressions echoes work on word/sentence embeddings; Bowman and others have explored neural reasoning on tree structures.

Strengths
- A clear and interesting formulation of identity discovery as guided search over an attribute grammar, with a concrete cost model.
- Two complementary learned strategies with curriculum training; results convincingly show learning improves reach over random search.
- A practical equivalence-checking mechanism via Zp descriptors, enabling scalable evaluation and integration with learning.
- Demonstration that an RNN can learn usable embeddings of algebraic structures, achieving low error on expression classification.
- Open-sourced code and many discovered identities with substantial complexity reductions.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
Quality
- Correctness guarantees for the Zp-based descriptor: please provide bounds on collision probability as a function of N and p, and report any cross-checks with exact symbolic verification on a held-out set of discovered identities. Clarify whether linear combination weights w are integral and how that maps back to algebraic identities over reals/integers.
- Proposal generation/scheduler: the paper suggests a greedy policy (“pick highest score”) but does not specify beam width, backtracking, or any exploration mechanism. A pseudocode description and an ablation (e.g., beam size, epsilon-greedy, MCTS) would strengthen the case and explain the success fractions.
- RNN architecture ambiguity: the text describes a “weight tensor W3 (l×l×l)” applied to two inputs, which resembles a neural tensor network (NTN) rather than a standard bilinear/affine RNN. Please clarify whether this is an NTN (as in Socher et al.) or a simpler composition, and describe how binary vs unary rules are handled. If it is an NTN, explain why this model (vs. e.g., TreeLSTM) was chosen and how capacity is controlled.
- Comparative baselines: consider comparing to (i) exhaustive/heuristic enumerative search with pruning, (ii) classical CAS simplifiers with added cost-directed rewriting, or (iii) non-learned heuristic orderings (e.g., A* with an admissible cost bound). This would contextualize the gains from learning.

Clarity
- Section 7.2: it is unclear whether the RNN in the search strategy is the same network trained for classification in 7.1 with the softmax head swapped, or whether a separate network/policy is trained. Please detail the training targets, data split across k, and measures taken to avoid leakage from test targets to policy training.
- Define precisely the cost model for each grammar rule and how overall complexity is estimated. In cases where matrix-matrix multiply is removed, make explicit how this constrains admissible solutions and the implications for completeness.
- Linear combinations of trees: describe how trees are generated before solving Fw = t, whether w is constrained (e.g., sparsity, non-negativity), and how you ensure the resulting linear combination corresponds to a valid algebraic identity expressed in the grammar.
- Provide details on scheduler hyperparameters (time budget per run, number of restarts, trees/sec) to make the success-rate plots reproducible.

Originality
- The combination of attribute grammars, randomized equivalence testing, and learned search is novel in this specific application. The learning-to-search angle for algebraic identities is timely and distinguishes the work from rule-based CAS and from prior grammar-based optimization without learning.

Significance
- The discovered identities (e.g., reductions from O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)), and polynomial-time surrogates for exponential sums up to moderate k) could be impactful in practice and suggest broader potential for integrating such systems into compilers/CAS.
- However, the problem setting is quite restricted (homogeneous polynomials, single variable A, scalar output). The paper would benefit from either relaxing these constraints or presenting stronger justification and a roadmap for extension (e.g., multiple variables, nonhomogeneous expressions, recursion or trigonometric rules).

Specific suggestions
- Justify the choice of RNN vs. neural tensor networks or TreeLSTMs, citing that tensor models often outperform simple RNNs on logical/symbolic tasks (e.g., Bowman; Socher et al.), or provide a small comparative study.
- Add beam search or limited-width best-first search; report sensitivity to beam width and dropout rate in the policy.
- Include a table of discovered identities with their complexity reductions and verification status; symbolically verify a subset.
- Analyze failure cases, especially for (RBM-2)k, and discuss what grammar extensions or representation changes might help.
- Provide a theoretical or empirical bound on N (descriptor length) needed to uniquely characterize classes up to degree k.

Minor issues
- Typos and grammar throughout; e.g., “help which the discovery” (should be “help with the discovery”), inconsistent spacing in O(()n3), multiple repeated phrases (the opening sentence appears twice). A careful proofread is needed.
- Figure 3 legend labels “TNN”; this should read “RNN”.
- Some equations and subscripts/superscripts have spacing/formatting issues (e.g., ∑i,j(AA T)bk/2c). Unify notation for transpose and elementwise operations.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and promising integration of learning with symbolic search; compelling demonstrations beyond brute force.
- Clear empirical benefit of learned guidance; reusable ideas (Zp descriptors, curriculum over k) for symbolic ML.
- Potentially impactful for program optimization and CAS.

Arguments against acceptance
- Important methodological ambiguities (RNN architecture, scheduler details, equivalence guarantees).
- Restricted problem scope limits generality; limited comparison to strong non-learned baselines.
- RNN underperforms n-gram in main discovery task, raising questions about the value added by the neural component without further analysis.
