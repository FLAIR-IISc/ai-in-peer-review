- Proposes an attribute-grammar framework to represent symbolic expressions and search for equivalent, lower-complexity forms.  
- Uses two learning-based search strategies: n-gram models capturing common sub-expressions and a recursive neural network that predicts grammar-rule expansions.  
- Positions the work relative to theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, noting the restricted polynomial domain avoids undecidability.  
- Strength (Quality): technically sound framework with appropriate experiments; RNN representation achieves good classification accuracy (≤5% error).  
- Strength (Originality): novel application of machine learning to discover mathematical identities; framework and discovered identities are new contributions.  
- Weakness (Clarity): presentation is confusing—grammar rules, search strategy, linear combinations, and the experimental section need clearer exposition and organization.  
- Weakness (Significance): problem domain is narrow and contrived; discovered identities have unclear practical relevance and tenuous application claims.  
- Pro acceptance arguments: novel ML-to-symbolic-computation approach, solid technical execution, genuinely new identities, and potentially useful learned representations.  
- Con acceptance arguments: highly specialized domain with limited practical impact, confusing presentation, speculative application claims, and n-gram often outperforming the RNN.  
- Overall: technically strong and original but limited in practical impact and motivation for a broad ML audience.  
- Recommendation: reject due to insufficient significance for NIPS despite the paper’s technical merits.
