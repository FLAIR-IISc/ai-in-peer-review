- Introduces a grammar-based framework and two learning approaches (n-gram, RNN) to discover efficient mathematically equivalent expressions.  
- Main contributions: grammar for symbolic expressions, ML-guided search, recursive neural representations, and discovery of new reduced-complexity identities.  
- Strength: Novel idea of using learning to guide symbolic search.  
- Strength: Demonstrated discovery of identities reducing complexity (e.g., O(n^3) to O(n^2)).  
- Strength: Learning methods outperform random search on complex expressions.  
- Strength: Use of numerical descriptors for symbolic expressions is practical and clever.  
- Weakness: Problem domain is overly restrictive—limited to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree k with a single matrix/vector variable.  
- Weakness: No clear justification for using a standard RNN over neural tensor networks; unclear if a tensor weight constitutes an NTN.  
- Weakness: Several clarity issues—unclear whether RNN is used during search, poorly explained proposal generation, mislabeled figures, and multiple typos.  
- Weakness: Evaluation ambiguity—unclear how "fraction of successful runs" is computed and whether RNN classification was used during search.  
- Technical concern: Claim that recursive representations enable classification is only partially supported, and the connection between learned representations and the search procedure is not well explained.  
- Recommendation: Broaden problem scope, compare/justify model choice (e.g., NTN vs RNN), clarify RNN’s role in search, and fix typos/grammar.  
- Overall verdict: Interesting and promising idea with strong empirical results for a restricted domain, but methodological, justification, and clarity issues lead to a recommendation to reject.
