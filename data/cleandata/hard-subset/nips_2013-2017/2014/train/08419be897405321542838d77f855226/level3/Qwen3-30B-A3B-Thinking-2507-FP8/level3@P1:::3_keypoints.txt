- Proposes a novel grammar-based representation and ML-guided search to discover efficient mathematical identities.  
- Applies NLP techniques (n-gram models and recursive neural networks) to symbolic computation, a novel cross-domain application.  
- Demonstrates clear computational efficiency gains (e.g., reducing O(n³) to O(n²)) and discovers previously unknown identities.  
- Uses integer-based descriptors for equivalence checking and learned continuous representations for symbolic expressions—clever practical solutions.  
- Main limitation: problem formulation restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree k, limiting applicability.  
- Authors should discuss extensions to more general expressions (e.g., trig functions, recursion) to increase significance.  
- RNN architecture explanation is unclear—mention of a weight tensor W3 (l×l×l) implies a neural tensor network but is not clarified or justified.  
- Reviewer recommends citing Bowman [3] and justifying choice of tensor/net architecture versus simpler RNNs.  
- Section 7.2 is unclear about whether the RNN is used during identity discovery classification or only for learned representations.  
- Proposal generation mechanism and how learning guides tree exploration are not adequately explained.  
- Technical issues: Figure 3 mislabels RNN as "TNN", multiple grammatical errors, and duplicated sentences reduce readability.  
- Originality is mixed: integration of attribute grammars and ML is novel, but restricted scope limits originality.  
- Significance constrained by problem restrictions and lack of demonstrated practical applications, though guiding search in combinatorial spaces is an important contribution.  
- Suggested improvements: state scope limits and extensions, clarify and justify network architecture, detail search/proposal generation, fix errors, and better connect theory to applications.  
- Overall: promising and interesting work but requires substantial clarification and revision to be a strong NIPS contribution; claimed advantages over simpler methods are not fully demonstrated.
