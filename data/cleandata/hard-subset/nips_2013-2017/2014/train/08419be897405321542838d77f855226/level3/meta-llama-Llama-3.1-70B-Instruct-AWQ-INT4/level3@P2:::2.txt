This paper proposes a novel approach to discovering efficient mathematical identities by leveraging machine learning techniques. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and develop two learning-based methods to guide the search for simpler expression trees. The first method utilizes n-gram models over sub-expression sequences, while the second employs a recursive neural network (RNN) to produce continuous subtree representations and predict combination rules.

The paper relates to previous work in theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, but distinguishes itself by focusing on the computation of polynomials with fixed degree k, where undecidability and the halting problem are not present. The authors also draw inspiration from natural language processing techniques, such as n-gram language models and recursive neural networks, and adapt them to the symbolic computation domain.

The paper's main contributions include the introduction of a novel grammar framework, the application of machine learning techniques to guide the search for efficient expressions, and the discovery of new mathematical identities that offer significant reductions in computational complexity. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on various families of expressions, showcasing the ability of their methods to discover efficient identities that are beyond the reach of brute-force search or human derivation.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and its relation to previous work. However, the clarity of the paper is somewhat limited, particularly in Section 7.2, where the experimental setup and results could be more clearly presented. The paper's originality is high, as it introduces a novel approach to discovering efficient mathematical identities and adapts machine learning techniques to the symbolic computation domain.

The significance of the paper is rated low, as the problem addressed appears contrived and unlikely to be adopted for efficient computations. While the authors demonstrate the ability of their methods to discover efficient identities, the practical usefulness of these methods for producing computationally efficient symbolic expressions is unclear. The paper's focus on a specific class of expressions (homogeneous polynomials of degree k) and the use of a custom grammar framework may limit the applicability of the approach to more general settings.

* The paper introduces a novel approach to discovering efficient mathematical identities, which is a significant contribution to the field.
