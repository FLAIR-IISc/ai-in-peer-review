- Novel, intriguing approach: learns representations for symbolic expressions to find computationally efficient identities.
- Framework: uses an attribute grammar to generate expression space and ML models (n-grams, RNN) to guide search.
- Distinct from traditional CAS: uses learned heuristics instead of hand-crafted rules.
- Main strength: originality—applying learned representations to navigate combinatorial symbolic spaces is novel.
- Potential significance: could optimize symbolic libraries and scientific code; authors demonstrate discovery of several non-trivial identities.
- Limited experimental scope: experiments restricted to a class of homogeneous polynomials.
- Lack of justification for generalization: no theoretical or empirical evidence the method extends to trigonometric, recursive, or more complex functions.
- Paper organization and clarity are poor; system components (scheduler, search strategy, linear solver) and their interplay are hard to follow.
- Technical ambiguities: algorithm does not explicitly enforce or bias toward lower computational complexity, despite problem statement.
- Unclear constraints on vector/matrix sizes in examples (e.g., RBM-1 may be invalid for k=1, n=1).
- Need to formalize and justify the restrictions described in Section 2.
- Overall assessment: promising proof-of-concept but incomplete and with significant clarity and scope concerns.
- Arguments for acceptance: highly original idea and demonstrated potential to discover useful identities.
- Arguments against acceptance: very limited scope, poor organization, and unclear/mismatched technical details.
