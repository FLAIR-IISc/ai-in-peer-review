This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering computationally efficient versions of mathematical expressions. The central idea is to use an attribute grammar to define a search space of symbolic expressions. Because a brute-force or random search of this space is intractable for complex targets, the authors propose using machine learning models to guide the search. The paper explores two such models: a simple n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN). These models are trained on solutions to simpler expressions and then used to predict promising grammar rules to apply when searching for solutions to more complex targets. This work connects ideas from program induction and theorem proving with modern machine learning techniques popular in NLP, such as learning distributed representations for structured data. The authors demonstrate their framework by discovering efficient polynomial-time identities for several expression families whose naive evaluation would be high-order polynomial or even exponential.

### Strengths

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—using machine learning to guide symbolic search for mathematical identities—is highly novel and significant. It tackles a fundamental AI problem of navigating a combinatorially large search space. Success in this area could have a broad impact on compiler optimization, scientific discovery, and automated reasoning. The idea of learning continuous representations for symbolic math expressions is also a powerful and promising direction.

2.  **Creative Application of ML Techniques:** The application of methods from natural language processing, such as n-gram models and recursive neural networks on tree structures, to the domain of symbolic mathematics is a creative and compelling transfer of ideas between fields.

3.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates the discovery of non-trivial mathematical identities that offer substantial computational speedups (e.g., reducing exponential complexity to polynomial). These results are a strong validation of the framework's potential and represent a solid empirical contribution.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Overly Restricted Problem Definition:** The problem is constrained to finding efficient versions of homogeneous polynomials of a single variable that produce a scalar output. While this provides a focused testbed, it significantly limits the immediate applicability of the work. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the challenges involved in relaxing these assumptions and a clearer roadmap for extending the framework to a more general class of mathematical expressions.

2.  **Lack of Clarity in Model Description:** The description of the recursive neural network is ambiguous in key places, hindering reproducibility and a full understanding of the model.
    *   The explanation for the "weight tensor W3 (of size lxlxl...)" is insufficient. It is unclear whether this is a standard trilinear form or if it implies a more complex architecture like a Neural Tensor Network (NTN), as used in other recursive models. Given the context, this detail is critical.
    *   The paper does not justify its choice of a standard RNN over other, potentially more powerful, recursive models. Work by Bowman ("Can recursive neural tensor networks learn logical reasoning," 2013) suggests that tensor networks often outperform standard RNNs on similar structured reasoning tasks. The authors should either provide a rationale for their model choice or include a comparison to this stronger baseline.

3.  **Unclear Experimental Details:** The description of the search and evaluation procedure lacks crucial details.
    *   In Section 7.2, it is not clear how the different search strategies are evaluated. For some expression families, the matrix-multiply rule is removed. It's ambiguous whether the RNN, which was trained on the full grammar, is still used to guide the search in this restricted setting and how this affects its performance.
    *   The proposal generation step within the search procedure is not well-explained. The paper mentions that the scheduler uses "scores" from the strategy model but does not specify how these scores (e.g., softmax probabilities from the RNN) are used to select the next rule. Is it a simple argmax, a sampling procedure, or something else? This is a key implementation detail that is currently missing.

4.  **Presentation and Writing Quality:** The manuscript is in need of careful proofreading. There are several typos, grammatical errors, and presentation mistakes that reduce the paper's clarity and professionalism.
    *   There are duplicate sentences; for example, the opening sentence of the introduction is repeated.
    *   Grammatical errors are present (e.g., "can help which the discovery of more complex ones").
    *   In Figure 3, the model is incorrectly labeled as "TNN" in the legend, which should be "RNN", adding to the confusion about the model architecture.

### Summary of Review

**Quality:** The paper presents a high-quality, original idea. However, the technical soundness is undermined by a lack of clarity in the model description and experimental methodology, making the work difficult to reproduce or build upon. The claims are supported by experimental results, but the paper feels incomplete due to the missing details.

**Clarity:** The paper is not clearly written. Key architectural details of the RNN and the search procedure are ambiguous or underspecified. The manuscript's organization could be improved, and the numerous grammatical errors and typos detract significantly from the reading experience.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The approach of learning a search policy for symbolic mathematical discovery is novel, and the application of RNNs to this domain represents a new and interesting research direction.

**Significance:** The results are potentially very significant. If the framework can be generalized, it could have a substantial impact on automated optimization and symbolic computation. The demonstration that continuous embeddings can effectively represent symbolic expressions is an important contribution in its own right.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a highly original and promising line of research. The core idea is strong, and the results are compelling. However, the significant issues with clarity in the model and experimental descriptions, combined with the numerous writing errors, prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. If the authors can substantially revise the paper to address the points above—particularly by clarifying the RNN architecture, justifying their model choice, and detailing the search procedure—it would represent a strong contribution to the field.
