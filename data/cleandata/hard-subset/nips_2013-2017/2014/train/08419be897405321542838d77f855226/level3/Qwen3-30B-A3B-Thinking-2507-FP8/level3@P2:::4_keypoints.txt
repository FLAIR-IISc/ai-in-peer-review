- Introduces a novel ML framework using attribute grammars to discover efficient mathematical identities by searching for equivalent, lower-complexity expressions.
- Uses n-gram models and recursive neural networks to guide search and mitigate combinatorial explosion.
- Demonstrates the method on polynomial expression families, claiming complexity reductions (e.g., O(n^3) to O(n^2)).
- Novel contribution: applies NLP-style models to symbolic computation, bridging statistical learning and symbolic systems.
- Attribute grammars provide a structured representation for expressions, aiding the search process.
- Learning-based search strategies offer a promising alternative to brute-force enumeration.
- Continuous representation of symbolic expressions via recursive neural networks is interesting and potentially widely applicable.
- Experimental validation is thorough across multiple expression families.
- Scope is highly restricted to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with a single matrix/vector variable, limiting applicability.
- Authors provide insufficient justification for the restrictive problem constraints.
- Framework does not enforce computational-complexity constraints during search, allowing potentially worse solutions—a major technical gap.
- Paper organization and explanations (e.g., scheduler vs. strategy interaction) are confusing and unclear.
- Claims of discovering identities beyond brute-force or human derivation lack adequate comparative analysis with existing symbolic computation methods.
- Technical concerns about expression representation correctness (e.g., RBM-1 example invalid for k=1, n=1) call for more rigorous mathematical justification.
- Quality: technically sound approach but weakened by scope limits and lack of complexity enforcement.
- Clarity: poor organization and unclear presentation of key components hinder understanding.
- Originality: conceptually novel use of n-grams and RNNs for symbolic identity discovery, but limited domain reduces impact and differentiation from related work.
- Significance: potential practical benefits are speculative due to the narrow problem domain and limited demonstrations.
- For acceptance: novel ML application to symbolic computation with promising technical ideas and representations.
- Against acceptance: narrow scope, failure to enforce complexity objectives, inadequate justification and benchmarking against prior work.
- Recommendation: reject; suggest expanding the framework’s scope, providing stronger theoretical justification, and demonstrating broader applicability.
