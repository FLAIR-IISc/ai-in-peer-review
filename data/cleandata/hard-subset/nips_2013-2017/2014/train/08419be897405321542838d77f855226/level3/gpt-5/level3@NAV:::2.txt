This paper explores learning-guided search for discovering computationally cheaper symbolic identities. The authors formalize expressions via an attribute grammar and cast identity discovery as searching over expression trees (and linear combinations thereof) that are provably equivalent (checked via integer descriptors modulo a large prime). To scale beyond brute-force, they learn search strategies from solved, lower-degree instances: (i) simple n-gram models over subexpression sequences; and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous subtree embeddings and predicts the next combination rule. The work connects to theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, and adapts techniques from NLP (n-grams; recursive nets in the style of Socher et al.) while contrasting with rule-based CAS systems (Maple/Mathematica) that do not target computational complexity. Empirically, learning improves search success, and the n-gram approach outperforms the RNN on two of three task families (per Fig. 3).

Quality: The framework is technically sound and complete, with code provided. The integer-descriptor equivalence check and linear-combination solver are well-motivated. However, key aspects would benefit from tighter support: collision risk in the modulo-p descriptor is not quantified; “efficiency” is sometimes guaranteed by removing matrix-multiply from the grammar (Section 7.2), confounding the evaluation of true computational gains; and baselines are limited (e.g., no beam search, A*, or MCTS with hand-crafted heuristics; no comparison to superoptimization-style search).

Clarity: The paper is often hard to follow, especially Section 7.2 (families, constraints, and success criteria interleave with grammar restrictions). The scheduler/strategy interface and exact training protocol (curriculum steps, hyperparameters, dropout schedule) should be described more systematically, and figures cross-referenced more clearly.

Originality: High. Casting identity discovery as learning over attribute grammars and demonstrating continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions are novel and interesting.

Significance: Low to moderate. The benchmarks feel contrived, and practical usefulness remains unclear: there are no end-to-end wall-clock speedups on real workloads, and the strongest method is the simple n-gram. Adoption in compilers/ML pipelines seems uncertain without broader grammars and stronger baselines.

Pros:
- Clear problem framing; elegant grammar-based search.
- Innovative use of learning to guide combinatorial search; open-source release.
- Empirical evidence that learned guidance expands feasible search regimes.

Cons:
- Practical impact unclear; constrained tasks and grammar restrictions.
- n-gram > RNN on most tasks; limited baselines.
- Clarity issues (notably Sec. 7.2); missing analysis of descriptor collisions and runtime gains.

Recommendation: Leaning reject due to limited significance and clarity, despite high quality and originality. Constructive path to strengthen: add stronger baselines, quantify end-to-end speedups, analyze descriptor reliability, and improve exposition.
