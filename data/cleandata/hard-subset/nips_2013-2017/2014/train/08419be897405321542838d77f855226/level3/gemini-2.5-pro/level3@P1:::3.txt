This paper introduces a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar and to search through the space of possible expression trees for one that is analytically equivalent to a target expression but has lower computational complexity. Recognizing that the search space is intractably large, the authors propose using machine learning to guide the search. They train models (n-grams and a recursive neural network) on solutions found for simpler expressions to learn a search strategy for more complex ones. The recursive neural network (RNN) is used to learn a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions, which is a key contribution. This work relates to existing research in automated theorem proving and program induction, but distinguishes itself by its focus on computational efficiency and its novel application of statistical machine learning techniques, particularly those recently successful in natural language processing like recursive neural networks for structured data (e.g., Socher et al., 2013).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a challenging and important problem at the intersection of machine learning and symbolic computation. The idea of "learning to discover" efficient identities is highly original and could have a significant impact on areas ranging from compiler optimization to theoretical science. Automatically finding non-obvious, efficient ways to compute complex expressions is a valuable goal.
2.  **Novel Application of RNNs:** The proposal to learn a distributed, continuous representation for symbolic expressions using an RNN is a major conceptual contribution. This approach has the potential to unlock the application of a wide range of powerful machine learning tools to symbolic domains, much as word embeddings have done for natural language processing. The high classification accuracy reported in Table 2 suggests this representation is effective at capturing semantic equivalence between expressions.
3.  **Demonstrated Potential:** The authors show that their learning-based strategies can discover complex identities for several families of expressions (e.g., related to symmetric polynomials and RBM partition functions) that are beyond the reach of random search. The discovery of potentially new, useful identities underscores the practical value of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity of the Method:** The paper suffers from several points of ambiguity that make the work difficult to understand and reproduce.
    *   The description of the RNN architecture is confusing. In Section 4.2, the authors describe an operation with a "weight tensor W3 (of size l x l x l)" and formulate the update as `max((W3 * a) * a, 0)`. It is unclear if this is a standard linear layer or a more complex tensor contraction, akin to a Recursive Neural Tensor Network (RNTN). If it is an RNTN, this should be stated clearly. If not, the notation and operation need a more precise definition.
    *   The search procedure, which is central to the contribution, is not explained in sufficient detail. The paper mentions a "scheduler" that uses scores from a "strategy" but provides little information on how new candidate expressions are proposed and generated at each step of the tree-building process.
    *   It is not clear how the RNN, trained as a classifier to distinguish between expression equivalence classes, is repurposed as a search strategy in Section 7.2. The paper states the softmax probabilities become scores, but the mechanism for predicting the *next* rule to apply based on the representation of the *current* expression(s) needs to be elaborated.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Justification:**
    *   The problem definition appears overly restricted to homogeneous polynomials of a single variable. While the authors acknowledge this, a more thorough discussion of the limitations this imposes and the challenges of extending the framework to more general classes of functions would strengthen the paper.
    *   The paper fails to cite or compare against relevant work that uses more powerful models for similar compositional tasks. For instance, Bowman (2013) demonstrated that tensor networks can outperform standard RNNs for learning logical reasoning. The choice of the specific RNN architecture here should be better motivated and justified, potentially with a comparison to alternatives like RNTNs.

3.  **Presentation Quality:** The manuscript requires careful proofreading, as several errors detract from its overall quality.
    *   There are numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "training models on simpler expressions can help which the discovery of more complex ones" in the introduction).
    *   The opening sentence of the introduction is repeated.
    *   Figure 3 incorrectly labels the model as "TNN" in the legend, which presumably should be "RNN". These issues suggest a lack of care in the manuscript's preparation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a highly novel and ambitious idea that bridges symbolic computation and modern machine learning in a creative way.
*   The concept of learning a continuous representation for mathematical expressions is a significant contribution with broad potential applicability.
*   The experimental results, despite the paper's flaws, demonstrate that the approach can discover non-trivial and useful mathematical identities.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The lack of clarity in describing the core methodology (the RNN architecture and the search procedure) is a major flaw, hindering reproducibility and a full assessment of the work's technical contributions.
*   The manuscript's quality is diminished by missing citations to highly relevant work, a restricted problem formulation without sufficient discussion, and several typographical errors.

In summary, this paper contains the seeds of a very exciting and potentially high-impact research direction. The core ideas are excellent. However, the execution in its current form is flawed, particularly with respect to the clarity of the methodology and the overall presentation. I would be inclined to accept a revised version that thoroughly addresses these issues, but in its current state, the paper falls short of the standards for clarity and completeness expected at NIPS.
