- Problem: discovers computationally efficient algebraic identities equivalent to a target expression using an attribute grammar and learned search.  
- Key methods: integer-valued modular descriptor for equivalence, Gaussian-elimination linear combination, and a recursive RNN embedding; search guided by n-grams or RNN policy.  
- Experiments: several expression families show learning-guided search finds lower-asymptotic-cost identities and extends degree range beyond random search; n-grams often beat RNN.  

- Strength: clear, relevant problem formulation targeting complexity-reducing rewrites.  
- Strength: novel combination of attribute grammars with learned search strategies and comparison of n-gram vs RNN.  
- Strength: practical, fast equivalence testing via modular arithmetic enabling scaling.  
- Strength: evidence that training on simpler identities improves discovery for harder ones; several nontrivial identities found.  
- Strength: code and data released for reproducibility.  

- Weakness: narrow scope — homogeneous polynomials, primarily single-variable/scalar outputs, limited operator set and no demonstrated extension to richer/multi-variable settings.  
- Weakness: cost objective not enforced during search; scheduler optimizes for matching target, not minimizing cost.  
- Weakness: paper organization is confusing; roles of grammar, descriptors, linear combination, and strategies introduced piecemeal.  
- Weakness: technical details underspecified or inconsistent (dimension constraints, choice of p and N, which ops count, subexpression reuse, memory).  
- Weakness: RNN underperforms n-grams in many cases with limited analysis of failure modes.  
- Weakness: limited baselines — no CAS/rule-based simplifiers, equality saturation, or cost-aware program synthesis comparisons.  

- Soundness: modular descriptor and linear solver are compelling but lack probabilistic collision bounds; Schwartz–Zippel analysis would help.  
- Completeness: shows end-to-end discovery but no guarantees of optimality or guaranteed lower-cost return; evaluation focuses on time-limited success rates.  
- Evaluation: reasonable families and learning benefit shown, but comparisons mostly to random search; missing cost-aware baselines.  

- Clarity: needs an early pipeline overview and pseudocode for scheduler/strategy interface.  
- Clarity: must explicitly define operator set, cost model, and subexpression-reuse policy.  
- Clarity: specify matrix/vector dimensions per rule and validate dimensionality in all families.  
- Minor typos noted.  

- Originality: novel pairing of attribute grammars with learned structural embeddings and search; RNN embedding of symbolic expressions is a noteworthy contribution.  
- Significance: promising for compiler/CAS optimization if extended to richer operators and multi-variable cases, but current scope limits immediate impact.  

- Suggestion: integrate complexity into search (cost-aware best-first/beam, A* with admissible heuristic, cost penalties in scoring, prune by minimum achievable cost).  
- Suggestion: provide formal bounds and empirical rates for descriptor collisions (choices of p and N).  
- Suggestion: clarify linear-combination selection, coefficient constraints, and rendering of human-readable formulas.  
- Suggestion: run ablations (curriculum, l, descriptor size, dropout schedule) and add a cost-aware non-learning baseline.  
- Suggestion: compare against CAS simplifiers or equality-saturation engines on overlapping tasks.  
- Suggestion: add an early figure summarizing pipeline and a table of operators, shapes, and costs.  

- Questions: clarify RBM-1 dimensions and how (v^T A)^k is formed for all n,k.  
- Questions: state typical p and N per family/degree and sensitivity/collision observations.  
- Questions: define complexity metric — which ops counted, asymptotics vs flop counts, and handling of common subexpression elimination.  
- Questions: why does RNN prefer high-cost but easy-to-match expressions; any cost-regularized training tried?  

- Pro-accept: novel, underexplored problem with solid components, public code, and potentially new identities.  
- Contra-accept: narrow scope, lack of principled cost integration, weak baselines and guarantees, clarity and technical ambiguities.  
- Overall recommendation: weak reject — promising work that needs broader scope, cost-aware search, stronger baselines, and clearer exposition.
