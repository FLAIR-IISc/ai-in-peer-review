## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper introduces a novel approach to discovering mathematical identities, specifically focusing on finding computationally efficient expressions equivalent to a target expression. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions as attribute grammars and leverage machine learning – n-gram models and recursive neural networks – to guide the search for more efficient compositions. This is a compelling direction, as it attempts to automate a traditionally human-driven process of algebraic simplification. The work builds upon concepts from attribute grammars [14] and integrates them with recent advances in representation learning and sequential modeling.

The main strength of this paper lies in its originality. Applying machine learning to this particular problem of symbolic identity discovery is a genuinely novel contribution. The framework itself, while building on established concepts, provides a structured way to approach the search for efficient expressions. Furthermore, the demonstration of discovering identities beyond manual derivation or exhaustive search is promising, suggesting the potential for uncovering new mathematical relationships. The potential impact on symbolic software libraries is significant, as automating the identification of more efficient computation paths could lead to substantial performance improvements.

However, the paper suffers from limitations, particularly in scope and justification. The study is restricted to relatively simple polynomial expressions, and the authors do not convincingly demonstrate the extensibility of their approach to more complex mathematical domains. The argument that restricting the problem simplifies analysis is reasonable, but further investigation into potential scalability challenges is needed. A key concern is the mismatch between the problem statement (seeking lower *computational complexity*) and the algorithm, which doesn't actively enforce complexity constraints during the search; limiting the search to low-complexity solutions is crucial. 

The paper's organization is somewhat convoluted. The flow between sections feels disjointed, and a clearer explanation of the overall pipeline – from grammar definition to search strategy to result verification – would greatly improve readability. Section 2’s restrictions are also insufficiently justified; the authors assert the scheduler is the bottleneck without supporting evidence, and empirical studies would strengthen this claim. Additionally, there are minor technical concerns regarding the vector/matrix size constraints, specifically the RBM-1 expression in the supplement which appears invalid for k=1, n=1. Finally, the paper contains a handful of minor typos (332, 148, 289, 338).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of machine learning to symbolic identity discovery.
*   Demonstrated ability to find identities beyond human derivation.
*   Potential impact on symbolic computation software.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Limited scope and lack of demonstrated extensibility.
*   Mismatch between problem statement and algorithmic enforcement of complexity.
*   Organization and clarity issues.



Overall, while the paper presents an interesting and original idea, its limitations in scope, justification, and clarity prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance at this time. I believe the authors could significantly improve the paper by addressing these concerns.
