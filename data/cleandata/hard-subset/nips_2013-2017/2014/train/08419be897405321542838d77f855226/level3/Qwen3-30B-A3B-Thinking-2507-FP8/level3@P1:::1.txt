

This paper proposes a novel application of machine learning techniques to discover efficient mathematical identities, using attribute grammars to represent symbolic expressions and learning approaches to guide the search for equivalent expressions with lower computational complexity. The authors frame this as a search problem over grammar trees, where they introduce n-gram models and recursive neural networks (RNNs) to learn from simpler expressions to guide the search for more complex identities. The work is related to recent NLP techniques (e.g., n-gram language models, recursive neural networks) applied to a new domain, and draws on attribute grammars from compiler construction.

The paper's main strengths lie in its originality and the thoughtful application of existing NLP techniques to a novel domain. The authors successfully demonstrate how learning can be integrated into symbolic search problems, creating a framework that discovers identities beyond brute-force reach. The use of integer-based numerical descriptors to handle symbolic equivalence is a clever technical contribution that avoids symbolic computation bottlenecks. The RNN-based continuous representation of symbolic expressions is particularly interesting, showing that these networks can learn meaningful representations for mathematical structures (Table 2 shows 95%+ accuracy for expression classification).

However, the paper has significant weaknesses across multiple criteria. The quality of the work is questionable: the experiments are limited to a very narrow domain (polynomials of fixed degree with single or few variables), and the discovered identities (e.g., Example 2) appear to be simple pattern repetitions that could likely be derived by hand with minimal effort. The claim that these identities are "previously undiscovered" is dubious given the simplicity of the patterns. The paper demonstrates no significant computational advantage for practical applications, as the identities discovered are either trivial or only relevant to highly constrained mathematical problems with limited real-world applicability.

The significance of the work is particularly weak. The paper's contribution to advancing the field of machine learning is minimal. The problem domain is so restricted that the approach could easily be replaced by domain-specific heuristics (a "sledgehammer to crack a nut" as noted in the key points). The authors claim the approach could be integrated with compilers or optimization tools, but provide no evidence of this applicability. The most challenging family of expressions (RBM-2) shows no meaningful advantage over random search, and the paper fails to demonstrate any scaling to more complex mathematical domains.

The paper's scope is problematic: the authors restrict themselves to homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree with a single variable (or two matrices in Figure 1), ignoring the vast space of mathematical expressions where such techniques might actually be useful. The grammar rules (Table 1) are extremely limited, and the paper does not address how these would extend to more general mathematical operations. The authors' claim that "the majority of these examples are too complex to be found manually" appears unfounded given the simple repetitive patterns in Examples 1 and 2.

Regarding clarity, the paper is generally well-written, with clear explanations of the framework and technical details. The experimental setup is adequately described, though the limited scope of the experiments is not sufficiently justified.

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- Novel application of NLP techniques to symbolic computation
- Demonstrates that RNNs can learn meaningful representations for mathematical structures
- Provides a framework for learning search strategies in symbolic domains

Con-acceptance arguments:
- Limited to a highly constrained mathematical domain with no demonstrated practical utility
- Results not significantly better than simple pattern matching or hand derivation
- Claims of "previously undiscovered identities" are unsubstantiated
- Fails to demonstrate scalability to more complex mathematical problems
- Lacks evidence of real-world applicability or impact on machine learning practice

The paper represents an interesting exploration of applying machine learning to symbolic problems, but falls short of NIPS publication standards for several reasons. The technical contribution is modest, the results are not compelling, and the significance is minimal given the narrow scope. The authors have created a framework that works for a very specific class of problems, but the paper fails to convincingly argue why this framework is better than existing domain-specific approaches for those problems.

While the idea of using learning to guide symbolic search is intriguing, the execution is too preliminary to warrant publication at NIPS. The paper would benefit from addressing the following: (1) expanding the scope to more complex mathematical expressions, (2) demonstrating clear computational advantages over hand-derived solutions, and (3) providing evidence of broader applicability beyond the limited examples presented. Without these, the work remains an interesting but ultimately narrow technical exercise that does not advance the field of machine learning in a meaningful way.

The authors have made an interesting attempt to bridge symbolic computation and machine learning, but the current version is too preliminary and lacks sufficient significance to merit acceptance at NIPS. The work would be better suited for a more specialized venue focused on symbolic computation or mathematical software.
