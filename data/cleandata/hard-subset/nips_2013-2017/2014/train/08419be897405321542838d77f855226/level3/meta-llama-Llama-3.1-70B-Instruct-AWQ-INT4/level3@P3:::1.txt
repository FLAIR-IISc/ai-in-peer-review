This paper explores the application of machine learning techniques to the discovery of efficient mathematical identities. The authors propose a novel framework based on attribute grammars, which allows symbolic expressions to be represented as a sequence of grammar rules. While the idea of applying deep neural networks (DNNs) to this problem is novel and interesting, the work presented in this paper feels preliminary and may not meet NIPS publication standards.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of a grammar framework for finding efficient versions of symbolic expressions and the application of machine learning techniques to guide the search. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several examples, including the discovery of complex identities that cannot be found by random or brute-force strategies. However, the performance of the method in discovering identities is not particularly impressive, especially considering the limited domain of the problem.

One of the major limitations of the paper is the use of a very limited set of input expressions. The authors consider only five families of expressions, which are chosen to fall within the scope of their grammar rules. While the authors claim that these families are representative of a broad range of mathematical expressions, it is unclear whether the approach would scale to more general expressions or whether scaling is justified given the modest results.

Furthermore, the authors' decision to use a recursive neural network (RNN) to learn a continuous representation of symbolic expressions is not well justified. While the RNN is able to learn an effective representation, it is unclear whether this is the best approach for this problem. Additionally, the authors' use of a curriculum to train the RNN is not well explained, and it is unclear whether this is necessary for the success of the method.

In terms of related work, the authors provide a good overview of the areas of theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, which are relevant to the problem addressed in this paper. However, the authors could have provided more context on how their approach differs from previous work in these areas.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of their framework and the machine learning techniques used to guide the search. However, some of the technical details, such as the use of a curriculum to train the RNN, could be better explained.

Overall, while the paper presents an interesting idea, the work feels preliminary, and the results are not particularly impressive. The authors could have provided more context on the limitations of their approach and the potential for scaling to more general expressions. Additionally, the authors could have provided more technical details on the machine learning techniques used to guide the search.
