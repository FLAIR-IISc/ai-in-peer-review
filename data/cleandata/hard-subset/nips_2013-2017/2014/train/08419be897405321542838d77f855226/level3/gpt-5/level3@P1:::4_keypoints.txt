- Paper proposes learning-guided discovery of algebraic identities using an attribute grammar, modular integer-valued equality oracle, and learned search strategies (n-grams and RNN embeddings) to find lower-cost equivalent expressions.
- Novel integration of attribute grammars, learned search policies, and continuous embeddings; modular evaluation as a fast equality oracle is clever and effective.
- Empirical results show nontrivial complexity reductions (e.g., O(nmp)→O(n(m+p)), O(n^3)→O(n^2)) and polynomial formulas for sums otherwise exponential; n-grams often outperform the RNN on tested families.
- Potential impact: could augment CAS systems and compilers by automatically replacing high-cost expressions with cheaper equivalents.
- Strong reproducibility: public code and datasets provided.
- Scope and generality are limited (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, mostly single-variable); authors should justify and empirically test these constraints and identify which components fail when relaxed.
- Complexity objective mismatch: search does not enforce complexity budgets; recommend cost-aware schedulers (A*, cost-sensitive beam search), learning cost-to-go, explicit budgets/pruning, and reporting time-to-solution vs. cost.
- Typing and dimensionality are underspecified; authors should provide a precise type system, operator signatures/output shapes, well-typedness checking in the scheduler, and guarantees for linear combinations preserving types.
- Equality oracle is probabilistic; provide bounds on collision probability (in terms of N and p), discuss adversarial/correlated inputs, and cross-check identities with a CAS for exact verification.
- Evaluation and baselines are limited; add CAS baselines (Mathematica/Maple), hand heuristics, search-efficiency plots, ablations (curriculum, RNN capacity, descriptor size), and a clear, consistent cost model with runtime measurements.
- Presentation and organization need improvement: add a single figure/pseudocode for the full pipeline, tighten notation for shapes/degree, and separate representation vs. strategy more clearly.
- Technical soundness: sound ideas and engineering (identity initialization, curriculum), but lack of oracle guarantees and weak coupling between search objective and cost reduce rigor.
- Clarity: generally readable but would benefit from restructuring, an explicit type system, early cost-model definition, and fixing minor typos.
- Originality: novel combination of grammars, learned n-grams/RNN embeddings for symbolic expressions, and modular equality testing; relate more to program superoptimization/grammar-based synthesis.
- Significance: promising direction with potential influence on symbolic computation and optimization, but current empirical scope and guarantees are narrow.
- Pros for acceptance: innovative methodology, demonstrated nontrivial identities, open-source implementation.
- Cons against acceptance: restricted scope, incomplete complexity optimization treatment, typing and correctness ambiguities, limited baselines, and presentation issues.
- Minor comments: fix typos, clarify dimensional constraints, and always report prime p and N with failure-probability bounds for the equality oracle.
- Overall recommendation: Borderline/weak accept contingent on clarifying the type system, adding cost-aware search, strengthening evaluation and baselines, and providing oracle guarantees.
