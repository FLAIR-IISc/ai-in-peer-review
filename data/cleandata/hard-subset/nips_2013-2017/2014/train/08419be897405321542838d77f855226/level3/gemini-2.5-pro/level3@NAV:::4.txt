This paper introduces a novel and intriguing approach to discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities by learning representations for symbolic expressions. The authors propose a framework using an attribute grammar to generate a space of expressions and then employ machine learning models—specifically n-grams and a recursive neural network (RNN)—to guide the search for equivalent but less complex formulations of a target expression. This work sits at an interesting intersection of symbolic computation, program induction, and machine learning, differing from traditional systems like Mathematica by using learned heuristics rather than hand-crafted rules.

The primary strength of the paper is its originality. Applying learned representations to navigate the combinatorial space of mathematical expressions is a highly novel contribution. The potential significance is also clear; if extensible, this approach could be a valuable tool for optimizing symbolic software libraries and scientific computing code, and the authors demonstrate its power by discovering several non-trivial identities.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that temper its impact. The most significant is the limited scope. The experiments are confined to a restricted class of homogeneous polynomials, and the paper offers no theoretical justification or empirical evidence to suggest the method can be extended to more complex expressions involving, for instance, trigonometric or recursive functions. This raises questions about the generalizability of the findings.

In terms of clarity, the paper's organization is difficult to follow. The interplay between the scheduler, the search strategy, and the linear solver for combining trees is not clearly explained, making the overall system hard to grasp. Furthermore, there are several technical points that require clarification. A key concern is a mismatch between the problem statement, which seeks lower computational complexity, and the algorithm, which does not explicitly enforce this. The authors should consider ways to bias the search towards lower-complexity solutions. Additionally, the constraints on vector/matrix sizes in the examples are unclear; for instance, the RBM-1 expression appears invalid for k=1, n=1. The justification for the restrictions described in Section 2 also needs to be formalized.

In summary, while the core idea is highly innovative and promising, the paper in its current form feels more like a proof-of-concept than a complete piece of work. The lack of clarity and unaddressed questions about scope are significant barriers to acceptance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Highly original idea applying modern ML techniques to a symbolic domain.
*   Demonstrates potential for discovering new, useful mathematical identities.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Scope is very limited with little evidence of generalizability.
*   Paper is poorly organized and difficult to understand.
*   Key technical details are unclear or mismatched with the stated goals.
