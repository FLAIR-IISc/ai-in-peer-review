This paper proposes a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to represent the space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is analytically equivalent to a given target expression but has a lower computational cost. Recognizing that the search space grows exponentially, making brute-force or random search intractable for complex targets, the authors introduce two machine learning methods to guide the search. These methods are trained on solutions found for simpler expressions in a curriculum-like fashion. The first method is an n-gram model which learns common sequences of operations from previously discovered identities. The second is a more sophisticated recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation of expression trees, which is then used to predict the next grammar rule to apply. The authors demonstrate their framework on five families of polynomial expressions, discovering several new identities and showing that the learning-based strategies significantly outperform a random search baseline.

This work connects to several fields. It is related to automated theorem proving and program induction, but it focuses specifically on computational complexity rather than just correctness. Unlike symbolic computation engines like Mathematica or Maple which are typically based on manually curated rules, this work introduces a learning-based approach. The techniques themselves are inspired by natural language processing, particularly the use of n-gram models and recursive neural networks for structured data, drawing a compelling parallel between the syntactic structure of language and mathematical expressions.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed framework, combining attribute grammars with a learned search strategy, is well-conceived and internally consistent. The two proposed learning methods are appropriate for the task, and the experimental evaluation is thorough, testing the methods on several challenging families of expressions. The claims are well-supported by the results presented in Figure 3 and Table 3, which clearly show the benefit of the learned strategies over a random baseline, especially as the complexity of the target expressions increases. The work is presented as a complete system, and the authors are transparent about its limitations (e.g., its failure on the most complex `(RBM-2)k` family for k>5).

**Clarity:** While the overall idea is clear, the manuscript could be improved in its clarity. The description of the experimental setup, particularly the five families of expressions in Section 7.2, is quite dense and difficult to parse. Providing a more explicit, step-by-step example for one or two of the families (e.g., writing out the expressions for k=2 and k=3) would greatly help the reader understand the nature and difficulty of the tasks. The overall system has many components (grammar, numerical/learned representations, linear solver, scheduler, strategy), and a diagram illustrating how these parts interact would be beneficial.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original. The central problem of learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities is novel and ambitious. Applying machine learning, specifically NLP-inspired techniques like n-gram models and recursive neural networks, to guide search in a symbolic mathematics domain is a creative and insightful contribution. The idea of learning continuous representations for mathematical expressions is particularly interesting and opens up possibilities for applying a wide range of standard machine learning tools to symbolic domains. This work is significantly different from prior contributions in symbolic computation and program synthesis.

**Significance:** The significance and potential impact of this work are less clear, which is its main weakness. The problem formulation, while interesting from a research perspective, feels somewhat contrived. The framework is demonstrated on homogeneous polynomials of a single variable, and it is not obvious how well these techniques would generalize to the more diverse and complex expressions encountered in practical scientific computing or compiler optimization. The discovered identities are novel for the specific families of expressions constructed by the authors, but their broader utility is not established. It is questionable whether practitioners would adopt this complex framework for general-purpose computational optimization, as the target expressions seem carefully chosen to have discoverable efficient forms within the defined grammar. While the paper makes a valuable contribution to the AI problem of guided search in combinatorial spaces, its direct practical impact on how computations are optimized seems limited at this stage.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Arguments for:**
1.  **High Originality:** The paper introduces a novel problem and a creative, learning-based approach to solving it. The application of RNNs to learn representations of symbolic expressions is particularly innovative.
2.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The framework is well-designed, and the paper demonstrates convincingly that learning can successfully guide search in this complex, combinatorial space.
3.  **Potential to Inspire Future Work:** The idea of bridging symbolic computation and statistical machine learning could open up new research directions in both fields.

**Arguments against:**
1.  **Limited Practical Significance:** The problem appears somewhat niche, and the practical applicability of the discovered identities and the overall framework is unclear. The work is more of a proof-of-concept on constructed problems than a tool ready for widespread application.
2.  **Clarity Issues:** The description of the experiments is difficult to follow, which hinders a full appreciation of the results.

In summary, this is a high-quality, original, and thought-provoking paper that introduces a novel learning framework for a challenging symbolic problem. While its immediate practical significance is debatable, its value as a creative research contribution that bridges machine learning and symbolic mathematics is high. The strengths in originality and technical execution outweigh the weakness in demonstrated practical impact.
