- Proposes learning-guided search to discover computationally cheaper symbolic identities using an attribute grammar over linear-algebra and elementwise operators.  
- Two learned search strategies compared: n-gram subtree statistics and an RNN that embeds expression trees and predicts next-rule choices.  
- Equivalence tested via integer evaluations modulo a large prime and modular Gaussian elimination for linear-combination matches.  
- Experiments on five expression families show learning improves discovery over random search; n-grams typically outperform the RNN.  
- Positions work relative to theorem proving and CAS but focuses on cost-reducing equivalences rather than canonical forms.  
- Framework and pipeline are coherent; attribute grammar and constraints keep the search tractable.  
- Equivalence verification uses modular arithmetic but the paper does not quantify collision probabilities or describe safeguards (e.g., multiple primes, Schwartz–Zippel bounds).  
- Empirical gains are modest: RNN underperforms n-grams and some families (e.g., RBM-2 for k>5) remain unsolved.  
- Baselines are limited — no comparisons to CAS with cost-aware rewrites, MCTS/ILP, A*, or human heuristics.  
- Claims of “many new identities” lack formal proofs or rigorous cross-validation with multiple primes/symbolic checks.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized with helpful examples and RNN implementation details, but some reproducibility details (exact grammar, prime count, descriptor sizes) are missing.  
- Novelty lies in combining learned tree embeddings and n-grams to guide grammar-based identity discovery and focusing on computational cost.  
- Scope is narrow (homogeneous single-variable polynomials, scalar outputs); scalability to richer operators, multiple variables, or non-polynomial expressions is unclear.  
- Practical impact is not demonstrated in an end-to-end ML system or compiler optimization context.  
- Strengths: creative idea, clear integration of grammar + learning + verification, public code/data, curriculum learning, and some identities beyond brute force.  
- Weaknesses: narrow domain, modest empirical improvements, RNN underperformance, limited baselines, unverifed equivalences, and possible overkill versus hand-crafted rules.  
- Key questions for authors: how collision probabilities are bounded/mitigated, comparison to CAS, sensitivity to grammar and descriptor size, and policy generalization to new operators/variables.  
- Suggested improvements: strengthen verification (multiple primes/symbolic checks), broaden baselines (CAS, MCTS, A*), expand operator/variable scope and applications, provide ablations, and improve the RNN (tree-LSTM/GNN/hybrid).  
- Recommendation: slightly below threshold — interesting and novel but preliminary due to restricted domain, limited empirical wins, and insufficient baselines/verification.  
- Reviewer confidence: Medium.
