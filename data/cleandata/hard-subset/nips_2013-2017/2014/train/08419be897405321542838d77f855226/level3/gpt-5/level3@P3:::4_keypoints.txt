- Proposes learning-guided search over an attribute grammar using n-gram and RNN policies to find algebraically equivalent but cheaper symbolic expressions.
- Uses numeric matching via modular descriptors and forms complex identities as linear combinations by solving modular linear systems.
- Novelty: introduces learned continuous embeddings and data-driven search policy for symbolic algebra, distinct from rule-based CAS.
- Potential impact: could augment CAS/compilers and help ML algorithms by automatically replacing costly patterns with cheaper identities.
- Empirical promise: n-gram/RNN policies find identities beyond random/brute-force and embeddings achieve low equivalence-class classification error.
- Reproducibility: code and data are promised.
- Limited scope: restricted to homogeneous single-variable polynomials with scalar outputs; extension to multiple variables/non-polynomial ops is not shown.
- Complexity mismatch: search does not explicitly enforce or guarantee low-complexity solutions; complexity control is ad hoc in experiments.
- Clarity/organization issues: pipeline components and assumptions are scattered and hard to follow; Section 2 leaves restrictions insufficiently justified.
- Technical gaps: typing/dimensionality constraints for grammar rules and RNN encoding are not fully formalized (e.g., RBM-1 appears ill-defined for some cases).
- Evaluation limitations: lacks comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., Mathematica/Maple), collision analysis for modular descriptors, and consistent reporting of actual computational cost.
- Methodologically sound as a proof-of-concept but needs objective encoding of computational cost and guarantees on descriptor collision probability.
- RNN training details are helpful, but more ablations (curriculum, dropout, initialization) and generalization tests are needed.
- Presentation improvements needed: provide a formal typed grammar, pseudocode/flow diagrams, and a consolidated complexity model.
- Unclear whether scheduler or grammar/representation is the main bottleneck; needs formal or empirical justification of restrictions.
- Originality: novel combination of attribute grammars, learned embeddings, and learning-guided search for discovering computational identities.
- Significance: promising direction but immediate impact limited by scope and missing baselines; potential grows with broader extensions.
- Suggestion: incorporate attribute-cost into policy, use A*-like search with admissible heuristics, or add grammar-level complexity budgets.
- Suggestion: formalize types/dimensions and ensure RNN/search respect operator preconditions.
- Suggestion: improve correctness/robustness by using multiple primes for descriptors and verifying final identities with a CAS or symbolic proof.
- Suggestion: add baselines (Mathematica/Maple), report operation counts and runtimes, and run ablations comparing random, n-gram, RNN.
- Suggestion: reorganize exposition into clear sections (problem, grammar, equality testing, search, linear combinations, experiments).
- Minor issues/typos noted (e.g., “has contains”, “is is”); clarify vector/matrix sizes and RBM definitions.
- Pros for acceptance: innovative approach, demonstrated nontrivial identities, and promising empirical results.
- Cons for acceptance: restricted scope, lack of explicit complexity control, organizational/specification clarity issues, and missing baselines.
- Overall: creative and promising but needs clearer specification, stronger complexity-aware search, and comparative evaluation to be a strong contribution.
