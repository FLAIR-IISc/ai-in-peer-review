This paper explores the application of machine learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities. The authors introduce an attribute grammar framework to represent symbolic expressions and aim to find equivalent expressions with lower complexity. Recognizing the exponential search space, they propose two learning approaches – an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN) – to guide the tree search process, learning from solutions to simpler expressions.  This work builds upon areas like theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming, but specifically focuses on polynomial simplification and seeks to avoid the issues of undecidability inherent in those broader fields. It also draws connections to NLP techniques such as n-gram language models and recursive neural networks for parsing, adapting them to the symbolic computation domain.

The core strength of this paper lies in its originality.  The combination of attribute grammars with machine learning for symbolic identity discovery is novel.  The authors successfully demonstrate that learning from simpler expressions *can* guide the search for more complex identities, achieving results beyond what brute-force or manual derivation could easily accomplish. The experimental results, while limited to specific expression families, show promising performance, particularly with the n-gram models. The provision of code and evaluation data (https://github.com/kkurach/math_learning) is a significant positive, enabling reproducibility and further research.  The use of integer-based descriptors to avoid floating-point precision issues is also a thoughtful detail. 

However, the paper suffers from issues with clarity. While the overall concept is sound, the description of the framework and the experimental setup is often dense and difficult to follow. Section 7.2, in particular, is poorly explained, making it challenging to understand the rationale for removing the matrix-multiply rule. The notation, while generally standard, could be improved for readability.  More detailed explanations of the RNN architecture and training process would also be beneficial. The reliance on Matlab-style syntax might be limiting for readers unfamiliar with the environment.

Regarding significance, the paper falls somewhat short. The problem itself, while interesting, feels somewhat contrived. While discovering computationally efficient identities is valuable, the limited scope – specifically focusing on homogeneous polynomials with a single matrix variable – raises concerns about the practical applicability of the approach.  The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how these techniques could be generalized to real-world computational problems where symbolic simplification is crucial. The examples, while clever, seem tailored to the framework rather than arising from naturally occurring computational bottlenecks. The fact that the best performing method is a relatively simple n-gram model further tempers the enthusiasm for the significance of the RNN component.  It's unclear whether practitioners or researchers outside of this specific niche would readily adopt these ideas. 

The quality of the technical work appears sound. The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of the relevant concepts and provide theoretical justification for their approach. The experiments are well-designed, and the results are presented clearly (albeit with some caveats regarding clarity of explanation). The careful evaluation, including comparisons to brute-force search and the inclusion of error bars, strengthens the credibility of the findings. However, the limited scope and lack of compelling practical applications hinder the overall impact.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of attribute grammars and machine learning.
*   Demonstrates that learning can effectively guide the search for efficient identities.
*   Provides a publicly available codebase for reproducibility.
*   Sound technical work with careful evaluation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited clarity and difficult to follow in places.
*   The problem seems contrived and lacks clear practical applications.
*   The significance of the results is questionable, and the best performance is achieved by a relatively simple n-gram model.
*   Limited scope of the experiments and the focus on specific expression families.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept**. While the paper is technically sound and presents a novel idea, the limitations in clarity and the questionable practical significance prevent a strong recommendation. The originality and the potential for future research are valuable, but further work is needed to demonstrate the broader applicability and impact of this approach. The authors should be encouraged to address the clarity issues and explore avenues for applying their framework to more realistic and relevant computational problems.
