This paper introduces an innovative approach to discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques. The authors propose a grammar framework for representing symbolic expressions and employ two novel learning approaches, an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN), to guide the search for efficient identities.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem definition is clearly stated. However, the problem definition appears overly restricted, as it only considers homogeneous polynomials of degree k* and single-variable expressions. While the authors acknowledge these restrictions, it is unclear how easily the approach can be extended to more general settings.

The authors' use of a recursive neural network to learn a continuous representation of symbolic expressions is a significant contribution. However, the explanation of the weight tensor W3 (of size lxlxl...) is unclear, and it is not evident if a neural tensor network is used instead of a linear network. The authors should provide more details on this aspect.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly in the areas of theorem proving, program induction, and probabilistic programming. The authors should also justify their choice of model, especially in light of Bowman's work, which shows that tensor networks often outperform RNNs in these tasks.

The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach in discovering efficient identities. However, Section 7.2 is unclear regarding whether the RNN is still used for classification and how different search strategies are evaluated. Additionally, the proposal generation in the search procedure is not clearly explained and requires more detail.

The paper contains several typos and grammatical errors throughout, which detract from the overall quality of the writing. Figure 3 incorrectly labels TNN, which should be RNN. Furthermore, there are duplicate/repeated sentences in the manuscript, including the opening sentence, which appears twice.

Despite these issues, the paper presents an interesting solution that showcases the potential of distributed, recursive neural representations in classifying algebraic identities. The authors' contributions, including the novel grammar framework, the application of machine learning techniques, and the discovery of new mathematical identities, are significant.

The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. However, the writing could be improved, and some aspects of the approach require more explanation.
