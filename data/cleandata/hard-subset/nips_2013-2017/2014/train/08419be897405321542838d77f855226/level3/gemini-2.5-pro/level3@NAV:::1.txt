This paper introduces a novel approach for discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities by framing the problem as a guided search over an attribute grammar. The core idea is to use machine learning models, specifically n-grams and a recursive neural network (RNN), to learn effective search strategies. These models are trained on solutions to simpler expressions to guide the discovery of more complex identities. This work lies at an interesting intersection of symbolic computation, program induction, and machine learning, differing from prior work by applying modern statistical learning methods to a traditionally rule-based symbolic domain.

The primary strength of this paper is its originality. Applying deep learning techniques to discover fundamental mathematical identities is a creative and thought-provoking research direction. The proposed method for learning a continuous representation of symbolic expressions using an RNN is particularly intriguing and could have broader applications. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its framework and experimental setup.

However, the work in its current form feels preliminary and raises several concerns. The main weakness lies in the empirical results, which are not particularly impressive. While the learned strategies outperform random search, the performance gains are modest, and simpler n-gram models often outperform the more sophisticated RNN. The experiments are conducted on a very limited set of five expression families, making it difficult to judge the generality of the approach. For such a restricted domain, one wonders if this complex learning machinery is a "sledgehammer to crack a nut," where hand-derived mathematical rules might prove simpler and more effective. It is unclear whether the method would scale to more general expressions or if the current results justify the significant computational overhead.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a fascinating and novel idea, it feels slightly below the threshold for acceptance at NIPS. The contribution is more of a proof-of-concept than a fully-fledged demonstration of a powerful new technique. The work would be substantially strengthened by testing on a more diverse and challenging set of mathematical problems and demonstrating a more significant performance advantage over strong, non-learning-based baselines.
