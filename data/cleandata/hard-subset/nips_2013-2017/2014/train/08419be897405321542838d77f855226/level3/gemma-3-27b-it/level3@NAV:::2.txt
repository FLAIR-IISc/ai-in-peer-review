## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper tackles the interesting, though arguably niche, problem of automatically discovering mathematically equivalent but computationally cheaper expressions. The core idea is to represent expressions as trees built using an attribute grammar and then leverage machine learning – specifically n-gram models and recursive neural networks – to guide the search for more efficient trees. This is a novel application of learning techniques to a traditionally symbolic domain, and the concept of using learned strategies to navigate the exponentially growing search space is compelling. The related work section is well-covered, appropriately situating the work within theorem proving, program induction, and NLP, and highlighting the novelty of the learning-based approach.

The paper’s strength lies in its originality and the technical soundness of the proposed methods. The use of attribute grammars provides a well-defined framework, and the experimental setup, while limited in scope, appears rigorous. The RNN-based representation of symbolic expressions is particularly noteworthy and represents a significant contribution. As demonstrated in Table 2, the classification accuracy of the RNN is high, demonstrating its ability to learn meaningful representations. The reported discovery of novel mathematical identities, while specific to the tested families, supports the feasibility of the approach.

However, the paper suffers from clarity issues. Section 7.2, in particular, is difficult to follow, and the motivations behind certain design choices (e.g., removing the matrix-multiply rule) are not always clearly articulated. While the experimental results demonstrate *that* the methods work, the significance of this achievement is questionable. The problem setup feels somewhat contrived – focusing on polynomial simplification with limited variable types – and the practical applicability to real-world computations is unclear. The RNN performs comparably to or worse than n-grams on many tasks (Figure 3), and the benefit of the more complex model isn’t fully justified. The scope of the experiments is also narrow, focusing on only a few families of expressions. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of machine learning to symbolic expression manipulation.
*   Technically sound approach with a well-defined framework.
*   Demonstrates the feasibility of learning search strategies for this domain.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited significance – the problem is artificially constrained and its real-world impact is uncertain.
*   Clarity issues hinder understanding of key aspects of the methodology.
*   RNN approach does not consistently outperform simpler methods.



**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem. However, addressing the clarity issues and providing a stronger justification for the significance of the work are crucial for elevating the paper’s impact. The authors should also consider expanding the experimental scope to demonstrate broader applicability.
