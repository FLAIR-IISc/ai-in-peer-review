Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes using machine learning to discover computationally efficient mathematical identities that are equivalent to a given target expression. Expressions are generated by an attribute grammar over linear-algebraic and elementwise operators; candidate expressions are represented either via randomized numerical descriptors over a finite field or via a learned continuous embedding from a recursive neural network (RNN). Discovery proceeds by building grammar trees and, when needed, combining multiple trees via a linear system in Zp to match the target descriptor. Because exhaustive search is exponential in the degree, the authors learn search heuristics from solutions to simpler targets: (i) an n-gram model over subtrees and (ii) a neural strategy that predicts the next rule using the RNN representation. Experiments on five families of expressions (including sums of powers of AAT, elementwise-squared variants, elementary symmetric polynomials, and RBM-inspired sums) show that learning-guided search can find some identities that reduce asymptotic cost (e.g., O(n3) to O(n2)), with n-gram models often outperforming the RNN.

This work draws on and contrasts with: symbolic algebra systems (Maple/Mathematica) that apply rule-based simplification; theorem proving and program induction, where search over symbolic structures is pervasive; probabilistic programming; and NLP work on n-grams and recursive neural networks. The specific application—neural guidance for identity discovery within an algebraic grammar—appears novel and is an interesting symbolic ML use case.

Strengths
- Novel and creative problem framing: using learned guidance to search for algebraic identities is a fresh idea at the ML–symbolic interface.
- Clear and generally well-organized presentation with useful examples; code availability is appreciated.
- The attribute-grammar formalization is clean and could be reused in other settings.
- The learned embedding achieves high accuracy on expression-equivalence classification, suggesting potential utility of continuous representations for symbolic structures.

Weaknesses and evaluation against NIPS criteria
Quality
- The empirical gains in identity discovery are modest. For the more interesting families (e.g., RBM-2), learning does not outperform random and scalability stalls at small k. For families with repetitive local structure, simple n-grams outperform the RNN, suggesting limited benefit from the heavy machinery.
- The evaluation omits strong baselines. It would be important to compare against: (a) modern computer algebra (e.g., Mathematica FullSimplify/Factor with a cost proxy); (b) standard algebraic techniques such as common-subexpression elimination, Horner-like factorizations, or Gröbner-basis-based simplification for polynomials; (c) hand-crafted rewrite systems targeted to these families. Without such comparisons, it is hard to assess the true added value of the learning-guided search.
- Verification relies on randomized testing in Zp and linear-system solving modulo p. While collisions may be rare, the paper provides no quantitative bounds or checks across multiple primes to ensure soundness, nor symbolic proofs of equivalence. A principled error analysis (or a deterministic final verification step) is needed.
- The domain is narrowly constrained (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, often a single variable; matrix–matrix multiply sometimes removed), and many identities reduce to simple telescoping patterns. Within this restricted space, task-specific rules might be a simpler and more reliable solution.
- There is no wall-clock/runtime validation that the discovered forms deliver actual speedups on real inputs and sizes (beyond asymptotic counts), nor a measurement of overhead for the search itself.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear. However: clearer specification of the grammar and cost model in the main paper (not only in a table) would help; an explicit statement of the linear-combination model’s expressivity and when multiple trees are required would be useful; and a more detailed description of the search scheduler and branching/pruning policy would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- The application of DNNs (RNNs) to guide discovery of mathematical identities is genuinely novel. The combination of attribute grammars with learned search heuristics is a reasonable and original synthesis, though each component is standard.

Significance
- As presented, the impact is limited. The method’s performance on the most challenging families is not particularly impressive, and the strongest results arise on cases where simpler statistical heuristics (n-grams) suffice. Given the narrow domain and modest empirical wins, it is unclear that the approach would scale to richer grammars or more general expressions—or that scaling is justified without stronger evidence.

Arguments for acceptance
- Interesting and timely idea that pushes on neural guidance in symbolic domains.
- Clean framework, code release, and some nontrivial identities found.
- The learned continuous representation for expressions may be of independent interest.

Arguments against acceptance
- Preliminary nature: limited scope, modest results, and lack of rigorous validation or strong baselines.
- Heavy machinery (RNNs) provides little benefit over n-grams in the reported experiments (“sledgehammer to crack a nut”).
- Unclear scalability and limited demonstration beyond a small set of target families.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add rigorous baselines using computer algebra systems and compiler-style optimizations (CSE/factorization), with a consistent cost model.
- Provide sound verification: symbolic equality checks or multi-prime certification with quantified collision bounds.
- Report real runtime speedups on standard BLAS-backed implementations, including the cost of any precomputations (e.g., sums) and memory traffic.
- Analyze failure modes (e.g., RBM-2), and characterize when learning helps (repetitive local motifs vs. global structure). Consider richer model classes or search strategies that capture long-range dependencies.
- Expand the grammar and demonstrate generalization beyond homogeneous polynomials and single-variable inputs, or justify the restriction with compelling applications.
- Ablate the training pipeline (curriculum, identity init, dropout) and quantify contributions.

Questions for the authors
- How often do false positives occur under the Zp descriptor, and did you validate identities with exact arithmetic?
- How would the approach integrate with existing CAS or compilers; can you show end-to-end speedups on ML workloads (e.g., RBMs, dropout variants)?
- Can you provide comparisons to Mathematica/Maple on your families under a cost proxy?

Recommendation
Interesting and creative work, but slightly below the NIPS acceptance bar due to limited scope, modest empirical gains, lack of strong baselines, and unclear scalability. I encourage a more thorough empirical and methodological validation along the lines above.
