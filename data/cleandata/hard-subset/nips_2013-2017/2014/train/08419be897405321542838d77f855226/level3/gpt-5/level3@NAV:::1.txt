This paper proposes a framework for discovering computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given target expression. Expressions are generated from an attribute grammar; equivalence is tested via randomized descriptors over Zp and (for complex targets) linear combinations of trees solved by modular Gaussian elimination. To make search tractable, the authors learn strategies from simpler instances: (i) an n-gram model over solution subtrees; and (ii) a recursive neural network that embeds partial trees and predicts the next rule. Experiments on several restricted families (matrix polynomials, symmetric polynomials, RBM-like sums) show learned strategies can outperform random search on some cases, though gains are modest and inconsistent. The work relates to theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic algebra; it also touches literature on symbolic regression and superoptimization.

Quality: The framework is technically sound and the learning components are implemented with care. However, the empirical evidence is preliminary. The evaluation domain is very narrow (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, often single variable), success rates on harder families are low, and the n-gram baseline often outperforms the RNN. Equivalence via randomized Zp descriptors is pragmatic but lacks formal guarantees; comparisons to established CAS simplifiers (Maple/Mathematica), algebraic rewriting, superoptimization, or GP-based symbolic regression are absent. No end-to-end runtime speedups on real computations are reported.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well organized. Pseudocode for the scheduler/strategy loop, a more explicit cost model for “complexity,” and fuller details on dataset construction and RNN training/regularization would improve reproducibility.

Originality: Applying learned models, especially RNN embeddings, to guide identity discovery is novel and interesting in the NeurIPS context. The connection to prior work could better position against symbolic regression and code optimization/search.

Significance: The potential impact is high if the approach scales, but current results are limited in scope and performance. Given the constrained grammar, hand-derived rules or dynamic programming may suffice (“sledgehammer to crack a nut”), and scalability to richer operators and multivariate settings is unclear.

Pros:
- Novel problem framing; clean grammar-based setup
- Interesting use of learning to guide discrete search
- Code/data availability; some nontrivial identities found

Cons:
- Narrow domain and modest empirical gains
- Weak scaling on harder families; RNN underperforms n-grams
- Missing strong baselines and real speedup measurements
- Equivalence test lacks formal guarantees

Recommendation: Weak reject. I find the direction exciting but slightly below the bar. Strengthen by broadening domains and operators, adding rigorous baselines (CAS, rewriting, symbolic regression, superoptimization), reporting real-world speedups, providing stronger equivalence guarantees, and deeper analysis of failure modes and scalability.
