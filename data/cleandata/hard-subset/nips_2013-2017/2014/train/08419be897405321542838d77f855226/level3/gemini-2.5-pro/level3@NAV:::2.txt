This paper addresses the problem of discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities for a given symbolic expression. The authors propose a framework using an attribute grammar to search the vast space of equivalent expressions. To make this search tractable, they introduce and evaluate two learning-based strategies to guide exploration: an n-gram model over sub-expression sequences from previously discovered solutions, and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous vector representation of expression trees. This work creatively connects concepts from program induction and automated theorem proving with modern machine learning techniques often seen in natural language processing, applying them to a novel symbolic mathematics domain.

The paper’s primary strengths are its high originality and technical quality. The application of machine learning to guide symbolic search is a novel and interesting direction that differs significantly from traditional, rule-based systems like Mathematica. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing the proposed methods against a random baseline on several non-trivial expression families. The authors are commendably transparent about their results, including instances where methods fail.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The clarity is limited; the writing is dense, and the experimental setup in Section 7.2 is particularly difficult to parse, making it challenging to fully appreciate the results. More importantly, the significance of the work is questionable. The problem of simplifying specific families of homogeneous polynomials feels contrived, and it is unclear if this approach would generalize or be adopted for real-world computational efficiency. The practical usefulness of the proposed search methods for producing efficient expressions remains unconvincing. It is also noteworthy that the simpler n-gram model outperforms the more sophisticated RNN on two of the three main tasks, which slightly diminishes the impact of the more complex contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Highly original application of ML techniques to a symbolic domain.
*   Technically sound execution with solid experimental validation.
*   The demonstration of learning continuous representations for symbolic expressions is an interesting contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The problem formulation appears narrow, leading to low perceived significance and unclear practical impact.
*   The paper suffers from clarity issues, hindering accessibility and understanding.

In summary, while this is a high-quality and original piece of research, its limited significance and issues with clarity are significant concerns.
