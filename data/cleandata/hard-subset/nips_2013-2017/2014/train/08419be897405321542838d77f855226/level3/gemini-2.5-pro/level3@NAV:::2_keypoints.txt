- Problem: discover computationally efficient mathematical identities for symbolic expressions using an attribute grammar to search equivalent expressions.
- Methods: propose two learning-based search guides — an n-gram model over sub-expression sequences and a recursive neural network (RNN) embedding expression trees.
- Novelty: creatively combines program induction and automated theorem proving ideas with ML/NLP for symbolic mathematics.
- Strength: high originality and technical quality, introducing ML-guided symbolic search unlike traditional rule-based systems.
- Strength: comprehensive experimental evaluation against a random baseline across several non-trivial expression families and transparency about failures.
- Strength: learning continuous vector representations for symbolic expressions is an interesting contribution.
- Weakness: clarity and presentation are limited; writing is dense and Section 7.2’s experimental setup is hard to parse.
- Weakness: significance is questionable because the focus on simplifying specific families of homogeneous polynomials seems narrow and potentially contrived.
- Weakness: practical usefulness is unconvincing and generalizability to real-world computational efficiency is unclear.
- Observation: the simpler n-gram model outperforms the more complex RNN on two of three main tasks, reducing the impact of the RNN contribution.
- For acceptance: highly original application of ML to a symbolic domain, technically sound, and novel continuous representations.
- Against acceptance: narrow problem formulation with low perceived significance and persistent clarity/accessibility issues.
- Summary: high-quality and original work but limited significance and clarity concerns are significant drawbacks.
