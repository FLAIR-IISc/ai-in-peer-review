## Review of "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

This paper explores a novel and interesting application of machine learning – discovering efficient mathematical identities. The authors propose an attribute grammar framework to represent symbolic expressions and then leverage both n-gram models and recursive neural networks to guide a search for computationally cheaper equivalent formulations of a target expression. This approach is particularly appealing as it attempts to automate a process traditionally reliant on human intuition and mathematical expertise. The connection to related work in theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation is well-established, and the authors correctly position their work as distinct from existing rule-based systems by introducing a learning component.

However, despite the novelty of the idea, the paper feels preliminary and falls short of the standards typically expected for NIPS publication. The primary weakness lies in the limited scope and performance of the method. The experiments are restricted to a relatively narrow set of expressions (polynomials with a single matrix variable), and even within this domain, the reported success rates are modest, particularly for more complex expressions. The fact that simple n-gram models often outperform the more sophisticated RNN suggests that the learned representations aren’t capturing sufficiently complex relationships to drive significant improvements. 

Furthermore, the authors acknowledge that hand-derived mathematical rules might be sufficient for the limited problem space they’ve defined – essentially arguing that they’ve used a “sledgehammer to crack a nut.” This raises concerns about the scalability and broader applicability of the approach. While the authors mention potential extensions to more general expressions, it’s unclear whether the current methodology would scale effectively, or if the computational cost of expanding the grammar and training the models would outweigh the benefits. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the learned approach offers a substantial advantage over more traditional methods, even within its constrained domain.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of machine learning to mathematical identity discovery.
*   Well-defined problem and clear presentation of the attribute grammar framework.
*   Demonstration of learning to guide the search process, even if modest in performance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of experiments and modest performance.
*   Questionable scalability and justification for the complexity of the approach.
*   Potential for simpler, hand-derived solutions within the defined domain.
*   RNN performance is not significantly better than n-gram models.



Overall, while the paper presents an interesting idea, the limited results and lack of compelling evidence for scalability make it a weak submission for NIPS. I recommend rejection at this time, but encourage the authors to explore broader problem domains, investigate more powerful learning architectures, and provide a more rigorous comparison to existing symbolic computation techniques.
