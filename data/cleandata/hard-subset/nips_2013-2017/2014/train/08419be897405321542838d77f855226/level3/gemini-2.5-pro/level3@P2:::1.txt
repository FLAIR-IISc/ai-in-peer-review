This paper proposes a novel framework for automatically discovering computationally efficient versions of symbolic mathematical expressions. The core idea is to define a space of possible expressions using an attribute grammar and then search this space for an expression that is analytically equivalent to a given target but has a lower computational cost. As brute-force search is intractable, the main contribution is the use of machine learning models—specifically n-gram models and a recursive neural network (RNN)—to learn a search strategy. These models are trained on solutions found for simpler expressions and then used to guide the search for more complex targets. The paper also introduces a method for learning a continuous vector representation of symbolic expressions using an RNN, analogous to word embeddings in natural language processing. This work relates to the fields of automated theorem proving, program induction, and symbolic computation, but distinguishes itself by applying statistical machine learning techniques to guide the search, rather than relying on hand-crafted rules or heuristics.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is its novelty. Applying modern machine learning techniques, particularly deep neural networks, to the symbolic problem of discovering mathematical identities is a creative and interesting research direction. The synthesis of attribute grammars, learned representations, and guided search is a unique approach to a long-standing problem in computer science.

2.  **Learned Representation:** The idea of learning a continuous, distributed representation for symbolic expressions via an RNN is a valuable contribution in its own right. If effective, this could open the door to applying a wide range of machine learning tools to symbolic domains, much as word embeddings have revolutionized natural language processing. The high classification accuracy reported in Table 2 suggests this representation is indeed capturing meaningful structural properties of the expressions.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors do a good job of explaining their framework, from the grammar and expression representations to the different search strategies. The problem is well-defined, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Preliminary Nature and Limited Scope:** The work feels more like a proof-of-concept than a fully developed method. The experiments are conducted on a very restricted set of expressions: five families of homogeneous polynomials involving a single variable. It is difficult to assess from these results how the approach would generalize to a broader class of mathematical expressions involving multiple variables, different data types, or non-polynomial operators (e.g., trigonometric, exponential, conditional).

2.  **Unconvincing Performance:** The success of the method in discovering new identities is not particularly impressive. While the learned strategies outperform random search, the improvement is incremental rather than transformative. For the most complex family, (RBM-2)k, none of the learning methods outperform the random baseline for k > 5. Furthermore, the simpler n-gram models frequently outperform the more sophisticated and computationally expensive RNN model. This undermines the claim that advanced deep learning models are key to solving this task and suggests that the local, repetitive patterns found in the solutions are captured just as well by simple counting methods.

3.  **Potentially Over-engineered Solution:** Given the limited domain of expressions explored, the proposed learning framework may be an example of using a "sledgehammer to crack a nut." For these specific polynomial families, it is plausible that a set of hand-derived rewrite rules or a more traditional symbolic algebra approach could have discovered these identities more efficiently and robustly. The paper lacks a comparison to such a baseline, making it hard to judge the practical utility of the complex learning machinery.

4.  **Scalability Concerns:** While the paper successfully uses learning to find identities for higher-degree polynomials than random search can, it is unclear if the approach truly addresses the underlying combinatorial explosion. The search space still grows exponentially, and the learning method only serves as a heuristic to navigate it. The paper does not provide strong evidence that this heuristic would remain effective as the grammar becomes more complex and the space of possible expressions grows.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a highly novel and creative application of machine learning to a fundamental problem in symbolic computation.
*   The concept of a learned continuous representation for mathematical expressions is an interesting contribution with potential for broader impact.
*   The work explores a challenging problem at the intersection of machine learning and artificial intelligence, which is a good fit for the NIPS audience.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental validation is narrow and feels preliminary, making it difficult to judge the generalizability and robustness of the method.
*   The performance gains over baselines are modest, and the simpler n-gram models often work as well or better than the more complex RNN, weakening the paper's core deep learning story.
*   The lack of comparison to non-learning-based symbolic manipulation methods makes it hard to assess the practical significance of the approach for the chosen problems.
*   The work raises more questions about scalability and generalizability than it answers.

In summary, this paper presents a fascinating and highly original idea. However, the execution feels preliminary, and the experimental results are not strong enough to demonstrate a significant breakthrough. The limited scope and modest performance gains leave the reader questioning the method's true power and scalability. While the direction is promising, the work in its current form feels slightly below the high bar for publication at NIPS. I would be more enthusiastic about this paper if it demonstrated success on a more diverse set of problems or showed a more compelling performance advantage over stronger, more conventional baselines.
