

This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning techniques applied to attribute grammars. The authors frame the problem as finding alternative symbolic expressions with lower computational complexity that are analytically equivalent to a target expression. They introduce two learning approaches to guide the search through the combinatorial space of possible expressions: an n-gram model that learns from sub-expressions in previously discovered solutions, and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous representations of symbolic expressions to predict next grammar rules.

The work builds on attribute grammars [14], which have been used in compiler construction and other domains, but applies them to a novel problem of discovering mathematical identities. It also draws inspiration from NLP techniques, particularly n-gram language models and recursive neural networks used for parsing [22, 23]. The paper makes a significant contribution by applying machine learning to symbolic computation, a domain where statistical approaches have been largely unexplored.

The technical quality of the paper is strong. The authors provide clear theoretical motivation for their approach, including a formal problem statement and proper experimental design. The representation learning component (Section 4.2) is particularly well-executed, with careful attention to training details like curriculum learning and weight initialization. The experiments (Section 7) demonstrate that both learning approaches significantly outperform random search for complex targets, with n-gram models achieving better results than the RNN on two of the three families of expressions tested (Fig. 3). The classification accuracy for the learned representation (≤5% error for k=3-6) demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach to continuous embedding of symbolic expressions.

The originality of the paper is high. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to apply learning techniques to discover mathematical identities through grammar-based search. While related to theorem proving [5, 9, 11] and program induction [18, 28], the authors clearly differentiate their approach by focusing on computational complexity reduction for polynomial expressions within a manageable search space.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues, particularly in Section 7.2 which describes the experimental setup. The description of the five expression families and their complexities is confusing, making it difficult to fully appreciate the results. The paper would benefit from clearer organization and more intuitive explanations of the technical details.

The significance of the work is questionable. The authors claim that the discovered identities could potentially be used to "give a closed-form version of dropout" or "compute the RBM partition function efficiently," but they provide no concrete evidence of this practical utility. The problem appears contrived, and the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate why these specific identities would be valuable in real-world applications. The most challenging family (RBM-2) shows no meaningful improvement over random search for k>5, further undermining the practical significance.

The practical usefulness of the proposed search methods remains unclear. While the paper shows that learning can guide the search for efficient identities, it doesn't provide strong motivation for why this would be adopted in practice. The framework is limited to a specific class of polynomial expressions with restrictive assumptions (single matrix variable, scalar output), which limits its applicability to broader mathematical problems.

The paper's contributions are well-motivated theoretically but lack compelling evidence of practical impact. The technical execution is strong, and the approach represents a novel combination of ideas from grammars, machine learning, and symbolic computation. However, the significance argument is weak, and the paper would benefit from a stronger connection to real-world applications where computational efficiency matters.

Pro arguments for acceptance: Strong technical quality, clear novelty in applying learning to symbolic computation, promising results on the defined problem, effective representation learning.

Con arguments against acceptance: Questionable significance, unclear practical utility, poor clarity in key sections, limited scope of the problem addressed.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with interesting ideas that push the boundaries of applying machine learning to symbolic domains. However, the lack of compelling evidence for practical significance and the unclear presentation of the experimental results make it difficult to recommend acceptance. The work represents a valuable contribution to the theoretical exploration of learning for symbolic computation, but it falls short of demonstrating significant impact for the NIPS community. The authors should strengthen their significance argument by providing concrete examples of how the discovered identities would be used in practice, and improve the clarity of their experimental presentation.
