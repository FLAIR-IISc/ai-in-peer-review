

# Review for "Learning to Discover Efficient Mathematical Identities"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The authors propose an attribute grammar-based approach to represent symbolic expressions, where they search for alternative expressions equivalent to a target but with lower computational complexity. To overcome the combinatorial explosion of the search space, they introduce two learning-based search strategies: n-gram models and recursive neural networks trained to predict promising grammar rules. The paper demonstrates their approach on several families of polynomial expressions, claiming to discover identities that reduce computational complexity from O(n^3) to O(n^2) or lower. The work draws inspiration from NLP techniques (n-grams, recursive neural networks) and applies them to symbolic computation, a novel application at NIPS.

## Strengths
The paper presents a genuinely novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation, bridging a gap between traditional symbolic manipulation systems and statistical learning approaches. The use of attribute grammars provides a structured representation of mathematical expressions, while the learning-based search strategies offer a promising alternative to brute-force enumeration. The authors demonstrate the approach on specific problem classes (e.g., matrix polynomial expressions) where they successfully discover identities that reduce computational complexity. The paper's technical execution is sound, with thorough experimental validation across multiple expression families. The continuous representation for symbolic expressions via recursive neural networks is particularly interesting and potentially broadly applicable.

## Weaknesses
The primary limitation of the work is its highly restricted scope. The paper focuses exclusively on homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree k with a single matrix or vector variable, which significantly limits the applicability of the discovered identities. The authors acknowledge this restriction but provide insufficient justification for why these constraints are necessary for the approach to work. The problem statement seeks lower computational complexity, yet the framework does not enforce complexity constraints during search, potentially yielding solutions with higher complexity than the target. This is a significant technical gap that weakens the paper's contribution.

The paper's organization is confusing, making it difficult to follow the progression of ideas. Key concepts like the scheduler, strategy, and how they interact are not clearly explained. The authors make several claims about the framework's capabilities without providing sufficient empirical evidence to support them. For instance, the assertion that the framework can discover identities "beyond reach of brute-force search, or human derivation" lacks proper comparative analysis with existing symbolic computation approaches.

There are also technical concerns regarding the representation of mathematical expressions. The RBM-1 family example in the supplementary material appears invalid for k=1, n=1, raising questions about the correctness of the framework's application to some problem classes. The paper would benefit from more rigorous mathematical justification of the constraints and limitations.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound in its approach to using machine learning to guide symbolic search, but it has significant limitations in scope and theoretical justification. The experimental results are well-documented, but the framework's failure to enforce complexity constraints during search is a major quality issue. The authors do not sufficiently address the limitations of their approach.

**Clarity**: The paper suffers from poor organization and unclear explanations of key concepts. The relationship between the attribute grammar, the scheduler, and the learning strategies is not well articulated. The paper would benefit from a clearer structure that better explains the progression of ideas and the role of each component.

**Originality**: The application of n-gram models and recursive neural networks to discover mathematical identities is novel, particularly the use of continuous representations for symbolic expressions. However, the restricted scope of the problem domain limits the broader impact of this contribution. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from related work in theorem proving and symbolic computation.

**Significance**: While the approach has potential to benefit symbolic software libraries, the highly restricted problem domain significantly reduces the significance of the work. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate how the discovered identities could be practically applied to real-world problems in machine learning or mathematics. The potential impact is promising but currently speculative due to the narrow scope.

## Arguments for and against acceptance

**For acceptance**: The paper presents a novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation, demonstrates a technically sound approach with promising results on specific problem classes, and introduces an interesting continuous representation for symbolic expressions. The work opens up new possibilities for applying machine learning to mathematical discovery.

**Against acceptance**: The highly restricted scope of the problem domain (single matrix/vector, homogeneous polynomials of fixed degree) significantly limits the applicability of the discovered identities. The framework does not enforce computational complexity constraints during search, undermining the core problem statement. The paper lacks sufficient justification for its limitations and does not adequately compare with existing symbolic computation approaches.

## Recommendation
The paper presents an interesting and novel application of machine learning to symbolic computation, but its limited scope and lack of technical justification for the constraints significantly weaken its contribution. While the approach has potential, the current work does not demonstrate sufficient significance or generalizability to warrant acceptance at NIPS. I recommend rejection with the suggestion that the authors expand the scope of their framework, provide better justification for their constraints, and demonstrate how their approach can be applied to more general mathematical problems.
