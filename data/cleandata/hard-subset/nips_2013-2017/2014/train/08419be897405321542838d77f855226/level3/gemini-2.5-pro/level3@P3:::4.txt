This paper introduces a novel and intriguing approach to discovering computationally efficient mathematical identities using machine learning. The core idea is to represent symbolic expressions within an attribute grammar framework and then search this space for an expression that is analytically equivalent to a given target expression but has lower computational complexity. Since the search space grows exponentially, the authors propose learning a search strategy from solutions to simpler problems. They explore two main learning methods: an n-gram model over grammar rules and a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns a continuous representation of symbolic expressions. This work connects ideas from symbolic computation, program induction, and natural language processing. While symbolic engines like Mathematica and Maple use rule-based simplification, this paper's contribution is the application of statistical learning to guide the discovery process, a largely unexplored direction. The use of an RNN to create embeddings for symbolic expressions is particularly novel, drawing a parallel to successful representation learning for words and sentences in NLP.

The paper has several significant strengths. The primary contribution is its originality: framing the discovery of mathematical identities as a learning-guided search problem is a creative and promising idea. It opens a new avenue for applying machine learning to a fundamentally symbolic domain. The potential impact is substantial; if this approach can be generalized, it could be integrated into symbolic software libraries or compilers to automatically find optimized computational pathways, a task that currently relies on extensive, manually-curated rule sets. The technical implementation is also noteworthy, particularly the learned continuous representation for expressions via an RNN. The reported classification accuracy suggests these representations effectively capture expression equivalence, which could be a valuable tool for other symbolic reasoning tasks. The experimental results, showing the discovery of non-trivial identities for complex polynomial families beyond the reach of brute-force search, successfully demonstrate the power of the learning-based guidance.

Despite its novelty, the paper has several weaknesses that limit its current impact. The most significant is the limited scope of the problem addressed. The framework is restricted to homogeneous polynomials of a single variable with a scalar output. While the authors acknowledge these restrictions, they do not provide a convincing argument or a clear path for extending the method to more general mathematical expressions (e.g., involving trigonometric functions, inequalities, or multiple distinct variables in complex interactions). Without a discussion of extensibility, it is difficult to assess whether this is a general-purpose tool or a highly specialized method for a narrow class of problems.

A second major issue is a mismatch between the problem statement and the proposed algorithm. The stated goal is to find expressions of lower computational complexity, yet the search strategies (n-gram, RNN) are trained only to find *any* equivalent expression, not necessarily a low-complexity one. The complexity attribute in the grammar is not used to guide the search. The authors circumvent this in some experiments by manually removing high-complexity rules (e.g., matrix-matrix multiplication) from the grammar. A more principled approach would be to incorporate complexity directly into the search objective, for instance by penalizing high-complexity rules or terminating search paths that exceed a complexity budget. The authors should consider how their learning framework could be adapted to explicitly search for low-complexity solutions.

The paper's clarity and organization could also be significantly improved. The overall system has several interacting components—the grammar, the numerical/learned representation, the scheduler, the search strategy, and the linear combination solver—but the relationship and data flow between them are difficult to follow. A diagram illustrating the complete pipeline, from a target expression to a discovered identity, would greatly aid reader comprehension. Furthermore, the restrictions outlined in Section 2 lack a clear justification. It is unclear whether these limitations are fundamental to the approach or merely simplifications for the experiments. A more formal discussion of why this particular subclass of expressions was chosen is needed.

Finally, there are some technical details that require clarification. For the discovered identities to be truly useful, their domain of validity must be clear. For instance, are there constraints on the dimensions of the input vectors or matrices for an identity to hold? The provided example concerning the RBM-1 expression in the supplement being invalid for k=1, n=1 highlights this concern. The authors should be more precise about the conditions under which their discovered formulas are valid.

In summary, this paper presents a highly original and thought-provoking application of machine learning to symbolic mathematics. The core idea is strong and the initial results are promising. However, the work in its current form feels more like a proof-of-concept than a complete piece of research, due to the very restricted problem setting, the lack of a clear path to generalization, and issues with clarity. While the novelty is high, the limited scope and the disconnect between the search objective and the complexity goal are significant concerns.
