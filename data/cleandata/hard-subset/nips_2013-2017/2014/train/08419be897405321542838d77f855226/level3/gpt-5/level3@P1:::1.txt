This paper proposes a learning-guided framework for discovering computationally efficient identities equivalent to a given symbolic target. Expressions are represented by attribute grammars whose productions are annotated with computational complexity; candidate expressions are built as trees and compared for equivalence using descriptors computed by evaluating expressions over random inputs modulo a large prime. Because the search space grows exponentially, the authors train search policies from solutions to simpler targets: (i) n-gram statistics over subtrees, and (ii) a recursive neural network (RNN) that learns continuous embeddings of expression trees and a softmax policy to select the next rule. On several families (e.g., ∑(AA^T)^k, ∑((A.*A)A^T)^k, symmetric polynomials, and RBM-inspired sums), the learning-guided strategies discover identities that reduce complexity (e.g., from O(nmp) to O(n(m+p)) in Example 1; from O(n^3) to O(n^2) or O(nm) in other cases). The n-gram models often outperform the RNN policy; on the hardest family (RBM-2), learning does not scale beyond k=5 under a 10-minute budget. The paper positions itself relative to theorem proving, program induction, probabilistic programming, and NLP-inspired tree models (e.g., Socher et al.), and releases code.

Quality
- Positives: The framework is technically coherent, with careful engineering of two representations (integer descriptors modulo p for robust equivalence checking; a learned RNN embedding for tree classification/policy). The learning-to-search setup is clearly defined (scheduler/strategy separation), and examples demonstrate nontrivial reductions in complexity. The empirical protocol (progressively increasing k and bootstrapping from prior solutions) is reasonable for this domain. Implementation details for the RNN (identity initialization, ReLUs, curriculum, dropout) are thoughtful and cite relevant precedents.
- Concerns: The evaluation is narrow and the performance is modest. On many families, simple n-grams outperform the RNN policy; on the most challenging family, no approach scales. Success is measured as fraction of runs that find a solution within a fixed time, without reporting search effort (nodes expanded), wall-clock improvements for concrete sizes n,m,p, or comparisons to stronger non-learning baselines (e.g., heuristic search, MCTS, A* with admissible heuristics, SMT/CAS-guided rewrites). For two matrix families, matrix-matrix multiply is removed from the grammar to force O(n^2) solutions, which alters the search space in a way that may bias results. Claims of “previously undiscovered” identities are not substantiated (e.g., via CAS cross-check or citations to algebraic literature). The equivalence check via random evaluation modulo p is practical, but the paper does not quantify collision risk or provide a formal verification step for discovered identities.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The grammar-based construction, search strategies, and learned embeddings are described with concrete examples (Examples 1–2). However, the core grammar (Table 1) and complexity attributes are referenced but not fully detailed in the main text, limiting reproducibility for readers who do not consult the supplement. More explicit reporting of hyperparameters (descriptor length N, choice of prime p, proof/collision guarantees), scheduler details (beam width, backtracking), and resource usage would improve clarity. Figures summarizing discovered identities are deferred to the supplement; including a few representative ones in the main paper would help.

Originality
- Applying learned policies (n-gram and RNN over expression trees) to discover algebraic identities is novel relative to prior work in theorem proving heuristics (e.g., Bridge et al.), program induction with grammars, and CAS simplification, which typically do not target computational complexity reduction nor use learned guidance. The use of an RNN to learn continuous embeddings for symbolic expressions parallels NLP tree models but in a distinct domain. That said, the n-gram policy’s success suggests the discovered solutions often reflect local repetitive patterns, and the contribution of the deep model to identity discovery is currently limited.

Significance
- If scalable, a learning-guided search for efficient identities could impact compiler optimization, fast approximate inference (e.g., RBM partition functions), and symbolic computation. In the present form, the domain is quite constrained (homogeneous polynomials, scalar outputs, single variable for most experiments), many successes hinge on rule restrictions that preclude expensive operations, and the most interesting family remains unsolved beyond small k. As such, the demonstrated advance is intriguing but preliminary for NIPS standards.

Strengths
- Clear, principled framework combining attribute grammars, equivalence checking, and learning to search.
- Novel application of RNNs to learn embeddings of symbolic expressions; solid classification accuracy on equivalence classes.
- Concrete examples with nontrivial complexity reductions; code and data released.

Weaknesses
- Limited scope of expressions and grammar; removal of key rules in some experiments.
- Modest empirical gains: n-grams outperform RNN; hardest family not solved beyond k=5.
- Evaluation metrics focus on success rate under time budget; lack of stronger baselines and ablations (e.g., beam search, MCTS, heuristic guidance without learning).
- No formal verification or literature cross-check to support novelty of identities; limited discussion of collision probabilities in Zp descriptors.

Suggestions for improvement
- Broaden the grammar (multiple variables, richer operators) and report scaling behavior with explicit search metrics (nodes expanded, branching factor).
- Add stronger baselines: heuristic/A* search with hand-designed cost-to-go, MCTS, CAS-assisted rewrites, and learned bandit/RL policies; report ablations (pretraining vs no pretraining).
- Quantify practical speedups on real matrix sizes and compare to vendor BLAS/CAS simplifications.
- Provide formal verification of discovered identities (e.g., polynomial identity testing with Schwartz–Zippel bounds, CAS proofs) and a literature audit to substantiate novelty claims.
- Analyze failure modes of the RNN policy and explore better policy learning (e.g., policy gradients over trees, value functions).

Arguments for acceptance
- Interesting and novel problem formulation with a plausible path toward impactful applications.
- Demonstrates that simple learned guidance can meaningfully expand the reachable search space.
- Bridges ML and symbolic computation with a reusable representation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Experimental scope and results are limited; deep model underperforms simple n-grams.
- Evaluation lacks strong baselines, formal verification, and practical benchmarks.
- Scalability to general expressions is unclear; current successes may be obtainable via hand-crafted heuristics.

Overall assessment: Interesting and creative direction, but the current empirical evidence and scope feel preliminary; slightly below the acceptance threshold.
