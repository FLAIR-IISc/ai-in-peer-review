- Introduces a novel approach to discovering efficient mathematical identities using attribute grammars guided by learned models.
- Core idea: use machine learning to navigate the exponentially large space of symbolic expressions.
- Proposes two prediction methods: an n-gram model and a recursive neural network (RNN).
- Builds on related areas (theorem proving, program induction, probabilistic programming) and avoids undecidability by restricting to homogeneous polynomials.
- Clever use of attribute grammars and adaptation of NLP techniques (n-gram, RNN) to symbolic expression search.
- Acknowledges prior symbolic computation systems (Maple, Mathematica) and contrasts their rule-based, non-learning approaches.
- Technical approach appears sound and experiments (Figure 3) demonstrate effectiveness.
- Provides concrete examples showing complexity reductions and supplies code and data for reproducibility.
- Clarity is a significant weakness, especially Section 7.2 on removing the matrix-multiply rule and its effects.
- RNN training details are unclear (dropout implementation and handling of input vector 'a').
- Originality is high due to combining attribute grammars, learning-guided search, and RNNs for symbolic expressions.
- Significance is limited: the problem feels contrived and narrowly scoped (homogeneous polynomials, single matrix/vector).
- Unclear generalization to more complex expressions or real-world optimization problems.
- Practical usefulness is questionable given modest complexity improvements and model training overhead.
- RNN underperforms the simpler n-gram model, raising doubts about its added value.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions; improve clarity, better articulate impact and limitations, and explain/address why n-gram outperforms the RNN.
