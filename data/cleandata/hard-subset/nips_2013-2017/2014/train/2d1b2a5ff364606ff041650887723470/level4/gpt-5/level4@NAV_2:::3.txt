The paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) within an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) to address blind source separation. The authors formulate a convex quadratic program for a relaxed AFHMM+SAC inference problem and demonstrate strong performance on both synthetic and real-world energy disaggregation data.

Pros:
- Clear exposition with a solid mathematical treatment.
- Strong problem motivation.
- Empirical results show notable gains on NDE and SAE.

Areas for improvement:
- The comparisons emphasize improvements over plain AFHMM and AFAMAP, but AFHMM+SAC leverages additional prior information (aggregate constraints), whereas the baselines do not. This raises fairness concerns. Are there alternative methods that could incorporate similar SAC information, perhaps with less efficient solvers, to enable apples-to-apples comparisons?
- The trends in Figure 1 are hard to interpret. For mismatches larger than about 10^4 between the provided aggregate and the true aggregate, AFAMAP appears to outperform AFHMM+SAC. Does this imply that poorly specified constraints can significantly degrade performance? The discussion seems to suggest uniformly better outcomes, which could be misleading without a nuanced robustness analysis.
- I expected a broader class of constraints to be integrated. Examples include mutual exclusivity (e.g., heater and cooler not simultaneously active), global activity limits (e.g., no more than 3 of 10 bulbs on at once), and duration constraints (e.g., a grinder rarely runs longer than 10 minutes). There are many such domain constraints one might specify; can the proposed approach be extended to handle these richer constraint types?

Overall, the paper is well written and provides compelling motivation for SACs in this setting. A natural avenue for future work is extending the framework to encompass a wider variety of constraints.
