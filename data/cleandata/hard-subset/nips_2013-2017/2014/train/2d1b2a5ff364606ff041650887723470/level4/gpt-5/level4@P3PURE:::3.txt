Summary
The paper proposes an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) augmented with signal aggregate constraints (SAC) to address blind source separation. The authors formulate a convex quadratic program for a relaxed AFHMM with SAC and demonstrate promising results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

Strengths
- The paper is well written and grounded in solid mathematical foundations.
- The problem motivation is clear and compelling.
- Empirical results show notable improvements in performance metrics, specifically NDE and SAE.

Areas for Improvement and Questions
- Fairness of comparisons: The reported improvements are shown relative to AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP. Both baselines appear to use less information than the proposed SACAFHMM, which makes the comparison potentially unfair. Are there alternative approaches that could also leverage SAC (even if they are computationally less efficient) to provide a more apples-to-apples comparison?
- Interpretation of Figure 1: The figure is not entirely clear. It appears that for distances greater than 10^4, AFAMAP outperforms SACAFHMM. Does this imply that poorly specified or incorrect constraints can lead to significantly worse performance? The accompanying discussion seems to suggest that results are always better with SAC, which could be misleading. Please clarify the conditions under which SAC helps versus hurts, and explain how sensitive the method is to constraint quality.
- Constraint expressiveness: I expected to see more complex constraints incorporated. Examples include:
  - Mutual exclusivity (e.g., a person cannot use the heater and cooler at the same time).
  - Cardinality constraints (e.g., no more than 3 out of 10 bulbs on simultaneously).
  - Duration constraints (e.g., grinders operating for less than 10 minutes).
  There are many practical constraints of these types. Is it possible to extend the algorithm to support such constraints as well? If so, what changes to the formulation or optimization would be required, and what are the computational implications?

Overall Assessment
The paper is well written and provides strong motivation for the problem. The proposed approach is promising and empirically effective. An important direction for future work is extending the model to support a broader class of constraints and clarifying the comparative evaluation to ensure fair use of information across methods.
