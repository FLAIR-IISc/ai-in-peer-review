This paper introduces an extension to the additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) by incorporating constraints that encourage the sum of individual chain outputs to align with expected aggregate values. The authors develop a convex relaxation for Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference within this constrained framework and demonstrate its application to energy disaggregation – inferring appliance-level energy consumption – using datasets from 100 UK homes with low-frequency readings and 10 homes with high-frequency readings (though appliance-level monitoring is absent in the latter).

While the core ideas are conceptually straightforward and implemented using a relatively simple optimization formulation, the work is compelling due to its application to large, real-world datasets. As a contribution that blends algorithmic development with practical application, this paper represents a successful effort. Several points, if addressed, could strengthen the manuscript.

The original motivation for the Additive Factorial MAP (AFAMAP) approach centered on enforcing a single active chain stage at a time, with the goal of improving the quality of the convex relaxation and increasing the likelihood of obtaining integer solutions. It would be valuable to understand whether the Sum-constrained Additive Factorial HMM (SACAFHMM) optimization achieves a similar effect. Specifically, do SACAFHMM solutions still demonstrate lower error rates even when they are not fully integral? A discussion of this aspect would clarify the distinctions between the two methods.

The AFAMAP and SACAFHMM approaches appear, at a fundamental level, to be orthogonal. It seems plausible to integrate the constraints used in SACAFHMM directly into the AFAMAP optimization formulation. While a response during the rebuttal period may not allow for implementation, I strongly recommend the authors explore this combination for a potential final version of the paper.

Throughout the paper, there's a tendency to conflate models with their corresponding optimization procedures. For example, comparing “AFHMM” results to “AFAMAP” is imprecise. AFAMAP is an algorithm designed to approximate MAP solutions *for* the AFHMM. The authors appear to use “AFHMM” to refer to alternating Viterbi optimization; confirmation of this understanding would be helpful. This also highlights the potential for synergistic performance gains by combining the strategies employed in the different algorithms presented – and may provide a direction for future work. 

In summary, the paper presents a straightforward extension and optimization technique for a variant of the AFHMM. The compelling results achieved on real-world energy disaggregation data significantly bolster the paper’s overall quality and justify its acceptance.
