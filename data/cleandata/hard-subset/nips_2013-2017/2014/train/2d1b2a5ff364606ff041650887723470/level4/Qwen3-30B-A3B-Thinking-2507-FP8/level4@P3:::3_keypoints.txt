- Introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) to improve identifiability in blind source separation for energy disaggregation.  
- Observes that total/aggregate usage of many sources is often known or has predictable expected values (e.g., appliance daily Wh).  
- Integrates SACs into an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM), converting inference into a convex quadratic program.  
- Demonstrates large empirical gains: 45% NDE improvement over AFHMM and 29% over AFAMAP on real-world data.  
- Builds on posterior regularization and related frameworks, framing SACs within established probabilistic constraint methods.  
- Distinguishes SACs (aggregate/time-series constraints) from prior state-level/temporal constraints like AFAMAP’s “one chain changes” rule.  
- Lists three main contributions: (1) SAC concept for BSS, (2) convex QP inference formulation, (3) empirical validation on synthetic and real data.  
- Technically sound with clear derivations and a solid theoretical foundation linking to prior work.  
- Evaluation is thorough, using synthetic tests (robustness to misspecified constraints) and real energy datasets.  
- Computational complexity of AFHMM+SAC scales comparably to AFHMM and AFAMAP, making it practical.  
- Concern: comparison with AFAMAP may be unfair since AFAMAP wasn’t adapted to include SACs; unclear if AFAMAP could match SACs with similar efficiency.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written with logical presentation, but some figures (notably Figure 1) need better axis labels and explanation.  
- Originality: SACs represent a novel, meaningful shift to aggregate constraints in HMM/BSS literature.  
- Significance: strong for energy disaggregation and potentially applicable across other BSS domains where aggregate knowledge exists.  
- Strengths: clear motivation, theoretical grounding, practical implementation, and strong empirical gains.  
- Weaknesses: AFAMAP comparison may mislead, Figure 1 suggests possible performance drops with large constraint errors, and the framework focuses on only one constraint type.  
- Suggests exploring whether SACs can be integrated into other methods and addressing extensions to other natural constraints (e.g., mutual exclusion, duration limits).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel concept, solid empirical and theoretical support, practical relevance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: fairness of comparisons, limited exploration of SAC limitations and extensions, and evidence of degradation under poor constraint specification.  
- Overall: strong paper with minor revisions needed to clarify comparisons, discuss constraint misspecification effects, and consider broader constraint types.
