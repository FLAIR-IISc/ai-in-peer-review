This paper proposes an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) augmented with signal-aggregate constraints (SAC) to address blind source separation. The authors formulate a convex quadratic program for a relaxed AFHMM with SAC and demonstrate promising results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

Pros:
- The paper is clearly written and grounded in solid mathematics.
- The problem motivation is convincing.
- The evaluation shows notable gains in NDE and SAE.

Areas for improvement:
- The primary comparisons are against AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP. Both baselines use less information than SACAFHMM (which leverages aggregate constraints), making the comparison potentially unfair. Are there alternative methods that can also incorporate SAC—even if they are computationally less efficient—that would serve as more appropriate baselines?
- The results in Figure 1 are unclear. It appears that for distances greater than 10^4, AFAMAP outperforms SACAFHMM. Does this imply that poorly chosen or inaccurate constraints can significantly degrade performance? The discussion seems to suggest that adding constraints always improves results, which could be misleading without a careful analysis of when constraints help versus when they hurt.
- I expected to see richer classes of constraints incorporated. For example:
  - Mutual exclusion: a person cannot use the heater and cooler simultaneously.
  - Cardinality limits: no more than 3 out of 10 bulbs active at once.
  - Duration constraints: appliances like grinders operating for less than 10 minutes.
  These are natural in many applications and would strengthen the practical relevance. Can the framework be extended to handle such constraints (e.g., mutual exclusivity, global cardinality, minimum/maximum durations, or temporal logic–like constraints)? A discussion of how these could be encoded within the proposed relaxation, and any computational or convexity implications, would be valuable.

Overall assessment:
The paper is well written, well motivated, and technically sound. The empirical results are promising, but the fairness of the comparisons should be addressed by including baselines that exploit the same aggregate information, or by controlling for the informational advantage. Clarifying the behavior under misspecified or adversarial constraints (as suggested by Figure 1) would improve trust in the method and help practitioners understand when SAC is beneficial. Finally, exploring or at least outlining how to support a broader set of practically important constraints would make the contribution more compelling. Future work extending the model to these richer constraint families would be a natural and impactful next step.
