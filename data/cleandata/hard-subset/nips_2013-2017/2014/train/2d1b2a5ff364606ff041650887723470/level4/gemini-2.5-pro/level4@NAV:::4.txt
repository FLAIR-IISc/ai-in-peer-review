This work introduces a constrained variant of the additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), incorporating a constraint that forces the sum of the latent state outputs to align with a known aggregate signal. For inference, the authors formulate a convex relaxation of the MAP problem and validate their approach on two real-world energy disaggregation datasets: one with low-frequency readings from 100 UK homes, and another with higher-frequency data from 10 homes lacking individual appliance monitoring.

While the core conceptual contribution is incremental and the optimization approach is relatively standard, the paper is nonetheless compelling. Its strength lies in the successful application of these techniques to large-scale, practical datasets. As a contribution that bridges algorithmic development with real-world application, the paper succeeds in its goals. The following suggestions are offered to further strengthen the manuscript:

In the AFAMAP method, the constraint allowing only one chain to change state at a time is motivated by its ability to yield integer solutions more frequently in the relaxed problem. It would be beneficial if the authors discussed whether their proposed SACAFHMM formulation provides a similar benefit. Specifically, does it also encourage integer solutions, or does it achieve superior performance through lower error rates even when the solutions are non-integral? Clarifying this point would significantly sharpen the distinction between the two approaches.

The core ideas behind AFAMAP and SACAFHMM appear to be orthogonal. This suggests that the constraints introduced in SACAFHMM could readily be integrated into the AFAMAP optimization framework. While this may be beyond the scope of an author rebuttal, I strongly recommend that the authors explore this combination for the camera-ready version of the paper.

There are a few instances where the paper could be more precise in its distinction between probabilistic models and the optimization algorithms used for inference. For example, the comparison between "AFHMM" and "AFAMAP" is slightly ambiguous. AFAMAP is an inference algorithm for the AFHMM model, so I presume the "AFHMM" baseline actually refers to an alternative inference method, such as alternating Viterbi optimization. Is this assumption correct? This lack of clarity reinforces the earlier point that the algorithmic components are modular and could potentially be combined. In summary, while the paper introduces a straightforward extension to solve an AFHMM variant, its convincing results on real-world data make it a solid contribution.
