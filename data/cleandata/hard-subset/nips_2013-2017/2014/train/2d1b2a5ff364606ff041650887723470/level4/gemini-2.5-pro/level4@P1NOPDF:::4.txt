This paper proposes an extension to the additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) by introducing constraints that require the sum of the individual chain outputs to approximate a known aggregate value. The authors propose a convex relaxation for MAP inference and apply their algorithm to two real-world energy disaggregation datasets: one from 100 UK homes with low-frequency readings, and another from 10 homes with higher-frequency readings where individual appliances were not monitored.

While the core ideas and optimization formulation are straightforward, the work is compelling due to its successful application to large, real-world datasets. As a paper that effectively combines both applied and algorithmic elements, it represents a solid contribution. The following comments are intended to help improve the paper further.

A key motivation for AFAMAP's constraint—that only one chain changes state at a time—is that it helps the convex relaxation yield integer solutions more frequently. Does the proposed SACAFHMM formulation accomplish something similar, or does it achieve lower error despite potentially yielding non-integral solutions? A discussion of this point would be very helpful, as it would further elucidate the distinctions between the two approaches.

Fundamentally, the contributions of AFAMAP and SACAFHMM appear to be orthogonal. One could easily incorporate the sum-to-aggregate constraints of SACAFHMM into the AFAMAP optimization formulation. While I do not expect the authors to explore this during the rebuttal period, I would strongly encourage them to investigate this combination for a final version of the paper.

The authors occasionally seem to conflate probabilistic models with their corresponding optimization procedures. For instance, comparing the results of "AFHMM" versus "AFAMAP" is imprecise. AFAMAP is an algorithm for approximately computing MAP solutions in the AFHMM model; I suspect that when the authors refer to "AFHMM" as a baseline, they are actually referring to an alternating Viterbi optimization procedure. Is this correct? This lack of clarity relates to the point above: the different algorithmic strategies presented here are not mutually exclusive and could likely be combined to achieve even better performance.

Despite these points for clarification, the paper proposes a simple yet effective extension to the AFHMM. The compelling results on real-world data make this a valuable contribution, and overall the paper is a reasonable submission for the conference.
