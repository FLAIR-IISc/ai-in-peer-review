

The paper introduces an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) with signal aggregation constraints (SAC) for blind source separation. The authors formulate a convex quadratic optimization problem to relax the AFHMM under SAC, demonstrating promising results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written with a strong mathematical foundation.
- The problem motivation is clear and compelling.
- Evaluation results show significant improvements in key metrics (NDE and SAE).

**Areas for Improvement:**
1. **Comparison Fairness:** The results demonstrate improvements over AFHMM (without SAC) and AFAMAP. However, both baselines use less data than SAC-AFHMM, making the comparison potentially unfair. It would be valuable to include a comparison against other approaches that could incorporate SAC but are computationally more efficient. Without this, the claimed advantage may not fully reflect SAC-AFHMM's practical utility.

2. **Figure 1 Interpretation:** The performance in Figure 1 becomes ambiguous for distances exceeding 10^4, where AFAMAP appears to outperform SAC-AFHMM. This contradicts the paper's assertion that SAC consistently improves results. The discussion should address whether poorly chosen constraints could degrade performance, as this observation suggests SAC is not universally beneficial.

3. **Constraint Generalization:** The paper focuses on a specific constraint type (signal aggregation). The authors could strengthen their contribution by discussing how the framework might accommodate more complex constraints. For instance:  
   - *Physical constraints:* Simultaneous operation of heating and cooling systems must be prohibited.  
   - *Count constraints:* No more than three of ten household bulbs can be active concurrently.  
   - *Temporal constraints:* Industrial grinders must operate for less than 10 minutes.  
   The current work leaves open whether SAC-AFHMM can generalize to such diverse constraint types. Explicitly addressing this limitation and outlining potential extensions would significantly enhance the paper's scope and impact. The future work section mentions extending to other constraints but lacks concrete technical pathways.

The paper provides a solid foundation and clear motivation. To elevate it, the authors should clarify the comparison methodology, resolve the Figure 1 discrepancy, and rigorously explore the algorithm's flexibility for broader constraint classes. Addressing these points would make the contribution more robust and widely applicable.
