This paper proposes an additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) with signal aggregate constraints (SAC) to address the blind source separation problem. The authors relax the AFHMM with SAC and formulate it as a convex quadratic problem. The technique demonstrates promising results on both synthetic and real-world data.

The paper has several strengths. First, it is well-written and provides a solid mathematical foundation for the proposed method. The problem motivation is clear and well-explained, making it easy for readers to understand the context and significance of the research. Furthermore, the evaluation results show a notable improvement in performance metrics, including NDE and SAE.

However, there are some areas that require improvement. One concern is that the comparison with existing methods, AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP, may not be entirely fair. Since these methods use less data than SACAFHMM, it is unclear whether the performance gain is solely due to the proposed method or the additional data used. It would be beneficial to compare SACAFHMM with other approaches that also utilize SAC but may not be as computationally efficient.

Additionally, the results presented in Figure 1 are not entirely clear. It appears that for distances greater than 10^4, AFAMAP outperforms SACAFHMM. This raises the question of whether poorly chosen constraints can lead to significantly worse performance. The discussion section seems to suggest that the results are always improved, which may be misleading. A more nuanced discussion of the potential pitfalls of using SAC would strengthen the paper.

Another area for improvement is the exploration of more complex constraints within the algorithm. For instance, incorporating constraints such as "a person cannot use the heater and cooler simultaneously" or "no more than three out of ten bulbs in the house can be active at the same time" could further enhance the model's applicability. The authors could also consider constraints with time limits, such as "grinders cannot be used for more than 10 minutes." Investigating the possibility of extending the algorithm to accommodate various types of constraints would be a valuable contribution.

In conclusion, the paper provides a good foundation for addressing the blind source separation problem using AFHMM with SAC. While the results are promising, a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods and a discussion of the potential limitations of using SAC would improve the paper's overall quality. Future work could focus on extending the model to incorporate more complex constraints, making it a more versatile tool for real-world applications.
