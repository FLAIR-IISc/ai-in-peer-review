This paper proposes a novel method for blind source separation by formulating the problem as an additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) and incorporating signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The motivation behind this approach is to leverage additional domain knowledge to improve the separation of time series into components. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this method using the example of energy disaggregation, where the components of domestic energy use (relating to individual appliances) can be better separated by incorporating information about the total (expected) usage of each appliance in a time period.

The paper is technically sound, with a strong simulation section that demonstrates the efficacy of the proposed method. The results show that the method performs favorably compared to regular AFMM and AFAMAP, a recently developed alternative method that does not incorporate SACs. The improvements are statistically significant, as confirmed by t-tests. Additionally, the authors show that the optimization speed of the proposed method is comparable to AFAMAP.

One question regarding the results section is the choice of a low noise variance in the toy example. It is unclear whether this was done to illustrate the method's ability to separate the signal clearly, as shown in Figure 2, or if such signal-to-noise ratios are common in real data. Clarification on this point would be helpful to ensure that the problem setup is not overly simplistic. Furthermore, the authors could provide additional insight into the signal-to-noise ratio between the mains readings and the aggregate appliance output in the energy application.

The theoretical aspects of the paper are well-presented and structured. However, a formal proof that the final procedure in equation (6) is indeed convex would strengthen the paper, as the provided reasoning is not entirely clear. Additionally, theoretical scaling arguments with respect to data length and number of hidden states would complement the simulation findings and provide insight into the method's tractability for larger data sets and state spaces. A comparison with AFAMAP in this regard would also be beneficial.

The paper is well-written and structured, with a clear motivation for the problem in the introduction and a thorough description of relevant literature. However, I am not an expert in this field and cannot definitively state that nothing has been omitted. I did notice a potential error on page 4 (line 202), in the unnumbered equation before (6), where the subscripts proceeding the object p appear to have unnecessary commas, inconsistent with earlier notation on page 3.

To my knowledge, the work presented in this paper is novel and original. While I am not an expert in the HMM community or the energy application studied, the authors' responses to my questions during the feedback phase were satisfactory, and I encourage them to incorporate similar clarifications in the next version of the paper. Overall, this paper proposes a method for performing blind source separation using AFHMMs with SACs, which demonstrates excellent performance, particularly with real data from an energy disaggregation problem.
