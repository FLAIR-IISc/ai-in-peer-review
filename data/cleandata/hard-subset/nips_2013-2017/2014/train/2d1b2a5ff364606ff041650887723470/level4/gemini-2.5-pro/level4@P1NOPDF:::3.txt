This paper proposes an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) enhanced with Signal Aggregate Constraints (SAC) to address the blind source separation problem. The authors formulate the inference for this model as a convex quadratic program. The proposed technique, SACAFHMM, demonstrates promising results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper is well-written and presents a strong mathematical foundation for the proposed model.
*   The motivation for the problem is clear and compelling.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a significant improvement in key performance metrics, namely the Normalized Denoising Error (NDE) and Signal Aggregate Error (SAE).

**Weaknesses:**
*   The fairness of the experimental comparison is a concern. The results position SACAFHMM as an improvement over a standard AFHMM and AFAMAP. However, the proposed method leverages additional information—the aggregate signal—that these baseline models do not have access to. A more insightful comparison would involve other methods that can also incorporate such aggregate constraints, even if they are less computationally efficient. This would help to isolate the benefits of the proposed convex formulation from the benefits of simply using more information.
*   The results presented in Figure 1 are not entirely clear and seem to contradict the claims in the text. For distances greater than 10^4, the baseline AFAMAP appears to outperform SACAFHMM. This raises an important question: can noisy or inaccurate aggregate constraints lead to a degradation in performance, potentially making the model worse than an unconstrained counterpart? The discussion in the paper suggests that the proposed method consistently improves results, which seems misleading in light of this figure. The authors should clarify this discrepancy and discuss the model's sensitivity to the quality of the constraints.
*   The paper could be strengthened by exploring the integration of more complex and realistic constraints. For example, many real-world scenarios involve mutual exclusion constraints (e.g., a heater and an air conditioner cannot be active simultaneously), cardinality constraints (e.g., no more than 3 out of 10 lights in a house can be on at once), or temporal constraints (e.g., a specific appliance like a grinder is typically used for less than 10 minutes). Could the authors comment on the feasibility of extending their framework to handle these more diverse types of constraints?

Overall, this is a well-written paper that provides good motivation and a solid technical contribution. A discussion on the points above would significantly strengthen the work. Addressing the potential for incorporating more varied constraints would be a particularly valuable direction for future work.
