- Paper adds signal aggregate constraints (SACs) to AFHMMs to encourage each source’s total contribution over time to match given aggregates and formulates MAP inference via a convex quadratic program (CQP) relaxation.  
- Evaluated on energy disaggregation with synthetic mains from 100 UK households and real mains from 9 households, showing significant NDE and SAE improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP; includes a toy robustness study.  
- Builds on existing AFHMM and posterior-regularization/measurement-learning literature and focuses on global, across-time aggregate constraints.  

Strengths:
- Clear, general idea: global aggregates are a domain-agnostic way to improve identifiability in BSS.  
- Sound formulation: standard convex relaxation, correct PSD structure, and appropriate posterior-regularization view.  
- Practical relevance: addresses challenging low-sampling-rate energy disaggregation with consistent empirical gains.  
- Empirical gains: meaningful, statistically tested reductions in NDE and SAE; comparable runtime.  
- Robustness: toy experiments quantify sensitivity to aggregate misspecification; extensible to multiple time-window constraints.  

Weaknesses / Clarifications needed:
- Integrality and rounding: no quantification of how often solutions are integral, rounding strategy, or empirical integrality gap.  
- Orthogonality to AFAMAP: lacks experiments or clear discussion on combining SACs with AFAMAP’s “one chain changes” constraint.  
- Dependence on aggregate priors: insufficient guidance on obtaining µ0 in deployment, household variability, and systematic bias sensitivity on real data.  
- Parameterization choices: fixing λi=1 and using 3-state k-means per appliance need justification and sensitivity analysis; duration models not compared.  
- Evaluation details: more per-appliance and time-of-use analyses needed; the oracle TTC result should be clearly labeled and interpreted.  
- Scalability: reliance on CVX for CQPs raises concerns for longer horizons/more sources; problem sizes, solver settings, and scalable alternatives not reported.  

Quality:
- Derivations and relaxation are technically sound and empirical methodology is reasonable, but the paper should separate model and inference descriptions and address integrality/scalability.  

Clarity:
- Generally clear but notationally dense and sometimes conflates model vs. algorithm; recommend consistent terminology, explicit baseline descriptions, and a summary of the rounding procedure.  

Originality:
- Moderately original: natural extension of posterior-regularization ideas to AFHMMs with novel application and empirical benefits in energy disaggregation.  

Significance:
- Likely adoptable in energy disaggregation and other BSS tasks where source totals are known or estimable; practically important gains at low sampling rates.  

Suggestions for improvement:
- Report integrality statistics, rounding method, and post-rounding objective degradation.  
- Demonstrate or discuss combining SACs with AFAMAP’s constraint.  
- Provide sensitivity analyses for λ and biased µ0 on real data and discuss methods to set µ0 (hierarchical priors, calibration).  
- Add per-appliance breakdowns and time-of-use accuracy metrics.  
- Give solver configuration, problem sizes, iteration counts, and propose scalable first-order methods (e.g., ADMM).  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Simple, well-motivated idea with clean probabilistic and optimization treatment and statistically significant improvements on realistic data.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient discussion of integrality/relaxation quality, missing combination with complementary AFHMM constraints, dependence on external priors, and unclear scalability beyond CVX.  

Overall assessment:
- Solid, practically motivated contribution that improves AFHMM-based disaggregation by incorporating global aggregates; leans toward acceptance pending clarifications on integrality, robustness, and scalability, and ideally a demo combining SACs with AFAMAP.
