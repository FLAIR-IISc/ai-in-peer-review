## Review of NIPS 2014 Submission

This paper presents an extension to the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) by incorporating constraints that enforce consistency between the sum of individual chain outputs and a known aggregate value. The authors propose a convex relaxation for Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference within this constrained framework, termed SACAFHMM, and demonstrate its application to the challenging real-world problem of energy disaggregation. They evaluate their approach on datasets comprising energy readings from 100 UK homes (low frequency) and 10 homes (high frequency, appliance-level monitoring unavailable).

Overall, the work is compelling. While the underlying ideas are relatively straightforward and the optimization formulation is comparatively simple, the application to large-scale, real-world data – and the successful combination of algorithmic development and applied experimentation – constitute a significant strength. The paper’s results are promising and suggest a valuable contribution. However, several points, addressed below, could substantially improve the clarity and impact of the presentation.

The authors motivate the single-chain-active constraint in their prior work, AFAMAP, by suggesting it improves the quality of the convex relaxation and promotes integer solutions. It would be beneficial to understand how SACAFHMM’s optimization formulation compares in this regard. Specifically, does the proposed approach also contribute to finding solutions closer to integer values, or do the observed performance gains stem from the optimization’s ability to effectively handle non-integral solutions? A discussion of this point would clarify the mechanism behind the improvements and further delineate the distinct contributions of each method.

Fundamentally, AFAMAP and SACAFHMM appear to represent orthogonal strategies. The constraints introduced in SACAFHMM could, in principle, be incorporated directly into the AFAMAP optimization formulation. While I do not expect the authors to implement this during the rebuttal period, I strongly encourage them to explore this combined approach for a final version of the paper. This exploration could reveal synergistic effects and potentially lead to superior performance.

Throughout the paper, there is a slight conflation of models and optimization procedures. For instance, comparisons between "AFHMM" and "AFAMAP" lack precision. AFAMAP is an algorithm designed to approximate MAP solutions for the AFHMM. It is my understanding that when the authors refer to “AFHMM” in these comparisons, they are actually describing the alternating Viterbi optimization method. Confirmation of this interpretation would be helpful. This ambiguity also highlights the point raised earlier – the various strategies presented could be readily combined, and their combined performance deserves investigation. 

In summary, this paper proposes a sensible extension and optimization method to address a variant of the AFHMM, and demonstrates compelling results on a relevant, real-world application. While the presentation could benefit from greater clarity regarding the interplay between the proposed methods and the distinction between models and algorithms, the overall work is promising and represents a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with the suggested revisions.
