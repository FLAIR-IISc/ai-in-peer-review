This paper extends the additive factorial HMM by adding constraints that encourage the sum of the individual chain outputs to match a known aggregate signal. The authors propose a convex relaxation for MAP inference and evaluate it on real-world energy disaggregation tasks: a low-frequency dataset from 100 UK homes and a higher-frequency dataset from 10 homes without per-appliance submetering.

The ideas are conceptually simple and the optimization setup is straightforward, yet the work is compelling. The method is applied to substantial real-world datasets, and as a paper that blends algorithmic development with an application of practical relevance, it is successful. The following comments could help strengthen the paper:

- Clarify integrality and relaxation behavior. A key motivation for AFAMAP’s “one chain changes state at a time” constraint is that it often yields integral solutions from the relaxation. Does the SACAFHMM optimization have a similar effect, or do you typically obtain non-integral solutions that nonetheless achieve lower error? A discussion of when and why SACAFHMM yields integral vs. fractional solutions, and how this relates to error, would be valuable and would help distinguish the approaches.

- Discuss orthogonality and potential combinations. The AFAMAP and SACAFHMM ideas appear largely orthogonal: the SACAFHMM constraints could, in principle, be incorporated into AFAMAP’s optimization framework. While I do not expect a full exploration in the rebuttal, I strongly encourage testing this combination for the final version; it may yield improved performance.

- Use precise terminology when comparing models and algorithms. In several places the paper seems to conflate the AFHMM model with particular optimization procedures. For example, comparing “AFHMM” to “AFAMAP” is imprecise: AFAMAP is an algorithm that approximately computes MAP solutions for the AFHMM. I suspect that by “AFHMM” in those comparisons you mean an alternating Viterbi optimization baseline. If so, please state this explicitly and keep the distinction between model (AFHMM) and inference/optimization method (e.g., AFAMAP, alternating Viterbi, your convex relaxation) clear throughout. This also connects to the point above: the various algorithmic strategies could likely be combined and might outperform the current standalone methods.

Overall, the paper proposes a clean extension and an efficient optimization approach for a useful AFHMM variant. The empirical results on real data are convincing, and the study is well-motivated. With clearer distinctions between models and algorithms and a discussion of integrality and relaxation effects, the paper would be even stronger.
