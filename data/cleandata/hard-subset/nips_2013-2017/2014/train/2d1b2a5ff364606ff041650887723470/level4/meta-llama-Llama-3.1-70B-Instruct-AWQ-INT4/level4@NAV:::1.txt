This paper presents a novel approach to blind source separation by formulating the problem as an additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) and incorporating signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The underlying motivation is to leverage additional domain knowledge to enhance the separation of time series into components. The authors illustrate this concept using energy disaggregation as an example, where the incorporation of information about total expected usage of each appliance in a time period improves the separation of domestic energy use components. The objective function, which maximizes the log of the posterior distribution of the hidden chains given the observed data, is transformed into a convex optimization problem. The method's efficacy is demonstrated using both a toy dataset and real data from domestic energy output readings.

The paper is technically sound, with a strong simulation section. The proposed method outperforms regular AFMM and AFAMAP, a recently developed alternative method that does not incorporate SACs. The improvements are shown to be significant using t-tests, and the optimization speed is comparable to AFAMAP. However, it would be beneficial to clarify the choice of low noise variance in the toy example. Was this choice made to illustrate the method's ability to separate signals effectively, or are such signal-to-noise ratios common in real data? Additionally, the authors could provide insight into the signal-to-noise ratio between the mains readings and the aggregate appliance output in the energy application.

The theoretical aspects of the paper are well-presented and structured. However, a proof that the final procedure in equation (6) is indeed convex would strengthen the paper, as the current reasoning is not entirely clear. Furthermore, theoretical scaling arguments with respect to data length and number of hidden states would complement the simulation findings and provide insight into the method's tractability for larger datasets and state spaces. A comparison with AFAMAP in this regard would also be beneficial.

The paper is well-written and structured, with a clear motivation in the introduction and a thorough description of relevant literature. However, I noticed a potential error on page 4 (line 202) in the unnumbered equation before (6), where the subscripts proceeding the object p may not be consistent with earlier notation. To my knowledge, the work is novel and original, although I am not an expert in the field and cannot comment on the significance of these results to the HMM community and the energy application.

In the feedback phase, the authors satisfactorily addressed my questions, and I encourage them to incorporate similar clarifications in the next version of the paper. Overall, this paper proposes a promising method for performing blind source separation using AFHMMs with SACs, which demonstrates excellent performance compared to state-of-the-art methods, particularly with real data from an energy disaggregation problem.
