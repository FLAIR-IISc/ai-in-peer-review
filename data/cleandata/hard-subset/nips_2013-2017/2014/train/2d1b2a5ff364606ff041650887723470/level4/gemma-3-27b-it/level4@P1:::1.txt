This paper develops a new method for blind source separation by formulating the problem as an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) and then applying signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The motivation is that incorporating domain knowledge can improve the separation of time series into components. The example used throughout the paper is energy disaggregation, where the components of domestic energy use (relating to individual appliances) can be better separated when information relating to total (expected) usage of each appliance in a time period is incorporated. The objective function maximized for separation—the log of the posterior distribution of the hidden chains given the observed data—is then transformed into a convex optimization problem. The method’s efficacy is demonstrated with both a toy dataset and real data from domestic energy readings.

The paper is technically sound and includes a strong experimental section. As expected, the method performs favorably compared to a standard AFHMM and AFAMAP (a recently developed alternative method)—neither of which incorporate SACs. The improvements are statistically significant, as shown by t-tests. The authors also demonstrate that the optimization speed is comparable to AFAMAP. A question arises regarding the results section: the low noise variance used in the toy example warrants clarification. Is this to clearly illustrate the method's separation capabilities, or do such signal-to-noise ratios occur in real data? The authors could also comment on the signal-to-noise ratio between the mains readings and the aggregate appliance output in the energy application.

The theoretical aspects of the paper are well presented and structured. The paper would benefit from a proof demonstrating that the procedure in equation (6) is indeed convex, as the current reasoning is not entirely clear. Additionally, including theoretical scaling arguments—with respect to data length and the number of hidden states—would strengthen the paper and complement the simulation findings. How tractable are these methods for much larger datasets and state spaces, and how would this compare with AFAMAP?

The paper is well-written and organized. The introduction provides a clear motivation for the problem and a comprehensive description of relevant literature. While I am not highly familiar with this problem domain, I cannot definitively say that any relevant work has been omitted. A minor point: there appears to be a typographical issue on page 4 (line 202), in the unnumbered equation before (6). The commas in the subscripts preceding the object *p* seem inconsistent with the notation used earlier on page 3 (unless new notation has been introduced).

To the best of my knowledge, the work is novel and original. However, as I am not an expert, I cannot fully assess the significance of these results for the HMM community and the energy application studied.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-motivated approach to a challenging problem.
*   Significant performance improvements demonstrated on real-world data.
*   Comparable computational efficiency to existing methods.
*   Clear writing and organization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The convexity proof could be more rigorous.
*   Theoretical scaling arguments would add value.
*   Clarification of the toy data setup and signal-to-noise ratios would be beneficial.
