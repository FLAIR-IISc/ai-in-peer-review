- Paper augments additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) with signal aggregate constraints (SACs) to force per-source totals to match targets and derives a relaxed convex quadratic program (CQP) for MAP inference.  
- Authors connect the CQP relaxation to posterior regularization, providing a principled interpretation.  
- Empirical evaluation on HES uses two setups (100 synthetic-aggregate homes and 9 real-mains homes) and shows AFHMM+SAC improves NDE and SAE over AFHMM and AFAMAP with similar runtimes.  
- Positioned relative to prior constraint frameworks (posterior regularization, generalized expectations) and existing NILM temporal constraints; SACs are complementary by adding global, cross-time information.  
- Strength: clear, broadly applicable idea—global aggregate constraints are intuitive for blind source separation and NILM.  
- Strength: solid technical derivation of a relaxed CQP with PSD structure and link to posterior regularization.  
- Strength: strong empirical gains on a large, low-frequency real-world dataset, addressing a challenging NILM regime.  
- Strength: practical runtimes comparable to baselines and robustness to imperfect targets studied on toy data.  
- Strength: SACs are potentially combinable with other constraints (e.g., duration or AFAMAP constraints).  
- Weakness: sensitivity to target aggregates µ0 — toy analysis shows misspecification effects; needs deeper, systematic analysis on real data.  
- Weakness: choice of regularization weights λ fixed to 1 in experiments; an ablation or CV strategy and performance vs. λ are requested.  
- Weakness: unclear impact on integrality—do SACs encourage integral state sequences; how are fractional assignments rounded/decoded and what fraction are integral?  
- Weakness: combining SACs with AFAMAP’s dynamics constraints is suggested but not tested; need discussion/experiments on feasibility and tractability.  
- Weakness: ambiguous usage of “model” vs “algorithm”—clarify what “AFHMM” baseline denotes (e.g., Viterbi vs CQP with λ=0) for fair comparison.  
- Weakness: parameter learning details lacking—training sizes, number of states per appliance, handling of unseen appliances, and hyperparameter sharing across homes.  
- Weakness: extension to multiple time-window SACs is described but only whole-day constraints are evaluated; a day/night experiment would strengthen claims.  
- Quality: technically sound and well referenced, but experiment rigor would improve with λ, µ0, integrality, and baseline clarifications.  
- Clarity: overall well written; recommend tighter terminology distinguishing models (AFHMM, AFHMM+SAC) from inference methods (Viterbi, AFAMAP, CQP) and summarizing CQP symbols/scaling.  
- Originality: novel application of global aggregate constraints in AFHMMs with a convex relaxation tied to posterior regularization, building on established frameworks.  
- Significance: substantial improvements on realistic NILM data and likely applicability to other BSS problems where aggregate information exists.  
- Pros for acceptance: simple, general, principled idea; strong empirical gains on real data; broad applicability and potential to combine with existing methods.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited analysis of µ0 and λ sensitivity on real data; unclear integrality handling and lack of combination with AFAMAP constraints; some ambiguity in baseline definitions.  
- Suggestions: report fraction of integral solutions and handling of fractional outputs; add λ and µ0 ablations on real data; clarify baselines and training details; discuss or prototype AFAMAP+SAC; include a small multiple-window SAC experiment.
