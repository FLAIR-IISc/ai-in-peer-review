The paper proposes an extension to the additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) model by incorporating additional constraints that require the sum of individual chain outputs to be close to a known aggregate value. The authors introduce a convex relaxation to MAP inference and apply the algorithm to real-world energy disaggregation datasets, comprising 100 UK homes with low-frequency readings and 10 homes with higher-frequency readings (albeit without monitoring individual appliances).

Although the ideas presented are relatively straightforward, and the authors employ a simple optimization formulation to solve these problems, the work remains compelling. The authors' application of these methods to relatively large real-world datasets is a significant strength, and as a paper that combines both applied and algorithmic elements, it is a success. The following comments are intended to help improve the paper:

A key motivation for the AFAMAP approach is to enforce the constraint that one chain stage is active at a time, which can improve the relaxation and achieve integer solutions more often. It would be helpful to discuss whether the SACAFHMM optimization formulation achieves a similar effect, or if the resulting solutions still exhibit lower error despite being non-integral. Elucidating these points would further clarify the distinctions between the approaches.

Fundamentally, the AFAMAP and SACAFHMM ideas appear to be orthogonal, suggesting that the SACAFHMM constraints could be easily incorporated into the AFAMAP optimization formulation. Although I do not expect feedback on this during the author response, I strongly encourage the authors to explore this combination for a final version.

The authors occasionally conflate models and optimization procedures. For instance, comparing the results of "AFHMM" versus "AFAMAP" is not precise, as AFAMAP is an algorithm for approximately computing MAP solutions to the AFHMM. I believe that by "AFHMM," the authors actually mean alternating Viterbi optimization. Clarifying this point would also highlight the potential for combining the strategies of the different algorithms presented, which might yield better performance than the current approaches. The paper proposes a simple extension and optimization to solve a variant of the AFHMM, and while the results on real-world data are compelling, the paper is reasonable overall.
