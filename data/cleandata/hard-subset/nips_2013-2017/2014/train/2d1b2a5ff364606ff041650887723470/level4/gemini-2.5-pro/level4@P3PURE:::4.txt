This paper proposes an extension to the additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) that adds constraints requiring the sum of individual chain outputs to approximate a known aggregate value. The authors present a convex relaxation for MAP inference and apply the resulting algorithm to two real-world energy disaggregation datasets: low-frequency readings from 100 UK homes, and higher-frequency readings from 10 homes where individual appliances were not monitored.

While the proposed ideas and optimization formulation are relatively straightforward, the work is nonetheless compelling. The successful application of these methods to large-scale, real-world datasets makes this paper a strong contribution, effectively combining both algorithmic and applied elements.

The following comments are intended to help improve the paper:

1.  A key motivation for the AFAMAP constraint—that only one chain changes state at a time—is to improve the convex relaxation's ability to produce integer solutions. The paper would benefit from a discussion on whether the SACAFHMM formulation achieves a similar effect. Specifically, do its solutions tend to be integral, or does it achieve lower error despite often yielding non-integral solutions? Clarifying this point would help to further elucidate the distinctions between the two approaches.

2.  Fundamentally, the ideas behind AFAMAP and SACAFHMM appear to be orthogonal. It seems possible to incorporate the SACAFHMM constraints into the AFAMAP optimization formulation. While an exploration of this is likely beyond the scope of the author response period, I would strongly encourage the authors to investigate this combination for the final version of the paper.

3.  The authors occasionally seem to conflate models with their corresponding optimization procedures. For instance, comparing the results of "AFHMM" versus "AFAMAP" lacks precision. AFAMAP is an algorithm for approximately computing MAP solutions for the AFHMM model. I suspect that when the authors refer to "AFHMM" in their results, they are referring to a baseline algorithm such as alternating Viterbi optimization. Is this correct? This lack of clarity reinforces the previous point that the different algorithmic strategies presented here could potentially be combined, possibly leading to improved performance.

Despite these points, the paper proposes a useful extension for a variant of the AFHMM, and the compelling results on real-world data make it a valuable contribution.
