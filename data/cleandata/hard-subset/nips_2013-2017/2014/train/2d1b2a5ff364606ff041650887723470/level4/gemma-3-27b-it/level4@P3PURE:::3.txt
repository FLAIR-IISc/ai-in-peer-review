The paper proposes an additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) with signal aggregate constraints (SAC) to address the blind source separation problem. The authors formulate a convex quadratic program for a relaxed version of the AFHMM with SAC, and demonstrate promising results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Pros:**

- The paper is well-written and presents a strong mathematical foundation.
- The problem motivation is clear and compelling.
- Evaluation results demonstrate a performance boost, as measured by NDE and SAE metrics.

**Areas for Improvement:**

- The presented results show improvements over AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP. However, these baselines utilize less data than the SAC-AFHMM. This difference in data usage appears to create an uneven comparison. Are there alternative approaches that could incorporate SAC but are not computationally as demanding?
- The results in Figure 1 are difficult to interpret. It appears that AFAMAP outperforms SAC-AFHMM for distances greater than 10<sup>4</sup>. This suggests that poorly specified constraints can lead to significantly degraded performance. The discussion implies consistent performance gains, which could be misleading given these observations.
- While the proposed constraints are effective, I expected to see exploration of more complex constraint types. For example, constraints such as a user not simultaneously operating a heater and cooler, or limitations on the number of active bulbs (e.g., no more than 3 out of 10), or time constraints on appliance usage (e.g., grinders operating for less than 10 minutes) could be considered. In summary, numerous types of constraints could potentially be specified. Is the algorithm extensible to handle such more diverse constraints? The paper is well-written and provides good motivation for the problem; future work appropriately identifies extending the model to incorporate a wider range of constraints.
