The paper presents an extension of the additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) that adds constraints ensuring the sum of individual chain outputs stays close to a known aggregate. The authors introduce a convex relaxation for MAP inference and evaluate their method on energy disaggregation datasets: 100 UK homes with low-frequency readings and 10 homes with higher-frequency readings (without per-appliance monitoring).

The ideas are relatively straightforward, and the optimization formulation is fairly simple, but the overall contribution remains compelling. The methods are applied to sizable real-world datasets, and as a blend of algorithmic and applied work, the paper is successful. The following comments could strengthen the manuscript:

A motivation for AFAMAP’s “one chain changes state at a time” constraint is that it can tighten the relaxation and yield integral solutions more frequently. Does the SACAFHMM optimization have a similar effect, or do the solutions remain fractional while still achieving lower error? Some discussion would be helpful and would further clarify the distinctions.

At a high level, AFAMAP and SACAFHMM appear orthogonal: one could incorporate SACAFHMM’s constraints into AFAMAP’s optimization. While I don’t expect results during the rebuttal, I strongly encourage exploring this for the final version.

There are places where models and optimization procedures are conflated. For example, comparing “AFHMM” to “AFAMAP” is imprecise: AFAMAP is an algorithm for (approximately) computing MAP solutions for the AFHMM, and I believe “AFHMM” here refers to alternating Viterbi optimization. Is that correct? This relates to the earlier point that the strategies of the different algorithms could be combined and might outperform the current approaches. The paper proposes a modest extension and an optimization method for a variant of the AFHMM, but the real-world results are compelling, and overall the paper is solid.
