This paper addresses the problem of identifiability in blind source separation (BSS), a critical challenge in applications such as energy disaggregation. The authors introduce a novel form of domain knowledge called Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), which regularize the solution by encouraging the total sum of each separated source signal to be close to a pre-specified, expected value. This idea is incorporated into the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a standard model for this task. As the SACs break the Markovian structure of the model, making exact inference intractable, the authors propose an efficient approximate inference scheme by relaxing the MAP optimization problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP). The effectiveness of this approach is demonstrated on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, where the proposed AFHMM+SAC model shows significant improvements over the standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP method.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper makes a valuable contribution by proposing a general and intuitive method for incorporating domain knowledge to tackle the fundamental issue of identifiability in BSS. The concept of using aggregate signal values as constraints is novel and appears to be a practical way to guide the model towards more meaningful solutions.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed methodology is technically sound. The formulation of the constrained MAP inference problem and its relaxation into a solvable CQP is rigorous and well-justified. The authors also thoughtfully connect their penalized objective function to the posterior regularization framework, providing a deeper theoretical grounding for the approach.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for the problem is strong, and the authors do an excellent job of explaining both the model and the inference procedure.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and the results are compelling. The significant performance gains on a challenging, low-frequency real-world dataset demonstrate the practical utility of SACs in energy disaggregation. The inclusion of an oracle experiment using the "True Total Consumption" (TTC) effectively showcases the potential of this type of constraint.

**Areas for Improvement:**
*   **Fairness of Comparison:** The primary baselines, AFHMM and AFAMAP, do not use the additional aggregate information that AFHMM+SAC leverages. This makes the comparison somewhat inequitable, as the performance gain may stem largely from this extra information rather than the modeling framework itself. The evaluation would be strengthened by a discussion of, or comparison to, alternative methods that might be capable of incorporating similar forms of aggregate-level prior knowledge. This would help to better isolate the specific benefits of the proposed CQP-based inference approach.
*   **Analysis of Sensitivity to Constraints:** The discussion of the results in Figure 1 could be more comprehensive. The figure indicates that when the provided aggregate constraint `µ0` is very inaccurate (i.e., the Euclidean distance from the true value is high), the model's performance degrades, potentially becoming worse than the baseline. This sensitivity to the quality of the prior information is a crucial aspect of the method. The paper would be more balanced if it explicitly acknowledged and discussed this limitation, cautioning that poorly specified constraints can negatively impact performance.
*   **Generality of Constraints:** The paper focuses exclusively on sum-based aggregate constraints over the entire time series. While powerful, this is just one type of domain knowledge available. The work could be made more impactful by briefly discussing the potential to extend the framework to handle other, more complex constraint types. For example, could the CQP formulation accommodate mutual exclusivity constraints (e.g., heating and cooling are never on simultaneously), cardinality constraints (e.g., at most 3 of 10 appliances are active), or temporal constraints (e.g., certain appliances are only used during specific times of day)? A discussion of the feasibility of incorporating such constraints would broaden the perceived applicability of the method and provide valuable directions for future work.
