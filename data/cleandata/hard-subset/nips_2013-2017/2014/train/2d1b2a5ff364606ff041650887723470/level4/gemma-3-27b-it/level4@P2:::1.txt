This paper introduces a novel method for blind source separation based on an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) augmented with signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The core idea is to incorporate domain knowledge – specifically, expectations about the total signal from each unknown source – to improve the identifiability of the separated components. The authors motivate this approach with the problem of energy disaggregation, aiming to decompose household electricity usage into individual appliance contributions. They formulate the inference problem as a convex quadratic program, demonstrating significant performance gains on both a synthetic dataset and a real-world energy dataset compared to standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method (AFAMAP).

The paper demonstrates strong technical merit. The proposed method yields substantial improvements over baseline approaches, as evidenced by statistically significant results and a comparable computational cost. The inclusion of both a toy example and a real-world application strengthens the evaluation. The authors clearly articulate the motivation, methodology, and results. The connection to posterior regularization is a valuable addition, providing a broader theoretical context.

However, a few points could be addressed to further enhance the paper. While the simulation results are promising, the choice of a relatively low noise variance in the toy example warrants further discussion. Clarifying whether these signal-to-noise ratios are representative of real-world scenarios would improve the interpretation of the results. Similarly, commenting on the signal-to-noise ratio observed in the energy application data would be beneficial. Regarding the theoretical foundation, a formal proof of the convexity of the optimization problem in equation (6) would solidify the technical contributions. Furthermore, providing scaling analysis – examining how computational complexity scales with data length and the number of hidden states – would enhance the understanding of the method's applicability to larger datasets. A comparison of the theoretical scaling properties of the proposed method with those of AFAMAP would also be valuable. 

The writing is generally clear and well-organized. A minor stylistic point: on page 4, line 202, reviewing the notation for equation (6) would benefit clarity. 

The work appears original and addresses a relevant problem in blind source separation. While I am not a specialist in this area, the proposed approach seems novel and potentially significant, particularly within the context of energy applications. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong empirical results:** Demonstrably outperforms existing methods on both simulated and real-world data.
*   **Novel approach:** Introduces a principled way to incorporate domain knowledge into blind source separation.
*   **Technical soundness:** Formulation as a convex quadratic program enables efficient computation.
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is well-written and motivates the problem effectively.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of convexity proof:** The claim of convexity requires more rigorous justification.
*   **Limited scalability analysis:**  The scalability of the method to larger datasets and state spaces is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Toy example SNR:** The low noise variance in the simulation requires justification or re-evaluation.
