

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs), addressing the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS) applications like energy disaggregation. The authors observe that in many BSS applications, the total signal for each source varies significantly across sources, and domain knowledge often provides reasonable estimates of these total values. SACs enforce these aggregate values as constraints, requiring the sum of each source signal to be close to a specified value. The authors incorporate SACs into AFHMMs, transforming the constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP) for efficient inference. They evaluate the approach on both synthetic data and real-world energy disaggregation data from the Household Electricity Survey (HES), demonstrating significant improvements over baseline AFHMM and state-of-the-art AFAMAP.

The paper relates well to prior work in BSS and energy disaggregation. It builds upon the AFHMM framework introduced by Ghahramani and Jordan (1997) and the AFAMAP algorithm proposed by Kolter and Jaakkola (2012), which constrains the number of state changes per time step. The authors also connect their work to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2010), showing that their constrained optimization can be derived from this framework. While SACs represent a novel constraint type for BSS, the paper effectively situates their contribution within the broader literature on incorporating domain knowledge into probabilistic models.

The paper's strengths are substantial. First, it addresses a genuine problem in BSS: identifiability. The authors provide a clear, intuitive motivation for SACs that resonates with practitioners. Second, the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using both synthetic data to demonstrate robustness to constraint errors and real-world energy data from 100 households to show practical impact. The results are compelling: AFHMM+SAC with National Total Consumption (NTC) constraints outperforms AFAMAP by 33% in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and 16% in signal aggregate error (SAE) across 100 households. The computational efficiency of the CQP approach is also well-documented, with similar inference times to AFHMM and AFAMAP.

However, several points warrant clarification. The paper conflates model names with optimization procedures, particularly when discussing "AFHMM" versus "AFAMAP." AFAMAP is an algorithm for approximately computing MAP solutions to the AFHMM, not a separate model. The paper would benefit from more precise terminology: "AFHMM (with alternating Viterbi optimization)" versus "AFAMAP." This confusion extends to the relationship between SACs and AFAMAP. The authors note that SACs and the AFAMAP constraint (limiting state changes) are orthogonal, but the paper does not explore whether combining these approaches could yield further improvements. This is a missed opportunity, as the paper's framework could easily incorporate both constraints.

The draft review rightly questions whether the SACs improve the relaxation to integer solutions, similar to how AFAMAP's constraint helps achieve better integer solutions. The authors should clarify whether SACs produce solutions closer to integer values or if the improved performance stems from the constraint itself rather than solution quality. The paper states that the relaxed CQP solution is "not guaranteed optimal due to the identifiability problem," but it would be valuable to know how much the SACs improve the quality of the relaxed solutions compared to the unconstrained case.

The paper also could better explain why SACs are particularly effective for energy disaggregation. While the paper notes that "extensive research has been undertaken to estimate average national consumption," it doesn't elaborate on why this specific type of constraint works well for energy data versus other BSS applications. A brief discussion of the domain-specific nature of SACs would strengthen the argument for their general applicability.

Overall, this is a strong paper with clear practical impact. The SACs provide an intuitive and effective way to incorporate domain knowledge into BSS, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate their value. The method is technically sound, the CQP formulation is well-motivated, and the evaluation on real-world data is thorough. The main areas for improvement are clarifying the relationship between models and optimization procedures, exploring the potential combination of SACs with existing constraints like AFAMAP, and providing more detailed analysis of how SACs improve the solution quality.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the energy disaggregation literature and demonstrates how domain knowledge can be effectively incorporated into probabilistic models. The work is both theoretically grounded and practically relevant, meeting NIPS's standards for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. I recommend acceptance.
