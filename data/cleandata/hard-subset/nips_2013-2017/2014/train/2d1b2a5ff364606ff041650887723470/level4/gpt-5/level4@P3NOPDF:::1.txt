Summary
This paper presents a method for blind source separation by modeling the problem with an Additive Factorial HMM (AFHMM) augmented with Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The key idea is to incorporate domain knowledge about aggregate usage over time windows to guide inference, thereby improving separation of component signals. The authors formulate a MAP objective (log posterior of hidden chains given observations) and transform it into a convex optimization problem, enabling efficient optimization. The method is evaluated on a toy dataset and on real energy disaggregation data, where individual appliance usage is recovered from aggregate mains readings.

Strengths
- Well-motivated approach that leverages domain knowledge (aggregate constraints) to improve identifiability in an otherwise ill-posed problem.
- Clear formulation connecting the AFHMM with convex optimization, yielding a practical inference procedure.
- Strong empirical results: the proposed method consistently outperforms a standard AFHMM baseline and AFAMAP (which do not incorporate SACs), on both synthetic and real data.
- Reported improvements are statistically significant (via t-tests), and runtime is comparable to AFAMAP.
- The paper is generally well written and structured, with a solid review of relevant literature.

Questions and suggestions
- Synthetic experiment noise level: The toy example appears to use an unusually low noise variance. If this choice is primarily for clarity of illustration (e.g., Figure 2), please say so. Otherwise, a brief justification would help address concerns that the setup may be overly favorable. In addition, contextualizing these noise levels with those typically observed in real data would strengthen the section.
- Signal-to-noise ratios (SNRs) on real data: Please comment on the SNR between the mains readings and appliance-level outputs in the energy disaggregation setting. Even approximate ranges or empirical SNR estimates would help readers understand the problem difficulty and the conditions under which the method excels.
- Convexity of the final optimization problem: The paper would benefit from a concise proof or a precise argument (or citation) establishing that the relaxed objective in Eq. (6) is convex under the stated constraints. The current reasoning is not entirely clear to me.
- Scalability and complexity: Some theoretical discussion of computational scaling with respect to time series length, number of appliances (chains), and per-chain state cardinality would be valuable. What are the dominant terms in time and memory complexity for the proposed optimization? How does this compare with AFAMAP as these dimensions grow? A brief complexity analysis would nicely complement the empirical timing comparisons.
- Statistical testing: While t-tests demonstrate significance, consider reporting effect sizes or confidence intervals to better convey practical significance. (This is optional but would improve the empirical section.)

Minor comments
- Notation consistency: On page 4 (line 202), in the unnumbered equation preceding Eq. (6), the subscripts following p appear to include commas. To match earlier notation (page 3), these commas may be unintended; please check and revise if necessary.
- Acronyms: Ensure consistent use of AFHMM for the baseline without SACs (the draft mentions “AFMM” in one place, which seems to be a typo).
- Clarity: A brief reminder of the role of SACs (e.g., windowed sums, priors on totals) at the point where they enter the optimization would aid readability.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely contribution. Incorporating aggregate constraints into an AFHMM addresses a key limitation in blind source separation and is particularly compelling for applications such as energy disaggregation. The methodological development is sound, the convex formulation is appealing from a computational perspective, and the empirical results convincingly show benefits over relevant baselines on both synthetic and real data. My main suggestions concern clarifying experimental choices (especially noise levels), providing a brief convexity argument for the final formulation, and adding a short scalability discussion.
