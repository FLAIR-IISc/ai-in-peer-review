Summary
This paper addresses blind source separation with additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs): penalties that encourage the total signal attributed to each latent source over a time window to match a specified target. The authors motivate SACs with energy disaggregation, where appliance-level totals are often known approximately from surveys or prior knowledge. They formulate MAP inference with quadratic penalties on source totals and derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) by relaxing the binary state indicators and introducing auxiliary transition variables. They also show the equivalence of their objective to the posterior regularization framework. Experiments on synthetic data and the Household Electricity Survey demonstrate notable gains in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) over a plain AFHMM and AFAMAP, including with realistic low-frequency (2–10 min) data.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates SACs relative to prior constraint-based improvements for FHMMs/energy disaggregation, including AFAMAP’s at-most-one-change-per-time constraint, non-homogeneous FHMMs, duration-constrained models (semi-Markov), and k-segment constraints. It also connects to frameworks for injecting side information such as posterior regularization, generalized expectation, and measurement-based learning. The key novelty here is focusing on aggregate constraints over entire sequences and showing a tractable convex relaxation for inference in an AFHMM.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for addressing identifiability in BSS through aggregate information that practitioners plausibly possess.
- Conceptually simple, general constraint that is easy to state and interpret for end-users (e.g., daily energy per appliance).
- Solid technical development: convex relaxation via auxiliary variables; PSD structure; link to posterior regularization clarifies the probabilistic interpretation.
- Empirical results on a challenging, realistic dataset show consistent improvements in NDE and SAE; oracle “true totals” results quantify the upper bound when perfect aggregate knowledge is available.
- Implementation appears practical; runtime is reported similar to AFHMM/AFAMAP in the tested settings.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Fairness of comparison and ablations: AFHMM+SAC uses side information (NTC/TTC) that AFHMM/AFAMAP do not. To isolate the value of SACs rather than the model class, please include baselines that incorporate the same aggregate penalty: (i) AFHMM+SAC with chainwise Viterbi (no convex relaxation), (ii) AFAMAP augmented with the same quadratic penalty (or hard equality constraint) on totals, or (iii) posterior regularization with AFAMAP. This would clarify whether the gains derive primarily from the constraint or the relaxation/solver.
- Sensitivity to misspecified constraints: The toy study suggests performance degrades as µ0 deviates from the truth; at large errors, constrained performance may fall below unconstrained baselines. Please quantify this more fully: include curves that also show AFHMM/AFAMAP as horizontal references, report the error region where AFHMM+SAC underperforms, and discuss practical guidance for when to trust NTC-derived µ0. Sensitivity to λi is also important; fixing λi=1 is convenient but likely suboptimal. Provide cross-validation or a robust-setting heuristic (e.g., tying λi to estimated variance of NTC errors).
- Inference details and scalability: The paper relaxes integrality; how are relaxed solutions converted to discrete state sequences for evaluation (e.g., argmax per time/chain, rounding, projection)? What is the empirical integrality gap? Runtime claims would benefit from details: problem sizes (T, I, Ki), number of CVX iterations, solver tolerances, and whether specialized QP solvers outperform CVX on larger instances. A brief complexity comparison to AFAMAP’s dynamic program would strengthen the claim of similar scaling.
- Scope of constraints: The SACs are compelling, but many practically useful constraints are local or combinatorial, e.g., mutual exclusivity (heater and cooler not simultaneously on), capacity constraints (no more than M bulbs on), duration constraints (grinders <10 minutes), or time-of-day windows. The paper briefly mentions multiple time-window aggregates but could add a short discussion of which additional constraints remain convex (e.g., linear knapsack-type, simple mutex via linear inequalities) and which would break convexity/tractability, with pointers to possible relaxations.
- Clarity and presentation: Ensure Figure 1 clearly labels axes, distance metric, and indicates baselines; if AFAMAP appears to outperform AFHMM+SAC under large µ0 error, state this explicitly. There are minor typos (“distibution,” “equavalent”) and inconsistent reference formatting that should be tidied.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound; the convex relaxation is well motivated and the posterior-regularization connection is correct. More thorough sensitivity and fairness analyses would improve robustness of the empirical claims.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized; a few places could be clarified as noted above.
- Originality: Moderate. Aggregate constraints themselves are intuitive and posterior regularization is known; the novelty lies in identifying SACs as a useful, widely available knowledge source for BSS and giving a workable convex relaxation in AFHMMs.
- Significance: Promising. Results on realistic data suggest practitioners can benefit by injecting coarse aggregate information. The idea likely extends to other BSS domains.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a core identifiability issue with an interpretable, general constraint.
- Clean formulation with a convex relaxation and probabilistic interpretation.
- Demonstrated improvements on real, low-frequency energy data.
- Method likely applicable beyond energy disaggregation.

Cons for acceptance
- Comparisons do not control for the benefit of side information; need baselines with the same SAC.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to inaccurate aggregates and penalty strengths.
- Limited discussion of rounding/integrality and large-scale solver behavior.
- Minimal exploration of other constraint classes.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you report AFHMM/AFAMAP augmented with the same aggregate penalty (or hard constraints) to control for side information?
2. How are relaxed solutions discretized, and what is the observed integrality gap on your datasets?
3. How sensitive are results to λi? Can you provide validation curves or a principled setting tied to expected µ0 error?
4. For NTC, what is the empirical distribution of appliance-level total errors, and how does this relate to the regimes in which SAC helps vs. hurts?
5. Which additional constraint types remain convex under your framework (e.g., mutual exclusion via linear constraints), and which would require nonconvex handling?

Overall assessment
This is a well-motivated, technically solid contribution with practical impact on energy disaggregation and potential broader applicability. With additional fairness-controlled baselines and sensitivity analyses, it would be a strong paper. As it stands, I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the comparison and analysis concerns.
