

The paper introduces an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) with signal aggregate constraints (SAC) for blind source separation, framing SAC as a convex quadratic relaxation of the constrained AFHMM. The approach demonstrates promising empirical results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.  

**Strengths:**  
The paper is well-structured with a strong mathematical foundation and clear problem motivation. Evaluation metrics (Normalized Distortion Error and Signal-to-Artifact Error) show consistent performance gains over baseline methods.  

**Key Concerns:**  
1. **Unfair Baseline Comparison:** The results compare SAC-AFHMM against AFHMM (without SAC) and AFAMAP. However, AFAMAP appears to operate on a reduced dataset (as noted in Section 3.2), making the comparison inequitable. The authors should clarify whether SAC-AFHMM uses identical data to baselines and address whether alternative methods incorporating SAC exist but suffer from higher computational complexity.  

2. **Figure 1 Ambiguity:** The figure suggests SAC-AFHMM underperforms AFAMAP for distances >10⁴, contradicting the paper’s claim that SAC *always* improves performance. This raises questions about constraint sensitivity—could poorly designed constraints degrade results? The discussion should address this edge case explicitly rather than implying universal improvement.  

3. **Constraint Flexibility:** The paper focuses narrowly on aggregate constraints (e.g., summing signals). Practical applications demand richer constraint types:  
   - *Temporal*: "Grinders must operate ≤10 minutes consecutively."  
   - *Mutual Exclusion*: "Heaters and coolers cannot activate simultaneously."  
   - *Cardinality*: "No more than 3 bulbs active in a household at once."  
   The current framework’s ability to generalize to such constraints is unclear. The authors should discuss whether SAC’s convex relaxation accommodates these or require model extensions.  

**Recommendation:**  
The work presents a novel and technically sound approach to constrained blind source separation. However, the comparison methodology requires rigorous justification, and the constraint scope should be expanded to address real-world applicability. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the contribution. The paper is well-written and provides a compelling foundation for future work on constraint-aware probabilistic models.
