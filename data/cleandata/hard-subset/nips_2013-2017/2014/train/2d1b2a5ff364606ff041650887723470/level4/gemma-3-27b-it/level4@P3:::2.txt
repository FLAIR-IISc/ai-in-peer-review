This paper addresses the challenging problem of blind source separation, specifically in the context of energy disaggregation. The authors introduce signal aggregate constraints (SACs) – a way to incorporate domain knowledge about expected total energy consumption of unknown appliances – into an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM). They demonstrate improved performance on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset compared to both the original AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach.

The core idea of leveraging domain knowledge through SACs is a valuable contribution. The energy disaggregation problem is a compelling application, and the inherent identifiability issues make incorporating prior information crucial. The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in blind source separation [3, 17, 2] and energy disaggregation [8, 12, 22], and correctly positions itself within this literature. The connections to posterior regularization [4] and related constraint-based approaches [12, 21] are also well-articulated.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The introduction of signal aggregate constraints as a general technique for BSS is a meaningful contribution, and the application to energy disaggregation is well-motivated.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over a recent state-of-the-art method [12] are significant and demonstrate the practical benefit of the proposed approach.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The derivation of the convex quadratic program for approximate inference and the connection to posterior regularization provide a solid theoretical foundation.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The authors clearly articulate the challenges of energy disaggregation and the identifiability problem, motivating the need for incorporating domain knowledge.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Readability:** The paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The excessive use of acronyms (e.g., SAC, AFHMM, AFAMAP) makes it difficult to follow the technical details, and several acronyms are quite long and cumbersome. The writing style can be dense and could benefit from more explanation and less reliance on jargon.
*   **Lack of Discussion of Constraint Selection:** While the paper demonstrates the benefit of SACs, it provides limited discussion on *how* to effectively select appropriate constraint values (µi0). The example using national average consumption is a good starting point, but a more detailed discussion of sensitivity to constraint accuracy and potential methods for learning these values would be valuable.
*   **Limited Generalization Discussion:** Although the authors state that SACs are “general across a wide variety of domains,” the paper focuses almost exclusively on energy disaggregation. Expanding the discussion to illustrate potential applications in other BSS problems (e.g., audio processing [17, 2]) would strengthen the argument for the broader applicability of the approach.
* **A priori vs a posteriori constraint.** The authors state they are placing a constraint on the posterior density but are actually placing a constraint on the prior.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated technique for incorporating domain knowledge into a challenging BSS problem.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods.
*   The theoretical derivation and connection to posterior regularization add to the rigor of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of clarity and readability hinders understanding and makes it difficult to assess the full impact of the work.
*   The limited discussion of constraint selection and generalization limits the broader applicability and practical usefulness of the approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field, but significant improvements in clarity and presentation are needed. The core idea is strong, and the empirical results are promising. If the authors can address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding readability and discussion of constraint selection, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept. I recommend acceptance contingent on significant revisions addressing the clarity and discussion points mentioned above. The authors should prioritize reducing jargon and providing more intuitive explanations of the technical details.
