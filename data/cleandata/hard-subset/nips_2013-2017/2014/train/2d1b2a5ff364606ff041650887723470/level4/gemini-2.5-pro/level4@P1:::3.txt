This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), particularly in the context of energy disaggregation, by introducing Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) into the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework. The core idea is to leverage domain knowledge about the expected total signal value for each source (e.g., the average daily energy consumption of an appliance) to resolve the inherent identifiability problem in BSS. To handle the non-Markovian nature of these global constraints, the authors formulate MAP inference as an optimization problem and propose an efficient, convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation. The proposed method, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world energy disaggregation datasets, where it demonstrates a significant improvement over the standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model.

**Strengths**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper introduces an intuitive and powerful method for incorporating practical domain knowledge into BSS models. The identifiability problem is a critical challenge in applications like energy disaggregation, and the proposed SACs offer a principled and effective way to address it. The contribution is significant as it provides a generalizable technique that could be valuable for other BSS problems where aggregate source statistics are known or can be estimated.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-grounded. The formulation of the constrained MAP inference problem and its relaxation into a convex quadratic program is elegant and provides a practical path to an otherwise intractable problem. The connection to posterior regularization further solidifies the theoretical underpinnings of the approach.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is well-argued, the model is described precisely, and the experimental setup and results are presented transparently.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough, using both synthetic data to test robustness and a real-world household electricity dataset to demonstrate practical utility. The results are compelling, showing that AFHMM+SAC yields substantial improvements in both Normalized Disaggregation Error (NDE) and Signal Aggregate Error (SAE) compared to existing methods.

**Areas for Improvement**

*   **Fairness of Experimental Comparison:** A key point of discussion is that the proposed AFHMM+SAC leverages additional prior information (the aggregate signal values, µi0) that the baseline models (AFHMM and AFAMAP) do not. While this effectively demonstrates the value of the SACs themselves, it makes a direct comparison of the underlying inference algorithms more difficult. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of whether other, perhaps less computationally efficient, methods could also incorporate such constraints. For instance, could a particle-based inference method handle SACs, and how would its performance and computational cost compare? This would help isolate the contribution of the CQP inference technique from the contribution of the additional prior information.
*   **Robustness to Inaccurate Priors:** The analysis of the model's robustness to inaccurate constraints could be clearer. In Figure 1, the performance of AFHMM+SAC degrades as the provided constraint value moves further from the true value. The plot appears to suggest a crossover point where, with a sufficiently inaccurate prior, the constrained model might perform worse than unconstrained alternatives. This is an important finding, but the discussion in Section 7.1 focuses only on the increasing error without explicitly addressing this potential failure mode. A more detailed discussion on the sensitivity to the quality of the prior information and guidance on how to assess when prior knowledge is "good enough" to be helpful would be beneficial.
*   **Generalization to Other Constraint Types:** The paper focuses exclusively on signal aggregate constraints. While these are shown to be very effective, many other forms of domain knowledge exist in applications like energy disaggregation. Examples include mutual exclusion constraints (e.g., a heater and an air conditioner are not used simultaneously), cardinality constraints (e.g., at most 'k' of 'N' lights are on), or state duration constraints (e.g., a microwave runs for short periods). The paper would be more impactful if it included a discussion on the feasibility of extending the proposed CQP framework to incorporate these other, more complex types of constraints. Can the proposed formulation be generalized, or would different classes of constraints require entirely new inference techniques?

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and effective method for incorporating domain knowledge into BSS models. The technical contribution is solid, and the experimental results are strong. While there are opportunities to strengthen the paper by addressing the fairness of the comparison, clarifying the model's sensitivity to poor priors, and discussing the potential for generalization, the work represents a significant contribution to the field of energy disaggregation and constrained probabilistic modeling.
