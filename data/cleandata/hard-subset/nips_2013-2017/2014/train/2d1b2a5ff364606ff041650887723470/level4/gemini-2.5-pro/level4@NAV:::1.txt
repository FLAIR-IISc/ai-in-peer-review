This paper introduces a novel method for blind source separation by framing the problem as an additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) integrated with signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The central motivation is to leverage domain-specific knowledge to improve the decomposition of a time series into its constituent components. The authors effectively use energy disaggregation as a running example, showing that incorporating information about an appliance's total expected usage over a period leads to better separation of its signal from the aggregate household load. The objective function, which corresponds to the log-posterior of the hidden states given the observed data, is transformed into a tractable convex optimization problem. The method's efficacy is demonstrated empirically using both a synthetic dataset and real-world data from domestic energy meters.

The paper is technically robust and features a strong experimental section. As hypothesized, the proposed method shows superior performance compared to both a standard AFHMM and AFAMAP, a recent state-of-the-art alternative, neither of which incorporate SACs. These improvements are shown to be statistically significant using t-tests. The authors also demonstrate that the computational speed of their optimization is comparable to that of AFAMAP. My only question regarding the results pertains to the low noise variance used in the toy example. It would be helpful if the authors could clarify whether this was chosen to make the performance difference in Figure 2 visually striking, or if such high signal-to-noise ratios are typical in real-world applications. A comment to this effect would prevent the problem setup from seeming overly simplified. On a related note, a discussion of the signal-to-noise ratio in the real-world energy application would also strengthen the analysis.

The theoretical foundations of the paper are well-presented and structured. The work would be enhanced, however, by the inclusion of a formal proof demonstrating that the final optimization procedure in equation (6) is indeed convex, as the provided reasoning is not immediately transparent. Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a theoretical analysis of the method's scalability with respect to data length and the number of hidden states. This would complement the empirical findings on runtime and provide insight into the method's tractability for much larger datasets and state spaces, especially in comparison to AFAMAP.

The manuscript is well-written and logically organized. The problem is clearly motivated in the introduction, which includes a solid review of the relevant literature. While I am not an expert in this specific domain, the survey of prior work appears thorough. I would like to point out a potential minor notational error on page 4 (line 202) in the unnumbered equation before (6); the commas within the subscripts of the object *p* appear inconsistent with notation established on page 3, unless new notation is being introduced.

To the best of my knowledge, the work is original and novel. However, as a non-specialist in this area, I cannot definitively comment on the broader significance of these results for the HMM community or for the specific energy application studied.

Addendum: The authors provided satisfactory answers to my questions during the rebuttal phase, and I encourage them to incorporate these clarifications into the revised manuscript. This paper proposes an effective method for blind source separation using Additive Factorial HMMs with Signal Aggregate Constraints. The technique performs exceptionally well against state-of-the-art benchmarks, proving particularly effective on a challenging real-world energy disaggregation problem.
