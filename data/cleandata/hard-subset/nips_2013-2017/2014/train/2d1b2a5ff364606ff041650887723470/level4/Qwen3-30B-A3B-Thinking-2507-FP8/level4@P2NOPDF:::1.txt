

This paper introduces a novel approach to blind source separation by modeling the problem as an Additive Factorial HMM (AFHMM) enhanced with Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The core insight is that incorporating domain-specific knowledge—such as expected appliance energy usage patterns—improves the separation of time series into distinct components. The method is demonstrated through energy disaggregation, where SACs leverage prior knowledge of individual appliance consumption to better isolate appliance-level energy signatures from aggregate household readings. The objective function, derived from the log-posterior of hidden states given observations, is transformed into a convex optimization problem. Performance is validated on both synthetic data and real-world energy datasets.

The technical contributions are sound, with a robust simulation section showing clear advantages over baseline methods (standard AFHMM and AFAMAP) that lack SACs. Statistical significance is established via t-tests, and computational efficiency is comparable to AFAMAP. However, the toy experiment uses an unusually low noise variance (e.g., σ²=0.01), raising questions about its realism. Is this choice deliberate to emphasize separation capability in Figure 2, or does it reflect typical real-world signal-to-noise ratios (SNR)? Clarification is needed—particularly regarding SNR in the mains vs. appliance readings for energy disaggregation—since the current setup may appear artificially simple. The authors should address whether the low-noise scenario aligns with practical data.

Theoretical presentation is generally clear but requires two key enhancements. First, the convexity of the optimization procedure (Equation 6) lacks a rigorous proof; the current justification is insufficient. Second, theoretical scaling analysis with respect to data length and hidden state count is missing. Such analysis would complement the simulations by addressing tractability for larger datasets—critical for real-world adoption—and provide a meaningful comparison to AFAMAP’s scalability.

The writing is precise and well-structured, with a strong motivation section and relevant literature context. A minor notation inconsistency appears on page 4 (line 202), where the unnumbered equation before (6) uses inconsistent subscripts (e.g., "p_{i,j,k}" vs. prior notation "p_{i,j,k}" on page 3). This should be standardized for consistency.

The work appears novel within the AFHMM literature, though the reviewer’s limited expertise in energy disaggregation prevents definitive assessment of its broader impact. The method’s strong empirical performance—especially on real energy data—suggests significant practical value. The SAC framework offers a principled way to integrate domain knowledge into blind separation, a gap previously unaddressed by AFHMM or AFAMAP.

In summary, this is a technically solid contribution with clear practical relevance. Addressing the convexity proof, scaling analysis, and noise variance justification would substantially strengthen the paper. The method’s performance gains over state-of-the-art baselines are compelling, particularly for energy disaggregation. The authors’ responsive feedback on initial queries further validates the approach’s rigor. With minor revisions, this paper would make a valuable addition to the literature.
