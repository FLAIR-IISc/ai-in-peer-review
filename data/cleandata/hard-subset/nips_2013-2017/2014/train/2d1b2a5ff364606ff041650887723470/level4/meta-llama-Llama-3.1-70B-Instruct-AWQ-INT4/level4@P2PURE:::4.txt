The paper proposes an extension to the additive factorial HMM model, incorporating additional constraints that require the sum of individual chain outputs to be close to a reasonable, known aggregate value. The authors introduce a convex relaxation to MAP inference and apply the algorithm to real-world datasets, specifically energy disaggregation tasks involving 100 UK homes with low-frequency readings and 10 homes with higher-frequency readings, albeit without monitoring individual appliances.

Although the ideas presented are relatively straightforward, and the authors employ a simple optimization formulation to address these problems, the work remains compelling due to its application to large, real-world datasets. As a paper that combines both applied and algorithmic elements, it is successful in its approach. The following comments aim to improve the paper:

A key motivation for AFAMAP is the enforcement of the constraint that one chain stages state at a time, which can improve the relaxation and achieve integer solutions more frequently. It would be beneficial to discuss whether the SACAFHMM optimization formulation achieves a similar outcome or if the resulting solutions, despite being non-integral, still exhibit lower error. Elucidating these points would help clarify the distinctions between the approaches.

Fundamentally, the AFAMAP and SACAFHMM ideas appear to be orthogonal, suggesting that the SACAFHMM constraints could be easily incorporated into the AFAMAP optimization formulation. While not expected to be addressed in the author response, it is strongly encouraged to explore this combination for a final version.

The authors occasionally conflate models and optimization procedures. For instance, comparing the results of "AFHMM" versus "AFAMAP" lacks precision, as AFAMAP is an algorithm for approximately computing MAP solutions to the AFHMM. It is believed that by AFHMM, the authors actually mean alternating Viterbi optimization. Clarification on this point would be helpful, as it highlights the potential for combining the strategies of the presented algorithms, which might yield better performance than the current approaches. The paper proposes a simple extension and optimization to solve a variant of the AFHMM, and although the results on real-world data are compelling, the overall presentation could be improved by addressing these points.
