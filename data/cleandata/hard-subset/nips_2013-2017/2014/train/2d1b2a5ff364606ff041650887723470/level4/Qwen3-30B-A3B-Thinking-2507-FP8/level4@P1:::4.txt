

This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) to address the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS), particularly for energy disaggregation. The authors extend the additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) by incorporating SACs, which enforce that the total signal for each source (e.g., appliance energy consumption) should approximate a specified value based on domain knowledge. They formulate a convex quadratic program for approximate MAP inference and demonstrate significant improvements over the original AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach (AFAMAP) on real-world energy disaggregation data.

The work builds on previous efforts to incorporate domain knowledge into probabilistic models, such as posterior regularization [4], the generalized expectation criterion [14], and measurement-based learning [13]. However, the authors identify a specific gap: while these frameworks provide general methods for incorporating constraints, they don't specify which types of constraints are most useful for practical applications. The authors' contribution is to identify SACs as a class of constraints that are both practically useful and computationally tractable, particularly for applications where the total signal varies widely across sources.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, showing a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM and a 29% improvement over AFAMAP on real-world energy data. The authors provide a clear theoretical justification, showing that SACs can be derived from the posterior regularization framework [4], and present a convex relaxation that enables efficient inference.

The main strengths of the paper include:
1. The clear identification of a practical constraint (SACs) that addresses a real problem in BSS applications.
2. The careful theoretical derivation showing the connection to posterior regularization.
3. The thorough experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real-world energy disaggregation data.
4. The demonstration of significant performance improvements over existing methods.

The paper has several minor weaknesses that could be addressed:
1. The distinction between the AFHMM model and the AFAMAP algorithm is sometimes blurred. AFAMAP is an algorithm for approximate MAP inference in the AFHMM, not a different model. The paper should clarify that they're comparing different inference algorithms for the same model.
2. The authors suggest SACs could be combined with AFAMAP, but this isn't explored in the paper. This would be a natural extension that could further strengthen the results.
3. The paper doesn't fully explain how SACs help avoid local optima in the inference procedure. The authors should clarify whether SACs help the optimization converge to better solutions or if the convex relaxation itself is the primary reason for improved performance.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a good balance between theoretical development and empirical validation. The technical details are presented clearly, and the experimental setup is appropriate for the problem.

The significance of the work is high. Energy disaggregation is an important application area with practical implications for energy conservation, and the authors demonstrate that their approach can significantly improve performance on real-world data. The SAC framework is general enough to be applicable to other BSS problems beyond energy disaggregation.

The quality of the work is strong. The theoretical claims are well-supported by both the derivation of the convex relaxation and the empirical results. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach, particularly the need for domain knowledge to specify the aggregate values.

In terms of originality, the paper identifies a specific type of constraint (SACs) that has not been previously used in BSS. While the overall approach of incorporating domain knowledge is not novel, the specific constraint formulation is new, and the authors make a convincing case for why SACs are particularly useful.

Overall, I believe this paper makes a solid contribution to the field. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and has clear practical implications. The authors have demonstrated that SACs significantly improve performance on a real-world energy disaggregation problem, and they've provided a theoretically grounded approach to implementing these constraints.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it combines theoretical insights with practical application to an important problem. The methods are clearly presented, and the results are compelling. While there are some minor points to clarify (as noted above), these don't significantly detract from the paper's contribution.

I recommend acceptance.
