This paper introduces an extension to the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) for blind source separation tasks. The core contribution is the incorporation of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), which regularize the model by encouraging the total sum of each latent source signal to approximate a known, expected value. To perform inference in this modified model, the authors propose a convex relaxation of the MAP inference problem, which can be solved as a convex quadratic program. The efficacy of this approach is demonstrated on the challenging real-world problem of energy disaggregation, using two substantial datasets from a UK household electricity survey.

The primary strength of this work lies in its compelling empirical results. The application of the proposed AFHMM+SAC model to large, real-world energy disaggregation datasets and the demonstration of significant performance improvements over existing methods, including the standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP, underscore the practical significance of the contribution. While the central idea of adding an aggregate constraint is conceptually straightforward, the paper successfully shows that this simple form of domain knowledge can lead to substantial gains on a difficult, underdetermined problem. The work serves as a strong example of a paper that effectively combines an algorithmic contribution with a meaningful, large-scale application.

While the paper is a solid contribution, several points could be addressed to further strengthen the manuscript:

*   **Clarity on Models vs. Inference:** The paper at times seems to conflate the probabilistic model with the optimization algorithm used for inference. For instance, the experiments compare the performance of "AFHMM" versus "AFAMAP". However, AFAMAP is an algorithm for approximate MAP inference in an AFHMM. It seems the baseline "AFHMM" likely refers to a different inference procedure, perhaps alternating Viterbi optimization. Clarifying this distinction would make the experimental setup and comparisons more precise.

*   **Analysis of the Relaxation:** The authors of AFAMAP motivated their constraint (at most one chain changes state at a time) by noting that it can help the convex relaxation yield integral solutions more frequently. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss whether their proposed SACs have a similar effect. Do the SACs also act as a regularizer that tightens the relaxation, or do they simply guide the solution towards a better region, even if the relaxed variables remain fractional? A brief discussion of this point would add technical depth.

*   **Orthogonality of Constraints:** The proposed SACs appear to be orthogonal to the structural constraints introduced in AFAMAP. This raises an interesting possibility: combining both types of constraints into a single optimization problem could potentially lead to even better performance. While implementing this is likely beyond the scope of the current work, acknowledging this potential synergy as a direction for future research would be a valuable addition and help contextualize the contribution.

In summary, this paper introduces a simple and effective method for incorporating domain knowledge into AFHMMs. The strong performance on a challenging real-world application makes a compelling case for its utility. Despite minor areas where the exposition could be clarified and deepened, the work represents a valuable contribution to the field.
