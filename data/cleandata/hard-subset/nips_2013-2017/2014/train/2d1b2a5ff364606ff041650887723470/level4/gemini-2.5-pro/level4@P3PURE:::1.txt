This paper introduces a novel method for blind source separation by formulating the problem as an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) augmented with Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The core motivation is to improve the separation of time series components by incorporating domain-specific knowledge. The authors use energy disaggregation as a running example, demonstrating how knowledge of an individual appliance's total expected usage over a time period can improve the separation of its signal from the aggregate domestic energy measurement. The inference problem, which involves maximizing the log-posterior of the hidden states given the observed data, is transformed into a convex optimization problem. The method's efficacy is demonstrated on both a synthetic toy dataset and real-world data from domestic energy readings.

The paper is technically sound and supported by a strong experimental section. As expected, the proposed method performs favorably compared to a standard AFHMM and AFAMAP (a recent alternative method), neither of which incorporate SACs. The improvements are shown to be statistically significant via t-tests, and the optimization speed is demonstrated to be comparable to that of AFAMAP.

My primary question regarding the results concerns the choice of a low noise variance in the toy example. While this choice makes the performance improvement in Figure 2 very clear, it raises the question of whether the problem setting is overly simplified. Clarification on whether this signal-to-noise ratio is representative of real-world data would be beneficial. In a similar vein, a discussion of the signal-to-noise ratio between the mains readings and the aggregate appliance output in the real-world energy application would provide valuable context.

While the theoretical aspects are generally well-presented, the paper would be strengthened by a formal proof of convexity for the final objective function in equation (6), as the current reasoning is not immediately obvious. Additionally, the inclusion of theoretical scaling arguments regarding the method's complexity with respect to data length and the number of hidden states would be a valuable addition. This analysis would complement the empirical timing results and clarify the method's tractability for larger datasets and state spaces, especially in comparison to AFAMAP.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates the work within the relevant literature, though as I am not an expert in this specific area, I cannot definitively state that the review is exhaustive. I noted a potential minor notational inconsistency on page 4 (line 202): in the unnumbered equation preceding (6), the commas in the subscripts of p appear to be inconsistent with the notation established on page 3.

To the best of my knowledge, the proposed approach is novel and original. However, given my limited familiarity with this subfield, I cannot definitively comment on the work's broader significance to the HMM and energy application communities.

Addendum: The authors provided satisfactory responses to my questions during the author feedback phase. I encourage them to incorporate these clarifications into the final version of the paper. This paper's method of performing blind source separation using Additive Factorial HMMs with Signal Aggregate Constraints demonstrates excellent performance, particularly on the real-world energy disaggregation problem, where it compares favorably against state-of-the-art alternatives.
