This paper addresses the challenge of blind source separation (BSS), focusing on the application of energy disaggregation. The authors introduce a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge called Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The core idea is to constrain the inference process by encouraging the total signal from each unobserved source to approximate a known or expected aggregate value (e.g., the typical daily energy consumption of an appliance). These constraints are integrated into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), and the resulting MAP inference problem is addressed via a convex quadratic programming relaxation. The efficacy of the proposed AFHMM+SAC model is demonstrated through experiments on a large, real-world energy dataset, where it shows a substantial reduction in disaggregation error compared to both a standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP algorithm.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles an important and challenging problem with significant practical applications. The proposed method of using aggregate constraints is an intuitive and effective way to mitigate the inherent unidentifiability of BSS problems. The strong empirical results on a difficult, low-frequency dataset demonstrate a clear advance for the energy disaggregation task.

2.  **Novelty and Simplicity:** While the underlying model (AFHMM) is not new, the introduction of SACs as a form of regularization is a simple, elegant, and novel contribution. The simplicity of the idea makes it appealing and likely to be adaptable to other BSS problems where similar prior knowledge is available.

3.  **Empirical Validation:** A major strength of the paper is its thorough evaluation on large-scale, real-world data. The experiments are well-designed, comparing the proposed method against relevant baselines, including a strong state-of-the-art competitor (AFAMAP). The reported performance gains are substantial and convincingly demonstrate the value of the proposed constraints.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is well-written and the work is compelling. The following suggestions are intended to further improve its clarity and technical depth.

1.  **Clarity on Models vs. Inference Algorithms:** The paper could be more precise in its terminology when comparing different approaches. For example, AFAMAP is an approximate MAP inference algorithm for the AFHMM model, not a distinct model itself. In the results section, the authors compare "AFHMM," "AFAMAP," and their proposed "AFHMM+SAC." It is unclear what inference procedure is used for the "AFHMM" baseline. Assuming it is the chainwise Viterbi algorithm mentioned in Section 4.1, this should be explicitly stated to ensure the comparison is unambiguous. Clarifying this distinction would strengthen the paper's technical precision.

2.  **Analysis of the Convex Relaxation:** A key motivation for the constraints in AFAMAP is that they encourage the relaxed solution to be more integral. It would be valuable to include a discussion on whether the proposed SACs have a similar regularizing effect on the CQP relaxation. Do the solutions for AFHMM+SAC tend to be less fractional than those for a standard AFHMM relaxation? Or are the performance gains primarily due to guiding the solution towards a better region of the search space, irrespective of integrality? Adding a brief analysis of this aspect would provide deeper insight into why the method works well.

3.  **Orthogonality with Existing Constraints:** The SACs proposed here appear to be orthogonal to the temporal constraints used in AFAMAP (i.e., that at most one appliance can change state at a time). SACs are global constraints over the entire time series, while AFAMAP's constraints are local. This suggests that combining both types of constraints could potentially lead to even better performance. While conducting new experiments is not expected, acknowledging this potential synergy and positioning it as a promising avenue for future work would be a valuable addition. This would also help to better contextualize the paper's contribution relative to existing methods.

**Conclusion**

This is a good paper that introduces a simple, effective, and well-motivated extension to the AFHMM for blind source separation. The strong performance on a challenging and important real-world application makes a compelling case for the value of the contribution. While minor clarifications regarding the experimental setup and a deeper analysis of the optimization would be beneficial, the paper is technically sound and presents a significant result. It is a solid contribution to the field.
