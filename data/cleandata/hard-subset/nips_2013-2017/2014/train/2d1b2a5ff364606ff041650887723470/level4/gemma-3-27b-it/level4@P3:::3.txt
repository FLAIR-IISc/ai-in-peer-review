This paper addresses the challenging problem of blind source separation, specifically in the context of energy disaggregation, by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs) into an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM). The authors propose a convex quadratic program for approximate inference with these constraints and demonstrate significant performance improvements on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset.

The core idea of leveraging domain knowledge through SACs – encouraging the total signal for each source to align with expected values – is a valuable contribution. The problem is well-motivated, and the paper clearly articulates the identifiability issues inherent in blind source separation and why incorporating prior knowledge is crucial. The mathematical formulation is sound, and the derivation of the convex quadratic program is a notable technical achievement. The experimental results, showing a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over a recent state-of-the-art method (AFAMAP), are compelling.

However, several points warrant further consideration. The comparison to AFAMAP and the baseline AFHMM feels slightly uneven. While the authors acknowledge using different training data sizes, it’s unclear whether the observed performance gains are solely attributable to the SACs or also benefit from the increased training data used for AFHMM+SAC. A more direct comparison with AFAMAP trained on the same dataset size would strengthen the claims. Furthermore, the paper doesn't discuss computational costs in detail beyond mentioning similar inference times; a more thorough analysis, particularly concerning scalability with increasing numbers of appliances/sources, would be beneficial. 

The discussion surrounding Figure 1 is somewhat misleading. The authors state SACs consistently improve performance, but the figure suggests that for larger distances between the predicted and true aggregate values, AFAMAP can outperform AFHMM+SAC. This indicates that poorly specified constraints can indeed degrade performance, and a more nuanced discussion acknowledging this potential drawback is necessary. Exploring the sensitivity of the method to the accuracy of the SAC values is crucial.

While the introduction of SACs is a strong contribution, the paper could benefit from a discussion of the potential for incorporating more complex or diverse constraints. The authors briefly mention possibilities like time-of-day usage patterns or co-occurrence restrictions (e.g., heater and cooler usage), but a deeper exploration of how the proposed framework could accommodate such constraints would significantly enhance the work's impact. Could the convex quadratic program be generalized to handle a wider range of constraint formulations, or would alternative optimization techniques be required?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to address a challenging problem.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating significant performance improvements.
*   Sound mathematical formulation and derivation of an efficient inference algorithm.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Slightly uneven comparison to existing methods due to varying training data sizes.
*   Potentially misleading discussion of results in Figure 1 regarding the impact of inaccurate constraints.
*   Limited exploration of the potential for incorporating more complex constraints.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation and energy disaggregation. The proposed SACs offer a practical way to incorporate domain knowledge and improve performance. While some aspects of the experimental evaluation and discussion could be strengthened, the overall quality of the work warrants acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly clarifying the comparison with existing methods and providing a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of the approach when constraints are poorly specified.
