Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses blind source separation (BSS) in time series, with a focus on energy disaggregation. The key idea is to inject domain knowledge in the form of signal aggregate constraints (SACs): soft constraints that encourage each source’s total signal over a time horizon to be close to a specified target (e.g., expected daily energy per appliance). The base model is an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM), where each source follows a small-state HMM and the observed signal is the sum across sources plus noise.

Because SACs couple all time steps of a chain, standard dynamic programming is not applicable. The authors propose a convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation by introducing pairwise transition variables, yielding a tractable optimization whose matrices are positive semidefinite. They also show that the SAC-regularized objective can be derived from the posterior regularization framework, thus connecting the method to prior work on incorporating constraints into probabilistic models. Empirically, on a toy example and the UK HES energy dataset (ultra-low frequency data), the method improves over AFHMM and over AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS 2012), reporting substantial gains in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) with similar runtimes.

The work sits within several lines of research: factorial HMMs for source separation (Ghahramani & Jordan), constrained inference such as AFAMAP’s “at-most-one-chain-changes” constraint (Kolter & Jaakkola), and duration/semi-Markov constraints (Johnson & Willsky; Kim et al.). It also connects to generalized expectation and posterior regularization frameworks for “softly” enforcing desired properties (Mann & McCallum; Ganchev et al.), and to aggregate constraints in other probabilistic settings (Yang et al.). The novelty here is the specific, broadly applicable class of aggregate constraints applied to AFHMMs and the associated convex relaxation for inference.

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity and generality: Aggregate constraints are natural in many BSS settings (e.g., total daily consumption per appliance) and can guide identifiability without restricting fine-grained dynamics.
- Principled formulation: The link to posterior regularization provides a solid theoretical grounding; the penalty can also be interpreted as a prior on state sequences.
- Tractable inference: The CQP relaxation is well-motivated and uses standard solvers; the PSD structure is explicit, and the construction via transition indicator variables is clear.
- Empirical gains: On both synthetic and real HES data, SACs markedly improve NDE and SAE over AFHMM and AFAMAP, including robustness to modest misspecification of aggregate targets.
- Practical relevance: The focus on low-frequency smart meter data targets an increasingly important and difficult regime for NILM.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
Quality
- Rounding/integrality: The paper relaxes binary variables to [0,1] and solves a CQP, but it is unclear how fractional solutions are mapped back to discrete state sequences and how this impacts quality. Please describe the rounding/decoding procedure and provide sensitivity or ablation results on integrality gap and its effect on NDE/SAE.
- Hyperparameter setting: λi is fixed to 1 for all experiments. Given that λi controls the strength of the SAC, provide guidance on selection (cross-validation, Bayesian treatment, or empirical heuristics). A small sensitivity study would strengthen the claims of robustness.
- Estimating µi0: When the true totals are unknown, the method uses national averages (NTC). Discuss practical procedures to estimate µi0 (e.g., hierarchical pooling, household-specific priors, or semi-supervised estimation), and analyze sensitivity to bias in µi0. The toy experiment partially addresses this; extending to real data would be valuable.
- Baselines and scope of comparisons: AFAMAP is a reasonable baseline, but given the emphasis on temporal constraints, comparisons to semi-Markov or duration-based models (Johnson & Willsky; Kim et al.) and to interleaved FHMMs (Zhong et al.) would clarify how SACs complement or compete with those constraints.
- Scalability: While AFHMM, AFAMAP, and AFHMM+SAC have similar runtimes in your setup, general-purpose CVX solvers may not scale to longer time series or larger state spaces. Please comment on solver complexity, possible specialized solvers (e.g., projected Newton, ADMM, or exploiting block structure), and memory footprint.

Clarity
- Acronyms and notation: The manuscript is difficult to read due to heavy use of acronyms and long variable names. Consider reducing acronyms, defining them once, and moving some of the vectorization and Kronecker-product notation to an appendix. Provide a compact notation table.
- Intuition for SACs: Early in the paper, add a concrete example (e.g., toaster vs. hot water heater) with numerical totals to build intuition for why SACs improve identifiability, and clarify the difference between hard constraints (δi) and soft penalties (λi).
- Prior vs posterior: Be explicit about the probabilistic interpretation. The penalty can be viewed as a prior over sequences or as a posterior regularization term; you ultimately implement the latter (Section 6). Clarify this early to avoid confusion.
- Dataset and protocol details: Specify how appliances not modeled in the training set are handled in the mains setting, and whether training/test splits avoid leakage. Report the distribution of state counts per appliance (you fix K=3 for all) and the effect of K on performance.

Originality
- The SAC concept in AFHMMs and the CQP relaxation appear novel. Emphasize how SACs differ from prior constraints (e.g., change-sparsity or duration) and why they better capture domain knowledge in NILM.

Significance
- The reported improvements on low-frequency data and the generality of SACs suggest meaningful impact for NILM and potentially other BSS problems (audio, biology). A brief discussion of transfer to other domains would broaden the paper’s appeal.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, general idea with solid theoretical grounding (posterior regularization).
- Convex relaxation enables practical inference.
- Consistent empirical improvements on realistic datasets.
- Addresses a key NILM challenge (low-frequency data, identifiability).

Cons for acceptance:
- Clarity issues (dense notation, acronym overload) hinder readability.
- Missing details on rounding, hyperparameter selection, and µi0 estimation.
- Limited baselines beyond AFAMAP; scalability via CVX not fully characterized.

Overall assessment:
This is a timely and well-motivated contribution that demonstrates clear empirical value and provides a principled framework for incorporating aggregate domain knowledge into AFHMMs. With revisions to improve clarity and to address rounding, hyperparameter selection, µi0 estimation, and baseline breadth, I would support acceptance. I lean toward a weak accept.
