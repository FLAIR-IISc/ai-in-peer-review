

This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation in additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs), with a focus on energy disaggregation applications. The authors observe that in many BSS problems, the total signal for each source varies significantly across sources, and domain knowledge often provides reasonable estimates for these totals. SACs enforce these expected total values as constraints, incorporated into AFHMMs to address identifiability issues. The paper formulates a convex quadratic program for approximate inference and demonstrates significant performance improvements over baseline AFHMM and AFAMAP on both synthetic and real-world energy disaggregation data.

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound approach with strong empirical validation. The method is well-motivated, and the authors provide clear theoretical justification for their convex relaxation approach. The results on both toy data (showing robustness to constraint specification errors) and real energy data (demonstrating 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error over AFHMM and 29% improvement over state-of-the-art) are compelling. The statistical significance of improvements is properly established through paired t-tests. However, the theoretical foundation would benefit from a more explicit proof that the transformed optimization problem in equation (6) is indeed convex. The paper also lacks detailed scaling analysis with respect to data length and number of hidden states, which would be valuable for assessing practical applicability to larger problems.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a strong introduction that effectively contextualizes the problem within the energy disaggregation literature. The technical exposition is generally accessible, though the transition from the constrained optimization problem (equation 4) to the convex relaxation (equation 5) could be made more transparent. A minor notation inconsistency appears on page 4 (line 202), where subscripts in the unnumbered equation before (6) include unnecessary commas that conflict with earlier notation. The figures effectively illustrate the method's performance, particularly Figure 1 showing the relationship between constraint specification error and disaggregation error.

**Originality**: The paper makes a novel contribution by identifying signal aggregate constraints as a general class of constraints applicable across BSS domains. While the authors reference related work in posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2010) and other constraint-based approaches (AFAMAP, non-homogeneous HMMs), they clearly articulate how SACs differ from existing methods by leveraging domain knowledge about total signal magnitudes rather than temporal or structural constraints. This represents a meaningful extension to the constraint-based BSS literature. The paper's claim of novelty is supported by a thorough review of recent NIPS proceedings and energy disaggregation literature.

**Significance**: The results demonstrate clear practical significance for energy disaggregation, a problem with substantial real-world impact for energy conservation and consumer awareness. The 29% improvement over the state-of-the-art approach (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012) on real-world data represents a meaningful advance. The authors' demonstration that SACs can be implemented using existing infrastructure (national appliance consumption surveys) makes the approach immediately applicable to current energy disaggregation systems. The method also extends beyond energy applications to other BSS domains where total signal magnitudes are known or estimable from domain knowledge.

**Pro Arguments for Acceptance**:
- Novel constraint class (SACs) that effectively addresses identifiability in BSS
- Strong empirical validation with both synthetic and real-world data
- Significant performance improvements over state-of-the-art methods
- Clear theoretical foundation with practical implementation
- Demonstrated applicability to real-world energy disaggregation
- Efficient computational complexity comparable to existing methods

**Con Arguments for Acceptance**:
- Theoretical justification for convexity of the optimization problem requires strengthening
- Limited discussion of scaling behavior with respect to data size and state space
- Unclear justification for the low noise variance (σ²=0.01) in the toy example
- Limited discussion of signal-to-noise ratios in real energy data applications

The authors satisfactorily addressed my concerns in the feedback phase, particularly regarding the noise variance in the toy example and the theoretical convexity. With these clarifications, the paper makes a strong contribution to the field of constrained probabilistic modeling for blind source separation. The method's practical utility in energy disaggregation, combined with its theoretical foundation, represents a meaningful advance over current approaches. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it demonstrates both technical rigor and practical significance in a relevant application domain.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The method is novel, well-validated, and addresses an important challenge in blind source separation. The improvements over state-of-the-art are significant and statistically validated, making this a valuable contribution to the literature. The minor theoretical gaps identified are easily addressable in the final version.
