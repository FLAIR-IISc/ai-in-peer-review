This paper proposes an extension to the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), introducing constraints that require the sum of outputs from individual chains to approximate a known aggregate value. The authors present a convex relaxation for Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference in this new model, which they term the Sum-Constrained Additive Factorial HMM (SACAFHMM). The algorithm is evaluated on two real-world energy disaggregation datasets: a low-frequency dataset spanning 100 UK homes and a higher-frequency dataset from 10 homes.

The paper's primary strength lies in its application of a well-motivated modeling extension to challenging, large-scale, real-world problems. While the proposed modification is intuitive and the optimization approach is based on standard techniques, the compelling empirical results on significant datasets make this a valuable contribution. The work successfully bridges algorithmic development with practical application.

The following suggestions could further strengthen the paper:

1.  **Clarity on Models vs. Inference Algorithms:** The paper could be improved by more carefully distinguishing between the probabilistic models (e.g., AFHMM) and the inference algorithms used to find solutions (e.g., AFAMAP, alternating Viterbi). For instance, the results section compares "AFHMM" with "AFAMAP". Since AFAMAP is an algorithm for approximate MAP inference in an AFHMM, this comparison is imprecise. Is the "AFHMM" baseline using a different inference method, such as alternating Viterbi? Clarifying this terminology would enhance the paper's precision.

2.  **Properties of the Proposed Relaxation:** The AFAMAP algorithm includes constraints to encourage only one chain to change state at a time, which is motivated by its ability to yield integral solutions more frequently from the relaxation. It would be beneficial to discuss whether the proposed SACAFHMM formulation has a similar effect. Do the sum-to-aggregate constraints also encourage integrality, or does the method achieve lower error despite potentially yielding more non-integral solutions? This discussion would better highlight the trade-offs and distinctions between the approaches.

3.  **Combining Constraints:** The sum-to-aggregate constraints in SACAFHMM and the one-state-change-at-a-time constraints in AFAMAP appear to be orthogonal. It seems possible to combine both sets of constraints into a single optimization problem, which might yield even better performance. While I do not expect new experiments on this for the author response, I would strongly encourage the authors to explore this combination for the final version of the paper, as it could represent a significant improvement.

In summary, the paper presents a straightforward but effective extension to the AFHMM, supported by convincing results on real-world data. The work is compelling, and addressing the points above regarding clarity and algorithmic comparison would make it even stronger.
