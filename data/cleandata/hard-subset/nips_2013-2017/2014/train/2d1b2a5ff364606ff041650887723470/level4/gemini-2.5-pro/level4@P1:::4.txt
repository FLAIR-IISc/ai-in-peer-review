This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation problems, specifically within the Additive Factorial HMM (AFHMM) framework. The core idea is to add constraints that encourage the total sum of each hidden source signal to be close to a known or expected aggregate value. To perform inference in this constrained model, the authors formulate the MAP problem as an integer quadratic program and then solve a convex relaxation using a convex quadratic program (CQP). The method is evaluated on the task of energy disaggregation using two real-world datasets from a large-scale UK study, demonstrating significant performance improvements over a standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP algorithm.

The paper is a strong contribution that combines a simple, intuitive idea with a solid experimental evaluation on challenging real-world data. While the proposed extension is straightforward, its successful application and the compelling results make the work a valuable addition to the field.

**Strengths:**

*   **Practical Significance:** The paper addresses the important problem of identifiability in blind source separation by providing a general mechanism (SACs) to incorporate domain knowledge. The application to energy disaggregation is well-motivated and demonstrates the real-world utility of the approach.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors validate their method on large and realistic datasets. The reported improvements—a 45% decrease in error compared to the baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over AFAMAP on one dataset—are substantial and convincingly demonstrate the value of the proposed constraints.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation, model, and inference procedure are all clearly explained.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarification on the Nature of the Solution:** The authors compare their method to AFAMAP, which constrains at most one chain to change state at a time. One motivation for the AFAMAP constraint is that it can lead to tighter relaxations that are more likely to yield integer solutions. It would be helpful if the authors could discuss whether the proposed SACs have a similar effect on the integrality of the relaxed solution. Are the performance gains achieved because the SACs also guide the solution towards being more integral, or is the lower error achieved even with non-integral solutions? A brief discussion on this point would help clarify the mechanism behind the improvement and further distinguish the proposed method from AFAMAP.

2.  **Orthogonality of Constraints:** The constraints used in AFAMAP (at most one state change) and the SACs proposed here appear to be orthogonal. It is plausible that combining both types of constraints within the same optimization framework could lead to even better performance. While this is likely beyond the scope of a rebuttal, I would strongly encourage the authors to consider exploring this combination for the final version of the paper, as it could represent a powerful new state-of-the-art approach.

3.  **Precision in Terminology (Models vs. Algorithms):** There is a slight lack of precision when comparing methods. For instance, the experiments compare "AFHMM", "AFAMAP", and "AFHMM+SAC". However, AFAMAP is an inference algorithm for the AFHMM model, not a distinct model. The "AFHMM" baseline appears to be the CQP relaxation of the original MAP problem (i.e., the proposed method with λ=0), while AFAMAP and AFHMM+SAC are CQP relaxations for MAP inference in AFHMMs with different sets of constraints. Clarifying that the comparison is between different inference algorithms for constrained and unconstrained versions of the AFHMM model would improve the paper's precision. For example, referring to the baseline as an "unconstrained CQP for AFHMM" would be clearer.

Despite these minor points for improvement, the paper presents a simple and effective extension to the AFHMM, and the strong results on a significant real-world problem make it a compelling piece of work.
