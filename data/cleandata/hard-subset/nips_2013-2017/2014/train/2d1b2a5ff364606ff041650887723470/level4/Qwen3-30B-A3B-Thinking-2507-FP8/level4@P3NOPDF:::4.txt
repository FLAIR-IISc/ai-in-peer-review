

This paper proposes an extension to the additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) by incorporating constraints that enforce the sum of individual chain outputs to approximate a known aggregate value. The authors develop a convex relaxation for MAP inference and apply their method to energy disaggregation tasks across two real-world datasets: 100 UK homes with low-frequency readings and 10 homes with higher-frequency readings (though appliance-level monitoring is absent in the latter). While the core ideas are relatively straightforward, the work successfully bridges applied and algorithmic contributions through compelling empirical results on large-scale data. Below are specific suggestions to strengthen the paper.

The paper should clarify the relationship between the optimization formulation (SACAFHMM) and the relaxation’s ability to yield integer solutions. Specifically, the motivation for AFAMAP’s constraint (enforcing one chain’s state at a time) is to improve the relaxation’s tendency toward integer solutions. Does SACAFHMM achieve a similar effect, or do its solutions maintain lower error despite non-integral outputs? A brief discussion addressing this would clarify the distinct advantages of each approach and strengthen the technical contribution.

Furthermore, the paper conflates model names and optimization procedures in key comparisons. For instance, contrasting "AFHMM" versus "AFAMAP" is imprecise: AFAMAP is an algorithm for approximating MAP solutions under the AFHMM framework (which itself relies on alternating Viterbi optimization), not a separate model. The authors should consistently refer to the underlying model (AFHMM) and specify the algorithm (AFAMAP or SACAFHMM) when discussing results. This distinction is critical, as it relates to the next point.

The core ideas of AFAMAP and SACAFHMM are fundamentally orthogonal, not complementary. The SACAFHMM constraints could readily be incorporated into the AFAMAP optimization framework (and vice versa), potentially yielding better performance than either standalone approach. While this is beyond the scope of the current submission, the authors should explicitly acknowledge this possibility and consider exploring it for future work. The current presentation risks implying these are competing solutions rather than modular components that could be combined.

The paper’s empirical section demonstrates strong applicability to energy disaggregation, but the ablation study lacks depth. For example, the paper reports SACAFHMM’s performance against AFAMAP but does not isolate the impact of the aggregate constraint versus the optimization relaxation. A controlled experiment—comparing SACAFHMM with and without the aggregate constraint, or AFAMAP with the constraint added—would better justify the necessity of the proposed extension. Additionally, the results for the high-frequency dataset (10 homes) should explicitly address the absence of appliance-level monitoring: how does the method handle this missing supervision, and what are the limitations?

The technical contribution is modest but well-executed. The convex relaxation is sound and well-motivated, and the real-world validation is convincing. However, the paper could better emphasize why the aggregate constraint is particularly suited to energy disaggregation (e.g., total household energy consumption is typically known from smart meter data, unlike appliance-level outputs). This contextual justification would strengthen the problem formulation.

Finally, the writing occasionally sacrifices precision for brevity. For instance, "the sum of individual chain outputs to be close to some reasonable (known) aggregate value" should specify that the aggregate value is *known* (not merely reasonable), as this is critical to the constraint’s feasibility. Similarly, "real-world data sets involves energy disaggregation" should read "involve energy disaggregation."

In summary, this is a solid applied work with clear real-world relevance. The method is well-motivated, the experiments are appropriately scaled, and the technical execution is sound. Addressing the clarifications above—particularly the model/algorithm distinction, the orthogonality of the approaches, and the ablation depth—would elevate the paper from "good" to "strong." The authors have successfully demonstrated the utility of their constraint in a high-impact domain, and with minor refinements to the presentation and analysis, this work would be a valuable contribution to the literature.
