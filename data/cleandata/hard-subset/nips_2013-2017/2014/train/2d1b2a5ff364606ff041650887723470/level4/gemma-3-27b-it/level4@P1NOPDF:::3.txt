The paper proposes an additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) with signal aggregate constraints (SAC) to address the blind source separation problem. The authors formulate a convex quadratic program for a relaxed version of the AFHMM with SAC. The technique demonstrates promising results on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Pros:**

- The paper is well-written and possesses a strong mathematical foundation.
- The problem motivation is clear and compelling.
- Evaluation results indicate a significant improvement in performance metrics, specifically Normalized Distance Error (NDE) and Source-wise Absolute Error (SAE).

**Areas for Improvement:**

- The results are presented as improvements over AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP. However, these baselines utilize less data than the SACAFHMM, which appears to be an unfair comparison. Are there other existing approaches that incorporate SAC but are not computationally as demanding? Exploring these would strengthen the evaluation.
- The results presented in Figure 1 are difficult to interpret. It appears that AFAMAP outperforms SACAFHMM when the distance exceeds 10<sup>4</sup>. This suggests that poorly specified constraints can lead to significantly degraded performance. The discussion implies consistent improvements across the board, which may be misleading given this observation. A more nuanced discussion of these results is warranted.
- I anticipated seeing more complex constraints integrated into the algorithm. For example, a constraint preventing simultaneous use of heating and cooling appliances, or a limit on the number of simultaneously active light bulbs (e.g., no more than 3 out of 10). Consider time-based constraints, such as limiting the operation of grinders to less than 10 minutes. In essence, a variety of constraints could be specified. Is it possible to extend the algorithm to accommodate these and other constraint types? The paper is well-written and provides strong motivation for the problem, and the authors mention extending the model to handle other constraints as future work. However, exploring the feasibility of incorporating more complex constraints would be beneficial.
