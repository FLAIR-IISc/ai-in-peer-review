The paper proposes an extension to the additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (HMM) by incorporating constraints that enforce the sum of individual chain outputs to be close to a reasonable, known aggregate value. The authors present a convex relaxation for Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference and demonstrate its application to real-world energy disaggregation datasets, including data from 100 UK homes with low-frequency readings and 10 homes with higher-frequency readings (though without individual appliance monitoring).

The ideas presented are relatively straightforward, and the authors employ a simple optimization formulation to address the problem; nevertheless, the work is compelling overall. Applying these methods to relatively large, real-world datasets, and combining both algorithmic and applied contributions, represents a successful endeavor. The following comments could help to improve the paper:

The motivation for enforcing a single chain stage state at a time in AFAMAP is that this constraint can improve the relaxation, leading to more frequent integer solutions. Does the SACAFHMM optimization formulation also achieve a similar effect, or do the solutions still exhibit lower error despite being non-integral? A discussion of these points would be particularly helpful in further clarifying the distinctions between the approaches.

Fundamentally, the AFAMAP and SACAFHMM ideas appear orthogonal: the SACAFHMM constraints could equally be incorporated into the optimization formulation for AFAMAP. While I do not expect to see this explored during the author response, I strongly encourage them to investigate this combination for a final version of the paper.

The authors occasionally conflate models and optimization procedures. For example, comparing “AFHMM” results with “AFAMAP” is not entirely precise, as AFAMAP is an algorithm for (approximately) computing MAP solutions to the AFHMM. I believe that by “AFHMM” in these comparisons, they actually refer to alternating Viterbi optimization. Is this correct? This relates to the previous point; the strategies employed by the different algorithms presented could readily be combined and may, in fact, yield improved performance compared to the current approaches. In summary, the paper proposes a simple extension and optimization to solve a variant of the AFHMM, but the results on real-world data are compelling, and the paper, overall, appears reasonable.
