Summary
The paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) to incorporate domain knowledge into blind source separation, focusing on energy disaggregation. The key idea is to encourage each latent source’s total signal over time to match a specified target (e.g., typical daily energy usage of an appliance). The authors integrate SACs into an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM), yielding AFHMM+SAC, and perform inference via a convex quadratic program (CQP) derived from a relaxation with auxiliary variables. They also show the equivalence of their objective to posterior regularization with quadratic constraints on aggregates. Experiments on synthetic data and the HES energy dataset demonstrate notable gains over a plain AFHMM and over AFAMAP, a strong baseline that constrains chain transitions.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on factorial HMMs for BSS, and on AFAMAP and related approaches that improve identifiability by constraining transitions or durations. It connects to general frameworks for constrained/regularized posteriors, including posterior regularization and related ideas such as measurement-based learning. The specific use of aggregate signal constraints for AFHMMs appears novel and practically motivated for low-frequency energy data.

Strengths
- Clear and useful modeling idea: enforcing plausible aggregate totals is intuitive, general, and relevant to disaggregation tasks where totals are often known or estimable.
- Solid computational approach: the relaxed CQP is convex and tractable; the construction with auxiliary variables is sensible.
- Empirical improvements: consistent gains over AFHMM and meaningful improvements over AFAMAP, including on realistic mains data; the method is robust to moderate misspecification of aggregate targets in the toy setting.
- Conceptual clarity via posterior regularization: the link to PR situates the method within a recognized framework.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and readability: the manuscript is difficult to follow due to heavy notation and many acronyms. Some variables are introduced with minimal intuition, and long subscripts/indexing impede accessibility.
- Probabilistic interpretation requires clarification: the paper alternates between “prior over S” and “posterior regularization” language. It would help to state upfront that SACs are implemented as quadratic penalties on latent state aggregates, equivalent to posterior regularization, not as conventional parameter priors.
- Practical dependence on aggregate targets: performance depends on the choice of µ0; the paper uses both national averages (NTC) and an oracle (TTC). More discussion on how to estimate µ0 in practice, sensitivity to bias, and strategies for robust selection would strengthen the case.
- Relaxation and rounding: the CQP relaxes discrete states, but the paper does not detail how fractional solutions are converted to discrete sequences, nor the impact on accuracy.
- Experimental scope: only 3-state appliances are used, and some experiments use aggregated sums from submetered appliances (without real mains), which may favor the model. Scalability and performance with larger state spaces or more appliances are not fully explored. Hyperparameter selection (λi) is fixed rather than tuned.
- Reproducibility: implementation details (e.g., solver settings, convergence criteria, rounding scheme) and code availability are not specified.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The derivations for the relaxed CQP and the PR connection are appropriate. Claims are supported by experiments on real and synthetic data. However, the inference details for handling relaxed states and the sensitivity to λi/µ0 need clearer treatment.

Clarity
Currently below NIPS standards. The paper would benefit from streamlining notation, reducing acronyms, and adding intuition and a small running example. Clarify the probabilistic view (posterior regularization vs priors on latent variables) and explicitly describe the rounding/post-processing of relaxed solutions.

Originality
Moderately original. While constraining factorial HMMs is not new, using aggregate signal constraints in this form for AFHMMs and connecting them to a convex relaxation appears novel and practically valuable.

Significance
Promising practical impact for energy disaggregation at low sampling rates, with measurable improvements over strong baselines. The SAC idea may generalize to other BSS domains where aggregate quantities are known or estimable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clearly state early: SACs are quadratic penalties on the aggregate of latent states, equivalent to posterior regularization; avoid calling them standard priors on parameters.
- Reduce acronyms and introduce a compact notation table. Provide intuitive explanations for key variables and constraints.
- Describe the relaxation’s post-processing: how are fractional S and H mapped to discrete states? Analyze its effect.
- Discuss how to estimate µ0 in practice (e.g., from historical data, surveys, hierarchical priors) and provide sensitivity analyses or robustness strategies.
- Report results for varying λi and potentially per-appliance λi; consider cross-validation strategies.
- Expand scalability analysis (more appliances/states) and provide timing/complexity breakdowns.
- Where possible, add experiments on truly measured mains without reconstructing aggregates, and include ablations (e.g., windowed SACs).

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, general, and effective idea with clear empirical benefits on a challenging low-frequency dataset.
- Convex relaxation enables practical inference; connection to PR grounds the method theoretically.
- Potential applicability beyond energy disaggregation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Presentation obscures the core ideas; heavy notation and inconsistent terminology.
- Limited discussion of practical estimation of µ0 and sensitivity to mismatch.
- Missing details on handling relaxed solutions and hyperparameter selection; limited scalability analysis.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The method is useful and shows solid gains, but the paper needs significant improvements in clarity and more thorough discussion of practical considerations (µ0 estimation, relaxation rounding, sensitivity/robustness) to maximize its impact.
