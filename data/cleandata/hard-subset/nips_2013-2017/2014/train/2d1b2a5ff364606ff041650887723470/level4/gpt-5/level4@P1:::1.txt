This paper proposes a method for blind source separation that augments an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) with signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The key idea is to encode domain knowledge about the total energy (or other aggregate signal) expected from each latent source over a time horizon. In energy disaggregation, this corresponds to placing soft constraints on the daily (or period-specific) energy use of individual appliances. The authors formulate MAP inference in the AFHMM with SACs and relax it to a convex quadratic program (CQP) via auxiliary variables capturing pairwise transitions. They also show the equivalence of their penalized objective to posterior regularization with quadratic constraints. Empirically, the method improves over a standard AFHMM and over AFAMAP (which enforces “at most one chain changes state per time step”) on both synthetic and real household electricity data, while maintaining comparable runtime.

Relation to prior work: The approach builds on AFHMMs for disaggregation and related constraint-based improvements (e.g., AFAMAP, semi-Markov models), and connects to general frameworks for incorporating prior knowledge such as posterior regularization. The novelty here is to introduce aggregate signal constraints that act globally over time on each chain and to solve a relaxed MAP problem via a CQP.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for SACs in domains where total signal per source is informative (e.g., appliance-level energy use).
- A principled formulation that ties the penalty to a posterior-regularized objective.
- Convex relaxation leading to an efficiently solvable CQP, with an implementation that scales similarly to AFHMM and AFAMAP in the reported experiments.
- Strong empirical performance on challenging low-frequency household electricity data, with statistically significant improvements in NDE and SAE over baselines. The runtime is comparable across methods.

Weaknesses and questions
- Toy data noise level: The toy example uses a very small noise variance. Is this chosen mainly for visualization clarity (to highlight the gain from SACs), or is it representative of real SNRs in target applications? Please comment on SNRs in the real mains-versus-appliance setting as well.
- Convexity and relaxation: While the relaxed problem is claimed to be a CQP, a brief proof or pointer would help. In particular, a concise argument that the quadratic terms are PSD and that the added transition variables and linear constraints make the relaxed problem convex would improve clarity.
- Scaling and tractability: The paper would benefit from explicit complexity/scaling discussion in terms of T (sequence length), I (number of chains), and Ki (states per chain), and a comparison to AFAMAP’s complexity. Are there practical limits (e.g., number of appliances, longer horizons) where the CQP becomes a bottleneck?
- Minor notation issue: On page 4 (line 202), in the unnumbered equation just before (6), the subscripts following p appear to include commas. To be consistent with earlier notation (page 3), these commas should be removed unless new notation is being introduced.

Quality
- The method is technically sound, the optimization is well motivated, and empirical evaluations are careful. Statistical significance is assessed with paired t-tests. A short convexity proof and clearer complexity arguments would further strengthen the technical presentation.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and well organized. The introduction and related work motivate the problem effectively. Adding the clarifications above (noise levels/SNRs, convexity of the relaxation, and scaling) would improve readability and reproducibility.

Originality
- Introducing SACs as global constraints in AFHMMs for disaggregation is, to my knowledge, novel, as is the particular convex relaxation used here. The link to posterior regularization is appropriate and helps position the work.

Significance
- The gains over AFAMAP and AFHMM on realistic, low-frequency HES data are compelling, particularly for a practically important task like energy disaggregation. The approach is likely to be useful in other BSS settings where aggregate source totals are known or can be estimated.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong empirical improvements on real data with statistical significance.
- Principled incorporation of domain knowledge via SACs.
- Convex relaxation enabling efficient inference; runtime comparable to prior art.
- Broad applicability to other BSS problems with aggregate priors.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited theoretical exposition on convexity and algorithmic scaling.
- Toy example uses extremely low noise; broader robustness analysis to realistic noise levels would be reassuring.
- Some minor notation inconsistencies.

Addendum
The authors addressed my questions during the rebuttal; I encourage incorporating those clarifications (especially on noise levels/SNRs, convexity, and scaling) into the final version. Overall, this is a solid contribution: a method for blind source separation using AFHMMs with signal aggregate constraints that achieves state-of-the-art performance on energy disaggregation.
