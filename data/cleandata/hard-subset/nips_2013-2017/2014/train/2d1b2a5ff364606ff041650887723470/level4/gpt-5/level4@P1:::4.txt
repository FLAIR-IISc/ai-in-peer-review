This paper extends the additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs) that bias each source’s total activity toward a specified aggregate value. The authors formulate MAP inference with these constraints and derive a convex quadratic relaxation, which they solve efficiently. They evaluate on energy disaggregation: a large set of UK homes with low-frequency readings (aggregated data) and a smaller set with mains readings but without monitors on every appliance. The results show substantial performance gains over a baseline AFHMM and over AFAMAP, a recent approximate MAP method that constrains at most one chain to change state at a time.

The idea is straightforward but well-motivated: in many blind source separation settings, we have credible prior knowledge about the total mass of each source, and encoding this improves identifiability. The optimization is simple and practical, and the empirical evaluation on real data is compelling for a paper that blends methodological and applied contributions.

Comments and suggestions:

- Integrality and relaxation behavior. A motivation for AFAMAP’s “one chain changes at a time” constraint is that it improves the relaxation so that solutions are more often integral. Does AFHMM+SAC have a similar effect? Or do the convex relaxations typically remain fractional, with improved accuracy despite non-integral solutions? Some discussion and diagnostics (e.g., fraction of integral assignments, rounding strategy, and its impact on NDE/SAE) would clarify how SACs influence the solution landscape and highlight distinctions from AFAMAP.

- Orthogonality of ideas. Conceptually, AFAMAP’s temporal change constraint and AFHMM+SAC’s aggregate constraints are orthogonal. It seems natural to incorporate SACs into the AFAMAP optimization as additional terms. While I don’t expect a full exploration in the rebuttal, I strongly encourage trying this combined approach for the final version, as it may yield further gains.

- Model versus algorithm terminology. The paper sometimes conflates models and inference procedures. AFAMAP is an algorithm for approximate MAP inference in the AFHMM; by “AFHMM” in the comparisons, I believe the authors mean alternating chainwise Viterbi. Please make this explicit in the experimental section and captions. This clarification also emphasizes that the proposed approach is an alternative inference/relaxation scheme for AFHMM that could be combined with other constraints like AFAMAP’s.

- Empirical scope and clarity. The empirical results are strong, especially given the challenging low-frequency data. A few clarifications would strengthen the presentation:
  - State assumptions: all appliances are modeled with three states; please comment on sensitivity to the number of states and the computational trade-off.
  - Hyperparameters: λi are set to 1 throughout; a brief justification or a short sensitivity analysis (or cross-validation note) would be useful, especially since λi play a role analogous to regularization strength.
  - Rounding and feasibility: since the optimization is a relaxed CQP, describe how you map fractional solutions back to discrete states, and whether constraints (aggregate targets) are met post-rounding.
  - Computational cost: runtime comparisons are appreciated; adding complexity scaling with T and total states (empirically) would help practitioners anticipate costs.

- Positioning relative to prior work. The paper briefly mentions posterior regularization and shows how the objective can be derived within that framework. This is a nice connection; consider expanding slightly to clarify how SACs differ from other moment constraints (e.g., generalized expectation) and why aggregate totals are especially effective in disaggregation.

- Notation and naming. Standardize the name of the proposed method (e.g., AFHMM+SAC) throughout the paper and figures/tables to avoid confusion with AFHMM and AFAMAP.

Assessment by NIPS criteria:

- Quality: The formulation is technically sound, and the convex relaxation is appropriate and efficiently solvable. The empirical evaluation is thorough for the application domain, with clear improvements over strong baselines. Clarifications on integrality/rounding and hyperparameter sensitivity would further strengthen the work.

- Clarity: The paper is generally well written, though it would benefit from clearer separation of model versus inference, standardized naming, and an explicit description of the rounding procedure. A brief discussion of why SACs improve identifiability in practice would also help.

- Originality: The SAC idea is simple but novel in this context, and the connection to posterior regularization is appropriate. Combining aggregate constraints with AFHMMs for disaggregation appears to be new and practically valuable.

- Significance: Results on real, large-scale, low-frequency data are meaningful for NILM and, more broadly, for BSS problems where aggregate priors are available. The approach is likely to be adopted and extended, particularly if combined with other constraints like AFAMAP’s.

Pros for acceptance:
- Practical, effective constraint that addresses identifiability.
- Clean convex relaxation and efficient implementation.
- Strong empirical gains on realistic datasets.
- Clear path to combining with existing constraints (e.g., AFAMAP).

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited discussion of integrality, rounding, and their impact on accuracy.
- Hyperparameter choice and sensitivity not explored.
- Occasional conflation of model and algorithm in exposition.

Overall, while the methodological advance is incremental, the paper is well executed and demonstrates clear, practical improvements on challenging real-world data. I find it a solid and useful contribution.
