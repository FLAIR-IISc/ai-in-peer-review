The paper proposes an extension to the additive factorial hidden Markov model (HMM) by adding constraints to ensure the sum of individual chain outputs closely matches a reasonable (and known) aggregate value. The authors present a convex relaxation for maximum a posteriori (MAP) inference and apply the resulting algorithm to real-world energy disaggregation datasets – specifically, data from 100 UK homes with low-frequency readings and 10 homes with higher-frequency readings (though individual appliance monitoring is not available).

The ideas presented are relatively straightforward, and the authors employ a simple optimization formulation to address these problems. Nevertheless, the work is compelling overall. Applying these methods to relatively large, real-world datasets, and combining both applied and algorithmic contributions, makes this a successful paper in my opinion. The following comments could help to improve the manuscript:

The motivation for enforcing a single chain state at a time in the AFAMAP model is that it improves the relaxation, leading to integer solutions more frequently. Does the SACAFHMM optimization formulation achieve a similar effect, or do the resulting solutions still exhibit lower error even when they are not integral? Discussion of these points would be very helpful and would further clarify the distinctions between the approaches.

Fundamentally, the AFAMAP and SACAFHMM ideas appear orthogonal. One could readily incorporate the SACAFHMM constraints into the optimization formulation for AFAMAP. While I don’t expect to see this explored in the author response, I strongly encourage them to investigate this for a final version of the paper.

The authors occasionally conflate models and optimization procedures. For instance, comparing the results of "AFHMM" versus "AFAMAP" lacks precision. AFAMAP is an algorithm for (approximately) computing MAP solutions *to* the AFHMM, and I believe what they refer to as “AFHMM” is actually alternating Viterbi optimization. Is this correct? This point ties into the previous one – the strategies of the different algorithms presented could easily be combined and might, in fact, outperform the current approaches. The paper proposes a simple extension and optimization to solve a variant of the AFHMM, but the results on real-world data are compelling, and overall, the paper appears reasonable.
