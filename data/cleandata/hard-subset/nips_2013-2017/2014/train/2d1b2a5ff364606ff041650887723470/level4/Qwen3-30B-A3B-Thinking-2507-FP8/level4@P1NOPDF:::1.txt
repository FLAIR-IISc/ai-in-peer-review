

This paper introduces a novel approach to blind source separation by modeling the problem as an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) with signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The core idea leverages domain knowledge—specifically, expected total usage per appliance—to enhance separation of time series components, demonstrated through energy disaggregation. The authors transform the log-posterior maximization objective into a convex optimization problem, validated via synthetic and real-world energy data.  

The technical contributions are sound, with a thorough simulation study. The method consistently outperforms standard AFHMM and AFAMAP (a recent SAC-free alternative), with statistically significant gains confirmed via t-tests. Optimization speed is comparable to AFAMAP, a notable practical advantage. However, the toy example employs unusually low noise variance (e.g., Figure 2). Was this deliberate to clearly demonstrate separation capabilities where competitors fail, or does it reflect typical signal-to-noise ratios in real energy data? Clarification would strengthen the setup’s realism and applicability. Similarly, the authors should address the signal-to-noise ratio between mains readings and aggregate appliance outputs in the energy application.  

The theoretical exposition is generally clear but would benefit from two additions. First, a formal proof that the procedure in Equation (6) is convex is needed—the current reasoning is insufficiently rigorous. Second, theoretical scaling analysis (e.g., with respect to sequence length and hidden state count) would complement the simulations. How does the method scale for larger datasets or state spaces compared to AFAMAP? This is critical for real-world deployment.  

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated, with a strong introduction outlining relevant literature. As a non-specialist in this domain, I cannot verify completeness of the literature review, but the problem context is compelling. A minor notational inconsistency appears on page 4 (line 202), where the unnumbered equation before (6) uses commas in subscripts (e.g., `p_{i,j,k}`) inconsistent with prior notation on page 3 (which omits commas). This should be standardized unless a deliberate notational shift is intended.  

The work appears novel within the AFHMM literature, though its significance to the HMM community and energy disaggregation field remains somewhat opaque without deeper contextualization from an expert.  

Addendum: The authors satisfactorily addressed all queries during the feedback phase. I recommend incorporating their clarifications—particularly regarding noise variance in the toy example and SNR in energy data—into the final version. The method demonstrates strong empirical performance, especially on real energy disaggregation data, and represents a meaningful advancement over state-of-the-art SAC-free approaches.
