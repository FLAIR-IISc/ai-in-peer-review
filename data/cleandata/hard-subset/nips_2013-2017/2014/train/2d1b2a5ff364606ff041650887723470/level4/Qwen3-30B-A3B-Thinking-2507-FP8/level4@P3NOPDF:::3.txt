

Review:  
This paper introduces Additive Factorial HMM with Signal Aggregation Constraints (AFHMM-SAC), a novel approach to blind source separation that integrates convex quadratic optimization to enforce aggregate signal constraints. The method demonstrates promising empirical performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets, achieving significant improvements in key metrics (Normalized Distance Error and Source Activity Error) over baseline approaches.  

**Strengths**  
The paper is well-structured and grounded in strong mathematical principles, with clear motivation for addressing limitations in existing blind source separation techniques. The theoretical foundation for relaxing AFHMM into a convex quadratic problem is sound, and the experimental validation—particularly the real-world application to sensor data—effectively demonstrates practical utility. The writing is precise and accessible, making the technical contributions approachable for the NIPS audience.  

**Key Concerns**  
1. **Unfair Baseline Comparison**: The reported performance gains (e.g., Figure 1) are framed as improvements over AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP. However, SACAFHMM explicitly uses more training data than these baselines, which likely accounts for part of the observed gain. The authors should compare against methods that *also incorporate SAC* but differ in computational efficiency (e.g., non-convex SAC variants or alternative constraint-handling techniques). Without this, the claimed "SAC advantage" risks conflating data volume with constraint efficacy.  

2. **Figure 1 Ambiguity**: The results in Figure 1 suggest SACAFHMM underperforms AFAMAP for signal distances exceeding \(10^4\), contradicting the paper’s assertion that SAC consistently improves results. This discrepancy requires clarification: Does SAC degrade performance under specific conditions (e.g., high noise or sparse signals), or is the figure misinterpreted? The discussion should address this tension rather than presenting SAC as universally beneficial.  

3. **Constraint Generality**: The paper focuses narrowly on linear aggregation constraints (e.g., total signal sum), yet real-world applications demand richer constraint types. For example:  
   - *Disjunctive constraints*: "A heater and cooler cannot operate simultaneously."  
   - *Cardinality constraints*: "At most 3 of 10 house bulbs may be active at once."  
   - *Temporal constraints*: "Grinders must run for <10 minutes."  
   The current framework does not explicitly address how SAC could accommodate such constraints. The authors should discuss whether SACAFHMM’s convex relaxation can be extended to handle non-linear, combinatorial, or temporal constraints, or if new methodological adaptations would be required. This gap limits the model’s applicability to complex real-world scenarios.  

**Recommendations for Revision**  
- **Clarify Baseline Comparisons**: Re-run experiments with baselines that use identical data volumes but differ in constraint handling. If SACAFHMM inherently requires more data, explicitly state this limitation and adjust performance claims.  
- **Reconcile Figure 1 Discrepancy**: Analyze why SACAFHMM underperforms at high distances (e.g., constraint violation sensitivity) and revise the discussion to reflect nuanced performance trade-offs.  
- **Expand Constraint Scope**: Discuss potential extensions for non-linear constraints (e.g., via Lagrangian duality or piecewise relaxations) and cite related work on constrained optimization in HMMs. The future work section should specify *how* the model could support diverse constraints—not merely state it as a vague goal.  

**Conclusion**  
The AFHMM-SAC framework presents a compelling theoretical and empirical advance for constrained blind source separation. Its core innovation—integrating signal aggregation into a convex optimization framework—holds significant promise. However, the current presentation risks overstating SAC’s benefits due to methodological inconsistencies in comparisons and an incomplete exploration of constraint diversity. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper’s contribution and broaden its relevance to real-world constraint-rich problems. With revisions to clarify the baseline comparison, resolve the Figure 1 inconsistency, and elaborate on constraint generality, this work would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
