

# Review for "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) to address the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS) for energy disaggregation. The authors observe that in many BSS applications, the total signal for each source varies widely and can be estimated from domain knowledge (e.g., a toaster's expected daily energy consumption is around 50 Wh). They incorporate these constraints into an Additive Factorial HMM (AFHMM), transforming the constrained inference problem into a convex quadratic program. The method is evaluated on both synthetic data and real-world energy disaggregation data, showing significant improvements over the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach.

The paper builds upon prior work in BSS and energy disaggregation. It relates to the AFHMM framework introduced by Ghahramani and Jordan (1997), as well as recent constrained approaches like AFAMAP (Kolter and Jaakkola, 2012), which enforces that at most one chain changes state at any time. It also connects to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2010) by demonstrating that SACs can be derived from this framework. The authors correctly identify a gap in the literature regarding how to incorporate meaningful domain knowledge into BSS models, and their SACs provide a principled approach to this problem.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound method that addresses a well-known challenge in energy disaggregation. The theoretical derivation of SACs as a convex quadratic program is correct, and the experimental results on both synthetic and real-world data demonstrate significant performance improvements. The authors carefully compare against relevant baselines (AFHMM and AFAMAP) and provide statistical validation of their results (paired t-tests). However, the paper could have included more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the method to the choice of constraint values (μi₀), which is crucial for practical application. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion but don't explore it in depth.

**Clarity:** The paper's writing suffers from excessive jargon and inconsistent notation, making it difficult to follow. The authors introduce numerous acronyms (AFHMM, SAC, NTC, TTC, SAE, NDE, etc.) without sufficient explanation, and some acronyms are overly long (e.g., "Signal Aggregate Constraint" becomes SAC, but the paper uses "SAC" without defining it in the abstract). The mathematical formulation is complex and could be simplified to improve readability. The transition between Sections 4 and 5 is particularly abrupt, with the authors introducing the convex quadratic program without adequately explaining why the previous approach is insufficient. The paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the SAC concept before diving into the mathematical details.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution to the energy disaggregation literature by introducing SACs as a principled way to incorporate domain knowledge. While the general idea of incorporating constraints into probabilistic models isn't entirely novel (posterior regularization, generalized expectation criterion), the specific application to energy disaggregation with aggregate constraints on the total signal is novel and well-motivated. The authors correctly position their work as addressing a gap in how to identify "classes of constraints for which we often have prior knowledge" (Section 1), which distinguishes their work from previous constraint-based approaches that impose state-level constraints rather than aggregate constraints.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is strong for the energy disaggregation community. Energy disaggregation is an important application with real-world impact, and the authors demonstrate that SACs can improve performance by 33% in terms of normalized disaggregation error compared to the state-of-the-art AFAMAP. The paper's approach could be extended to other BSS applications beyond energy disaggregation where aggregate constraints are meaningful, such as audio source separation or biological signal processing. The authors also correctly note that their method could be applied to unsupervised disaggregation approaches by using national survey data to estimate μi₀.

**Strengths:**
- Clear motivation for SACs based on domain knowledge in energy disaggregation
- Demonstrated significant performance improvements over strong baselines
- Theoretical derivation showing SACs can be incorporated into posterior regularization
- Convex quadratic programming formulation that is computationally efficient
- Evaluation on real-world data with statistical validation

**Weaknesses:**
- Poor writing quality with excessive jargon and inconsistent notation
- Insufficient exploration of sensitivity to constraint values (μi₀)
- Abrupt transition between theoretical formulation and algorithmic implementation
- Limited discussion of limitations and failure cases
- The method assumes the total energy consumption (μi₀) is known, which may not be practical in all scenarios

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper addresses a meaningful problem with a technically sound approach that demonstrates clear improvements over existing methods. The main issues are with the paper's presentation, which needs to be clarified and simplified to improve readability. The authors should:
1. Define all acronyms at first use
2. Simplify the mathematical exposition where possible
3. Add a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity to constraint values
4. Clarify the relationship between SACs and posterior regularization in the main text
5. Provide a more intuitive explanation of the SAC concept before presenting the mathematical formulation

The paper's contribution is significant for the energy disaggregation community, and the method could be broadly applicable to other BSS problems. With the suggested revisions to improve clarity, this paper would make a valuable contribution to NIPS.
