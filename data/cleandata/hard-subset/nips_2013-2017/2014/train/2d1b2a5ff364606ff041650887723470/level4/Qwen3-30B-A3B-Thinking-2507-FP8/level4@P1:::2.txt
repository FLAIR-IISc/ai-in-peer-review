

This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) to address the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS), with a specific application to energy disaggregation. The authors propose incorporating SACs into an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM), where constraints are placed on the total signal magnitude for each source to match domain-specific expectations (e.g., expected daily energy consumption for specific appliances). They formulate the problem as a convex quadratic program for efficient inference and demonstrate significant improvements over baseline methods on real-world energy data.

The work builds upon the AFHMM framework for energy disaggregation [8, 12, 15], which has been widely used in the field. While previous approaches have explored various constraints on HMM states (e.g., [12, 21]), this paper makes a novel contribution by focusing on aggregate signal constraints rather than state transition constraints. The authors also relate their approach to posterior regularization [4], showing that SACs can be derived from this framework, which provides theoretical grounding for their method.

The paper's strengths are clear: the SAC concept is well-motivated by the energy disaggregation problem, the convex quadratic program formulation is technically sound and computationally efficient, and the empirical results are compelling. On the Household Electricity Survey dataset, the AFHMM+SAC model with national total consumption constraints (NTC) achieves a 33% reduction in normalized disaggregation error and a 16% improvement in signal aggregate error over the state-of-the-art AFAMAP approach [12]. The 45% improvement over the original AFHMM is particularly notable.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that affect its readability and impact. First, the excessive use of acronyms (SAC, AFHMM, NDE, SAE, etc.) without sufficient explanation makes the paper difficult to follow. The authors introduce "signal aggregate constraints" but immediately refer to them as SACs without defining the term consistently, and they use 11-letter acronyms like "AFHMM" without clear justification. This makes the paper challenging for readers unfamiliar with the specific energy disaggregation literature.

Second, the connection between SACs and posterior regularization requires more careful explanation. While Section 6 mentions this relationship, the paper doesn't clearly explain how their constraint formulation relates to the posterior regularization framework. Specifically, the authors state that "the constraint terms could be viewed as the logarithm of the prior distributions," but this is not developed sufficiently to show how SACs fit within the broader context of constrained inference methods.

Third, the paper doesn't adequately address how the constraint values μi0 would be obtained in real-world applications. In their experiments, they use the true total consumption (TTC) as an oracle value, which would not be available in practice. While they propose using national total consumption (NTC) as a practical approximation, they don't discuss how reliable this approximation is across different households or how it might be improved. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of practical methods for estimating μi0, perhaps referencing existing work on appliance energy modeling [23].

The paper also lacks sufficient comparison with recent NIPS work on energy disaggregation. While they reference [12] as state-of-the-art, there have been several relevant NIPS papers since then, such as those exploring deep learning approaches to load monitoring [24]. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen their claims about the significance of their contribution.

The writing quality is a major concern. The paper contains numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasings that make it difficult to follow (e.g., "the total signal often varies widely across the unknown sources, and we often have a good idea of what total values to expect" could be simplified to "the total signal magnitudes vary significantly across sources, and domain knowledge often provides reasonable estimates of these values"). The excessive use of passive voice ("it is assumed that," "it was shown that") further reduces clarity.

The paper would benefit from several specific improvements: (1) a glossary of acronyms to aid readability, (2) clearer explanation of the relationship between SACs and posterior regularization, (3) a more thorough discussion of practical methods for obtaining μi0, and (4) a more comprehensive comparison with recent NIPS work in the energy disaggregation domain.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound approach to a meaningful problem in blind source separation with compelling empirical results. The SAC concept is novel and well-motivated, and the convex optimization formulation is elegant. However, the poor writing quality and lack of sufficient discussion on practical implementation details significantly reduce the paper's impact. With the suggested revisions, this work would make a strong contribution to the field.
