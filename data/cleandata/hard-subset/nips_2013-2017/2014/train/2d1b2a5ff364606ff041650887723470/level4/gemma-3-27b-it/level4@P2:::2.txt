This paper addresses the challenging problem of blind source separation, specifically in the context of energy disaggregation. The authors introduce signal aggregate constraints (SACs) – a method for incorporating domain knowledge about expected total energy consumption for individual appliances – into an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM). They demonstrate that incorporating these constraints significantly improves disaggregation performance on a real-world dataset, exceeding the performance of both the original AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach.

The core idea is well-motivated. Energy disaggregation inherently suffers from identifiability issues; knowing *a priori* reasonable energy consumption ranges for appliances can substantially improve the quality of the separation. The proposed SACs provide a principled way to encode this knowledge. The approach of formulating the problem as a convex quadratic program is also a significant contribution, enabling efficient inference. The results on the HES dataset are compelling, showing substantial improvements in both normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE). 

However, the paper suffers from clarity issues. The writing is dense, and the frequent use of acronyms (especially SAC, AFHMM, and AFAMAP) hinders readability. While common in this field, some acronyms are lengthy and appear unnecessarily often, disrupting the flow. The connection to posterior regularization, while interesting, is presented somewhat abruptly and could benefit from a more intuitive explanation. The description of the relaxed optimization problem and the derivation of the convex quadratic program could also be clarified. While mathematically sound, the presentation feels rushed.  A more detailed explanation of the correspondence between the constraint parameters (δi, λi) and their effect on the results would be valuable.

Regarding originality, while the AFHMM itself is not new, the introduction of SACs as a general framework for incorporating domain knowledge into BSS and specifically adapting it for energy disaggregation represents a novel contribution. The connection to posterior regularization adds further depth. The paper appropriately references related work in energy disaggregation, probabilistic modeling, and constraint-based inference. 

In terms of significance, the results demonstrate a tangible improvement in energy disaggregation accuracy, a problem with significant practical implications for energy conservation and consumer awareness. The authors convincingly argue that the SAC approach is broadly applicable to other BSS problems where prior knowledge about aggregate values is available. The development of an efficient convex program for inference further enhances the practical utility of the method.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty: SACs offer a principled way to incorporate domain knowledge into BSS, a generally challenging problem.
*   Strong Empirical Results: Significant improvements in disaggregation performance on a real-world dataset.
*   Computational Efficiency: The formulation as a convex quadratic program enables efficient inference.
*   Potential Impact: Applicable to a range of BSS problems beyond energy disaggregation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Clarity: The paper is difficult to read due to dense writing and excessive use of acronyms.
*   Presentation: The derivation and explanation of the optimization problem could be more accessible.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a worthwhile contribution to the field. The core idea is sound, the results are promising, and the method has the potential to impact practical applications. However, significant improvements in clarity and presentation are needed. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions** contingent upon addressing the clarity issues and providing a more accessible explanation of the technical details. Specifically, the authors should focus on reducing the density of the writing, minimizing the use of acronyms, and elaborating on the connection to posterior regularization and the optimization formulation.
