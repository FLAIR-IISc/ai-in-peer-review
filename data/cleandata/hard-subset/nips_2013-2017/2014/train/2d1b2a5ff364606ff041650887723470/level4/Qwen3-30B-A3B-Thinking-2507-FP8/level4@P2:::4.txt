

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation problems, particularly applied to energy disaggregation. SACs require the total signal for each source to be close to a specified value, addressing the identifiability problem inherent in such tasks. The authors incorporate SACs into Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs), formulating a convex quadratic program for approximate inference. The approach is evaluated on real-world energy disaggregation data from 100 UK households (with low-frequency readings) and 9 homes with mains measurements, demonstrating significant improvements over baseline methods.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the energy disaggregation literature, which has seen substantial interest at NIPS (e.g., [8, 12, 15, 22]). While prior work has explored constraints on HMM state transitions (e.g., AFAMAP [12]), SACs represent a novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge about expected total energy consumption. This complements recent frameworks like posterior regularization [4] by providing a concrete, generalizable constraint type applicable across domains. The authors' evaluation on real-world data provides compelling evidence of SACs' practical utility, with AFHMM+SAC achieving a 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM and 29% improvement over state-of-the-art AFAMAP.

Strengths include:
1. A clear, well-motivated problem statement that effectively connects SACs to practical energy disaggregation needs
2. A technically sound convex optimization approach that enables efficient inference despite the non-Markovian nature of SACs
3. Comprehensive empirical evaluation on multiple real-world datasets, with meaningful metrics (NDE and SAE) that capture both signal-level and aggregate performance
4. Strong results demonstrating significant practical improvements (33% and 16% better NDE and SAE, respectively, over AFAMAP using national consumption data)

The paper would benefit from addressing several points to strengthen its contribution:
1. The authors conflate model and algorithm terminology. Specifically, when comparing "AFHMM" versus "AFAMAP," they should clarify that AFAMAP is an algorithm for approximately computing MAP solutions to the AFHMM model, while the baseline "AFHMM" likely refers to the alternating Viterbi optimization approach. This confusion is particularly problematic when suggesting that SACs could be incorporated into AFAMAP (as they do in Section 7.2.2), since AFAMAP is an inference algorithm, not a model.

2. The relationship between SACs and other constraint approaches deserves deeper exploration. While the authors note that SACs are orthogonal to AFAMAP's single-chain transition constraint, they don't discuss how these might be combined. The paper's claim that "SACs raise difficult inference issues" due to non-Markovian constraints could be strengthened by comparing the computational complexity and solution quality of SACs versus other constraint types.

3. The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the relaxation from the integer problem to the convex quadratic program. Specifically, the authors should clarify whether the resulting non-integral solutions still achieve meaningful improvements over the baseline, and how these solutions compare to the integer solutions that would be obtained via exact inference (if feasible).

4. The motivation for why SACs improve performance beyond what AFAMAP achieves needs further elaboration. The paper shows SACs improve results, but doesn't explain whether this is because SACs capture complementary information to AFAMAP's constraints or because they directly address a different aspect of the identifiability problem.

The paper demonstrates significant practical value with its application to energy disaggregation, a problem of increasing importance in smart grid research. The method is technically sound, and the empirical results are compelling. While the core idea of SACs is relatively simple, their effective application to a challenging real-world problem with measurable performance gains constitutes a meaningful contribution to the field.

The authors should consider incorporating SACs into the AFAMAP framework to explore whether the combination yields even better results, as this would further demonstrate the flexibility and potential of their constraint approach. This would also help clarify the relationship between SACs and other constraint methods.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the energy disaggregation literature. The technical approach is sound, the empirical evaluation is thorough, and the results demonstrate significant practical improvements. With minor revisions to clarify terminology and better articulate the relationship between SACs and existing constraint methods, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. The work is well-suited to NIPS' focus on both theoretical advances and practical applications, and I believe it meets the conference's standards for quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
