This paper introduces a novel blind source separation technique grounded in an additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework, enhanced by the incorporation of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The core idea is to leverage domain-specific knowledge – in this instance, expected appliance usage patterns – to improve the decomposition of time series data into constituent components. The work focuses on the application of energy disaggregation, aiming to more accurately separate the energy consumption of individual appliances within a household. The resulting objective function, representing the log posterior distribution, is skillfully recast as a convex optimization problem, enabling efficient solution. The method’s performance is demonstrated convincingly through both simulations using synthetic data, and analysis of real-world domestic energy consumption data.

The paper is technically robust, featuring a strong experimental evaluation. As anticipated, the proposed method outperforms standard AFHMM and the recent AFAMAP algorithm – both of which lack the SAC enhancements – with statistically significant improvements confirmed via t-tests. Computational efficiency is also reported to be comparable to AFAMAP. A point of clarification regarding the results would be helpful: the choice of a low noise variance in the toy example warrants further explanation. Is this intended to clearly illustrate the separation capability, or does it reflect realistic signal-to-noise ratios? Similarly, commenting on the signal-to-noise ratio between mains readings and aggregate appliance output in the energy application would be beneficial. 

The theoretical development is well-organized and clearly presented. While the overall approach is sound, a formal proof demonstrating the convexity of the procedure outlined in equation (6) would strengthen the manuscript. Furthermore, the inclusion of theoretical scaling analysis – relating computational complexity to data length and the number of hidden states – would complement the empirical findings. Addressing the scalability of the method to larger datasets and state spaces, and contrasting its performance with AFAMAP in such scenarios, would be valuable.

The paper is well-written and logically structured, with a strong introduction that effectively motivates the problem and contextualizes it within the existing literature. While I lack expertise in this specific area, the literature review appears comprehensive. A minor notational concern arises on page 4 (line 202) in the equation preceding (6); consistency with the notation used on page 3 suggests the commas in the subscripts of 'p' may be unnecessary.

To the best of my knowledge, this work presents an original contribution. However, I defer to experts within the HMM community and the energy application domain to assess the broader significance of these results. 

Addendum: The authors have provided satisfactory responses to my initial queries, and I recommend incorporating these clarifications into a revised version of the manuscript. This work introduces a promising blind source separation method utilizing Additive Factorial HMMs with Signal Aggregate Constraints, demonstrating strong performance compared to existing approaches, particularly within the context of energy disaggregation.
