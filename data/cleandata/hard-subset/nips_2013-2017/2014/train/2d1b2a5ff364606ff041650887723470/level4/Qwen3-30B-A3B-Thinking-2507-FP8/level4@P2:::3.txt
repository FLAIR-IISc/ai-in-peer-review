

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) to address the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS), particularly for energy disaggregation. The authors propose incorporating SACs into Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs), where each source's total signal is constrained to be close to a specified value. The key technical contribution is transforming the constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP) for efficient approximate inference. The method is evaluated on both synthetic data and real-world energy disaggregation tasks, showing significant improvements over the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach (AFAMAP).

**Quality**: The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical foundation is sound, with a clear derivation of the CQP formulation from the constrained MAP inference problem. The experimental design is robust, with comprehensive evaluation on both synthetic data (to validate the method's robustness to constraint specification) and real-world energy data (from the Household Electricity Survey). The results show consistent improvement in both NDE (Normalized Disaggregation Error) and SAE (Signal Aggregate Error) metrics, with statistically significant improvements over AFAMAP (33% and 16% improvements in NDE and SAE, respectively). The authors properly address the identifiability challenge by incorporating domain knowledge about expected appliance energy usage, which is a critical limitation in standard BSS approaches.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The problem statement, motivation, and technical contributions are presented logically. The mathematical formulation is precise and well-explained, making the CQP derivation accessible to readers familiar with HMMs and optimization. The experimental section provides sufficient detail about the datasets, evaluation metrics, and implementation choices (e.g., state assignments, hyperparameter settings). The figures (particularly Figure 1) effectively illustrate the relationship between constraint specification and performance, though the interpretation of the results for large constraint distances could be clarified.

**Originality**: The introduction of SACs represents a novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge in BSS. While previous work has explored posterior regularization and similar frameworks, the authors identify a specific class of constraints (aggregate signal values) that are both practically relevant and computationally feasible. This distinguishes their work from prior constraints like the AFAMAP state constraint (at most one chain changing at any time) or duration-based constraints. The paper thoroughly contextualizes SACs within the broader literature on energy disaggregation and BSS, with appropriate citations to recent NIPS and related work.

**Significance**: The significance of this work is substantial. Energy disaggregation is an important practical problem with real-world impact, and the authors demonstrate that SACs can significantly improve performance with minimal computational overhead (similar inference times to AFHMM and AFAMAP). The method has clear potential for broader application in other BSS domains where aggregate signal constraints are known. The 45% reduction in NDE compared to the baseline AFHMM and 29% improvement over AFAMAP on real-world data represent meaningful advances in the state of the art.

**Strengths**: The paper is exceptionally strong in its problem formulation, theoretical grounding, and empirical validation. The motivation for SACs is compelling, as it directly addresses a critical limitation in BSS (identifiability) using domain knowledge that is often available in practical applications. The CQP formulation provides an elegant solution to the otherwise intractable constrained optimization problem. The evaluation on real-world data with meaningful metrics (NDE and SAE) demonstrates clear practical value.

**Areas for Improvement**:
1. The comparison with AFAMAP could be strengthened by showing results against other recent methods that might incorporate similar constraints (e.g., [12] or [16]). While AFAMAP is a reasonable baseline, it uses fewer constraints than SACAFHMM, which may make the comparison somewhat unbalanced. It would be valuable to clarify whether SACs could be incorporated into AFAMAP or other approaches without significant computational overhead.

2. Figure 1 presents results showing that for large Euclidean distances between the specified constraint vector and the true aggregate values, AFAMAP outperforms SACAFHMM. This contradicts the paper's claim that "as the Euclidean distance between the input vector µ0 and the true signal aggregate vector increases, both the NDE and SAE increase." The authors should clarify whether this represents a failure case for SACs or if the results for large distances are statistically significant. The discussion should acknowledge that poorly specified constraints can degrade performance, which is an important consideration for practical application.

3. The paper focuses exclusively on aggregate signal constraints but does not explore how to incorporate more complex constraints (e.g., "heater and cooler cannot be active simultaneously" or "grinder usage duration < 10 minutes"). While the authors note that "a series of constraints could be made," they do not explore the computational implications or provide examples of how such constraints would be formulated. A brief discussion of the generalizability of the SAC framework to other constraint types would strengthen the paper's contribution.

**Overall Assessment**: This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of blind source separation with practical applications in energy disaggregation. The SAC framework is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages. The minor concerns about comparison fairness, interpretation of Figure 1, and potential extension to more complex constraints are important but do not detract from the paper's overall strength. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address these points.
