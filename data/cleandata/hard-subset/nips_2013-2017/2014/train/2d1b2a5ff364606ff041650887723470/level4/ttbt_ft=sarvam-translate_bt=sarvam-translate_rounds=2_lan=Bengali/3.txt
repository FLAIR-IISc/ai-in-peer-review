Additive Factorial HMM (AFHMM) proposes a solution to blind source separation problems with Signal Aggregate Constraints (SAC). The authors propose a convex quadratic problem for relaxed AFHMM with SAC. This technique shows promising results in both synthetic and real-world data.

Pros:
- The paper is well-written and has a good mathematical foundation.
- The motivation for the problem is good.
- Evaluation results indicate good improvements in performance metrics, NDE, and SAA.

Areas for improvement:
- The results are shown as improvements compared to AFHMM without SAC and AFMAPP. Both of these use less data than SACAFHMM, which seems like an unfair comparison. Is there any other method that can use SAC but is not as efficient in calculations?
- Figure 1 is not clear. It seems that AFMAPP performs better than SACAFHMM for distances greater than 104. Could this be due to poor performance limitations? The discussion suggests that the results are always good, which seems misleading.
- I expected the algorithm to include more complex limitations. For example, a person cannot use a heater and a cooler at the same time. What if no more than 3 out of 10 bulbs are active in the house at the same time? There may be a limit of less than 10 minutes for things like grinders. In short, different types of limitations can be specified. Is it possible to extend the algorithm for other types of limitations? The paper is well-written and provides a good motivation for the problem. Future work is to further extend the model for other types of limitations.
