This paper introduces a novel approach to blind source separation by formulating the problem within an additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework and incorporating signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The core motivation is to leverage domain knowledge – specifically, expected aggregate behavior of source components – to improve separation performance. The authors demonstrate this concept through the application of energy disaggregation, aiming to decompose total household energy consumption into the contributions of individual appliances. The resulting objective function, representing the log posterior distribution of hidden component states given observed data, is skillfully transformed into a convex optimization problem, enabling efficient solution. The method’s effectiveness is empirically validated using both synthetic and real-world datasets derived from domestic energy meter readings.

The paper is technically strong, with a compelling experimental evaluation. As expected, the proposed method outperforms standard AFHMM and the recent AFAMAP algorithm, which lack the benefit of SACs. The performance gains are statistically significant, as demonstrated by t-tests, and the computational cost is comparable to AFAMAP. A minor point of clarification concerns the low noise variance employed in the toy example. While the clear separation in Figure 2 is illustrative, it would be helpful to understand the rationale for this choice. Is it intended to highlight the method’s capabilities, or do such signal-to-noise ratios commonly occur in real-world data? Relatedly, the authors could enrich the discussion by reporting the signal-to-noise ratio observed between the aggregate meter readings and individual appliance outputs in the energy disaggregation application.

The theoretical foundations of the paper are well-presented and logically structured. To further strengthen the work, a formal proof establishing the convexity of the procedure outlined in equation (6) would be beneficial, as the provided reasoning isn't entirely conclusive. Moreover, incorporating theoretical scaling analyses – considering data length and the number of hidden states – would complement the empirical findings. Specifically, addressing the tractability of the method with significantly larger datasets and state spaces, and contrasting its scalability with that of AFAMAP, would provide valuable insights.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and provides a comprehensive overview of relevant literature. While my familiarity with this specific problem domain is limited, I find the literature review to be thorough, though a more expert reader may identify potential omissions. I noted a potential typographical error on page 4, line 202, in the unnumbered equation preceding equation (6). The commas in the subscripts of ‘p’ appear inconsistent with the notation established on page 3; their removal would align with previous conventions, unless a new notation is being introduced.

To the best of my knowledge, the work presents a novel and original contribution. However, I am not sufficiently specialized to assess the significance of these results within the broader HMM community or for the energy application domain. 

**Addendum:** The authors have provided satisfactory responses to my questions during the feedback phase, and I recommend incorporating these clarifications into a revised version of the paper. In summary, this paper proposes an effective method for blind source separation utilizing Additive Factorial HMMs with Signal Aggregate Constraints. The method achieves strong performance, particularly in the context of real-world energy disaggregation, and represents a valuable advancement in the field.
