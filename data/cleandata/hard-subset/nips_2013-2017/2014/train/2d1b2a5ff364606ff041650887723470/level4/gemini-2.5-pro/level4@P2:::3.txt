This paper addresses the challenging problem of unidentifiability in blind source separation (BSS), particularly within the context of additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs). The core contribution is the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge into the BSS problem. The key idea is that for many applications, such as the paper's focus on energy disaggregation, one often has prior knowledge about the total expected signal from each source over a given period (e.g., the typical daily energy consumption of a refrigerator).

The authors formulate a new model, AFHMM+SAC, which augments the standard AFHMM objective function with a penalty term that encourages the inferred source signals to match these known aggregate values. As these global constraints break the Markov property, making standard inference algorithms like the Viterbi algorithm inapplicable, the authors propose an efficient approximate inference scheme. They relax the integer program for MAP inference into a convex quadratic program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The method is evaluated on both synthetic data and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, where it is shown to significantly outperform a standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP.

**Strengths**

1.  **Quality & Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and presents its mathematical formulation rigorously. The motivation is excellent; the problem of unidentifiability is a fundamental barrier to the practical application of BSS models, and the paper proposes a simple yet powerful form of domain knowledge to address it. The proposed solution, framing the relaxed inference problem as a CQP, is technically sound and provides an elegant method for incorporating the proposed constraints.

2.  **Originality:** The idea of using signal aggregate values as a form of prior constraint in AFHMMs is novel. While the use of prior constraints in probabilistic models is not new (as noted in the related work on posterior regularization), the specific class of constraints proposed here is well-suited for many BSS problems and has not been explored in this context. The formulation of the problem as a CQP for efficient inference is also a solid contribution.

3.  **Significance:** The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate clear practical value. On the real-world energy data, the proposed AFHMM+SAC achieves a 45% error reduction over the baseline AFHMM and a 29% reduction over the state-of-the-art AFAMAP. This is a substantial improvement on a difficult problem and suggests that the approach is highly effective and likely to be adopted by researchers in this area.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Framing of Experimental Comparison:** The proposed AFHMM+SAC model is compared against baselines (AFHMM, AFAMAP) that, by design, do not use the additional prior information encapsulated in the SACs. While the results convincingly demonstrate the benefit of using this information, the comparison could be perceived as slightly imbalanced, as the proposed model has access to more data (in the form of priors). The paper would be strengthened by a discussion that explicitly acknowledges this, framing the contribution not just as a superior model, but as an effective framework for incorporating a specific, valuable type of prior knowledge. It would be interesting to know if there are alternative, perhaps less efficient, ways of incorporating similar aggregate knowledge into the baseline models for a more direct comparison.

2.  **Sensitivity to Constraint Quality:** The toy data experiment in Section 7.1 and Figure 1 is very informative, as it correctly shows that performance degrades as the provided constraint values (μ₀) deviate from the true aggregate values. However, the discussion of this result could be more nuanced. The figure suggests that with a sufficiently poor prior (e.g., Euclidean distance > 10⁴), the method's error increases substantially. Acknowledging this trade-off—that a poorly specified constraint can be actively detrimental to performance—is important for providing a complete and balanced picture of the method's practical applicability. This would help a practitioner understand the risks associated with using inaccurate domain knowledge.

3.  **Generality of the Constraint Framework:** The paper focuses exclusively on signal *aggregate* constraints (i.e., sums over time). While this is a novel and useful contribution, the energy disaggregation domain is rich with other potential constraints that could further improve identifiability. Examples include:
    *   **Mutual exclusion:** An air conditioner and a heater are unlikely to be on simultaneously.
    *   **Temporal duration:** A microwave runs for minutes, while a refrigerator runs for hours.
    *   **Cardinality:** No more than a certain number of lights are likely to be on at once.
    The paper would be significantly enhanced by a discussion on whether the proposed CQP framework could be extended to incorporate these more complex, logical constraints. This would provide a clearer roadmap for future work and better frame the significance of the current contribution as a foundational step in a broader research direction.

**Summary**

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the BSS literature. It introduces a practical and well-motivated class of constraints and provides a sound and efficient inference method to incorporate them. The experimental results clearly validate the approach. The primary weaknesses are minor and relate to the framing of the experimental comparison and a limited discussion of the method's sensitivity and potential for generalization. Addressing these points, perhaps in the discussion or conclusion, would further strengthen an already solid piece of work. The paper is a clear candidate for acceptance.
