This paper introduces an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) augmented with signal aggregate constraints (SAC) to tackle blind source separation. The authors formulate a convex quadratic optimization for a relaxed AFHMM with SAC and report promising results on both synthetic and real datasets.

Pros:
- The paper is clearly written and grounded in solid mathematical analysis.
- The problem is well motivated.
- Empirical results show substantial improvements in NDE and SAE.

Areas for improvement:
- Fairness of comparisons: The reported gains are against AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP, both of which use less information than SAC-AFHMM. This makes the comparison potentially unfair. Are there baseline methods that can also incorporate SAC (even if computationally heavier) so that the benefit of SAC can be isolated from the benefit of the particular optimization technique?
- Interpretation of Figure 1: The figure appears to show that for distances above 10^4, AFAMAP outperforms SAC-AFHMM. Does this imply that poorly specified or noisy constraints can significantly degrade performance? The discussion seems to suggest that SAC always helps, which may be misleading without a careful treatment of failure modes and sensitivity to constraint quality.
- Constraint expressiveness: I expected to see richer classes of constraints. Examples include:
  - Mutual exclusivity: the heater and cooler cannot be on simultaneously.
  - Global cardinality: no more than 3 of 10 bulbs can be active at once.
  - Temporal/duration constraints: appliances like grinders operate for less than 10 minutes.
  Please discuss whether the proposed framework can be extended to support these constraints (e.g., via linear inequalities, integer variables, or soft penalties), and the resulting computational trade-offs.

Minor suggestions:
- Clarify how SAC is obtained in practice (data sources, noise models for aggregates) and how robust the method is to misspecified or inconsistent aggregates.
- Provide ablations to separate the effects of the convex relaxation from the inclusion of SAC, and sensitivity analyses showing performance as constraints are perturbed or partially incorrect.

Overall assessment:
The paper is well written, well motivated, and shows encouraging empirical performance. Addressing the fairness of the comparisons and providing a more nuanced discussion of robustness to constraint quality would strengthen the contribution. Extending the framework to a broader set of constraints and analyzing the associated complexity–accuracy trade-offs would be valuable directions for future work.
