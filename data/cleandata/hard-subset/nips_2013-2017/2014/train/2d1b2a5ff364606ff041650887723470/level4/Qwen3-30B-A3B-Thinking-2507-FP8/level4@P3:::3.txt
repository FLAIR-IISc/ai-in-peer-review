

# Review for "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) as a novel approach to address the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS) for energy disaggregation. The authors observe that in many BSS applications, the total signal for each source varies widely and often has known expected values (e.g., a toaster might use 50 Wh per day). They incorporate these constraints into an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM), transforming the inference problem into a convex quadratic program. The paper demonstrates significant improvements over baseline AFHMM and AFAMAP on both synthetic and real-world energy disaggregation data.

The work builds upon previous efforts to incorporate domain knowledge into probabilistic models, particularly the posterior regularization framework [4] and related approaches [13, 14]. Unlike prior methods that focus on state-level constraints (e.g., AFAMAP's "at most one chain changes state at any time" constraint [12]), SACs operate on aggregate signal values across the entire time series. This is a significant conceptual advance as it identifies a class of domain knowledge that is generally applicable across BSS problems but has been previously underutilized in the literature.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) the introduction of SACs for BSS problems; (2) a convex quadratic program formulation for approximate inference with SACs; and (3) an evaluation demonstrating the effectiveness of SACs in energy disaggregation. The authors show that SACs improve normalized disaggregation error (NDE) by 45% over the original AFHMM and 29% over a recent state-of-the-art approach [12].

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a solid theoretical foundation. The authors provide a clear derivation of the convex quadratic program formulation, and the connection to posterior regularization [4] is well-established. The evaluation is thorough, using both synthetic data to demonstrate robustness to constraint specification errors and real-world energy data to show practical utility. The computational complexity analysis shows that AFHMM+SAC scales similarly to AFHMM and AFAMAP, which is important for real-world applications. One concern is the comparison with AFAMAP: the authors use AFAMAP's standard implementation (which doesn't incorporate SACs), but it's unclear whether AFAMAP could be adapted to use SACs with similar computational efficiency. The paper would benefit from a more direct comparison with approaches that might also incorporate similar domain knowledge.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear mathematical formulations. The problem setting, model extensions, and optimization approach are presented logically. The use of both synthetic and real-world evaluations helps readers understand the method's behavior across different scenarios. The figures (particularly Figure 1) would be clearer with more detailed axis labels and a better explanation of the relationship between constraint specification error and performance degradation.

**Originality:** The SAC approach represents a meaningful contribution to the literature. While prior work has explored various constraints for HMMs [10, 11, 12, 19], SACs specifically target aggregate signal properties rather than temporal or state-level constraints. This is a novel direction that addresses a fundamental limitation of BSS problems. The paper effectively demonstrates how SACs differ from previous approaches and why they are particularly suited for energy disaggregation (where total daily usage is well-known for common appliances).

**Significance:** The significance of this work is strong, particularly for energy disaggregation applications. The 29% improvement over state-of-the-art methods on real-world data represents a meaningful advance. More broadly, the paper identifies a class of domain knowledge that could be applied across multiple BSS domains, not just energy disaggregation. The approach is practical, as the required aggregate values can often be obtained from existing surveys or expert knowledge (e.g., [23]). This bridges the gap between theoretical BSS and practical applications where domain knowledge is available.

**Strengths:** 
- Clear problem formulation with strong motivation from energy disaggregation
- Solid theoretical foundation connecting SACs to posterior regularization
- Thorough evaluation on both synthetic and real data
- Practical implementation with reasonable computational complexity
- Well-written with clear mathematical formulations

**Weaknesses:**
- The comparison with AFAMAP may be misleading since AFAMAP doesn't incorporate SACs, while the authors' approach does. It would be valuable to see how SACs could be integrated into AFAMAP or whether other methods could benefit similarly from SACs.
- Figure 1's interpretation is confusing; it suggests that with large constraint errors, AFAMAP outperforms SACAFHMM, yet the paper claims SACs "always improve performance." The discussion should address this potential limitation.
- The paper focuses on a single type of constraint (aggregate values), but energy disaggregation involves many other natural constraints (e.g., "a heater and cooler cannot operate simultaneously," "a blender cannot run for more than 10 minutes"). The paper would be stronger if it discussed whether the framework could accommodate these other constraints.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The SAC concept is novel and addresses a significant gap in BSS approaches
- The paper provides strong empirical evidence of the approach's effectiveness
- The theoretical foundation is solid and well-connected to existing literature
- The method has clear practical applications in energy disaggregation and potentially other domains

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The comparison with AFAMAP is not entirely fair as it doesn't incorporate the same constraint framework
- The paper doesn't sufficiently explore the limitations of SACs or how they might be extended to other constraint types
- The results in Figure 1 suggest potential performance degradation with poor constraint specification, which isn't adequately addressed

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the blind source separation literature. The SAC concept is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear practical benefits. With minor revisions addressing the comparison with AFAMAP and the discussion of constraint limitations, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The authors should clarify the relationship between SACs and other constraint types, and more thoroughly discuss the potential limitations of their approach when constraints are misspecified.
