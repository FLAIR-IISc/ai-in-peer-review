Summary
This paper extends the additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) by introducing soft aggregate constraints that encourage the sum of individual chain outputs to match a known aggregate signal. The authors develop a convex relaxation for MAP inference under these constraints and evaluate the approach on two real-world energy disaggregation settings: (i) 100 UK homes with low-frequency meter readings and (ii) 10 homes with higher-frequency readings but without per-appliance submetering. While the methodological ideas are relatively straightforward, the work is compelling in its combination of a clean optimization formulation with solid empirical evaluation on sizable datasets.

Strengths
- Practical relevance: The aggregate-constraint idea directly matches the structure of energy disaggregation and similar source-separation problems.
- Convex relaxation: The proposed relaxation appears simple and scalable, making it attractive for large datasets.
- Empirical scope: Experiments on substantial real-world datasets increase the paper’s credibility and potential impact.
- Clarity of motivation: Using known aggregate totals to regularize disaggregation is intuitive and well motivated.

Main questions and suggestions
- Relationship to AFAMAP and integrality: A key motivation behind AFAMAP’s “one chain changes state at a time” constraint is that it often improves the relaxation’s tightness, yielding integral (or near-integral) solutions more frequently. Does the proposed SACAFHMM optimization achieve a similar effect? Specifically:
  - How often does the relaxation return integral solutions in practice, and how does this compare to AFAMAP?
  - When solutions are fractional, do they nonetheless lead to lower reconstruction error than AFAMAP’s typically more integral solutions?
  - Please discuss the empirical trade-off between integrality and accuracy, and whether the aggregate constraints tighten the relaxation.

- Orthogonality and potential combination of ideas: Conceptually, AFAMAP’s “single-chain transition” constraint and the SACAFHMM aggregate constraints appear orthogonal. It seems feasible to incorporate the SACAFHMM constraints into the AFAMAP formulation. I encourage the authors to consider (or at least discuss) this combined approach, which could plausibly yield tighter relaxations and improved performance. Even a brief feasibility discussion and expected computational impact would be helpful for the camera-ready version.

- Clarify models vs. inference procedures: The manuscript occasionally conflates the AFHMM model with specific optimization procedures. For instance, comparisons labeled “AFHMM” versus “AFAMAP” are imprecise: AFAMAP is an algorithm for (approximately) computing MAP solutions to the AFHMM, whereas “AFHMM” in the experiments appears to correspond to alternating Viterbi or another heuristic inference method. Please:
  - Use consistent nomenclature that distinguishes the probabilistic model (AFHMM) from inference algorithms (e.g., AFAMAP, alternating Viterbi).
  - State explicitly which inference method each baseline uses and how hyperparameters are tuned.
  - If feasible, report results using a common set of constraints across algorithms to isolate the contribution of the inference method.

Additional recommendations for clarity and completeness
- Constraint strength and robustness: Since the method relies on a known or estimated aggregate, report sensitivity to the weight of the aggregate constraint and to noise or bias in the aggregate measurements. How does performance degrade if the aggregate is misspecified?
- Computational profile: Provide wall-clock times and scaling behavior relative to the number of chains, states per chain, and time steps, and compare to AFAMAP and alternating Viterbi on representative datasets.
- Evaluation details: Briefly justify the choice of datasets and sampling rates, and clarify whether training and test periods are separated. If appliance-level ground truth is unavailable in the higher-frequency dataset, explain how accuracy is assessed.
- Practical guidance: Offer recommendations for selecting the aggregate-constraint penalty and any regularization parameters, as well as default settings that worked well across homes.

Overall assessment
The paper proposes a simple and sensible extension to AFHMMs and introduces an effective convex relaxation for incorporating aggregate constraints. The empirical results on real-world energy data are convincing, and the approach seems scalable and practically useful. The primary improvements needed are clarifications around the distinction between models and inference algorithms, a more explicit comparison to AFAMAP in terms of relaxation tightness and integrality, and a discussion of how the proposed constraints could be combined with AFAMAP’s structure. With these clarifications and analyses, I believe the paper would be a strong contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
