This paper introduces a novel approach for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation (BSS) problems through the use of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The core idea is that for many BSS applications, one has prior knowledge about the expected total signal value for each source over a given period. The authors formulate this idea within the framework of an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) and apply it to the challenging real-world problem of energy disaggregation. Due to the non-Markovian nature of the constraints, exact MAP inference is intractable. The authors address this by developing a relaxed optimization problem that can be solved efficiently as a convex quadratic program (CQP). The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on both a synthetic dataset and a real-world energy consumption dataset, where it shows significant improvements over the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints is an intuitive, novel, and generalizable method for incorporating valuable domain knowledge into BSS models. This directly addresses the critical issue of source identifiability. The application to low-frequency energy disaggregation is timely and demonstrates clear practical importance, showing a significant advance over existing techniques.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of SACs as a penalty term in the AFHMM objective function is well-motivated, and the derivation of an efficient CQP for approximate inference is a solid contribution.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is strong and convincing. The proposed AFHMM+SAC model substantially outperforms both the baseline AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP on a real-world energy dataset, with improvements shown to be statistically significant. The authors also thoughtfully include an "oracle" experiment (using the True Total Consumption) which effectively illustrates the maximum potential benefit of their approach.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The problem is well-motivated in the introduction, and the proposed method is described in sufficient detail.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

While this is a strong paper, addressing the following points could further enhance its clarity and impact in the final version.

*   **Experimental Details:**
    *   In the toy data experiment (Section 7.1), the noise variance is set to a very low value (σ² = 0.01). It would be helpful to justify this choice. Are such high signal-to-noise ratios typical in real-world applications? A brief discussion on the method's robustness to higher levels of noise would strengthen this section.
    *   For the energy disaggregation experiments (Section 7.2), it would be beneficial to comment on the typical signal-to-noise ratio, specifically the proportion of the measured mains signal that is accounted for by the modeled appliances versus unmodeled background load. This would provide valuable context for the results.

*   **Technical Justification:**
    *   The convexity of the relaxed optimization problem in Equation (6) is central to the proposed inference method. While plausible, the claim is not explicitly proven. The paper would be strengthened by including a brief argument or proof sketch confirming that the objective function L(S,H) is indeed concave (since it is a maximization problem) after the relaxation.
    *   The empirical results show that the proposed method's runtime is comparable to that of competitors. This is a good practical result, but the paper would benefit from a theoretical complexity analysis. A discussion of how the method scales with the time series length (T), the number of HMM chains (I), and the number of states per chain (K) would be a valuable addition and would complement the empirical findings.

*   **Minor Point:**
    *   There appears to be a minor notational inconsistency in the unnumbered equation preceding Equation (6) on page 4. The subscripts for the transition probabilities `p` seem to include commas (`p(i)jk`), which differs from the notation used earlier on page 3. This should be checked for consistency.

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents a novel and effective method for improving blind source separation by incorporating signal aggregate constraints. The approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates significant performance gains on a challenging real-world problem. The weaknesses identified are minor and can be addressed with additional clarification in the final manuscript. The authors' responses during the discussion phase satisfactorily addressed my initial questions. I believe this work is a valuable contribution to the field and recommend acceptance.
