This paper proposes incorporating signal aggregate constraints (SACs) into additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs) for blind source separation, with a focus on energy disaggregation. The key idea is to encode domain knowledge about the total expected signal of each latent source over a time horizon (e.g., daily energy usage of appliances) and to enforce these as soft constraints during inference. The authors formulate MAP inference with SACs and show that, after introducing transition indicator variables, a convex quadratic program (CQP) arises under a relaxation of the binary constraints. They further interpret the approach via posterior regularization, demonstrating that the SAC-penalized objective corresponds to an optimally constrained posterior. Empirically, they evaluate on a synthetic example and on the UK HES dataset, reporting sizable improvements over AFHMM and the AFAMAP method, with comparable runtime.

Relation to prior work: AFHMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan) are a standard model for additive sources; AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola) imposes temporal change constraints but does not use aggregate information. Posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.) provides a general framework for constrained posteriors, which the authors leverage to motivate their penalty. The proposed SACs represent a distinct and practical class of constraints that cut across application domains, particularly natural for energy disaggregation.

Strengths
- Quality/technical soundness: The modeling is coherent, the CQP relaxation is well motivated, and the posterior-regularization view is elegant. The inference scheme is clearly specified and uses standard convex tools. The empirical section is extensive and uses realistic, low-frequency mains data, a challenging setting.
- Originality: SACs are a simple but novel way to inject domain knowledge that is often available but underused. Framing AFHMM+SAC as a convex relaxation and connecting it to posterior regularization are conceptually appealing.
- Significance: The reported improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP are substantial (both in normalized disaggregation error and aggregate error), with statistical significance checks. Given the importance of energy disaggregation and the generality of BSS, the method is likely to be useful to practitioners.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written, with a good overview of related approaches and applications.

Weaknesses and questions
- Toy example noise level: The synthetic experiment uses a very small observation noise variance. Is this representative of realistic SNRs in the targeted applications, or primarily for visualization? Please justify the choice and, ideally, provide sensitivity to SNR to avoid an “overly easy” setup. Relatedly, it would help to report typical SNRs between mains and aggregate appliance readings in the HES data.
- Convexity and relaxation: While Λi and Vt are PSD, a brief, formal argument that the relaxed problem in Eq. (6) is convex (objective and feasible set) would strengthen the exposition. In addition, please clarify how fractional solutions are handled: is there a rounding scheme to obtain valid discrete state sequences, and how does this affect performance?
- Computational scaling: Provide explicit complexity in terms of I (chains), Ki (states), and T (time steps), and contrast with AFAMAP. Since the method relies on a CQP with O(TΣiKi) variables plus transition variables, a discussion of practical scalability (e.g., solver time as a function of T and total states) would complement the runtime tables.
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: The experiments set λi = 1. How sensitive are results to λi, and how would one set them in practice (e.g., cross-validation, weak priors)? The toy study varies µ0 but not λi; a short ablation on λi would be informative.
- Dependence on aggregate priors: Performance gains hinge on the accuracy of µi0 (e.g., national totals versus oracle totals). Please discuss robustness when priors are biased or uncertain, and whether one could model µi0 as random with credible intervals, yielding soft constraints calibrated to uncertainty.
- Comparative breadth: The related work section mentions duration models and non-homogeneous HMMs; a brief empirical comparison or discussion of how SACs might combine with those constraints would situate the contribution more clearly.

Clarity and minor issues
- Notation: On page 4 (line ~202), in the unnumbered equation before Eq. (6), the subscripts on p appear to use commas inconsistently with earlier notation; please standardize or explain any new notation.
- Practical details: Summarize how µi are learned (k-means on appliance signals) and whether more than three states materially change performance-runtime trade-offs.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, generalizable idea (SACs) with solid theoretical grounding via posterior regularization.
- Convex relaxation enables efficient approximate inference with standard tools.
- Strong empirical gains on realistic energy disaggregation data, with significance tests.
- Method likely to be useful beyond energy, wherever aggregate priors are available.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited analysis of convexity, relaxation tightness, and rounding effects.
- Sparse complexity discussion and scalability analysis versus AFAMAP for larger I, Ki, T.
- Sensitivity to λi and to misspecified µi0 not fully explored.
- Synthetic experiment setup (very low noise) may overstate improvements.

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed and practically motivated contribution. With added clarification on convexity/rounding, computational scaling, and noise/parameter sensitivity, the paper would be even stronger. I lean toward acceptance.

Addendum
After the rebuttal, the authors addressed my questions satisfactorily; I encourage them to incorporate those clarifications (on SNR choices, convexity details, and complexity) into the final version.
