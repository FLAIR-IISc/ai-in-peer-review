The paper proposes an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) with signal aggregate constraints (SAC) to address the blind source separation problem. The authors introduce a convex quadratic program for the relaxed AFHMM with SAC, which shows promising results on both synthetic and real-world data.

The paper is well-written and has a good mathematical foundation. The problem motivation is clear, and the evaluation results indicate a significant improvement in performance metrics, specifically the normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE).

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to incorporate domain knowledge into the AFHMM through SAC. This approach helps to address the identifiability problem inherent in blind source separation. The authors also provide a clear explanation of the relationship between their method and posterior regularization.

However, there are some areas that could be improved. Firstly, the comparison with AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP may not be entirely fair, as SACAFHMM uses more data. It would be beneficial to compare the proposed method with other approaches that could utilize SAC but may not be as computationally efficient.

The results presented in Figure 1 are not entirely clear. It appears that for larger distances, AFAMAP outperforms SACAFHMM. This raises the question of whether poor constraints can lead to significantly worse performance. The discussion seems to suggest that the results are always better, which may be misleading.

Furthermore, the paper could benefit from exploring more complex constraints. For instance, incorporating constraints that reflect real-world scenarios, such as a person not being able to use a heater and cooler simultaneously, or having no more than three out of ten bulbs active at the same time. The authors could also consider constraints with time limits, such as grinders having a maximum usage time of ten minutes. Extending the algorithm to accommodate these types of constraints would enhance its applicability and usefulness.

In conclusion, the paper provides a good motivation for the problem and presents a well-written and mathematically sound solution. Future work should focus on extending the model to incorporate more complex constraints, which would further improve its performance and applicability.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to addressing the blind source separation problem.
* The method shows promising results on both synthetic and real-world data.
* The paper provides a clear explanation of the relationship between the proposed method and posterior regularization.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The comparison with other methods may not be entirely fair.
* The results presented in Figure 1 could be misleading.
* The paper could benefit from exploring more complex constraints.

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns raised above, particularly regarding the comparison with other methods and the exploration of more complex constraints.
