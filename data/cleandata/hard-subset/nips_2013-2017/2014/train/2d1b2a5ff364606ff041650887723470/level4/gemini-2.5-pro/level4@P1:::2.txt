This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), specifically in the application of energy disaggregation, where an aggregated signal (e.g., total household electricity usage) must be separated into its constituent source signals (e.g., individual appliances). The core challenge in this domain is the inherent unidentifiability of the sources. The authors propose a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge to mitigate this issue, which they term Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The main idea is to constrain the model such that the total sum of each source signal over a given time period is close to a known or expected value.

The authors incorporate SACs into the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a standard approach for this problem. As exact inference in the resulting model (AFHMM+SAC) is intractable due to the non-Markovian nature of the global constraints, the authors propose a relaxation of the MAP inference problem, which can be formulated and efficiently solved as a convex quadratic program (CQP). The proposed method is evaluated on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, where it is shown to significantly outperform both the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints is a simple, intuitive, and powerful idea. It provides a practical way to incorporate valuable domain knowledge into a difficult, underdetermined problem. While other forms of constraints for HMMs exist, this focus on the aggregate signal value is novel and particularly well-suited for applications like energy disaggregation, where such prior knowledge is often available (e.g., from national surveys or appliance specifications). The approach is general and could likely be applied to other BSS problems.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the constrained MAP inference problem and its subsequent relaxation into a solvable convex quadratic program is a solid contribution. The connection to the posterior regularization framework is also well-articulated, providing a complementary theoretical justification for the approach.

3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results are strong and convincing. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on a challenging, low-frequency real-world dataset. The performance improvements over both the baseline AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model are substantial. The inclusion of an "oracle" experiment using the true total consumption for the constraints is a valuable addition, as it clearly illustrates the potential of the proposed method when provided with accurate prior information. The paper is also honest about computational costs, showing they are comparable to existing methods.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity and Readability:** The primary weakness of the paper is its clarity. The writing is very dense and relies heavily on a large number of acronyms (AFHMM, SAC, AFAMAP, CQP, NDE, SAE, NTC, TTC). While these are standard in the field, their sheer quantity makes the paper difficult to parse, particularly for readers who are not specialists in this exact niche. Reducing the number of acronyms or providing more consistent reminders for the reader would significantly improve the paper's accessibility and impact.

2.  **Framing of the Constraint:** The motivation and presentation of the constraint could be made more direct. The authors first introduce the constraint as a penalty term in the MAP objective function (Section 4.2), suggesting it can be viewed as a log-prior. Later, in Section 6, they show it can also be derived from the posterior regularization framework. This presentation feels slightly indirect. A more straightforward approach would be to frame the constraint from the outset as an informative prior distribution over the aggregate state sequence of each chain. This would more clearly establish that an *a priori* constraint is being used to guide the posterior inference. The connection to posterior regularization could then be presented as an interesting alternative perspective, rather than the primary justification. This would not change the technical contribution but would improve the clarity of the conceptual framing.

**Summary:**

Overall, this is a good paper with a strong core contribution. The proposed Signal Aggregate Constraints offer a novel and effective way to address the key identifiability issue in BSS for energy disaggregation. The technical approach is sound, and the experimental validation is thorough and compelling. The main weaknesses relate to the clarity of the presentation. If the authors can address the issues regarding the heavy use of acronyms and clarify the framing of their core contribution, this will be an excellent addition to the proceedings.
