This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), specifically in the context of energy disaggregation, where a single aggregated time series (total household electricity usage) must be separated into its constituent source signals (individual appliances). The core challenge in BSS is the inherent unidentifiability of the sources. The authors propose a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge to mitigate this issue, which they term Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The main idea is that for many BSS problems, one can form a reasonable prior expectation for the total aggregate value of each source signal over a given time period (e.g., the total daily energy consumption of a refrigerator).

The authors incorporate SACs into the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a standard probabilistic model for this task. As the SACs are constraints over the entire time sequence for each source, they break the Markov property of the model, rendering standard inference algorithms like the Viterbi algorithm inapplicable. To overcome this, the authors formulate a relaxed version of the maximum a posteriori (MAP) inference problem as a convex quadratic program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently.

The proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES). The results demonstrate that incorporating SACs leads to a substantial reduction in disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM and a significant improvement over AFAMAP, a recent state-of-the-art method.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central contribution—the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints—is a simple, intuitive, and powerful idea. It proposes a new and general way to inject domain knowledge into a notoriously difficult and underdetermined problem. This type of constraint is broadly applicable beyond energy disaggregation to other BSS domains where prior knowledge about signal totals exists.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a technically sound approach to a challenging inference problem. The formulation of the relaxed MAP inference problem as a convex quadratic program is a key contribution that makes the proposed model practical and computationally tractable. The connection to the posterior regularization framework in Section 6 also provides a solid theoretical grounding for the approach.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is convincing. The authors show substantial improvements (a 45% error reduction vs. AFHMM and 29% vs. AFAMAP on one dataset) on a challenging, low-frequency, real-world dataset. The inclusion of an "oracle" experiment using the true total consumption (TTC) is a valuable addition, as it effectively demonstrates the upper bound on performance improvement achievable with this type of constraint.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** While the core idea is clear, the technical exposition, particularly in Section 5, is quite dense and can be difficult to follow. The introduction of numerous new notations for the CQP formulation (`µ∗i`, `Λi`, `µ̃i`, `π̃i`, etc.) without sufficient intuition makes the section challenging to parse. A table summarizing these variables and their dimensions could significantly improve readability.

2.  **Overuse of Acronyms:** The paper relies heavily on acronyms (AFHMM, AFAMAP, AFHMM+SAC, CQP, NTC, TTC, NDE, SAE). While standard in the field, their high density makes the paper less accessible. For instance, repeatedly switching between AFHMM, AFAMAP, and AFHMM+SAC can be confusing. The authors could consider reducing their use or reminding the reader of their meaning to improve the flow of the text.

3.  **Conceptual Framing:** The motivation for the constraints is well-explained, but the way they are introduced into the model could be framed more clearly. The penalty term in Equation (5) is functionally equivalent to placing a Gaussian prior on the aggregate signal value for each source. Stating this explicitly from the outset—that the paper introduces an *a priori* constraint on the latent state sequences, which consequently constrains the posterior—would improve the paper's narrative. The link to posterior regularization in Section 6 is a good justification, but a more direct "Bayesian prior" interpretation at the beginning would be more intuitive.

4.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The choice of the complexity parameter `λ` is set to 1 for all experiments without justification. This parameter balances the likelihood term against the constraint. The performance of the model is likely sensitive to this choice. The paper would be strengthened by including a discussion on how this hyperparameter could be set in practice (e.g., using a validation set) and ideally a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with `λ`.

**Summary of Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by a principled inference method and strong experimental results on a real-world problem.
*   **Clarity:** The paper's clarity is its main weakness. The writing is dense in the technical sections, and the heavy use of acronyms hinders readability. The suggestions above could help address this.
*   **Originality:** The idea of using signal aggregate constraints is novel and provides a new direction for incorporating domain knowledge into BSS models. It is clearly distinguished from prior work that focused on constraints on state transitions.
*   **Significance:** The results are important and demonstrate a clear advance on the state of the art for energy disaggregation. The proposed SACs are general enough that they are likely to be adopted or built upon by researchers in other fields facing similar BSS problems.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel idea, a solid technical contribution, and impressive results. Its primary weakness is in the clarity of its presentation. If the authors can address the suggestions for improving readability and the discussion around hyperparameter selection, this will be an excellent contribution to the NIPS proceedings.
