

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) to enhance blind source separation in Additive Factorial HMMs (AFHMMs), specifically applied to energy disaggregation. SACs incorporate domain knowledge by constraining the total expected energy consumption of each appliance over a time period, addressing the inherent identifiability challenge in BSS. The authors formulate SACs as convex quadratic programs (CQPs) for efficient inference, demonstrating significant improvements over baseline AFHMM and AFAMAP on both synthetic and real-world energy data.

**Strengths**  
The paper makes a technically sound contribution. The SAC framework is well-motivated by the domain knowledge gap in existing BSS approaches (e.g., AFAMAP only constrains state transitions). The CQP formulation (Section 5) effectively handles SACs’ non-Markovian constraints, and the theoretical link to posterior regularization (Section 6) strengthens the method’s foundation. Empirical results are robust: on real energy data (HES dataset), AFHMM+SAC with National Total Consumption (NTC) constraints reduces normalized disaggregation error (NDE) by 33% and signal aggregate error (SAE) by 16% over AFAMAP (the state-of-the-art), with statistically significant t-tests (p<0.05). The computational efficiency (Table 1) matches AFAMAP, making SACs practical for large-scale applications. The toy data experiments (Section 7.1) clearly show SACs’ sensitivity to constraint accuracy, reinforcing their utility.

**Weaknesses**  
The clarity of the theoretical derivation requires improvement. While the paper claims the relaxed problem (Equation 6) is convex, it lacks a proof (e.g., showing the Hessian is positive semidefinite) or explicit discussion of convexity conditions. This omission weakens the theoretical rigor. Additionally, the toy experiment’s low noise variance (σ²=0.01) is not justified—this appears to simplify the separation task unrealistically. The authors should clarify whether this reflects typical real-world signal-to-noise ratios (SNR) or is a deliberate simplification for illustrative purposes. A brief discussion of SNR in the energy data (e.g., mains vs. appliance signals) would also strengthen the practical relevance. Minor notation inconsistencies exist (e.g., "AFMM" vs. "AFHMM" on page 1), though these are easily fixable.

**Originality and Significance**  
The SAC concept is novel for BSS, addressing a gap in prior work (e.g., posterior regularization [4] focuses on *how* to constrain, not *what* constraints to use). It generalizes beyond energy disaggregation (e.g., to audio processing), as noted in the introduction. The significance is high: SACs provide a simple yet powerful mechanism to inject domain knowledge into BSS, directly improving real-world applications. The 45% NDE reduction over AFHMM (Section 7.2.2) demonstrates tangible progress over prior methods, and the method’s scalability (Section 7.2) ensures practical adoption.

**Overall Assessment**  
The paper is a strong contribution to NIPS. It is technically rigorous, clearly motivated, and demonstrates significant empirical gains. The minor theoretical gaps and experimental clarifications (noted above) are easily addressable in revision. The work advances the state-of-the-art in BSS and energy disaggregation, with clear potential for broader impact in constrained probabilistic modeling.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro*: Novel SAC framework; strong empirical validation on real-world energy data; efficient CQP solution; clear connection to posterior regularization.  
*Con*: Theoretical convexity proof omitted; toy experiment SNR not justified; minor notation errors.  

The authors satisfactorily addressed concerns in the rebuttal (e.g., clarifying the toy data SNR), confirming the method’s validity. This paper delivers a significant, well-executed advance in BSS with broad applicability, making it highly suitable for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
