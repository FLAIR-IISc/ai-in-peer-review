This paper introduces a blind source separation approach based on additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) augmented with signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The core idea is to encode domain knowledge about expected total energy per source to improve identifiability and separation. The motivating application is energy disaggregation, where per-appliance usage is recovered from a single aggregate meter by encouraging each device’s total daily consumption to match a specified target. The authors perform MAP inference and reformulate the objective (the log posterior over hidden chains given the observations) into a convex quadratic program via a relaxation with auxiliary variables. Performance is demonstrated on a synthetic example and on real household electricity data.

The paper appears technically sound and is supported by a solid set of experiments. As expected, the proposed method outperforms a standard AFHMM and AFAMAP (a recent alternative that does not use SACs), with improvements verified by t-tests. The optimization runtime is also comparable to AFAMAP. One question about the results is the very small noise variance used in the toy experiment—was this chosen primarily to make the separation in the illustrative figure especially clear, and to show that competing methods fail under those conditions? Clarification would help, as otherwise the setup risks appearing overly simplistic. Relatedly, it would be helpful to comment on the signal-to-noise ratios between the mains readings and the latent appliance signals in the real energy data.

The theoretical presentation is generally clear and well structured. That said, the paper would benefit from an explicit proof or citation establishing that the relaxed program in equation (6) is convex under the stated transformations, as the current argument is not entirely transparent. In addition, including scaling analysis with respect to time-series length and the number of HMM states would complement the empirical results. Are the proposed relaxations and solver choices tractable at substantially larger scales, and how would this compare to AFAMAP?

The manuscript is well written and organized, and the introduction motivates the problem effectively with relevant related work (though I cannot guarantee that no important references are missing). A minor point: on page 4 (line 202), in the unnumbered equation preceding (6), the commas in the subscripts following p seem inconsistent with the notation used earlier (page 3), unless new notation is being introduced.

To the best of my knowledge the approach is novel and original, though I am not a domain expert and therefore cannot fully assess the broader impact on the HMM literature and on the energy disaggregation community.

Addendum: The authors’ rebuttal addressed my questions satisfactorily; I encourage incorporating those clarifications into the revision. Overall, this work proposes AFHMMs with SACs for blind source separation and shows strong performance relative to the state of the art, particularly on real energy disaggregation data.
