This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) within an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) to address the inherent identifiability challenges of blind source separation, specifically focusing on energy disaggregation. The authors formulate the problem as a convex quadratic program for approximate inference and demonstrate substantial performance improvements on both simulated and real-world datasets.

The paper is well-written and clearly motivated. The problem of blind source separation is compelling, and the introduction effectively highlights the difficulties in achieving meaningful source identification without incorporating domain knowledge. The proposed SACs offer a principled way to integrate such knowledge, and the mathematical formulation is sound. The experimental results, demonstrating significant reductions in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE), are promising. The connection to posterior regularization, presented in Section 6, adds theoretical depth to the work.

However, several points warrant further consideration. While the performance gains over AFHMM and AFAMAP are notable, the comparison appears somewhat unbalanced. The AFHMM+SAC model utilizes information (µi0) not explicitly used by the baseline models, potentially offering an unfair advantage. It would be beneficial to understand if equivalent performance gains can be achieved with other approaches that also leverage prior knowledge, but perhaps with different computational complexities. Exploring existing methods that *could* utilize SACs, but aren't as computationally tractable, would strengthen the argument for the proposed approach. 

Figure 1, illustrating the impact of constraint accuracy, is unclear. The figure suggests that AFHMM+SAC can perform *worse* than AFAMAP when the constraint distance exceeds 10^4. This is a crucial observation that requires further discussion. The current presentation implies consistent improvement with SACs, which appears misleading given this result. A more nuanced discussion acknowledging the potential for performance degradation with inaccurate constraints is needed. The sensitivity of the method to the choice of the constraint value (µi0) should be addressed.

The paper focuses on relatively simple aggregate constraints (total energy consumption per appliance). While a good starting point, the inherent flexibility of the problem suggests the possibility of more complex, and potentially more informative, constraints. For example, constraints relating to co-occurrence (e.g., a heater and cooler are unlikely to operate simultaneously) or duration (e.g., a grinder typically operates for less than 10 minutes) could be explored. Discussing the potential for extending the framework to accommodate such constraints, and the associated challenges, would enhance the paper's impact. Could the authors outline how their convex quadratic program would need to be adapted to accommodate such constraints?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel incorporation of signal aggregate constraints into an AFHMM framework.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating significant improvements in energy disaggregation performance.
*   Sound mathematical formulation and efficient convex quadratic program solution.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Potential for unfair comparison with baseline models due to differing information usage.
*   Misleading impression of consistent performance improvement based on Figure 1.
*   Limited exploration of more complex and potentially informative constraints.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of energy disaggregation and blind source separation. Addressing the concerns regarding the fairness of the comparison, the interpretation of Figure 1, and the potential for more complex constraints would further strengthen the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
