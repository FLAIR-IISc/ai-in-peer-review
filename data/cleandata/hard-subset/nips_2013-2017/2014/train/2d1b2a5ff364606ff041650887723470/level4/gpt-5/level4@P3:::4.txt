Summary and relation to prior work
This paper augments additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) for blind source separation by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs): priors that encourage each latent source’s total contribution over a time horizon to match a specified aggregate (e.g., total daily energy per appliance). The authors formulate MAP inference for AFHMM+SAC and derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) via a standard relaxation that introduces pairwise transition variables; they also show the objective arises from the posterior regularization framework. The approach is evaluated on energy disaggregation, using ultra-low-frequency data from the UK Household Electricity Survey: (i) 100 households where “mains” signals are synthetically constructed by summing appliance-level measurements; and (ii) 9 households with real mains measurements. The method significantly improves normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) over both a simple AFHMM baseline and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012). A toy study explores robustness to misspecified aggregates.

The work builds on a long line of AFHMM-based disaggregation methods (e.g., factorial HMMs, Ghahramani & Jordan; AFAMAP’s “at most one chain changes at a time” constraint, Kolter & Jaakkola; interleaved factorial non-homogeneous HMMs; semi-Markov and duration models; k-segment constraints). It connects cleanly to frameworks for encoding side information such as posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.), generalized expectation (Mann & McCallum), and measurement-based learning (Liang et al.), but focuses on a specific and widely applicable class of global, across-time constraints.

Strengths
- Clear, general idea: enforcing global aggregates is a natural, domain-agnostic way to address identifiability in BSS, especially when totals by source vary widely.
- Sound formulation: the convex relaxation is standard, the PSD structure is correct, and the posterior regularization perspective is appropriate.
- Practical relevance: energy disaggregation at low sampling rates is challenging; demonstrating consistent gains on HES data is valuable.
- Empirical gains: meaningful reductions in NDE and SAE versus AFHMM and AFAMAP; significance testing is reported; runtime is comparable in practice.
- Robustness: toy results quantify sensitivity to aggregate misspecification; the paper notes straightforward extensions to multiple time-window constraints.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Integrality and rounding: The CQP is a relaxation, yet the paper does not quantify how often solutions are integral, how fractional state assignments are handled (rounding scheme), or the empirical integrality gap. AFAMAP’s constraint was partly motivated by increasing the chance of integral solutions; analogous evidence here would be useful.
- Orthogonality to AFAMAP: SACs are complementary to “one chain changes at a time.” Combining both seems natural; even a small-scale experiment or analysis (or a clear discussion of how to incorporate SACs into AFAMAP’s formulation) would clarify the relative and joint benefits.
- Dependence on aggregate priors: Results with “national total consumption” (NTC) are encouraging, but guidance on obtaining µ0 in deployment, handling household-specific variability, and sensitivity to systematic bias would strengthen the case. The toy study is helpful; a similar perturbation analysis on real data (e.g., scaling NTC by ±x%) would be compelling.
- Parameterization and estimation: Fixing λi = 1 and using k-means with 3 states for all appliances simplifies the setup, but it would help to justify these choices, report sensitivity to λ, and discuss per-appliance state numbers or duration modeling (semi-Markov) as baselines or ablations.
- Evaluation details: While NDE and SAE are standard, more per-appliance analysis (e.g., which loads benefit most, time-of-use accuracy) would provide insight. The oracle “true total consumption” (TTC) result is useful as an upper bound but should be clearly labeled as such throughout and interpreted cautiously.
- Scalability: Solving CQPs with CVX is convenient but may not scale to longer horizons or many sources. Reporting problem sizes, solver settings, iteration counts, and any warm-starting/ADMM alternatives would help assess scalability.

Quality
The derivations are technically sound, the relaxation is appropriate, and the posterior-regularization link is correct. The empirical methodology is reasonable and uses a challenging dataset. However, the paper should more carefully distinguish between models and inference procedures (see below) and address integrality and scalability.

Clarity
Overall clear, but some notational density and occasional conflation of model and algorithm:
- Please use consistent terminology (e.g., “AFHMM+SAC”) and explicitly state that “AFHMM” in the tables refers to chainwise/alternating Viterbi as the inference baseline, whereas “AFAMAP” is an approximate MAP algorithm for the same AFHMM family with an additional constraint.
- Summarize the rounding procedure from relaxed to discrete sequences.
- Minor editorial issues exist in the references and notation; a careful proofreading would help.

Originality
Moderate but meaningful. The specific class of global aggregate constraints for AFHMMs, and their convex relaxation, are natural extensions of posterior regularization/measurement learning, but applying them to energy disaggregation with clear empirical benefits is novel and useful.

Significance
The method is likely to be adopted in energy disaggregation and other BSS applications where source totals are known or estimable (e.g., biology, audio with track-level loudness priors). The gains over strong AFHMM baselines at low sampling rates are practically important.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report integrality statistics, rounding strategy, and post-rounding objective degradation.
- Discuss and, if possible, demonstrate combining SACs with AFAMAP’s one-change constraint.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for λ and for biased µ0 on real data; discuss how to set µ0 (e.g., hierarchical priors, per-house calibration).
- Expand evaluation with per-appliance breakdowns and time-of-use accuracy.
- Add details on solver configuration, problem sizes, and a path toward scalable first-order methods.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated idea with a clean probabilistic and optimization treatment.
- Demonstrated, statistically significant improvements on realistic, challenging data.
- General technique likely to transfer to other BSS settings.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited discussion of integrality and relaxation quality; no combination with existing AFHMM constraints that may be complementary.
- Dependence on external aggregate priors without extensive robustness analysis.
- Scalability beyond CVX is not fully addressed.

Overall assessment
A solid, practically motivated contribution that improves AFHMM-based disaggregation by incorporating global aggregate knowledge. With clarifications on integrality, robustness, and scalability—and ideally a demonstration combining SACs with AFAMAP’s constraint—this would be a strong paper. I lean toward acceptance.
