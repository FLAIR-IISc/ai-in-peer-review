The paper proposes an extension to the additive factorial HMM model where additional constraints are added to require that the sum of individual chain outputs be close to some reasonable (known) aggregate value. The authors propose a convex relaxation to MAP inference and apply the algorithm to real-world data sets involving energy disaggregation, both across 100 UK homes with low frequency readings, and 9 homes with higher frequency readings (and appliances monitored).

The ideas presented here are fairly straightforward, and the authors employ a relatively simple optimization formulation to address these problems, but overall the work is compelling. The authors are applying these methods to relatively large real-world data sets, and as a paper that combines both applied and algorithmic elements, I think that it is a success. The following comments could help to improve the paper:

A motivation for AFAMAP is to enforce the constraint that only one chain changes state at a time, which aims to improve the relaxation and facilitate integer solutions. Does the SAC-AFHMM optimization formulation also achieve a similar effect, or do the resulting solutions still exhibit lower error despite being non-integral? Discussion of these points would be helpful to further clarify the distinctions between the approaches.

Fundamentally, the AFAMAP and SAC-AFHMM ideas appear orthogonal: one could incorporate the SAC-AFHMM constraints into the optimization formulation for AFAMAP. While I do not expect the authors to address this during the response phase, I strongly encourage them to explore this combination for a potential final version.

The authors occasionally conflate models and optimization procedures. For instance, comparing the results of "AFHMM" versus "AFAMAP" isn't entirely precise; AFAMAP is an algorithm for (approximately) computing MAP solutions to the AFHMM, and the authors seem to use “AFHMM” to refer to alternating Viterbi optimization. Is this correct? This also relates to the point above, where the strategies of the different algorithms presented here could be readily combined and might even yield superior performance. 

The paper proposes a straightforward extension and optimization to solve a variant of the AFHMM, but the results on real-world data are compelling, and overall the paper appears reasonable.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practical problem (energy disaggregation) with a well-motivated approach.
*   Demonstrates significant performance improvements over existing methods on real-world datasets.
*   The proposed convex relaxation provides a computationally efficient solution.
*   Clearly presented and well-written.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical novelty is somewhat limited, as it builds upon existing AFHMM work.
*   The authors could further explore the potential synergy between SAC-AFHMM and AFAMAP.
*   Minor ambiguity in the description of AFHMM vs. Viterbi optimization could be clarified.

**Overall assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field and warrants acceptance. While the theoretical novelty is not groundbreaking, the practical results and clear presentation make it a valuable addition to the conference. The suggested improvements would further strengthen the paper.
