This paper extends the additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) by adding constraints that require the sum of the individual chain outputs to be close to a reasonable, known aggregate value. The authors propose a convex relaxation for MAP inference and apply the method to energy disaggregation on two real-world datasets: (i) 100 UK homes with low-frequency readings, and (ii) 10 homes with higher-frequency readings (but without per-appliance monitoring).

The ideas are fairly straightforward and the optimization formulation is relatively simple, but the work remains compelling. The methods are applied to sizable real-world datasets, and as a paper that blends applied and algorithmic elements, it is largely successful. The following comments could improve the paper:

- Clarify the role of integer solutions. A motivation for AFAMAP’s constraint that only one chain changes state at a time is that it can strengthen the relaxation and yield integral solutions more often. Does the SACAFHMM optimization achieve a similar effect, or do its solutions remain non-integral while still achieving lower error? A discussion of this point would help clarify the distinctions between the approaches.

- Discuss orthogonality and potential combination. The AFAMAP and SACAFHMM ideas appear orthogonal: one could plausibly incorporate the SACAFHMM constraints into AFAMAP’s optimization formulation. While I do not expect an implementation during the author response, I strongly encourage exploring this combination for the final version, as it might further improve performance.

- Be precise about models versus algorithms. In a few places, the paper appears to conflate models with optimization procedures. For example, comparing “AFHMM” versus “AFAMAP” is imprecise: AFAMAP is an algorithm for (approximately) computing MAP solutions to the AFHMM, whereas what is labeled “AFHMM” in the comparisons seems to correspond to alternating Viterbi optimization. Is that correct? This also reinforces the point above: the algorithmic strategies presented here could likely be combined and might outperform the current standalone approaches.

Overall, the paper proposes a simple extension and an optimization method to solve a variant of the AFHMM. The results on real-world datasets are compelling, and the paper is, in my view, reasonable and worthy of consideration. Clarifying the points above would strengthen the contribution and improve reproducibility and interpretability.
