The paper proposes an extension to the additive factorial HMM model, incorporating signal aggregate constraints (SACs) to require that the sum of individual chain outputs be close to some reasonable (known) aggregate value. The authors propose a convex relaxation to MAP inference and apply the algorithm to real-world data sets involving energy disaggregation, both across 100 UK homes with low frequency readings, and 9 homes with higher frequency readings (but without each appliance monitored).

The main idea of the paper is to introduce SACs for blind source separation problems, specifically in the context of energy disaggregation. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by comparing the performance of their method, AFHMM+SAC, with that of the original AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach, AFAMAP. The results show that AFHMM+SAC significantly improves performance, resulting in a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM, and a significant improvement (29%) in performance compared to AFAMAP.

The paper is well-structured, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their method and its application to energy disaggregation. The use of a toy data set and real-world data sets helps to demonstrate the robustness and effectiveness of the method. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of related work, including other approaches to constraining the states of hidden Markov chains.

One potential area for improvement is in the discussion of the relationship between AFAMAP and SACAFHMM. The authors note that the ideas presented in the paper are orthogonal to those of AFAMAP, but they do not explore the possibility of combining the two approaches. It would be interesting to see how the performance of the method changes when the SACAFHMM constraints are included in the optimization formulation for AFAMAP.

The authors also seem to conflate models and optimization procedures at times. For instance, comparing the results of "AFHMM" versus "AFAMAP" is not entirely precise, as AFAMAP is an algorithm for (approximately) computing MAP solutions to the AFHMM. The authors should clarify this distinction to avoid confusion.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful to evaluate both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, and they provide a thorough discussion of the limitations of their method.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their method and its application to energy disaggregation. The use of figures and tables helps to illustrate the results and make the paper more readable.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel extension to the additive factorial HMM model, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in a real-world application. The use of signal aggregate constraints is a new contribution, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of the benefits and limitations of their approach.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a difficult problem in energy disaggregation, and the authors provide a novel solution that improves upon existing state-of-the-art methods. The results have the potential to impact the field of energy disaggregation and related areas.
