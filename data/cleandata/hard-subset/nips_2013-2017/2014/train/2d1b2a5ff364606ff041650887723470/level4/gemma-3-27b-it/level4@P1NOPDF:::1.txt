This paper develops a new method for blind source separation, formulating the problem as an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) and incorporating signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The motivation is that leveraging additional domain knowledge can improve the separation of time series into constituent components. The paper illustrates this with energy disaggregation, where incorporating information about the expected usage of individual appliances enhances the separation of overall domestic energy consumption patterns. The objective function, maximizing the log posterior distribution of the hidden chains given the observed data, is transformed into a convex optimization problem. The method’s efficacy is demonstrated using both a toy dataset and real-world data from domestic energy readings.

The paper is technically sound and features a strong experimental section. As expected, the method outperforms standard AFHMM and AFAMAP (a recently developed alternative) – neither of which utilizes SACs. The improvements are statistically significant, as shown by t-tests, and the optimization speed is comparable to that of AFAMAP. My primary question regarding the results concerns the low noise variance used in the toy example. Is this to clearly illustrate the method’s superior separation capabilities in Figure 2? Some clarification would be beneficial; otherwise, the setup seems overly simplified. Alternatively, are such signal-to-noise ratios common in real-world data? Relatedly, the authors could also comment on the signal-to-noise ratio between the mains readings and the aggregate appliance output in the energy application.

The theoretical aspects of the paper are well presented and structured overall. A proof demonstrating the convexity of the final procedure in equation (6) would strengthen the paper, as the provided reasoning isn't entirely convincing. Furthermore, theoretical scaling arguments regarding data length and the number of hidden states would be valuable, complementing the empirical findings from the simulations. Specifically, how tractable are these methods when applied to much larger datasets and state spaces, and how would this performance compare to AFAMAP?

The paper is well written and clearly structured. The introduction provides a compelling motivation for the problem and a thorough review of relevant literature. While I am not deeply familiar with this specific problem domain, I cannot identify any obvious omissions in the literature review. I noted a potential minor error on page 4 (line 202), in the unnumbered equation preceding (6), where the commas in the subscripts of *p* appear inconsistent with the notation on page 3 – unless a new convention has been introduced.

To the best of my knowledge, the work is novel and original. However, I lack the expertise to fully assess the significance of these results within the broader HMM community or the energy application field.

Addendum: The authors have satisfactorily addressed my questions during the feedback phase. I encourage them to incorporate similar clarifications into the next version of the paper. This paper presents a method for performing blind source separation using Additive Factorial HMMs with Signal Aggregate Constraints. The method performs exceptionally well compared to state-of-the-art techniques, particularly with real data from an energy disaggregation problem.
