The paper extends the additive factorial HMM by adding constraints that require the sum of the individual chains’ outputs to remain close to a known, reasonable aggregate signal. The authors present a convex relaxation for MAP inference and apply it to energy-disaggregation data from two real-world settings: (i) 100 UK homes with low-frequency aggregate readings, and (ii) 10 homes with higher-frequency aggregate readings without per-appliance submetering.

The ideas are fairly straightforward, and the optimization formulation is relatively simple, but the work is still compelling. The methods are evaluated on sizable real datasets, and as a paper that blends applied and algorithmic elements, it succeeds. The following comments could improve the paper:

- A motivation for AFAMAP’s constraint that at most one chain changes state at a time is that it can tighten the relaxation and yield integral solutions more often. Does the SACAFHMM optimization achieve a similar effect, or do its solutions remain fractional while still attaining lower error? A discussion of this would clarify the distinctions.

- Conceptually, AFAMAP and SACAFHMM seem orthogonal: one could incorporate the SACAFHMM constraints into AFAMAP’s optimization. While a detailed response may be difficult during the rebuttal, I strongly encourage exploring this for the final version.

- The paper sometimes conflates models and optimization procedures. Comparing “AFHMM” to “AFAMAP” is imprecise: AFAMAP is an algorithm for (approximately) computing MAP solutions to the AFHMM, whereas what is labeled “AFHMM” appears to be alternating Viterbi optimization. Is that correct? This also relates to the point above: the strategies of the different algorithms could likely be combined and might outperform the current approaches.

Overall, the paper proposes a simple extension and an optimization method to solve a variant of the AFHMM, the real-world results are compelling, and the submission is reasonable and promising.
