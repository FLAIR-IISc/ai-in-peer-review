The paper extends the additive factorial HMM by introducing constraints that encourage each chain’s total output to match a prespecified aggregate value. The authors devise a convex relaxation for MAP inference under these constraints and evaluate the approach on real-world energy disaggregation data: a cohort of 100 UK homes with low-frequency measurements, and a second set of roughly 10 homes with higher-rate readings where not every appliance is instrumented.

The core ideas are relatively simple and the optimization setup is fairly direct, yet the contribution remains persuasive. The method is demonstrated on sizable, realistic datasets, and as a synthesis of methodological and applied work, the paper is effective. The following comments could strengthen the manuscript:

- AFAMAP’s “at-most-one-chain-changes-at-a-time” constraint is partly motivated by making the relaxation tighter and yielding integral solutions more frequently. Does the SAC-augmented AFHMM have a similar effect on integrality, or are performance gains achieved even when solutions remain fractional? A discussion of this would clarify the distinctions and shed light on when each approach is preferable.

- The AFAMAP and SAC-augmented AFHMM ideas appear orthogonal: one could incorporate the SAC terms directly into the AFAMAP optimization. While an implementation may be beyond the scope of the rebuttal, I strongly encourage exploring this combination for the final version.

- The paper occasionally conflates models and inference procedures. For example, contrasting “AFHMM” with “AFAMAP” is imprecise: AFAMAP is an approximate MAP algorithm for the AFHMM, whereas “AFHMM” in the experiments seems to refer to alternating chainwise Viterbi. Is that correct? This also reinforces the point above that the proposed strategies could be combined and might outperform the current baselines. Overall, the paper presents a modest extension and an accompanying optimization, and the real-world results are compelling; the work seems solid.
