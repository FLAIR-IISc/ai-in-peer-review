Summary
This paper augments additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) with signal aggregate constraints (SACs) that encourage the total output of each latent source to match a specified target (e.g., expected daily energy per appliance). The authors formulate MAP inference with these constraints and derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) via relaxation and auxiliary variables, with an accompanying interpretation through posterior regularization. Empirically, on the Household Electricity Survey (HES), they evaluate two scenarios: (1) 100 households where mains are not measured and aggregate signals are created by summing appliance-level readings; and (2) 9 houses with measured mains (a more realistic and challenging setting). AFHMM+SAC yields sizable improvements in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) over an AFHMM baseline and over the AFAMAP method, with similar runtimes.

Relation to prior work
The approach is positioned relative to general frameworks for incorporating constraints in probabilistic models (posterior regularization, generalized expectations, measurement-based learning) and to prior NILM methods that constrain AFHMM dynamics (e.g., AFAMAP’s “at most one chain changes state” constraint, semi-Markov durations, k-segment constraints). The SACs are complementary to these temporal constraints: they inject global, cross-time information expected in many BSS problems (e.g., appliance totals), and the authors show how to incorporate them efficiently via a convex relaxation.

Strengths
- Clear and broadly applicable idea: global aggregate constraints are intuitive for BSS and especially relevant in NILM.
- Solid technical formulation: a relaxed CQP with PSD structure, and a principled link to posterior regularization.
- Empirical relevance: strong gains on a large, low-frequency real-world dataset (HES), a challenging NILM regime.
- Practicality: similar runtime to AFHMM and AFAMAP; robustness to imperfect targets is examined on toy data.
- Potential generality: SACs could be combined with other constraints (e.g., durations, AFAMAP change constraints).

Weaknesses and questions
- Dependence on target aggregates µ0: The toy study shows sensitivity to misspecification, but a deeper analysis on real data would be helpful. For instance, vary µ0 systematically around the NTC estimates and report NDE/SAE sensitivity and per-appliance effects.
- Choice of λ: The experiments fix λi = 1. Please provide an ablation or a simple cross-validation strategy and report performance vs. λ to guide practitioners.
- Integrality and rounding: AFAMAP’s one-change constraint often improves integrality of solutions. Do SACs similarly encourage integral state sequences in the relaxed problem? How are fractional state assignments handled for evaluation (e.g., rounding, decoding, or using fractional expectations)? Reporting the fraction of integral assignments and the impact of rounding would clarify interpretability.
- Combining orthogonal constraints: SACs and AFAMAP’s dynamics constraints appear complementary. It would be valuable to test a combined formulation (AFAMAP + SAC), or at least discuss the feasibility and expected behavior (e.g., does the CQP remain tractable; how to encode both sets of constraints).
- Clarify “model” vs. “algorithm”: AFAMAP is an inference algorithm for an AFHMM with a specific constraint; the paper sometimes contrasts “AFHMM” vs. “AFAMAP” as if they were distinct models. Please clarify what “AFHMM” denotes in experiments (e.g., chainwise Viterbi baseline vs. your CQP with λ = 0) to ensure fair comparisons and reproducibility.
- Parameter learning: θ are assumed known and are estimated with k-means and simple HMM learning. More detail would help: training set sizes, number of states per appliance, how appliances not present in training are handled in the mains setting, and whether hyperparameters are shared across homes.
- Multiple SACs: The text describes an extension to multiple time-window constraints, but experiments use single, whole-day constraints. A brief experiment with daytime/nighttime constraints (even on a subset) could further support the claim that the extension is straightforward and effective.

Quality
- Generally technically sound and appropriately referenced, with a clear derivation of the relaxed CQP and a link to posterior regularization.
- Some experimental details (λ selection, integrality handling, baseline definition) should be clarified to strengthen rigor.

Clarity
- The paper is well written overall. It would benefit from tighter terminology distinguishing models (AFHMM, AFHMM+SAC) from inference procedures (Viterbi, AFAMAP, CQP relaxation).
- Minor suggestions: summarize symbols used in the CQP once; briefly discuss computational scaling in terms of T and total states, and typical problem sizes solved with CVX.

Originality
- The specific use of global aggregate constraints in AFHMMs, cast as a convex relaxation and tied to posterior regularization, is a novel and useful contribution within NILM/BSS, although it draws on established frameworks.

Significance
- Improvements on realistic, low-frequency NILM data are substantial; the idea should transfer to other BSS domains where aggregate information is available. The approach opens a path to combining global SACs with existing temporal constraints.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general idea with clear motivation and principled formulation.
- Strong empirical gains on real-world data with practical runtimes.
- Broad applicability beyond NILM and clear potential to combine with existing methods.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to µ0 and λ on real data.
- Unclear handling of integrality and potential benefits of combining SACs with AFAMAP constraints.
- Some ambiguity in experimental baselines and model/algorithm terminology.

Suggestions
- Report how fractional solutions are handled and the rate of integral solutions; add ablations for λ and µ0 (real data).
- Clarify baselines and provide implementation details for reproducibility.
- Discuss or prototype a combined AFAMAP + SAC formulation.
- If space allows, include a small experiment with multiple SAC windows (e.g., day/night).
