

This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) to address the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS), specifically applied to energy disaggregation. The authors propose incorporating SACs into an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) framework, where constraints encourage the total signal for each source to be close to a specified value (e.g., expected daily energy consumption for an appliance). They transform the constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program for efficient inference. On both synthetic data and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, they demonstrate significant improvements over baseline AFHMM and AFAMAP approaches.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) introducing SACs as a general framework for incorporating domain knowledge in BSS problems, (2) providing a convex quadratic programming solution for inference with SACs, and (3) demonstrating substantial performance gains on energy disaggregation data.

The paper is well-motivated and properly situates its contribution within the context of prior work in energy disaggregation [8, 12, 22] and related BSS approaches. It clearly relates to posterior regularization [4] and generalized expectation criteria [14], while highlighting the specific gap it fills in identifying domain-general constraints that can be efficiently incorporated into probabilistic models.

Quality: The technical approach is sound, with a clear derivation of the convex quadratic program for inference. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using synthetic data to demonstrate robustness to constraint errors and real-world data to show significant performance gains (45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error over AFHMM, 29% improvement over AFAMAP). The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the need for prior knowledge about expected signal aggregates, and the fact that the solution is not guaranteed to be optimal due to identifiability issues. However, the evaluation could be strengthened by comparing against other constraint-based approaches that might also incorporate SACs. It would be helpful to see how SACs compare with frameworks like k-segment constraints [19] or non-homogeneous HMMs [21] that also constrain HMM state transitions.

Clarity: The paper is well-written with clear mathematical formulations and logical flow. The technical details are presented at an appropriate level for the intended audience. The derivation of the convex quadratic program is particularly well-executed. The figures are clear and effectively illustrate key results, though Figure 1 would benefit from more explicit labeling of the axes to clarify the relationship between constraint specification error and performance.

Originality: The SAC framework represents a novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge in BSS problems. While the paper acknowledges related work in posterior regularization, it makes a specific contribution by identifying a class of constraints (aggregate values) that are both generally applicable across domains and computationally tractable. The extension to multiple time-period constraints (mentioned in Section 4.2) further demonstrates the framework's flexibility. However, the paper could more clearly articulate how SACs differ from other constraint approaches like those in [12, 21] that also constrain HMM state transitions.

Significance: The results on energy disaggregation are compelling, showing meaningful improvements over state-of-the-art approaches. This has clear practical implications for non-intrusive load monitoring, where accurate appliance-level energy consumption is valuable for energy conservation efforts. The approach could potentially be extended to other BSS domains where aggregate signal knowledge is available (e.g., audio source separation with known total volume for each source). However, the paper could better elaborate on the broader applicability of SACs beyond energy disaggregation.

Strengths:
- Strong technical foundation with well-motivated problem formulation
- Clear mathematical derivation of the convex quadratic program
- Comprehensive evaluation showing significant improvements over baselines
- Practical application to energy disaggregation with meaningful results
- Good discussion of the relationship to posterior regularization framework

Weaknesses:
- The comparison with AFAMAP is potentially unfair since AFAMAP uses different constraints (e.g., at most one chain changing state at a time) while SACAFHMM uses aggregate constraints; it would be helpful to see how SACs perform with other constraint frameworks
- Figure 1's interpretation is unclear - the performance trend for large constraint errors is not properly explained, which could mislead readers about SACs' robustness
- The paper could more explicitly discuss the limitations of SACs (e.g., when aggregate knowledge is unavailable or inaccurate)
- While the paper mentions potential extensions to other constraint types (e.g., "person cannot use heater and cooler at the same time"), it doesn't demonstrate how the framework could handle such constraints, which would strengthen the claim of generality

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel approach to a significant problem in blind source separation. The SAC framework is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear empirical benefits. With some additional clarification on the comparison with other constraint approaches and more detailed discussion of SACs' limitations and potential extensions, this paper would make an excellent contribution to NIPS.

Pros: Novel constraint framework, strong empirical results, clear technical presentation.
Cons: Limited discussion of comparison with other constraint-based approaches, unclear interpretation of Figure 1, insufficient exploration of SACs' limitations.
