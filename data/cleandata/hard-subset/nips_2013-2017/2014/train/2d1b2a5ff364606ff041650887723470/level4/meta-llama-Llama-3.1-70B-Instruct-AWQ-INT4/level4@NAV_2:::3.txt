The paper proposes an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) with signal aggregate constraints (SACs) to address the blind source separation problem. The authors incorporate SACs into the AFHMM, resulting in a new model called AFHMM+SAC. This model is particularly useful for energy disaggregation problems, where the goal is to recover the component signals from an aggregated signal.

The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, discussing the challenges of blind source separation and the need for incorporating domain knowledge into the model. They also provide a thorough review of related work, highlighting the contributions of their approach.

The technical content of the paper is sound, and the authors provide a detailed derivation of the AFHMM+SAC model. They also discuss the computational complexity of the model and provide a convex quadratic program for the relaxed problem.

The evaluation results indicate a significant improvement in performance metrics, including normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE), compared to the original AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach. The authors also demonstrate the robustness of the method to errors in specification of the constraint value.

One potential area for improvement is in the comparison of the AFHMM+SAC model to other approaches. The authors compare their model to the AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP, but these approaches use less data than the AFHMM+SAC. It would be useful to compare the AFHMM+SAC model to other approaches that use similar amounts of data.

Additionally, the results in Figure 1 are not entirely clear. It appears that for distances greater than 10^4, the AFAMAP model outperforms the AFHMM+SAC model. This suggests that poor constraints can result in poorer performance, which is not fully discussed in the paper.

The authors also mention the possibility of extending the algorithm to other kinds of constraints, such as person-specific constraints or time constraints. This is an exciting area of future work, and it would be useful to provide more details on how the algorithm could be extended to accommodate these types of constraints.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a good motivation for the problem. The technical content is sound, and the evaluation results are promising. With some additional work on comparisons and extensions, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
