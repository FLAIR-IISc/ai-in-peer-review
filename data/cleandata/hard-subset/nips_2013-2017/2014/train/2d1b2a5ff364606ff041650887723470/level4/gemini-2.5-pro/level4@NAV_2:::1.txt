This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), a novel form of prior domain knowledge for addressing the identifiability problem in blind source separation. The authors incorporate these constraints into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), motivated by the observation that in many applications, the total aggregate value of each underlying source signal is often known or can be well-estimated. The primary application explored is energy disaggregation, where knowing the expected daily consumption of an appliance helps to isolate its signature from the total household energy signal. The authors formulate MAP inference in the resulting AFHMM+SAC model as a penalized objective function and derive an efficient, approximate inference procedure by relaxing the problem into a convex quadratic program. The method's value is validated on both synthetic data and a real-world energy consumption dataset.

The main strengths of the paper are its novel approach and strong empirical results. The introduction of SACs is an intuitive and powerful idea for regularizing a difficult, underdetermined problem. The work is technically sound, and the experimental evaluation is thorough.

*   **Significance & Quality:** The paper demonstrates substantial and statistically significant performance gains on a real-world energy disaggregation task compared to both a standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method (AFAMAP). This highlights the practical utility of the proposed constraints. The fact that the method's computational runtime is comparable to existing approaches further strengthens its value as a practical tool.

*   **Originality:** The formulation of signal aggregate constraints within the AFHMM framework for blind source separation appears to be a novel contribution. This general-purpose technique for encoding prior knowledge could prove influential in other application domains where similar aggregate information is available.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The motivation is compelling, and the related work is adequately covered.

While the work is of high quality, the manuscript could be strengthened by addressing a few points:

*   **Theoretical Justification:** The paper states that the relaxed problem is a convex quadratic program. While this claim seems correct, the presentation would be more rigorous with a more formal justification or brief proof showing that the objective function is indeed convex with respect to the relaxed variables.

*   **Complexity Analysis:** The empirical runtime comparison is useful, but the paper would benefit from a theoretical analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed CQP formulation. An analysis in terms of the number of time steps, source chains, and states would complement the experimental findings and allow for a clearer understanding of the method's scalability.

*   **Experimental Details:** In the toy example, the noise variance is quite low. It would be helpful to clarify whether this setup was chosen for illustrative clarity or if it is representative of SNRs in real data. For the real-world application, a brief discussion on the signal-to-noise ratio (e.g., the proportion of the mains signal explained by the modeled appliances) would provide valuable context for interpreting the results.

*   **Minor Notational Issue:** There appears to be a small inconsistency in the notation for transition probabilities in the equation preceding Equation (6) on page 4. This is a minor point that should be simple to correct.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that presents a well-motivated, novel, and effective method for a challenging problem. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate clear state-of-the-art performance on an important application. The suggested additions are minor and intended to further improve the paper's completeness. I strongly recommend acceptance.
