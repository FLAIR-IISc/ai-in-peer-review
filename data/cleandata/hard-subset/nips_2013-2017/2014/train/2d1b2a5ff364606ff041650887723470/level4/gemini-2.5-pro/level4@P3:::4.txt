This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation problems, particularly for the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM). The core idea is that in many applications, one has prior knowledge about the expected total signal (e.g., total energy consumption) for each underlying source over a given time period. The authors incorporate this knowledge by adding a penalty term to the MAP inference objective function, encouraging the aggregate of each inferred source signal to be close to a known target value. As this new objective function makes direct inference with dynamic programming intractable, the authors propose a convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation to find an approximate MAP solution efficiently. The method is evaluated on the challenging real-world problem of energy disaggregation using a large dataset of UK homes. The results demonstrate that the proposed AFHMM+SAC model significantly outperforms both a standard AFHMM and AFAMAP, a recent state-of-the-art approach.

**Evaluation**

This is a well-executed paper that combines a simple, intuitive idea with a solid technical formulation and compelling empirical results on a significant real-world problem.

**Quality and Significance:**
The paper's main strength lies in its practical impact and the significance of its results. The proposed SACs are a simple yet powerful form of regularization that is particularly well-suited for problems like energy disaggregation. The empirical evaluation is thorough, using a large, low-frequency dataset (HES) which is known to be challenging for disaggregation tasks. The authors demonstrate substantial performance gains, reporting a 45% error reduction compared to the baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over the state-of-the-art AFAMAP on realistic data with mains readings. These are impressive and highly significant results for the energy disaggregation community.

The technical approach is sound. The formulation of the constrained MAP problem and its subsequent relaxation into a CQP is a standard and effective technique for this class of problems. The experimental design is also strong, particularly the use of both a "true" total consumption (TTC) oracle and a more realistic national total consumption (NTC) to define the constraints. This analysis clearly shows both the potential of the method under ideal conditions and its practical utility with accessible prior knowledge. The idea of SACs is also general and could likely be beneficial in other domains where BSS is applied and aggregate knowledge is available.

**Originality:**
While the constituent parts—AFHMMs, MAP inference, and CQP relaxations—are not new, the introduction of signal *aggregate* constraints as a form of domain knowledge for this problem is a novel and valuable contribution. The paper correctly relates the work to broader frameworks like posterior regularization, which helps to ground the contribution in existing literature. The key originality lies in identifying a specific, practical, and highly effective class of constraints for a difficult, underdetermined problem.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for SACs is presented very clearly, and the model is described in sufficient detail. However, the clarity could be improved in a few areas, primarily concerning the distinction between models and inference algorithms.
*   The paper compares the performance of "AFHMM", "AFAMAP", and "AFHMM+SAC". This framing could be more precise. AFAMAP is an inference algorithm for the AFHMM model, which uses a specific constraint (at most one HMM changes state at a time) to enable an efficient dual decomposition approach. The proposed AFHMM+SAC is also an AFHMM model, but with a different inference objective and optimization strategy. It would be clearer if the authors explicitly stated which inference algorithm was used for the "AFHMM" baseline. Based on Section 5, it seems to be the CQP relaxation of the original MAP problem (i.e., AFHMM+SAC with λ=0), but confirming this would remove ambiguity.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
The paper is strong, but the following points could further improve it.

1.  **Integrality of the Relaxed Solution:** One motivation for the constraint in AFAMAP is that it can lead to integer solutions from the relaxed optimization more frequently. It would be very insightful to include a discussion of whether the SAC formulation has a similar effect. Do the solutions to the CQP relaxation tend to be integral, or does the method achieve lower error despite having non-integral (i.e., probabilistic) state assignments? This would provide a deeper understanding of the mechanism behind the performance improvement and a more complete comparison with AFAMAP.

2.  **Orthogonality of Constraints:** The constraint used in AFAMAP (at most one chain changes state at a time) and the SACs proposed here seem to be addressing different aspects of the problem and appear to be orthogonal. A natural and exciting extension would be to combine both types of constraints within a single optimization framework. While a full experimental evaluation of this is likely beyond the scope of a rebuttal, acknowledging this possibility and discussing it as a promising direction for future work would strengthen the paper.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:**
    *   Introduces a simple, novel, and highly effective method (SACs) for incorporating domain knowledge into AFHMMs.
    *   Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance with substantial, statistically significant improvements on a large and challenging real-world energy disaggregation dataset.
    *   The proposed CQP-based inference is technically sound and shown to be computationally efficient.
    *   The paper is a successful blend of algorithmic contribution and high-impact application, making it a strong fit for the NIPS audience.

*   **Arguments against Acceptance (minor):**
    *   The paper could be more precise in its terminology, particularly in distinguishing between probabilistic models and the inference algorithms used to solve them.
    *   A deeper analysis of the properties of the proposed CQP relaxation (e.g., regarding the integrality of its solutions) is missing.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear and useful contribution. The impressive empirical results on a difficult problem make a compelling case for acceptance. The suggested improvements are intended to help the authors refine what is already a very good piece of work for the final version.
