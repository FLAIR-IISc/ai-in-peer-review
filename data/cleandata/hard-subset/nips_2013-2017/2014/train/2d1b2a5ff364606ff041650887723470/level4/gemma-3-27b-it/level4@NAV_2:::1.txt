This paper develops a new method of performing blind source separation, by formulating the problem as an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM), and then applying signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The motivation behind this is that additional domain knowledge can be incorporated to improve the separation of the time series into components. The example used throughout the paper is energy disaggregation, where the components of domestic energy use (relating to individual appliances) can be better separated when information relating to total (expected) usage of each appliance in a time period is incorporated. The objective function that is maximized to perform the separation (which is the log of the posterior distribution of the hidden chains given the observed data) is then transformed into a convex optimization problem. The efficacy of the method is demonstrated in practice with both a toy data set, and with real data from domestic energy output readings.

The paper is technically sound with a strong experimental section. The method, as expected, performs favorably in contrast to a regular AFHMM and also AFAMAP (a recently developed alternative method) – neither of which incorporate the SACs. The improvements are shown to be significant using t-tests. The authors also show that the speed of optimization is comparable to AFAMAP. A question regarding the results section is whether the low noise variance used in the toy example was chosen to clearly illustrate the method’s separation capabilities, and if so, whether this reflects realistic signal-to-noise ratios. Clarification on this point would be valuable. Furthermore, the authors could comment on the signal-to-noise ratio observed between the mains readings and the aggregate appliance output in the energy application.

The theoretical aspects of the paper are well presented and structured. A formal proof demonstrating the convexity of the procedure in equation (6) would strengthen the manuscript, as the current reasoning is not fully conclusive. Additionally, theoretical scaling arguments with respect to data length and the number of hidden states would be beneficial and complement the simulation findings. Specifically, discussing the tractability of the method for much larger datasets and state spaces, and comparing it to AFAMAP, would be insightful.

The paper is well written and organized. The problem is nicely motivated in the introduction, with a clear description of relevant literature. While not an expert in this specific area, I found the literature review to be comprehensive. A potential minor correction on page 4 (line 202), in the unnumbered equation before (6), suggests that the commas in the subscripts preceding the object *p* may be unnecessary, given the notation used on page 3.

To my knowledge, the work is novel and original. However, as a non-expert, I cannot definitively assess the significance of these results to the HMM community and the energy application domain.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-motivated approach to blind source separation.
*   Significant performance improvements over existing methods (AFHMM, AFAMAP) on both simulated and real-world data.
*   Computational efficiency comparable to state-of-the-art methods.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of a formal convexity proof for the optimization problem.
*   Absence of theoretical scaling analysis for large datasets and state spaces.
*   Potential for clarification regarding the signal-to-noise ratio in the toy example.
