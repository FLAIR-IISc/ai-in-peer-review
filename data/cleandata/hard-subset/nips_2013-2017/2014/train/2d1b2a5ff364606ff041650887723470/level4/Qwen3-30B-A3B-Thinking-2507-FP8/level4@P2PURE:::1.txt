

This paper introduces a novel approach to blind source separation by modeling the problem as an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) with signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The core motivation is incorporating domain knowledge to enhance time series decomposition, demonstrated through energy disaggregation where components corresponding to individual appliances are better separated by leveraging expected appliance usage over time periods. The optimization objective—a log-posterior distribution of hidden chains given observations—is transformed into a convex problem. Efficacy is validated on both synthetic and real-world domestic energy data.

The technical contributions are sound, with robust simulations showing favorable performance against baseline AFHMM and AFAMAP (a recent alternative that omits SACs), with statistically significant improvements confirmed via t-tests. Optimization speed is comparable to AFAMAP. A minor concern arises regarding the toy dataset: the unusually low noise variance (page 4, line 202) appears designed to clearly illustrate separation in Figure 2, but this setup seems overly simplistic. Clarification is needed on whether such low noise levels reflect real-world scenarios or if the signal-to-noise ratios in energy disaggregation (e.g., between mains readings and appliance aggregates) justify this choice.

The theoretical exposition is generally clear but would benefit from two additions: (1) a proof establishing convexity of the procedure in Equation (6), as the current reasoning is insufficient; (2) theoretical scaling analysis (with respect to data length and hidden states) to complement simulation results, particularly regarding tractability for larger datasets or state spaces compared to AFAMAP.

The paper is well-written and structured, with a compelling introduction and literature context. A minor notation inconsistency is noted on page 4 (line 202): the unnumbered equation preceding (6) uses inconsistent subscripts (commas in p's subscripts) compared to page 3's notation. The work appears novel within the HMM domain, though the reviewer, not being a domain specialist, cannot fully assess its significance to energy applications.

The authors satisfactorily addressed these points in their response, and the paper presents a strong method for blind source separation, particularly effective in energy disaggregation compared to state-of-the-art approaches.
