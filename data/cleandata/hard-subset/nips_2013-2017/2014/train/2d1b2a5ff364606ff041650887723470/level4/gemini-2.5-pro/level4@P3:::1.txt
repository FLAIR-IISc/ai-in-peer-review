### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), a challenging task due to the inherent unidentifiability of the source signals. The authors propose a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge into the BSS framework through what they term Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The core idea is that for many problems, one has prior knowledge about the expected total value (or aggregate) of each source signal over a given time period.

The authors integrate these SACs into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a common model for BSS, with a specific application to energy disaggregation. In this context, the aggregate energy consumption of individual appliances over a day is often roughly known. Because the SACs introduce long-range dependencies that break the Markov property, standard inference methods like the Viterbi algorithm are not applicable. To overcome this, the authors formulate MAP inference as a penalized optimization problem and derive a convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation, which can be solved efficiently.

The proposed method, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on both synthetic data and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset. The results demonstrate that incorporating SACs leads to a substantial and statistically significant improvement in disaggregation accuracy compared to both the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP, while maintaining comparable computational efficiency.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novel and Intuitive Contribution:** The introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints is a simple, intuitive, and novel way to regularize the BSS problem. It provides a general mechanism for injecting valuable domain knowledge that is often available in practical applications but is not easily captured by standard models.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides a compelling empirical evaluation. On a challenging, real-world energy disaggregation dataset, the proposed method reduces the normalized disaggregation error by 45% compared to the baseline AFHMM and 29% compared to the state-of-the-art AFAMAP. These improvements are shown to be statistically significant, clearly demonstrating the efficacy of the approach.

3.  **Technically Sound Inference:** The authors provide a sound technical contribution by formulating the challenging non-Markovian inference problem as a CQP. This makes the inference tractable and scalable, as demonstrated by the runtime comparisons which show it to be competitive with existing methods.

4.  **Well-Written and Motivated:** The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and the problem is well-motivated with the energy disaggregation example. The connection to the posterior regularization framework in Section 6 further strengthens the theoretical grounding of the proposed regularizer.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is strong overall, but could be improved with some additional clarification. My initial questions were satisfactorily addressed by the authors during the response period, and I recommend that these clarifications be incorporated into the final manuscript.

1.  **Justification of Experimental Parameters:**
    *   In the toy data experiment (Section 7.1), the noise variance is set to a very low value (σ² = 0.01). It would be beneficial to briefly justify this choice, clarifying whether this signal-to-noise ratio is representative of real-world scenarios or was selected primarily for a clear illustration.
    *   Similarly, for the real-data experiments (Section 7.2.2), a brief comment on the signal-to-noise ratio (e.g., the proportion of the total mains signal that comes from unmodeled appliances) would provide valuable context for interpreting the results.

2.  **Clarity of Theoretical Claims:**
    *   The paper states that the relaxed optimization problem is a convex quadratic program. While this follows from the positive semidefinite nature of the matrices Λi and Vt, the argument would be more self-contained and convincing with a more explicit statement or brief justification of the objective function's convexity.
    *   The empirical timing results are useful, but the paper would be strengthened by the inclusion of a theoretical complexity analysis. A discussion of how the proposed CQP's runtime scales with respect to the time series length (T), number of sources (I), and number of states per source (K) would complement the empirical findings and provide deeper insight into the method's scalability.

### **Minor Comments**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Originality & Significance:** To my knowledge, the work is original. The proposed SACs appear to be a significant and practical contribution, particularly for applied fields like energy disaggregation where such aggregate prior knowledge is available. The results suggest this method advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.
*   **Typo:** There may be a minor notational inconsistency in the unnumbered equation before Equation (6) on page 4. The transition probability subscript appears as `p(i)jk`, which seems inconsistent with the `p(i)jk` notation used elsewhere. Please check for consistency.

### **Conclusion**

This paper introduces a novel, effective, and practical method for improving blind source separation by incorporating signal aggregate constraints. The approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and supported by strong empirical evidence on a challenging real-world task. The authors' response clarified my main questions, and if these clarifications are included in the final version, the paper will be an excellent contribution to the conference. I recommend acceptance.
