This paper introduces a novel extension to the additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) by incorporating constraints that enforce consistency between individual chain outputs and a known aggregate value. The authors develop a convex relaxation for approximate Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference and demonstrate its effectiveness on energy disaggregation tasks using both low-frequency data from 100 UK homes and higher-frequency data from 10 homes (without appliance-level monitoring).

While the core concepts are relatively direct and the optimization approach is uncomplicated, the work is nonetheless impactful. The application of these methods to substantial real-world datasets, coupled with the blend of algorithmic development and practical application, positions this as a successful contribution. Several points, if addressed, could further strengthen the manuscript:

The authors motivate the single-chain-state constraint in AFAMAP by suggesting it improves the likelihood of obtaining integer solutions within the relaxation. It would be valuable to understand whether the SACAFHMM optimization achieves a similar effect, or if its performance advantage persists even with non-integral solutions. A discussion clarifying this aspect would be insightful and further differentiate the two approaches.

The AFAMAP and SACAFHMM approaches appear fundamentally complementary; the constraints used in SACAFHMM could conceivably be integrated into the AFAMAP optimization framework. While not expecting a response during rebuttal, I encourage the authors to explore this combination for a potential final version.

Throughout the paper, there is some blurring of the lines between models and optimization procedures. For example, comparing “AFHMM” and “AFAMAP” lacks precision, as AFAMAP is an algorithm for approximating MAP estimates within the AFHMM framework. Is the “AFHMM” referred to in these comparisons actually the alternating Viterbi optimization? Clarification on this point would be helpful, and it reinforces the potential for combining the strategies employed by the different algorithms presented, potentially leading to improved performance. 

In summary, this paper presents a straightforward extension and optimization strategy for a variant of the AFHMM. The compelling results obtained on real-world data, however, make this a sound and reasonable contribution.
