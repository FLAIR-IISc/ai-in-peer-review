This paper proposes an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) incorporating Signal Aggregate Constraints (SAC) to address the problem of blind source separation. The authors formulate inference for a relaxed version of the model as a convex quadratic program. The proposed technique demonstrates promising results on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper is well-written and is built on a solid mathematical foundation.
*   The motivation for the problem is clear and compelling.
*   The evaluation results indicate significant improvements in the performance metrics used, namely Normalized Decomposition Error (NDE) and Signal to Artifact Error (SAE).

**Areas for Improvement:**
*   The proposed model, SACAFHMM, is compared against AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP. This comparison seems potentially unfair, as SACAFHMM leverages additional information (the aggregate signal) that the baseline models do not have access to. A more compelling comparison would be against other methods that can also incorporate signal aggregate constraints. Are there such alternative approaches, even if they are less computationally efficient? This would help isolate the contribution of the proposed formulation from the benefit of simply using more information.

*   The results presented in Figure 1 are not entirely clear. It appears that for distances greater than 10^4, the baseline AFAMAP outperforms SACAFHMM. This raises an important question: can noisy or inaccurate constraints lead to a degradation in performance compared to methods that do not use them? The discussion in the paper seems to suggest that the proposed method always improves performance, which appears to be contradicted by this result and could be misleading.

*   The paper focuses on signal aggregate constraints, but it would be valuable to discuss the model's potential to incorporate more complex or diverse types of constraints. For example:
    *   Mutually exclusive states (e.g., a person cannot use a heater and a cooler simultaneously).
    *   Cardinality constraints (e.g., no more than 3 out of 10 light bulbs can be active at once).
    *   Temporal constraints (e.g., a grinder can only be active for less than 10 minutes).
    A discussion on the feasibility of extending the proposed framework to handle these varied constraint types would significantly strengthen the paper and its potential for future work.
