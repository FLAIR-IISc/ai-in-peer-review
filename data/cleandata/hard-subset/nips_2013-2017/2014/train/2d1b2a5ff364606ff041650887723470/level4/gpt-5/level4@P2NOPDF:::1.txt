This paper proposes a new approach to blind source separation by framing the task as an Additive Factorial HMM (AFHMM) augmented with Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The key idea is to inject domain knowledge—e.g., expected usage totals over intervals—so the latent time-series components are better identified. The primary application studied is energy disaggregation, where appliance-level consumption is separated from mains readings; incorporating aggregate usage priors for appliances improves identifiability. The authors maximize the posterior log-probability of the hidden chains given observations and reformulate the resulting objective as a convex optimization problem. The method is demonstrated on both synthetic data and real household energy data.

The paper is technically solid and the experiments are thorough. As expected, the proposed method outperforms a standard AFHMM baseline and AFAMAP (which do not use SACs). Reported gains are statistically significant via t-tests, and optimization time is comparable to AFAMAP. One question on the experiments: the toy example appears to use a very low observation noise variance. Was this choice made primarily to highlight the separation performance in Figure 2? If so, please state this explicitly; otherwise the example risks appearing too easy. Are such signal-to-noise ratios representative of real settings? Relatedly, for the energy application, it would be helpful to report typical SNRs between mains readings and the sum of appliance-level outputs.

The theoretical development is generally clear and well organized. However, a formal proof that the final program in equation (6) is convex would strengthen the paper; the current justification was not entirely convincing to me. In addition, some complexity and scaling analysis—e.g., as a function of sequence length and the number of hidden states—would be valuable. How does the proposed method scale to substantially larger datasets or state spaces, and how does this compare in practice and theory to AFAMAP?

The paper is well written and nicely structured. The introduction provides a clear motivation and a helpful review of related work. I am not an expert in this specific subarea, so I cannot definitively confirm that all pertinent literature is covered, but the positioning seems reasonable. I did notice a possible notational inconsistency on page 4 (line 202): in the unnumbered equation immediately before (6), the subscripts following p appear to include commas; this seems inconsistent with the notation on page 3 unless new notation is being introduced.

To the best of my knowledge, the approach is novel and original. While I cannot fully assess its significance for the HMM community or for energy disaggregation, the empirical results are promising and the idea of integrating aggregate constraints into AFHMMs is compelling.

Addendum: The authors satisfactorily addressed my questions during the rebuttal. I encourage them to incorporate those clarifications in the revised manuscript. Overall, this work presents an effective blind source separation method based on AFHMMs with SACs, showing strong performance relative to state-of-the-art baselines, especially on real energy disaggregation data.
