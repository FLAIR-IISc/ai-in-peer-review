This paper introduces a novel method for blind source separation by formulating the problem within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework and incorporating Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The core motivation is that by integrating domain-specific knowledge—in the form of constraints on the expected aggregate value of each source over a given period—the separation of the mixed time series can be significantly improved. The authors use energy disaggregation as a compelling running example, demonstrating how knowledge of an individual appliance's total expected energy consumption can help isolate its signal from the aggregate household energy reading. A key contribution is the transformation of the log-posterior objective function into a convex optimization problem, ensuring an efficient and reliable inference procedure. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on both a synthetic dataset and real-world domestic energy data.

The paper is technically sound and supported by a strong experimental section. The proposed method, AFHMM-SAC, performs favorably against both a standard AFHMM and the recently developed AFAMAP, neither of which incorporate aggregate constraints. The performance improvements are shown to be statistically significant via t-tests, and the optimization speed is demonstrated to be comparable to that of AFAMAP.

My initial review raised a few points that the authors have since clarified satisfactorily during the author feedback phase. I recommend that these clarifications be incorporated into the final version of the paper to strengthen the presentation. Specifically:

1.  **Experimental Setup:** The toy example utilized a very low noise variance. While this choice clearly illustrated the performance gap between the proposed method and the baselines in Figure 2, it initially made the problem appear somewhat simplified. The authors should include their justification for this choice in the paper and, importantly, comment on how the signal-to-noise ratios in both the toy example and the real-world energy application compare to typical values found in this problem domain.

2.  **Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical aspects of the paper are well-presented. However, the manuscript would be strengthened by including a formal proof that the final optimization problem in equation (6) is convex. The reasoning provided in the current draft is brief, and a more detailed derivation would add rigor and clarity.

3.  **Scalability Analysis:** While the empirical results on optimization speed are valuable, the paper would benefit from a theoretical analysis of the method's scalability. This analysis should discuss the computational complexity with respect to the length of the time series and the number of hidden states. A direct comparison of these scaling properties with those of AFAMAP would be particularly insightful and would complement the simulation findings, helping readers understand the method's tractability for much larger datasets.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and provides a concise overview of the relevant literature. The work appears to be novel and original. A minor potential error was noted on page 4 (line 202): in the unnumbered equation preceding (6), the commas in the subscripts of *p* seem inconsistent with the notation established on page 3. This should be reviewed for consistency.

In summary, this paper presents a well-executed and valuable contribution. The proposed method of incorporating signal aggregate constraints into an AFHMM framework is elegant and effective. Its superior performance, particularly on the real-world energy disaggregation task, is convincingly demonstrated. The authors have addressed the main concerns raised during the review process, and if the recommended clarifications regarding the experimental design, convexity proof, and scalability are incorporated into the final manuscript, it will represent a strong and impactful publication.
