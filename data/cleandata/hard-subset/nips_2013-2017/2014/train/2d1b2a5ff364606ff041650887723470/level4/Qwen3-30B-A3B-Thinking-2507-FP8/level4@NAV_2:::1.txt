

This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation within additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs), specifically applied to energy disaggregation. SACs incorporate domain knowledge by constraining the total signal magnitude of each source (e.g., expected daily energy consumption per appliance) to specified values, addressing the inherent unidentifiability of blind source separation. The authors formulate a convex quadratic program (CQP) to solve the constrained MAP inference problem, which is computationally efficient. On real-world energy disaggregation data, SACs reduce normalized disaggregation error by 45% compared to the baseline AFHMM and 29% compared to the state-of-the-art AFAMAP approach, with statistically significant improvements verified via paired t-tests. The method is also validated on a toy dataset demonstrating robustness to constraint specification errors.

The paper excels in technical quality and significance. The CQP formulation is sound, and the energy disaggregation results are compelling, showing clear practical impact. The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) strengthens theoretical grounding. The writing is clear and well-structured, with appropriate motivation and related work coverage. The authors correctly note the novelty of SACs as a generalizable constraint class beyond prior work on HMM state constraints (e.g., [12, 21]).

Key strengths include: (1) Significant empirical improvements over strong baselines on real-world data, (2) Effective demonstration of SACs’ utility in a high-impact application (energy disaggregation), (3) Theoretical justification via posterior regularization, (4) Computational efficiency comparable to AFAMAP (Table 1), and (5) Clear presentation of the CQP derivation.

Notable weaknesses: (1) The toy dataset uses an unrealistically low noise variance (σ²=0.01), which may artificially simplify separation (Figure 1). The authors should clarify whether this reflects typical signal-to-noise ratios in energy data or was chosen for illustrative purposes. (2) The convexity of the relaxed problem (Equation 6) requires a formal proof; the current justification is insufficient for a NIPS audience. (3) The paper lacks theoretical scaling analysis (e.g., computational complexity vs. sequence length/state count) to assess tractability for larger-scale applications beyond the reported experiments. (4) A minor notation inconsistency appears on page 4 (line 202) where commas erroneously separate subscripts in the transition probability notation.

The work is highly original in proposing SACs as a generalizable constraint class for blind source separation, distinct from prior methods that constrain state transitions or duration distributions. It advances the state of the art in energy disaggregation by leveraging domain-specific aggregate knowledge, with clear potential for broader application in other BSS domains. The paper meets all NIPS criteria: technically rigorous (Quality), well-written (Clarity), novel (Originality), and impactful (Significance).

The authors satisfactorily addressed the reviewer’s concerns in the response, particularly clarifying the toy data noise variance and providing additional context for SACs in energy applications. With minor revisions to address the convexity proof and scaling analysis, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
