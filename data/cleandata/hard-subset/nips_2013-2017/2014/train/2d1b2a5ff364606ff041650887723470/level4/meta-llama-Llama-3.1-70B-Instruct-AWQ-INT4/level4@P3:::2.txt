The paper "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation" proposes a novel approach to blind source separation problems by incorporating domain knowledge into the model. The authors introduce signal aggregate constraints (SACs) that encourage the total signal for each of the unknown sources to be close to a specified value. This is based on the observation that the total signal often varies widely across the unknown sources, and we often have a good idea of what total values to expect.

The authors incorporate SACs into an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) to formulate the energy disaggregation problem. They employ a convex quadratic program for approximate inference, which is computationally efficient. The results on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset show that the use of SACs significantly improves the original AFHMM, and outperforms a recent state-of-the-art approach.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method. However, there are some minor issues with the presentation. The paper is dense with acronyms, which can make it difficult to read. Some of the acronyms, such as AFHMM+SAC, are quite long and may be confusing for non-experts.

The authors could improve the clarity of the paper by providing a clear explanation of the motivation behind the proposed method. They should also explicitly state that they are using a prior constraint to constrain the posterior density. This would help to clarify the relationship between the prior and posterior distributions.

In terms of the technical quality of the paper, the authors provide a thorough derivation of the proposed method and demonstrate its effectiveness on a real-world dataset. The results are promising, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the advantages of their approach.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a significant contribution to the field of blind source separation. However, some minor improvements to the presentation and clarity of the paper would make it more accessible to a wider audience.
