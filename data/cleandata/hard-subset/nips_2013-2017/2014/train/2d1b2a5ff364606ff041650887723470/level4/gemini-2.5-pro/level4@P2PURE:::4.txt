This paper extends the additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) by introducing constraints that require the sum of outputs from individual chains to approximate a known aggregate value. The authors propose a convex relaxation for MAP inference and apply their method to two real-world energy disaggregation datasets: one with low-frequency readings from 100 UK homes, and another with high-frequency readings from 10 homes where individual appliances were not monitored.

While the proposed ideas and optimization formulation are relatively straightforward, the work is compelling. The application of these methods to large-scale, real-world datasets is a key strength, and the paper successfully balances both applied and algorithmic contributions. The following points could help improve the paper:

The paper could be strengthened by clarifying the distinction between models and inference algorithms. For instance, the text compares the performance of "AFHMM" versus "AFAMAP". This is imprecise, as AFAMAP is an algorithm for approximate MAP inference in the AFHMM model. It seems the authors use "AFHMM" to refer to a baseline inference method, perhaps alternating Viterbi optimization, but this should be stated explicitly.

This imprecision obscures a key point: the core ideas of AFAMAP and the proposed SACAFHMM appear to be orthogonal. The sum-to-aggregate constraints from SACAFHMM could presumably be incorporated into the AFAMAP formulation. While I do not expect this to be addressed during the author response, I would strongly encourage the authors to explore this combination for the final version, as it might outperform either method alone.

In a similar vein, the paper would benefit from a deeper comparison of the two methods' relaxation properties. A motivation for AFAMAP's constraint—that only one chain changes state at a time—is that it encourages integer solutions. Does the SACAFHMM formulation have a similar effect, or do its solutions achieve lower error despite being non-integral? A discussion of this point would help elucidate the trade-offs between the two approaches.
