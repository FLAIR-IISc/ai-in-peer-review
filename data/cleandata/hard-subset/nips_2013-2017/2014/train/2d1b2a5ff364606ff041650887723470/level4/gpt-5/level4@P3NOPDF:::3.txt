Summary:
The paper introduces an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) augmented with signal aggregate constraints (SAC) to address blind source separation. The authors formulate a convex quadratic program by relaxing the discrete states and incorporating linear aggregate constraints, enabling efficient optimization. Experiments on synthetic and real datasets show promising gains in standard metrics (NDE and SAE) over AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation and a mathematically well-grounded formulation.
- The convex relaxation with linear constraints is elegant and appears practically useful.
- Empirical results indicate meaningful performance improvements in both synthetic and real settings.
- The work is relevant to applications such as energy disaggregation, where aggregate information is available.

Areas for improvement and questions:
- Fairness of comparisons: The proposed method leverages additional information (SAC) that baselines (AFHMM without SAC, AFAMAP) do not. This makes the head-to-head comparison potentially unfair. Are there existing approaches that can incorporate similar aggregate constraints (even if computationally heavier), and how does the proposed method compare to them in accuracy and runtime? For example, a constrained-MAP or MIP/Viterbi variant, or Lagrangian-penalized inference that uses the same constraints, would provide a more apples-to-apples baseline.
- Sensitivity to constraint quality: Figure 1 appears to show that for distances beyond 10^4, AFAMAP can outperform SAC-AFHMM, suggesting that poorly specified or noisy constraints may harm performance. Yet the discussion seems to imply monotonic improvements. Please clarify:
  - What precisely is plotted (axis labels, metric definitions) and how to interpret the threshold at 10^4?
  - How sensitive is the method to constraint noise or misspecification? Can you provide a robustness analysis where constraint quality is systematically varied?
  - Does the optimization include regularization or soft penalties that can downweight unreliable constraints, and how are these weights selected?
- Constraint expressiveness: The paper focuses on aggregate constraints (e.g., totals over windows). It would strengthen the contribution to discuss or demonstrate support for richer and practically important constraints:
  - Mutual exclusivity: e.g., heater and cooler cannot be on simultaneously.
  - Cardinality limits: e.g., no more than k devices active at once.
  - Duration and temporal logic: e.g., grinders run for less than 10 minutes; minimum on/off durations; time-of-day schedules.
  - Can these be expressed as linear inequalities compatible with the convex relaxation, or would they require integer variables/MIP? What is the computational impact?
- Clarity of figures and interpretation: The current presentation of Figure 1 is hard to parse. Please improve labeling, include error bars or confidence intervals, and align the textual claims with what the figure supports (avoiding suggestions that performance is always better).
- Computational aspects: Since the main contribution is an efficiently solvable convex program, please report:
  - Runtimes and scaling with number of chains, time steps, and constraints.
  - Solver details, convergence tolerances, and whether warm starts are used.
  - How rounding/integer recovery is performed and its impact on accuracy (e.g., integrality gap, frequency of constraint violations after rounding).
- Hyperparameter selection: How are weights on constraint penalties chosen (e.g., cross-validation, held-out data), and how sensitive are results to these choices?
- Data usage and realism: Are aggregate constraints derived in a way that is realistic for deployment, and is there any risk of test-time leakage? A brief discussion would increase confidence in the reported gains.

Suggestions to strengthen the paper:
- Add a baseline that uses the same constraints (even if slower), such as a MIP or constrained Viterbi approach, to separate the value of constraints from the value of the proposed relaxation.
- Include an ablation varying the quantity and accuracy of constraints to characterize robustness and identify regimes where constraints help or hurt.
- Provide at least one example of each richer constraint type (mutual exclusivity, cardinality, duration) and indicate whether they can be integrated without losing convexity; if not, outline a hybrid approach (e.g., convex core with a few binary variables) and its trade-offs.
- Improve figure clarity and ensure the narrative does not overstate monotonic improvements.
- Report detailed runtime and scaling results.

Minor comments:
- Expand NDE and SAE on first use.
- Standardize terminology (e.g., SAC-AFHMM) and ensure consistent symbols between text and equations.

Overall assessment:
This is a well-written and technically solid paper that addresses an important practical gap by leveraging aggregate constraints through a convex relaxation. The empirical results are promising. However, the comparison fairness, sensitivity to constraint quality, and constraint expressiveness need clearer treatment. With added analyses and stronger baselines that use the same information, the contribution would be compelling. I lean toward acceptance if these issues are addressed.
