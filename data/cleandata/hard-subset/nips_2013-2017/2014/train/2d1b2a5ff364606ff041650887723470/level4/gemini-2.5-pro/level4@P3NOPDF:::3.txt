This paper introduces the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model with Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACAFHMM), a novel approach for blind source separation. The core contribution is the incorporation of constraints based on the known aggregate signal into the AFHMM framework. The authors formulate the inference problem as a convex quadratic program for a relaxed version of the model, which allows for efficient optimization. The proposed technique is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating promising improvements over existing methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clarity and Rigor:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The mathematical foundations of the proposed model are sound and rigorously presented, making the technical contribution clear.

2.  **Strong Motivation:** The motivation for the problem is compelling and practical. In many real-world applications, such as energy disaggregation, the aggregate signal is readily available. Leveraging this information as an explicit constraint is an intuitive and impactful idea that directly addresses a common scenario.

3.  **Empirical Performance:** The evaluation results show a significant performance boost on key metrics like Normalized Disaggregation Error (NDE) and Signal Aggregate Error (SAE) when compared to baselines. The validation on both synthetic and real-world data strengthens the claims and highlights the practical utility of the method.

**Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparison:** The primary baselines for comparison are the standard AFHMM (without SAC) and AFAMAP. A key concern is that the proposed SACAFHMM method is given access to an additional source of information—the aggregate signal—which the baselines do not use. This makes the comparison potentially inequitable, as the performance gain might stem more from the additional data than from the superiority of the model's architecture itself. To provide a more conclusive comparison, it would be beneficial to discuss or evaluate against other methods that could also incorporate such aggregate constraints, even if they are computationally less efficient. This would help to better isolate and validate the contribution of the proposed formulation.

2.  **Nuance in Results Discussion:** The interpretation of the results in Figure 1 could be more nuanced. The figure appears to show that for distances greater than 10^4, the AFAMAP baseline actually outperforms SACAFHMM. This finding seems to contradict the general claim in the text that the proposed method is consistently better. This discrepancy should be explicitly addressed. It raises an important question: can noisy or inaccurate constraints lead to performance degradation, potentially making the model perform worse than simpler alternatives? A discussion on the model's sensitivity to the quality of the aggregate signal constraint would significantly enhance the paper's contribution and provide a more complete picture of the method's limitations.

3.  **Extensibility to More Complex Constraints:** The paper effectively demonstrates the utility of a sum-to-total constraint. However, many real-world problems feature a much richer set of possible constraints. It would strengthen the paper to discuss the potential for extending the framework to incorporate these. Examples include:
    *   **Mutual Exclusivity:** Certain sources cannot be active simultaneously (e.g., a heater and an air conditioner).
    *   **Cardinality Constraints:** No more than a certain number of sources can be active at one time (e.g., at most 3 out of 10 lights in a house).
    *   **Temporal Constraints:** A specific source may have operational time limits (e.g., a kitchen grinder running for less than 10 minutes).
    A discussion on whether the proposed convex quadratic programming framework can be extended to handle such linear or logical constraints—or if it would require a fundamental reformulation—would provide valuable insight into the broader applicability of this approach.

**Conclusion:**

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and well-executed contribution to the field of blind source separation. The idea of incorporating signal aggregate constraints into the AFHMM framework is novel and practical, and the empirical results are strong. The work could be further improved by strengthening the experimental comparison, providing a more transparent discussion of the model's limitations, and exploring its extensibility to more complex and varied real-world constraints. Addressing these points would significantly enhance the paper's impact.
