This paper develops a new method for blind source separation by incorporating signal aggregate constraints (SACs) into an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) framework. The motivation is to leverage domain knowledge to improve the decomposition of a time series into its constituent components. This approach is demonstrated using the application of energy disaggregation, where the aggregate energy signal from a household is separated into signals from individual appliances. The separation is improved by incorporating constraints on the expected total usage of each appliance over a given time period. The resulting objective function, the log-posterior of the hidden states given the observations, is then transformed into a convex optimization problem. The efficacy of the method is demonstrated on both a toy dataset and real-world energy consumption data.

The paper is technically sound and is supported by a strong experimental section. As expected, the proposed method outperforms a standard AFHMM and the recently developed AFAMAP, neither of which incorporate SACs. The improvements are shown to be statistically significant via t-tests. The authors also show that the method's computational runtime is comparable to that of AFAMAP.

My only question regarding the results section concerns the low noise variance used in the toy example. Was this choice made to more clearly illustrate the method's superior performance in Figure 2, where it effectively separates the signals while other methods fail? Clarification on this point would be welcome, as the current problem setup could be perceived as artificially simple. Alternatively, do such signal-to-noise ratios reflect those found in real-world data? On a related note, the authors could strengthen the paper by commenting on the signal-to-noise ratio between the mains readings and the aggregate appliance signals in the real-world energy application.

The theoretical aspects of the paper are generally well-presented and structured. However, the paper would be strengthened by a formal proof of convexity for the optimization problem in Equation (6), as the justification provided is not immediately obvious. In addition, a theoretical analysis of the method's scalability with respect to data length and the number of hidden states would be a valuable addition and would complement the empirical findings. Such an analysis should address the method's tractability for larger datasets and state spaces and compare its scaling properties to those of AFAMAP.

The paper is well-written and structured. The introduction provides strong motivation for the problem and a clear description of the relevant literature, although my expertise in this specific area is limited, so I cannot definitively comment on the completeness of the literature review. I would like to point out a potential notational inconsistency on page 4 (line 202) in the unnumbered equation preceding (6). To be consistent with the notation on page 3, I believe there should be no commas in the subscripts following the variable *p*.

To my knowledge, the work is novel and original. However, as I am not an expert in this subfield, I cannot comment on the significance of these results to the broader HMM community or to the specific energy application studied.

**Addendum:** The authors satisfactorily answered my questions in the feedback phase, and I encourage them to incorporate these clarifications into the final version of the paper. This paper proposes an effective method for performing blind source separation using Additive Factorial HMMs with Signal Aggregate Constraints. The method demonstrates excellent performance compared to the state-of-the-art, particularly on a real-world energy disaggregation problem.
