The paper proposes an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) with signal aggregate constraints (SAC) to address the blind source separation problem. The authors present a convex quadratic program for approximate inference with the relaxed AFHMM+SAC model. The technique demonstrates promising results on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Summary of Contributions:** The paper introduces a method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation via signal aggregate constraints, specifically within the framework of an additive factorial HMM. They formulate a computationally efficient convex quadratic program for approximate inference and demonstrate significant performance improvements on an energy disaggregation dataset. This work builds on prior research in probabilistic models with prior constraints [4, 13, 14] and advances the state-of-the-art in energy disaggregation [12, 22].

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Technical Foundation:** The paper is well-written and mathematically sound, clearly explaining the model and optimization procedure.
*   **Motivating Problem:** The motivation for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation, particularly in the context of energy disaggregation, is well-articulated. The challenges of identifiability and interpretability are clearly explained.
*   **Empirically Validated:** The evaluation results demonstrate a substantial improvement in performance metrics (NDE and SAE) compared to baseline methods.
*   **Novelty:** The introduction of signal aggregate constraints and their integration into the AFHMM framework is a novel contribution. The derivation of the convex quadratic program is also a significant result.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Comparison Fairness:** The results are presented as improvements over AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP. However, AFHMM+SAC utilizes information (the expected aggregate values) not directly used by the other methods. While justified as domain knowledge, it's unclear if the comparison is entirely fair. It would be valuable to discuss whether other approaches could *also* incorporate similar prior information, and if so, whether AFHMM+SAC maintains its advantage in efficiency or accuracy.
*   **Figure 1 Clarity:** The results presented in Figure 1 are not entirely clear. It appears that for distances greater than 10<sup>4</sup>, AFAMAP outperforms AFHMM+SAC. This raises the question of whether inaccurate or poorly chosen constraints can lead to significantly degraded performance. The discussion suggests consistently better results, which might be misleading. A more nuanced discussion of the sensitivity to constraint accuracy is needed.
*   **Constraint Complexity:** The paper focuses on relatively simple aggregate constraints (total energy consumption). While a good starting point, the potential for more complex constraints is not fully explored. Examples like mutually exclusive appliance usage (heater and cooler) or temporal constraints (grinder usage duration) could be discussed. Investigating the extensibility of the algorithm to handle more sophisticated constraints would strengthen the work. 

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation and energy disaggregation. The proposed method offers a principled way to incorporate domain knowledge, leading to improved performance. The technical details are clearly explained, and the experiments are well-designed. While the comparison to other methods could be further refined, and a discussion of constraint sensitivity is warranted, the overall quality of the work is high.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge into a well-established BSS framework.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating significant performance gains on a real-world dataset.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the technical details.
*   Potentially impactful for applications such as energy disaggregation and other BSS problems.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The fairness of the comparison to existing methods could be debated.
*   The sensitivity of the approach to inaccurate constraints is not fully explored.
*   The limited scope of constraint complexity may restrict the applicability of the method.



**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions. I recommend the authors address the concerns regarding comparison fairness and constraint sensitivity in the revised version. Exploring the potential for more complex constraints could also be a valuable addition.
