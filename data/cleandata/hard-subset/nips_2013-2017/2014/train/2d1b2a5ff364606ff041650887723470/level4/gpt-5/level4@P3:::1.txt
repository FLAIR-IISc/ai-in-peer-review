Summary
This paper addresses blind source separation (BSS) when only a single aggregate signal is observed, with a focus on energy disaggregation. The authors augment an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) with signal aggregate constraints (SACs) that encode domain knowledge about expected total signal per source (e.g., daily energy used by each appliance). They formulate MAP inference with these constraints and show that, by introducing transition-indicator variables, the problem can be relaxed to a convex quadratic program (CQP) that is efficiently solvable. They also connect their formulation to posterior regularization, showing the SAC penalty arises naturally from constraining the posterior moments. Empirically, on a synthetic dataset and on the UK HES energy data, AFHMM+SAC substantially improves normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) relative to AFHMM and the interleaved-change AFAMAP method, with comparable runtime.

Relation to prior work
The approach complements prior attempts to improve identifiability in factorial HMMs for energy disaggregation, including constraints on the number/timing of state changes (AFAMAP), non-homogeneous transitions, and semi-Markov duration models. Unlike these state-dynamics constraints, SACs encode aggregate information that is often available across domains. The connection to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.) and related frameworks (generalized expectation, measurement-based learning) is appropriate and well explained; here, the authors instantiate a particularly useful class of constraints with a tractable relaxation.

Quality
- Soundness: The modeling idea is clear and well grounded. The optimization proceeds by introducing pairwise transition variables Hit, yielding an integer quadratic program whose relaxation is a CQP. The objective has concave quadratic terms in S (due to negative semidefinite Hessians from −Λi and −Vt) plus linear terms in H, and the S–H coupling is enforced via linear constraints, so the relaxed program is convex. Providing a brief formal proof (e.g., stating the Hessian structure and sign definiteness) would strengthen the presentation.
- Empirics: Results on both synthetic and real data are convincing. Improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP are consistent across settings and statistically significant via paired t-tests. Runtime is comparable across methods. A useful toy experiment varies the SAC targets to illustrate robustness to misspecification.
- Questions: The toy experiment uses very low noise variance; it would help to justify this choice or report performance across a range of signal-to-noise ratios (SNRs). For the energy application, a brief characterization of typical mains vs. appliance SNRs (and how measurement noise is modeled) would add context.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well structured. The introduction motivates identifiability and the value of domain knowledge; related work is broad and balanced. A few suggestions:
- Convexity: As noted above, add a concise argument that the relaxed problem is a convex maximization of a concave quadratic subject to linear constraints, or provide a pointer to standard results.
- Scaling: Include complexity guidance beyond a qualitative statement, e.g., number of variables/constraints and asymptotic runtime in terms of T and ∑i Ki. A simple accounting suggests O(T ∑i Ki) variables for S and O(T ∑i Ki^2) for H, with linear constraints of the same order. Discuss how this compares to AFAMAP’s scaling.
- Notation/typos: On p.4 (line ~202), in the unnumbered equation before (6), please check the subscript commas in the transition probabilities to ensure consistency with the earlier p(i)jk notation.
- Hyperparameters: You set λi = 1 in all experiments; consider providing a brief sensitivity analysis or cross-validation protocol, especially when µ0 is noisy (e.g., national averages).

Originality
The specific class of constraints (aggregate totals over time) and their integration into AFHMMs via a convex relaxation is, to my knowledge, novel and practically compelling. The posterior-regularization perspective is a solid theoretical anchor, and the extension to multiple time-window constraints is a useful generalization.

Significance
Energy disaggregation is a prominent application with practical impact, and the proposed constraints are readily available from external sources (e.g., national surveys). The empirical gains—especially on low-frequency, realistic HES data—suggest this approach can materially improve downstream analysis. Beyond energy, the idea should transfer to other BSS problems where total signal is informative (e.g., biology, audio). The reliance on a general-purpose CQP solver (CVX) may limit out-of-the-box scalability, but the structure could be exploited by specialized solvers.

Pros
- Clear and useful modeling idea: encode aggregate domain knowledge via SACs.
- Solid derivation, with a principled link to posterior regularization.
- Convex relaxation enabling reliable optimization.
- Strong empirical improvements on realistic, low-frequency data; statistical significance established.
- Comparable runtime to baselines.

Cons
- Convexity and scaling arguments could be made more explicit.
- Dependence on CVX may hinder large-scale adoption without guidance on specialized implementations.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to SNR and to misspecified µ0 on real data.
- Minor notation issue.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a short convexity proof and explicit complexity accounting.
- Report sensitivity to noise (SNR) on the toy data, and briefly characterize noise levels in the real data.
- Provide robustness results on real data for imperfect µ0 (e.g., perturb national averages), and discuss how to set λi in practice.
- Consider reporting effect sizes in addition to t-tests, or include nonparametric tests given metric distributions.
- Comment on identifiability: SACs help but do not fully resolve source exchangeability; clarify remaining ambiguities.

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed paper with a simple, general idea that yields substantial empirical benefits on an important application. The contributions meet NIPS standards of quality, clarity, originality, and significance. With minor clarifications on convexity, scaling, and noise robustness, I recommend acceptance.
