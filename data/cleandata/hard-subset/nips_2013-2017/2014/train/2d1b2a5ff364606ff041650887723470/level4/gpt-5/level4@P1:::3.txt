Summary
This paper addresses blind source separation (BSS) with an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs) that encourage the total energy of each source over a time horizon to match specified values. The authors formulate AFHMM+SAC, derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) for a relaxed MAP inference problem by introducing transition variables to obtain a PSD quadratic objective, and show an equivalent interpretation under the posterior regularization framework. Experiments on a toy dataset and the HES low-frequency energy data demonstrate improvements in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) compared to an AFHMM and the AFAMAP baseline.

Relation to prior work is well framed: AFAMAP and related constraints on FHMMs (e.g., one-chain-change, semi-Markov durations, non-homogeneous FHMMs, k-segment constraints) aim to improve identifiability via temporal structure, whereas SACs inject prior information on aggregates—complementary to those approaches. The link to posterior regularization and measurement-based learning clarifies how the aggregate priors act as soft constraints.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The relaxation yields a convex quadratic objective with PSD terms (from both the Gaussian likelihood and the quadratic aggregate penalty). The equivalence to posterior regularization is correctly derived. However, the paper should clarify how fractional solutions from the CQP are used in practice: Are Sit/Hit rounded, decoded to discrete states, or are continuous mixtures acceptable because metrics are computed on reconstructed signals? If rounding is applied, please describe the procedure and its effect on objective values and performance.
- Empirical evaluation: Results on HES (a realistic, ultra-low-frequency dataset) are meaningful, and the reported average gains (e.g., 45% NDE improvement over AFHMM; ~29–33% over AFAMAP) are notable. The toy experiment illustrates sensitivity to constraint misspecification. It would strengthen the evaluation to report performance as a function of λ (now fixed to 1) and to include sensitivity analyses for the accuracy of µ0 on real data (e.g., scaling or noise in national totals).
- Computational aspects: Solving a CQP with CVX is practical at the scales shown, and run times are reported as comparable to AFHMM/AFAMAP. Please include more detail on problem sizes (T, I, ∑Ki) and solver tolerances, and discuss scalability beyond the current setting and whether specialized solvers or problem structure (block separability over t) could be exploited.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and mathematically precise. The derivations are clear, and the posterior-regularization connection is helpful. A few issues:
- Figure 1 is hard to interpret: for large deviations between µ0 and the true aggregates, AFAMAP appears to outperform AFHMM+SAC. Please make the axes, scales, and regimes explicit, and discuss this behavior directly in the text.
- Minor typos/notation: “equavalent,” “distibution,” and some overloaded symbols could be cleaned up in the final version.

Originality
Using aggregate constraints as soft priors within an AFHMM and deriving a tractable CQP for inference is a neat and practical contribution. While posterior regularization and related frameworks are known, instantiating them with aggregate constraints for BSS/energy disaggregation and demonstrating effectiveness at low sampling rates is novel and useful.

Significance
The reported gains on realistic data, with statistically significant improvements over AFAMAP, indicate practical impact for NILM. The approach is general and could be applied to other BSS domains where aggregate priors are available.

Main concerns and suggestions
- Fair comparison and use of additional information: AFHMM+SAC leverages extra prior information (NTC/TTC) that AFHMM and AFAMAP do not. This makes the comparison somewhat asymmetric. Could you:
  - Include a baseline that incorporates the same aggregate penalty with AFAMAP or with standard AFHMM (e.g., posterior-regularized AFAMAP), even if solved approximately?
  - Compare to the relaxed AFHMM (λ=0) solved with the same CQP to isolate the benefit of SACs from the benefit of the relaxation/solver?
  - Clarify how NTC is obtained on test days and its typical error relative to ground truth (e.g., distribution of |∑t xit − µi0|)?
- Robustness to misspecification: The toy results suggest that badly chosen constraints can harm performance. Please (i) discuss this explicitly; (ii) show sensitivity curves on real data (vary µ0 by noise factors); and (iii) explore cross-validation or Bayesian variance on aggregate priors to adapt λ per appliance.
- Richer constraints: The paper hints at multiple time-window aggregates. It would be valuable to discuss other practically relevant constraints and whether they can be handled within the same convex framework, for example:
  - Mutual exclusion: Sit,heater + Sit,cooler ≤ 1.
  - Cardinality: at most K appliances active at time t, ∑i 1{Zit ≠ off} ≤ K (or a convex surrogate).
  - Duration constraints: penalties on too-short runs or explicit semi-Markov duration priors.
  - Rate-of-change or interleaving constraints (as in AFAMAP), possibly combined with SACs.
A brief discussion on convexity and tractability of these extensions would strengthen the paper.
- Model assumptions and hyperparameters: All appliances are modeled with Ki=3. Please comment on sensitivity to Ki and to errors in θ estimation. Provide guidance on setting λ (e.g., from uncertainty on µ0) and whether per-appliance λ improves results.

Pros
- Clear motivation and formulation; solid mathematical grounding.
- Practical, convex inference procedure with demonstrated gains on realistic, low-frequency data.
- Useful connection to posterior regularization; broadly applicable idea.

Cons
- Comparisons are somewhat unfair due to the use of extra information; missing baselines that also exploit aggregate priors.
- Limited robustness and sensitivity analysis to mis-specified constraints and to λ.
- Figure 1 presentation and discussion could better reflect cases where SACs hurt performance.
- Extensions to richer, practically motivated constraints are only briefly sketched.

Overall, this is a well-motivated and technically sound paper with promising results. Addressing the fairness of comparisons, robustness to misspecification, and feasibility of richer constraints would substantially strengthen the contribution.
