This paper develops a novel method for blind source separation by formulating the problem as an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) and incorporating signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The motivation stems from the potential to leverage domain knowledge to enhance the separation of time series into constituent components. The authors illustrate this with the application of energy disaggregation, where improved separation of individual appliance energy usage is achieved by incorporating information about the total expected usage of each appliance over time. The objective function, representing the log of the posterior distribution of the hidden chains given the observed data, is transformed into a convex optimization problem. The method's efficacy is demonstrated through experiments using both a synthetic dataset and real-world domestic energy consumption data.

The paper is technically sound and features a strong empirical evaluation. As expected, the proposed method outperforms standard AFHMM and AFAMAP – a recently introduced alternative – which do not utilize SACs. The improvements are statistically significant, as demonstrated by t-tests, and the optimization speed is comparable to that of AFAMAP. I have one question regarding the results section: the authors employ a relatively low noise variance in their synthetic example. Clarification would be beneficial – is this to clearly illustrate the method’s separation capabilities in Figure 2, particularly in contrast to other approaches? Otherwise, the synthetic setup appears somewhat simplistic. Alternatively, are these signal-to-noise ratios representative of those encountered in real-world data? Relatedly, the authors could also comment on the signal-to-noise ratio between the total mains readings and the aggregate appliance output in the energy disaggregation application.

The theoretical aspects of the paper are well presented and structured. A proof demonstrating the convexity of the final procedure in equation (6) would strengthen the paper, as the provided reasoning is not entirely conclusive. Furthermore, including theoretical scaling arguments—specifically with respect to data length and the number of hidden states—would complement the simulation results. Investigating the method’s tractability for larger datasets and state spaces, and contrasting its performance with AFAMAP in such scenarios, would be valuable.

The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and provides a clear overview of related work. While I am not deeply familiar with this specific research area, I believe the literature review is comprehensive. I note a potential minor error on page 4 (line 202), in the unnumbered equation preceding (6), where the commas in the subscripts of *p* appear inconsistent with the notation used on page 3, unless a new convention has been introduced.

To the best of my knowledge, the work is novel and original. However, as a non-expert, I cannot fully assess the significance of these findings to the HMM community or to the field of energy disaggregation.

Addendum: The authors have satisfactorily addressed my questions during the feedback phase, and I encourage them to incorporate similar clarifications into the final version of the paper. This work proposes a method for blind source separation using Additive Factorial HMMs with Signal Aggregate Constraints. The method achieves strong performance compared to state-of-the-art approaches, particularly in the context of a real-world energy disaggregation problem.
