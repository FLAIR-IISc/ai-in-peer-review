The paper proposes an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) augmented with signal-aggregate constraints (SAC) to address blind source separation. The authors formulate a convex quadratic program for a relaxed AFHMM with SAC. The approach demonstrates promising results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

Pros:
- The paper is well written and grounded in solid mathematics.
- The problem motivation is clear and compelling.
- The evaluation shows substantial gains in NDE and SAE.

Areas for improvement:
- The comparisons are primarily against AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP. Both baselines use less information than the proposed AFHMM with SAC, which makes the comparison potentially unfair. Are there alternative approaches that could also exploit SAC (even if computationally less efficient) to provide a fairer benchmark?
- The results in Figure 1 are unclear. It appears that for distances larger than 10^4, AFAMAP outperforms the SAC-enhanced AFHMM. Does this suggest that poorly specified constraints can significantly degrade performance? The discussion seems to imply that constraints always help, which is potentially misleading; please clarify the conditions under which constraints help or hurt.
- I expected the algorithm to incorporate more complex constraints. For example: mutual exclusivity (a person cannot use the heater and cooler simultaneously); cardinality limits (no more than three out of ten bulbs on at once); and duration constraints (appliances like grinders operating for less than 10 minutes). There are many such constraint types one might wish to impose. Can the method be extended to support these broader classes of constraints? The paper is well written and provides strong motivation; future work should extend the model to accommodate additional constraint types.
