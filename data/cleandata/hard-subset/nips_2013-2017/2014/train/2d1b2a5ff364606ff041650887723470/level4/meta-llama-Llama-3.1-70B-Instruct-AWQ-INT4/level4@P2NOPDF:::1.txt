This paper presents a novel approach to blind source separation by formulating the problem as an additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) and incorporating signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The primary motivation behind this approach is to leverage additional domain knowledge to improve the separation of time series data into its constituent components. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this method using the example of energy disaggregation, where incorporating information about the total expected usage of each appliance in a given time period enhances the separation of domestic energy use components.

The paper is technically sound, with a strong simulation section that showcases the method's performance. The results indicate that the proposed method outperforms both regular AFHMM and AFAMAP, a recently developed alternative method that does not incorporate SACs. The improvements are statistically significant, as confirmed by t-tests. Additionally, the authors demonstrate that the optimization speed of their method is comparable to AFAMAP.

One potential area for clarification in the results section is the choice of a low noise variance in the toy example. It is unclear whether this choice was made to facilitate the illustration in Figure 2 or if such signal-to-noise ratios are commonly encountered in real-world data. The authors could provide additional insight into this decision and also comment on the signal-to-noise ratio between the mains readings and the aggregate appliance output in the energy application.

The theoretical aspects of the paper are well-presented and structured. However, a formal proof that the final procedure in equation (6) is convex would strengthen the paper, as the provided reasoning is not entirely clear. Furthermore, including theoretical scaling arguments with respect to data length and the number of hidden states would complement the simulation findings and provide valuable insights into the method's tractability for larger datasets and state spaces. A comparison with AFAMAP in this regard would also be beneficial.

The paper is well-written and structured, with a clear problem motivation in the introduction and a thorough description of relevant literature. However, I am not an expert in this field and cannot definitively state that the literature review is comprehensive. I did notice a potential error on page 4 (line 202), in the unnumbered equation before (6), where the subscripts proceeding the object p appear to be inconsistent with earlier notation on page 3.

To my knowledge, the work presented in this paper is novel and original. While I am not an expert in the HMM community or the energy application studied, the authors' responses to my questions during the feedback phase were satisfactory, and I encourage them to incorporate similar clarifications in the next version of the paper. Overall, this paper proposes a promising method for performing blind source separation using AFHMMs with SACs, which demonstrates excellent performance, particularly with real data from an energy disaggregation problem.
