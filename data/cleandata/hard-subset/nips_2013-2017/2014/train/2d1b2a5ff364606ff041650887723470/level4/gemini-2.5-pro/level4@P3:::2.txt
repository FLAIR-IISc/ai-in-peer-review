This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation (BSS) problems, with a specific application to energy disaggregation. The core idea is that for many BSS tasks, one has prior knowledge about the expected total signal from each unobserved source over a given time period. For example, in energy disaggregation, we can estimate the typical daily energy consumption of an appliance like a refrigerator or a toaster.

The authors incorporate these SACs into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a standard probabilistic model for this type of problem. The resulting model, AFHMM+SAC, aims to find the MAP estimate of the hidden source states, subject to these new aggregate constraints. Because the SACs are non-Markovian (i.e., they depend on the entire state sequence for a source), standard inference methods like the Viterbi algorithm are not directly applicable. To overcome this, the authors formulate a relaxed version of the MAP inference problem as a convex quadratic program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently.

The proposed method is evaluated on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES). The results show that AFHMM+SAC significantly outperforms both the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP, reducing the normalized disaggregation error by 45% and 29% respectively on one of the experimental setups.

This work builds upon a line of research that seeks to improve the identifiability of factorial models by adding constraints. It is well-positioned relative to prior work, such as Kolter & Jaakkola (2012) who introduced temporal sparsity constraints (AFAMAP), and other works using semi-Markov models or non-homogeneous HMMs. The key distinction is the nature of the constraint: SACs are global constraints on the sum of a source's signal over time, rather than local constraints on state transitions. The paper also provides a nice theoretical connection by showing that their penalized objective function can be derived from the posterior regularization framework of Ganchev et al. (2010).

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the problem and the derivation of the CQP for approximate inference are clear and correct. The claims of improved performance are well-supported by a thorough experimental evaluation on a large, real-world dataset. The authors are careful to test their method in two realistic scenarios: one with a synthesized aggregate signal and another using actual mains readings, which includes unmodeled sources. The inclusion of an "oracle" experiment using the true total consumption (TTC) is a valuable addition, as it demonstrates the upper bound on performance improvement achievable with this type of constraint.

**Clarity:** While the technical content is strong, the paper's clarity could be significantly improved. The primary issue is a proliferation of acronyms (AFHMM, SAC, AFAMAP, CQP, NDE, SAE, NTC, TTC), which makes the text dense and difficult to follow, particularly in the results section. A second point of confusion is the framing of the constraints. The introduction suggests the goal is to incorporate domain knowledge, which is naturally framed as a prior belief. Section 4.2 presents this as a penalty term in the MAP objective function. Section 6 then relates this penalty to the posterior regularization framework. The motivation could be presented more directly: the SACs represent prior knowledge about the sources, which can be incorporated into the MAP objective as a log-prior term. Framing this as a simple, well-motivated prior from the outset would make the model's justification more intuitive and direct.

**Originality:** The primary contribution—the concept of signal aggregate constraints for BSS—is novel and well-motivated. While adding constraints to AFHMMs is not a new idea, this specific form of global, non-Markovian constraint is a new and valuable contribution to the field. It provides a practical and intuitive way to inject domain knowledge that is often readily available but difficult to incorporate into standard models.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for the energy disaggregation community. A 29% improvement over a recent state-of-the-art method is a substantial advance. More broadly, the idea of SACs is generalizable and could be impactful in other BSS domains where aggregate source statistics are known or can be estimated (e.g., separating instrument tracks in audio, or cell populations in bioinformatics). The fact that the proposed method is computationally efficient and scales similarly to existing approaches increases its potential for practical adoption.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Intuitive Contribution:** The paper introduces a new and practical type of constraint (SAC) for improving identifiability in BSS.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates a significant and robust performance improvement over existing state-of-the-art on a challenging, real-world application.
3.  **Tractable Inference:** The authors provide an efficient approximate inference algorithm by formulating the problem as a convex quadratic program.
4.  **Generalizability:** The core idea has the potential for broad impact beyond the specific application of energy disaggregation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Clarity Issues:** The paper's readability is hampered by an over-reliance on acronyms and a slightly convoluted motivation for the constraints. These are primarily presentational issues but detract from the paper's overall quality.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper with a novel idea, a sound technical approach, and impressive results. The weaknesses are primarily related to presentation and could be addressed in a final version. The work represents a significant contribution to the field of energy disaggregation and offers a potentially valuable tool for a wider range of blind source separation problems.
