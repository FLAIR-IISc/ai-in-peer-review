## Review of Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models with Signal Aggregate Constraints for Blind Source Separation

This paper introduces Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models with Signal Aggregate Constraints (AFHMM-SAC) as a novel approach to the blind source separation problem. The authors formulate a computationally tractable, convex quadratic relaxation of the AFHMM-SAC model, and demonstrate promising results on both synthetic and real-world datasets. Overall, this is a well-written paper with a solid mathematical foundation and clear motivation.

**Strengths:**

The paper is clearly written and presents a technically sound contribution to the field of blind source separation. The motivation for incorporating signal aggregate constraints is well articulated – leveraging prior knowledge about the relationships between sources to improve separation performance. The mathematical formulation is rigorous, and the proposed convex relaxation offers a practical pathway to solving the potentially intractable AFHMM-SAC model. The experimental results, demonstrating improvements in Normalized Distance Error (NDE) and Source Amplitudes Error (SAE), are encouraging. 

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

While the presented results are positive, the comparison to existing methods raises some concerns regarding fairness. The evaluation compares AFHMM-SAC against AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP. Notably, AFHMM-SAC utilizes significantly more data during training than these baselines. This disparity could artificially inflate the observed performance gain. It would be beneficial to discuss this limitation and ideally, compare against alternative methods that *also* leverage signal aggregate constraints, even if they are computationally less efficient. Investigating such alternatives would strengthen the claim that the proposed method’s efficiency is a key advantage.

The visualization of results in Figure 1 is not entirely clear. The figure appears to indicate that AFAMAP outperforms AFHMM-SAC when the distance parameter exceeds 10<sup>4</sup>. If true, this suggests that poorly chosen constraints can, in certain scenarios, lead to degraded performance. The current discussion implies consistently superior results from the proposed method, which could be misleading. A more nuanced discussion acknowledging potential limitations and conditions under which AFHMM-SAC might not outperform other approaches is necessary. Specifically, the paper should analyze the conditions that lead to the observed performance drop at higher distances and explore the impact of different constraint formulations.

The current implementation focuses on relatively simple aggregate constraints (e.g., total power consumption). While effective, the paper would benefit from a discussion of the potential for incorporating more complex and realistic constraints. For instance, the authors could consider constraints that model mutual exclusivity (e.g., a person cannot simultaneously use a heater and air conditioner) or resource limitations (e.g., a maximum number of active light bulbs). Temporal constraints, such as limiting the operation time of certain appliances (e.g., a grinder running for less than 10 minutes), could also be explored. While the authors mention extending the model to other constraints in the future work section, discussing the *challenges* and potential approaches to accommodate such constraints would significantly strengthen the paper.  Would the current convex relaxation still be applicable? Would a different optimization strategy be required?

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation. The AFHMM-SAC model provides a principled framework for incorporating prior knowledge about source relationships, and the convex relaxation offers a practical solution. However, the issues raised regarding the fairness of comparisons and the clarity of Figure 1 need to be addressed. Furthermore, a more thorough discussion of the potential for extending the model to more complex constraint types would enhance the paper’s impact.

I recommend accepting this paper, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns raised above. Specifically:

*   **Clarify the comparison:** Provide a more equitable comparison to existing methods, ideally including alternatives that also utilize signal aggregate constraints.
*   **Revisit Figure 1:**  Provide a detailed explanation of the observed performance drop at higher distances and discuss the implications for constraint selection.
*   **Expand the discussion of constraints:**  Elaborate on the challenges and potential strategies for incorporating more complex constraint types into the model.
