This paper addresses the problem of identifiability in blind source separation (BSS), a critical challenge in applications like energy disaggregation where the goal is to recover meaningful, interpretable source signals. The authors introduce a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge called Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The core idea is that for many BSS problems, one has prior knowledge about the expected total value of each source signal over a period. SACs are integrated into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a standard model for this task. As these global constraints break the Markov property, making exact inference intractable, the authors formulate MAP inference as a penalized optimization problem and derive a convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation that can be solved efficiently. The proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on a large-scale, real-world energy disaggregation dataset, demonstrating significant performance improvements over the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Contribution:** The paper effectively identifies a key weakness in BSS—the identifiability problem—and proposes an intuitive and practical solution. The "franken-appliance" example clearly illustrates the need for constraints that preserve the identity of sources. The concept of using signal aggregate values as a form of domain knowledge is novel, generalizable to other BSS domains, and highly relevant to the energy disaggregation task.

2.  **Technically Sound Formulation:** The mathematical formulation is clear and rigorous. The authors provide a principled way to incorporate SACs into the AFHMM objective function. The derivation of the convex quadratic program for approximate inference is a solid technical contribution, providing a computationally feasible way to solve an otherwise intractable inference problem. The connection drawn to the posterior regularization framework further strengthens the theoretical grounding of the approach.

3.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results are convincing. The authors demonstrate the value of their approach on both synthetic data and a challenging, ultra-low-frequency real-world dataset (HES). The performance gains are substantial: on the most realistic dataset with mains readings, AFHMM+SAC reduces the normalized disaggregation error by 45% compared to AFHMM and 29% compared to the state-of-the-art AFAMAP. The analysis includes an oracle experiment (using True Total Consumption) which effectively bounds the potential benefit of the proposed constraints, and a practical experiment (using National Total Consumption) that shows strong results with realistically obtainable prior knowledge.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Comparison and the Value of Prior Information:** The performance gains over AFHMM and AFAMAP are impressive, but it is important to acknowledge that AFHMM+SAC has access to additional prior information (the aggregate values `µi0`) that the baselines do not. While the paper's main contribution is precisely *how* to use this information, the comparison could be further contextualized. Could the authors discuss other potential ways this aggregate information could be incorporated into baseline models? For instance, could it be used to initialize the HMM parameters or as a post-processing step? A brief discussion on this would help clarify whether the performance gain is solely from the availability of new information or also from the specific CQP formulation proposed.

2.  **Sensitivity to Constraint Accuracy:** The toy data experiment in Section 7.1 is a valuable analysis of the model's robustness to inaccurate constraint values. As Figure 1 shows, performance degrades as the specified aggregate `µ0` deviates from the true value. This is a critical point that warrants more discussion. The paper would be strengthened by explicitly discussing the practical implications: How sensitive is the model to these priors? At what point of error in the prior does the benefit of the SAC diminish entirely, or potentially even harm performance compared to an unconstrained model? A discussion on how a user might assess the quality of available prior information or manage uncertainty in `µi0` would be very useful.

3.  **Scope of Constraints:** The paper focuses exclusively on signal aggregate constraints. While this is a powerful and well-developed idea, the authors mention in Section 4.2 that other constraints are possible. This seems like a missed opportunity to discuss the broader potential of the framework. Many other realistic constraints exist in the energy domain, such as mutual exclusivity (e.g., a heater and air conditioner are not used simultaneously), temporal constraints (e.g., an oven is on for at least 10 minutes but rarely more than 2 hours), or cardinality constraints (e.g., at most 3 of 10 lights are on at once). A discussion on the feasibility of incorporating such logical or structural constraints into the CQP framework would significantly enhance the paper's impact and provide a clearer roadmap for future work.

### Summary

Overall, this is a well-written and technically solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation and its application to energy disaggregation. The introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints is a novel and effective way to tackle the persistent identifiability problem. The proposed CQP-based inference method is sound and is shown to yield significant performance improvements on a challenging, real-world task. While the review raises some points regarding the framing of comparisons and the potential for extending the constraint framework, these are primarily suggestions for strengthening the discussion. The core contributions of the paper are clear, significant, and well-supported.
