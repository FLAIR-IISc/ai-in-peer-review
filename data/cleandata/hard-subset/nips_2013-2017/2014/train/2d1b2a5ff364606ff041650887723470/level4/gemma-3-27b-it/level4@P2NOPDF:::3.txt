This paper introduces an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model with Signal Aggregate Constraints (AFHMM-SAC) as a novel approach to the blind source separation problem. The authors formulate a convex quadratic program for a relaxed version of the AFHMM-SAC model and demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**

The paper is well-written and presents a strong mathematical foundation for the proposed method. The motivation for addressing the blind source separation problem is clearly articulated. The experimental evaluation demonstrates a notable improvement in performance, as measured by Normalized Distance Error (NDE) and Source-wise Absolute Error (SAE).

**Weaknesses and Questions:**

While the results are promising, the comparisons presented raise some concerns regarding fairness. The AFHMM-SAC method utilizes more data than the baselines, AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP. This difference in data usage could contribute to the observed performance gains, and it's unclear whether the improvement is solely attributable to the signal aggregate constraints. It would be beneficial to understand if other approaches could incorporate SAC, and if so, how their computational efficiency compares to AFHMM-SAC.

Furthermore, the results depicted in Figure 1 require clarification. The figure appears to suggest that AFAMAP outperforms AFHMM-SAC when the distance exceeds 10<sup>4</sup>. If this is accurate, it highlights a potential drawback: poorly chosen constraints could lead to significantly degraded performance. The current discussion implies consistently superior results for AFHMM-SAC, which seems potentially misleading given this observation. A more nuanced discussion acknowledging the potential for negative impacts from suboptimal constraints is warranted.

The paper focuses on relatively simple aggregate constraints. While effective, it would be valuable to consider the potential for incorporating more complex and realistic constraints. For example, constraints could model mutually exclusive events (e.g., a person cannot simultaneously use both a heater and an air conditioner) or resource limitations (e.g., a maximum number of active light bulbs, or a time limit on appliance usage). Exploring the algorithm’s extensibility to handle such diverse constraint types would significantly broaden its applicability. Could the proposed framework be generalized to accommodate different constraint structures, and if so, what are the associated computational challenges?

**Overall:**

This is a well-written and motivated paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation. The proposed AFHMM-SAC model offers a promising approach, and the experimental results demonstrate its potential. However, addressing the concerns regarding the fairness of comparisons and the impact of poorly specified constraints, as well as exploring the potential for handling more complex constraint types, would further strengthen the paper. The authors acknowledge the extension to other constraints as future work, and this direction appears particularly promising.
