Summary
This paper addresses blind source separation (BSS) in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs). SACs encode domain knowledge about the total activity of each latent source over a time horizon (e.g., the daily energy usage of an appliance), encouraging each source’s aggregate signal to match a specified target. The authors show how to incorporate SACs into AFHMMs and derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) for approximate MAP inference by relaxing the integer constraints and introducing auxiliary transition variables. They further relate the objective to posterior regularization. Experiments on synthetic data and a large real-world dataset for energy disaggregation (HES) demonstrate substantial gains in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) over a standard AFHMM and the AFAMAP method, with similar runtime.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on AFHMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan) and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola). It connects the SAC objective to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.) and contrasts with duration-based constraints (semi-Markov), interleaving constraints (at most one chain changes at a time), and k-segment constraints. The contribution is to formalize aggregate constraints that are simple, broadly available in practice, and computationally tractable via a convex relaxation.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: identifiability is a core challenge in BSS and energy disaggregation; aggregate priors are realistic and informative.
- Solid technical development: a principled CQP formulation with PSD structure; clean connection to posterior regularization.
- Empirical results: consistent improvements in NDE and SAE on synthetic and realistic low-frequency HES data; comparisons to established baselines; similar computational cost reported.
- Robustness study: toy experiments illustrate how performance varies with misspecification of aggregate targets.

Weaknesses and questions
- Fairness of comparisons: AFHMM+SAC uses additional side information (aggregate targets), whereas AFHMM and AFAMAP do not. While this is inherent to the contribution, it would help to compare against methods that also exploit the same SACs, e.g., (a) AFAMAP augmented with a SAC penalty (even if solved approximately), (b) a posterior-regularized AFHMM baseline, or (c) duration/semi-Markov or k-segment approaches enriched with aggregate priors. This would more cleanly attribute gains to the constraint family rather than the solver.
- Sensitivity to mis-specified constraints: Figure 1 suggests that when the aggregate targets are far from the truth, AFHMM+SAC can underperform AFAMAP. Please quantify the regime where SACs start to hurt (e.g., breakpoints versus Euclidean error in µ0), and clarify whether “results are always better” is intended only when µ0 is reasonably accurate. Practical guidance for setting λ (and its relation to misspecification) would be valuable.
- Use of oracle constraints: Results with TTC are informative bounds, but the paper should emphasize that they represent an oracle scenario. For NTC, clarify precisely how µ0 is estimated (per-house vs. cross-house, per-appliance variance), and whether there is any risk of leakage from test to train.
- Convex relaxation details: How are fractional solutions rounded to valid state sequences, and what is the integrality gap in practice? Are there cases where the relaxed solution violates the Markov structure materially after rounding? Some statistics on solution integrality would bolster confidence.
- Scalability: CVX is convenient but may not scale to longer time series or many appliances. Can the authors comment on specialized solvers, decomposition (e.g., ADMM), or exploiting banded structure for improved scalability? Empirical runtime versus T and total states would strengthen the claims.
- Richer constraints: The paper briefly mentions time-windowed SACs; many other realistic constraints are important in NILM: mutual exclusivity (heater vs. cooler), cardinality constraints (no more than K bulbs on), minimum/maximum duration, or limits on simultaneous state changes. Which of these can be expressed as linear/quadratic constraints compatible with the CQP (e.g., linear sums over Sit), and which would break convexity? A short discussion and perhaps a small example would enhance the paper’s generality.

Clarity and presentation
- Improve Figure 1: clarify axes, units, and whether AFAMAP outperforms AFHMM+SAC at large target errors. Include error bars if applicable.
- Provide more detail on training: number of states per appliance, how µi and transition matrices are learned, and how λi were chosen (the text says λi = 1; discuss tuning or robustness).
- Reproducibility: specify CVX settings, stopping criteria, and any rounding/post-processing steps. Public code would be valuable, if possible.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a central identifiability issue with a simple, practical form of prior knowledge.
- Technically principled formulation with a convex relaxation and connection to posterior regularization.
- Strong empirical gains on realistic datasets with similar computational cost.

Cons for acceptance
- Comparisons do not fully isolate the effect of SACs versus extra information; missing baselines that also exploit the same constraints.
- Limited analysis of failure modes under constraint misspecification and limited guidance on hyperparameter selection.
- Discussion of richer, practically relevant constraints is brief and could be more concrete.

Overall
A well-written and technically sound paper with a compelling idea—using aggregate priors—to improve BSS in AFHMMs. The results are promising and practically relevant. Strengthening the fairness of comparisons, the sensitivity analysis, and the discussion of extensible constraints would further improve the contribution. Future work extending the framework to richer linear/quadratic constraints and more scalable solvers would be valuable.
