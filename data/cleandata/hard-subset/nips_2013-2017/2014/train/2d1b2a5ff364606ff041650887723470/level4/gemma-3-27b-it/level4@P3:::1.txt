This paper develops a novel method for blind source separation by formulating the problem as an additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) and incorporating signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The core idea is to leverage domain knowledge – specifically, expectations about the total signal from unknown sources – to improve separation performance. The application motivating this work is energy disaggregation, where the goal is to decompose total household energy usage into individual appliance consumption. The authors formulate an optimization problem for maximizing the posterior distribution of hidden states, and demonstrate its equivalence to a convex program, enabling efficient solution. The method’s efficacy is demonstrated through experiments with both simulated and real-world energy data, showing significant improvements over baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach (AFAMAP).

The paper is technically sound and well-motivated. The proposed SACs represent a sensible and practical way to incorporate prior information into the BSS problem, addressing the inherent identifiability issues. The experimental results are compelling, showing statistically significant improvements on the energy disaggregation task. The comparison with AFAMAP is particularly valuable, demonstrating a clear advantage for the proposed method. The comparable computational cost is also a positive aspect. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations and potential challenges, such as the reliance on accurate prior estimates for the aggregate signal values.

Several strengths stand out. First, the formulation as a convex program is a significant contribution, allowing for efficient inference. Second, the clear connection to posterior regularization provides a theoretical grounding for the proposed method. Third, the empirical evaluation on a real-world dataset (HES) strengthens the practical relevance of the work. The inclusion of both a toy example and a realistic energy disaggregation experiment provides a balanced assessment.

However, there are areas for improvement. The justification for the low noise variance in the toy example is not entirely convincing; clarifying this choice and discussing the robustness to noise levels would be beneficial. While the authors mention the signal-to-noise ratio in the energy application, further discussion of its impact on performance, and potential methods for handling noisy data, would be valuable. Furthermore, a formal proof of the convexity of the optimization problem in equation (6) would enhance the paper’s theoretical rigor.  Discussing the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method, particularly in relation to the number of time steps and hidden states, is also important. Specifically, how does the computational cost scale with increasing data size and model complexity compared to AFAMAP? This discussion could benefit from theoretical scaling arguments complementing the empirical results. A minor point: on page 4, line 202, the comma in the subscripts of *p* appears inconsistent with earlier notation.

Regarding originality, the paper presents a novel combination of techniques – specifically, integrating signal aggregate constraints within an AFHMM framework and formulating the inference problem as a convex program. While AFHMMs are established, the SAC approach and the resulting optimization formulation appear to be new. The thorough comparison with related work, including posterior regularization and other constraint-based methods, suggests a clear differentiation from existing contributions. 

The significance of this work lies in its potential to improve the accuracy and reliability of blind source separation techniques, particularly in applications where domain knowledge is available. The demonstrated success in energy disaggregation suggests a practical impact on energy conservation and user understanding of energy consumption patterns. The general applicability of SACs to other BSS problems, as hinted at by the authors, further enhances the significance of this contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-motivated approach to BSS.
*   Significant improvements in performance on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset.
*   Novel combination of techniques and a clear theoretical grounding.
*   Efficient convex program formulation for tractable inference.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Justification for the toy example's noise variance is weak.
*   Missing formal proof of convexity.
*   Lack of detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability.
*   Limited analysis of the impact of signal-to-noise ratio.



Overall, this is a strong paper with clear contributions to the field. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further enhance its quality and impact. I recommend acceptance.
