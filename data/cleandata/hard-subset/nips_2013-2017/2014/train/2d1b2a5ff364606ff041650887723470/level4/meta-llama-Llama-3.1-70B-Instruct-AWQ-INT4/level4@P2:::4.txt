This paper proposes an extension to the additive factorial HMM model, incorporating signal aggregate constraints (SACs) to require that the sum of individual chain outputs be close to some reasonable (known) aggregate value. The authors develop a convex relaxation to MAP inference and apply the algorithm to real-world data sets involving energy disaggregation. The ideas presented here are fairly straightforward, and the authors use a relatively simple optimization formulation to solve these problems. However, the work is still compelling, as it combines both applied and algorithmic elements and demonstrates promising results on large real-world data sets.

The paper provides a clear and thorough introduction to the problem of blind source separation and the challenges associated with it, particularly in the context of energy disaggregation. The authors effectively motivate the need for incorporating domain knowledge into the model and introduce SACs as a means to address this issue. The proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, is well-explained, and the optimization formulation is clearly presented.

The experimental results on both toy data and real-world energy disaggregation data sets are impressive, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach in improving the performance of the original AFHMM. The comparison with the state-of-the-art AFAMAP algorithm is also informative, highlighting the benefits of incorporating SACs.

Some minor suggestions for improvement:

1.  The authors could provide more discussion on the relationship between the proposed SACAFHMM optimization formulation and the AFAMAP algorithm. Specifically, they could elaborate on whether the SACAFHMM formulation also accomplishes the goal of achieving integer solutions more often, as mentioned in the context of AFAMAP.
2.  The authors could consider combining the SACAFHMM constraints with the optimization formulation for AFAMAP, as suggested in the review. This could potentially lead to even better performance and would be an interesting direction for future work.
3.  The authors could be more precise in their terminology, distinguishing between models and optimization procedures. For instance, they could clarify that by AFHMM, they mean alternating Viterbi optimization, rather than the model itself.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the results are compelling. With some minor revisions to address the above points, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
