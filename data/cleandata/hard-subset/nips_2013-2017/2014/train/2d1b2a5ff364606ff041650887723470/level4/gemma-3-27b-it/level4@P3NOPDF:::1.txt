This paper introduces a novel method for blind source separation, formulating the problem within an additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework and incorporating Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The core motivation is to leverage domain knowledge – specifically, expectations about component-wise aggregate behavior – to improve the separation of time series into constituent parts. The authors illustrate this approach with energy disaggregation, where separating the energy consumption of individual appliances benefits from knowledge of their expected total usage. The resulting objective function, representing the log posterior distribution of hidden component states given the observed data, is skillfully transformed into a convex optimization problem. The method’s efficacy is demonstrated through experiments using both synthetic and real-world domestic energy consumption data.

The technical presentation is strong and supported by a comprehensive simulation section. As anticipated, the proposed method consistently outperforms standard AFHMM and the recent AFAMAP algorithm – neither of which utilize SACs – with the improvements statistically validated using t-tests. Notably, the optimization speed is shown to be comparable to AFAMAP. A minor point regarding the results concerns the choice of a relatively low noise variance in the toy example. Clarification would be beneficial; is this low variance intended to highlight the method’s separation capabilities, and if so, is it representative of typical signal-to-noise ratios encountered in real-world data? Furthermore, discussion of the signal-to-noise ratio between the total energy readings and the aggregate appliance outputs in the energy disaggregation application would strengthen the practical interpretation of the results.

The theoretical foundations of the paper are well-presented and logically structured. However, a formal proof demonstrating the convexity of the procedure detailed in equation (6) would enhance clarity, as the current reasoning is not entirely conclusive. In addition, providing theoretical scaling arguments – specifically, with respect to data length and the number of hidden states – would complement the empirical findings presented in the simulations. Understanding the method’s tractability when applied to substantially larger datasets and state spaces, and contrasting its scalability with that of AFAMAP, would be highly valuable.

The paper is well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and provides a clear overview of relevant literature, though my limited familiarity with the field prevents a definitive assessment of completeness. I noted a potential minor inconsistency on page 4 (line 202), in the unnumbered equation preceding (6), where the commas in the subscripts of ‘p’ appear unnecessary, given the notation established on page 3. 

To the best of my knowledge, the work represents a novel and original contribution. However, I am not sufficiently expert to fully evaluate the significance of these results within the broader HMM community or within the energy disaggregation domain.

**Addendum:** The authors have satisfactorily addressed my questions during the feedback phase, and I encourage them to incorporate similar clarifications into the revised manuscript. This paper proposes a robust method for blind source separation using Additive Factorial HMMs enhanced with Signal Aggregate Constraints. The method demonstrates strong performance, particularly when applied to real-world energy disaggregation problems, outperforming state-of-the-art alternatives.
