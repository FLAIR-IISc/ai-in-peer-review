This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) as a way to incorporate domain knowledge into blind source separation problems, specifically within the context of additive factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs) applied to energy disaggregation. The core idea is to encourage the total signal for each unknown source to be close to a pre-specified value, leveraging the observation that these aggregate values are often known or estimable. The authors formulate a convex quadratic program for approximate inference and demonstrate significant improvements over the original AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset.

This work builds on the established field of blind source separation (BSS) [3, 17, 2] and extends the AFHMM framework commonly used for this purpose [17]. Recent approaches to improving AFHMMs, such as AFAMAP [12] and non-homogeneous HMMs [21], focus on constraining state transitions. The authors’ contribution lies in introducing a different type of prior – aggregate signal constraints – and demonstrating its effectiveness. This is also related to broader efforts in incorporating prior knowledge into probabilistic models via techniques like posterior regularization [4] and measurement-based learning [13], although the specific constraint considered here hasn’t been extensively explored.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The introduction of signal aggregate constraints is a sensible and potentially widely applicable method for incorporating domain knowledge into BSS problems.
*   **Practicality:** The formulation as a convex quadratic program allows for efficient computation, making the method scalable to real-world datasets.
*   **Empirical Results:** The significant performance improvements on the energy disaggregation dataset, both compared to the base AFHMM and a state-of-the-art method [12], provide strong evidence for the efficacy of the proposed approach. Applying it to large-scale, low-frequency data from the HES survey is a significant achievement.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors demonstrate strong empirical results, a deeper theoretical analysis of the properties of SACs and their impact on the identifiability of the model would be beneficial.
*   **Orthogonality of Approaches:** The authors acknowledge, but do not fully explore, the potential for combining SACs with other techniques like AFAMAP. Investigating this combination could potentially lead to even better performance.
*   **Conflation of Models and Algorithms:** The paper occasionally blurs the distinction between the AFHMM model itself and the algorithms used to perform inference (e.g., Viterbi vs. AFAMAP). Clarifying this distinction would improve the clarity of the presentation.
*   **Discussion of Solution Integrality:** It would be helpful to discuss whether the convex relaxation leads to solutions with non-integral states and, if so, whether this affects the interpretation or performance of the method. Comparing the solutions against the constraints would provide more clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a challenging problem in a practical domain with a novel and well-motivated approach.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods.
*   The method is computationally efficient and scalable.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis is limited.
*   The potential for combining SACs with other techniques is not fully explored.
*   The occasional conflation of models and algorithms could lead to confusion.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation and energy disaggregation. The proposed signal aggregate constraints offer a practical and effective way to incorporate domain knowledge into the modeling process, and the empirical results are convincing. While the theoretical analysis could be strengthened and the potential for combining SACs with other methods deserves further investigation, the paper is of sufficient quality for acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper, with minor revisions to address the points raised above. Specifically, clarifying the relationship between the different inference algorithms (Viterbi, AFAMAP) and the underlying AFHMM model, and elaborating on the impact of the convex relaxation on solution integrality would significantly improve the paper.
