

The paper introduces an extension to the additive factorial HMM model by incorporating constraints that enforce the sum of individual chain outputs to approximate a known aggregate value. The authors develop a convex relaxation for MAP inference and demonstrate the approach on real-world energy disaggregation datasets: one involving 100 UK homes with low-frequency readings, and another with 10 homes featuring higher-frequency readings (without per-appliance monitoring). While the core ideas are straightforward and the optimization formulation is relatively simple, the work remains compelling due to its application to large-scale real-world datasets. As a paper bridging applied and algorithmic contributions, I consider it a success. The following points could enhance the paper:  
The motivation for AFAMAP to enforce sequential chain state transitions appears to improve relaxation quality toward integer solutions. Does the SACAFHMM formulation similarly achieve this, or do its solutions maintain lower error despite non-integral values? Clarifying this would strengthen the distinction between methods.  
Fundamentally, AFAMAP and SACAFHMM concepts appear orthogonal; SACAFHMM constraints could readily be integrated into AFAMAP's optimization. I strongly encourage authors to explore this combination in a final revision.  
The authors conflate models and optimization procedures at times—for instance, comparing "AFHMM" versus "AFAMAP" is imprecise. AFAMAP is an algorithm for approximating MAP solutions to AFHMM (where AFHMM likely refers to alternating Viterbi optimization), not a distinct model. This confusion obscures the potential for combining these strategies, which could yield superior performance. The paper presents a clear extension and optimization for a variant of AFHMM, and its real-world results are compelling, making the overall contribution reasonable.
