The paper proposes an extension to the additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) that adds constraints requiring the sum of individual chain outputs to be close to a known aggregate. The authors develop a convex relaxation of MAP inference and apply the method to real-world energy disaggregation: one dataset of 100 UK homes with low-frequency readings, and another of 10 homes with higher-frequency readings (without per-appliance submetering).

The ideas are fairly straightforward, and the optimization formulation is simple, but the work is still compelling. The authors evaluate on relatively large, real datasets, and as a paper that blends applied and algorithmic elements, I view it as a success. The comments below could strengthen the paper:

- A motivation for AFAMAP’s constraint that at most one chain changes state at a time is that it can tighten the relaxation and yield integral solutions more often. Does the SACAFHMM optimization achieve a similar effect, or do its solutions remain fractional while still yielding lower error? A discussion of this would clarify the distinctions between the approaches.

- Conceptually, AFAMAP and SACAFHMM seem orthogonal: one could incorporate the SACAFHMM constraints directly into AFAMAP’s optimization formulation. While I do not expect a full exploration in the rebuttal, I strongly encourage the authors to try this for the final version.

- The paper occasionally conflates models and optimization procedures. For example, comparing “AFHMM” to “AFAMAP” is imprecise: AFAMAP is an algorithm for (approximately) computing MAP solutions to the AFHMM, whereas by “AFHMM” here the authors appear to mean alternating Viterbi optimization. Is that correct? This also relates to the point above: the strategies behind the different algorithms could likely be combined and might outperform the current standalone approaches.

Overall, the paper proposes a simple extension and a corresponding optimization for a variant of the AFHMM. The empirical results on real-world data are compelling, and the paper is, on balance, reasonable.
