The paper proposes an extension to the additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) model by incorporating additional constraints that require the sum of individual chain outputs to be close to a reasonable, known aggregate value. This extension, referred to as the Sum-Constrained Additive Factorial HMM (SACAFHMM), is solved using a convex relaxation for maximum a posteriori (MAP) inference. The authors apply this algorithm to real-world datasets involving energy disaggregation, specifically analyzing data from 100 UK homes with low-frequency readings and 10 homes with higher-frequency readings (albeit without individual appliance monitoring).

Although the ideas presented in the paper are relatively straightforward, and the authors employ a simple optimization formulation to address these problems, the work remains compelling due to its application to relatively large real-world datasets. As a paper that effectively combines both applied and algorithmic elements, it can be considered a success.

To further improve the paper, several points warrant discussion. Firstly, the authors motivate the use of AFAMAP by highlighting its ability to enforce the constraint that one chain stages state at a time, thereby improving the relaxation to achieve integer solutions more frequently. It would be beneficial to discuss whether the SACAFHMM optimization formulation achieves a similar outcome or if the resulting solutions still exhibit lower error despite having non-integral solutions. Elucidating these points would provide valuable insight into the distinctions between these approaches.

Furthermore, it appears that the AFAMAP and SACAFHMM ideas are orthogonal, suggesting that the SACAFHMM constraints could be easily incorporated into the AFAMAP optimization formulation. Although not expected in the author response, the authors are strongly encouraged to explore this combination in a future version, as it may yield improved performance.

The authors occasionally conflate models and optimization procedures. For instance, comparing the results of "AFHMM" versus "AFAMAP" lacks precision, as AFAMAP is an algorithm for approximately computing MAP solutions to the AFHMM. It is believed that the authors actually refer to alternating Viterbi optimization when mentioning AFHMM. Clarification on this point would be helpful, as it highlights the potential for combining the strategies of different algorithms presented in the paper, which may lead to better performance than the current approaches.

In conclusion, the paper proposes a simple yet effective extension and optimization to solve a variant of the AFHMM. While the ideas presented are not overly complex, the results on real-world data are compelling, and the paper is generally well-written.
