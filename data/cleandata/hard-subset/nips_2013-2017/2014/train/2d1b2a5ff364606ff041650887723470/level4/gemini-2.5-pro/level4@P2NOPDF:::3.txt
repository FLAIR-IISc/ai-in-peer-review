This paper introduces the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model with Signal Aggregate Constraints (SAC-AFHMM) for the blind source separation problem, specifically in the context of energy disaggregation. The authors formulate the inference problem for a relaxed version of the model as a convex quadratic program. The proposed method demonstrates promising performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper is clearly written and builds on a solid mathematical foundation. The derivation of the convex quadratic programming formulation is well-presented.
*   The problem is well-motivated. The use of an easily obtainable aggregate signal as a constraint is a practical and clever approach to improving the performance of energy disaggregation.
*   The empirical evaluation shows significant improvements over baseline methods, as measured by the Normalized Disaggregation Error (NDE) and Signal Aggregate Error (SAE) metrics.

**Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparison:** The primary baselines for comparison are AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP. However, the proposed SAC-AFHMM leverages the aggregate signal, which is additional information not available to these baselines. This raises a concern about the fairness of the comparison, as the performance gains might stem from access to more data rather than the superiority of the model architecture alone. It would strengthen the evaluation to compare against other methods that can also incorporate such aggregate constraints, even if they are computationally less efficient. This would help isolate the contribution of the proposed optimization framework from the benefit of simply using more information.

2.  **Clarity of Results and Discussion of Failure Modes:** The results presented in Figure 1 are somewhat ambiguous. It appears that for distances greater than 10^4, the AFAMAP baseline outperforms the proposed SAC-AFHMM. This suggests that the introduction of constraints, particularly if they are inaccurate or poorly suited to the data, could potentially degrade performance. This possibility is not fully addressed in the discussion, which generally implies that the proposed method consistently improves upon the baselines. A more nuanced discussion of this specific result and the potential failure modes of the SAC framework would be beneficial for the reader.

3.  **Generality and Extensibility to More Complex Constraints:** While the paper effectively demonstrates the utility of signal aggregate constraints, the framework's potential could be further explored by considering more complex and varied types of real-world constraints. For instance, many scenarios involve:
    *   **Mutual exclusion constraints:** (e.g., a heater and an air conditioner cannot be active simultaneously).
    *   **Cardinality constraints:** (e.g., no more than 3 out of 10 specific lights are on at once).
    *   **Temporal constraints:** (e.g., an appliance like a coffee grinder operates for a short, continuous duration).

    A discussion on whether the proposed convex quadratic programming formulation can be extended to incorporate these richer, non-additive constraints would significantly enhance the paper's contribution. If the current framework cannot accommodate them, an analysis of the theoretical or computational barriers to doing so would be valuable and would provide a clearer roadmap for future work.
