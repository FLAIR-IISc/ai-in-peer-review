This paper addresses the challenge of unidentifiability in blind source separation (BSS), specifically within the context of energy disaggregation. The authors introduce a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge called Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The core idea is that in many BSS problems, one has prior knowledge about the expected total aggregate value of each hidden source signal over a period of time. These constraints are integrated into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a model commonly used for this task. As the SACs are global over the time series and break the Markov property, exact MAP inference becomes intractable. To overcome this, the authors formulate a relaxed version of the inference problem as a convex quadratic program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. This work relates to previous efforts to constrain AFHMMs, such as AFAMAP [12], but proposes a different and complementary type of constraint. The proposed AFHMM+SAC method is evaluated on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, where it is shown to substantially outperform both the standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP algorithm.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The central idea of using signal aggregate constraints is intuitive, novel, and addresses a fundamental problem in BSS models: the non-identifiability of the separated sources. It provides a general and practical mechanism for injecting domain knowledge into these models. The application to energy disaggregation is highly relevant, and the reported performance gains are significant (e.g., a 45% decrease in error compared to AFHMM and 29% compared to AFAMAP on real data), demonstrating a clear advance in the state of the art.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the constrained MAP problem and its relaxation into a solvable CQP is a solid technical contribution that makes the proposed model practical. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The use of a challenging, low-frequency real-world dataset, along with comparisons to relevant baselines, effectively validates the method's utility. The inclusion of results using both an oracle constraint (TTC) and a realistic one (NTC) provides a clear understanding of the constraint's impact and the method's practical potential.
*   **Theoretical Grounding:** The authors successfully connect their penalized objective function to the established posterior regularization framework [4], which lends theoretical justification to their approach.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement):**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** While the paper's technical content is strong, its readability could be improved. The text is dense with acronyms (AFHMM, SAC, AFAMAP, CQP, NTC, TTC, etc.), which can disrupt the flow and make the paper challenging for readers not deeply familiar with this specific subfield. The authors could consider reducing their use or providing a small reference table. Furthermore, the CQP formulation in Section 5 introduces a significant amount of specialized notation in a very condensed manner, which could be made more accessible with additional explanation.
*   **Framing of the Model:** The authors introduce the SAC as a penalty term added to the MAP objective function. While this is a valid approach, and is later connected to posterior regularization, the model's motivation could be strengthened by presenting it from a more direct Bayesian perspective from the outset. The penalty term in Equation (5) can be interpreted as the logarithm of a prior distribution over the sequences of hidden states, where the prior favors sequences whose aggregate signal matches a known value. Stating clearly that this is an *a priori* constraint on the latent variables, which in turn constrains the posterior, would make the probabilistic justification for the model more direct and explicit from the beginning.
