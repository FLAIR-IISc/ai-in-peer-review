

This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation problems, with a specific focus on energy disaggregation. SACs incorporate domain knowledge by constraining the total signal for each source to be close to a specified value (e.g., expected daily energy consumption of an appliance). The authors integrate SACs into an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) and formulate a convex quadratic program for approximate inference. The method is evaluated on both synthetic data and real-world energy disaggregation data, demonstrating significant improvements over baseline AFHMM and AFAMAP (a recent state-of-the-art method).

The paper makes a strong contribution to the blind source separation literature by identifying a specific class of constraints (SACs) that are both practically useful and computationally tractable. Previous work on incorporating domain knowledge into probabilistic models includes posterior regularization [4], generalized expectation criterion [14], and measurement-based learning [13], but these approaches do not specify what types of domain knowledge are most useful. This paper addresses this gap by demonstrating that signal aggregate constraints are generally applicable across domains and provide significant performance improvements.

Quality: The technical approach is sound, with a well-motivated convex relaxation for the inference problem. The experimental results are thorough, demonstrating significant improvements (45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to AFHMM and 29% over AFAMAP on real energy data). The authors appropriately use paired t-tests to establish statistical significance. However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed theoretical justification for the convexity of the optimization problem (equation 6), as the current reasoning is somewhat opaque. Additionally, the authors could provide more information about the computational complexity scaling with respect to data length and number of hidden states, which would help assess the method's practicality for larger applications.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and logically structured, with a clear problem statement, motivation, and methodological explanation. The energy disaggregation application is well-motivated, and the experimental setup is clearly described. A minor inconsistency in notation is noted on page 4 (line 202) where commas appear in subscripts for the transition probability matrix p(i)jk, which differs from the consistent notation used earlier in the paper.

Originality: The SAC concept represents a novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation. While there are related works on constraints for HMMs (e.g., [12, 21]), the specific formulation of SACs as aggregate constraints on the total signal, with a focus on practical applicability across domains, is original. The paper also demonstrates how SACs can be derived within the posterior regularization framework [4], which strengthens the theoretical foundation.

Significance: The results on energy disaggregation are compelling, with the method significantly outperforming existing approaches. The improvements in normalized disaggregation error (45% vs. AFHMM, 29% vs. AFAMAP) and signal aggregate error (16% vs. AFAMAP) demonstrate clear practical value. The approach could be applied to other blind source separation problems beyond energy disaggregation, suggesting broader significance. The paper also provides a clear path for future work, such as incorporating SACs into unsupervised disaggregation approaches using national surveys to estimate constraint values.

Strengths:
- Clear identification of a useful class of constraints for blind source separation
- Strong experimental validation on real-world data
- Well-motivated convex optimization approach
- Significant performance improvements over state-of-the-art methods
- Clear theoretical connection to posterior regularization

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient theoretical justification for convexity of the optimization problem
- Limited discussion of computational scaling properties
- Minor notational inconsistency in the paper
- The toy data example uses a very low noise variance (σ²=0.01), which may not reflect realistic signal-to-noise ratios; clarification would be helpful

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the blind source separation literature with clear practical applications. The method is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates significant improvements over existing approaches. With minor revisions addressing the theoretical and notational issues, this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

Recommendation: Accept
