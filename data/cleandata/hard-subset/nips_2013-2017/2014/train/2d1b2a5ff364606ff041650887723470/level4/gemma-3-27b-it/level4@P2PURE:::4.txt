The paper introduces an extension to the additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) by incorporating constraints that enforce the sum of individual chain outputs to approximate a known aggregate value. The authors develop a convex relaxation for Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) inference and demonstrate its application to energy disaggregation tasks using real-world datasets – encompassing low-frequency readings from 100 UK homes and higher-frequency readings from 10 homes (though without individual appliance monitoring).

The presented ideas are conceptually clear, and the authors employ a relatively straightforward optimization framework. Despite this simplicity, the work is compelling due to its application to large, real-world datasets, and represents a successful combination of algorithmic and applied contributions. The following suggestions could strengthen the paper:

The AFAMAP model motivates enforcing a single active chain state at a time to improve the relaxation and increase the likelihood of integer solutions. It would be beneficial to understand whether the SACAFHMM optimization formulation achieves a similar effect, or if its lower error rates are maintained even with non-integer solutions. Discussion of these points would clarify the distinctions between the approaches. 

The AFAMAP and SACAFHMM approaches appear fundamentally orthogonal; the SACAFHMM constraints could conceivably be incorporated into the AFAMAP optimization formulation. While not expecting an implementation during the response phase, I encourage the authors to investigate this combined approach for a final version.

The paper occasionally conflates models and optimization procedures. For example, comparing “AFHMM” and “AFAMAP” lacks precision, as AFAMAP is an algorithm for approximating MAP solutions to the AFHMM. I believe “AFHMM” in these comparisons refers to alternating Viterbi optimization – is this correct? This relates to the point above, suggesting that the strategies employed by the different algorithms could be combined and potentially yield superior performance. In summary, the paper proposes a simple extension and optimization for a variant of the AFHMM, and the compelling results on real-world data suggest this is a reasonable and valuable contribution.
