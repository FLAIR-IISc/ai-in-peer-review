The paper proposes an extension to the additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) model by incorporating additional constraints that require the sum of individual chain outputs to be close to a reasonable, known aggregate value. The authors develop a convex relaxation for MAP inference and apply the algorithm to real-world datasets involving energy disaggregation, specifically analyzing data from 100 UK homes with low-frequency readings and 10 homes with higher-frequency readings (although without monitoring individual appliances).

While the ideas presented in the paper are fairly straightforward, and the authors employ a relatively simple optimization formulation to address these problems, the work remains compelling. The authors successfully apply these methods to relatively large real-world datasets, and as a paper that combines both applied and algorithmic elements, it can be considered a success. The following comments are intended to help improve the paper:

One motivation for the Additive Factorial Approximate MAP (AFAMAP) approach is to enforce the constraint that one chain changes state at a time, which can improve the relaxation and achieve integer solutions more often. It would be helpful to discuss whether the Semi-Aggregative Convex Additive Factorial HMM (SACAFHMM) optimization formulation accomplishes a similar goal or if the resulting solutions still exhibit lower error despite having non-integral solutions. Elucidating these points would further clarify the distinctions between the approaches.

Fundamentally, the AFAMAP and SACAFHMM ideas appear to be orthogonal, suggesting that the SACAFHMM constraints could be easily incorporated into the optimization formulation for AFAMAP. Although I do not expect the authors to address this during their response, I strongly encourage them to explore this possibility for a final version of the paper.

The authors occasionally conflate models and optimization procedures. For instance, comparing the results of "AFHMM" versus "AFAMAP" is not precise, as AFAMAP is an algorithm for approximately computing MAP solutions to the AFHMM. I believe that by "AFHMM," the authors actually mean alternating Viterbi optimization; confirmation of this would be appreciated. This conflation also highlights the potential for combining the strategies of different algorithms presented in the paper, which might yield better performance than the current approaches.

The paper proposes a simple extension and optimization to solve a variant of the AFHMM, and the results on real-world data are compelling. Overall, the paper appears reasonable, and with some revisions to address the mentioned points, it has the potential to be even stronger.
