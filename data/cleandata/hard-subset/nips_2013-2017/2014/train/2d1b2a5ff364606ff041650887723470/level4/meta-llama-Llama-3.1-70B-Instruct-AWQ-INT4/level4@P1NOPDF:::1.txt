This paper develops a novel method for performing blind source separation by formulating the problem as an additive factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) and applying signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The motivation behind this approach is to incorporate additional domain knowledge to improve the separation of time series into components. The authors use energy disaggregation as a motivating example, where the components of domestic energy use (relating to individual appliances) can be better separated when information about the total (expected) usage of each appliance in a time period is incorporated. The objective function, which is the log of the posterior distribution of the hidden chains given the observed data, is transformed into a convex optimization problem to perform the separation. The efficacy of the method is demonstrated in practice using both a toy dataset and real data from domestic energy output readings.

The paper is technically sound, with a strong simulation section. The method, as expected, outperforms regular AFHMM and AFAMAP (a recently developed alternative method), neither of which incorporate SACs. The improvements are shown to be significant using t-tests. The authors also demonstrate that the speed of optimization is comparable to AFAMAP. However, I have a question regarding the results section: why did the authors use such a low noise variance in their toy example? Was this done to make the illustration in Figure 2 clearer, showing that their method can separate the signal well while other methods cannot? Some clarification would be helpful here, as the problem setup seems overly simplistic and easy. Alternatively, the authors could comment on whether such signal-to-noise ratios are common in real data. Additionally, it would be beneficial to comment on the signal-to-noise ratio between the mains readings and the aggregate appliance output in the energy application.

The theoretical aspects of the paper are well presented and structured. However, the paper would benefit from a proof that the final procedure in equation (6) is indeed convex, as it is not immediately clear from the provided reasoning. Furthermore, theoretical scaling arguments (with respect to data length and number of hidden states) would complement the simulation findings and provide insight into the tractability of the method for larger data sets and state spaces. How does the proposed method compare to AFAMAP in terms of scalability?

The paper is well written and structured. The problem is nicely motivated in the introduction, with a clear description of relevant literature. However, I am not familiar with this problem, so I cannot definitively state that nothing has been omitted. I did notice a small potential error on page 4 (line 202), in the unnumbered equation before (6), where I believe there should be no commas in the subscripts preceding the object p, to be consistent with earlier notation on page 3 (unless new notation has been introduced here?).

To my knowledge, the work is novel and original, although I am not an expert and cannot comment on the significance of these results to the HMM community and the energy application studied.

Addendum: The authors satisfactorily addressed my questions in the feedback phase, and I encourage them to incorporate similar clarifications in the next version of the paper. This paper proposes a method for performing blind source separation using Additive Factorial HMMs with Signal Aggregate Constraints, which performs very well compared to the state-of-the-art, particularly with real data from an energy disaggregation problem.
