This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation, specifically within the context of additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs) for energy disaggregation. The core idea is to incorporate prior knowledge about the total energy consumption of individual appliances, improving the identifiability of the separated signals. The authors formulate this as a convex quadratic program for approximate inference and demonstrate significant performance gains on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, surpassing both the original AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach.

The paper is well-motivated. The problem of energy disaggregation is clearly articulated, and the inherent challenges of identifiability in blind source separation are highlighted. The introduction of SACs feels like a natural and intuitive way to inject domain knowledge into the problem, and the observation that total signal values are often known *a priori* is reasonable. The connection to posterior regularization is also a valuable addition, framing the work within a broader probabilistic modeling context.

The technical contributions – the formulation of SACs and the derivation of the convex quadratic program – are solid. The mathematical derivation appears sound, and the decision to relax the problem for efficient computation is justified. The presentation of the CQP is clear, and the use of CVX for implementation is practical. The inclusion of both simulated and real-world experimental results strengthens the paper. The results on the HES dataset are particularly compelling, showing a substantial improvement over existing methods. The ablation study comparing different approaches to setting the µ0 values for SACs is insightful.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. While the mathematical formulation is clear, the intuition behind *why* the convex relaxation works well isn't fully explored. A brief discussion of the impact of the relaxation on the solution quality would be beneficial. Although the authors mention the potential to generalize the constraints to multiple time periods, they don't explore this extension in detail. This feels like a potentially valuable avenue for future work that could be briefly acknowledged.

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, but could be strengthened by discussing recent advances in deep learning approaches to energy disaggregation. While the authors focus on probabilistic models, acknowledging the successes (and limitations) of neural network-based methods would provide a more complete picture of the field. The paper leans heavily on the HES dataset, and exploring performance on other datasets, even if synthetic, would increase the generalizability of the findings. Additionally, the choice of 3 states for all appliances seems somewhat arbitrary. A brief justification or sensitivity analysis concerning the number of states would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and intuitive approach to incorporating domain knowledge into BSS.
*   Solid technical contributions (SAC formulation and CQP derivation).
*   Significant performance improvements on a real-world dataset.
*   Clear presentation and well-written paper.
*   Connection to broader probabilistic modeling frameworks (posterior regularization).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of the impact of the convex relaxation.
*   Lack of exploration of more general constraint formulations (e.g., multiple time periods).
*   Insufficient consideration of recent deep learning approaches in the related work.
*   Limited dataset diversity.
*   Arbitrary choice of number of states without justification.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation and energy disaggregation. The introduction of SACs is a clever and effective way to leverage domain knowledge, and the experimental results are convincing. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above (expanding the related work, discussing the convex relaxation, and briefly acknowledging potential generalizations of the constraints).
