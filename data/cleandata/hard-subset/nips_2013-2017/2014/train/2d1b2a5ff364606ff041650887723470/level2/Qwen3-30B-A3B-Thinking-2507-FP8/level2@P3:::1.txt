

The paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation using additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs). SACs encourage the total signal for each source to be close to a specified value, addressing the identifiability problem in blind source separation where the goal is to recover meaningful source signals. The authors incorporate these constraints into AFHMM, formulate a convex quadratic program for approximate inference, and demonstrate significant improvements on real-world energy disaggregation data compared to the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach.

The work relates to previous NIPS papers on energy disaggregation, particularly [12] which introduced AFAMAP with constraints on state transitions. Unlike previous constraint-based approaches that focus on temporal structure (e.g., limiting state changes), SACs focus on the aggregate signal values across the entire time series, representing a novel constraint type. The paper also connects SACs to posterior regularization [4], showing theoretical grounding for the approach.

The technical contribution is strong, with clear mathematical formulation of SACs and derivation of the convex quadratic program for inference. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including both synthetic toy data to validate robustness to constraint specification errors and real-world energy disaggregation data to demonstrate practical utility. The results are compelling, showing a 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over a recent state-of-the-art method. The paper provides appropriate statistical validation (paired t-tests) for the results.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The problem setting, model formulation, and evaluation metrics (NDE and SAE) are well-defined, and the results are presented with appropriate figures and tables. The authors carefully address limitations, particularly the need for accurate prior knowledge about total signal values.

The originality is significant. While previous work has explored various constraints for AFHMMs (e.g., [12] on state transitions, [11] on semi-Markov models), SACs represent a fundamentally different constraint targeting aggregate signal values rather than temporal structure. The paper properly contextualizes its work against existing literature and clearly articulates how SACs differ from previous approaches.

The significance is high. Energy disaggregation is a practical problem with real-world implications for energy conservation and consumer awareness. The paper demonstrates that incorporating domain knowledge about expected total energy consumption can significantly improve disaggregation performance. The approach is generalizable to other blind source separation problems where aggregate signal values are known or can be estimated.

A minor weakness is the limited discussion of how to obtain SAC values (µi0) in practice when prior knowledge is limited. While the paper mentions these values could be obtained from "expert knowledge or by experiments" and references national surveys, it doesn't provide a systematic approach for estimating these values when such surveys are unavailable. A reasonable suggestion would be to briefly discuss methods for estimating SAC values when prior knowledge is limited, such as using small amounts of labeled data or transfer learning from similar domains.

Another minor suggestion is to more explicitly discuss the computational complexity comparison with other approaches. While the paper mentions computational time is similar to AFHMM and AFAMAP, a brief comparison of time complexity would be valuable for practitioners.

Overall, the paper makes a strong contribution to blind source separation and energy disaggregation. The technical approach is sound, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results are compelling. The work is original, significant, and well-written. The minor weaknesses identified are reasonable to address but do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a framework for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation problems.
