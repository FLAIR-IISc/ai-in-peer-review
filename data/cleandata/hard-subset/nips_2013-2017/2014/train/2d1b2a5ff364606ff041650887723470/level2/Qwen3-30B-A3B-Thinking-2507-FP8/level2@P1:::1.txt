

This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation. The authors observe that in many applications, the total signal (e.g., daily energy consumption) varies significantly across sources, and domain knowledge often provides reasonable estimates of these totals. They incorporate these constraints into an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM), formulating a relaxed optimization problem that can be efficiently solved as a convex quadratic program. The paper demonstrates significant improvements on real-world energy disaggregation data compared to baseline methods.

The paper is well-motivated by the identifiability problem in blind source separation, particularly in energy disaggregation where the goal is to identify meaningful appliance-level consumption patterns. SACs represent a novel approach by focusing on aggregate signal values rather than the more common constraints on state transitions or duration. The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) provides a strong theoretical foundation.

Strengths:
1. The problem is well-motivated with clear real-world relevance to energy disaggregation, a significant practical problem.
2. The SAC approach is theoretically sound and well-justified, with the connection to posterior regularization providing important context.
3. The experimental evaluation is thorough, including synthetic data to validate the approach and real-world energy data to demonstrate practical impact.
4. The paper provides clear evidence of improvement (33% reduction in normalized disaggregation error over AFAMAP on real-world data) with statistical significance verified via paired t-tests.
5. Computational efficiency is well-documented, with inference times comparable to baseline methods.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper could better clarify how SACs differ from other constraint approaches (e.g., AFAMAP's constraint on state transitions). While related work mentions these approaches, the paper doesn't explicitly contrast SACs with them in terms of knowledge requirements or computational overhead.
2. The sensitivity of SACs to errors in the constraint values (μi0) is not thoroughly explored in the real-world data. The synthetic data experiment shows degradation as constraint values deviate from true values, but the paper doesn't discuss how robust the approach is to errors in practice when estimating μi0 from domain knowledge.
3. The paper doesn't explore combining SACs with other constraints (e.g., AFAMAP's single-state-change constraint), which could yield further improvements.
4. While Section 4.2 mentions generalizing SACs to multiple time periods, this extension isn't explored in the experiments, which would strengthen the paper's claim of general applicability.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with clear theoretical foundations. The convex quadratic programming approach is well-motivated and correctly derived. The experimental evaluation is thorough and properly validated with statistical tests. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of SACs (e.g., requiring accurate prior knowledge of total consumption).

Clarity: The paper is well-written and well-organized. The problem statement, theoretical framework, and experimental evaluation are clearly presented. The mathematical derivations are generally clear, though the CQP derivation in Section 5 could benefit from more intuitive explanation.

Originality: SACs represent a novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation. While the paper correctly cites related work on posterior regularization and other constraint approaches, the specific focus on aggregate signal values appears to be new. The connection to posterior regularization provides an important theoretical link.

Significance: Energy disaggregation has significant practical importance for energy conservation. The paper demonstrates a meaningful improvement (33% reduction in NDE) over a state-of-the-art method (AFAMAP) on real-world data, representing a tangible advance with potential for practical implementation. The approach is general enough to potentially apply to other blind source separation problems where aggregate signal information is available.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of blind source separation. The SAC approach is both novel and practically valuable, with solid theoretical foundation and compelling experimental results. The minor weaknesses noted above do not detract significantly from the paper's overall quality and contribution.

Recommendation: Accept

Pro: Well-motivated problem, strong theoretical foundation, clear experimental results showing significant improvement over baselines, good connection to posterior regularization framework, practical significance for energy disaggregation.

Con: Could better contrast SACs with other constraint approaches, more exploration of sensitivity to constraint value errors, and brief exploration of combining SACs with other constraints would strengthen the paper.
