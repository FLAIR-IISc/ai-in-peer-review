This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), particularly in the underdetermined case where a single time series is an aggregate of multiple unknown source signals. The authors correctly point out that this problem is ill-posed due to identifiability issues, making it difficult to recover meaningful sources. The main contribution of the paper is the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), a novel and intuitive method for incorporating domain knowledge into the separation process. The key idea is that for many applications, the total aggregate value (e.g., the sum) of each source signal over a given period is approximately known.

The authors apply this idea to the problem of energy disaggregation, where the goal is to separate a household's total electricity consumption into the consumption of individual appliances. They incorporate SACs into a standard Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), resulting in a model they call AFHMM+SAC. Since the SACs are non-Markovian and couple all time steps for a given source, exact MAP inference via standard dynamic programming (like the Viterbi algorithm) is intractable. To overcome this, the authors formulate a relaxed version of the MAP inference problem as a convex quadratic program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The paper also provides a nice connection between their penalized objective function and the posterior regularization framework.

The proposed method is evaluated on a toy dataset and a large, real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES). The experiments compare AFHMM+SAC against the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP. The results demonstrate that incorporating SACs leads to dramatic performance improvements, significantly reducing both the point-wise disaggregation error and the error in estimating the total energy consumed by each appliance.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Core Idea:** The central idea of using Signal Aggregate Constraints is simple, elegant, and highly effective. It provides a very natural way to inject prior knowledge into a notoriously difficult problem. While other constraints have been proposed for AFHMMs (e.g., limiting state transitions), SACs tackle the identifiability problem from a different and complementary angle. The idea seems broadly applicable to other BSS domains where aggregate source properties are known or can be estimated.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical development is sound. The authors correctly identify the computational challenge posed by the non-local nature of the constraints and propose a principled and practical solution by relaxing the problem into a CQP. The connection to posterior regularization provides additional theoretical grounding for the approach.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is strong and convincing. The use of a toy dataset effectively illustrates the robustness of the method to noisy specifications of the constraint values. The experiments on the real-world HES dataset are comprehensive, testing the model on both synthetically aggregated data and true mains readings from multiple homes. The comparison against a strong baseline (AFAMAP) and the inclusion of an "oracle" result (using the true total consumption as the constraint) clearly demonstrate the value of the proposed constraints. The reported performance gains are substantial and statistically significant.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the proposed method is explained logically, and the experiments are well-designed and their results are presented effectively.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The regularization parameters λi, which control the strength of the constraints, are set to 1 for all experiments. While this simplifies the evaluation, it leaves open the question of how sensitive the model's performance is to this choice. A brief discussion on how one might set these parameters in practice (e.g., via cross-validation on a small validation set) and a small sensitivity analysis would strengthen the paper. For instance, how does performance degrade as λ moves away from an optimal value?

2.  **Clarity on Training:** The paper focuses on the inference problem, assuming the HMM parameters (state means, transition probabilities) are known. It is mentioned that these are estimated from training data. It would be helpful to be more explicit about this training process. The current setup appears to be supervised, where individual appliance data is available for training. Clarifying this would help position the work, as the ultimate goal in energy disaggregation is often fully unsupervised operation. The authors mention this as future work, but a clearer statement on the current paper's setting would be beneficial.

3.  **Relation to Other Constraints:** The paper mentions other types of constraints in the related work, such as the "at most one chain can change state" constraint used in AFAMAP. It would be interesting to discuss the relationship between SACs and these other constraints. Are they mutually exclusive or could they be combined? It seems plausible that combining the local, temporal constraint of AFAMAP with the global, aggregate constraint of SACs could yield even better performance. A brief discussion of this possibility would be a valuable addition.

4.  **Generalization of SACs:** The authors briefly mention generalizing SACs to cover multiple, shorter time periods (e.g., constraining daytime vs. nighttime usage). This is an excellent idea that would make the model even more powerful. While a full experimental evaluation is likely beyond the scope of this paper, expanding this short paragraph with more detail or potential use cases would highlight the flexibility of the proposed framework.

In summary, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel, practical, and highly effective method for improving blind source separation in AFHMMs. The core contribution of Signal Aggregate Constraints is significant and well-motivated. The technical approach is sound, and the experimental results are compelling. The weaknesses identified are minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the work. The paper is a clear contribution to the field and is likely to be impactful, particularly in the energy disaggregation community.
