This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation (BSS) problems, with a specific application to energy disaggregation. The central idea is that for many BSS problems, the total aggregate value of each source signal over a given time period is either known a priori or can be well-estimated. The work incorporates these constraints into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a common model for this task. As the SACs are global constraints that break the Markov property, standard inference techniques are not applicable. To address this, the paper proposes an approximate inference method by formulating the MAP estimation problem as a penalized objective function, which is then relaxed into a computationally tractable Convex Quadratic Program (CQP). The effectiveness of this approach, termed AFHMM+SAC, is demonstrated on a real-world, low-frequency energy disaggregation dataset, where it shows a significant performance improvement over both a standard AFHMM and the AFAMAP algorithm, a recent state-of-the-art method.

**Quality**
The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work. The motivation for using SACs to address the inherent unidentifiability of BSS is compelling and clearly articulated. The formulation of the constrained optimization problem and its subsequent relaxation into a CQP is well-derived. The claims of performance improvement are strongly supported by a thorough experimental evaluation. The use of a toy dataset helps build intuition, while the two experiments on the HES real-world dataset provide convincing evidence. The inclusion of an oracle experiment using the "True Total Consumption" (TTC) is a strength, as it effectively demonstrates the upper bound on performance and validates the utility of the constraint structure itself. The comparison against a strong baseline (AFAMAP) further solidifies the paper's claims.

**Clarity**
The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The problem, proposed model, and inference procedure are explained logically. However, there are a few points that could be clarified to improve the paper and aid in reproducibility:

*   The CQP solves a relaxed version of the problem where the state indicator variables `Sitk` can take values in [0, 1]. The paper does not specify how these fractional values are converted into discrete state assignments to compute the estimated signal `x̂it` for evaluation using the NDE and SAE metrics. This is a critical detail that should be included. For instance, are the values rounded, or is the state with the maximum value chosen (argmax)?
*   In Section 4.2, the paper notes that the constraint can be generalized to multiple time periods. While the paper understandably focuses on a single constraint for simplicity, providing a brief, concrete example could better illustrate the power and flexibility of this generalization.

**Originality**
The core contribution—the introduction of signal aggregate constraints for regularizing BSS problems—appears to be novel. While constrained HMMs are an established area, this specific form of global, sum-based constraint is a new and useful addition to the toolbox. The application of a CQP relaxation for inference in this context is also a valuable contribution. The paper does a good job of positioning its work relative to prior research in energy disaggregation and constrained probabilistic models, correctly identifying its unique contribution.

**Significance**
The results presented are significant and likely to be of high interest to researchers and practitioners in energy disaggregation and other BSS domains. The demonstrated 45% reduction in error over AFHMM and 29% reduction over AFAMAP on a challenging, realistic dataset represents a clear and substantial advance in the state of the art. The SAC concept is general and intuitive, suggesting it could be readily applied to other domains where similar aggregate knowledge is available (e.g., total word counts in topic modeling, or total gene expression levels in computational biology). The work provides a practical and effective method for injecting valuable domain knowledge into a difficult inference problem.

**Suggestions for Improvement (in order of importance)**
1.  **Essential:** Clarify the procedure for obtaining discrete state assignments from the relaxed CQP solution. This is necessary for reproducibility.
2.  **Essential:** The regularization parameter `λi` is set to 1 in all experiments. A discussion on the sensitivity of the model to this hyperparameter would strengthen the paper. It would be valuable to include brief comments on how one might set this parameter in practice (e.g., using a validation set) and perhaps a small experiment showing performance as `λi` varies.
3.  **Recommended:** The paper notes that SACs are orthogonal to other constraints, such as the one used in AFAMAP (at most one appliance changes state at a time). A brief discussion in the conclusion or future work section about the potential of combining different types of constraints could highlight promising research directions.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   Presents a novel, intuitive, and general method (SACs) for incorporating domain knowledge into BSS models.
    *   Provides a technically sound and tractable CQP-based approximate inference algorithm.
    *   Demonstrates substantial and statistically significant performance improvements over strong baselines on a challenging, real-world energy disaggregation task.
    *   The work is well-written and the contributions are clear.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    *   The paper lacks a crucial detail for reproducibility regarding the handling of the CQP's relaxed solution.
    *   The sensitivity to the main hyperparameter (`λi`) is not explored.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses, which are minor and can be addressed with straightforward revisions. The work presents a valuable contribution to the field with clear potential for impact.
