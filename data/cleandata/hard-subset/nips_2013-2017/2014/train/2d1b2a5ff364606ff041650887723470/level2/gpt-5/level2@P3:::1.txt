Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses identifiability in blind source separation (BSS), with a focus on non-intrusive load monitoring (energy disaggregation). It proposes signal aggregate constraints (SACs), which encode prior knowledge about the total signal (e.g., total daily energy) for each source. SACs are incorporated into an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM), resulting in AFHMM+SAC. Because aggregate constraints couple all time steps and break Markov structure, the paper derives a convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation for MAP inference by introducing transition-indicator variables H and relaxing the binary simplex constraints. The objective is also shown to arise from posterior regularization [4], thereby connecting the method to a broader framework for incorporating side information. Experiments on a toy dataset and a challenging, low-frequency real dataset (HES) demonstrate substantial improvements over AFHMM and over AFAMAP [12], a state-of-the-art AFHMM variant that enforces “at most one chain changes state per time step.” The work is related to prior approaches that add constraints or side information to FHMMs (e.g., AFAMAP [12], duration models/HSMMs [10, 11], interleaved/non-homogeneous FHMMs [21]) and to frameworks for constrained learning (posterior regularization [4], generalized expectation [14], measurement-based learning [13]). Unlike prior probabilistic DB work on aggregates [20], the proposed aggregate constraints apply to structured temporal models.

Strengths
- Clear, general idea: leveraging prior knowledge on per-source totals addresses a common identifiability failure mode in BSS, especially for interpretability-driven applications such as energy disaggregation.
- Sound formulation: the AFHMM+SAC objective is a concave quadratic plus linear terms with linear constraints after relaxation; the use of auxiliary transition variables makes the relaxation tractable. The connection to posterior regularization clarifies the statistical meaning of the penalty.
- Practical impact: substantial error reductions on low-frequency energy data, which is a realistic NILM setting. Reported improvements vs. AFAMAP are consistent across 100 households (synthetic mains) and 9 homes with real mains, with significance testing.
- Reasonable computational profile: although solved with a generic CQP solver (CVX), runtimes are reported comparable to AFHMM/AFAMAP on the evaluated scales.
- The approach is modular: SACs are conceptually orthogonal to many other constraints (e.g., duration or interleaving) and could be combined in future work.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Essential revisions (feasible):
- Sensitivity and calibration of SACs: Real-data robustness to mis-specified µ0 is not quantified. The toy experiment explores sensitivity, but results for HES with perturbed or biased µ0 (e.g., ±20–50% of NTC, or household-personalized priors when available) would strengthen the claim that SACs are beneficial under realistic prior uncertainty. Reporting performance vs. λ would help practitioners tune regularization.
- From relaxed to discrete solutions: The relaxation permits fractional state indicators. The paper should state how discrete sequences or continuous estimates are produced from the relaxed solution (e.g., using the relaxed S to form convex combinations of µi, or via rounding/projection/decoding). An ablation showing the effect of any rounding on NDE/SAE would improve clarity and reproducibility.
- Unknown/“other” load: In the mains setting, power from unmodeled appliances exists. Clarify whether an “other” chain is included, or how mismatch between modeled appliances and mains is handled (e.g., increased noise variance, slack variable). A brief analysis of how SACs interact with unmodeled loads would be valuable.
- Comparative baselines: Since related work includes HSMMs [10, 11] and interleaved/non-homogeneous FHMMs [21], adding at least one such baseline (or explaining why not feasible on HES) would better position the contribution and quantify complementarity.

Optional but beneficial:
- Interval/robust SACs: The squared penalty implies precise targets. Many domains provide ranges or uncertain totals. An interval or robust penalty (e.g., hinge/ε-insensitive/Huber around µ0, or a Gaussian prior with known variance) could better reflect uncertainty; even a brief discussion would guide adoption.
- Specialized optimization: CVX is convenient but may not scale to very long sequences or many appliances. Outlining a tailored solver exploiting block-tridiagonal structure (from temporal coupling and per-time quadratic term) and simplex constraints could make the method more broadly applicable; a complexity vs. AFAMAP plot would be informative.
- Broader constraints: The paper notes straightforward extension to windowed SACs (e.g., daytime/nighttime). Including a small experiment with such constraints would underline generality and improve practical relevance.

Evaluation of review criteria
- Quality: The technical development is sound and consistent with convex optimization practice. The derivation from posterior regularization is correct and illuminating. Empirical evidence is positive and includes significance testing. Some methodological details (relaxation decoding, handling of “other” loads, hyperparameter selection) should be clarified.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized. Notation is heavy around the CQP; a compact summary of dimensions and a schematic of variables/constraints would aid readability. Explicitly stating the inference output from the relaxed solution would reduce ambiguity.
- Originality: Combining aggregate constraints with FHMMs via a convex relaxation appears novel in this setting. The connection to PR is known, but the particular aggregate form and its application to NILM are new. Prior aggregate-constraint work does not handle temporal graphical models of this complexity.
- Significance: The approach provides a simple, broadly applicable mechanism to inject domain knowledge and yields meaningful gains on a realistic, low-frequency dataset. The idea should be of interest to BSS and structured prediction communities, with potential impact beyond NILM.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Simple, general idea with clear motivation.
- Solid convex formulation and link to posterior regularization.
- Strong empirical gains on realistic data; statistically significant.
- Likely to be adopted and extended in BSS and NILM.

Cons:
- Limited analysis of robustness to prior misspecification on real data.
- Missing details on converting relaxed solutions to usable sequences/signals.
- Baseline comparisons omit some closely related constrained FHMMs.
- Reliance on generic CQP solver raises scalability questions for larger deployments.

Overall recommendation
A well-motivated, technically sound contribution that demonstrates practical improvements in a challenging application. With clarifications on decoding, robustness, and baseline scope, the paper would be a strong fit.
