## Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a method to incorporate domain knowledge into blind source separation problems, specifically within the context of energy disaggregation using Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs). The core idea – leveraging prior knowledge about the total energy consumption of appliances – is intuitive and addresses a crucial limitation of BSS: identifiability. The authors formulate SACs as constraints on the total signal for each source and propose a convex quadratic program for approximate inference, demonstrating significant performance improvements on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset compared to the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. The connection to existing work in posterior regularization is a valuable addition, positioning SACs within a broader framework of incorporating prior knowledge. The authors effectively highlight the limitations of existing approaches and the practical challenges of energy disaggregation, like the inherent underdetermined nature of the problem and the need for interpretable results. The experimental results are compelling, showing a substantial reduction in both Normalized Disaggregation Error (NDE) and Signal Aggregate Error (SAE). 

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the convex relaxation employed. While computationally efficient, the authors acknowledge it doesn't guarantee optimality. Exploring the gap between the relaxed solution and the true MAP estimate, even through empirical analysis, would strengthen the contribution. Additionally, the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the regularization parameter (λ) or the slack variable (δ) should be examined in more detail. While the authors mention tuning these, further analysis on the impact of their values would be useful. The assumption of known aggregate values (µi0) is also a limitation. Exploring methods for estimating these values from data or leveraging broader population statistics could be discussed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and intuitive approach to address the identifiability problem in BSS.
*   Strong empirical results on a real-world dataset, significantly outperforming existing methods.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation of the methodology and experimental setup.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Relaxation to a convex program introduces a potential optimality gap.
*   Sensitivity analysis of regularization parameters could be more comprehensive.
*   Assumption of known aggregate values might limit practical applicability. 

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of energy disaggregation and BSS. Despite some minor limitations, the novelty of the proposed approach, strong empirical results, and clear presentation warrant acceptance, with encouragement to the authors to address the identified limitations in future work.
