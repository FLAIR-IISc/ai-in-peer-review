Summary
The paper addresses identifiability in blind source separation by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs), which penalize deviations between the total energy of each latent source and a specified target. The authors instantiate this idea in an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM), yielding AFHMM+SAC, and show that MAP inference with these non-Markovian penalties can be relaxed to a convex quadratic program (CQP) via auxiliary transition variables. They also provide a posterior-regularization interpretation of the objective. Experiments on a toy problem and on the UK HES low-frequency energy dataset demonstrate substantial improvements over a baseline AFHMM and over AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS 2012), including statistically significant gains in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE), with similar runtimes in practice.

Relation to prior work
Factorial HMMs for disaggregation are well established (Ghahramani & Jordan; Roweis; Kolter & Jaakkola). Prior constraints such as at-most-one-chain-change (AFAMAP), non-homogeneous transitions, and semi-Markov durations have been explored. The idea of constraining posteriors with expectation-based constraints is connected to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.) and measurement-based learning (Liang et al.). The novelty here is to target aggregate (sum-over-time) constraints on each chain’s signal within an AFHMM and to derive a tractable convex relaxation for MAP inference. Aggregate constraints have appeared in probabilistic databases under independence assumptions, but not (to my knowledge) for coupled latent time-series models like AFHMMs.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable constraint family that matches practical domain knowledge: total energy per source is often known approximately.
- Solid formulation: the MAP objective with quadratic aggregate penalties becomes concave in the relaxed variables (negative PSD quadratics plus linear terms), leading to a CQP with linear constraints using auxiliary transition variables. The PR derivation strengthens the conceptual grounding.
- Empirical results on a realistic, challenging low-frequency dataset show clear, significant improvements over strong baselines (AFAMAP), including on true mains signals where unmodeled loads exist. Statistical tests are provided.
- Practicality: using national averages as SAC targets is realistic; the method is robust on a toy study to moderate mis-specification of totals.
- Implementation and runtime claims appear reasonable for the evaluated settings; comparison includes timing.

Weaknesses and limitations
Major:
- Dependence on the accuracy of µ0: While the toy experiment explores sensitivity, the real-data analysis primarily contrasts national averages (NTC) vs. oracle totals (TTC). A more systematic robustness study on realistic deviations (e.g., ±20–50% perturbations of NTC per appliance, distributional shifts across households) would better quantify sensitivity and practical reliability.
- Evaluation scope: Metrics focus on energy reconstruction (NDE/SAE). For NILM, on/off event detection and appliance-level precision/recall are also important; the method’s behavior on temporal localization is not assessed. Since the relaxation can yield fractional S, this may advantage MSE metrics relative to discrete baselines and leaves segmentation accuracy unquantified.
- Scalability details: The variable count grows with T∑iKi + T∑iKi^2 due to H variables. Beyond empirical timings, explicit complexity scaling and memory use would help; reliance on CVX may limit larger-scale deployments.
- Hyperparameters: λi are fixed to 1 without tuning. Results could be stronger (or more variable) with cross-validated λ; guidance on selection is missing.
- Modeling assumptions: All appliances are modeled with 3 states estimated via k-means; σt and other noise parameters are not fully specified. Some houses contain unmodeled loads; how the penalty interacts with such model mismatch is not deeply analyzed.

Minor:
- Clarity: Some notational density in Section 5; a short proposition formalizing concavity and constraints would aid reproducibility. Explicitly state how continuous relaxed solutions are mapped to signals for evaluation (likely x̂it = µi^T Sit).
- Related work: Consider citing works that use aggregate/long-horizon constraints or priors in time-series beyond databases (if any), and more NILM comparisons at low frequency, if available.

Quality
The technical formulation is sound; the convexity argument is plausible and consistent with the PSD structure and sign of quadratic terms. The PR perspective is correct. Experiments are carefully executed with significance testing. Some design choices (fixed λ, 3-state models, CVX solver) could be better justified but do not undermine the main claims.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. Some sections (CQP derivation) are dense; adding a brief statement of the convexity result and practical notes on solver settings and stopping criteria would improve clarity. Provide precise details on σt estimation and preprocessing.

Originality
Moderately novel. Posterior/expectation constraints are known, but specifying sum-over-time constraints for each chain within AFHMM, and deriving an efficient convex relaxation, is a meaningful and (to my knowledge) new combination for BSS/NILM.

Significance
Likely useful in NILM and other BSS problems where total per-source usage is available (e.g., biology, audio with known source budgets). The approach is simple to adopt and yields sizeable gains on a realistic dataset.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add robustness experiments on real data by perturbing NTC per appliance, and analyze degradation vs. mismatch magnitude.
- Report event-detection metrics (F1, precision/recall) and duration errors to complement NDE/SAE; compare with discrete outputs (AFAMAP) fairly.
- Explore windowed SACs (daytime/nighttime) to reflect usage patterns; include an ablation to show additional benefit vs. single global constraint.
- Provide guidance on selecting λi (e.g., via cross-validation or Bayesian treatment), and study sensitivity.
- Include complexity analysis and practical solver details; consider a specialized projected Newton/QP solver for scalability beyond CVX.
- Clarify treatment of unmodeled loads and discuss incorporating a slack/“other” appliance or a residual component.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated idea with strong empirical gains on a challenging, relevant dataset.
- Sound optimization with a principled PR connection.
- Simple and general technique likely to see adoption.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited robustness analysis to realistic mis-specified totals on real data.
- Missing temporal/event-level evaluation; potential advantage from continuous relaxations on MSE metrics.
- Scalability and hyperparameter selection not fully addressed.

Overall assessment
A solid and practical contribution that advances AFHMM-based disaggregation by injecting realistic aggregate priors through a tractable convex relaxation. Despite some limitations in evaluation breadth and robustness analysis, the paper is technically sound, clearly motivated, and shows meaningful improvements. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the suggested analyses where feasible.
