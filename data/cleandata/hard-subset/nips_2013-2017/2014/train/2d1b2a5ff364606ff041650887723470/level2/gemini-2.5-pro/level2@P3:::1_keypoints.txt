- Introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) to incorporate known/estimated total source sums into blind source separation (BSS).  
- Applies SACs within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) for energy disaggregation.  
- SACs are global constraints that break the Markov property, making standard inference infeasible.  
- Proposes approximate MAP inference by penalizing constraint violations and relaxing to a convex quadratic program (CQP).  
- The resulting method (AFHMM+SAC) is computationally tractable and evaluated empirically.  
- Demonstrates substantial improvement on a real low-frequency energy dataset versus AFHMM and AFAMAP.  
- Uses a toy dataset and two HES experiments to build intuition and validate results.  
- Includes an oracle experiment with True Total Consumption (TTC) to show an upper-bound and validate constraint utility.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-derived, and the performance claims are strongly supported by experiments.  
- Well-written and clearly structured, with logical explanation of problem, model, and inference.  
- Missing detail: does not specify how fractional CQP state indicators are converted to discrete states for evaluation.  
- Missing detail: lacks a concrete example for generalizing constraints to multiple time periods.  
- Core contribution (SACs) is novel and positioned well relative to prior constrained HMM work.  
- CQP relaxation for inference in this context is a valuable methodological contribution.  
- Results (45% error reduction over AFHMM, 29% over AFAMAP) are significant and broadly applicable to other BSS domains.  
- Essential revision needed: clarify discretization procedure from relaxed CQP solution for reproducibility.  
- Essential revision needed: analyze sensitivity and selection strategy for regularization parameter λi (currently fixed at 1).  
- Recommended: discuss potential combination of SACs with other constraint types (e.g., AFAMAP’s single-change constraint).  
- Overall assessment: strengths outweigh weaknesses; minor clarifications would make the work more reproducible and stronger.
