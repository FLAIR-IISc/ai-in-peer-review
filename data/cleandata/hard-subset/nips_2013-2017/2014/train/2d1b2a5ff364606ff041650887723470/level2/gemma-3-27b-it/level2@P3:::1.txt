This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) as a way to incorporate domain knowledge into blind source separation problems, specifically within the context of additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs) applied to energy disaggregation. The core idea is that knowing approximate total energy consumption for different appliance types can significantly improve the accuracy of disaggregation. The work proposes a convex quadratic program for approximate inference with SACs and demonstrates substantial performance gains on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, outperforming both the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses an important problem – the identifiability of source signals in BSS. The introduction clearly articulates the challenges of energy disaggregation and positions SACs as a promising approach to address them. The connection to posterior regularization is also a valuable contribution, providing a broader theoretical grounding for the proposed method. The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating a significant improvement in disaggregation accuracy with SACs. The comparison to AFAMAP [12] is particularly strong, showcasing the benefits of the proposed constraints. The computational efficiency argument, stating comparable runtime to existing methods, is also important for practical applicability.

However, there are several areas where the paper could be strengthened. While the paper mentions that the choice of λ (or δ) is a hyperparameter, there is no discussion of how this parameter is selected or tuned. A brief exploration of sensitivity to this parameter, perhaps via cross-validation, would add robustness to the claims.  The discussion of the relationship to probabilistic databases [20] feels somewhat cursory. While the authors note the difference in model complexity, a more detailed comparison of the types of aggregate constraints considered would be beneficial. The section on related work, while covering relevant literature, could be broadened. For instance, the paper could mention recent work leveraging sparsity-inducing priors for energy disaggregation. 

The derivation of the convex quadratic program, while presented concisely, could benefit from more detailed explanations of certain steps, especially for readers unfamiliar with the underlying mathematical techniques. The notation, while generally clear, becomes somewhat dense in Section 5, making it harder to follow the derivation. Providing intermediate steps or simplifying the notation where possible would improve readability. 

A more in-depth analysis of the failure cases would be beneficial.  Are there specific appliance types or usage patterns where SACs perform poorly? Understanding these limitations is crucial for identifying potential areas for future work.  The paper relies heavily on the HES dataset. While valuable, results on additional datasets (even synthetic ones) would further strengthen the generalizability of the findings. The claim that the computational cost scales similarly to existing methods is made without providing specific runtime numbers for varying dataset sizes. Providing a scalability analysis, even a preliminary one, would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge into BSS via SACs.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating significant performance improvements on a real-world dataset.
*   Clear presentation of the problem and proposed solution.
*   Connections to relevant theoretical frameworks (posterior regularization).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis.
*   Somewhat superficial discussion of related work.
*   Dense mathematical notation in Section 5.
*   Lack of detailed failure case analysis and scalability analysis.



Overall, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of energy disaggregation and BSS. The proposed SACs offer a principled way to incorporate domain knowledge, and the empirical results are highly encouraging. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a more impactful contribution to the NIPS community. While not essential, a more thorough investigation into hyperparameter sensitivity and scalability would be particularly beneficial.
