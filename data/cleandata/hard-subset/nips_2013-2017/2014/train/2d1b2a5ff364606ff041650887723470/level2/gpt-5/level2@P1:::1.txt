Summary
The paper addresses identifiability in blind source separation (BSS), with a focus on energy disaggregation (NILM). It introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) that encode prior knowledge about the total energy (or other aggregates) of each latent source over a time window. These constraints are incorporated into an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM), yielding AFHMM+SAC. The authors derive a relaxed convex quadratic program (CQP) that enables efficient approximate MAP inference by introducing auxiliary transition variables and exploiting the PSD structure of the quadratic terms. They also connect the objective to the posterior regularization framework. Empirically, on UK HES low-frequency data, AFHMM+SAC outperforms a vanilla AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12), and shows robustness in a toy example to moderate mismatch in the aggregate priors.

Relation to prior work
The approach is closely related to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., JMLR’10), generalized expectation (Mann & McCallum, ACL’08), and measurement-based learning (Liang et al., ICML’09), but makes a concrete proposal of aggregate constraints that are common in BSS and particularly relevant for NILM. Within NILM, it complements constraints on state dynamics such as one-change-per-time (AFAMAP), non-homogeneous FHMMs, and semi-Markov duration models; it instead adds global (non-Markovian) constraints across time. The convex relaxation and use of auxiliary pairwise transition variables is in the spirit of prior convex approximations for factorial models, but the specific SAC formulation and resulting CQP appear novel and practically effective.

Quality
- Theory/algorithm: The optimization is carefully derived. By introducing H variables and showing Λi and Vt are PSD, the objective becomes concave in the relaxed variables; maximizing it under linear constraints yields a convex program solvable by CVX. The posterior-regularization derivation is correct and clarifies the probabilistic interpretation.
- Assumptions: SACs presume reasonable priors on daily (or windowed) totals per source. The toy study shows graceful degradation as priors deviate from truth. The real-data “NTC” prior is plausible and the oracle “TTC” bound is informative.
- Empirics: Experiments on 100 households (no mains) and 9 houses (with mains) show consistent improvements in NDE and SAE, with statistical tests reported. Runtime is reported and comparable to baselines at the scales considered.

Areas needing clarification or further validation:
- Rounding/decoding: The relaxed solution yields fractional Sit; how are discrete state sequences or per-appliance traces produced? Is the output the expectation µi·Sit, or is there a projection/rounding step? Please detail any post-processing and its effect on metrics.
- Hyperparameters: λi are fixed to 1. Sensitivity analyses (beyond the toy µ0 sweep) and a principled selection strategy (cross-validation, evidence maximization, or adaptive weighting by uncertainty in µ0) would strengthen the claims.
- Acquisition of µ0: For deployment, how are per-appliance daily aggregates estimated a priori? The paper uses national averages; could seasonality, day-of-week, weather, occupancy, or short-term history be leveraged to make µ0 context-aware?
- Scalability: The CQP has O(T∑iKi + T∑iKi^2) variables. Please characterize runtime vs T and number of appliances, and whether specialized solvers exploiting block structure could scale to longer horizons (weeks) or more states. CVX may not be practical at larger scales.
- Robustness to model misspecification: States are set via k-means with K=3 for all appliances. Some loads are multi-modal or have continuous consumption; how sensitive are results to K and to misestimated σt?

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The optimization derivation is concise; adding a short algorithmic summary (inputs, outputs, constraints, solver, and any rounding) would aid reproducibility. The extension to multiple time-window constraints is a nice touch; a brief note on how these are added to the objective would help readers implement it.

Originality
Introducing SACs as global aggregate constraints for factorial HMMs, with a convex relaxation and a concrete application to NILM, is a useful and, to my knowledge, new contribution. The connection to posterior regularization is appropriate, but the instantiation to AFHMMs and the demonstration that such aggregate priors are both available and impactful in practice are the novel aspects.

Significance
The proposed idea is simple, broadly applicable to BSS, and yields meaningful improvements on a challenging, socially relevant task (low-frequency NILM). Because aggregate priors are common in science and engineering (e.g., total counts, energy budgets), this approach could be used beyond NILM (e.g., gene expression deconvolution, mixture-of-experts with budget constraints). The empirical gains over AFAMAP are notable.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated idea: global aggregate constraints that encode easy-to-obtain priors.
- Sound convex relaxation with a probabilistic interpretation via posterior regularization.
- Consistent empirical improvements on real, low-frequency NILM data; statistical tests provided.
- Practical and computationally comparable (at evaluated scales) to strong baselines.

Cons
- Dependence on aggregate priors; method may degrade if priors are substantially mis-specified (only partially explored).
- No systematic tuning/learning of λi; sensitivity and selection strategy are underdeveloped.
- Limited discussion of rounding/decoding and scalability to longer horizons/more appliances.
- Comparison to additional duration-constrained/semi-Markov NILM baselines would be informative.

Questions for the authors
1) How are fractional Sit handled at inference time for producing appliance-level traces? Any rounding/projection? Does it affect NDE/SAE?
2) How sensitive are results to λi and K per appliance? Could a hierarchical prior tie λi to estimated µ0 uncertainty?
3) For “with mains” data, how are unmodeled appliances handled? Is there a slack/noise term per time or an extra “other” chain?
4) Could you report runtime scaling with T and number of appliances, and whether first-order methods or specialized QP solvers improve efficiency?
5) Have you tried time-of-day SACs (your multi-interval extension) on HES (e.g., daytime-only toaster usage)?

Suggestions
- Learn or adaptively set µ0 from contextual features and recent history; consider a Bayesian treatment with uncertainty-weighted SACs.
- Provide open-source code or solver details and a small reproducible example.
- Explore combining SACs with duration models (semi-Markov) or AFAMAP’s change constraints.
- Investigate automatic rounding to feasible discrete sequences (e.g., projection via chainwise Viterbi initialized from the relaxed solution).

Overall evaluation
A well-executed and practically meaningful contribution that introduces a simple but powerful class of constraints for factorial HMMs, with solid derivation and promising empirical results. I recommend acceptance, contingent mainly on clarifying decoding, sensitivity to λi, and providing more detail on scalability.
