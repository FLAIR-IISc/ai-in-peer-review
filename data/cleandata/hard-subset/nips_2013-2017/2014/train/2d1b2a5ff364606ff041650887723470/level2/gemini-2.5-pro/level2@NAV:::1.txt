This paper addresses the problem of identifiability in blind source separation (BSS), particularly within the context of energy disaggregation using Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs). The main contribution is the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge. The core idea is that for many BSS problems, one has a reasonable prior estimate for the total aggregated signal of each source over a given time period. The authors incorporate this constraint into the AFHMM objective function and derive a tractable inference algorithm by relaxing the problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP). This work builds upon a line of research aimed at improving AFHMMs for disaggregation, but offers a distinct approach compared to prior methods like AFAMAP, which constrain state transitions, or semi-Markov models, which constrain state durations.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality & Significance:** The central idea of using a constraint on the total signal aggregate is both simple, intuitive, and novel. It provides a practical way to inject valuable domain knowledge into an otherwise underdetermined problem. The application to energy disaggregation is highly relevant, and the significant performance improvements over a strong, recent baseline (AFAMAP) demonstrate a clear advance in the state of the art. The idea is general enough to be applicable to other BSS domains.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the problem and its relaxation into a CQP are well-executed. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors validate their method on both toy data to show robustness and a challenging real-world dataset (HES). The use of both a realistic prior (National Total Consumption) and an oracle prior (True Total Consumption) is an excellent design choice that effectively bounds the method's potential and demonstrates its practical utility. Claims are well-supported with quantitative results and statistical significance tests.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the contributions are explicitly stated, and the methodology is presented logically.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The primary limitation is the dependence on the quality of the prior for the aggregate values (µi0). While the authors show some robustness, performance will naturally degrade with poor priors.
*   The choice of the complexity parameter λ is not deeply explored; it was set to 1 for all experiments. While the results are strong regardless, a brief sensitivity analysis or discussion on setting this hyperparameter would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Introduces a novel and impactful idea (SACs) for constraining BSS models.
2.  Demonstrates a significant, statistically verified improvement over state-of-the-art methods on a real-world task.
3.  Presents a technically sound and tractable inference algorithm.
4.  The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and the experiments are rigorous.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The hyperparameter selection for λ could be more principled, though this is a minor point.

This is a strong paper with a clear and significant contribution. I recommend acceptance.
