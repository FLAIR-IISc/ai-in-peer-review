

This paper proposes "signal aggregate constraints" (SACs) to address the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS), particularly applied to energy disaggregation. SACs encourage the total signal for each source to be close to a specified value, which the authors argue is often known in advance (e.g., average daily energy consumption of appliances). They incorporate SACs into an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) and formulate a convex quadratic program for approximate inference. The paper demonstrates significant improvements over the original AFHMM (45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error) and a recent state-of-the-art approach (29% improvement) on real-world energy disaggregation data.

Strengths: The paper addresses an important practical problem in energy disaggregation where identifiability is a major challenge. The SAC approach is conceptually simple yet effective, and the authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of the problem and a computationally efficient solution via convex quadratic programming. The experimental evaluation is thorough, including both synthetic toy data and real-world energy datasets. The comparison with AFHMM and AFAMAP is well-executed, and the statistical significance of the results is properly assessed.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks sufficient justification for why SACs are particularly well-suited for energy disaggregation compared to other constraint-based approaches. While they reference AFAMAP [12] which uses constraints, they don't clearly explain how SACs differ from or improve upon the constraints used in AFAMAP. The claim that "in many applications of BSS, the total signal often varies widely across the different unknown sources" is not substantiated with evidence beyond energy disaggregation. The paper also doesn't adequately address how sensitive the method is to the choice of constraint values μi0, despite showing some sensitivity in Figure 1 (toy data) but not in the real-world experiments. The authors use "true total consumption" (TTC) as an oracle constraint value in their experiments, which is unrealistic in practice, yet they don't discuss how the method would perform with less accurate constraint values.

The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) is interesting but feels tacked on rather than integrated into the main narrative. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how SACs relate to other constraint-based approaches in the literature, particularly those mentioned in Section 2. The authors claim SACs are "general across a wide variety of domains," but provide no evidence beyond energy disaggregation. This overstatement weakens the significance claim.

For clarity, the paper would benefit from a more concise introduction to the energy disaggregation problem, particularly clarifying why the identifiability issue is so critical for end users. The mathematical formulation in Section 5 is dense and could be made more accessible with additional intuitive explanations.

The paper makes a useful contribution to energy disaggregation by demonstrating that incorporating domain knowledge about total energy consumption can substantially improve disaggregation performance. However, the significance would be stronger if the authors provided more evidence that SACs would generalize to other BSS problems beyond energy disaggregation. The current evaluation is limited to one application domain.

The paper is technically sound with well-executed experiments, but the theoretical justification for SACs as a general approach to BSS is weak. The authors should clarify how SACs differ from previous constraint-based approaches in the literature, provide evidence for the general applicability of SACs beyond energy disaggregation, and more thoroughly address the sensitivity of their method to constraint values.

Constructive suggestions:
1. Provide a more detailed comparison with other constraint-based approaches (e.g., AFAMAP's constraints) to clarify the novelty of SACs.
2. Include a sensitivity analysis showing how performance degrades as the constraint values μi0 deviate from the true values in the real-world experiments.
3. Expand the discussion on how SACs might generalize to other BSS applications, perhaps with a brief example from another domain.
4. Clarify the relationship between SACs and posterior regularization in the main narrative rather than as a separate section.
5. Address the practical challenge of obtaining accurate constraint values in real-world settings, rather than relying on the unrealistic TTC.

Overall, this is a solid empirical contribution to energy disaggregation that demonstrates a practical way to incorporate domain knowledge. However, the paper's claims about the general applicability of SACs to BSS are overstated, and the theoretical justification is insufficient. With more careful comparison to related work and better substantiation of the general applicability claim, this paper would be a stronger contribution to NIPS.
