This paper addresses the problem of unidentifiability in blind source separation (BSS), focusing on the single-channel case where an observed time series is a sum of several hidden source signals. The authors propose a novel and intuitive method for incorporating domain knowledge called Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The core idea is that for many applications, one has prior knowledge about the expected total value (the aggregate) of each source signal over a given time period.

The authors incorporate SACs into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a standard model for this task, by adding a quadratic penalty to the MAP inference objective function. This penalty term encourages the aggregate of each inferred source signal to be close to a pre-specified value. Since this constraint breaks the Markov property of the model and makes exact inference via dynamic programming intractable, the authors formulate a convex relaxation of the MAP problem. This relaxation results in a convex quadratic program (CQP) which can be solved efficiently, building upon similar approximate inference techniques used for AFHMMs.

The proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on the important real-world problem of energy disaggregation. Using a large household electricity dataset, the paper demonstrates that AFHMM+SAC provides substantial improvements over both the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP [12].

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Simplicity:** The central idea of using signal aggregate constraints is novel, simple to understand, and powerful. It formalizes a type of domain knowledge that is often available in practice but has not been previously leveraged in this manner for AFHMM-based BSS. The connection drawn to the posterior regularization framework provides a solid theoretical grounding for the approach.

2.  **Significance and Practical Impact:** The paper tackles a challenging and practically relevant problem in energy disaggregation. The experimental results are convincing, showing a 29% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to a strong, recent baseline on real-world data with measured mains readings. This is a significant improvement that demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed method. The idea of SACs is also general and could likely be applied to BSS problems in other domains.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The technical development is sound. The formulation of the constrained MAP objective and its subsequent relaxation into a CQP is clear and well-executed. The experiments are thorough and well-designed, including a toy data experiment to show robustness, an oracle experiment to demonstrate the potential of the constraints, and, most importantly, a realistic evaluation using real mains data and priors derived from survey data.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The motivation for the problem is clear, the proposed solution is explained logically, and the results are presented effectively.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper sets the penalty parameter `λi = 1` for all experiments. While this simplifies the evaluation, the performance of the model is likely sensitive to this choice. A discussion on how these parameters could be set in practice (e.g., via cross-validation on a held-out set) would be beneficial. A brief sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with `λi` would further strengthen the paper's claims.

2.  **Quality of Priors:** The method's success hinges on the availability of reasonably accurate priors for the signal aggregates (`µi0`). The experiments show that national averages work well, but it would be useful to discuss the limitations of this assumption. For instance, how would the model perform for households with consumption patterns that deviate significantly from the national average? The toy data experiment provides some insight, but a more direct discussion in the context of the real-world application would be valuable.

3.  **Computational Complexity:** While the paper mentions that the CQP has polynomial-time complexity and that the runtime is similar to the baselines, a more formal analysis of the computational complexity would be a welcome addition. Specifically, detailing how the complexity scales with the number of time steps (T), sources (I), and states per source (K) would provide useful information for practitioners seeking to apply this method.

4.  **Presentation of CQP Formulation:** Section 5, which details the CQP formulation, is quite dense with notation. While correct, its readability could be improved. A brief intuitive explanation of the new variables (e.g., `Hit`) and the role of the Kronecker products could help readers more easily grasp the transformation from the penalized objective to the final CQP.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel, effective, and broadly applicable idea for improving blind source separation. The proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and backed by strong empirical evidence on a challenging real-world task. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed with additional discussion and analysis. The paper represents a significant contribution to the field and is a clear candidate for acceptance.
