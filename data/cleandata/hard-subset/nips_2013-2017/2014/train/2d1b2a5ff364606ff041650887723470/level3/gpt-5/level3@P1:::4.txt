This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation and integrates them into an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) to encourage each latent source’s total signal to match a known or estimated aggregate. The authors derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) for relaxed MAP inference by augmenting the AFHMM with transition-indicator variables and show that the SAC objective can also be seen as posterior regularization. Empirically, they apply the method to energy disaggregation (NILM) using large real-world data (UK HES): (i) 100 households with simulated mains by summing appliance channels and (ii) 9 households with true mains. They evaluate with NDE and SAE and report sizable improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP with similar computation time.

Relation to prior work: The AFHMM builds on Ghahramani & Jordan’s factorial HMMs and the NILM literature (Hart; reviews by Ziefman & Roth, Zoha et al.). The method is complementary to recent constraints for identifiability in NILM, including AFAMAP’s “at most one chain changes state per time” constraint (Kolter & Jaakkola), duration modeling via semi-Markov (Johnson & Willsky), and non-homogeneous variants. Conceptually, SACs instantiate a natural, task-driven constraint class and connect cleanly to measurement-based learning and posterior regularization (Liang et al., Ganchev et al.), but are tailored to the AFHMM with an efficient CQP relaxation.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling idea: many BSS/NILM tasks have reliable priors over total energy per source; encoding these aggregates is intuitive and widely applicable.
- Solid algorithmic framing: a convex relaxation with PSD structure; equivalence to posterior regularization clarifies the objective.
- Strong empirical evidence: large-scale evaluation on realistic, low-frequency data; consistent, statistically significant gains (e.g., ~33% NDE and ~16% SAE over AFAMAP with national total consumption; additional gains with true mains).
- Practical relevance: the constraints reflect deployable domain knowledge (national or household-level priors) and address identifiability.
- Runtime comparable to strong baselines.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Integrality/rounding: The CQP relaxes binary variables. Please clarify (i) how solutions are rounded to discrete states, (ii) the prevalence and magnitude of fractional Sit,Hit, (iii) whether performance gains persist after rounding, and (iv) whether SACs implicitly promote integral solutions (vs AFAMAP’s explicit structural constraint). A small study quantifying the integrality gap would strengthen the technical claims.
- Orthogonality to AFAMAP: AFAMAP’s one-change constraint and SACs address distinct failure modes. A combined AFAMAP+SAC baseline would be a very informative addition and likely stronger in practice.
- Sensitivity and source of µ0 and λ: Results use fixed λ=1 and two types of µ0 (NTC and TTC). Please (i) report sensitivity to λ, (ii) quantify robustness when µ0 is mis-specified (beyond the toy study), and (iii) discuss how µ0 would be estimated in a deployment (e.g., from covariates, household class, Bayesian priors with uncertainty).
- Scope of supervision and generality: Training assumes per-appliance sub-metered data to fit θ and k-means state levels with K=3. It would help to clarify to what extent the method benefits in fully unsupervised or weakly supervised settings (e.g., by combining SACs with unsupervised parameter learning), and whether increasing K changes results materially.
- Scaling and solver dependence: CVX is convenient but can be slow for very long sequences or many devices. Please report scaling behavior (T, I, K) and any specialized solvers or warm starts, and discuss feasibility for higher-frequency NILM.
- Model/algorithm nomenclature: At times the paper conflates models and inference procedures (e.g., AFHMM vs AFAMAP). Please be precise that AFAMAP is an approximate MAP algorithm for AFHMM with an additional structural constraint, while your AFHMM baseline appears to use chainwise/alternating Viterbi; make this explicit in the experimental section.
- Additional baselines/ablations: Consider a duration-constrained/semi-Markov baseline, and an ablation showing AFHMM+SAC vs AFHMM (λ=0) under the same relaxed inference to isolate the contribution of SACs vs the relaxation.

Quality
- Technically sound derivation with a clear convex relaxation and a principled connection to posterior regularization. The main open point is integrality/rounding and its effect on accuracy. Experiments are thorough and statistically supported, though adding the combined AFAMAP+SAC and sensitivity analyses would further bolster the case.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized with careful notation, but tightening the distinction between model and inference would improve readability. Please detail rounding and reconstruction of x̂ from fractional S, and provide more guidance on choosing µ0 and λ in practice.

Originality
- Moderate but meaningful. Aggregate constraints are a natural idea, yet their specific formulation, convex relaxation for AFHMM, and posterior-regularization view are nicely executed. The contribution is more pragmatic than theoretically novel, but appropriate for NIPS given the impact on a challenging real problem.

Significance
- High for NILM and potentially for other BSS applications where aggregate priors exist (audio source separation with known loudness budgets, biological time series with known totals). The empirical gains on a difficult, low-frequency dataset indicate practical value.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, general idea that directly addresses identifiability with domain knowledge.
- Principled and efficient inference via CQP; clear connection to posterior regularization.
- Convincing improvements on large, realistic datasets with statistical tests and similar runtime.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited analysis of the relaxation’s integrality and rounding effects.
- Missing combined baseline with AFAMAP or other structural constraints.
- Limited sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters and µ0 estimation, and reliance on CVX may limit scalability.

Minor comments
- Provide details on how fractional solutions are converted to discrete states/signals.
- Clarify whether multi-interval SACs (Section 4.2) were tried and their computational impact.
- State computing environment and per-instance runtimes vs problem size.
- Fix minor notation inconsistencies and ensure all matrices claimed PSD are explicitly justified.
