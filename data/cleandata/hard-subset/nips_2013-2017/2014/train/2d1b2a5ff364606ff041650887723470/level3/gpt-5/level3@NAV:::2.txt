Summary: The paper proposes an additive factorial HMM with signal aggregate constraints (AFHMM+SAC) for blind source separation, focusing on energy disaggregation from a single aggregate meter. The key idea is to inject domain knowledge about total per-source usage via soft constraints on aggregates (e.g., daily Wh per appliance). MAP inference under these constraints is relaxed to a convex quadratic program by introducing transition indicator variables, enabling efficient optimization with CVX. The approach is related to prior work on factorial HMMs and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12), and to constraint-based learning such as posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., JMLR’10) and generalized expectation (Mann & McCallum). Experiments on HES data show substantial improvements in normalized disaggregation error and aggregate error over AFHMM and AFAMAP, with additional oracle results demonstrating the potential upper bound when true aggregates are known.

Quality: The modeling idea is sound and well-motivated by identifiability. The relaxation appears correct (quadratic terms are negative semidefinite in the maximization, giving a concave objective) and the additional variables ensure linear constraints. However, the paper fixes λi=1 without tuning and provides limited sensitivity analysis beyond a small toy; more systematic study (e.g., cross-validation, robustness to misspecified µ0, per-appliance ablations) would strengthen claims. Scalability claims based on CVX warrants more careful reporting (problem sizes, solver settings), and a specialized solver could be discussed.

Clarity: Overall quality is good but the manuscript is very hard to read. There is excessive use of acronyms (some unusually long), and many are introduced before being needed. Consider defining only the essential ones, expanding them in figure/table captions, and moving heavy notation to an appendix with an intuitive running example in the main text. Also, explicitly state whether the SACs are imposed as a priori constraints (priors) versus direct posterior constraints; the current text alternates between a prior interpretation (Section 4.2) and posterior regularization (Section 6). Please choose one and be explicit that the a priori constraint indirectly shapes the posterior.

Originality: Using aggregate, cross-time constraints as soft priors within factorial HMMs is a novel and practically meaningful contribution distinct from “single-switch” constraints, k-segment HMMs, or duration models.

Significance: NILM is a long-standing NIPS topic (graphical models, structured prediction). The reported gains over AFAMAP on realistic low-frequency data are promising and likely to influence follow-on work in constrained inference for BSS.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, general idea (aggregate priors) addressing identifiability in BSS.
- Principled optimization via a convex relaxation.
- Empirical improvements on a challenging dataset.

Cons against acceptance:
- Readability issues and acronym overload.
- Ambiguity about whether constraints are on priors vs posteriors.
- Limited sensitivity/scalability analysis; λ selection and robustness need deeper treatment.
- Baselines could include more recent or non-HMM NILM methods.
