

This paper proposes Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs), addressing the identifiability problem in blind source separation for energy disaggregation. SACs constrain the total signal of each source to be close to specified values (e.g., expected daily appliance energy usage), incorporating domain knowledge into the model. The authors formulate a convex quadratic program for inference and demonstrate significant improvements over baseline AFHMM (45% lower normalized disaggregation error) and a recent state-of-the-art approach (29% improvement) on real-world energy data.

The paper makes a strong technical contribution by connecting SACs to posterior regularization (Section 6) and providing a theoretically sound approach to a challenging identifiability problem. The empirical results are compelling, with clear demonstration of SACs' value across multiple energy disaggregation scenarios (Sections 7.2.1-7.2.2). The work extends prior constraints like AFAMAP [12] by introducing a more domain-aware approach that leverages aggregate signal knowledge.

However, the paper suffers from severe readability issues that undermine its technical merits. The manuscript uses excessive acronyms (e.g., SAC, AFHMM, NDE, SAE, NTC, TTC, HES), with some reaching 11+ letters, and introduces them without sufficient explanation. This makes the paper difficult to follow, especially for readers unfamiliar with the specific energy disaggregation literature. Additionally, the authors place the constraint on the prior rather than directly on the posterior, failing to explicitly clarify that this constitutes an a priori constraint that indirectly constrains the posterior (Section 4.2).

The paper's quality is strong technically (sound theory, thorough evaluation), but its clarity is poor. The significance is evident for energy disaggregation applications, and the approach shows clear originality compared to prior work (e.g., [12, 21]). 

Pro: Novel constraint approach, strong empirical results, theoretical connection to posterior regularization.  
Con: Poor readability due to excessive acronyms, ambiguous constraint placement, difficult manuscript organization.

Given the paper's strong technical contribution and significant empirical improvements, I recommend acceptance, but the authors must substantially improve clarity and fix the constraint explanation before publication.
