## Review of “Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation”

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a method to incorporate domain knowledge into Blind Source Separation (BSS) problems, specifically applying it to energy disaggregation using an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM). The core idea is to encourage the total signal for each source to be close to a pre-defined value, leveraging prior knowledge about expected energy consumption for different appliances. The authors formulate this as a constraint on the optimization problem and propose a convex quadratic program for approximate inference. Experimental results on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset demonstrate significant performance improvements over the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach. 

This work builds upon a rich history of research in BSS and energy disaggregation. The AFHMM itself is a well-established model [17], with numerous extensions proposed to address identifiability issues [12, 21, 11]. The authors appropriately position their contribution as a means to incorporate *what* type of prior knowledge to use, building on broader frameworks like posterior regularization [4] and measurement-based learning [13]. The problem of energy disaggregation is also well-contextualized, referencing key surveys and prior work [8, 22, 15].

The strengths of this paper lie in its clear motivation, novel constraint formulation, and strong empirical results. The SACs offer an intuitive way to leverage domain expertise, and the experimental improvements – a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error and a 29% improvement over a recent SOTA method – are substantial. The application to a real-world dataset is a major plus. 

However, the paper suffers significantly from a lack of clarity. The excessive and often long acronyms (e.g., AFHMM+SAC, TTC, NTC) make the text dense and difficult to follow. More importantly, the authors state they are placing the constraint on the prior, but then discuss constraining the posterior. This should be explicitly clarified. The formulation of SACs as an *a priori* constraint impacting the posterior distribution needs a clearer explanation. While the CQP formulation is a technical achievement, the description feels rushed and could benefit from more detailed explanation. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Novel and effective approach to incorporating domain knowledge in BSS.
* Strong empirical results on a challenging real-world dataset.
* Addresses a critical issue in energy disaggregation: identifiability.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
* Extremely poor readability due to excessive acronyms.
* Lack of clarity regarding the precise placement of the constraint (prior vs. posterior).
* The derivation of the CQP and explanation of the relaxed problem could be more thorough. 



Overall, despite the strong technical contribution, the significant readability issues hinder a strong recommendation. I recommend **conditional acceptance** contingent on substantial improvements to clarity and a more precise articulation of the constraints. The authors should prioritize reducing acronym usage and clearly explaining the role of the constraint in the inference process.
