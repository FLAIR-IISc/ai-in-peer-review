This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation and integrates them into an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM), yielding AFHMM+SAC. The key idea is to encode domain knowledge about the total (e.g., daily) usage of each latent source, encouraging each chain’s aggregate to match a provided target. Because these constraints are non-Markovian, the authors derive a convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation for MAP inference by introducing auxiliary transition variables and relaxing the binary constraints. They also show the equivalence to posterior regularization. Empirically, on a toy example and on the HES energy-disaggregation dataset, AFHMM+SAC improves normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) over AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola), with large gains when constraint targets are accurate.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on factorial HMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan), AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola), semi-Markov NILM models (Johnson & Willsky; Parson et al.), change-at-one-chain constraints (Zhong et al.), and general frameworks for incorporating constraints (posterior regularization, generalized expectation, measurement-based learning). Unlike AFAMAP’s transition-structure constraints, SACs target global aggregates, a common and valuable form of domain knowledge in energy disaggregation and other BSS tasks. The convex relaxation follows prior relaxed formulations but is tailored to the aggregate penalties.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The relaxation and auxiliary variables are standard; the quadratic terms constructed from σ−2 μ μ^T are PSD, so maximizing a concave quadratic subject to linear constraints gives a convex program after relaxation. The link to posterior regularization is correct and clarifies the probabilistic meaning of the penalties. One missing detail is how relaxed solutions are rounded back to valid discrete sequences while preserving transition consistency; a description of decoding (e.g., projection or argmax with feasibility repair) and its effect on accuracy would strengthen the method section.
- Empirical evaluation: Results on both synthetic and real data are promising. However, comparisons may be unfair: AFHMM+SAC uses additional side information (the NTC or even oracle TTC) that AFHMM/AFAMAP do not. A more equitable baseline would give the same aggregate knowledge to competing methods (e.g., posterior-regularized AFAMAP or an AFHMM with aggregate priors but without the CQP relaxation) even if less efficient. It would also help to report performance as a function of constraint misspecification and to include confidence intervals.
- Robustness: The toy experiment suggests performance degrades as constraints become inaccurate; in particular, when the distance between μ0 and the true aggregate is large (e.g., >1e4), AFAMAP can surpass AFHMM+SAC. The paper should temper claims that SACs “dramatically improve” performance and clearly articulate conditions under which they help or harm.
- Scalability: Solving a CQP with CVX is convenient but may be slow for many chains/time steps; a brief complexity analysis with empirical runtimes and a discussion of specialized solvers (e.g., ADMM) would be useful.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and well motivated. The notation around μi (state means) and μi0 (aggregate target) could be made clearer (perhaps using a distinct symbol for aggregates). Figure 1’s axes and baselines need clarification; if AFAMAP is included in the toy plot, state that explicitly and explain the crossover. Describe the rounding step from relaxed variables to discrete sequences.
- Organization and references are appropriate; the connection to posterior regularization is a strong clarifying piece.

Originality:
- Introducing aggregate constraints as a generally useful and portable prior for BSS is a nice idea, distinct from existing transition/duration constraints. The CQP relaxation is a careful adaptation of known tools to a new constraint family. The extension to multiple time-window constraints is a useful generalization.

Significance:
- In NILM, totals are often what matters to users; directly constraining aggregates is practically impactful. The reported gains on low-frequency HES data are encouraging and suggest broader applicability (audio, biology). That said, significance hinges on robustness to imperfect constraints and fair comparisons when all methods are given the same side information.

Pros:
- Simple, well-motivated constraint that reflects real domain knowledge.
- Solid mathematical treatment and link to posterior regularization.
- Consistent empirical gains in NDE/SAE on synthetic and real data.
- Applicable beyond NILM.

Cons:
- Comparisons may be unfair; baselines don’t use the same aggregate information.
- Sensitivity to misspecified constraints; in some regimes AFAMAP can outperform AFHMM+SAC.
- Missing details on rounding/projection from relaxed solutions.
- Limited exploration of richer constraint classes beyond single-chain aggregates.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide baselines that also exploit aggregate information: e.g., posterior-regularized AFAMAP; AFHMM with aggregate penalties but solved by message passing with auxiliary Lagrange multipliers; or nonnegative matrix factorization with aggregate constraints.
- Quantify robustness: sweep errors in μ0, report the breakpoint where AFHMM+SAC ceases to help, include confidence intervals/paired tests for all plots.
- Evaluate additional constraint types suggested by the application: mutual exclusivity among appliances sharing a circuit, cardinality limits on the number of simultaneously active appliances, minimum/maximum duration constraints, and time-window aggregates (day/night), as briefly noted in Section 4.2.
- Detail the rounding procedure and its effect on feasibility and metrics.
- Discuss scalability and, if possible, provide a more efficient solver or open-source implementation.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a clear, useful idea with solid theory and promising results. Addressing fairness of comparisons and robustness to misspecification would strengthen the contribution and its impact.
