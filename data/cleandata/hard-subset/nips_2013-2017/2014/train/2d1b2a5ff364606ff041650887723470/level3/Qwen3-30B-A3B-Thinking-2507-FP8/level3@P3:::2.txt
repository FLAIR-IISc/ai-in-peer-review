

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation using Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs). SACs constrain the total signal for each source to be close to a specified value (e.g., knowing a toaster uses ~50 Wh/day), addressing the identifiability problem inherent in BSS. The authors incorporate SACs into AFHMMs by formulating a convex quadratic program for approximate inference, and demonstrate significant improvements on real-world energy disaggregation data.

The paper builds on previous work in energy disaggregation, including AFHMM (Ghahramani & Jordan, 1997) and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012), which constrain state transitions. It also relates to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2010) and generalized expectation criterion (Liang et al., 2009), but focuses on a specific constraint type that can be efficiently incorporated. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from prior work by focusing on aggregate signal constraints rather than state transition constraints.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound, with a well-motivated convex relaxation for inference. The experimental results are thorough, showing a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM and a 29% improvement over AFAMAP on real-world energy data. The theoretical connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) is a strength. However, the paper could have provided more theoretical guarantees about the convex relaxation. A key issue is that the authors place the constraint on the prior rather than directly on the posterior, and should explicitly state it is an a priori constraint (not a posterior constraint), which would clarify the probabilistic interpretation.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is difficult to read due to excessive use of acronyms. The paper introduces SACs (Signal Aggregate Constraints), but then uses "SAC" extensively. More problematic are acronyms like "AFHMM+SAC" (11 characters), "NTC" (National Total Consumption), "TTC" (True Total Consumption), "NDE" (Normalized Disaggregation Error), and "SAE" (Signal Aggregate Error), which are all introduced without sufficient context. The mathematical notation is dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations. A significant portion of the paper is devoted to technical details that could be better presented to improve readability.

**Originality:** The SAC concept is novel in this context, and the paper clearly differentiates itself from previous approaches. The authors properly cite related work, though the connection to posterior regularization could be more explicitly articulated. The problem formulation addresses a gap in prior work regarding what types of domain knowledge should be incorporated into BSS models.

**Significance:** Energy disaggregation is an important practical problem with real-world applications. The results show substantial improvements over state-of-the-art methods, suggesting the SAC approach could be valuable for practitioners. The method could potentially be applied to other BSS problems beyond energy disaggregation, enhancing its broader significance.

**Strengths:**
- Clear problem statement addressing a real-world challenge (identifiability in BSS)
- Novel constraint type (SACs) that addresses a practical knowledge gap
- Strong experimental results showing significant performance gains
- Theoretical connection to posterior regularization framework
- Well-motivated convex optimization approach

**Weaknesses:**
- Excessive use of acronyms (including unusually long ones) significantly harms readability
- Ambiguous constraint placement (on prior rather than posterior, without explicit clarification)
- Dense mathematical presentation that would benefit from more intuitive explanations
- Limited discussion of when SACs would be applicable versus other constraint types

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with significant practical results, but the manuscript's poor readability due to acronym proliferation is a major issue. If the authors were to significantly revise the manuscript to reduce acronyms, clarify the constraint placement as an a priori constraint, and improve the presentation of mathematical concepts, this would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As written, the clarity issues substantially diminish the impact of an otherwise good technical contribution.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the energy disaggregation literature and demonstrates how domain knowledge can be effectively incorporated into probabilistic models. While the core technical contribution is strong, the presentation issues prevent the paper from reaching its full potential.
