The paper "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation" proposes an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) that incorporates signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for energy usage. The model aims to address the issue of identifiability in blind source separation problems by incorporating domain knowledge.

The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, discussing the challenges of blind source separation and the need for incorporating domain knowledge. They also provide a thorough review of related work in the field.

The proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, is well-motivated and theoretically sound. The authors provide a detailed derivation of the model and its optimization problem, which is solved using a convex quadratic program. The use of SACs is shown to improve the performance of the AFHMM on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset.

The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear headings and concise sections. However, the manuscript is very hard to read due to the excessive use of acronyms, some of which are unusually long (≈11 letters). This harms readability and makes it difficult for non-experts to understand the paper.

The authors appear to place the constraint on the prior rather than directly on the posterior, which should be explicitly stated as an a priori constraint, thereby constraining the posterior. This is a minor point, but it would improve the clarity of the paper.

Overall, the paper is a good scientific contribution to the field of energy disaggregation and blind source separation. The use of SACs is a novel and effective way to incorporate domain knowledge into the AFHMM, and the results on the real-world dataset are impressive.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and effective way to incorporate domain knowledge into the AFHMM for energy disaggregation.
* The results on the real-world dataset are impressive, showing a significant improvement over the original AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach.
* The paper is well-motivated and theoretically sound, with a clear and concise introduction to the problem and a thorough review of related work.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The manuscript is very hard to read due to the excessive use of acronyms.
* The authors appear to place the constraint on the prior rather than directly on the posterior, which should be explicitly stated.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Reduce the use of acronyms and provide clear explanations for each acronym used.
* Explicitly state that the constraint is placed on the prior rather than directly on the posterior.
* Consider providing more details on the computational complexity of the proposed model and its optimization problem.
