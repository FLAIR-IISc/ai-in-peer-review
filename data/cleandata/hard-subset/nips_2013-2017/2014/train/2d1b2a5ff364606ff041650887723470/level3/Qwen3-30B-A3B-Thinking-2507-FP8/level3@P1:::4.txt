

The paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) to address identifiability in blind source separation (BSS), specifically extending Additive Factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) for energy disaggregation. SACs enforce that the total signal for each source (e.g., appliance energy usage) aligns with domain-specified aggregate values (e.g., national average consumption), a natural constraint often available in real-world applications. This differs from prior work on posterior regularization [4] or AFAMAP [12] (which enforces single-chain state changes), as SACs target global signal aggregates rather than local state transitions. The authors formulate SACs as convex quadratic programs (CQPs) for efficient MAP inference, avoiding the intractability of exact inference under non-Markovian constraints. On the UK Household Electricity Survey (HES) dataset (100 homes, low-frequency data), SAC-AFHMM achieves 33% lower normalized disaggregation error (NDE) than AFAMAP (state-of-the-art) and 45% lower than baseline AFHMM, with statistically significant improvements (paired t-tests, p<0.05). The method also demonstrates robustness to moderate errors in aggregate specifications (Figure 1).

Strengths are substantial. The empirical validation on large-scale, real-world data (100+ households) is compelling and addresses a critical practical problem in smart energy monitoring. The 33% NDE improvement over AFAMAP represents a meaningful advance for a field where marginal gains are common. The CQP formulation is technically sound, leveraging convex optimization for tractability while maintaining the core probabilistic model. The paper clearly articulates SACs as a *new class* of constraints (Section 4), distinct from prior approaches, and demonstrates their value through both toy data (Section 7.1) and real applications (Section 7.2). The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) adds theoretical grounding without overcomplicating the contribution.

Key weaknesses require clarification for acceptance. First, the paper conflates model and inference procedures. AFHMM is a model; AFAMAP is an *approximate inference algorithm* for it [12]. Table 1 and Section 7.2 refer to "AFHMM+SAC" results, but the method actually uses AFAMAP's inference (as stated in Section 5: "a convex quadratic program for a relaxed version of the MAP problem"). This mislabeling undermines precision—AFHMM+SAC is the *model*, but inference uses AFAMAP-like techniques. The authors must clarify terminology throughout (e.g., "AFHMM+SAC with AFAMAP inference" or "SAC-AFHMM"). Second, SAC-AFHMM uses a *relaxed* CQP (S_it ∈ [0,1]), unlike AFAMAP's combinatorial constraint that yields integer solutions. The paper does not address whether non-integral solutions degrade practical utility (e.g., for appliance energy estimates). A brief analysis of solution integrality (e.g., percentage of states near 0/1) would strengthen claims. Third, SACs and AFAMAP’s single-chain constraint are orthogonal; combining them (e.g., SAC-AFHMM+AFAMAP) could yield further gains. The authors note this as a future direction but omit it as a simple experiment for the current version.

The significance is high: SACs provide a plug-and-play mechanism to inject domain knowledge into BSS, with clear applicability beyond energy disaggregation (e.g., audio source separation). The paper avoids overclaiming—SACs are a constraint *class*, not a complete model—yet demonstrate tangible gains. The quality is strong: the CQP derivation (Section 5) is rigorous, experiments are well-designed (NTC vs. TTC baselines), and computational cost remains comparable to AFAMAP (Table 1). Clarity is mostly good, though the model/inference confusion needs fixing. Originality is evident: SACs are a novel constraint type not covered in prior NIPS work (e.g., [4, 12, 21, 24]).

Pro/Con Summary for Acceptance:  
**Pro**: Significant empirical gains on large-scale real data; novel constraint class addressing a key BSS limitation; clean convex optimization solution; strong theoretical connection to posterior regularization.  
**Con**: Terminology errors (model vs. inference), unaddressed integrality of solutions, and missed opportunity to combine SACs with AFAMAP’s constraint.

The paper is a strong contribution to NIPS’ focus on principled, scalable methods for real-world problems. The minor clarifications requested (terminology precision, integrality analysis) are easily addressed. The results convincingly demonstrate SACs’ value: they transform a fundamentally unidentifiable problem into a practical tool for energy disaggregation, advancing the state of the art with minimal added complexity. I recommend acceptance.
