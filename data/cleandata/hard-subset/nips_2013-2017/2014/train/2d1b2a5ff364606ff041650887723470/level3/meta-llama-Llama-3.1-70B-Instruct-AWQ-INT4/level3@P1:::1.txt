The paper "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation" proposes a novel approach to blind source separation (BSS) problems by incorporating domain knowledge into the problem. The authors introduce signal aggregate constraints (SACs) that encourage the total signal for each of the unknown sources to be close to a specified value. This approach is particularly useful in applications where the total signal varies widely across the unknown sources, and prior knowledge about the expected total values is available.

The authors incorporate SACs into an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) to formulate the energy disaggregation problem, where only one mixture signal is assumed to be observed. They transform the optimization problem into a convex quadratic program, which is computationally efficient. The proposed approach is evaluated on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset and compared to a recent state-of-the-art approach.

The strengths of the paper include:

1. Novel approach: The paper proposes a new approach to BSS problems by incorporating domain knowledge through SACs.
2. Theoretical soundness: The authors provide a solid theoretical foundation for their approach, including a convex quadratic program for the relaxed AFHMM with SACs.
3. Experimental evaluation: The paper includes a thorough experimental evaluation on a real-world dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4. Comparison to state-of-the-art: The authors compare their approach to a recent state-of-the-art method, showing significant improvements in performance.

However, there are also some weaknesses and areas for improvement:

1. Toy experiment: The toy experiment has a very low noise variance, which may not be realistic. The authors should clarify whether this is a realistic scenario or if it was chosen to make the separation easy.
2. SNR discussion: The authors should provide a discussion on the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) for the mains vs. aggregate appliance signals.
3. Theoretical proof: The authors should provide a formal proof that the procedure in equation (6) is convex.
4. Complexity analysis: The authors should provide a theoretical scaling/complexity analysis (data length, number of hidden states) and compare the tractability of their approach to AFAMAP for larger datasets.
5. Notation issue: There is a minor notation issue with extra commas in subscripts before p on page 4 (line 202).

Overall, the paper presents a novel and original approach to BSS problems, and the authors have addressed reviewer queries satisfactorily in their feedback. However, some minor issues and areas for improvement remain.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to BSS problems, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The authors provide a thorough experimental evaluation on a real-world dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.
* The paper includes a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed approach.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The toy experiment has a very low noise variance, which may not be realistic.
* The authors should provide a formal proof that the procedure in equation (6) is convex.
* The paper lacks a theoretical scaling/complexity analysis and comparison to AFAMAP for larger datasets.

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address the issues mentioned above.
