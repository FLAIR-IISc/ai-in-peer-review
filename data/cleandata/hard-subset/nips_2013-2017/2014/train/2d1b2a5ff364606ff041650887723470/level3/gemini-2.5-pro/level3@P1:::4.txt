### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation problems, specifically within the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework. The core idea is that for many source separation tasks, one has prior knowledge about the expected total aggregate value of each individual source signal over a given time period. The authors incorporate this prior as a quadratic penalty term in the AFHMM's MAP inference objective. Since this penalty breaks the Markov property and makes exact inference intractable, the authors propose solving a convex relaxation of the problem via a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP). The primary application and evaluation are in the domain of energy disaggregation, where the goal is to separate a household's total electricity consumption into the consumption of individual appliances. The proposed AFHMM+SAC model is evaluated on a large, real-world dataset of UK homes and shown to significantly outperform both a baseline AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP algorithm [12].

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles the important and challenging problem of energy disaggregation. The proposed method provides a practical and effective way to address the inherent unidentifiability in blind source separation by leveraging readily available domain knowledge (e.g., average daily consumption of an appliance). The strong empirical results on a large-scale, low-frequency, real-world dataset suggest that this approach could have a tangible impact in this applied field.

2.  **Originality:** The introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints is a simple, intuitive, and novel form of regularization for AFHMMs. While other constraints have been proposed, such as the sparsity-of-change constraint in AFAMAP [12], SACs are fundamentally different and complementary. They constrain the global behavior of a source over a long time horizon, rather than its local, step-to-step dynamics. The connection to posterior regularization [4] also provides a nice theoretical grounding for the approach.

3.  **Quality of Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is a key strength of this work. The authors use a large dataset (100 households from the HES survey) and test their method in two settings: one with synthetically aggregated mains signals and a more realistic and difficult one using actual measured mains signals from 9 homes. The demonstrated performance improvements over AFAMAP—a strong and recent baseline—are substantial and statistically significant. The inclusion of an oracle experiment using the true total consumption (TTC) effectively bounds the potential benefit of the SACs and highlights their value.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on Baselines and Inference:** The paper could be more precise in its terminology. For instance, AFAMAP [12] is presented as a competing model, but it is more accurately described as an approximate inference algorithm for the AFHMM that introduces specific constraints into the optimization problem. The paper's "AFHMM" baseline presumably uses the CQP relaxation with λi = 0, as mentioned in Section 5, but this should be stated explicitly for clarity. A brief clarification that AFHMM is the underlying probabilistic model, while AFAMAP and AFHMM+SAC are different constrained inference objectives for that model, would improve precision.

2.  **Analysis of the CQP Solution:** A valuable piece of analysis is missing. The authors of AFAMAP note that their "one state change at a time" constraint often encourages the relaxed variables to take on more integral values. It would be very insightful to know if the SACs have a similar effect. Does the proposed method achieve its lower error by finding solutions that are "more integral" (i.e., closer to a valid discrete state assignment), or does it succeed even with highly fractional solutions? A short discussion or empirical analysis of the integrality of the CQP solutions would strengthen the paper's contribution.

3.  **Orthogonality of Constraints:** The SACs are largely orthogonal to the constraints used in AFAMAP. SACs constrain the global sum, while AFAMAP constrains local state transitions. This orthogonality presents a clear and exciting opportunity to combine them. It seems straightforward to incorporate the SAC penalty term into the AFAMAP optimization problem. Evaluating an "AFAMAP+SAC" model would be a very compelling addition to the paper. If such a combination yields further improvements, it would significantly strengthen the claim that SACs are a valuable and complementary tool for this class of problems. This experiment is strongly encouraged for the final version of the paper.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a simple, novel, and well-motivated idea for improving a widely used model class (AFHMMs).
*   The proposed method is validated with a thorough and convincing empirical study on a challenging, large-scale, real-world problem.
*   The performance gains over a strong, state-of-the-art baseline (AFAMAP) are significant and practically meaningful.
*   The work represents a successful and compelling combination of an algorithmic contribution with a high-impact application, which is a good fit for the NIPS community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The core technical idea, while effective, is a relatively straightforward extension of the existing AFHMM CQP inference framework.
*   The paper misses the opportunity to provide a deeper analysis of the method's properties (e.g., solution integrality) and to explore the obvious combination with complementary state-of-the-art constraints from AFAMAP.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a simple but effective idea with strong empirical backing. The main strengths lie in the novelty of the proposed constraints and the quality of the experimental evaluation on a difficult real-world task. While the paper could be improved by addressing the points raised above—particularly by clarifying the relationship with AFAMAP and exploring a combined approach—the current contribution is sound, interesting, and impactful enough to warrant acceptance.
