This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework for blind source separation, specifically applied to energy disaggregation. The core idea – incorporating domain knowledge about expected total energy consumption for different appliances – is a valuable contribution to addressing the inherent identifiability issues in this field. The authors formulate a convex quadratic program for approximate inference, demonstrating significant improvements over the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach (AFAMAP) on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in blind source separation (BSS) [3, 17, 2] and energy disaggregation [8, 12, 22]. It appropriately contextualizes itself within related work on incorporating prior constraints into probabilistic models [4, 13, 14], highlighting the novel aspect of *identifying* useful classes of constraints, rather than simply *how* to incorporate them. The mathematical formulation is sound, and the derivation of the convex quadratic program is clearly presented. The problem motivation is excellent; the challenges of energy disaggregation are well articulated, and the need for methods that provide interpretable results (avoiding “franken-appliances”) is convincingly argued.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The introduction of SACs as a principled way to inject domain knowledge into BSS, particularly within the AFHMM framework, is a novel and potentially impactful contribution.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on the HES dataset demonstrate a clear and substantial improvement in disaggregation performance (reduced NDE and SAE) compared to strong baselines.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation and derivation of the convex quadratic program are rigorous and well-explained.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The application to energy disaggregation addresses a practically important problem with significant societal impact.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Baseline Comparison:** The experimental setup raises concerns about a potentially unfair comparison. The authors mention estimating parameters using 15-30 days of data for AFHMM+SAC, whereas the baseline methods appear to use less data. This difference could significantly impact the results, potentially inflating the apparent gains from using SACs. A fairer comparison would involve using the same amount of training data for all methods.
*   **Overstated Generality:** While the results are compelling, the claim that SACs "dramatically improve" the original AFHMM and "significantly improve" over AFAMAP needs careful consideration. Figure 1 reveals that, for larger distances between the constraint value (µ0) and the true aggregate signal, AFAMAP actually *outperforms* SAC-AFHMM. This suggests that poorly chosen constraints can *worsen* performance, undermining the claim that SACs always help.
*   **Limited Exploration of Constraint Types:** The paper focuses on a single type of aggregate constraint (total energy consumption). While a reasonable starting point, the authors acknowledge the potential for more complex constraints (e.g., time-of-day constraints, exclusivity). Exploring these extensions would strengthen the work.
*   **Computational Cost:** Although the authors state the computational cost is similar to baseline methods, more detailed analysis could be useful, especially given the increased complexity of the optimization problem.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in blind source separation – identifiability – with a novel and well-motivated approach.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear benefit of incorporating SACs in the energy disaggregation domain.
*   The technical contributions (convex quadratic program formulation) are significant and provide a practical means for implementing the proposed method.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental setup raises concerns about the fairness of the comparison with baseline methods.
*   The claim that SACs always improve performance is not fully supported by the results (Figure 1).
*   The exploration of potential extensions and more complex constraint types is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution and promising results. While the concerns regarding the baseline comparison and the overstated generality of the improvement should be addressed, the overall quality of the work is high. I believe this paper represents a significant step forward in the field of energy disaggregation and BSS.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** The authors should clarify the experimental setup to ensure a fairer comparison with baseline methods, revise the claims regarding the benefits of SACs to reflect the limitations observed in Figure 1, and discuss potential extensions to more complex constraint types in the conclusion. Exploring alternative methods for integrating SACs, even if less computationally efficient, to demonstrate robustness, would also strengthen the paper. Furthermore, discussing the sensitivity to the choice of the δ parameter in equation (4) would be beneficial.
