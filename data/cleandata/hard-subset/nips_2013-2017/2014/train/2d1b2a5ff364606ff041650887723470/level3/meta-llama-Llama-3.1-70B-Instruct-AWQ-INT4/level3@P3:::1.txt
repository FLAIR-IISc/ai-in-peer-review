This paper proposes an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) with signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation, specifically in the context of energy disaggregation. The authors introduce SACs, which encourage the total signal for each unknown source to be close to a specified value, as a way to incorporate domain knowledge into the problem. This approach is based on the observation that the total signal often varies widely across unknown sources, and prior knowledge of expected total values can be used to improve the separation.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem, the proposed approach, and the related work. The introduction provides a good overview of the blind source separation problem and its challenges, and the authors effectively motivate the need for incorporating domain knowledge into the problem.

The proposed AFHMM+SAC model is a natural extension of the AFHMM, and the authors provide a clear derivation of the model and its parameters. The use of SACs is a novel contribution, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in improving the performance of the AFHMM on both simulated and real-world data.

The experimental results are impressive, with the AFHMM+SAC outperforming both the AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP algorithm on energy disaggregation tasks. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of the proposed approach, including a comparison of the normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) metrics.

The paper also provides a good discussion of the related work, including a comparison with other approaches that have been proposed for energy disaggregation. The authors effectively highlight the strengths and weaknesses of their approach and provide a clear explanation of how it differs from other methods.

One potential weakness of the paper is the lack of a formal proof that the procedure in equation (6) is convex. The paper could also benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed approach. While the authors provide some discussion of the computational complexity, they do not provide a detailed analysis of how the approach scales with the number of hidden states and the length of the data.

With some minor revisions to address the issues mentioned above, this paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.
