

This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation (BSS) problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation using additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs). The authors observe that in many BSS applications, the total signal for each source varies significantly and often has known expected values, and propose incorporating these as constraints to address identifiability issues. SACs are integrated into AFHMMs by adding a convex quadratic regularization term to the MAP inference objective, transforming it into a solvable convex quadratic program. The approach is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world energy disaggregation data, showing significant improvements over the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method (AFAMAP).

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the energy disaggregation literature, which has been a recurring topic at NIPS (e.g., [8, 12, 15, 22]). While previous work has explored various constraints for AFHMMs (e.g., AFAMAP's single-chain state change constraint [12], non-homogeneous HMMs [21], semi-Markov models [10, 11]), SACs represent a novel class of constraints based on aggregate signal properties rather than temporal dynamics. The connection to posterior regularization [4] provides theoretical grounding for the approach, though the paper could more explicitly position SACs within this framework.

Strengths:
1. The problem addressed is significant - energy disaggregation remains a challenging real-world problem with important applications in energy conservation. The paper demonstrates clear improvements over strong baselines (33% reduction in NDE over AFAMAP on real data).
2. The technical approach is sound - the convex quadratic program formulation for inference is well-justified and computationally efficient. The theoretical connection to posterior regularization [4] provides a solid foundation.
3. The evaluation is thorough - the authors conduct experiments on both synthetic data (to demonstrate robustness to constraint specification errors) and real-world energy data from the Household Electricity Survey (HES), using appropriate metrics (NDE and SAE).
4. The application to energy disaggregation is particularly compelling, with SACs effectively leveraging domain knowledge about typical appliance energy consumption.

Weaknesses:
1. Clarity is severely compromised by excessive and unnecessarily long acronyms (e.g., "AFHMM+SAC", "NTC", "TTC" - some exceeding 11 letters). This significantly hinders readability, especially for readers unfamiliar with the specific domain. The paper would benefit from defining acronyms more judiciously and using more intuitive notation.
2. The paper's presentation is dense and difficult to follow. The mathematical formulation in Section 5 is particularly challenging, with numerous new variables introduced without clear motivation. A more intuitive explanation of the CQP transformation would greatly improve accessibility.
3. The authors state SACs "encourage the aggregate values... to be close to some specified values" but then describe them as "a prior" in the posterior regularization section. This is confusing - SACs are a priori constraints, not posterior regularizers. The paper should explicitly clarify that these are constraints imposed on the prior distribution, thereby constraining the posterior, rather than being directly part of the posterior.
4. The paper claims SACs "dramatically improve" the original AFHMM, but the comparison with AFAMAP is the most significant improvement (29% better than AFAMAP). The paper should clarify that SACs are primarily complementary to existing constraints rather than replacing them.

Quality: The technical approach is sound and well-justified, with appropriate theoretical grounding and thorough experimental evaluation. The results are convincing and the improvements are statistically significant.

Clarity: The manuscript suffers from poor readability due to excessive acronyms, dense mathematical presentation, and lack of intuitive explanations. This significantly impedes understanding of the core contributions.

Originality: SACs represent a novel class of constraints for BSS problems. While constraints have been previously explored in energy disaggregation, SACs are a new approach that leverages aggregate signal properties rather than temporal dynamics or state transitions.

Significance: The paper addresses a practical problem with clear real-world impact. The demonstrated improvements over state-of-the-art methods (33% reduction in NDE) make this a valuable contribution to the energy disaggregation literature, with potential applications to other BSS problems.

Arguments for acceptance: Strong technical contribution, significant empirical improvements over baselines, clear connection to posterior regularization framework, and meaningful application to energy disaggregation.

Arguments against acceptance: Poor manuscript clarity due to excessive acronyms and dense presentation, lack of explicit clarification about the nature of SACs as a priori constraints, and insufficient emphasis on how SACs complement rather than replace existing constraints.

Overall, this paper presents a novel and valuable approach to energy disaggregation that demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods. However, the poor clarity and presentation significantly hinder the communication of these contributions. With substantial revision to improve readability and clarify the theoretical position of SACs, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As is, the quality of the contribution is strong but the presentation is problematic.
