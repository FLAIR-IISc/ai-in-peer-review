Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles blind source separation (BSS) in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) by injecting domain knowledge in the form of signal aggregate constraints (SACs): soft constraints that each latent source’s total activity over a horizon matches a specified aggregate. The authors show that adding these non-Markovian constraints leads to a non-decomposable MAP problem, and propose a convex quadratic relaxation by introducing pairwise transition variables and relaxing the binary indicators. The objective is concave (sum of linear terms and negative PSD quadratics), so maximizing subject to linear constraints yields a convex QP solvable with CVX. They further show the same objective arises from the posterior regularization framework. Experiments on synthetic data and the HES energy disaggregation dataset report improvements in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) over an AFHMM baseline and AFAMAP [Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12]. The work is positioned relative to AFAMAP, HSMM-based approaches [Johnson & Willsky; Kim et al.], interleaved factorial NHMMs [Zhong et al.], k-segment constraints [Titsias et al.], and general posterior regularization [Ganchev et al.].

Quality:
- Soundness: The relaxation is carefully derived, with clear notation, and the convexity argument is correct given PSD structure of the quadratic terms. The posterior regularization view is accurate and helpful. However, two methodological details are underspecified: (i) how fractional posteriors over states are converted to sequences/signals for reporting (is discretization performed, or are expected means used?), and (ii) sensitivity to λ (set to 1 everywhere) is not explored; cross-validation or robustness analyses would strengthen claims.
- Empirics: Real-data results are promising in both NDE and SAE. That said, the fairness of comparisons is debatable because SACAFHMM uses additional information (NTC/TTC aggregate priors) that AFHMM and AFAMAP do not. An oracle TTC condition is useful as an upper bound but must be clearly separated from realistic settings. Runtime claims are plausible but would benefit from more detailed scaling curves w.r.t. T, number of chains, and states.

Clarity:
The paper is well written and mathematically clean. The derivation from (5) to the CQP is readable, and the connection to posterior regularization is clear. One clarity issue is Figure 1: as the distance between the provided µ0 and the true aggregate grows (>1e4 in the toy setup), AFAMAP appears to outperform SACAFHMM. This strongly suggests that poorly specified constraints can harm performance, so the statement that SACs “dramatically improve” or that they are “robust” needs qualification, and the figure should be reworked with labels, axes units, and confidence intervals to avoid misinterpretation.

Originality:
The specific notion of per-source aggregate constraints for AFHMMs and the particular convex relaxation are novel and timely. While posterior regularization is known, identifying a broadly applicable aggregate constraint and deriving an efficient convex surrogate for factorial models is a worthwhile contribution.

Significance:
Energy disaggregation is a high-impact application, and the idea of leveraging coarse aggregate priors is compelling and broadly applicable to other BSS domains (audio, biology). If generalized, this approach could influence a line of work on incorporating global constraints in latent-variable time-series.

Strengths:
- Clear problem motivation grounded in identifiability and interpretability.
- Elegant formulation of SACs and principled derivation via both optimization and posterior regularization.
- Convex quadratic relaxation that is implementable with off-the-shelf solvers.
- Demonstrated gains in NDE and SAE on synthetic and real datasets; results on ultra-low-frequency HES data are particularly relevant.
- Writing, organization, and related work coverage are strong.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Fairness of baselines: SACAFHMM uses additional aggregate information (NTC/TTC) that AFAMAP and AFHMM are not given. Please either (a) provide AFAMAP/AFHMM variants augmented with the same SAC penalty (e.g., via posterior regularization or Lagrangian terms) even if they are less computationally efficient, or (b) clearly separate “uses extra info” vs “does not” settings. This will isolate the benefit of SACs from the benefit of the relaxation.
- Figure 1 and robustness: The toy results show that misspecified µ0 can degrade performance, with AFAMAP surpassing SACAFHMM at large errors. Please clarify the claim that SAC “always helps”; consider reporting ranges where it helps vs hurts, with variability over seeds, and show sensitivity to λ.
- Constraint expressivity: The paper hints at time-window aggregates; it would be valuable to discuss or prototype broader constraint classes commonly needed in NILM: mutual exclusivity (e.g., at most one device in a category active), cardinality limits (k activations per day), and duration/dwell-time distributions (semi-Markov). Can your CQP be extended to encode these (e.g., additional linear constraints, penalty terms), or would it require moving to mixed-integer formulations?
- Rounding/interpretable states: If outputs are fractional, how are final appliance signals/states produced, and how much does rounding affect NDE/SAE and interpretability?
- Hyperparameters and uncertainty: λ is fixed to 1. Please include sensitivity curves and/or a method to set λ from uncertainty in µ0 (e.g., variance-weighted penalties or robust/Huberized penalties). Consider learning µ0 from hierarchical priors with uncertainty rather than point estimates.
- Scaling: Provide memory/runtime scaling with T, number of appliances, and states; CVX can struggle for large T×K. Practical heuristics (block-coordinate updates, specialized QP solvers, warm starts) would help.

Suggestions for additional experiments:
- Augment AFAMAP (and perhaps a chainwise Viterbi AFHMM) with the same SAC penalty via posterior regularization, even if approximate, to test whether SAC is the key ingredient independent of your relaxation.
- Include baselines that can incorporate SAC: HSMMs with duration priors and an aggregate penalty; k-segment HMMs with an aggregate penalty; dual-decomposition or projected gradient methods with a global-aggregate factor.
- Ablations: AFHMM with your convex relaxation but λ=0 (relaxation-only), vs chainwise Viterbi; and sensitivity to λ and to mis-specification of µ0.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, principled integration of global aggregate priors into factorial HMMs.
- Solid math and a convex surrogate that is likely reusable.
- Meaningful improvements on realistic NILM data and metrics of practical interest (SAE).
- Clear presentation and good positioning in related work.

Cons against acceptance:
- Baseline fairness concerns due to unequal access to aggregate information.
- Robustness to mis-specified constraints is not convincingly demonstrated; Figure 1 suggests potential harm.
- Limited exploration of broader constraint types and limited analysis of λ and scaling.

Overall assessment:
A strong and well-motivated paper with a solid mathematical foundation and promising empirical results. Addressing the fairness of comparisons, clarifying robustness, and outlining extensions to richer constraints (mutual exclusivity, cardinality, duration) would significantly strengthen the contribution. I recommend acceptance after revisions that incorporate the above clarifications and additional experiments where feasible.
