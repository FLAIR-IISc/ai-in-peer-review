This paper proposes AFHMM with Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation, demonstrated on energy disaggregation. The key idea is to inject domain knowledge via constraints on each source’s aggregate signal (e.g., daily Wh per appliance). The authors reformulate MAP inference in AFHMMs by introducing auxiliary transition variables and relaxing the binary constraints, yielding a convex quadratic program (CQP) that can be solved efficiently with CVX. Empirically, the method outperforms AFHMM and AFAMAP on a toy example and on the HES domestic energy dataset, with paired t-tests showing statistically significant improvements and with runtime comparable to AFAMAP. The approach relates to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.) and builds on FHMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan) and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola), adding a new, practically motivated class of constraints.

Quality: The modeling and optimization pipeline is sound and well-motivated, and the experiments are thorough for the targeted application. However, the convexity claim needs to be formalized: please provide a proof that the relaxed objective in Eq. (6) is convex (e.g., show Λi and Vt are PSD and that all constraints are affine) and clearly distinguish the nonconvex IQP from its convex relaxation. Also include a theoretical complexity analysis in terms of T (sequence length), I (number of chains), and Ki (states per chain), and compare scaling to AFAMAP for large T, I.

Clarity: The exposition is generally clear, with a helpful link to posterior regularization. Minor issue: on p.4, line 202, there appear to be extra commas in subscripts before p; please fix notation. The feedback responses addressed my earlier questions; please incorporate those clarifications in the camera-ready.

Originality: SACs are a simple but novel constraint class for BSS, and their convex relaxation within AFHMMs appears original relative to prior NILM work.

Significance: The demonstrated gains and comparable runtime suggest practical impact for energy disaggregation, and the idea could transfer to other BSS settings.

Requests and questions:
- Toy experiment uses σ2=0.01; please justify whether this noise level is realistic or chosen to ease separation. Provide SNRs for mains and aggregate appliance signals in both toy and real data.
- Report wall-clock and memory scaling on larger instances; discuss tractability relative to AFAMAP.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, simple idea with strong empirical gains and statistical tests
- Convex relaxation with practical solvers; comparable speed to AFAMAP
- Useful connection to posterior regularization

Cons against acceptance:
- Missing formal convexity proof and scaling analysis
- Limited discussion of noise realism/SNR
- Reliance on quality of µ0; more guidance on obtaining robust priors would help

Overall, a novel and well-executed contribution; I lean positive, contingent on the theoretical and clarity fixes above.
