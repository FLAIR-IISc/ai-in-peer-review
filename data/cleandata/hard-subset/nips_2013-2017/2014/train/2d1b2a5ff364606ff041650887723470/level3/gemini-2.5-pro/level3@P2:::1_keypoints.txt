- Introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) to incorporate prior expectations about each source's total signal into blind source separation (BSS).
- Implements SACs within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) for tasks like energy disaggregation.
- Non‑Markovian nature of SACs makes standard inference (e.g., Viterbi) inapplicable.
- Reformulates MAP inference as a penalized objective and derives a relaxed convex quadratic program (CQP) for efficient solution.
- Evaluated on a toy dataset and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, showing strong empirical performance.
- AFHMM+SAC achieves dramatic improvement over standard AFHMM and significant gains over AFAMAP.
- Connects the penalized objective to the posterior regularization framework.
- Technical strength: CQP formulation makes the proposed constraints practical and computationally tractable.
- Empirical strength: thorough evaluation with two data variants (with/without mains) and appropriate metrics (NDE, SAE).
- Use of both national-average and oracle total-consumption constraints clarifies practical utility and theoretical potential.
- Weakness: lacks a formal proof or more rigorous argument that the relaxed objective is convex (positive semidefiniteness claim).
- Weakness: toy experiment uses very low noise variance (σ² = 0.01); SNR justification and comparison to real data noise are missing.
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized; motivation and methods are clearly explained.
- Minor suggestion: small proofreading issues (e.g., subscript formatting like Zi,t-1).
- Originality: SACs are a novel, intuitive constraint addressing identifiability in BSS differently from prior work.
- Significance: substantial empirical gains (e.g., 29% NDE reduction) and generality for other BSS domains; computational cost comparable to existing methods.
- Request for clarification: missing formal computational complexity analysis of the CQP in terms of T, I, K.
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, tractable CQP inference, strong empirical results, and general applicability.
- Arguments against acceptance: missing theoretical details (convexity proof, complexity) and insufficient justification of toy experiment noise settings.
- Overall recommendation: strong paper recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to address convexity, complexity analysis, and experimental noise discussion.
