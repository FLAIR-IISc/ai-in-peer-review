Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs). The key idea is to encode prior domain knowledge about the aggregate (e.g., daily total) of each source signal by encouraging ∑t xit to match a specified target µi0. The authors instantiate this idea in energy disaggregation, where appliance-level daily totals are often known approximately from surveys or expert knowledge. They formulate MAP inference with SACs as a constrained optimization (equations (4)–(5)) and derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) for a relaxed problem by introducing auxiliary transition variables Hit, leading to a computationally tractable solver via CVX. They also show the objective can be derived from posterior regularization, connecting to a broader body of work on incorporating constraints into latent-variable models (e.g., Ganchev et al. 2010; Mann & McCallum 2008; Liang et al. 2009). Empirically, they evaluate on a toy example and the real-world HES low-frequency electricity dataset, comparing to AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola 2012). SACs yield substantial improvements in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE), with paired t-tests showing significance, while maintaining comparable runtime to AFAMAP.

Strengths
- Clear and useful modeling idea: global aggregate constraints directly address identifiability by favoring plausible sources without micromanaging transitions.
- Solid integration with AFHMMs, with an efficient relaxed inference procedure. The posterior-regularization derivation nicely situates the method within existing frameworks.
- Empirical results on a challenging, low-frequency, realistic dataset are strong; improvements over AFAMAP are consistent and statistically significant. Runtime appears comparable.
- The approach is general and likely transferable to other BSS domains where aggregate priors exist.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Convexity of the relaxed program: While Λi and Vt are PSD and constraints are linear in (S,H), the paper stops short of a formal statement/proof that the relaxed objective in (6) is convex. Please include a formal argument (e.g., write the quadratic terms in block-diagonal PSD form over St and show linearity in Hit) and state all assumptions under which convexity holds.
- Complexity/scalability: Provide an explicit complexity analysis in terms of T, I, and Ki, including the number of variables and nonzeros (S has O(T∑i Ki) entries; H has O(T∑i Ki^2)); discuss the practical scaling limits of interior-point solvers like CVX (worst-case O(n^3) per iteration), and contrast with AFAMAP/chainwise Viterbi complexity. A tractability comparison on larger T and Ki would strengthen the case.
- Toy experiment realism: The toy noise variance σ2=0.01 seems very low; please report SNR and discuss whether conclusions hold at more realistic SNRs. For the mains experiments, report typical SNR (signal vs. unmodeled/apparatus noise) and how performance varies with noise levels.
- Hyperparameter selection: λi are fixed to 1; please discuss sensitivity to λi, and whether cross-validation or calibration from survey uncertainty is advisable. Similarly, comment on robustness to misspecified µi0; Figure 1 is helpful—consider quantifying the slope of degradation and its dependence on λi and Ki.
- Fairness and practicality of baselines: AFAMAP and AFHMM parameter estimation details (e.g., state counts, transition priors, and their tuning) should be fully described to ensure a fair comparison. The “oracle” TTC setting is useful as an upper bound; please make this framing explicit in the main text and add ablations with time-windowed constraints (as you note they are straightforward).
- Reproducibility and solver details: Specify CVX settings, solver used, stopping criteria, and any warm starts or heuristics. Reporting wall-clock time on a standard machine, along with problem sizes, would aid reproducibility.

Quality
Technically sound in conception and empirically well supported. The main gap is the missing formal convexity proof and a more thorough complexity analysis. Claims about performance are supported with statistical tests. The discussion of posterior regularization is correct and helpful.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The optimization and notation sections could be tightened:
- Provide a concise table of symbols and dimensions for S, St, Si, µ∗i, Λi, Vt, ut, and Hit.
- Make explicit where bilinear terms are avoided and why the relaxed objective is quadratic in S with linear terms in H.
- Minor notation/typo: there appear to be extra commas in subscripts before p on p.4 (around line 202). Also fix “equavalent”→“equivalent” and minor grammatical slips.

Originality
The use of global aggregate constraints in AFHMMs and the relaxed CQP formulation are, to my knowledge, novel in the energy disaggregation literature and a meaningful extension beyond AFAMAP’s local transition constraints. The posterior-regularization perspective further differentiates the contribution.

Significance
The improvements on HES (low-frequency, real homes) are practically meaningful and likely to influence subsequent NILM work. The idea is general and could impact other BSS settings where aggregate priors are available (audio, biology). Dependency on µi0 quality is a limitation but is well-motivated in many domains.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general idea that addresses a core identifiability issue.
- Strong empirical gains on realistic data with significance tests.
- Clean connection to posterior regularization and a tractable inference method.
- Comparable runtime to a strong baseline.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing formal convexity proof and limited complexity/scaling analysis.
- Limited exploration of sensitivity to λi and SNR.
- Heavy reliance on an external solver (CVX) may limit scalability.

Questions for the authors
- Please provide a formal convexity proof of the relaxed problem in (6).
- How does performance vary with SNR in toy and real datasets? What are typical SNRs for the mains signals?
- How sensitive are results to λi and to errors in µi0? Can λi reflect uncertainty in µi0 (e.g., via variance-weighted penalties)?
- How does the method scale to larger T, I, and Ki without CVX (e.g., specialized QP solvers or first-order methods)?
- Did you consider time-windowed SACs in practice (e.g., daytime vs nighttime) and do they further help?

Post-rebuttal note
The authors’ feedback addressed the toy SNR and convexity intuitions; please integrate those clarifications, including a formal convexity argument and SNR reporting, into the final version.

Overall recommendation
Accept (borderline to weak accept). The paper presents a clear, novel idea with solid empirical benefits on a real, challenging task. Addressing the convexity and scalability clarifications will make the contribution stronger and more broadly useful. Reviewer confidence: medium.
