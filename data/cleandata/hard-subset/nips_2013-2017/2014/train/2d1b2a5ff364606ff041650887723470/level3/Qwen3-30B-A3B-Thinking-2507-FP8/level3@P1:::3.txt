

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation using Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs). The authors observe that in many BSS applications, the total signal for each source varies widely and is often known in advance, motivating SACs that encourage the aggregate signal for each source to be close to specified values. The paper formulates a relaxed optimization problem as a convex quadratic program (CQP), enabling efficient inference. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over baseline AFHMM and AFAMAP on both synthetic and real-world energy disaggregation data, showing a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) compared to AFHMM and 29% improvement over AFAMAP on the Household Electricity Survey dataset.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature by identifying a practical class of domain knowledge constraints that can be incorporated into BSS models. Previous work on incorporating constraints into probabilistic models (e.g., posterior regularization [4], generalized expectation criterion [14]) has not specifically addressed the question of what types of constraints are generally useful across applications. SACs fill this gap by leveraging aggregate signal knowledge that is often available in applications like energy disaggregation. The mathematical derivation of the SAC formulation as a CQP is solid, and the connection to posterior regularization provides theoretical grounding. The paper is well-written, with clear problem motivation and a logical flow from problem statement to method to evaluation.

The main strengths of the paper include: (1) identification of a practically useful class of constraints that address the identifiability problem in BSS; (2) a theoretically sound and computationally efficient solution via CQP; (3) compelling empirical results showing significant improvements in both synthetic and real-world settings; and (4) clear connections to prior work in energy disaggregation [8, 12, 22] and constraint-based learning [4, 14].

However, the paper has several weaknesses that affect the strength of its claims. First, the comparison with baselines appears potentially unfair. The SAC-AFHMM uses a total consumption vector derived from training data (NTC), while the baselines (AFHMM and AFAMAP) do not incorporate this prior information. The paper states that "in practice, the µi0 could be obtained from expert knowledge or by experiments" but fails to demonstrate how SAC would perform with the same training data as the baselines. The authors should compare SAC-AFHMM with a version of AFHMM that also incorporates aggregate constraints (even if not as efficiently) to fairly establish SAC's benefit.

Second, Figure 1 is problematic as it shows AFAMAP outperforming SAC-AFHMM when the constraint error exceeds 1e4, suggesting that poorly specified constraints can worsen performance. This directly contradicts the paper's claim that SAC "dramatically improves" the original AFHMM, as the authors suggest SAC should always help. The paper should acknowledge this limitation and discuss how to select appropriate constraint values.

Third, while SACs are a useful constraint type, the paper does not explore how to extend the framework to handle more complex constraints that are also common in energy disaggregation, such as mutual exclusivity (at most one appliance on at a time), cardinality limits (number of appliances), or duration constraints (minimum on time for appliances). These constraints could be incorporated into the SAC framework with similar mathematical elegance, and the authors should discuss this extension.

The paper's evaluation is generally strong but could be improved by: (1) demonstrating SAC's effectiveness with a wider range of constraint values; (2) comparing SAC-AFHMM with other constraint-based methods that might incorporate aggregate constraints; and (3) providing more detailed analysis of when SACs fail and how to select appropriate constraint values.

The quality of the paper is high, with technically sound methods and clear experimental results. The clarity is excellent, with well-organized sections that make the technical content accessible. The originality is moderate - SACs are a specific type of constraint that has not been previously explored in this context, but the underlying CQP approach is somewhat similar to prior work. The significance is strong for energy disaggregation applications, where domain knowledge is often available but underutilized in BSS models.

Pro acceptance: Strong contribution to addressing identifiability in BSS with practical constraints; solid theoretical and empirical results; clear connection to energy disaggregation application.

Con acceptance: Potential unfair comparison with baselines; Figure 1 undermines the main claim; failure to explore more complex constraint types; limited discussion of constraint selection challenges.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the energy disaggregation literature. The SAC framework is well-motivated and provides a practical way to incorporate domain knowledge into BSS models. The main weakness is the misleading presentation of SAC's benefits in Figure 1 and the potential unfair comparison with baselines. With minor revisions addressing these issues, this paper would make a valuable contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
