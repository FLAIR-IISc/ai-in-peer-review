

# Review for "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation using Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs). SACs encourage the total signal for each source to be close to a specified value, which is a natural form of domain knowledge in many applications (e.g., knowing a toaster typically uses 50 Wh in a day). The authors incorporate SACs into AFHMMs and transform the inference problem into a convex quadratic program. The method is evaluated on both toy data and real-world energy disaggregation data, showing significant improvements over the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach (AFAMAP).

The paper relates well to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. It builds on the AFHMM framework introduced in Ghahramani & Jordan (2000) and further developed in Kolter & Jaakkola (2012) for energy disaggregation. It also connects to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2010) as a special case, demonstrating how SACs can be derived within that framework. The energy disaggregation problem has been studied extensively at NIPS (e.g., Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012; Parson et al., 2012), and this paper makes a clear contribution by introducing a new class of constraints that effectively incorporate domain knowledge.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The empirical results on real-world energy data are compelling, showing a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM and a 29% improvement over AFAMAP. The toy experiment demonstrates robustness to errors in constraint specification. However, the paper could strengthen its theoretical foundation by providing a formal proof that the optimization problem in equation (6) is convex (as suggested in the key points). The toy experiment uses an unrealistically low noise variance (σ²=0.01), which may make the problem easier than realistic scenarios. The authors should discuss signal-to-noise ratios for the mains vs. appliance signals in the real data to better contextualize the results.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The mathematical derivations are clear, though the convexity argument could be more rigorous. Minor notation issues exist (e.g., extra commas in subscripts on page 4, line 202). The authors could improve clarity by providing a theoretical scaling/complexity analysis (data length, number of hidden states) and a tractability comparison to AFAMAP for larger datasets.

**Originality:** SACs represent a novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation. The paper clearly articulates how this differs from previous constraint approaches (e.g., AFAMAP's constraint that at most one chain changes state at a time). The method is applicable to many BSS problems beyond energy disaggregation, making it a potentially general contribution to the field. The paper adequately references related work, including recent NIPS papers on energy disaggregation.

**Significance:** The energy disaggregation application is significant for real-world energy management, and the improvements over state-of-the-art are substantial and validated with statistical significance tests. The computational efficiency (similar to AFAMAP) makes the approach practical for real-world deployment. However, the paper could better articulate the broader significance of SACs beyond energy disaggregation and discuss how widely applicable this constraint approach is to other BSS problems.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel constraint approach (SACs) that effectively incorporates domain knowledge into blind source separation. It provides strong empirical results on a real-world problem with significant improvements over baselines. The convex optimization approach is theoretically sound and well-motivated. The connection to posterior regularization is a nice theoretical touch. The method is computationally efficient with similar scaling to AFAMAP.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The paper lacks a formal convexity proof for the optimization problem. The toy experiment uses unrealistically low noise variance, which may not reflect realistic conditions. The discussion of signal-to-noise ratios in the real data is insufficient. The broader significance beyond energy disaggregation could be better articulated.

**Recommendations for improvement:** 
1. Provide a formal proof of convexity for the optimization problem in equation (6)
2. Discuss signal-to-noise ratios for the mains vs. appliance signals in the real data
3. Include a theoretical scaling/complexity analysis for the method
4. Address the realistic noise levels in the toy experiment
5. Expand the discussion of broader applicability beyond energy disaggregation

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the energy disaggregation literature and provides a novel approach for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation. The empirical results are compelling, and the method is well-motivated. With the suggested revisions to address the theoretical and experimental concerns, this paper would make an excellent contribution to NIPS.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it presents a technically sound contribution with clear empirical validation and potential impact in an important application domain. The authors have addressed reviewer queries satisfactorily in feedback, suggesting that incorporating these clarifications into the paper would strengthen it further.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the convexity proof, noise variance discussion, and broader significance.
