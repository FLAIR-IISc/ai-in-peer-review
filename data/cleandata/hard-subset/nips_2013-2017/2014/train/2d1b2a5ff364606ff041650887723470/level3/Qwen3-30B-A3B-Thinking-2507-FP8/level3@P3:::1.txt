

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation (BSS), specifically applied to energy disaggregation. The authors address the fundamental identifiability problem in BSS by incorporating domain knowledge about expected total signal values for each source. SACs encourage the aggregate value of each source signal over time to be close to a specified value, which is often available from domain knowledge (e.g., average energy consumption of appliances). The authors integrate SACs into an Additive Factorial HMM (AFHMM), transforming the non-convex inference problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP) that can be efficiently solved.

The paper builds upon previous work on posterior regularization [4] and extends it with a specific constraint type that is both practically useful and theoretically tractable. It relates to energy disaggregation work by Hart [8], Kolter and Jaakkola [12], and others, but offers a novel constraint mechanism that significantly improves performance over state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Contribution:** SACs represent a meaningful and practical approach to addressing identifiability in BSS problems. The authors clearly articulate why total signal values represent a useful constraint class that is often available in practice.

2. **Technical Soundness:** The transformation of the inference problem into a convex optimization problem is well-motivated and technically sound. The paper demonstrates strong empirical results on both synthetic and real-world energy data, showing significant improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP (45% and 29% reduction in normalized disaggregation error, respectively).

3. **Practical Evaluation:** The energy disaggregation application is well-chosen and relevant to a growing area of research. The real-world evaluation on the Household Electricity Survey (HES) data demonstrates the practical utility of SACs.

4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear explanations of the model, optimization approach, and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Toy Experiment Realism:** The toy experiment uses an extremely low noise variance (σ² = 0.01), which may not reflect realistic conditions. The authors should clarify whether this was chosen to make separation easier or if it represents a realistic scenario. A discussion of SNR for mains vs. aggregate appliance signals would strengthen the evaluation.

2. **Lack of Formal Convexity Proof:** While the paper claims the optimization problem is convex, it lacks a formal proof that the procedure in equation (6) is indeed convex. This is a significant omission for a paper claiming to solve a convex optimization problem.

3. **Missing Complexity Analysis:** The paper does not provide a theoretical scaling/complexity analysis (data length, number of hidden states) or a tractability comparison to AFAMAP for larger datasets. The statement that "our implementations of AFHMM, AFAMAP, and AFHMM+SAC scale similarly" needs to be supported with more concrete analysis.

4. **Minor Notation Issue:** There appears to be an extra comma in subscripts before p on page 4 (line 202), which should be corrected.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the BSS problem by introducing SACs, which are both practically useful and theoretically tractable. The empirical results are compelling and well-supported. The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

The main weaknesses are the lack of formal convexity proof, the questionable realism of the toy experiment's noise variance, and the absence of theoretical complexity analysis. However, these issues are not fundamental to the paper's contribution and could be addressed in a minor revision. The authors have already satisfactorily addressed reviewer queries in the feedback, suggesting they can incorporate these clarifications.

The paper's contribution is original to the NIPS community, as it introduces a new constraint mechanism for BSS that can be applied to multiple domains, with a specific and compelling application to energy disaggregation. The results demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods with statistical significance.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Novel constraint mechanism addressing a fundamental problem in BSS
- Strong empirical results on both synthetic and real-world data
- Clear technical contribution with a convex optimization solution
- Well-written and well-organized paper

*Con:*
- Missing formal convexity proof
- Toy experiment uses unrealistic noise variance
- Lack of theoretical complexity analysis
- Minor notation error

Given the paper's strong technical contribution, compelling empirical results, and the fact that the weaknesses can be addressed in a minor revision, I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the energy disaggregation literature and provides a useful framework for incorporating domain knowledge into BSS problems.
