This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) to improve blind source separation, specifically tackling the energy disaggregation problem. The core idea is to incorporate domain knowledge – expected total signal values for each source – as constraints during inference, addressing the inherent identifiability issues in BSS.  A convex quadratic program is derived for approximate MAP inference, allowing for efficient computation. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach (AFAMAP) on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, measured by reductions in Normalized Disaggregation Error (NDE) and Signal Aggregate Error (SAE).

The motivation for this work is strong. BSS is a fundamental challenge across multiple fields, and the difficulty of interpreting results when solutions are not identifiable is a practical limitation. The authors correctly identify the need for incorporating domain knowledge to address this. This work builds upon prior efforts in incorporating prior constraints into probabilistic models (posterior regularization, generalized expectation criterion, measurement-based learning), and specifically focuses on a previously unexplored class of constraints – aggregate values. The approach leverages the well-established AFHMM framework, a common starting point for energy disaggregation problems.

The strengths of the paper are substantial.  The technical development is sound, and the derivation of the convex quadratic program is a significant contribution. The results are compelling, showing substantial improvements on a challenging real-world dataset. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making the complex mathematical details accessible.  The authors thoughtfully discuss the limitations and provide directions for future research. The connection to posterior regularization is a useful clarification.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The comparison to baseline methods appears potentially unfair.  The AFHMM+SAC is trained on a longer time series than the AFHMM and AFAMAP, potentially giving it an unfair advantage. It would be valuable to see a more controlled comparison, perhaps using the same training data for all methods. Furthermore, while AFAMAP is a strong baseline, exploring other methods that could also incorporate SACs, even if computationally more expensive, would strengthen the analysis and demonstrate the unique benefits of the proposed approach. 

Figure 1 raises concerns about the robustness of the approach. The results show that beyond a certain distance between the constraint values (µi0) and the true aggregate values, AFAMAP actually *outperforms* SAC-AFHMM. This suggests that poorly chosen constraints can worsen performance, and the claim that SAC always improves results is misleading. A more detailed discussion of this phenomenon and its implications would be beneficial.

The current work focuses on a relatively simple form of SAC – a single constraint per component signal over the entire time series.  Exploring more complex constraints, such as time-varying constraints (as briefly mentioned but not pursued), mutual exclusivity between appliances, cardinality limits (maximum number of appliances active at any given time), or duration limits, would be a valuable extension.  These more nuanced constraints could lead to further improvements in disaggregation accuracy. 

Finally, while the authors demonstrate the benefits of SAC in the context of AFHMM, the potential for generalizing this approach to other BSS models is not fully explored. Extending the algorithm to handle broader classes of constraints and models would significantly broaden its impact.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to incorporating domain knowledge into BSS.
*   Significant empirical improvements on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the technical details.
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem with practical implications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Potentially unfair comparison to baseline methods.
*   Figure 1 suggests potential drawbacks of SAC with poorly specified constraints.
*   Limited exploration of more complex constraint types.
*   Lack of generalization to other BSS models.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation. The proposed SAC approach is novel, theoretically well-founded, and demonstrates promising results. The identified limitations represent directions for future research rather than fundamental flaws.  I believe this paper is of sufficient quality to be accepted to NIPS, with the caveat that the authors address the concerns regarding the fairness of the comparison to baselines and clarify the conditions under which SAC performs optimally. A minor revision to address these points is recommended.
