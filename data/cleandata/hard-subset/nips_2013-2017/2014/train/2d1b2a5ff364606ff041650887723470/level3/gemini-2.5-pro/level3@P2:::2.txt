This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), particularly in the context of energy disaggregation where a single aggregated time series (total household electricity usage) must be separated into its constituent source signals (individual appliances). The core challenge in this domain is the inherent unidentifiability of the sources, which makes interpreting the separated signals difficult. The authors propose a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge to mitigate this problem by introducing Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs).

The main idea is to constrain the total energy consumption of each inferred source signal over a given time period to be close to a known or expected value. For instance, prior knowledge suggests a refrigerator's daily energy usage falls within a certain range, which is very different from that of a toaster. These constraints are integrated into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a standard model for this task. As the SACs are non-Markovian and complicate inference, the authors formulate the MAP inference problem as a penalized objective function and derive a convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation, which can be solved efficiently. The authors relate this formulation to the posterior regularization framework. The method is evaluated on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, demonstrating significant improvements over both the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central contribution, the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints, is a simple, intuitive, and powerful idea. It provides a practical way to inject valuable domain knowledge into an otherwise underdetermined problem. This type of constraint is broadly applicable to many BSS problems beyond energy disaggregation where aggregate statistics of the sources are known or can be estimated.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the constrained optimization problem and its subsequent relaxation into a solvable convex quadratic program is well-executed. The connection drawn to the posterior regularization framework in Section 6 adds theoretical depth and provides an alternative perspective on the proposed penalty term.

3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results are strong and convincing. The authors demonstrate substantial improvements over relevant baselines on a challenging, low-frequency, real-world energy dataset. The 29% improvement in normalized disaggregation error over the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model on a realistic task (Section 7.2.2) is a significant result. The use of both an oracle constraint (True Total Consumption) and a practical one (National Total Consumption) is a thoughtful experimental design that effectively showcases the potential and practical utility of the method.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Readability:** The primary weakness of this paper is its clarity. The manuscript is very difficult to read, which significantly detracts from the quality of the work and may hinder its impact. The prose is dense, and the presentation could be substantially improved to make the core ideas more accessible.

2.  **Excessive Use of Acronyms:** The readability issue is exacerbated by an over-reliance on acronyms, some of which are quite long and cumbersome (e.g., AFAMAP, AFHMM+SAC). While acronyms are common, their density here forces the reader to constantly pause and recall definitions (e.g., NDE, SAE, NTC, TTC), disrupting the flow of the paper. It is strongly recommended that the authors reduce the number of acronyms or reintroduce them more frequently to aid the reader.

3.  **Ambiguity in Constraint Formulation:** There is a subtle but important point of ambiguity in the technical presentation. In Section 4.2, the penalty term for the SAC is introduced and described as being viewable as "the logarithm of the prior distributions over the states S." This suggests the constraint is placed *a priori* on the space of hidden state sequences, which in turn constrains the posterior. However, Section 6 frames the entire objective function as the result of posterior regularization, where constraints are placed directly on the posterior distribution. While these views are related, they are not identical. The authors should explicitly clarify the precise statistical interpretation of their objective function in Eq. (5). Is it a MAP objective under a new, more constrained prior, or is it a variational approximation to a "true" posterior? Clarifying this point would strengthen the paper's technical foundation.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   The paper introduces a novel, simple, and effective method (SACs) for incorporating domain knowledge into BSS models.
*   It addresses a significant and challenging real-world problem in energy disaggregation.
*   The empirical results are strong, showing a clear and substantial improvement over existing state-of-the-art methods.
*   The technical approach (CQP relaxation) is sound and practical.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

*   The paper is poorly written and very difficult to follow. The lack of clarity is a major flaw that obscures the paper's contributions.

### Conclusion

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution with a novel idea and strong empirical backing. The proposed signal aggregate constraints are a significant addition to the toolbox for tackling blind source separation problems. However, the paper is seriously hampered by poor clarity and readability. I would be inclined to accept this paper on the condition of a major revision focused on improving the writing and clarifying the technical presentation as noted above. The ideas and results are strong enough to merit publication at NIPS, but the presentation must be brought up to the same standard.
