- Introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation and incorporates them into AFHMMs.  
- Transforms inference with SACs into a convex quadratic program for efficient optimization.  
- Evaluated on toy and real-world energy disaggregation data, showing large improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP.  
- Empirical gains: ~45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error vs AFHMM and ~29% improvement vs AFAMAP.  
- Relates to prior AFHMM work (Ghahramani & Jordan; Kolter & Jaakkola) and connects SACs to posterior regularization.  
- Technically sound with compelling real-data results, but lacks a formal convexity proof for Eq. (6).  
- Toy experiment uses an unrealistically low noise variance (σ² = 0.01), possibly overstating robustness.  
- Paper should discuss signal-to-noise ratios for mains vs appliance signals to contextualize results.  
- Generally well-written and organized; minor notation issues and a need for a more rigorous convexity argument.  
- Recommend adding theoretical scaling/complexity analysis and tractability comparison to AFAMAP for larger datasets.  
- SACs are novel, broadly applicable to other BSS problems, and different from prior constraint approaches.  
- Significance: substantial, real-world improvements and computational efficiency similar to AFAMAP, but broader applicability needs clearer articulation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel constraint approach, strong empirical validation, computationally efficient, and theoretical connection to posterior regularization.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing convexity proof, unrealistic toy noise levels, insufficient SNR discussion, and limited discussion of broader significance.  
- Recommendations: prove convexity of Eq. (6); discuss SNR in real data; provide scaling/complexity analysis; use realistic noise levels in toy experiments; expand on broader applicability.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address theoretical and experimental concerns; reviewer notes authors addressed queries satisfactorily in feedback.
