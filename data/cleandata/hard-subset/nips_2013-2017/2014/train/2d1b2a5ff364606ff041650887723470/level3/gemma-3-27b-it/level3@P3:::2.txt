This paper proposes an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) augmented with signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for the problem of energy disaggregation. The core idea is to incorporate prior knowledge about the expected total energy consumption of appliances into the model, thereby improving identifiability and disaggregation accuracy. The authors formulate this as a convex quadratic program for approximate inference and demonstrate significant performance gains on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, outperforming both a standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach.

The problem of blind source separation, and specifically energy disaggregation, is well-motivated, as highlighted by references to Hart [8], Ziefman [24], and others. The inherent underdetermined nature of the problem is accurately identified, and the need for domain knowledge to address identifiability is clearly articulated. The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature on incorporating prior constraints, referencing work by Ganchev et al. [4], Liang et al. [13], and others. The motivation for SACs – leveraging knowledge of expected aggregate values – is intuitive and practical.  The connection to posterior regularization, discussed in Section 6, is a valuable addition, providing a theoretical grounding for the proposed method.

The strengths of the paper are significant. The proposed SACs appear to be a genuinely useful constraint for energy disaggregation, offering a tangible way to incorporate prior knowledge. The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating a clear improvement over baseline methods on a challenging real-world dataset (HES). The conversion to a convex quadratic program allows for efficient inference. The inclusion of both simulated data and real-world experiments adds to the credibility of the findings.  The authors acknowledge the limitations of the relaxed optimization problem.

However, the paper suffers from a significant weakness: its readability. The authors rely heavily on acronyms, some of which are unusually long (e.g., AFHMM+SAC, µ̃i).  This constant barrage of abbreviations makes it difficult to follow the technical details, even for readers familiar with the field. The writing style is dense and could benefit from greater clarity and more descriptive explanations. 

Critically, the authors’ implementation of the constraint feels subtly off. They apply the constraint to the *prior* distribution over states (through the penalized objective function in equation 5), which, while mathematically equivalent, effectively *constrains the posterior*. This should be explicitly stated. The consequence is that the resulting inference doesn’t directly enforce the aggregate constraint on the estimated source signals; rather, it biases the inference towards solutions that *would* satisfy the constraint a priori.  A more direct approach could involve post-processing the inferred states to enforce the constraint, or incorporating it directly into the likelihood function.

Regarding originality, the AFHMM itself is not new [5], but the *combination* of AFHMM with signal aggregate constraints is a novel contribution. The formulation of the SACs and the derivation of the convex quadratic program are also original.  The paper adequately references related work, including recent advances in AFHMM variations [10, 12, 21].

The significance of the work is noteworthy. Improved energy disaggregation techniques have the potential to empower consumers to reduce energy consumption and contribute to a more sustainable energy future.  The SAC approach is broadly applicable to other blind source separation problems where prior knowledge about aggregate values is available. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and effective application of domain knowledge (SACs) to a challenging problem (energy disaggregation).
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating significant performance gains.
*   Theoretical grounding through the connection to posterior regularization.
*   Efficient inference through convex quadratic programming.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Poor readability due to excessive and often long acronyms.
*   Subtle but important clarification needed regarding the implementation of the SAC as a prior constraint.
*   Dense writing style hinders understanding of technical details.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the readability issues, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of energy disaggregation. The SAC approach is a clever and effective way to incorporate prior knowledge, and the experimental results are compelling. I recommend acceptance, *contingent upon the authors significantly improving the clarity and readability of the manuscript*, specifically by reducing the reliance on acronyms and clarifying the application of the constraint. Addressing the issue of whether the constraint is applied to the prior or posterior, and making this explicit, is also important.
