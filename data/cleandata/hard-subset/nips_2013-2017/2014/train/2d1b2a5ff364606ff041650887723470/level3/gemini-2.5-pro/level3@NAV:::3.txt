This paper addresses the critical problem of identifiability in blind source separation (BSS) by proposing a novel method to incorporate domain knowledge. The authors introduce Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a common approach for problems like energy disaggregation. This new model, SACAFHMM, encourages the total signal for each separated source to be close to a known or estimated value. To make inference tractable, the authors present a convex quadratic programming relaxation of the MAP problem. This work builds upon previous efforts to constrain AFHMMs, such as AFAMAP, by introducing a different and complementary form of prior knowledge.

**Strengths:**
*   **Quality and Significance:** The paper is technically sound, with a solid mathematical foundation for the model and its convex relaxation. It addresses a significant and well-motivated problem—the inherent unidentifiability of BSS models—with an intuitive and practical solution. The application to energy disaggregation is compelling and the demonstrated improvements in Normalized Disaggregation Error (NDE) and Signal Aggregate Error (SAE) on real-world data are substantial.
*   **Clarity and Originality:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The introduction of SACs as a quadratic penalty in the AFHMM objective and the corresponding CQP solution is a novel contribution that is clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Fairness of Comparison:** A primary concern is that the experimental comparison may be unfair. The proposed SACAFHMM method is provided with additional information (the aggregate signal targets `µi0`) that the baselines (AFHMM and AFAMAP) do not receive. While the results are promising, the improvement is at least partially attributable to this extra data.
*   **Unsupported Claims:** The claim that SACs consistently help is not fully supported by the paper's own results. Figure 1 clearly shows that when the constraints are poor (Euclidean distance > 1e4), the baseline AFAMAP outperforms SACAFHMM. This crucial finding—that bad constraints can worsen performance—should be explicitly discussed, as it contradicts the broader narrative.
*   **Limited Scope and Evaluation:** The paper could be strengthened by evaluating other methods that could incorporate SACs, even if they are less computationally efficient, to provide a more complete picture. Furthermore, the framework could be extended to handle more complex and realistic constraint types, such as mutual exclusivity (e.g., a furnace and air conditioner are not on simultaneously), cardinality limits, or state duration limits.

In summary, this paper introduces a valuable and original idea. However, the claims of superiority need to be tempered by a more nuanced discussion of the experimental setup and the impact of inaccurate priors. I would encourage the authors to reframe the comparison to acknowledge the additional information used and to discuss future work on generalizing the algorithm to handle a broader and more expressive class of constraints.
