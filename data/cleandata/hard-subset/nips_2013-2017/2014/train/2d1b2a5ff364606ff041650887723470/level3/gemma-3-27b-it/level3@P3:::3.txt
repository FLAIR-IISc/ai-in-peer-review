This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework for blind source separation, specifically targeting energy disaggregation. The core idea – incorporating prior knowledge about expected total energy consumption for appliances – is a sensible and potentially valuable approach to address the inherent identifiability issues in this field. The mathematical formulation is sound, and the paper is well-written, clearly motivating the problem and presenting the proposed solution with a solid theoretical foundation. The results on both synthetic and real-world data demonstrate promising improvements over existing methods, particularly in Normalized Disaggregation Error (NDE) and Signal Aggregate Error (SAE).

However, several aspects warrant careful consideration. A primary concern is the experimental setup. The comparison between SACAFHMM and the baselines (AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP) appears potentially unfair. The paper states the baselines use less data, which immediately raises questions about the validity of the performance gains attributed solely to SACs. Using differing dataset sizes introduces a confounding factor, and a fairer comparison would involve training and testing all models on the same data split. 

Further, Figure 1, illustrating the relationship between the distance of µ0 (the constraint value) and the error rates, is misleading. The observation that AFAMAP outperforms SACAFHMM at distances greater than 1e4 suggests that poorly chosen constraints can *worsen* performance, contradicting the implicit claim that SACs always provide a benefit. This critical finding needs to be highlighted more prominently and discussed in detail – the paper should acknowledge the potential for SACs to be detrimental if the prior knowledge is inaccurate.

The paper's contributions are valuable, but limited in scope. The authors acknowledge this and appropriately suggest extending the work to more complex constraint types. Specifically, exploring constraints such as mutual exclusivity (only one appliance can be on at a time), cardinality limits (a maximum number of appliances can be on simultaneously), and duration limits (an appliance must be on for a minimum or maximum duration) would significantly broaden the applicability of this approach. Generalizing the algorithm to handle broader classes of constraints beyond simple aggregate values is a logical and important next step.

The relation to posterior regularization is well-established and strengthens the theoretical grounding of the work. The computational complexity analysis, noting the similar scaling of the three methods, is reassuring.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of signal aggregate constraints to the blind source separation problem.
*   Solid theoretical foundation and clear mathematical formulation.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.
*   Identifies a promising direction for incorporating domain knowledge into BSS problems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Potentially unfair comparison with baselines due to differing data usage.
*   Misleading Figure 1 suggesting SACs can degrade performance.
*   Limited scope – focuses solely on simple aggregate constraints.
*   Lack of exploration of the sensitivity of the method to the accuracy of the constraint values (µ0).



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. Claims are generally supported by experimental results, although the experimental design requires scrutiny. It presents a complete piece of work regarding the specific contribution of SACs within an AFHMM framework. The authors are reasonably careful in evaluating their work, but the issue with Figure 1 and the data discrepancy needs addressing.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The mathematical notation is well-defined, and the explanations are concise yet comprehensive. Sufficient information is provided to understand the approach and its implementation.

**Originality:** The combination of SACs with AFHMM is novel. While AFHMMs are established in this domain, the incorporation of this specific type of prior knowledge represents a significant contribution. Related work is adequately referenced.

**Significance:** The results are important, as they demonstrate a viable approach for improving energy disaggregation accuracy. Practitioners and researchers in this field are likely to find these ideas useful. Addressing the identifiability problem in BSS is a challenging task, and this work provides a valuable step forward.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors must address the concerns regarding the experimental setup (equalize dataset sizes for fair comparison) and provide a more nuanced discussion of the results presented in Figure 1. They should also explicitly acknowledge the potential drawbacks of SACs when the prior knowledge is inaccurate. While extending the work to more complex constraints is a clear direction for future research, it is not essential for acceptance in its current form. Addressing the experimental validity and clarifying the limitations of SACs would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.
