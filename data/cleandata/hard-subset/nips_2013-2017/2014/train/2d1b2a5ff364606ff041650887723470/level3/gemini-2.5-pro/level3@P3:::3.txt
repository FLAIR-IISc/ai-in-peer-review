This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation problems, with a specific application to energy disaggregation. The core idea is to regularize the solution by encouraging the total aggregate signal for each separated source to be close to a known or expected value. The authors incorporate SACs into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) and derive an efficient approximate inference algorithm based on a convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation. This work builds upon previous efforts to constrain AFHMMs, such as AFAMAP, which enforces a one-state-change-at-a-time constraint, but proposes a different and complementary type of domain knowledge. The formulation is also elegantly connected to the posterior regularization framework.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel, intuitive, and practical way to inject prior knowledge into a notoriously difficult and underdetermined problem. The concept of a "signal aggregate" is a natural piece of information that may be available in many domains (e.g., average daily consumption of an appliance, total expected expression level of a gene). The application to energy disaggregation is well-motivated and tackles a problem of significant real-world interest. The reported improvements in disaggregation accuracy are substantial.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The mathematical foundation is solid. The authors clearly formulate the constrained MAP inference problem and present a sound CQP relaxation for tractable inference. The connection drawn to the posterior regularization framework provides strong theoretical justification for the proposed penalized objective function.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for SACs is compelling, the model is described precisely, and the experimental setup and results are presented intelligibly.

4.  **Empirical Results:** The experiments on both synthetic and real-world energy datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The SACAFHMM model shows significant improvements over the standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model in terms of both Normalized Disaggregation Error (NDE) and Signal Aggregate Error (SAE), highlighting the value of the proposed constraints.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Fairness of Comparison:** The experimental comparison, while showing strong results for SACAFHMM, may be slightly unfair. The proposed model is provided with additional information—the national total consumption (NTC) vector—which the baseline AFHMM and AFAMAP models do not use. The performance gain is thus a combination of the novel constraint and this extra data. A more compelling comparison would involve either modifying the baselines to leverage this aggregate information as well (if possible) or comparing against other methods that can incorporate similar priors. This would help isolate the contribution of the specific SAC formulation versus simply using more domain knowledge.

2.  **Impact of Inaccurate Constraints:** The paper's conclusion that SACs are beneficial should be moderated. Figure 1 shows that as the provided constraint (`µ0`) deviates from the true aggregate value, both NDE and SAE increase. This implies that a sufficiently inaccurate constraint could make performance worse than a model with no constraint at all. For example, at a distance of 1e4, the NDE is already quite high. It would be valuable for the authors to explicitly show the crossover point where SACAFHMM's performance drops below that of AFAMAP or AFHMM. This would provide a clearer picture of the model's robustness and highlight the critical importance of having reasonably accurate prior knowledge for the constraints to be helpful.

3.  **Limited Scope of Constraint Types:** The paper focuses exclusively on sum-based aggregate constraints over the entire time series. While the authors briefly mention that this could be extended to different time windows, the framework could be much more powerful. Many real-world problems could benefit from more expressive constraints. I would encourage the authors to discuss the potential for extending their framework to handle other types, such as:
    *   **Mutual Exclusivity:** Constraints that prevent certain appliances (e.g., heating and air conditioning) from being active simultaneously.
    *   **Cardinality Limits:** Constraints on the number of sources that can be active at any given time.
    *   **Duration Limits:** Constraints that enforce typical operating durations for certain states (e.g., a microwave runs for minutes, not hours).
    A discussion of these extensions would strengthen the paper by outlining a clear path for future work and highlighting the generality of the underlying idea.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and useful contribution to the field of blind source separation. The proposed SACs are a valuable tool for incorporating domain knowledge, and the technical execution is sound. The weaknesses identified primarily relate to the framing of the experimental evaluation and the scope of the investigation, rather than fundamental flaws in the method itself. These points can be addressed in a final version of the paper. I recommend acceptance.
