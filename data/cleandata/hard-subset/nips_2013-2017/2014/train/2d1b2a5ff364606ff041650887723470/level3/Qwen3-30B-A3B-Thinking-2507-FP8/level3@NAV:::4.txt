

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for Additive Factorial HMMs (AFHMMs), addressing the identifiability problem in blind source separation by incorporating domain knowledge about expected total signal aggregates. The authors propose SACAFHMM, which constrains the sum of each hidden chain's output to match known aggregate values, and derive a convex quadratic program for efficient MAP inference. The method is applied to energy disaggregation on large-scale real-world datasets (100 UK homes with low-frequency data and 10 homes with higher-frequency data), demonstrating significant improvements over baseline AFHMM and state-of-the-art AFAMAP.

The work makes a compelling contribution by bridging theoretical modeling with practical application. The convex relaxation approach is technically sound and well-justified through connection to posterior regularization. The empirical results are convincing, showing 33% and 16% improvements in NDE and SAE over AFAMAP on real-world energy data, with computational complexity comparable to existing methods. The paper clearly demonstrates how domain knowledge can be systematically incorporated to improve unsupervised learning.

However, some important clarifications are needed. The paper conflates model names and inference procedures (e.g., referring to AFAMAP as a model rather than an algorithm for AFHMM), which creates confusion. The authors should precisely clarify that AFAMAP is an approximate MAP inference algorithm for AFHMM, while AFHMM results likely refer to alternating Viterbi. Additionally, the paper does not address whether SACAFHMM yields more integral solutions (like AFAMAP's "one chain state at a time" constraint) or simply attains lower error through non-integral solutions. The authors should investigate this directly.

The orthogonal relationship between AFAMAP and SACAFHMM suggests a promising direction: combining SAC constraints into AFAMAP could yield further improvements. The paper would be strengthened by exploring this combination in a revision.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper with clear significance for energy disaggregation and a valuable contribution to the literature on incorporating domain knowledge into probabilistic models. The empirical validation on large-scale real-world data provides strong evidence for the approach's effectiveness. The minor clarifications requested would substantially enhance the paper's technical precision without requiring major revisions. This work represents a meaningful advancement in the field with clear practical implications.
