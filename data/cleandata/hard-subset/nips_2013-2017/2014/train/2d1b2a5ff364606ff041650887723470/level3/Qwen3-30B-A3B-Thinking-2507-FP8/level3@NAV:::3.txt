

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation within an Additive Factorial HMM framework, specifically applied to energy disaggregation. SACs encourage source signals to aggregate to known total values (e.g., expected daily energy usage per appliance), addressing the identifiability problem in BSS. The authors formulate a convex quadratic program to efficiently solve the constrained optimization problem, demonstrating significant improvements over baseline AFHMM and AFAMAP on both synthetic and real-world energy data.

The paper excels in clarity and mathematical foundation. The problem motivation is strong, particularly for energy disaggregation where domain knowledge about appliance usage is available. The technical approach is sound, with the convex relaxation well-justified and implemented using standard tools. Results are compelling: SACAFHMM achieves 45% lower NDE and 29% better SAE than AFAMAP on real-world data (Table 3), with statistical significance confirmed via paired t-tests.

However, several concerns warrant attention. The comparison methodology appears unfair: baselines (AFHMM and AFAMAP) use less training data than SACAFHMM, potentially inflating performance differences. Figure 1 is particularly problematic—it shows AFAMAP outperforming SACAFHMM at large constraint distances (>1e4), contradicting the claim that SACs "always help" and suggesting poorly specified constraints can degrade performance. This undermines the paper's central claim and requires clarification. The authors should acknowledge this limitation and explore how to select appropriate constraint values.

The paper could be strengthened by extending SACs to more complex constraints (e.g., mutual exclusivity, duration limits) mentioned in the key points. While the current focus on aggregate sums is valuable, broader constraint types would enhance applicability. The relation to posterior regularization (Section 6) is well-handled but could be more explicitly contrasted with related work.

Overall, this is a high-quality technical contribution with strong experimental validation. The core idea of SACs is novel for BSS and well-motivated. Addressing the comparison fairness and Figure 1's misleading implication would significantly strengthen the paper. The results demonstrate clear significance for energy disaggregation, a practically important problem. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify limitations and improve methodology transparency.
