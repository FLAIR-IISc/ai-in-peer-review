## Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation problems, specifically within the context of energy disaggregation using an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM). The core idea is to constrain the total signal for each unknown source to be close to a specified value, leveraging prior knowledge about expected energy consumption levels. The authors formulate this as a convex quadratic program for approximate inference and demonstrate significant performance improvements on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, exceeding both a baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach.

The problem of energy disaggregation is well-motivated, and the inherent identifiability issues in blind source separation are clearly articulated. The proposed SACs represent a sensible approach to injecting prior knowledge, building on existing work in posterior regularization and related techniques. The connection to posterior regularization, explicitly drawn in Section 6, is a valuable addition, providing a theoretical grounding for the proposed method. The empirical results are promising, demonstrating a substantial improvement in disaggregation accuracy. 

However, the paper suffers significantly from clarity issues. The excessive use of acronyms – many of which are quite long (e.g., AFHMM+SAC) – makes the text dense and difficult to follow. While acronyms are common, their proliferation here hinders readability. More importantly, the authors implicitly apply the constraint as a prior, influencing the posterior distribution. This crucial point needs explicit clarification; stating that the constraint is *a priori* and therefore constrains the posterior would significantly improve the paper’s rigor. 

Furthermore, while the authors acknowledge the relaxed nature of their optimization problem, a more detailed discussion of the implications of this relaxation would be beneficial. The computational complexity discussion is brief; a more thorough analysis, perhaps comparing scaling with the number of states and chains, would strengthen the evaluation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of signal aggregate constraints to address identifiability in BSS.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements on a real-world dataset.
*   Clear motivation and connection to a relevant application area.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Poor readability due to excessive and lengthy acronyms.
*   Lack of explicit statement regarding the a priori nature of the constraint.
*   Limited discussion of the implications of the optimization relaxation.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution with a promising approach. However, substantial improvements to clarity and rigor are needed before it is suitable for publication. I recommend acceptance with major revisions, specifically addressing the issues of acronym usage and the clarification of the constraint’s application as a prior.
