- Introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation, applied to energy disaggregation with AFHMMs.  
- Motivates SACs by noting aggregate source totals are often known and vary widely, so constraints encourage source aggregates to match specified values.  
- Formulates a relaxed optimization as a convex quadratic program (CQP) enabling efficient inference.  
- Demonstrates large empirical gains: 45% NDE reduction vs AFHMM and 29% improvement vs AFAMAP on Household Electricity Survey data.  
- Identifies a practical class of domain-knowledge constraints that improve identifiability in BSS.  
- Provides theoretical grounding and links SAC formulation to posterior regularization.  
- Paper is well-written with clear motivation and logical flow from method to evaluation.  
- Main strength: theoretically sound, computationally efficient CQP solution for a useful constraint type.  
- Main strength: compelling empirical results on synthetic and real-world datasets.  
- Weakness: potential unfair baseline comparison because SAC-AFHMM uses a training-derived total consumption vector (NTC) while baselines do not.  
- Weakness: Figure 1 shows SAC-AFHMM can be outperformed by AFAMAP when constraint error is large, indicating poorly specified constraints can worsen performance.  
- Weakness: insufficient exploration of extending SACs to other common energy-disaggregation constraints (mutual exclusivity, cardinality, duration).  
- Evaluation could be improved by testing a wider range of constraint values and comparing to other aggregate-constraint methods.  
- Evaluation suggestion: compare SAC-AFHMM to an AFHMM variant that also incorporates aggregate constraints to ensure a fair comparison.  
- Evaluation suggestion: provide detailed analysis of failure modes and guidance for selecting appropriate constraint values.  
- Quality: high; Clarity: excellent; Originality: moderate; Significance: strong for energy disaggregation applications.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address fairness of comparisons, Figure 1’s implications, and extensions/discussion of more complex constraints.
