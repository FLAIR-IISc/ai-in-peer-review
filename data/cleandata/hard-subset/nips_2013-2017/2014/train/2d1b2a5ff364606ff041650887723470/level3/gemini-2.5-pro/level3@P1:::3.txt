### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), specifically in the context of underdetermined systems where a single mixed signal must be decomposed into multiple source signals. The authors focus on the application of energy disaggregation, where the goal is to separate a household's total electricity consumption into the consumption of individual appliances. The core challenge in this domain is the inherent unidentifiability of the sources, which makes it difficult to recover meaningful, interpretable signals.

The main contribution of this work is the introduction of "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) as a form of domain knowledge to regularize the BSS problem. SACs are based on the observation that the total energy consumed by each source (e.g., an appliance) over a period is often known or can be well-estimated. The authors incorporate these constraints into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a standard model for this task. Since the SACs are non-Markovian and complicate inference, the authors propose a convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation to find an approximate MAP solution efficiently. The paper also demonstrates the connection between their penalized objective function and the posterior regularization framework. The proposed method, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on both synthetic data and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES), showing significant improvements over the baseline AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP algorithm.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints is a simple yet powerful idea for injecting valuable domain knowledge into a notoriously difficult, underdetermined problem. This type of constraint is intuitive and practical for many BSS applications beyond energy disaggregation (e.g., estimating total expression levels in bioinformatics or total speaking time in audio separation). The work addresses a significant challenge in making BSS solutions interpretable and practically useful.

2.  **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the problem is clear, and the derivation of the convex quadratic program for approximate inference is well-explained. Grounding the approach in the posterior regularization framework adds theoretical justification and connects it to a broader class of methods for incorporating prior knowledge into probabilistic models. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using a relevant and challenging real-world dataset.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the problem is clearly established in the introduction, the proposed model is described logically, and the results are presented effectively. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the intuition behind SACs and the necessity of the CQP relaxation for inference.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparison:** The primary weakness of the evaluation is that the proposed AFHMM+SAC method is provided with additional information (the target aggregate values, µi0) that the baseline AFHMM and AFAMAP models do not have access to. While the results demonstrate the value of this information, it makes the comparison somewhat unfair. The improvement may stem more from the additional data than from the superiority of the modeling framework itself. A fairer comparison might involve trying to incorporate this aggregate information into the baselines, for instance, by using it to initialize the parameters or as a post-processing step to resolve label permutations.

2.  **Sensitivity to Constraint Quality:** The toy data experiment in Figure 1 is meant to show robustness, but it also reveals a potential fragility. When the Euclidean distance between the provided µ0 and the true signal aggregate exceeds ~1.2e4, the NDE of AFHMM+SAC becomes *worse* than that of AFAMAP. This suggests that if the prior knowledge encapsulated in the SAC is sufficiently inaccurate, the constraint can be actively harmful to performance. This is a critical point that is under-explored in the paper. The authors should explicitly discuss this limitation and the sensitivity of the model to the quality of the µi0 values, as this has significant implications for practical deployment.

3.  **Generality of the Constraint Framework:** The paper briefly mentions extending SACs to specific time windows but focuses only on a single aggregate constraint per chain. The proposed SACs are a specific type of linear, sum-based constraint. A key question is how well the proposed CQP framework could be generalized to handle other, more complex types of domain knowledge that are common in these problems. For instance:
    *   **Mutual Exclusivity Constraints:** E.g., a heating system and an air conditioning system are never on at the same time.
    *   **Cardinality Constraints:** E.g., at most 'k' high-power appliances are active simultaneously.
    *   **Duration Constraints:** E.g., a refrigerator compressor runs for at least 5 minutes once it starts.
    A discussion of the framework's potential (or limitations) for incorporating such constraints would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and highlight its broader significance for structured inference problems.

**Summary of Recommendation**

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel, well-motivated, and practical idea for improving blind source separation. The technical execution is solid, and the experimental results are compelling. While the experimental comparison could be made fairer and the discussion of the method's limitations could be expanded, these points do not detract from the core contribution. The paper is well-suited for the NIPS audience and is likely to inspire follow-up work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and highly intuitive method (SACs) for regularizing a challenging, underdetermined problem.
*   The CQP formulation for inference is technically sound and efficient.
*   Demonstrates significant empirical improvements over strong baselines on a relevant, real-world task.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments for rejection/revision:**
*   The experimental comparison is potentially unfair, as the proposed method uses extra information unavailable to the baselines.
*   The paper does not adequately address the model's sensitivity to inaccurate constraints, a critical practical concern.
*   The scope of the constraint framework is limited, and its generalizability to other common types of constraints is not discussed.
