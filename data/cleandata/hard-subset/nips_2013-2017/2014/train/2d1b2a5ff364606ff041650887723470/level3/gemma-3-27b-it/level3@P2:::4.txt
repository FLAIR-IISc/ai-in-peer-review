## Review of Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation, specifically within the framework of Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs). The core idea is to encourage the total signal for each unknown source to align with expected values, a constraint often readily available in real-world applications like energy disaggregation. The authors formulate this as a convex quadratic program for approximate inference and demonstrate significant performance improvements on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, surpassing both the original AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in blind source separation and energy disaggregation, properly referencing key papers such as [8, 12, 17, 22]. The problem of identifiability in BSS is well-motivated, and the authors effectively highlight the practical need for incorporating prior knowledge to achieve meaningful results, particularly in applications where interpretability is crucial. The discussion of related work, including posterior regularization [4], the generalized expectation criterion [14], and measurement-based learning [13], positions SACs within a broader context of prior incorporation techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Practical Relevance:** The application to energy disaggregation is a strong point. This is a challenging, real-world problem with significant practical implications, and the demonstrated improvements are compelling. The use of the HES dataset, a large and representative dataset, adds credibility.
* **Simple and Effective Idea:** The core concept of SACs is intuitive and easy to understand. The mathematical formulation, while involving a convex relaxation, appears tractable and well-motivated.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results presented in Section 7 demonstrate a clear benefit from incorporating SACs, with a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over AFAMAP [12].
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results. The mathematical notation is reasonably accessible.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors address the computational complexity and show that the proposed method scales similarly to existing approaches (AFHMM and AFAMAP).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Integral vs. Non-Integral Solutions:** A crucial point raised by the key points is the nature of the solutions obtained. Does the SAC formulation tend towards more integral solutions – similar to the “one chain state at a time” constraint of AFAMAP – or does it achieve lower error despite yielding non-integral state occupancies? Clarification on this would be valuable. 
* **Model/Inference Conflation:** The paper occasionally conflates models and inference procedures. For instance, referring to “AFAMAP” without explicitly stating it’s an approximate MAP *algorithm* for the AFHMM can be misleading.  Similarly, stating “AFHMM results” may refer to alternating Viterbi, which is an inference method and not inherent to the AFHMM model itself. More precision in this regard would improve clarity.
* **Limited Exploration of Combinations:** While the authors acknowledge the orthogonality of SACAFHMM and AFAMAP, they don’t explore the potential benefits of combining these approaches. Incorporating SACs *into* AFAMAP, as suggested, is a promising direction for future work and could potentially yield even more significant improvements.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning the complexity parameter λ, but the details of this process aren’t fully described. A more thorough discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and selection would strengthen the analysis.

**Originality:**

The introduction of signal aggregate constraints is a novel contribution. While the AFHMM itself is established, the specific incorporation of these constraints represents a new approach to addressing the identifiability problem in BSS. The convex programming formulation for efficient inference is also a valuable addition. The work demonstrates a non-trivial extension of existing techniques to leverage domain knowledge in a principled manner.

**Significance:**

The results presented suggest that SACs have the potential to significantly improve the accuracy of energy disaggregation systems, a technology with considerable environmental and economic benefits. Beyond energy disaggregation, the concept of incorporating aggregate constraints is broadly applicable to other BSS problems where prior knowledge about the total signal of individual sources is available. The method provides a practical and computationally efficient way to leverage this knowledge.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a challenging problem with strong practical relevance.
* Introduces a novel and intuitive approach to incorporating prior knowledge.
* Demonstrates significant performance improvements on a real-world dataset.
* The method is computationally tractable and well-motivated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Requires clarification regarding the nature of the solutions obtained (integral vs. non-integral).
* Needs greater precision in differentiating between models and inference procedures.
* Could benefit from exploring combinations with other techniques like AFAMAP.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation and energy disaggregation. The proposed approach is simple, effective, and supported by compelling empirical results. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above – specifically clarifying the nature of the solutions and sharpening the distinction between models and inference – would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon these clarifications and a brief discussion of the potential for combining SACAFHMM with other techniques like AFAMAP.
