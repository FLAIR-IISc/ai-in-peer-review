This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation problems, specifically within the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework. The core idea is to regularize the MAP inference objective by penalizing solutions where the total, time-aggregated output of a hidden source chain deviates from a known or expected aggregate value. The authors formulate this constrained optimization problem as a penalized log-posterior and derive a convex relaxation that can be solved efficiently as a convex quadratic program (CQP). This approach is situated in the context of prior work on energy disaggregation, particularly the standard AFHMM and the AFAMAP algorithm by Kolter & Jaakkola (2012), which uses a different type of structural constraint. The method is validated on the challenging problem of energy disaggregation using two large, real-world datasets from the UK Household Electricity Survey, demonstrating significant improvements over both baseline AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP.

### Strengths

*   **Simplicity and Intuition:** The proposed Signal Aggregate Constraint is a straightforward, intuitive, and powerful way to incorporate domain knowledge. In many BSS problems, like energy disaggregation, priors on the total consumption of an appliance are often available from national surveys or manufacturer specifications. The paper's approach of directly penalizing deviations from these priors is elegant and well-motivated.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper's main strength lies in its convincing and large-scale empirical evaluation. The use of the HES dataset, with its low sampling frequency, presents a difficult and realistic challenge. The experiments on 100 households (with synthesized aggregate signals) and 9 households (with true mains readings) provide robust evidence for the method's effectiveness. The reported improvements over AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP are substantial and statistically significant.
*   **Combined Contribution:** The paper successfully combines an algorithmic contribution (the CQP formulation for SACs) with a strong applied result on a problem of significant practical interest. This blend of novel methodology and impactful application makes the work compelling and highly relevant to the NIPS community.
*   **Sound Technical Approach:** The formulation of the problem and its relaxation into a CQP is technically sound and well-explained. The connection to the posterior regularization framework further solidifies the theoretical grounding of the approach.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, there are several points that could be clarified or expanded upon to further increase its impact.

1.  **Properties of the CQP Solution:** The AFAMAP paper argues that its "one chain at a time" constraint is effective partly because it pushes the relaxed CQP solution towards being more integral (i.e., closer to binary 0/1 values). It is unclear from the current manuscript whether the SAC formulation has a similar effect. Does adding SACs lead to solutions that are more integral, or does the method achieve lower error despite potentially having highly fractional solutions? An analysis of the integrality gap or the distribution of the relaxed state variables `S_itk` with and without SACs would provide valuable insight into the mechanism behind the performance improvement.

2.  **Clarity on Models vs. Inference:** The paper occasionally conflates models with their inference procedures. For instance, AFAMAP is described alongside AFHMM as if it were a distinct model. To improve precision, it would be beneficial to consistently frame AFAMAP as an *approximate MAP inference algorithm* for the AFHMM model. Similarly, the baseline "AFHMM" results should be more clearly attributed to a specific inference procedure, which is presumably the chainwise alternating Viterbi algorithm mentioned in Section 4.1.

3.  **Combining Orthogonal Constraints:** The constraints introduced in this paper (aggregate-based) and those in AFAMAP (transition-based) are fundamentally orthogonal. This is an exciting observation, as it suggests they might be complementary. A very promising experiment, which would significantly strengthen a final version of the paper, would be to combine both approaches. This could be done by incorporating the SAC penalty term from equation (5) into the CQP formulation of AFAMAP. It is plausible that constraining both the aggregate totals and the instantaneous state transitions would yield even better performance than either method alone. This possibility is a key takeaway and should be discussed, even if experiments are left for future work.

### Overall Assessment

This is a well-written and well-executed paper that makes a clear and valuable contribution. The proposed extension to AFHMMs is simple but highly effective, and the authors support their claims with a thorough and convincing empirical evaluation on a challenging, real-world problem. The work successfully demonstrates how simple, intuitive domain knowledge can be elegantly incorporated into a probabilistic model to achieve state-of-the-art results. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to requests for deeper analysis and clarification, along with suggestions for very promising future experiments. The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance. Addressing the suggested points would further enhance an already successful piece of work.
