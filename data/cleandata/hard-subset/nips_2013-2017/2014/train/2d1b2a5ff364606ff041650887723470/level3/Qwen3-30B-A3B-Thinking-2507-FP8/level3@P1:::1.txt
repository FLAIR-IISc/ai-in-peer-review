

The paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation, specifically applied to energy disaggregation using Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs). SACs encourage the total signal for each source to be close to a specified value, leveraging domain knowledge about expected appliance energy consumption. The authors formulate the constrained optimization as a convex quadratic program (CQP), enabling efficient inference. This work extends AFHMMs, which have been applied to energy disaggregation [12, 22], and connects to posterior regularization [4] as shown in Section 6.

The paper's strengths are significant. First, SACs represent a novel constraint type for blind source separation, well-motivated by energy disaggregation where total appliance consumption is often known from prior studies. The theoretical contribution of transforming the constrained optimization into a CQP is well-executed, with clear derivation in Section 5 and insightful connection to posterior regularization in Section 6. The empirical evaluation is thorough: the toy dataset demonstrates SACs' robustness to errors in constraint specification (Figure 1), while real-world energy data shows statistically significant improvements over both the baseline AFHMM (45% NDE reduction) and state-of-the-art AFAMAP [12] (29% NDE improvement, p<0.05). The computational efficiency is comparable to AFAMAP, making the method practical for real-world applications.

However, several weaknesses require attention. The toy experiment uses an unrealistically low noise variance (σ²=0.01) in equation (1), creating a very high signal-to-noise ratio that makes source separation trivial. The authors should clarify whether this was chosen for demonstration purposes or reflects realistic energy data conditions, and provide a discussion of SNR in the energy data (both mains readings and appliance signals). The paper claims the problem in equation (6) is convex but lacks a formal proof, which is critical given the non-convex nature of the original problem. Additionally, while the authors state the CQP has "polynomial time in the number of time steps times the total number of states," they omit explicit theoretical complexity analysis. A detailed scaling analysis comparing tractability with AFAMAP for larger datasets would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with only a minor notation issue on page 4 (line 202) where an extra comma appears before p in "p(i)jk." The significance is strong for energy disaggregation, where the method provides practical improvements, and the SAC concept could potentially apply to other blind source separation domains where total signal information is available. The quality is high with technically sound methods and well-supported claims, and the originality is clear as SACs represent a new constraint class distinct from previous approaches that constrain state transitions [12, 21].

For acceptance, the paper offers a valuable contribution to blind source separation by providing a principled way to incorporate domain knowledge about total signal magnitudes. The main weaknesses (noise variance, convexity proof, complexity analysis) are fixable with minor revisions. The significance is particularly strong for energy disaggregation, where the method directly addresses a practical challenge in interpreting disaggregated appliance usage. The authors have satisfactorily addressed reviewer queries in feedback, suggesting these clarifications can be incorporated into the final version.

Arguments for acceptance: Novel SAC concept, strong empirical results with significant improvements over state-of-the-art, well-executed theoretical derivation, good relevance to energy disaggregation.

Arguments against acceptance: Toy experiment with unrealistically low noise variance, missing formal convexity proof, incomplete theoretical complexity analysis, minor notation issue.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to blind source separation by introducing SACs and demonstrating their effectiveness in energy disaggregation. With minor revisions to address the theoretical concerns and clarify the toy experiment setup, this work would be a strong contribution to the NIPS community. The significance for energy disaggregation is substantial, and the method has potential for broader application in other blind source separation domains. I recommend acceptance.
