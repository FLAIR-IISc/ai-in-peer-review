This paper proposes an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) with signal aggregate constraints (SAC) for blind source separation problems, specifically in the context of energy disaggregation. The authors introduce a novel approach to incorporate domain knowledge into the problem by encouraging the total signal for each of the unknown sources to be close to a specified value. This is achieved through the use of SACs, which are incorporated into the AFHMM to formulate the energy disaggregation problem. A convex quadratic program is employed for approximate inference, allowing for efficient computation.

The paper is well-written and has a solid mathematical foundation, providing a clear motivation for the problem and a thorough explanation of the proposed approach. The authors demonstrate promising results on both synthetic and real-world data, showing significant improvements in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) compared to the original AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach.

However, there are some concerns regarding the comparison with baseline methods. Specifically, the baselines (AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP) use less data than the SACAFHMM, which may give the latter an unfair advantage. It would be beneficial to evaluate other methods that could incorporate SAC, even if they are less computationally efficient, to provide a more comprehensive comparison.

Furthermore, Figure 1 is unclear, and it appears that AFAMAP outperforms SACAFHMM at distances greater than 1e4, suggesting that bad constraints can worsen performance. This contradicts the claim that SAC always helps, and it would be helpful to clarify this point.

The authors suggest evaluating other methods that could incorporate SAC and extending the approach to more complex constraint types, such as mutual exclusivity, cardinality limits, and duration limits. This is a promising direction for future work, as it could allow for more flexible and robust modeling of complex systems.

In terms of the quality of the paper, it is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed approach and its advantages. The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work and demonstrate a clear understanding of the problem domain.

However, there are some minor issues with the writing style. For example, some of the sentences are quite long and convoluted, making them difficult to follow. Additionally, there are some typos and grammatical errors throughout the paper.

Overall, this paper presents a novel and promising approach to blind source separation problems, specifically in the context of energy disaggregation. While there are some concerns regarding the comparison with baseline methods and the clarity of some of the figures, the paper is well-written and provides a solid mathematical foundation for the proposed approach.
