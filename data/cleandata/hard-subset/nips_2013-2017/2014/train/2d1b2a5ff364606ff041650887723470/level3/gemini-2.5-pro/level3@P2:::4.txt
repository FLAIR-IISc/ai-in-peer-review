This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation (BSS) problems, with a specific application to energy disaggregation. The central idea is that for many BSS tasks, one has prior knowledge about the expected total aggregate value of each hidden source signal over a period of time. The authors incorporate these constraints into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a common probabilistic model for such problems. As exact inference in this model (AFHMM+SAC) is intractable due to the global nature of the constraints, the authors formulate MAP inference as a penalized optimization problem and derive a convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation that can be solved efficiently. This work relates to prior efforts that add constraints to AFHMMs, such as AFAMAP [12], which constrains state transitions, and semi-Markov models [11, 10], which constrain state durations. The SAC approach provides a novel and complementary form of regularization. The method is evaluated extensively on a large, real-world low-frequency energy consumption dataset from over 100 households, demonstrating significant performance improvements over both a baseline AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP algorithm.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses the critical problem of identifiability in BSS by introducing a simple, intuitive, and highly effective form of domain knowledge. The idea of constraining the aggregate signal is broadly applicable beyond energy disaggregation to other domains where such priors are available.
*   **Quality:** The empirical evaluation is a major strength. The use of a large-scale, challenging, real-world dataset (the UK HES dataset) provides convincing evidence for the method's effectiveness. The results, showing a 29-45% error reduction over strong baselines, are impressive and clearly demonstrate the value of the proposed constraints.
*   **Originality:** While building on the established AFHMM framework, the introduction of SACs is a novel contribution. The formulation of the problem and its solution via a CQP relaxation is technically sound and provides a practical algorithm. The combination of a straightforward algorithmic idea with a compelling, large-scale application makes for a strong NIPS paper.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Analysis of the CQP Solution:** The authors solve a relaxed version of the integer program. The related AFAMAP work [12] argues that its sparsity-promoting constraints encourage more integral solutions from its convex relaxation. It is unclear if the proposed SACs have a similar regularizing effect on the solution's integrality. Does AFHMM+SAC achieve its superior performance by finding solutions that are inherently "more discrete" than the standard AFHMM relaxation, or does it simply find a better fractional solution? A short discussion or analysis of the integrality gap of the CQP solutions would provide valuable insight and strengthen the paper's technical contribution.
*   **Combining Orthogonal Constraints:** The proposed SACs constrain the global sum of each source, whereas the constraints in AFAMAP focus on local state transitions (i.e., at most one appliance changes state at a time). These two approaches are fundamentally orthogonal and complementary. A natural and promising experiment would be to combine both types of constraints by adding the SAC penalty term to the AFAMAP objective function. This could potentially lead to even better performance and would substantially strengthen the paper's contribution by showing how these different forms of domain knowledge can be unified. If conducting this experiment is not feasible for the final version, a discussion of this possibility is highly recommended.
*   **Precision in Terminology (Models vs. Inference):** The paper could be more precise in its distinction between probabilistic models and the inference algorithms used to solve them. For example, AFAMAP is presented as a competing approach, but it is more accurately an approximate MAP inference algorithm for the AFHMM model. The baseline results for "AFHMM" presumably arise from a specific inference procedure (e.g., the CQP relaxation with λi=0, or alternating Viterbi as mentioned in Section 4.1). Clarifying this would improve the paper's technical precision (e.g., by referring to "AFHMM with AFAMAP inference" vs. "AFHMM with SAC-penalized CQP inference").

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-executed paper presenting a simple but compelling idea with strong empirical backing. The introduction of signal aggregate constraints is a valuable contribution to the energy disaggregation literature and the broader field of blind source separation. The experimental results on a large-scale, real-world dataset are convincing. While the paper would benefit from the clarifications and additional analysis suggested above—particularly regarding the combination with AFAMAP's constraints—it stands as a solid and successful contribution. I recommend acceptance.
