This paper introduces a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation (BSS) problems by proposing "signal aggregate constraints" (SACs). The core idea is that for many BSS applications, one has prior knowledge about the expected total aggregate value of each hidden source signal over a period of time. The authors formalize this by adding a penalty term to the objective function of an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM), encouraging the sum of each source signal to be close to a specified value. Since this constraint is non-Markovian and makes exact MAP inference intractable, the authors propose an efficient approximate inference scheme based on a convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation. The paper relates this formulation to the posterior regularization framework. The method is evaluated on a toy dataset and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, where it is shown to significantly outperform a standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP algorithm.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The mathematical formulation is sound, and the derivation of the CQP for approximate inference is clear and correct. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using both synthetic data to demonstrate the method's robustness and a challenging real-world dataset (HES) to show its practical utility. The reported improvements, particularly the 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) compared to the standard AFHMM and a 29% improvement over AFAMAP, are substantial.

However, there are a few points that weaken the claims:
1.  The experimental comparison may not be entirely fair. The proposed AFHMM+SAC is provided with additional information (the target aggregate values `µ_i0`) that the baseline models (AFHMM and AFAMAP) do not have access to. While demonstrating the value of this information is the paper's goal, the improvement is not just due to a better model or inference algorithm but also to this extra data. It would strengthen the paper to discuss how this type of prior knowledge could be integrated into the baselines for a more direct comparison, or to more explicitly frame the contribution as a way to leverage this specific kind of prior information.
2.  The paper claims that the use of SACs "dramatically improves" performance. While true when the prior knowledge is accurate, the analysis could be more nuanced. In Figure 1, the NDE and SAE increase as the provided constraint value moves away from the true value. It would be valuable to show at what point a poorly specified constraint becomes detrimental, potentially making performance worse than the unconstrained AFHMM or AFAMAP. This is crucial for practitioners who may only have rough estimates for the aggregate values.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for addressing the unidentifiability problem in BSS is compelling, and the proposed solution is presented logically. The problem setting, model, and inference algorithm are all described with sufficient detail for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work.

**Originality:**
The core contribution—the concept of signal aggregate constraints for BSS—is novel and insightful. While constrained HMMs are an established research area (e.g., AFAMAP [12], k-segment constraints [19]), the use of a global, non-Markovian constraint on the total signal sum is a new approach. The proposed CQP formulation for this specific problem is also a novel contribution. The paper does a good job of situating its work within the context of related literature on energy disaggregation and constrained probabilistic models.

**Significance:**
The work is highly significant for the energy disaggregation community and has broader implications for other BSS applications where similar aggregate priors are available (e.g., audio source separation where the total energy of a speaker is known, or in computational biology). The paper demonstrates a practical and computationally efficient method to incorporate valuable domain knowledge, leading to substantial performance gains on a difficult real-world problem.

The significance could be further enhanced by discussing the framework's generality. The SACs presented are a specific type of linear constraint on the state variables. It would be interesting to see a discussion on how this CQP-based framework could be extended to incorporate other useful constraint types, such as:
*   Mutual exclusivity constraints (e.g., a refrigerator compressor and its defrost cycle are never on simultaneously).
*   Cardinality constraints (e.g., at most `k` appliances are on at any time).
*   Duration constraints (e.g., an appliance must remain in a state for a minimum amount of time), which are often handled by semi-Markov models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Introduces a novel, well-motivated, and intuitive type of constraint (SAC) for addressing the critical identifiability issue in BSS.
2.  Provides a technically sound and computationally efficient CQP-based inference algorithm.
3.  Demonstrates significant and compelling performance improvements over strong baselines on a challenging and important real-world application.
4.  The paper is very clearly written and structured.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The experimental comparison is slightly weakened by the fact that the proposed model receives additional prior information that the baselines do not.
2.  The robustness of the method to inaccurate prior information is not fully explored, which is a key practical consideration. The potential for bad constraints to worsen performance is understated.

**Recommendation:**
Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel and significant contribution that is technically sound and empirically validated. I recommend acceptance. The authors are encouraged to address the points regarding the fairness of the comparison and the sensitivity to inaccurate priors in the final version of the paper. A discussion on generalizing the framework to other constraint types would also be a valuable addition.
