This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation problems and incorporates them into an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) to address the identifiability problem. The authors propose a convex quadratic program for approximate inference, which is computationally efficient. The paper is well-written, and the mathematical foundation is solid. The problem motivation is clear, and the authors provide a good overview of the related work.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of SACs, which encourage the total signal for each of the unknown sources to be close to a specified value. This is a novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge into the blind source separation problem. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, showing a significant improvement over the original AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach.

The experimental results are promising, with the AFHMM+SAC outperforming the AFHMM and AFAMAP in terms of both normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE). The authors also show that the AFHMM+SAC is robust to errors in specification of the constraint value.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The comparison between the AFHMM+SAC and the baselines (AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP) may be unfair, as the baselines use less data than the AFHMM+SAC. The authors should consider evaluating other methods that could incorporate SACs, even if they are less computationally efficient.

Additionally, Figure 1 is unclear, and the authors should provide more details about the plot. The figure suggests that AFAMAP outperforms the AFHMM+SAC at distances greater than 1e4, which implies that bad constraints can worsen performance. This contradicts the claim that SACs always help.

The authors suggest evaluating other methods that could incorporate SACs, which is a good direction for future work. They also recommend extending the algorithm to handle more complex constraint types, such as mutual exclusivity, cardinality limits, and duration limits.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the contributions are significant. The authors provide a novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge into the blind source separation problem, and the experimental results are promising. However, the comparison between the AFHMM+SAC and the baselines should be more carefully evaluated, and the authors should provide more details about the plots.
