Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation and instantiates them in an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) for electricity disaggregation. The core idea is to encode domain knowledge about the total energy used by each latent source over a time horizon and to penalize deviations from these totals. The AFHMM+SAC yields a penalized MAP objective that does not factor over time; the authors introduce transition-indicator variables to derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) relaxation, which they solve with CVX. Empirically, SACs substantially improve disaggregation accuracy on a large, low-frequency, real-world dataset (HES). The paper situates itself relative to AFHMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan), AFAMAP’s one-change constraint (Kolter & Jaakkola), semi-Markov approaches to durations, and general frameworks for incorporating domain knowledge such as posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.), generalized expectation, and measurement-based learning.

Strengths
- Clear modeling contribution: a simple, intuitive class of constraints (aggregated energy totals) that are broadly applicable in BSS and well-motivated in NILM.
- Solid optimization: convex relaxation with PSD structure is neatly derived; the additional transition variables are standard yet effective.
- Empirical gains: sizable improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP on realistic, low-frequency data; robustness to moderate misspecification of totals demonstrated on a toy example.
- Practical relevance: leverages external statistics (e.g., national appliance totals) that are often available, addressing identifiability in a domain-relevant way.

Weaknesses
- Clarity/readability: the manuscript is notation-heavy and very hard to read; excessive use of acronyms (some unusually long) harms accessibility.
- Posterior vs prior language: the constraint is effectively imposed as an a priori quadratic potential on latent trajectories; the paper should explicitly state this and clarify the relationship to posterior regularization to avoid confusion.
- Discrete solution and scalability: the CQP is relaxed; the paper does not clearly describe how fractional solutions are projected to discrete state sequences, nor provide complexity bounds beyond “similar scaling” and CVX runtimes.
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: λ is fixed to 1 without principled selection; sensitivity to λ and to errors in µ0 is only briefly explored.
- Evaluation details: reliance on TTC oracle highlights an upper bound but could be overemphasized; ablations (e.g., number of states, effect of NTC quality, windowed SACs) are limited.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The CQP derivation appears correct; Λi and Vt being PSD makes the relaxation convex. The connection to posterior regularization is sketched and aligns with tilting the model posterior by exp(−∑i λiϕi). However:
  1) Discretization: how are continuous Sit and hitjk mapped back to valid discrete sequences for reporting NDE/SAE? If rounding or decoding is used, please detail the procedure and its theoretical/empirical impact.
  2) Complexity and scalability: solving large CQPs with CVX can be slow; provide per-problem sizes (I, Ki, T) and asymptotic complexity, and discuss more scalable solvers (e.g., specialized QP, ADMM).
  3) Hyperparameter selection: report cross-validation or heuristics for λ, and a sensitivity sweep on real data (beyond the toy).
  4) Parameter learning: θ is treated as known; clarify training protocol, regularization, initialization (k-means for µi), and sensitivity to Ki.

Clarity
- The paper is difficult to read due to dense notation, excessive acronyms, and long variable blocks. Suggestions:
  - Reduce and standardize acronyms; avoid introducing rarely used or unusually long acronyms; consider a small glossary.
  - Provide a high-level overview figure illustrating AFHMM+SAC and the aggregate constraints.
  - Reorder Sections 4–6 to present the SAC intuition and its effect on the posterior first, then the MAP objective, then the CQP derivation.
  - Add a brief algorithmic summary (inputs, outputs, solver, post-processing).
  - Include a notational table and simplify repeated Kronecker constructions with clear indexing.
- Posterior vs prior language: please state explicitly that SACs are an a priori constraint on latent sequences (a quadratic prior over S) that consequently constrains the posterior; then relate this to the posterior-regularization view (tilting of P(S|Y)).

Originality
- The specific formulation of signal-total constraints within an AFHMM and the associated convex relaxation is, to my knowledge, novel in the NILM/BSS literature. The work is well-related to AFAMAP, duration modeling, and posterior regularization/measurement-based learning. While “total-sum” priors are conceptually simple, the combination with AFHMM inference and the clean convex relaxation represents a meaningful, original contribution.

Significance
- The demonstrated gains on a challenging, low-frequency dataset are practically important for NILM and broadly applicable to BSS tasks where aggregate totals are known. The approach is simple enough to be adopted and extended (e.g., windowed SACs, hierarchical priors over µ0). Clarifying scalability and providing code would further enhance impact.

Specific questions and suggestions
1) Discretization: Describe how you obtain discrete Zit from the relaxed solution. If you use argmax per time/chain plus local repair, please state it; report the fractionality observed and its effect on performance.
2) Hyperparameters: Provide a small grid over λ on HES and report NDE/SAE sensitivity. Consider learning λ by evidence maximization or via L-curve heuristics.
3) Uncertainty in µ0: Explore modeling µ0 with uncertainty (e.g., Gaussian prior) leading to an L2 penalty with data-driven weight; or learn µ0 jointly with S under weak priors.
4) Windowed SACs: Include a brief experiment with daytime/nighttime aggregates to demonstrate the flexibility of the constraint family.
5) Comparisons: Consider AFAMAP augmented with SACs to isolate the benefit of the aggregate information from the benefit of the convex relaxation itself.
6) Runtime: Report problem sizes and wall-clock times by house/day; discuss alternatives to CVX for scalability.
7) Reproducibility: Release code and preprocessing scripts; specify training/test splits, appliance lists, and exact feature scaling.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated idea with clear practical value.
- Sound convex formulation and substantial empirical gains on real data.
- Broad applicability beyond energy disaggregation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Manuscript is hard to read; heavy notation and acronyms hurt accessibility.
- Missing details on discretization, hyperparameter selection, and scalability.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to µ0 and λ on real data.

Overall recommendation
Leaning accept. The paper presents a useful, original modeling idea that delivers strong empirical improvements on an important application. Addressing clarity and the noted technical details would make it a strong contribution.
