Summary
The paper tackles blind source separation (BSS) with a focus on energy disaggregation. It introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs): soft constraints that encourage each latent source’s total signal over time to match a specified target. The model embeds SACs into an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM), yielding AFHMM+SAC. Because SACs are non-Markovian, the authors derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) by introducing transition variables and relaxing integrality, enabling efficient approximate MAP inference. They also connect their objective to posterior regularization. Experiments on synthetic data and on the low-frequency HES energy dataset show notable gains in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) relative to AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12), with similar runtimes.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well positioned relative to: posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., JMLR’10), GE criteria (Mann & McCallum), measurement-based learning (Liang et al.), AFHMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan), AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola), semi-Markov approaches (Johnson & Willsky), interleaved factorial non-homogeneous HMMs (Zhong et al.), k-segment constraints (Titsias et al.), and aggregate constraints in probabilistic DBs. The proposed SACs are a natural and practically motivated class of posterior constraints for BSS; the convex relaxation for AFHMM with aggregate penalties appears new.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: aggregate usage is often known or can be estimated (e.g., national appliance consumption), addressing identifiability.
- Solid technical contribution: careful derivation of a CQP via auxiliary transition variables and demonstration that the quadratic terms are PSD, making the maximization concave.
- Useful connection to posterior regularization clarifies the statistical meaning of the penalty.
- Empirical gains on a realistic, low-frequency dataset where disaggregation is hard; improvements over AFAMAP are practically meaningful.
- Good writing and organization; the modeling choices and optimization are easy to follow.

Weaknesses
- Fairness of comparisons: AFHMM+SAC uses side information (NTC or even TTC/oracle) that baselines do not. This may conflate the benefits of the model architecture with the benefits of additional supervision.
- Limited baselines: strong alternatives that could incorporate SACs are not evaluated (e.g., AFAMAP with the same aggregate penalty; semi-Markov or k-segment HMM variants with SACs), even if slower.
- Robustness claims: the toy study shows degradation when µ0 is far from the truth; Figure 1 suggests AFAMAP can outperform AFHMM+SAC when constraints are poor. The paper sometimes reads as if SACs “always help,” which is misleading.
- Hyperparameter handling: λi is fixed to 1 across all experiments. Lack of tuning makes it unclear whether results are near-optimal and how sensitive performance is to λ.
- Discrete interpretability: the relaxation yields fractional state occupancies; the paper does not discuss rounding/projection and its impact on interpretability, which is central to disaggregation.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The relaxation is well justified; the objective is concave in the relaxed variables with linear constraints, solvable by off-the-shelf solvers. The posterior-regularization derivation is correct. Empirical methodology is mostly reasonable, but:
- Comparisons should control for side information. A fair test would add the same SAC penalty to AFAMAP (or AFHMM) and compare.
- Hyperparameters should be tuned (e.g., cross-validation or validation set). Reporting sensitivity curves for λi would strengthen the case.
- Provide a rounding or decoding procedure from fractional S to discrete states, and report any effect on NDE/SAE.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, with helpful notation and derivations. Suggestions:
- Clarify Figure 1 axes and explicitly discuss the regime where incorrect constraints hurt (e.g., beyond a distance threshold ~1e4 in the toy). Temper claims accordingly.
- State precisely how x̂ is computed from fractional S (expected signal vs. rounded states).
- Expand on solver details (tolerances, warm starts, typical T, I, Ki, number of variables/constraints) to aid reproducibility.

Originality
Moderately novel. While posterior regularization is established, the identification, formulation, and efficient convex relaxation of aggregate constraints for AFHMMs is a useful contribution to BSS and disaggregation. The paper also extends the constraint idea beyond energy and argues for generality.

Significance
High practical relevance for non-intrusive load monitoring, especially at low sampling rates. The idea is likely reusable in other BSS settings (audio, biology) where aggregate priors exist. If the fairness and robustness concerns are addressed, the work could influence future constrained-inference methods for factorial models.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fair comparisons: augment AFAMAP and plain AFHMM with exactly the same SAC penalty and report results; also include a variant where SACs are learned from training data for all methods.
- Robustness: provide experiments where µ0 is misspecified by varying degrees, and show how tuning λ mitigates harm. Consider interval/inequality SACs (lower/upper bounds) or learned slack variables to reduce brittleness.
- Hyperparameter selection: tune λi; report sensitivity analyses.
- Broader constraints: discuss or prototype extensions to:
  - mutual exclusivity and cardinality limits across chains at a time t,
  - duration/sojourn constraints (semi-Markov style),
  - time-of-day windowed SACs (already hinted, but show a small experiment).
  Provide the corresponding convex relaxations where possible.
- Decoding: present a rounding scheme (e.g., per-time argmax, or projection to vertices) and its effect on interpretability and metrics.
- Reproducibility: release code or pseudocode and solver settings; specify the HES preprocessing.

Pros for acceptance
- Well-motivated, general idea; technically sound convex relaxation.
- Demonstrated, sizeable gains on a challenging real dataset.
- Clear link to posterior regularization; likely to inspire follow-on work.

Cons for acceptance
- Comparisons do not control for side information; the main gain may partly stem from extra supervision.
- Limited analysis of robustness to constraint misspecification and of hyperparameter sensitivity.
- No evaluation of stronger baselines augmented with the same SACs.

Recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on clarifying fairness and robustness and improving the empirical baselines. The core idea and formulation are valuable, and the empirical results are promising, but a more balanced evaluation would strengthen the case.

Reviewer confidence
High on modeling/optimization aspects; medium on the empirical claims pending fairer comparisons and robustness analysis.
