## Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework for blind source separation, specifically demonstrated through energy disaggregation. The core idea – incorporating prior knowledge about expected total signal values for unknown sources – is a sensible approach to address the inherent identifiability issues in BSS. The authors transform the resulting optimization problem into a convex quadratic program, enabling efficient inference. 

The work builds upon existing AFHMM approaches like AFAMAP and addresses limitations by leveraging domain-specific constraints. It’s positioned well within the broader context of incorporating prior knowledge into probabilistic models, referencing posterior regularization and related techniques. The paper clearly articulates the motivation for SACs, highlighting the practical need for interpretable source signals in applications like energy disaggregation. 

The strengths of the paper lie in its novel approach, strong empirical validation, and clear presentation. The simulation results convincingly demonstrate the performance gains of AFHMM+SAC over AFMM and AFAMAP, with statistically significant improvements confirmed by t-tests. The comparable optimization speed to AFAMAP is also a positive aspect. The authors have adequately addressed initial reviewer concerns in their response, which is commendable.

However, some areas require further attention. The toy experiment utilizes a very low noise variance, and clarification is needed regarding its realism or the rationale for its selection. A discussion of the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) for both the mains signal and the aggregate appliance signals would be valuable for understanding the experimental setup. While the theoretical presentation is good, a formal proof demonstrating the convexity of the procedure in equation (6) is essential. Furthermore, a theoretical scaling/complexity analysis (with respect to data length and the number of hidden states) and a tractability comparison to AFAMAP for larger datasets would strengthen the paper. A minor notation issue exists on page 4 (line 202) with possible extra commas in subscripts before 'p'.

While I am not an expert in the HMM community or energy applications, the work appears original and potentially significant. The authors’ responsiveness to feedback suggests a willingness to refine the work further. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to BSS using SACs.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating performance improvements.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Efficient convex optimization formulation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of formal convexity proof.
*   Missing complexity analysis and scalability discussion.
*   Need for clarification on toy experiment setup and SNR values.



Overall, this is a promising paper with a novel contribution. Addressing the identified weaknesses would significantly enhance its impact and justify acceptance.
