This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation (BSS) and integrates them into an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) for energy disaggregation. The core idea is to encode prior knowledge about the total (aggregate) contribution of each latent source across a time horizon, using quadratic penalties around specified totals. Because these constraints are non-Markovian, the authors relax the MAP inference problem and reformulate it as a convex quadratic program (CQP) over relaxed state variables and pairwise transition variables. They also show how the same objective arises from posterior regularization. Empirically, the method (AFHMM+SAC) outperforms a baseline AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS 2012) on a toy task and on real-world electricity datasets (UK HES), with statistically significant gains in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE), and runtime comparable to AFAMAP.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on factorial HMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan), constrained inference/learning frameworks such as posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.), GE criteria (Mann & McCallum), and prior AFHMM constraints for energy disaggregation, including at-most-one-switch constraints (AFAMAP), non-homogeneous HMMs, semi-Markov variants, and k-segment constraints. The proposed SACs are complementary: they do not constrain local dynamics but regularize global aggregates, which are often available from domain knowledge (e.g., national appliance consumption).

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The relaxation to a CQP is well motivated. The introduction of transition variables H enables linearization of the Markov terms, and the quadratic terms arise from the likelihood and the SAC penalties. The connection to posterior regularization is correct and strengthens the interpretation.
- However, a more formal convexity argument for the relaxed problem (Eq. 6) is needed. Specifically, when maximizing L(S,H), the quadratic terms are concave because −(1/2) S_t^T V_t S_t and −S_i^T Λ_i S_i have negative semidefinite Hessians given V_t, Λ_i are PSD. Equivalently, minimizing −L yields a standard convex QP with PSD quadratic form and linear constraints. Please add a short proof sketch (Hessian structure and PSD arguments), explicitly stating the optimization direction and the convexity class.
- The rounding/decoding step from relaxed solutions to discrete states is not described. Do you output soft states to reconstruct signals (µ-weighted sums) or project to vertices (e.g., per-time argmax or flow-preserving rounding consistent with H)? Please clarify and discuss any impact on metrics.
- Complexity: The paper states polynomial time and shows empirical parity with AFAMAP, but a theoretical scaling would help. Please express variables/constraints as functions of T, I, and {K_i} (e.g., |S| = O(T ΣK_i), |H| = O(T ΣK_i^2)), and discuss per-iteration complexity of interior-point methods and memory requirements versus AFAMAP’s specialized dynamic programming. A tractability discussion for larger T and richer state spaces would be valuable.

Clarity:
- The exposition is generally clear, with a useful derivation and notational setup. The posterior regularization section is concise and accurate.
- Minor notational/typo issues: there appear to be extra commas in subscripts before p on page 4 (around line 202); please fix. Consider adding a brief mapping table for symbols (S, H, µ*, V_t, Λ_i) to ease readability.
- The paper would benefit from an explicit statement that V_t and Λ_i are PSD and from collecting the convexity proof in an appendix.
- In the experiments, please describe the decoding strategy from the relaxed solution and any post-processing.

Originality:
- The notion of imposing global aggregate constraints on latent source totals in AFHMMs appears novel and distinct from existing local dynamic constraints (AFAMAP, semi-Markov, k-segment). The link to posterior regularization is elegant and generalizable.
- The idea is potentially applicable beyond energy to speech/audio and biological BSS, which increases its novelty and scope.

Significance:
- The performance gains on realistic, low-frequency HES data are meaningful for the NILM community, where identifiability and robustness are key. The approach addresses a core practical obstacle: using domain knowledge (aggregate priors) to prevent “franken-sources.”
- The method’s generality and convex relaxation are attractive; however, adoption may hinge on scalability. A short discussion on deploying first-order/ADMM solvers or problem-specific structure exploitation would strengthen the paper’s practical impact.

Empirical evaluation:
- The toy experiment uses a very low noise variance (σ^2 = 0.01). Please justify whether this SNR is representative, and add a noise/SNR sweep to show robustness.
- For mains vs. aggregate-from-appliances settings, report SNRs in dB and quantify the fraction of unmodeled loads. A robustness analysis to missing appliances would be helpful.
- Hyperparameters: λ_i are fixed to 1. Please include sensitivity or cross-validation results and comment on how to choose µ_0 and λ_i when national priors are imperfect.
- The t-test reporting is appreciated; include effect sizes and confidence intervals for NDE/SAE improvements.

Broader impact and extensions:
- The section on multiple interval constraints is promising; a brief experiment (e.g., daytime/nighttime SACs) would showcase practical benefits.
- Consider whether hierarchical priors on µ_0 (per-household deviation from national priors) could further improve robustness.

Pros:
- Clear, principled formulation linking domain priors to a convex relaxation.
- Demonstrated, statistically significant improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP on realistic data.
- Conceptually general and potentially impactful beyond energy disaggregation.
- Solid theoretical framing via posterior regularization.

Cons:
- Missing formal convexity proof and decoding details for the relaxed solution.
- Limited complexity/scaling analysis; reliance on generic CVX may limit large-scale adoption.
- Toy experiment noise level may be unrealistically favorable; robustness to SNR and missing loads could be better explored.
- Minor notation/typo issues.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The idea is novel and useful, the empirical gains are convincing, and the theory is sound but should be tightened (convexity proof, decoding). With the suggested clarifications (many addressed in the rebuttal), this would be a valuable contribution.

Reviewer confidence: Medium. I am not a specialist in FHMM inference but am familiar with constrained learning and NILM; the core claims seem well supported.
