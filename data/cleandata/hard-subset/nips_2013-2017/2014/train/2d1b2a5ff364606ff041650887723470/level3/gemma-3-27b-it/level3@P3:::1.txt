This paper introduces a novel approach to blind source separation, specifically within the context of energy disaggregation, by incorporating Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM). The core idea – leveraging prior knowledge about expected total energy consumption for different appliances – is a sensible and practical one. The authors convincingly demonstrate through simulations and real-world data that this constraint significantly improves performance over the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method (AFAMAP). The transformation of the inference problem into a convex quadratic program is a key technical contribution, enabling efficient computation.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in blind source separation (BSS) [3, 17, 2] and energy disaggregation [8, 12, 22]. It appropriately positions itself within the broader context of incorporating prior knowledge into probabilistic models [4, 13, 14]. The motivation, stemming from the identifiability issues inherent in BSS, is clearly articulated. The paper effectively addresses a critical practical concern: making the disaggregated results interpretable and trustworthy for end-users.  Related work is well-referenced, though a citation to recent advances in sparse methods for load disaggregation could be beneficial.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The introduction of SACs as a means of incorporating domain knowledge into AFHMMs is a novel contribution.
* **Practicality:** The approach is grounded in a realistic understanding of energy disaggregation, recognizing the availability of prior information about appliance consumption.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The simulation results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of SACs, with statistically significant improvements over competing methods. The real-world data results further support these findings.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors demonstrate that the optimization speed of the proposed method is comparable to AFAMAP, addressing a potential concern about increased computational cost.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the concepts are explained clearly. The derivation of the convex quadratic program is well-structured.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Toy Experiment Realism:** The toy experiment utilizes a very low noise variance (σ2 = 0.01). It’s unclear whether this value is representative of real-world scenarios or was deliberately chosen to make the separation easier. Clarification on this point is crucial.  Furthermore, a discussion of the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) for both the mains signal and the aggregate appliance signals would provide valuable context.
* **Convexity Proof:** While the authors state that they transform the problem into a convex quadratic program, a formal proof demonstrating the convexity of the procedure outlined in equation (6) is missing. This would significantly strengthen the theoretical foundation of the work.
* **Scalability Analysis:**  The paper lacks a theoretical scaling/complexity analysis, specifically addressing how the computational cost scales with data length (T) and the number of hidden states (Ki). A comparison of tractability with AFAMAP for larger datasets (e.g., longer time series, more appliances) would be highly valuable.
* **Minor Notation:** A potential minor issue exists with the notation on page 4, line 20, where extra commas appear in the subscripts before ‘p’.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to a challenging problem.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
* The technical contribution of formulating the inference problem as a convex quadratic program is significant.
* The authors have addressed reviewer concerns satisfactorily during the rebuttal phase (as noted).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a formal convexity proof and scalability analysis weakens the theoretical foundation of the work.
* The realism of the toy experiment’s noise level is questionable.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation and energy disaggregation. While the theoretical gaps regarding convexity and scalability are notable, the compelling empirical results and practical relevance of the work outweigh these weaknesses. The authors have demonstrated a clear understanding of the problem and have developed a novel solution that significantly improves performance. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns regarding the toy experiment’s noise level, providing a formal proof of convexity, and incorporating the clarifications from the rebuttal into the final manuscript.  Given the positive response to reviewer queries, I am confident these revisions will further strengthen the paper.
