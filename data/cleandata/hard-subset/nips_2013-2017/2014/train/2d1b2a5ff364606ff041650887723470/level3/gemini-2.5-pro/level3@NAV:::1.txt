This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation, specifically in the context of energy disaggregation, by introducing Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM). This is a well-motivated approach to tackle the inherent identifiability problem in BSS, building upon prior work in factorial HMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan, 1997) and more recent constrained inference methods like AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012). The core contribution is the use of domain knowledge about the expected total signal from each source to regularize the posterior objective. The authors demonstrate how to transform this non-Markovian objective into a tractable convex quadratic program for approximate MAP inference.

**Strengths:**
The paper’s main strength lies in its novel and intuitive contribution. The idea of using SACs is a practical and effective way to incorporate prior knowledge, which is crucial for the ill-posed disaggregation problem. The technical quality is high; the formulation as a convex optimization problem is elegant and enables efficient inference. The experimental validation is another strong point. The proposed AFHMM+SAC method is shown to significantly outperform both the standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP on real-world energy data, with improvements validated by t-tests. Importantly, this performance gain comes at a computational cost comparable to AFAMAP, making it a practical advance.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  **Theoretical Justification:** While the theoretical presentation is clear, the paper would be stronger with a formal proof that the relaxed optimization problem in equation (6) is indeed convex. Explicitly showing that the quadratic forms are based on positive semidefinite matrices would solidify the technical claims.
2.  **Scalability Analysis:** The authors should provide a theoretical complexity analysis of their method with respect to data length (T) and the number of hidden states. A discussion on its tractability compared to AFAMAP on larger datasets would be a valuable addition.
3.  **Experimental Clarification:** In the toy experiment, the noise variance is very low (σ² = 0.01). The authors should clarify if this setting is realistic or chosen to simplify the separation task. A discussion of the signal-to-noise ratio in the real-world experiments (e.g., mains signal vs. aggregate appliance signals) would help contextualize the results.
4.  **Minor Issue:** There appears to be a minor notational typo with extra commas in the subscripts on page 4 (line 202).

**Recommendation:**
This is an original and significant paper presenting a novel method with strong empirical results on a challenging real-world problem. While I am not an expert in the HMM community, the contribution appears solid. The weaknesses are addressable. The authors’ clarifications during the feedback period were satisfactory, and I recommend that they be incorporated into the final manuscript to strengthen it further. I recommend acceptance.
