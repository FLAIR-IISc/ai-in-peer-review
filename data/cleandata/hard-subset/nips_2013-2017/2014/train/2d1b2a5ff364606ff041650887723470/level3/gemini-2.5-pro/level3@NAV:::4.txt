This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a method for incorporating domain knowledge into Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs), with a specific application to energy disaggregation. The core idea is to constrain the model such that the total output of each hidden source (e.g., an appliance's total energy consumption) over a time period matches a known aggregate value. To make inference tractable, the authors propose an approximate MAP inference procedure based on a convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation. This work builds upon a well-established line of research using factorial HMMs for blind source separation and specifically addresses the energy disaggregation problem, offering a novel constraint-based approach that is orthogonal to recent methods like AFAMAP, which constrains state transitions.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Quality:** The paper's primary strength lies in its convincing empirical results on large, challenging, real-world datasets. The application to low-frequency energy data from over 100 homes demonstrates the method's practical utility and its significant performance improvement over existing state-of-the-art approaches. The combination of a novel algorithmic contribution with a successful, large-scale application is compelling.
*   **Originality and Clarity:** The introduction of SACs is a simple, intuitive, and novel way to inject valuable domain knowledge into the disaggregation problem. The optimization formulation is straightforward and technically sound.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper occasionally conflates models with their inference procedures. For instance, AFAMAP is presented as a competing model, when it is more accurately described as an approximate MAP inference algorithm for the AFHMM. The paper should be more precise, clarifying that the baseline "AFHMM" results likely use an alternating Viterbi algorithm. This precision is crucial for reproducibility and clear positioning of the work.
*   **Analysis of Results:** The authors should provide more insight into *why* their method works so well. For example, does the SAC regularizer in the CQP formulation encourage more integral (i.e., integer-like) solutions, similar to the effect of AFAMAP’s constraints? Or does the model achieve lower error even with highly fractional solutions? A brief analysis of this would strengthen the paper's technical contribution.
*   **Missed Experimental Opportunity:** The authors correctly identify that their proposed constraints and those in AFAMAP are fundamentally orthogonal. This presents a clear opportunity for a powerful experiment: combining the SACs with the AFAMAP inference framework. Such an experiment could potentially yield state-of-the-art results and would be a very valuable addition for a final version of the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a solid and well-executed paper. It proposes a simple but effective extension to AFHMMs and validates it thoroughly on a difficult, real-world problem. While the core idea is not a major theoretical leap, its practical significance and strong empirical backing make this a valuable contribution to the field. With the suggested clarifications and the addition of experiments combining orthogonal techniques, this paper would be even stronger. It is a successful piece of work that is likely to be impactful in its application domain.
