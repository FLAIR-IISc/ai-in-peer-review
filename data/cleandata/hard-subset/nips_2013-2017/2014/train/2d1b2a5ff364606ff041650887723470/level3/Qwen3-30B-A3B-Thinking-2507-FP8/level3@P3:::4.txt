

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation. SACs constrain the total signal (sum) of each source to be close to a known value, addressing the identifiability problem inherent in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs). The authors formulate a convex quadratic program (CQP) for approximate inference that incorporates these constraints, demonstrating significant improvements over baseline AFHMM and the recent AFAMAP approach on real-world energy disaggregation datasets.

The work builds on NIPS-related prior work on posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2010) and extends the AFAMAP framework (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012) for energy disaggregation. While prior work has explored various constraints for HMMs (e.g., "one chain state at a time" in AFAMAP), SACs represent a fundamentally different constraint type focused on aggregate signal values rather than temporal state transitions.

The paper's main strengths lie in its compelling real-world application and clear demonstration of practical impact. The authors evaluate their method on two large-scale energy disaggregation datasets: 100 UK homes with low-frequency monitoring and 10 homes with higher-frequency data. The results are impressive - SACAFHMM achieves a 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to AFHMM and a 29% improvement over AFAMAP. The computational efficiency is also notable, with inference times comparable to baseline methods despite the additional constraints.

However, the paper would benefit from greater precision in distinguishing between models and inference procedures. The authors frequently conflate AFAMAP (an approximate MAP inference algorithm for AFHMM) with the underlying AFHMM model. For example, the paper refers to "AFAMAP results" when discussing inference procedures that are actually applied to AFHMM. This confusion should be clarified throughout the paper, particularly in the experimental sections where results are attributed to specific methods.

A critical question not addressed is whether SACAFHMM's formulation yields more integral (integer) solutions compared to AFAMAP's "one chain state at a time" constraint. The paper states that SACAFHMM uses a convex relaxation, but it doesn't clarify whether the solutions obtained are more integral than those from AFAMAP. This is important because the authors claim SACAFHMM "significantly improves" over AFAMAP, but it's unclear whether this is due to better approximate solutions or the constraint itself.

The paper also suggests that SACAFHMM and AFAMAP are fundamentally orthogonal approaches. The authors should explicitly propose combining SACAFHMM's constraints into AFAMAP as a promising direction for future work, which would be a natural extension of their current findings.

Regarding quality, the empirical results are well-supported with statistical significance testing (paired t-tests), and the theoretical connection to posterior regularization is well-established. The paper is technically sound but could be strengthened by addressing the integral solution question and clarifying the model-inference distinction.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, though the model/inference confusion mentioned above detracts from the presentation. The application to energy disaggregation is well-motivated, and the experimental setup is realistic and relevant.

The originality of SACs is clear - while posterior regularization has been used for other constraints, SACs represent a novel constraint type specifically designed for aggregate signal values. The paper properly references related work, though it could better distinguish between the constraint types.

The significance of the work is high. Energy disaggregation has important practical applications for energy conservation and management, and the demonstrated performance improvements are substantial. The approach could be applied to other blind source separation problems beyond energy disaggregation.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Strong empirical results on large real-world datasets
- Clear connection to posterior regularization framework
- Practical impact on energy disaggregation with meaningful performance gains
- Simple extension with compelling results

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of clarity regarding integral solution quality
- Conflation of models and inference procedures
- Limited discussion of how SACAFHMM differs from AFAMAP in solution quality

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a compelling case for SACs in energy disaggregation. The empirical results are convincing, and the approach is straightforward yet effective. With minor clarifications regarding the integral nature of solutions and better distinction between models and inference procedures, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. The paper is reasonable and successful, and with these suggested improvements, it would make a valuable addition to the conference.
