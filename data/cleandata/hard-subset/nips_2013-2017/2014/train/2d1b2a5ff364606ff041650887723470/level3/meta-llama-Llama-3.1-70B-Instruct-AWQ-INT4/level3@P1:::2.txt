The paper "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation" presents a novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge into the problem of blind source separation (BSS) in the context of energy disaggregation. The authors introduce signal aggregate constraints (SACs) that encourage the total signal for each of the unknown sources to be close to a specified value. This is a reasonable assumption in many applications, including energy disaggregation, where the total signal often varies widely across the unknown sources, and we often have a good idea of what total values to expect.

The authors incorporate SACs into an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) to formulate the energy disaggregation problem. They derive a convex quadratic program for approximate inference, which is computationally efficient. The results on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset show that the use of SACs significantly improves the original AFHMM and outperforms a recent state-of-the-art approach.

The paper is well-structured, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem, the proposed approach, and the experimental results. The writing style is formal and suitable for a technical paper. However, the manuscript is indeed very hard to read, mainly due to the excessive use of acronyms, some of which are unusually long (≈11 letters). This harms readability and makes it difficult for non-experts to follow the paper.

One potential issue with the paper is that the authors appear to place the constraint on the prior rather than directly on the posterior. It would be helpful to explicitly state that the SAC is an a priori constraint, thereby constraining the posterior. This would provide a clearer understanding of the relationship between the prior and posterior distributions.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge into the BSS problem. The use of SACs is a reasonable assumption in many applications, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in the context of energy disaggregation. The paper also provides a clear comparison with existing approaches, including a recent state-of-the-art method.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to improve the accuracy of energy disaggregation systems. The results show that the use of SACs can significantly improve the performance of the AFHMM, which is a commonly used model for BSS. This has implications for the development of more accurate and efficient energy disaggregation systems, which can help households and businesses to better understand their energy usage patterns.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed approach and the experimental results. However, the excessive use of acronyms and the potential issue with the placement of the constraint on the prior rather than the posterior are areas that could be improved.
