

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a novel approach to address identifiability issues in blind source separation (BSS) problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation. The authors observe that in many BSS applications, the total signal for each source varies significantly and often has known expected values. SACs enforce these expected total values as constraints, which they incorporate into the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework. The key contribution is a convex quadratic programming relaxation that allows efficient inference despite the non-Markovian nature of the constraints.

The paper builds on previous work in energy disaggregation using AFHMM [8, 12, 15], as well as constraint-based approaches like posterior regularization [4] and AFAMAP [12]. The authors clearly differentiate SACs from prior constraint approaches by emphasizing their domain knowledge basis (known total signal values) and their mathematical tractability through convex relaxation.

## Strengths

* **Strong theoretical foundation**: The derivation of the convex quadratic program for SACs is mathematically rigorous and well-motivated. The connection to posterior regularization provides a principled theoretical basis for the approach.

* **Clear problem motivation**: The paper effectively explains the identifiability problem in BSS and why SACs are a natural constraint to incorporate, particularly in energy disaggregation where appliance consumption totals are often known from prior studies.

* **Promising empirical results**: The method shows significant improvements over AFHMM (45% decrease in NDE) and AFAMAP (29% improvement) on real-world energy disaggregation data. The synthetic data experiments (Figure 1) demonstrate the expected relationship between constraint accuracy and performance.

* **Well-written and organized**: The paper is clearly structured, with detailed mathematical derivations and a logical progression from problem statement to solution to evaluation.

## Weaknesses

* **Unfair comparison with baselines**: The paper compares SACAFHMM against AFHMM and AFAMAP using different amounts of data. Specifically, SACAFHMM uses NTC (national total consumption) derived from the training data, while AFAMAP does not appear to use this additional information. This makes the comparison potentially unfair, as AFAMAP might benefit from similar constraint incorporation. The authors should clarify whether they attempted to incorporate similar constraint knowledge into AFAMAP.

* **Misleading claim about SACs**: Figure 1 shows that when the constraint distance exceeds 1e4 (i.e., the constraint is very inaccurate), AFAMAP outperforms SACAFHMM. This contradicts the paper's claim that SACs "always help" and suggests that poorly specified constraints can degrade performance. The authors should temper their claims about SACs' universal benefits.

* **Limited constraint types**: The paper focuses on simple aggregate constraints but does not address how to incorporate more complex constraints that might be relevant in energy disaggregation (e.g., mutual exclusivity, cardinality limits, duration constraints). The authors should acknowledge this limitation and suggest how their framework might be extended.

* **Insufficient discussion of computational complexity**: While the paper states that SACAFHMM scales similarly to AFHMM and AFAMAP, it does not provide a detailed computational complexity analysis, especially compared to other constraint-based approaches.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. The authors should re-evaluate the comparison with baselines to ensure fairness, potentially by incorporating similar constraint knowledge into AFAMAP or using a more appropriate baseline.

2. The paper should acknowledge that SACs can degrade performance when constraints are poorly specified (as shown in Figure 1) and provide guidance on selecting appropriate constraint values.

3. The authors should extend their discussion to include how their framework might handle more complex constraint types (mutual exclusivity, duration limits, etc.) that would be valuable in energy disaggregation.

4. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of SACAFHMM compared to other constraint-based approaches.

## Overall Assessment

This is a solid contribution with a clear problem statement, strong theoretical foundation, and promising empirical results. The SAC framework represents a novel and practically useful approach to incorporating domain knowledge into BSS problems. While the experimental comparison could be strengthened and the claims about SACs' universal benefits need tempering, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the energy disaggregation literature and to the broader field of constrained BSS.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria well:
- **Quality**: Technically sound with well-supported claims and solid experimental results (though comparison needs refinement)
- **Clarity**: Well-written and well-organized
- **Originality**: Introduces a novel constraint type with clear differentiation from previous work
- **Significance**: Addresses an important application with demonstrable improvements over state-of-the-art

The authors have made a valuable contribution that should be of interest to researchers working on constrained BSS, energy disaggregation, and probabilistic modeling. With minor revisions addressing the comparison fairness and constraint limitations, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
