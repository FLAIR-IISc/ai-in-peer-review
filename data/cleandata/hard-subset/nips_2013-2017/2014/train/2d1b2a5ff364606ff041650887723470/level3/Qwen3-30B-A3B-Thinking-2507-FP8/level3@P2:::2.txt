

# Review for "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation. SACs constrain the total signal (sum over time) for each source to be close to a specified value, addressing the identifiability problem that plagues blind source separation. The authors incorporate SACs into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), transforming the constrained optimization into a convex quadratic program (CQP) that can be efficiently solved. The approach is evaluated on real-world energy disaggregation data, showing a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM and a 29% improvement over a recent state-of-the-art approach (AFAMAP).

The paper builds on prior work in energy disaggregation, particularly the AFHMM framework [8, 17, 22] and constrained variants like AFAMAP [12] that limit state transitions. SACs differ from these prior constraints by focusing on the aggregate signal over time rather than state transitions or durations. The authors also connect their approach to posterior regularization [4], though this connection requires clarification as discussed below.

**Strengths:**
- The problem of identifiability in blind source separation is well-motivated, especially in energy disaggregation where interpretable results are crucial for practical applications.
- SACs are intuitive and leverage domain knowledge (e.g., average appliance energy consumption), which is a natural and practical way to incorporate prior information.
- The technical approach is sound, with a clear transformation to a convex quadratic program that enables efficient computation.
- The empirical results are strong, showing significant improvements over baselines on real-world energy disaggregation data, with statistically significant improvements (paired t-tests at 5% significance level).
- The paper provides a good theoretical connection to posterior regularization, though this could be more clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**
- The manuscript is difficult to read due to excessive use of acronyms. For example, "AFHMM+SAC" (11 characters) is used repeatedly, and the paper introduces multiple acronyms without sufficient explanation, harming readability. This is a significant issue that should be addressed.
- The paper frames SACs as being placed on the prior rather than directly on the posterior, which creates confusion. The authors should explicitly state that SACs are a priori constraints that constrain the posterior distribution, rather than being part of the prior distribution itself. This is a critical conceptual point that needs clarification.
- The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) is somewhat confusing. The authors state that "the constraint terms could be viewed as the logarithm of the prior distributions over the states S," but this misrepresents the posterior regularization framework, which directly constrains the posterior distribution.
- The paper lacks discussion of limitations: when might SACs not be applicable? How sensitive are results to errors in the specified aggregate values?
- The toy data experiments (Section 7.1) are somewhat simplistic and don't fully explore the behavior of SACs under varying constraint specifications.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear transformation to a convex quadratic program that is well-justified. The experimental results are well-designed and statistically validated. However, the unclear framing of SACs as a prior constraint rather than a posterior constraint is a significant technical weakness.

**Clarity:** The paper suffers from poor clarity due to excessive acronyms and a confusing presentation of the constraint framework. The manuscript would benefit from simplifying acronyms and explicitly stating that SACs are a priori constraints on the posterior distribution. The mathematical formulation is dense without sufficient explanation for non-experts.

**Originality:** SACs represent a novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation, distinct from prior constraints on state transitions. The paper effectively differentiates its contribution from related work on constrained HMMs.

**Significance:** The improvements in energy disaggregation are significant (29-45% error reduction), and the approach could be applied to other blind source separation problems. The constraint is intuitive and can be easily incorporated with domain knowledge, making it practically valuable for energy disaggregation and potentially other domains.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel and intuitive approach to addressing identifiability in blind source separation
- Strong empirical results showing significant improvements over state-of-the-art
- Practical application to energy disaggregation, a problem of growing importance
- Well-motivated constraint that leverages domain knowledge

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Manuscript is difficult to read due to excessive acronyms
- Critical confusion between prior and posterior constraints that needs clarification
- Limited discussion of limitations and sensitivity to constraint specifications
- The connection to posterior regularization is not clearly articulated

Overall, this is a good paper with a strong technical contribution that addresses a meaningful problem in energy disaggregation. However, the clarity issues and conceptual confusion regarding the constraint's relationship to the posterior significantly impact the paper's quality. With revisions to clarify the constraint framework and improve readability, this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS. As written, the manuscript would benefit from significant revision before acceptance.
