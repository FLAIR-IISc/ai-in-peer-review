This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation (BSS) problems. The core idea is that for many BSS applications, one has a reasonable prior expectation for the total aggregate signal from each source over a given time period. The authors formulate this idea as a constraint and incorporate it into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a model commonly used for BSS tasks like energy disaggregation.

A key challenge is that these aggregate constraints are non-Markovian, making standard inference techniques like the Viterbi algorithm inapplicable. The authors address this by reformulating the MAP inference problem as a penalized objective function and then deriving a convex quadratic program (CQP) for a relaxed version of this problem, which can be solved efficiently. The proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on a toy dataset and a challenging real-world energy disaggregation dataset. The results demonstrate that incorporating SACs provides a dramatic improvement in performance over the standard AFHMM and a significant improvement over AFAMAP, a recent state-of-the-art method for this problem. The paper also provides a brief but insightful connection between their penalized objective and the posterior regularization framework.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work. The claims are well-supported by extensive experiments on real-world data, showing statistically significant improvements over relevant baselines.
*   **Strengths:** The formulation of the inference problem as a CQP is a strong technical contribution that makes the proposed constraints practical. The empirical evaluation is thorough, using two variants of real-world data (with and without measured mains readings) and appropriate error metrics (NDE and SAE). The use of both national average consumption and oracle "true total consumption" as constraints provides a clear picture of the method's practical utility and its theoretical potential.
*   **Weaknesses:** The technical presentation could be strengthened in a few areas. While the paper states that the relaxed problem in equation (6) is a CQP because the matrices involved are positive semidefinite, a more formal argument or proof of the objective function's convexity would make the claim more rigorous. Furthermore, the toy data experiment in Section 7.1 uses a very low noise variance (σ² = 0.01). It is unclear if this is a realistic setting. The paper would be improved by a discussion of the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) in this experiment and how it compares to the SNR in the real-world energy data, which seems to contain significant unmodeled noise (as seen in the "with mains" experiment).

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for SACs is compelling and explained well in the introduction. The model and inference procedure are described logically. The experimental setup and results are presented in a way that is easy to follow.
*   **Minor Suggestion:** A minor proofreading check is recommended. For example, in Section 4.1, there appear to be extra commas in some subscripts (e.g., `Zi,t-1`), which could be simplified to `Z_{i, t-1}` for consistency.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While constrained HMMs are an active area of research, the proposed signal aggregate constraint is a novel type of constraint that is both intuitive and powerful. It addresses the identifiability problem in BSS from a different angle than previous work like AFAMAP, which constrains state transitions locally in time. The idea of constraining the global sum of a source's signal is new and well-suited for applications where cumulative totals are meaningful, such as energy consumption. The work is clearly differentiated from related literature, which is adequately cited.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant and likely to be of high interest to researchers in energy disaggregation and other BSS application areas.
*   **Strengths:** The demonstrated performance improvement over a recent state-of-the-art method is substantial (a 29% reduction in NDE on the more realistic dataset). The proposed SAC framework is general and could inspire similar work in other domains where BSS is applied, such as audio processing or computational biology. The computational cost is shown to be comparable to existing methods, making it a practical solution.
*   **Request for Clarification:** The paper does not include a formal analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed CQP in terms of the number of time steps (T), sources (I), and states (K). While empirical timings are provided, a theoretical scaling analysis would be valuable for readers interested in applying the method to larger-scale problems and would allow for a more direct tractability comparison with AFAMAP.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty:** Proposes a novel, intuitive, and effective form of domain knowledge (Signal Aggregate Constraints) for the challenging BSS problem.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** Provides a tractable inference algorithm by formulating the problem as a convex quadratic program.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on a difficult and important real-world application, with statistically significant improvements.
4.  **Generality:** The core idea of SACs is general and could be impactful in other application domains beyond energy disaggregation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Missing Theoretical Details:** The paper would be stronger with a formal proof of convexity for the objective function and a computational complexity analysis.
2.  **Experimental Details:** The choice of parameters (e.g., noise variance) in the toy experiment could be better justified or discussed in the context of real-world scenarios.

**Overall Recommendation:** This is a strong paper with a novel idea, a solid technical contribution, and impressive results. The weaknesses identified are minor and can be addressed in a final version. I recommend acceptance. I would encourage the authors to incorporate a discussion of the points raised regarding convexity, computational complexity, and the experimental noise settings to further improve the manuscript.
