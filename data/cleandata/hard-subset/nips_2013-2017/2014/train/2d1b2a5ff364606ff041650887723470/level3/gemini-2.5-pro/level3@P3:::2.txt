This paper introduces a method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation (BSS) problems by proposing Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The authors apply this idea to the energy disaggregation task, where the goal is to separate a household's total electricity consumption signal into the signals of individual appliances. The core contribution is to constrain an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) by assuming prior knowledge about the total energy an appliance is expected to consume over a given time period. Since this constraint is non-Markovian, making exact inference intractable, the authors formulate MAP inference as a penalized optimization problem and solve a convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation. This approach is related to prior work on constrained HMMs, such as the AFAMAP model of Kolter & Jaakkola (2012), which constrains state transitions. However, the SAC framework introduces a novel type of global constraint based on aggregate signal properties. The paper demonstrates experimentally that adding SACs significantly improves disaggregation accuracy over the standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model.

**Quality**
The paper is technically sound. The proposed model is well-motivated, and the formulation of the inference problem as a CQP is a solid technical contribution that makes the approach practical. The experimental evaluation is convincing, using a real-world dataset to show substantial improvements over relevant and strong baselines. The authors are careful in their evaluation, using both a realistic constraint based on national averages (NTC) and an oracle constraint (TTC) to demonstrate the potential of their method.

One area for improvement lies in the technical description of how the constraint is incorporated. The authors frame the problem as maximizing a penalized log-posterior and later mention that the penalty term can be viewed as the log of a prior over the state sequences. This connection is correct but could be stated more clearly and upfront. It would be beneficial to explicitly state that the SAC is an *a priori* constraint on the entire latent state sequence `S`, which in turn shapes the posterior distribution `P(S|Y)`. The current phrasing might lead to confusion about whether the constraint is being applied to the prior or directly to the posterior. Clarifying this would strengthen the probabilistic interpretation of the model.

**Clarity**
While the paper's technical quality is high, its clarity is a significant weakness. The manuscript is very difficult to read. The prose is dense, and the overall narrative could be structured more effectively to guide the reader through the problem, the proposed solution, and the results.

A major contributor to this lack of clarity is the excessive and often cumbersome use of acronyms. Acronyms such as AFHMM, AFAMAP, AFHMM+SAC, NDE, SAE, NTC, and TTC are used extensively throughout the text. Some, like AFHMM+SAC, are unusually long (11 characters) and make sentences difficult to parse. This forces the reader to constantly refer back to definitions, which disrupts the reading flow and makes it challenging to absorb the paper's core message. The authors are strongly encouraged to perform a thorough revision to reduce their reliance on acronyms, perhaps by using more descriptive phrases or by re-defining terms more frequently in the text.

**Originality**
The paper presents a novel and valuable contribution. The idea of using a signal's aggregate value as a constraint in BSS is, to my knowledge, original. It is an intuitive form of domain knowledge that is particularly well-suited for applications like energy disaggregation but is generalizable to other domains. While the paper builds on the established AFHMM framework and uses known optimization techniques (CQP), the novel combination to incorporate this new class of constraints is a significant contribution. The work is well-contextualized with respect to previous literature.

**Significance**
The results are highly significant for the energy disaggregation community and potentially for other BSS applications. The problem is of considerable practical importance for sustainability and energy efficiency. The paper demonstrates a substantial improvement over a strong, recently published baseline on a challenging, low-frequency real-world dataset. The fact that significant gains are achieved using easily obtainable prior information (national average consumption data) makes the method highly practical. These results advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way, and other researchers are likely to use these ideas or build on them.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, intuitive, and effective method (Signal Aggregate Constraints) for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation models.
*   Provides a tractable inference procedure based on a convex quadratic programming relaxation.
*   Demonstrates significant, state-of-the-art performance on a challenging and important real-world application (energy disaggregation).
*   The core idea has high potential for impact and could be adapted to other domains.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is very poorly written. The clarity is low enough that it significantly obscures the technical contributions and makes the work difficult to understand and build upon.
*   The overwhelming use of acronyms severely harms readability and is not in line with the standards of a top-tier publication.
