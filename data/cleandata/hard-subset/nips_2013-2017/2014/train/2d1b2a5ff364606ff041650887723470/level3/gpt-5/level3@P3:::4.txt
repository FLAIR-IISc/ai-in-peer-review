Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation and incorporates them into an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM), yielding AFHMM+SAC. The key idea is to encode prior knowledge about the total contribution of each source over a horizon (e.g., total daily Wh per appliance) and to add a convex quadratic penalty that encourages each chain’s aggregate to match a specified value. Because these constraints are non-Markovian, the authors propose a convex relaxation and solve a convex quadratic program (CQP) by introducing auxiliary transition variables (H) that make the relaxed objective convex. They also show the equivalence to posterior regularization.

The most relevant lines of prior work are: AFHMMs and approximate MAP inference (e.g., chainwise/alternating Viterbi) for NILM; AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS 2012), which imposes “at most one chain changes state per time step” for tractable approximate MAP; semi-Markov/duration models; non-homogeneous factorial HMMs; and general frameworks for constraints and weak supervision (posterior regularization, generalized expectation, measurement-based learning). Conceptually, AFHMM+SAC is orthogonal to AFAMAP: AFAMAP changes the inference model by imposing temporal structure; SACs add global information about totals. The paper’s contribution is a simple yet effective instantiation of posterior-style constraints tailor-made for disaggregation, with a scalable convex relaxation.

Empirically, the method is evaluated on a large real dataset (HES): (a) 100 UK homes with 2–10 minute sampling where aggregates are synthesized by summing submetered appliance channels (no measured mains), and (b) 9 homes with measured mains and many unmodeled loads. AFHMM+SAC reduces normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) versus AFHMM and AFAMAP, often significantly, while running in comparable time.

Strengths
- Clear, focused contribution: a principled way to encode aggregate priors in AFHMMs via a convex relaxation, tied to posterior regularization.
- Practical relevance: aggregate priors are natural in NILM and many BSS tasks; the optimization is straightforward and implementable.
- Strong empirical scale and realism: 100-house evaluation at ultra-low frequency and a mains setting with unmodeled loads; statistically significant gains vs. AFAMAP.
- Good methodological clarity on the CQP construction, PSD structure, and complexity.
- Useful analysis of robustness to mis-specified aggregates on toy data.

Weaknesses and questions
- Integrality and rounding: The CQP yields fractional Sit and Hit. Are solutions near-integral in practice? How are they rounded to discrete states, and how sensitive are results to rounding? Please quantify integrality (e.g., fraction of Sitk within {0,1}±ε; gap to best integer solution).
- Attribution of gains: The AFHMM baseline appears to use alternating (chainwise) Viterbi. Since the authors note that AFHMM with λ=0 also admits a convex relaxation, a direct comparison between AFHMM (chainwise Viterbi), AFHMM (CQP, λ=0), and AFHMM+SAC (CQP, λ>0) would better isolate the effect of SACs vs. the effect of the convex relaxation.
- Hyperparameters and priors: λi are fixed to 1 throughout. How sensitive are results to λ? Any cross-validation, Bayesian treatment, or per-appliance scaling?
- Aggregate prior quality: The NTC prior is estimated from training data across households. How robust is performance to domain shift (e.g., households with atypical usage)? Could you report results stratified by NTC–TTC mismatch?
- Clarity on models vs. inference: The manuscript occasionally blurs AFHMM (the model) with AFAMAP (an approximate MAP algorithm for AFHMM). Please be precise in text, figures, and tables: “AFHMM + alternating Viterbi,” “AFHMM + CQP (λ=0),” and “AFHMM+SAC (CQP, λ>0).”
- Generalization beyond supervised training: While the paper mentions future unsupervised extensions, the current approach relies on per-appliance training. A short discussion of how SACs would integrate with unsupervised NILM would help scope significance.

Quality
Technically sound and well-grounded in posterior regularization and convex optimization. The optimization is correct and scalable. Empirical evidence is strong, though the additional baselines suggested above would clarify where the gains come from. More detail on integrality and rounding would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
Overall well written, with a clear derivation of the CQP and links to prior work. Please tighten nomenclature (AFHMM vs. AFAMAP), specify exactly which inference algorithm is used for each baseline, and document the rounding procedure and λ settings. A brief algorithmic summary (pseudocode) would aid reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. SACs are a natural, domain-informed instantiation of posterior regularization for AFHMMs. The convex relaxation is neat but conceptually simple. The novelty lies in the constraint choice and its empirical validation at scale.

Significance
High for NILM and related BSS applications: the method is easy to adopt and yields consistent gains on challenging, low-frequency data from many homes. The idea of aggregate priors is likely to be reused and extended.

Suggestions for improvement and further experiments
- Report integrality metrics and rounding details; compare relaxed vs. rounded objective values and errors.
- Compare AFHMM (chainwise Viterbi) to AFHMM (CQP, λ=0) to decouple relaxation from SAC benefits.
- Combine AFAMAP with SACs: impose the “one-chain-change” constraint and add SAC penalties, or use AFAMAP’s dynamic program within a Lagrangian/dual loop for SAC enforcement. This orthogonal combination is a promising experiment for the final version.
- Explore multiple interval SACs (day/night windows) as suggested in Section 4.2.
- Provide sensitivity to λ and to NTC bias; possibly learn λi per appliance.
- Discuss or provide a lightweight solver implementation beyond CVX for larger T.

Pros
- Simple, principled idea with a clean optimization formulation.
- Strong, real-world empirical results at scale; statistically significant improvements over AFAMAP.
- Method generalizes conceptually to other BSS domains and to interval constraints.
- Solid connection to posterior regularization.

Cons
- Limited analysis of integrality and rounding; unclear how often the relaxation yields near-integer solutions.
- Baseline comparisons could better separate effects of SACs vs. the convex relaxation.
- Reliance on supervised appliance models; limited exploration of unsupervised settings.
- Some terminological imprecision (model vs. inference algorithm).

Recommendation
Accept. A straightforward but impactful extension with convincing empirical evidence and clear potential for broader use. With the requested clarifications and the suggested AFAMAP+SAC experiment, the paper would be even stronger.
