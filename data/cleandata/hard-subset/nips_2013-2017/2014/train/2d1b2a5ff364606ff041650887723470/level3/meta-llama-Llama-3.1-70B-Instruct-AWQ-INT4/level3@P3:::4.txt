This paper proposes an extension to the additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) by incorporating signal aggregate constraints (SACs) to address the issue of identifiability in blind source separation problems. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on energy disaggregation tasks using real-world datasets.

The main idea of the paper is to introduce SACs, which encourage the total signal for each of the unknown sources to be close to a specified value. This is based on the observation that the total signal often varies widely across the unknown sources, and we often have a good idea of what total values to expect. The authors incorporate SACs into the AFHMM, yielding a new model AFHMM+SAC, and derive a convex quadratic program for approximate inference.

The authors evaluate their approach on two energy disaggregation datasets: one with 100 UK homes (low-frequency data) and another with 10 homes (higher-frequency data without per-appliance monitoring). The results show that AFHMM+SAC outperforms both the original AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach, AFAMAP, in terms of normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE).

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and its relation to previous work. The evaluation is thorough, and the results are convincing. However, there are a few points that could be improved.

Firstly, the authors could provide more clarification on whether the SACAFHMM formulation yields more integral solutions like AFAMAP's "one chain state at a time" constraint, or whether it attains lower error despite non-integral solutions. This would help to better understand the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

Secondly, the authors occasionally conflate models and inference procedures (e.g., labeling AFAMAP vs AFHMM). It would be helpful to provide more precision, such as noting that AFAMAP is an approximate MAP algorithm for AFHMM and that AFHMM results likely refer to alternating Viterbi.

Lastly, the authors suggest combining the different algorithmic strategies to improve performance, which is a promising direction for future work.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and adequately informs the reader. The authors provide enough information for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel combination of SACs and AFHMM, which is a new contribution to the field. The approach is not a straightforward extension of previous work, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how their work differs from previous contributions.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a difficult problem in energy disaggregation, which is an important application domain. The results are significant, and the approach has the potential to be used in practice.

Overall, the paper is a reasonable and successful contribution to the field, and with some minor clarifications and experiments, it has the potential to be even stronger.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and effective approach to energy disaggregation.
* The evaluation is thorough, and the results are convincing.
* The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and its relation to previous work.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from more clarification on certain points, such as the comparison with AFAMAP.
* The authors occasionally conflate models and inference procedures.
* The paper could be improved with more precision in the notation and terminology.
