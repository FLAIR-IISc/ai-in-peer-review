- Paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation and embeds them into an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM+SAC).
- SACs are non‑Markovian, so authors derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) by adding transition variables and relaxing integrality for efficient approximate MAP inference.
- The quadratic terms are PSD, making the relaxed objective concave and solvable with off‑the‑shelf solvers.
- Authors connect the SAC objective to posterior regularization, clarifying its statistical interpretation.
- Experiments on synthetic data and low‑frequency HES energy data show notable NDE and SAE gains over AFHMM and AFAMAP with similar runtimes.
- Paper situates work relative to posterior regularization, GE, measurement‑based learning, AFHMM/AFAMAP, semi‑Markov and segment constraints, and aggregate constraints in probabilistic DBs.
- The convex relaxation for AFHMM with aggregate penalties appears to be a new, useful contribution to BSS.
- Strength: clear motivation—aggregate usage is often known or estimable and helps identifiability.
- Strength: solid technical derivation of the CQP and proof of PSD quadratic terms.
- Strength: empirical gains on a realistic, challenging low‑frequency dataset are practically meaningful.
- Strength: good writing and organization; modeling and optimization are easy to follow.
- Weakness: comparisons may be unfair because AFHMM+SAC uses side information (NTC/TTC/oracle) that baselines do not.
- Weakness: limited baselines—strong alternatives that could incorporate SACs (e.g., AFAMAP with SAC, semi‑Markov/k‑segment variants) are not evaluated.
- Weakness: robustness concerns—performance degrades when µ0 is misspecified and AFAMAP can outperform AFHMM+SAC in that regime.
- Weakness: hyperparameter handling—λi fixed to 1 with no tuning or sensitivity analysis.
- Weakness: discrete interpretability not addressed—the relaxation yields fractional occupancies but no rounding/decoding procedure or impact analysis.
- Quality: technically sound relaxation and posterior‑regularization derivation; empirical methodology mostly reasonable but needs fairer comparisons and hyperparameter tuning.
- Clarity: paper is clear but should clarify Figure 1 axes, specify how x̂ is computed from fractional S, and give solver details (tolerances, problem sizes).
- Originality: moderately novel—applies established posterior regularization ideas but the identification and convex relaxation of aggregate constraints for AFHMMs is useful.
- Significance: high practical relevance for non‑intrusive load monitoring and other BSS settings where aggregate priors exist.
- Suggestions: run fair comparisons by adding identical SACs to baselines, tune λi and report sensitivity, test robustness to µ0 misspecification, consider interval/inequality SACs, and provide rounding/decoding procedures.
- Suggestions: prototype broader constraints (mutual exclusivity, duration constraints, time‑of‑day SACs) and corresponding relaxations.
- Suggestions: improve reproducibility by releasing code/pseudocode, solver settings, and HES preprocessing details.
- Pros for acceptance: well‑motivated, technically sound convex relaxation with demonstrated gains and clear link to posterior regularization.
- Cons for acceptance: uncontrolled side information in comparisons, limited robustness analysis, and no evaluation of stronger baselines augmented with SACs.
- Recommendation: weak accept contingent on addressing fairness, robustness, and stronger empirical baselines.
- Reviewer confidence: high on modeling/optimization aspects; medium on empirical claims pending fairer evaluation.
