### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), specifically in the context of energy disaggregation where a single aggregate signal (total household electricity usage) must be separated into its constituent appliance-level signals. The authors correctly identify that this problem is ill-posed and suffers from an identifiability issue, where sources are exchangeable. To address this, they propose a novel form of domain knowledge called Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). A SAC posits that the total energy consumed by a specific source (appliance) over a given time period should be close to a known or expected value.

The authors incorporate this idea into the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a common framework for this task. The resulting model, AFHMM+SAC, adds a penalty term to the standard AFHMM objective function, encouraging the inferred source signals to have aggregate sums that match predefined targets. Since this constraint is non-Markovian and complicates inference, the authors propose a tractable approximate inference scheme by relaxing the problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP), building upon similar relaxation techniques used for the standard AFHMM. The paper also provides a theoretical justification for their approach by relating it to the posterior regularization framework.

The proposed method is evaluated on both synthetic data and a challenging, real-world low-frequency energy dataset (HES). The results demonstrate that incorporating SACs leads to a substantial improvement in disaggregation accuracy, reducing error by 45% compared to the baseline AFHMM and 29% compared to the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model, with comparable computational cost.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints—is both novel and highly intuitive for a wide range of BSS problems. While incorporating prior knowledge is a common theme, this specific form of constraint on the *aggregate* signal is a simple yet powerful idea that directly targets a key aspect of many real-world sources. The application to energy disaggregation is well-motivated, as average consumption data for appliances is often available from national surveys or manufacturer specifications. This makes the approach practically significant and likely to be adopted or built upon by other researchers in the field.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical development is sound. The formulation of the constrained optimization problem and its subsequent relaxation into a solvable CQP is a solid technical contribution that makes the proposed idea computationally feasible. The connection drawn to the posterior regularization framework in Section 6 adds theoretical depth and situates the work within a more general line of research on constrained probabilistic modeling.

3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results are strong and convincing. The authors compare their method against relevant and strong baselines (AFHMM and AFAMAP). The use of the HES dataset, which features challenging low-frequency data, demonstrates the robustness of the approach. The inclusion of an "oracle" experiment using the true total consumption (TTC) is particularly insightful, as it effectively bounds the potential improvement and clearly illustrates the value of the SAC concept. The reported performance gains are substantial and statistically significant, clearly demonstrating the efficacy of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance)**

1.  **Clarity and Readability:** The main weakness of this paper is its poor clarity. The manuscript is very dense and difficult to read, which significantly hinders the communication of its otherwise excellent ideas. A major contributing factor is the overuse of acronyms, some of which are non-standard and unusually long (e.g., AFHMM+SAC, AFAMAP, NDE, SAE, NTC, TTC). This forces the reader to constantly refer back to definitions, breaking the flow of the text and increasing cognitive load. The paper would be substantially improved by defining these acronyms once and then using more descriptive phrases (e.g., "the model with aggregate constraints" instead of "AFHMM+SAC," "normalized error" instead of "NDE").

2.  **Clarity of Model Formulation:** There is a subtle but important ambiguity in the presentation of the constraint. The authors frame the problem as MAP inference on the posterior `P(S|Y)` subject to the SACs (Eq. 4), but then reformulate this as a penalized objective function (Eq. 5). In Section 4.2, they state, "the constraint terms could be viewed as the logarithm of the prior distributions over the states S." This is a critical insight, as the SAC is effectively a non-Markovian prior over the entire state sequence for each chain. However, this point is somewhat buried. It would be much clearer to introduce the SAC from the outset as an *a priori* constraint that modifies the prior distribution over state sequences, which in turn shapes the posterior. The current framing could lead readers to mistakenly believe this is a post-hoc constraint applied directly to the posterior, rather than a fundamental part of the generative model's assumptions. Explicitly stating that this is an a priori constraint that makes the posterior non-Markovian would clarify the model's structure and the nature of the inference challenge.

**Detailed Comments**

*   The generalization of SACs mentioned at the end of Section 4.2 to cover different time periods (e.g., daytime vs. nighttime usage) is an interesting extension. While the authors state it is a straightforward extension, providing a brief example of how the CQP formulation would change could strengthen the paper.
*   The choice of `λi = 1` for all experiments seems arbitrary. While cross-validation is mentioned as a possibility, a brief sensitivity analysis or discussion on how this hyperparameter could be set in practice would be beneficial for reproducibility and practical application.

**Conclusion**

This paper introduces a valuable and novel contribution to the field of blind source separation by proposing signal aggregate constraints. The technical approach is sound, and the empirical results are strong and significant. The work addresses a real and important problem in a demonstrable way. However, the paper's impact is severely limited by its poor readability and a lack of clarity in the model's framing. The core ideas are strong enough to warrant publication at NIPS, but a substantial revision is necessary to improve the presentation. I would recommend acceptance on the condition that the authors thoroughly revise the manuscript to address the clarity issues, particularly regarding the overuse of acronyms and the framing of the constraint as an a priori assumption.
