## Review of “Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation”

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework to address the inherent identifiability issues in blind source separation, specifically demonstrated through energy disaggregation. The authors motivate the inclusion of domain knowledge via SACs – enforcing expected aggregate signal values for each source – and present a method for transforming the resulting optimization problem into a convex quadratic program, allowing for efficient approximate inference. The approach is validated on both a toy dataset and a real-world household energy consumption dataset.

The work builds upon existing research in BSS, particularly the AFHMM family, referencing approaches like AFAMAP and non-homogeneous HMMs for constraining hidden states. It also positions itself within the broader context of incorporating prior knowledge into probabilistic models via techniques like posterior regularization. The key novelty lies in the *type* of constraint proposed – focusing on aggregate signal values – which appears under-explored in this context. 

A significant strength of this paper is the strong empirical performance. Results on both datasets show consistent improvements over AFHMM and, importantly, a statistically significant improvement over the recent state-of-the-art AFAMAP approach. The comparable optimization speed to AFAMAP is also a positive attribute. The theoretical presentation is clear and well-structured. 

However, several points warrant consideration. The toy experiment utilizes a very low noise variance; clarification is needed whether this is representative of realistic scenarios or a deliberate choice to facilitate separation. A discussion of the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) for both the mains signal and the individual appliance signals would be beneficial. Crucially, while the authors state the procedure in equation (6) results in a convex problem, a formal proof of this convexity is missing and should be included. Furthermore, a theoretical scaling/complexity analysis (with respect to data length and the number of hidden states) and a tractability comparison to AFAMAP for larger datasets would strengthen the paper. Finally, a minor notational issue exists on page 4 (line 202) with possible extra commas in subscripts.

While I lack the expertise to fully assess the significance of this work to the broader HMM community or the energy application domain, it appears novel and original. The authors have satisfactorily addressed my initial queries in feedback, and incorporating those clarifications into the manuscript would further enhance its quality. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** Strong empirical results, novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge, clear presentation, comparable computational cost.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Lack of convexity proof, missing complexity analysis, questionable toy dataset noise level, need for SNR discussion.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions addressing the points outlined above.
