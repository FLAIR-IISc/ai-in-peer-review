## Review of “Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation”

This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) within an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) framework to improve energy disaggregation performance. The core idea – incorporating domain knowledge about expected total energy consumption for each appliance – is intuitively appealing and addresses a key challenge in blind source separation: identifiability. The authors formulate this as a convex quadratic program for approximate MAP inference and demonstrate substantial performance gains on both simulated and real-world datasets (100 UK homes at low frequency, 10 at higher frequency), outperforming a state-of-the-art approach. 

The work is grounded in a solid understanding of related techniques like AFAMAP and posterior regularization. The application to a large-scale, real-world energy disaggregation problem is a significant strength, moving beyond toy examples and demonstrating practical relevance. The optimization formulation, while straightforward, is effectively leveraged to tackle a challenging problem. The empirical results are compelling, showcasing a noticeable improvement over existing methods.

However, the paper would benefit from clarification on the nature of the solutions obtained with SACAFHMM. While showing error reduction, it’s unclear whether the SAC formulation actively encourages more integer-like solutions (similar to AFAMAP’s “one chain state at a time” constraint) or achieves lower error even with non-integral state assignments. Addressing this would provide further insight into the mechanism driving the performance improvement.

The reviewer’s note correctly points out the potential synergy between SACAFHMM and AFAMAP. The authors acknowledge these are orthogonal approaches, and exploring a hybrid approach – incorporating SAC constraints *within* the AFAMAP framework – seems like a promising next step. The paper occasionally conflates models (AFHMM) and inference procedures (AFAMAP), and improved precision in terminology would enhance clarity. Specifically, consistently identifying AFAMAP as an approximate MAP algorithm *for* AFHMM, and clarifying that AFHMM results likely refer to alternating Viterbi, is necessary.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a challenging problem (energy disaggregation) with a novel and intuitive approach.
*   Demonstrates significant empirical improvements on real-world data at scale.
*   Solid theoretical foundation and clear connection to existing work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity regarding the integrality of solutions.
*   Potential for further improvement through combining with AFAMAP.
*   Occasional terminological imprecision.



Overall, this is a reasonable and successful paper. Addressing the suggested clarifications and potential experiments (specifically, integrating SAC constraints into AFAMAP) would strengthen the contribution and further solidify its acceptance.
