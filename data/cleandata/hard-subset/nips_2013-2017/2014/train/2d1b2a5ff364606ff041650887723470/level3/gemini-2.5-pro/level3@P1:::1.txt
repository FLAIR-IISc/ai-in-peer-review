This paper introduces a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation (BSS) problems, specifically targeting the inherent identifiability issue. The proposed method, called Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), encourages the total aggregate value of each recovered source signal to be close to a known or expected value. The authors integrate SACs into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a common model for BSS tasks like energy disaggregation. A key technical contribution is the development of an efficient inference method for this constrained model. Since the SACs are non-Markovian and complicate inference, the authors formulate the MAP inference problem as a penalized objective function, relax the integer constraints, and solve the resulting convex quadratic program (CQP). The work is motivated by and demonstrated on the problem of energy disaggregation, where prior knowledge about the typical daily energy consumption of different appliances is often available. The paper also draws a useful connection between the proposed penalized objective and the posterior regularization framework.

The proposed AFHMM+SAC model is evaluated on both a synthetic toy dataset and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES). The results are compared against the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP. The experiments demonstrate that AFHMM+SAC significantly outperforms both baselines, reducing normalized disaggregation error by 33-45% and signal aggregate error by 16-29% compared to AFAMAP, with statistical significance confirmed by paired t-tests.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of using signal aggregate constraints is both novel and intuitive. It addresses a fundamental challenge in BSS—identifiability—by providing a principled way to incorporate valuable, and often readily available, domain knowledge. While the paper focuses on energy disaggregation, the concept of SACs appears to be a general and powerful tool that could be applied to other BSS domains, such as audio processing or computational biology. The results demonstrate that this approach can lead to substantial performance gains.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper presents a sound technical contribution by framing the complex, non-Markovian inference problem as a convex quadratic program. This makes inference tractable and computationally efficient, with reported runtimes similar to the AFAMAP baseline. The connection to the posterior regularization framework further strengthens the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed regularizer.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The problem is well-defined, the proposed model is explained in detail, and the relationship to prior work is clearly articulated.

4.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is a major strength. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on both synthetic and challenging real-world data. The use of two experimental settings for the real data (synthetically aggregated and actual mains readings) provides a comprehensive view of performance. The experiments convincingly show that AFHMM+SAC surpasses a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP. Furthermore, the analysis of the model's robustness to errors in the constraint values (µi0) on the toy data is a valuable addition.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for Improvement):**

1.  **Details of Experimental Setup:** In the toy data experiment, the noise variance is set to σ² = 0.01, which seems very low. This may make the separation problem artificially easy. It would be beneficial for the authors to clarify the rationale for this choice and potentially include results showing how performance degrades as the noise level increases. Additionally, for the real-world experiments (Section 7.2.2), where the mains signal includes unmodeled appliances, a discussion of the effective signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) would provide valuable context for the results.

2.  **Formal Justification of Convexity:** While the paper states that the relaxed optimization problem (6) is a CQP, a more formal justification would improve the paper's technical rigor. The authors mention that the matrices Λi and Vt are positive semidefinite, which is the key condition. A brief proof or a clearer derivation showing this property would make the convexity claim more self-contained and convincing for the reader.

3.  **Complexity Analysis:** The paper mentions that the CQP has polynomial time complexity and that the empirical runtime is similar to baselines. However, a formal theoretical complexity analysis would be a valuable addition. Providing the complexity in terms of the number of time steps (T), sources (I), and states per HMM (Ki) would allow readers to better understand the scalability of the method and compare it theoretically to AFAMAP and other potential approaches, especially for larger datasets.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a novel, well-motivated, and significant contribution to the field of blind source separation and its applications. The proposed Signal Aggregate Constraints offer a practical and effective way to overcome the identifiability problem in AFHMMs. The technical approach is sound, and the empirical results are strong and convincing, showing clear superiority over existing methods on a challenging real-world problem. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to requests for additional clarification and detail, which would further strengthen an already excellent paper. The work is of high quality and is likely to be of interest to researchers in machine learning, signal processing, and application domains like smart energy. I strongly recommend acceptance.
