

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation using Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs). The authors address the fundamental identifiability problem in blind source separation by incorporating domain knowledge through constraints on the total signal for each source. This builds upon the AFHMM framework introduced by Ghahramani and Jordan (2002) and extended by Kolter and Jaakkola (2012), while connecting to posterior regularization work by Ganchev et al. (2010). SACs differ from previous approaches like AFAMAP (Kolter and Jaakkola, 2012) that constrain state transitions, instead focusing on the aggregate values of source signals over time.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a well-defined mathematical formulation. The authors successfully transform the constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program that can be efficiently solved. The theoretical connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) provides strong justification for the approach. The experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real-world energy disaggregation data is comprehensive, showing a 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM and a 29% improvement over AFAMAP. The computational complexity analysis demonstrates that the proposed method scales similarly to existing approaches. However, the comparison with baselines may be unfair, as the AFHMM and AFAMAP baselines appear to use less data than the SAC-AFHMM (which leverages national consumption data for constraints), potentially biasing the results in favor of the proposed method.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations and good explanations of the problem. The connection between the problem motivation and the proposed solution is particularly strong. However, Figure 1 is confusing: it shows AFAMAP outperforming SAC-AFHMM when the constraint values are far from the true values, contradicting the paper's claim that SACs "always help." This undermines the paper's main claim and should be clarified in the revision. The authors should explain why SACs sometimes worsen performance when the constraints are mis-specified.

**Originality:** The SAC framework represents a novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation. While related to posterior regularization, SACs focus on a specific, practically important class of constraints (aggregate signal values) that have not been explored in previous NIPS work. The paper clearly differentiates SACs from prior constraint approaches like AFAMAP (which constrains state transitions) and k-segment constraints. This is a meaningful contribution that addresses a gap in the literature regarding what types of domain knowledge are most useful for BSS.

**Significance:** Energy disaggregation is a significant real-world problem with practical applications. The demonstrated improvements (29% reduction in error over state-of-the-art) are substantial and would be valuable to practitioners. The approach could potentially be applied to other BSS problems beyond energy disaggregation, such as audio source separation or computational biology. The paper makes a clear contribution to advancing the state of the art in this domain.

**Strengths:**
- Strong motivation for addressing the identifiability problem in BSS
- Solid mathematical foundation with convex relaxation
- Clear experimental results showing significant improvements
- Good connection to existing frameworks (posterior regularization)
- Well-written and well-organized paper

**Weaknesses:**
- Unfair comparison with baselines (AFHMM and AFAMAP) that may use less data
- Figure 1 contradicts the paper's main claim about SACs always improving performance
- Limited exploration of more complex constraint types (e.g., mutual exclusivity, duration limits)
- The claim that SACs "always help" is questionable based on the results

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the energy disaggregation literature with a novel and well-motivated approach. The technical content is strong, and the results are compelling. The main issues that need addressing are clarifying the comparison with baselines (ensuring fair data usage), explaining the counterintuitive results in Figure 1, and expanding the discussion of constraint types to better address the limitations of the current framework.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel constraint framework (SACs) that addresses a fundamental problem in BSS
- Strong experimental results showing significant improvements over state-of-the-art
- Solid theoretical foundation connecting to posterior regularization
- Clear writing and well-structured paper

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Unfair comparison with baseline methods
- Contradictory results in Figure 1 undermine the main claim
- Limited exploration of more complex constraint types

The authors should address these concerns in a revision to strengthen the paper's contribution and ensure the claims are fully supported by the evidence. With these clarifications, this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.
