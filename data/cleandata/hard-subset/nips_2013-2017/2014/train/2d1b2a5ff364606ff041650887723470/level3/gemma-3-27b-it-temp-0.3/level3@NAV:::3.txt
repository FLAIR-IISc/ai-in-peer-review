## Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework for blind source separation, specifically targeting energy disaggregation. The authors motivate this work by highlighting the identifiability issues inherent in BSS and propose SACs as a way to incorporate domain knowledge – namely, expected total energy consumption for different appliances. They present a convex quadratic relaxation for approximate inference and demonstrate promising results on both synthetic and real-world energy disaggregation datasets, achieving improvements in Normalized Disaggregation Error (NDE) and Signal Aggregate Error (SAE) compared to baseline methods and a state-of-the-art approach (AFAMAP).

The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the problem, proposed solution, and experimental setup. The mathematical foundation is solid, and the motivation for incorporating SACs is compelling. The connection to posterior regularization is a nice addition, providing a broader theoretical context. The experimental results are encouraging, demonstrating the potential of SACs to improve disaggregation accuracy. 

However, there are some weaknesses. The comparison to baselines appears potentially unfair. The AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP are trained on less data than the SAC-AFHMM, which could inflate the performance gains observed. A more equitable comparison, using the same training data for all methods, is needed. Furthermore, while the authors demonstrate the benefits of SACs, Figure 1 suggests that poorly chosen constraints can *worsen* performance, contradicting the claim that SACs always help. This point needs to be addressed more thoroughly. 

The paper would benefit from exploring other methods capable of incorporating SACs, even if computationally more expensive. This would strengthen the argument for the proposed approach and demonstrate a broader understanding of the solution space. Finally, the authors should consider extending the framework to handle more complex constraint types, such as mutual exclusivity (only one appliance can be on at a time), cardinality limits (maximum number of appliances on), or duration limits (appliance cannot be on for an unreasonably long time). Generalizing the algorithm to accommodate a wider range of constraints would significantly increase its applicability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of constraints to address identifiability in BSS.
*   Strong experimental results on a real-world dataset.
*   Clear writing and solid mathematical foundation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Potentially unfair comparison to baselines.
*   Figure 1 suggests SACs can degrade performance.
*   Limited exploration of alternative methods and constraint types.



**Overall:** This is a promising paper with a novel approach to a challenging problem. Addressing the concerns regarding the baseline comparison and the potential for detrimental constraints would significantly strengthen the contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
