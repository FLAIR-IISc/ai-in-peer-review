This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation within an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM), motivated by the insight that in many domains the total mass (e.g., daily energy per appliance) varies across sources and is often approximately known. The authors incorporate SACs as quadratic penalties that encourage each chain’s total energy to match a specified target and derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) for relaxed MAP inference by augmenting the AFHMM with auxiliary transition variables. They relate the objective to posterior regularization and demonstrate substantial gains on low-frequency energy disaggregation data (HES), outperforming a plain AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12), with additional oracle results showing the upper bound of benefit from perfect aggregate priors. The idea is clearly connected to prior NIPS work on factorial HMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan), structured posterior constraints (Ganchev et al.), generalized expectation (Mann & McCallum), measurement-based learning (Liang et al.), duration/semi-Markov constraints (Johnson & Willsky), and other HMM regularizations (e.g., k-segment constraints by Titsias et al.).

Strengths
- Simple, general insight (constrain aggregate signal) that squarely addresses identifiability in BSS and NILM, with a broadly applicable formulation.
- Clean optimization: a tractable CQP via relaxation with PSD structure; practical implementation using CVX; similar runtime to AFHMM/AFAMAP in the reported settings.
- Conceptual connection to posterior regularization clarifies the statistical meaning of the penalty and situates the method in a recognized framework.
- Empirical results on a challenging, realistic low-frequency dataset (HES) show sizable NDE/SAE improvements over strong baselines, including statistically significant gains versus AFAMAP.
- Useful extension notes (multiple time-window constraints) increase practical relevance.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity/readability: The manuscript is hard to read due to heavy notation and excessive use of long acronyms (AFHMM+SAC, NDE, SAE, NTC, TTC, etc.). Consider:
  - Reducing acronyms; define once and use names in prose when possible.
  - Moving dense notation and Kronecker-based derivations to an appendix and adding a high-level algorithm box with inputs/outputs.
  - Providing an intuition-first walk-through of how SACs shape the posterior (e.g., a schematic of how aggregate penalties bias the solution away from “franken-appliances”).
- Prior vs. posterior constraint: The main objective is presented as adding a quadratic prior over aggregate energy (Eq. 5), and later re-derived via posterior regularization. Please reconcile these views explicitly. State clearly that the constraint is a priori on aggregates (operationalized as a prior/regularizer), which in turn shapes the posterior, and contrast with direct posterior constraints.
- Hyperparameters and priors: λi are fixed to 1; no tuning or sensitivity is reported on real data. Provide cross-validation or empirical Bayes guidance, and analyze robustness to misspecified µ0 beyond the toy study (e.g., bias-variance trade-off as λ varies; per-appliance sensitivity).
- Rounding/discreteness: The CQP relaxes Sit and hitjk to [0,1]. Explain how a discrete state sequence (if needed) is recovered, and whether fractional Sit are used directly to compute x̂it = µT i Sit. Report any performance difference between relaxed and rounded solutions.
- Baselines and ablations: Consider comparisons to duration/semi-Markov NILM models (e.g., HSMM-based approaches) and to AFAMAP with additional priors (if available). An ablation on windowed SACs (daytime-only constraints) would strengthen the case for the generalized constraints described in Section 4.2.
- External knowledge and portability: The NTC prior may be biased by demographics/seasonality. Discuss how to learn µ0 from limited meta-data, how to handle missing appliances, and how performance degrades when unseen loads contribute to the mains.
- Experimental reporting: Only 9 houses with mains are used; provide more details on variability, appliance coverage, and confidence intervals across households. Clarify training/test splits, state-count choice (3 states for all appliances), and computational scaling with I, Ki, and T.

Quality
- Technically sound formulation; the optimization and PSD structure are correct, and the posterior-regularization link is appropriate. However, more details on rounding and hyperparameter selection would improve reproducibility and completeness.

Clarity
- The paper needs significant editorial work to reduce acronym density, streamline notation, and add algorithmic summaries and intuition. Figures/tables that emphasize practical flow would help.

Originality
- The specific notion of constraining aggregate signal totals in an AFHMM, with a CQP relaxation and PR connection, is novel relative to prior NILM work that focuses on transition/duration constraints. The contribution is incremental conceptually but well-targeted and practically meaningful.

Significance
- Strong improvements on a realistic benchmark and a generally applicable idea for BSS make this useful to both NILM and broader latent variable modeling communities. The method is likely to be adopted where aggregates are known or estimable.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a core identifiability issue in a principled, general, and computationally tractable way.
- Demonstrates substantial empirical gains on challenging real data with significance tests.
- Provides a clear bridge between engineered priors and posterior regularization.

Arguments against acceptance
- Presentation is difficult to follow; excessive acronyms and dense notation impede understanding.
- Limited exploration of hyperparameters, robustness, rounding, and alternative strong baselines (e.g., HSMM).
- Dependency on external aggregate priors is not thoroughly stress-tested for distribution shift.

Actionable feedback to improve the manuscript
- Reduce acronyms, add an algorithm box, and move heavy algebra to an appendix with a narrative derivation in the main text.
- Clearly state that SACs are implemented as an a priori aggregate prior (quadratic penalty) that induces the desired posterior, and reconcile this with the PR derivation.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for λi and µ0, document rounding, and expand baselines/ablations (including windowed SACs).
- Discuss how to set µ0 in practice (e.g., hierarchical priors, covariate-conditioned estimates) and report performance under mis-specification.
