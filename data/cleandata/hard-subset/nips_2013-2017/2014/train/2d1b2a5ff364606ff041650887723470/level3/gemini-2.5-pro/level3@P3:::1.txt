This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge into blind source separation problems, addressing the critical issue of source identifiability. The authors apply this concept to an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a common framework for this task. The primary application and evaluation domain is energy disaggregation, where the goal is to separate a household's total electricity consumption signal into the signals of individual appliances. The core technical contribution is a method for tractable inference in the proposed AFHMM+SAC model. The authors transform the non-Markovian posterior log-objective function, which includes the aggregate constraints, into a relaxed convex quadratic program (CQP) that can be solved efficiently. This work builds upon previous efforts to constrain AFHMMs for energy disaggregation, such as the AFAMAP model by Kolter & Jaakkola (2012), by introducing a new, intuitive, and effective type of constraint based on prior knowledge of the total expected energy consumption of each source.

### Strengths

*   **Novelty and Originality:** The central idea of using Signal Aggregate Constraints is both novel and highly intuitive. In many BSS applications, particularly energy disaggregation, priors on the total consumption or signal contribution of a source are often available (e.g., from manufacturer specifications or national surveys). This work provides a principled way to inject this powerful domain knowledge into the model to resolve identifiability issues, moving beyond purely structural constraints (like in AFAMAP).
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper presents a solid technical contribution by formulating MAP inference in the AFHMM+SAC model as a CQP. This is a non-trivial step, as the SACs break the Markovian structure of the model, rendering standard dynamic programming algorithms like Viterbi inapplicable. By relaxing the integer constraints, the authors derive a tractable inference algorithm that is shown to be effective in practice. The connection drawn to the posterior regularization framework further strengthens the theoretical grounding of the proposed penalized objective function.
*   **Significance and Impact:** The application to energy disaggregation is well-motivated and of significant practical interest. The experimental results are strong and compelling. The proposed AFHMM+SAC method demonstrates a substantial and statistically significant improvement over both the baseline AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model on a challenging, real-world, low-frequency dataset. The reported 29% reduction in disaggregation error compared to AFAMAP on real mains data is a noteworthy advancement. The fact that the optimization speed is comparable to AFAMAP makes it a practical and superior alternative.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem is well-defined, the proposed model is explained logically, and the experimental setup and results are presented effectively.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, there are several areas where it could be improved:

1.  **Justification of Convexity:** The paper states that the relaxed optimization problem (6) is a Convex Quadratic Program because the matrices Λi and Vt are positive semidefinite. While this is correct, the technical presentation would be more rigorous if the authors included a short, formal proof or a more detailed justification of the problem's convexity. This would add to the completeness of the technical contribution.

2.  **Toy Experiment Realism:** The toy experiment uses a very low noise variance (σ² = 0.01). This raises a question about whether the setting is realistic or chosen specifically to make the separation problem easier. It would be valuable for the authors to clarify the rationale for this choice. Furthermore, adding a brief discussion of the typical signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) in the real-world energy data—comparing the magnitude of individual appliance signals to the aggregate mains signal and noise—would provide better context for the difficulty of the task and the robustness of the method.

3.  **Lack of Complexity Analysis:** The paper empirically demonstrates that the inference time is comparable to AFAMAP, which is a useful practical result. However, the work would be strengthened by a formal theoretical complexity analysis. The authors should provide an analysis of how the computational cost of solving the CQP scales with the key parameters of the problem: the length of the time series (T), the number of sources/appliances (I), and the total number of hidden states. A direct theoretical comparison of this scaling to that of the inference method used in AFAMAP would be particularly insightful for readers interested in applying these methods to very large datasets.

4.  **Minor Notational Issue:** On page 4, in the generalization of the constraints within Section 4.2, there appear to be extra commas in the subscripts before 'p' (e.g., `Si,τ(i)j`). This seems to be a minor typographical error that should be corrected for clarity.

### Final Remarks

Overall, this paper presents a novel, well-motivated, and effective approach for a challenging problem. The introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints is an important contribution to the field of blind source separation and its applications. The strong empirical results on a real-world energy disaggregation task clearly demonstrate the value of the proposed method. The weaknesses identified are primarily suggestions for improving the paper's completeness and rigor rather than fundamental flaws.

The authors provided satisfactory clarifications to reviewer queries in their feedback. I strongly recommend that these clarifications, particularly concerning the experimental details and model properties, be incorporated into the final version of the paper to benefit all readers.

Given the novelty of the idea, the solid technical execution, and the significant empirical improvements over state-of-the-art methods, I recommend this paper for acceptance.
