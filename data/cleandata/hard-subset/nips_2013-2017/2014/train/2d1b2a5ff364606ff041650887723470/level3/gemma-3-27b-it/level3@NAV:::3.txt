## Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework for blind source separation, specifically tackling the energy disaggregation problem. The core idea – incorporating domain knowledge about expected total energy consumption of appliances – is well-motivated and addresses a key challenge in BSS: identifiability. The authors propose a convex quadratic relaxation for approximate inference, demonstrating promising results on both synthetic and real-world (HES dataset) data, showing improvements in Normalized Disaggregation Error (NDE) and Signal Aggregate Error (SAE) compared to existing methods, notably AFAMAP. 

The paper is well-written, clearly articulating the problem, proposed solution, and experimental setup. The mathematical foundation is solid, and the connection to related work – particularly in posterior regularization – is appropriately established. The authors convincingly demonstrate a tangible benefit to incorporating aggregate constraints, a potentially valuable contribution to the field of energy disaggregation and BSS more broadly.

However, the experimental setup raises some concerns regarding fairness. The baseline methods (AFHMM without SAC and AFAMAP) appear to be trained on significantly less data than the SAC-AFHMM, potentially inflating the performance gain observed. A more equitable comparison with a similar data volume would strengthen the claims. Furthermore, while the paper demonstrates the benefits of SACs, it would be valuable to investigate whether other methods could similarly benefit from incorporating such constraints, even if at a higher computational cost. This would better establish the unique contribution of the proposed approach.

Figure 1 is particularly troubling. The observation that AFAMAP outperforms SAC-AFHMM at distances > 1e4 contradicts the claim that SACs *always* help, and suggests that poorly specified constraints can actually *worsen* performance. This requires further investigation and a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach. 

Future work should explore extending SACs to more complex constraint types, such as mutual exclusivity (an appliance cannot be on simultaneously with another), cardinality limits (limiting the number of active appliances), and duration limits (specifying typical appliance usage times). Generalizing the algorithm to handle broader classes of constraints would significantly broaden its applicability and impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of domain knowledge through SACs.
*   Solid mathematical formulation and a tractable solution via convex relaxation.
*   Demonstrated improvements over existing methods on a real-world dataset.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Potential unfairness in experimental comparison due to differing data volumes.
*   Figure 1 suggests SACs aren't universally beneficial.
*   Limited exploration of alternative approaches for incorporating SACs.



Overall, this paper presents a promising approach to a challenging problem. Addressing the concerns regarding experimental fairness and the implications of Figure 1 would significantly strengthen the contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
