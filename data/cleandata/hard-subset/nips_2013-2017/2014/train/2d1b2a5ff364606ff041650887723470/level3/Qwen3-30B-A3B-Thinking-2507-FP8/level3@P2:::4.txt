

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) for blind source separation problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation using additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs). SACs constrain the total sum of each source signal to match a known value, addressing the identifiability problem in BSS. The authors formulate this as a convex quadratic program for approximate MAP inference, which is computationally efficient. This work builds on previous approaches to energy disaggregation like AFHMM [17] and AFAMAP [12], which use constraints on state transitions, but differs by incorporating aggregate knowledge rather than temporal constraints. The approach is related to posterior regularization [4] as shown in Section 6, where the authors demonstrate that their objective function can be derived from the posterior regularization framework.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The authors present a compelling application of SACs to energy disaggregation, a problem with significant practical importance. The experimental results are thorough and convincing: on real-world UK household energy data (100 households with low-frequency data and 9 households with mains readings), SACs improve performance by 45% over the original AFHMM and 29% over the recent state-of-the-art AFAMAP approach. The paper demonstrates both normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) improvements, which are particularly relevant for energy applications where total appliance consumption is important. The computational efficiency is well-demonstrated, with similar inference times across AFHMM, AFAMAP, and SAC-enhanced versions.

The technical contribution is well-motivated and the convex relaxation approach is sound. The authors correctly note that SACs represent a novel constraint type that complements rather than replaces existing approaches. The paper's simplicity is a strength - the SAC formulation is straightforward to implement and integrates cleanly with existing AFHMM infrastructure.

However, several points require clarification. The paper conflates models and inference procedures in places (e.g., referring to "AFHMM results" when describing alternating Viterbi, and "AFAMAP" when discussing the constrained inference procedure). It would be helpful to clarify that AFAMAP is an approximate MAP algorithm for AFHMM, while SACAFHMM is a modified model with additional constraints. The authors should also address whether SACAFHMM yields more integral solutions (like AFAMAP's "one chain state at a time" constraint) or whether the improved performance is achieved despite non-integral solutions.

A particularly interesting suggestion is that SACAFHMM and AFAMAP are fundamentally orthogonal approaches. The authors should consider exploring the combination of SACs with AFAMAP in future work, as this could yield even better performance. The paper currently lacks discussion of how SACs might interact with other constraint-based approaches.

The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, though some minor improvements would enhance clarity. The experimental setup is appropriate for the energy disaggregation domain, and the use of both simulated and real-world data provides strong validation. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the need for prior knowledge about aggregate consumption values.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with strong experimental validation. The theoretical foundation is clear, and the convex optimization approach is well-justified. The work represents a complete piece of research with clear contributions to both the theory and practice of energy disaggregation.

Regarding originality, SACs represent a novel constraint type for BSS problems. While related work on posterior regularization [4] and other constraints exists, the specific use of aggregate constraints is new and well-motivated by the energy disaggregation application. The paper appropriately cites relevant NIPS work from previous years.

The significance of this work is high. Energy disaggregation has real-world impact for energy conservation, and the improvements demonstrated here are substantial. The approach could be applied to other domains where aggregate knowledge is available, such as audio source separation (where total energy per source might be known) or biological signal processing.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a simple but effective extension to existing methods that yields significant empirical improvements. The work is well-executed and addresses an important practical problem with clear scientific contributions. The suggested clarifications regarding model/inference distinctions and the potential combination with AFAMAP would further strengthen the paper.

Pro:
- Novel and well-motivated constraint type (SACs) for BSS
- Significant performance improvements on real-world energy disaggregation data
- Clear and efficient convex optimization formulation
- Strong empirical validation across multiple datasets
- Practical relevance to energy monitoring applications

Con:
- Some conflation of models and inference procedures
- Could better clarify relationship with AFAMAP
- Should consider exploring combination of SACs with AFAMAP
- Limited discussion of whether SACs yield more integral solutions

I recommend acceptance. This is a solid contribution to the field with clear theoretical and practical merits. The paper addresses a meaningful problem with an effective solution that demonstrates significant improvements over existing approaches. The suggested clarifications are minor and would be straightforward to address in the final version.
