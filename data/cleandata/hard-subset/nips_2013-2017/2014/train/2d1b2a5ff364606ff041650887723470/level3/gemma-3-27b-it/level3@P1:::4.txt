This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) into Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs) to address the inherent unidentifiability of blind source separation problems, with a specific application to energy disaggregation. The authors observe that prior knowledge about the total signal expected from unknown sources can be leveraged by encouraging the aggregate values of source signals to be close to specified values. They formulate this as a convex quadratic program for approximate MAP inference and demonstrate significant performance improvements on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, surpassing both the original AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method (AFAMAP).

The work builds on a well-established line of research in blind source separation and energy disaggregation, citing key works such as Hart (1978) and Kolter & Jaakkola (2012). The paper appropriately positions itself within the broader context of incorporating prior knowledge into probabilistic models, referencing posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2009) and measurement-based learning (Liang et al., 2011).  The authors effectively highlight the challenge of identifiability in BSS and the importance of domain-specific constraints, a problem also addressed by approaches like AFAMAP which constrains state changes within the HMMs.

**Strengths:**

* **Practical Relevance:** The application to energy disaggregation is compelling and addresses a practical problem with significant societal impact. Using a large, real-world dataset (HES) lends credibility to the findings.
* **Clear Formulation:** The introduction of SACs is intuitive and well-motivated.  The mathematical formulation, including the convex relaxation, is clearly presented and justified. The connection to posterior regularization provides a strong theoretical foundation.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported performance improvements – a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM and a 29% improvement over AFAMAP – are substantial and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Computational Efficiency:**  The authors acknowledge and address the computational challenges associated with the SACs, and successfully formulate a solvable convex quadratic program. They also point out the similar computational cost to other methods.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimentation includes both simulated data for robustness testing and a realistic energy disaggregation scenario, making the results convincing.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Integral vs. Non-Integral Solutions:** While the results are strong, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of the nature of the solutions obtained. It would be valuable to clarify whether the SAC formulation yields solutions closer to integer values (like AFAMAP’s single state change constraint) or achieves lower error despite primarily relying on non-integral solutions.  Understanding this aspect could provide insights into the practical interpretability of the disaggregated signals.
* **Model & Inference Conflation:** There are instances where the paper appears to conflate the model (AFHMM) with the inference procedure (AFAMAP).  It’s important to consistently clarify that AFAMAP is an approximate MAP algorithm *for* the AFHMM, and when referencing “AFHMM” results, it likely refers to using the alternating Viterbi algorithm.  Precision in this regard would enhance clarity.
* **Limited Exploration of Combined Approaches:** The authors correctly point out that AFAMAP and SACAFHMM are fundamentally orthogonal constraints. While they mention this as a potential future direction, further discussion of the potential benefits and challenges of combining these approaches within a single framework would be valuable. Specifically, incorporating SACs *into* the AFAMAP algorithm warrants investigation.
* **Parameter Tuning:** While the paper addresses hyperparameter selection, a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the results to the choice of λ (or δ) would be useful.  



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a challenging problem in blind source separation with a novel and well-motivated approach.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods on a real-world dataset.
* The mathematical formulation is clear and rigorous.
* The paper is well-written and provides a solid contribution to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed analysis regarding the nature of the solutions (integral vs. non-integral) is a minor weakness.
* The potential for combining SACs with other inference techniques (like AFAMAP) is not fully explored.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a simple yet effective extension to the AFHMM for blind source separation. The introduction of signal aggregate constraints is a clever way to incorporate domain knowledge and improve the performance of the model. The empirical results are convincing, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above – clarifying solution characteristics, elaborating on the potential for combining approaches, and providing a more detailed discussion of parameter tuning – would further strengthen the contribution.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
