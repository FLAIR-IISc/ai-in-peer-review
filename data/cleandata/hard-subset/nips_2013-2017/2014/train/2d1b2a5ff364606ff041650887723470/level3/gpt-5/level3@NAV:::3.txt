This paper proposes an Additive Factorial HMM augmented with Signal Aggregate Constraints (SAC) and an efficient convex quadratic relaxation for MAP inference in blind source separation, with a primary application to energy disaggregation. The key idea is to inject domain knowledge about per-source totals (e.g., daily Wh per appliance) into AFHMMs via quadratic penalties, and to solve a relaxed CQP by introducing transition variables. The approach is related to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.) and builds on AFHMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan) and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola). It also connects to prior constraints used in disaggregation (duration/semi-Markov, one-change constraints, k-segment constraints).

Quality: The technical development is solid: the SAC penalty is well-motivated, the CQP is carefully constructed with PSD structure, and the link to posterior regularization is clean. Synthetic and HES results show improvements in NDE and SAE. However, the empirical comparison may be unfair: AFHMM+SAC leverages extra information (NTC/TTC) that AFHMM/AFAMAP do not, and TTC is an oracle. The sensitivity to misspecified constraints is only partially explored; Figure 1 suggests that with large mismatch (distance >1e4), AFAMAP can outperform SACAFHMM, so SAC does not “always help.” The relaxation gap and choice/tuning of λ (fixed to 1) are not analyzed.

Clarity: Overall well written and well organized with a solid mathematical foundation. Figure 1 is unclear (axes/units, takeaway), and the claim that SAC is universally helpful should be tempered. Please detail hyperparameter selection, computational scaling beyond CVX, and provide enough information for reproduction.

Originality: Incorporating aggregate constraints into AFHMM via a convex relaxation is a novel and useful instantiation of posterior regularization for BSS. While the framework leverages known ideas, the specific formulation for energy disaggregation is original.

Significance: The idea is broadly applicable to BSS where totals are known or estimable, and the gains on realistic, low-frequency data are promising. Broader impact would increase with more general constraint classes and fairer baselines.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled contribution (SAC) with a convex relaxation and strong motivation.
- Demonstrated improvements in NDE/SAE on challenging data.
- Connection to posterior regularization broadens applicability.

Cons for acceptance:
- Potentially unfair comparisons; baselines lack the same side information.
- Robustness and failure modes insufficiently characterized; Figure 1 contradicts “always helps.”
- Constraint class limited (only aggregates); no experiments with richer constraints.

Suggestions:
- Evaluate baselines augmented with SAC (e.g., AFAMAP+SAC) or other methods that can incorporate SAC, even if slower; control for “information budget.”
- Add sensitivity studies for λ and µ0 (including mis-specification), and discuss the relaxation gap.
- Extend to richer constraints (mutual exclusivity, cardinality, duration/segment/dwell-time limits) and discuss how the CQP generalizes.
