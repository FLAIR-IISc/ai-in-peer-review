This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) within an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) to improve blind source separation, specifically addressing the energy disaggregation problem. The core idea is to incorporate domain knowledge – expectations about the total energy consumption of individual appliances – to enhance identifiability and accuracy. The authors formulate this as a convex quadratic program for approximate inference and demonstrate significant performance improvements on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, outperforming both the baseline AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in blind source separation (BSS) [3, 17, 2] and energy disaggregation [8, 12, 22, 15]. It addresses a critical challenge in BSS: the inherent unidentifiability of source signals. Previous efforts to incorporate prior knowledge include posterior regularization [4], the generalized expectation criterion [14], and measurement-based learning [13], however, this paper specifically focuses on *what* kind of prior knowledge to incorporate, proposing SACs as a generally applicable and computationally tractable solution. The use of AFHMMs is well-established in this domain [17], providing a solid foundation for the proposed extension. Recent advances in constraining AFHMMs, such as the AFAMAP algorithm [12] and non-homogeneous HMMs [21], are acknowledged and appropriately contextualized.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The introduction of signal aggregate constraints as a principled way to incorporate domain knowledge into BSS, and specifically into AFHMMs, is a valuable contribution. The authors clearly articulate the motivation and potential benefits of this approach.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The formulation of the problem as a convex quadratic program demonstrates a solid understanding of optimization techniques. The theoretical justification for the approach, linking it to posterior regularization [6], strengthens the technical foundation.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on the HES dataset are compelling. The consistent performance gains over the baseline AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP method [12] provide strong evidence of the effectiveness of SACs. The authors thoughtfully present results with and without mains readings, revealing robustness.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Energy disaggregation is a practically important problem with significant potential for energy conservation. The demonstrated improvements in disaggregation accuracy have direct real-world implications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Readability:** The paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The excessive use of acronyms (SAC, AFHMM, AFAMAP, etc.) makes it difficult to follow the arguments. Some acronyms are unusually long and detract from readability.  The writing style is dense and could be significantly improved through simplification and more descriptive phrasing.
*   **Prior Specification:** While the authors mention the constraint could be obtained from expert knowledge or prior experiments, the paper doesn’t fully explore the sensitivity of the method to the accuracy of the prior (µi0). A more detailed discussion of how inaccuracies in µi0 affect performance would be beneficial.
*   **A Priori vs. A Posteriori:** The authors implicitly treat the SAC as a prior constraint, but this is not explicitly stated. It's crucial to clearly articulate that the constraints are applied to the prior distribution, thereby shaping the posterior distribution. This clarification is essential for a complete understanding of the method.
*   **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While the authors claim similar computational cost to AFHMM and AFAMAP, a more detailed analysis of scalability with respect to the number of appliances and the length of the time series would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in BSS.
*   The empirical results demonstrate significant performance improvements on a real-world dataset.
*   The work addresses a critical issue in BSS: the need to incorporate domain knowledge to improve identifiability.
*   The formulation as a convex quadratic program allows for efficient computation.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The paper's clarity and readability are significantly hampered by the overuse of acronyms and a dense writing style.
*   The discussion of prior specification and sensitivity is limited.
*   The explicit framing of the constraints as a prior is missing.




**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the clarity issues, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation and energy disaggregation. The core idea of using signal aggregate constraints is both novel and effective, and the empirical results are compelling. However, the authors *must* address the readability concerns and clarify the nature of the constraints (a priori vs. a posteriori) to make the paper accessible to a wider audience.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should prioritize improving the clarity and readability of the manuscript by reducing the use of acronyms, simplifying the writing style, and explicitly stating the constraints are applied to the prior distribution. A more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the method to inaccuracies in the prior would also strengthen the paper.
