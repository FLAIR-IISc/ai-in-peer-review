This paper introduces a compelling and practical extension to Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs) by incorporating Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for the challenging problem of energy disaggregation. The approach is well-motivated, leverages a convex relaxation for efficient inference, and demonstrates significant empirical improvements on real-world datasets, including a large-scale UK household study.

The core idea – leveraging prior knowledge about expected total energy consumption for different appliances – is intuitively strong and addresses a key limitation of standard BSS techniques, namely identifiability. The authors correctly position the work within the broader context of blind source separation and related efforts to incorporate domain knowledge into probabilistic models. The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) is insightful and provides a valuable theoretical grounding. 

The strength of the paper lies in its successful application to a realistically sized and complex dataset. Applying the method to 100 UK homes with low-frequency data (and a smaller set with higher frequency data) is a substantial contribution. The demonstrated improvements over both the baseline AFHMM and a state-of-the-art approach (AFAMAP) are convincing. The clarity of presentation regarding the optimization formulation and the use of CVX is appreciated.

However, several points require clarification. The paper occasionally conflates the *model* (AFHMM) with the *inference procedure* (AFAMAP).  AFAMAP is explicitly described as an approximate MAP algorithm *for* AFHMM, and results attributed to “AFHMM” likely refer to results obtained via alternating Viterbi. Precision in this distinction is crucial.

A key question raised by the authors themselves – whether the SACAFHMM formulation yields more integral (integer) solutions, similar to AFAMAP’s “one chain state at a time” constraint, or achieves lower error despite potentially non-integral solutions – remains unanswered and warrants further discussion. It is important to understand *why* SACAFHMM performs better; is it simply minimizing error, or is it also guiding the solution towards more interpretable, physically plausible states?

The authors correctly suggest that AFAMAP and SACAFHMM are fundamentally orthogonal. Combining the constraints inherent in AFAMAP (limiting chain state changes) *within* the SACAFHMM framework, as suggested, is a promising direction for future work and would likely further improve performance.  Exploring other constraint combinations (e.g., the k-segment constraints mentioned in the related work) could also be fruitful.

The claim regarding computational efficiency (similar runtime to AFHMM and AFAMAP) should be substantiated with more detailed runtime comparisons, perhaps in an appendix. While the authors state the complexity scales polynomially, providing actual runtimes on the datasets used would be helpful.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The convex relaxation is well-justified, and the experimental validation is robust. The authors acknowledge limitations and potential areas for improvement.
**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, although the aforementioned conflation of models and inference procedures occasionally introduces ambiguity.
**Originality:** The introduction of signal aggregate constraints as a general approach to BSS, and its successful application to energy disaggregation, is novel. The combination of a straightforward algorithmic extension with a significant empirical impact is noteworthy.
**Significance:** The results are important and address a difficult problem in a practical setting. The potential for broader application of SACs to other BSS problems is significant. The use of real-world data and the demonstrated performance gains suggest the work is likely to be adopted by researchers and practitioners in the energy disaggregation field.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem with a novel and well-motivated approach.
*   Demonstrates significant empirical improvements on real-world datasets.
*   Provides a clear and concise presentation of the method and results.
*   Identifies promising directions for future research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Occasional conflation of models and inference procedures requires clarification.
*   The question of integral vs. non-integral solutions needs further investigation.
*   More detailed runtime comparisons would strengthen the claims regarding computational efficiency.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a reasonable and successful paper. The contributions are valuable, and the empirical results are convincing. Addressing the minor points raised above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
