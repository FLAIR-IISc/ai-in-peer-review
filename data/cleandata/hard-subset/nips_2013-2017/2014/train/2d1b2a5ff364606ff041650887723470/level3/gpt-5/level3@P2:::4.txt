Summary
The paper proposes Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs). SACs encode prior knowledge about the total signal produced by each latent source over a time interval (e.g., daily appliance energy), encouraging ∑t xit ≈ μi0. The authors incorporate SACs into AFHMM (AFHMM+SAC) by adding a quadratic penalty to the MAP objective. Because this penalty couples all time steps and breaks Markov decomposability, they introduce auxiliary transition variables and derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) relaxation over state marginals S and transition marginals H, solvable with CVX. They also relate the objective to posterior regularization.

Empirically, on a toy dataset they show robustness to moderate mismatch in μ0. On the UK HES dataset with ultra-low frequency data, AFHMM+SAC improves normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) over AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12), both when aggregate “mains” is simulated (100 households, one test day each) and when it is measured (9 households). They also show significance via paired t-tests and report comparable runtimes.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on AFHMMs for disaggregation, AFAMAP’s “one-chain-change-at-a-time” constraint, semi-Markov models, and recent frameworks for incorporating constraints into posteriors (posterior regularization, generalized expectation, measurement-based learning). Compared to these, the novelty is identifying total-signal constraints as practically available, task-agnostic priors and giving a tractable convex relaxation within AFHMMs.
- Aggregate constraints have appeared in probabilistic databases for independent variables; this work extends the idea to coupled temporal models with HMM structure.

Quality
- Method: The objective is well-motivated and the derivation of the CQP with auxiliary H variables is sound; the PSD structure of Vt and Λi justifies convexity after relaxation. The posterior regularization derivation is a nice alternative view.
- Inference: One key missing detail is how fractional marginals from the relaxation are converted to discrete state sequences (or whether fractional solutions are directly used to compute x̂). Rounding/projection strategy, its effect on constraint satisfaction and error, and integrality rates should be reported. Clarify whether SAC encourages integrality compared to the unconstrained relaxation.
- Experiments: The HES evaluation is compelling: low-frequency data, significant improvements, paired t-tests, and reasonable compute time. However, selection of λi is fixed (λi=1); some sensitivity analysis or cross-validation would strengthen claims. Also, the “oracle” TTC experiments are informative but should be clearly separated from practical settings.
- Comparisons: AFHMM+SAC is compared to AFHMM and AFAMAP; since AFAMAP and SAC are orthogonal, it would be natural to add SAC to AFAMAP and report results (this could be a straightforward extension of the same CQP framework or AFAMAP’s IQP). Comparisons to semi-Markov baselines on HES (as in related work) would also contextualize gains.
- Statistical analysis: Please provide details of the paired t-tests (per-day pairing, number of test days, normality assumptions). Consider reporting effect sizes and per-appliance SAE.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-structured. A few places conflate models and inference: AFHMM is the generative model; AFAMAP is an approximate MAP inference algorithm for AFHMM with an extra constraint; AFHMM “results” likely use alternating/chainwise Viterbi. Please standardize terminology accordingly.
- Specify the rounding/post-processing used after solving the CQP. If using argmax per time/chain, explain how you maintain H–S consistency and what happens to SAC satisfaction after rounding.
- Provide explicit complexity in terms of T, I, and Ki, solver settings for CVX, and practical scalability limits (e.g., memory/time vs T). The claim that runtimes are “similar” would benefit from more detail (e.g., median seconds per day, interquartile ranges).
- Minor: clean up references and ensure complete bibliographic fields.

Originality
- The idea of penalizing deviations of total signal per source and embedding this in AFHMMs with a convex relaxation is simple but novel and practically useful. The mapping to posterior regularization is elegant and pedagogical.

Significance
- The improvements on a large, realistic low-frequency dataset are meaningful for NILM, and SACs are broadly applicable to other BSS domains where totals are known or estimable. The approach is likely to be adopted, especially given its simplicity and compatibility with existing AFHMM variants.

Strengths
- Clear, task-driven prior (SAC) that addresses identifiability in a realistic way.
- Convex relaxation with an efficient formulation; principled link to posterior regularization.
- Strong empirical gains on challenging low-frequency HES data; statistical significance; reasonable compute times.
- Straightforward to generalize (e.g., windowed SACs, time-of-day priors).

Weaknesses
- Missing details on integrality/rounding and the practical impact of relaxation.
- No experiment combining SAC with AFAMAP’s orthogonal constraint.
- Limited sensitivity analysis for λ and robustness to mis-specified μ0 on real data.
- Comparisons could include semi-Markov or duration-model baselines on the same data.

Questions/clarifications for the authors
- Do SACs increase the integrality of solutions compared to the unconstrained relaxation? What fraction of Sit and Hit are integral at the optimum? How are solutions rounded?
- How strictly are SACs satisfied after rounding? Please report the average squared deviation per chain.
- How are λi chosen in practice? Any validation or heuristic based on appliance variability?
- For NTC, how is μ0 estimated per household/day; is there risk of bias or leakage across splits?
- Could you report per-appliance SAE and NDE, and an analysis of which appliances benefit most?
- Can SAC be added to AFAMAP within your framework, and if so, what are the results?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a combined AFAMAP+SAC baseline.
- Include a short robustness study on HES varying μ0 quality (e.g., perturb NTC by ±20%).
- Describe rounding explicitly and report integrality/feasibility metrics.
- Provide clearer runtime scaling and solver details; consider a specialized solver or warm-starts.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, general idea with clear motivation and strong empirical gains on a substantial real dataset.
- Methodologically sound with a clean convex formulation; connects to known frameworks.
- Likely impact in NILM and other BSS settings where aggregate priors are available.

Arguments against acceptance
- Some methodological details (rounding/integrality) are underspecified.
- Limited exploration of orthogonal constraints and ablations; missing combined baseline with AFAMAP.
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters and μ0 quality on real data not fully explored.
