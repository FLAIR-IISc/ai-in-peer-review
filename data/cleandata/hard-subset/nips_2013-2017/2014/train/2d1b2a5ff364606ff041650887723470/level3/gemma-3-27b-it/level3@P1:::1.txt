This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) into Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs) to improve blind source separation, specifically focusing on energy disaggregation. The core idea is to incorporate domain knowledge about expected total energy consumption for different appliances, addressing the inherent identifiability problem in BSS. The authors formulate this as a convex quadratic program (CQP) for approximate inference and demonstrate significant performance gains on both a toy dataset and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, outperforming both a standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art approach (AFAMAP).

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in BSS, including early work on AFHMMs [Ghahramani & Jordan, 2000] and recent advances in energy disaggregation [Hart, 1992; Zoha et al., 2014]. The paper appropriately situates itself within the context of existing techniques for incorporating prior knowledge into probabilistic models, referencing posterior regularization [Ganchev et al., 2009] and measurement-based learning [Liang et al., 2011]. The novelty lies in the specific type of constraint – SACs – and its effective integration into the AFHMM framework, enabled by the convex relaxation and efficient CQP formulation.  Related work on constraining HMM states, such as AFAMAP [Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012] and non-homogeneous HMMs [Zhong et al., 2013], is also well-acknowledged.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation and derivation of the CQP appear well-reasoned and presented. The connection to posterior regularization is a valuable theoretical grounding. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the underlying concepts and address the challenges of non-Markovian constraints effectively.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The results on both the toy dataset and, critically, the real-world energy disaggregation data are compelling. The substantial performance improvements over existing methods, confirmed by paired t-tests, demonstrate the practical benefit of SACs.
*   **Computational Efficiency:**  The authors highlight that the computational cost of the proposed method is comparable to AFAMAP, which is an important consideration for real-world applicability.
*   **Novelty:** The introduction of SACs as a general constraint for BSS is a novel contribution. The application to energy disaggregation is particularly relevant given the increasing importance of smart energy management.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the motivation, method, and results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Convexity Proof:** While the authors state that the resulting optimization problem is convex, a formal proof demonstrating this would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Complexity Analysis:** A theoretical analysis of the computational complexity (scaling with data length and number of hidden states) and a comparison of tractability to AFAMAP for larger datasets would be valuable. This would help to assess the practical limitations of the approach.
*   **Toy Dataset Considerations:** The toy dataset uses a very low noise variance. Clarification on whether this is representative of real-world scenarios or was chosen to simplify separation would be helpful, along with a discussion of signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) for the mains and aggregate appliance signals.
*   **Minor Notation:** There appears to be a minor notation issue on page 4 (line 202) with possible extra commas in subscripts.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in BSS and proposes a novel and effective solution.
*   The theoretical contributions are sound, and the empirical results are convincing.
*   The method has the potential for real-world impact in energy management.
*   The authors have addressed reviewer queries thoroughly in their response.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a formal convexity proof is a minor concern.
*   The absence of a complexity analysis limits the assessment of scalability.
*   While the reviewer lacks deep expertise in the HMM and energy application areas, the method's overall quality suggests a strong contribution despite this.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated contribution. The introduction of SACs represents a significant advance in the application of probabilistic models to blind source separation and energy disaggregation. While the minor weaknesses regarding the convexity proof and complexity analysis could be addressed, they do not detract substantially from the overall quality of the work.  The authors have satisfactorily addressed reviewer queries, indicating responsiveness and a strong understanding of their work. I recommend acceptance of this paper.
