Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles blind source separation for energy disaggregation by augmenting an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) with signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The key idea is to encode domain knowledge about the total amount of signal one expects from each source (e.g., an appliance’s daily energy) and to bias inference so that the per-chain aggregate ∑t xit is close to a pre-specified value µi0. Because such global constraints couple all time steps and are non-Markovian, the authors relax MAP inference to a convex quadratic program (CQP) by introducing auxiliary transition variables and box-relaxing the binary indicators. They show that the objective can also be derived from posterior regularization, and equivalently interpreted as imposing a prior over state sequences p(S) ∝ exp(−∑i λi(∑t µTi Sit − µi0)2). Empirically, on a toy example and on the HES low-frequency electricity dataset, AFHMM+SAC substantially improves normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) versus AFHMM and AFAMAP, with comparable runtime.

The work is well-situated relative to: AFHMM/AFAMAP constraints (e.g., “at most one chain changes state”); semi-Markov duration models; non-homogeneous HMMs; posterior regularization, generalized expectation, and measurement-based learning; and k-segment constraints. The distinctive contribution is to formalize and exploit aggregate constraints over long horizons, which are common in practice yet not captured by prior FHMM constraints focused on local transitions or durations.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The relaxation is carefully set up. With PSD matrices Λi and Vt, the quadratic terms enter with negative sign, yielding a concave objective under linear constraints; maximizing this gives a valid convex program. The posterior-regularization derivation is also correct.
- Empirical support: Results show consistent gains on realistic data. Reporting includes both NDE (shape fidelity) and SAE (aggregate accuracy), which matches the intended use. Oracle constraints (true totals) appropriately serve as an upper bound.
- Missing details: Please clarify how relaxed solutions are converted to discrete state sequences or to per-appliance power traces for evaluation (e.g., are fractional Sit used directly to form x̂it = µT i Sit? Any rounding or Viterbi back-projection?). Also discuss the relaxation gap (integrality conditions, if any) and its observed impact.
- Hyperparameters: λi are fixed to 1; a brief sensitivity analysis or cross-validation protocol would strengthen the claims and guide practitioners.
- Scalability: Using CVX is reasonable for a proof-of-concept but can limit scale. A tailored solver (e.g., block-coordinate ascent with projected Newton steps or ADMM) and complexity/runtimes as a function of T and ∑i Ki would make the approach more convincing for larger deployments.

Clarity
- The manuscript is hard to read due to heavy notation and extensive acronyms (some unusually long). Please:
  - Define only essential acronyms and avoid compounding (e.g., consider “AFHMM with aggregate constraints” instead of “AFHMM+SAC” throughout).
  - Add a small notation table and a schematic of variables (S, H) and constraints.
  - Move dense vectorization (Kronecker products, stacked vectors) to an appendix; keep the main text intuitive with per-time/per-chain forms first.
  - State early and explicitly that the aggregate constraint is imposed as an a priori preference over sequences (a prior or posterior-regularization penalty), which in turn shapes the posterior; the current phrasing can be misconstrued as a “posterior constraint.”
  - Provide a short algorithm box outlining inputs (µ0, λ), outputs, and solver steps.

Originality
- The particular class of constraints—global aggregates over long windows—and their embedding in an FHMM via a tractable convex relaxation appear novel for energy disaggregation and, more broadly, for FHMM-based BSS. The connection to posterior regularization is well-known but appropriate.

Significance
- The reported improvements (e.g., ≈45% lower NDE vs AFHMM and ≈29% vs AFAMAP in some settings; consistent gains with national total consumption priors) are meaningful for low-frequency NILM, where identifiability is challenging.
- The idea is general and could transfer to other BSS settings (audio, biology) where totals or budgets are known.
- Practical impact depends on how µ0 is obtained. Using national averages is promising; please discuss how to adapt µ0 per household (e.g., hierarchical priors, Bayesian treatment with uncertainty intervals) and how robust performance is when µ0 is biased.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable constraint that addresses identifiability.
- Sound convex relaxation and clear link to posterior regularization.
- Strong empirical gains on a realistic, challenging dataset with appropriate metrics.
- Comparable runtime to strong baselines on the reported scales.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Readability and notation issues substantially hinder accessibility.
- Limited analysis of relaxation tightness and the mapping from relaxed to discrete solutions.
- Sparse guidance on setting λ and constructing µ0 in practice; robustness beyond toy analysis is limited.
- Evaluation could be broadened (e.g., REDD; comparisons to duration/semi-Markov constraints on the same data).

Questions for the authors
- How are fractional Sit handled in producing x̂it and reported metrics? Any post-processing to yield discrete sequences?
- How sensitive are results to λi and to mis-specified µ0 on the real dataset? Could you report NDE/SAE vs λ on a subset?
- Can µ0 be learned jointly (e.g., with priors over appliance totals) rather than fixed?
- What are the largest T and ∑i Ki you have solved without CVX timing out? Any plans for a bespoke solver?

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the prior vs posterior role of the constraints (state it is an a priori constraint that regularizes the posterior).
- Reduce acronyms and provide a notation crib sheet; move heavy linear-algebraic notation to an appendix.
- Add a brief algorithm summary and a discussion of scalability and relaxation tightness.
- Include sensitivity to λ and to biased µ0 on the energy data; consider time-windowed aggregates (day/night) as you note.
- If space allows, compare against a duration-constrained/semi-Markov baseline on the same data.

Overall recommendation
- I lean toward weak accept. The idea is timely and useful, the optimization is principled, and the empirical gains on realistic data are convincing. With improved presentation and added analysis of the relaxation and hyperparameters, this would be a solid contribution.
