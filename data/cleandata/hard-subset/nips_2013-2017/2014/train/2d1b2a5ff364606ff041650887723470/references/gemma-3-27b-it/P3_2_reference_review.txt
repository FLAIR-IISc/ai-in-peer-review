## Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and well-motivated approach to improving blind source separation, specifically within the context of energy disaggregation. The introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) is a logical and effective way to incorporate domain knowledge and address the inherent identifiability issues in this problem. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and demonstrates significant performance improvements on a real-world dataset. While the core idea isn’t radically new in the sense of introducing entirely novel machine learning techniques, the application of SACs within the AFHMM framework, coupled with the efficient CQP relaxation, constitutes a valuable contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The paper tackles the problem of blind source separation (BSS), specifically energy disaggregation, where the goal is to identify the contributions of individual appliances to overall household energy consumption.  Traditional BSS methods struggle with identifiability.  The authors propose incorporating prior knowledge about the expected aggregate usage of each source (e.g., a toaster uses ~50Wh/day) through Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). These constraints are integrated into an AFHMM, a common probabilistic model for BSS, and efficiently solved via a convex quadratic program (CQP) relaxation. The authors demonstrate substantial improvements over baseline AFHMM and a state-of-the-art disaggregation approach on real-world energy data.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper appropriately situates itself within the existing literature on energy disaggregation (citing Hart [8], reviews [22, 24]), and BSS more broadly. It acknowledges prior work on constraining HMMs in this context (AFAMAP [12], non-homogeneous HMMs [21], semi-Markov models [11, 10]) and differentiates itself by focusing on *what* constraints to impose, rather than *how* to constrain. The authors also correctly position their work in relation to broader areas like posterior regularization [4] and measurement-based learning [13], highlighting the novelty of applying aggregate constraints specifically.  The connection to probabilistic databases [20] is acknowledged, with a clear explanation of why that work’s simpler graphical models don't directly apply.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the inherent challenges of energy disaggregation and the importance of addressing identifiability. The explanation of why a "franken-appliance" result is undesirable is particularly effective.
* **Sound Methodology:** The introduction of SACs is a logical extension of incorporating domain knowledge into probabilistic models. The CQP relaxation provides an efficient solution to the otherwise intractable inference problem.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM and 29% improvement over AFAMAP [12] is substantial and demonstrates the practical value of the proposed method.  The results on the HES dataset, particularly the comparison to a recent state-of-the-art method, are convincing.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper evaluates the method on both simulated and real-world data. The analysis of the impact of differing constraint values (µ0) in the toy data experiment is insightful.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors acknowledge and address the computational complexity of the CQP, noting its polynomial scaling.  The observation that the computational cost is comparable to AFAMAP and AFHMM is encouraging.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of Constraint Selection:** While the paper mentions obtaining µ0 from expert knowledge or surveys [23], it lacks a more detailed discussion of strategies for *automatically* learning or refining these constraint values. Cross-validation is mentioned briefly, but a more in-depth exploration of constraint selection methods would be beneficial.
* **Sensitivity to Constraint Accuracy:**  The toy data experiment demonstrates sensitivity to the accuracy of µ0.  While the authors acknowledge this, a more detailed analysis of robustness to noise or uncertainty in these values would be valuable. How does performance degrade with less accurate estimates of aggregate consumption?
* **Assumptions of AFHMM:** The reliance on the AFHMM framework itself introduces certain assumptions (factorization, Markov property) that may not hold in all real-world scenarios.  The paper doesn't deeply discuss the potential limitations stemming from these assumptions.
* **Generalizability Beyond Energy Disaggregation:** Although the authors argue for the general applicability of SACs, the evaluation is primarily focused on energy disaggregation. Demonstrating the effectiveness of SACs in other BSS domains would strengthen the claim of broader applicability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a practical and important problem (energy disaggregation).
* Presents a novel and effective approach (SACs in AFHMM).
* Achieves substantial performance improvements on a real-world dataset.
* Technically sound and well-written.
* Has the potential to impact the field of BSS and energy management.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  Relatively narrow focus on a single application domain.
*  Limited discussion of constraint selection and robustness.
*  Reliance on the assumptions inherent in the AFHMM framework.



**Recommendations:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors:

* **Expand the discussion of constraint selection:** Elaborate on methods for automatically learning or refining the µ0 values, potentially including cross-validation strategies or techniques for incorporating uncertainty.
* **Analyze robustness to constraint inaccuracies:** Include a more detailed analysis of the method's sensitivity to errors in the specified aggregate values.
* **Briefly address the limitations of the AFHMM framework:** Acknowledge the assumptions of the AFHMM and discuss potential scenarios where those assumptions might not hold.
* **Consider a brief discussion of potential applications beyond energy disaggregation:**  Even a short paragraph outlining possible applications in other BSS domains would strengthen the argument for broader significance.




**Confidence:** 8/10.  The paper is strong and well-executed, but the minor suggestions above would further enhance its impact and generalizability.
