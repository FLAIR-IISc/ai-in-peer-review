### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), specifically in the application of energy disaggregation, where a single time series of aggregate household electricity consumption must be separated into the consumption signals of individual appliances. The core challenge in this domain is the inherent unidentifiability of the sources, which makes it difficult to recover meaningful, interpretable component signals. The authors propose to address this by incorporating prior domain knowledge into the modeling process.

The main contribution is the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) within the framework of an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a common model for this task. A SAC encourages the total aggregate value of each source signal over a given time period (e.g., total daily energy consumption of an appliance) to be close to a known, expected value. This form of prior knowledge is often available from external sources, such as national surveys on appliance usage.

Since the SACs are non-Markovian (each constraint depends on the entire state sequence of a source), standard inference methods like the Viterbi algorithm are not applicable. To perform MAP inference, the authors formulate the problem as an integer quadratic program (IQP) and then relax the integer constraints to arrive at a convex quadratic program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The paper also demonstrates that this formulation is equivalent to a form of posterior regularization.

The proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on a real-world energy dataset and compared against a standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP [12]. The results show that AFHMM+SAC significantly reduces disaggregation error compared to both baselines.

### Quality

The paper is technically sound. The proposed method of adding a quadratic penalty term to the AFHMM's log-posterior objective function to enforce the aggregate constraint is well-motivated and clearly formulated in Equation (5). The subsequent transformation of this non-Markovian inference problem into a CQP is a valid and standard approach for making such problems tractable. The derivation is clear and appears correct. The link established to the posterior regularization framework [4] provides additional theoretical grounding for the approach.

The experimental evaluation is thorough. The use of a toy dataset effectively demonstrates the model's robustness to misspecification in the constraint values (`µi0`). The evaluation on a large, real-world dataset (HES) is comprehensive, testing the model in two scenarios: one with a synthesized aggregate signal and another with a more realistic measured mains signal. The choice of comparison methods (AFHMM and AFAMAP) is appropriate, as they represent the standard baseline and a strong recent competitor, respectively. The use of both Normalized Disaggregation Error (NDE) and Signal Aggregate Error (SAE) provides a multi-faceted view of performance. The claims of significant performance improvement are well-supported by the results presented and validated with statistical significance tests.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written and well-organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem of unidentifiability in BSS and introduces the core idea of using aggregate constraints. The problem setting and models (AFHMM and the proposed AFHMM+SAC) are described with sufficient mathematical detail. The derivation of the CQP is presented systematically. The experimental setup and results are easy to understand. The figures and tables are informative and support the paper's claims effectively. The authors provide enough detail for an expert to understand the contribution and potentially reproduce the work.

### Originality

The paper's primary contribution—the use of signal aggregate constraints for BSS in AFHMMs—is novel. While the idea of constraining HMMs is not new (e.g., [12, 19, 21]), the specific type of constraint proposed here is. Previous work like AFAMAP [12] constrains the local transition dynamics (at most one HMM can change state at a time), whereas this work introduces a global constraint on the entire sequence for each source. This is an intuitive and practical form of prior knowledge that has not been exploited in this manner before in this context. The formulation as a CQP builds upon existing techniques [12], but its application to this new model with SACs is a novel contribution. The related work section is adequate and correctly positions the paper with respect to prior art in energy disaggregation and constrained HMMs.

### Significance

The results presented are significant and likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners in energy disaggregation and potentially other BSS domains. The demonstrated performance improvement is substantial: on the most realistic task (disaggregation of measured mains data), the proposed method achieves a 29% reduction in NDE compared to the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model. This is a meaningful advance in a challenging real-world problem. The general idea of incorporating priors on signal aggregates is broadly applicable to other BSS problems where such domain knowledge exists (e.g., total expression level of a gene over an experiment, or total speaking time of a person in an audio recording). The fact that this improvement is achieved with comparable computational inference time to existing methods further increases its practical appeal.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **Novelty:** Proposes a new, intuitive, and effective type of constraint (SAC) for the AFHMM framework to address the key problem of source unidentifiability.
2.  **Significance:** Demonstrates substantial and statistically significant performance improvements over strong baselines, including a recent state-of-the-art method, on a challenging real-world application.
3.  **Technical Soundness:** The method is well-grounded, with a correct and practical formulation for inference via convex relaxation. The connection to posterior regularization adds theoretical weight.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and easy to follow.

### Arguments against Acceptance:

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper sets the constraint strength parameter `λi = 1` for all experiments without justification or analysis of the model's sensitivity to this choice. While the robustness to the constraint *value* is shown, the robustness to its *weight* is an important practical consideration that is not addressed.
2.  **Limited Scope of Constraints:** The experiments only use a single aggregate constraint over the entire time series. The paper mentions the possibility of using multiple constraints over smaller time windows (e.g., to model diurnal patterns), which seems crucial for many applications, but this is not explored experimentally.
3.  **Supervised Setting:** The model parameters are learned from labeled training data. While the authors mention extending the work to an unsupervised setting as future work, the current reliance on a fully supervised setup may limit its applicability in scenarios where labeled data for individual sources is unavailable.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novel contribution and significant empirical results, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. The paper presents a complete and valuable contribution to the field.
