- Paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation, integrates them into Additive Factorial HMMs, relaxes to a convex quadratic program, and shows strong gains on real energy-disaggregation data.  
- Clear motivation: addresses identifiability in BSS and the practical problem of "franken-appliances."  
- Strong technical contribution: transforms non‑Markovian constraints into a solvable convex quadratic program and links SACs to posterior regularization.  
- Compelling empirical results: 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error vs AFHMM and 29% improvement over AFAMAP on HES data, with paired t‑tests for significance.  
- Practical relevance: method is computationally efficient and suitable for deployment in energy‑disaggregation applications.  
- Theoretical limitation: no analysis of the relaxation quality or relationship between the relaxed solution and the true MAP solution.  
- Parameter sensitivity: lacks discussion or principled method for setting λi/δi and sensitivity analysis.  
- Comparative evaluation gap: missing comparisons to more recent state‑of‑the‑art methods and omission of datasets like REDD limits claims.  
- Practical feasibility concern: reliance on "true total consumption" as an oracle is unrealistic; need quantification of required prior knowledge and discussion of NTC practicality.  
- Limited generality discussion: paper focuses on energy disaggregation and should discuss applicability and limits of SACs for other BSS tasks (e.g., audio).  
- Relation to prior work: correctly cites relevant literature but should better distinguish SACs from other constraint‑based approaches (e.g., k‑segment constraints).  
- Overall assessment: strong, well‑motivated, technically sound paper with novel contribution and impressive empirical validation.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Suggested camera‑ready improvements: add sensitivity analysis for λi/δi, compare to more recent SOTA on standard datasets, discuss real‑world feasibility of aggregate constraints, and broaden discussion of SAC generalization to other BSS problems.
