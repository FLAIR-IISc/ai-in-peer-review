### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), specifically in the context of energy disaggregation, where an aggregate time series (total household electricity usage) must be decomposed into its constituent source signals (individual appliances). The core challenge is the inherent non-identifiability of the problem. The authors propose to mitigate this by incorporating domain knowledge in the form of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) into a standard Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM). A SAC is a prior belief that the sum of a source signal over a given time period should be close to a known value (e.g., the typical daily energy consumption of a toaster).

To incorporate these non-Markovian constraints, the authors add a quadratic penalty term to the MAP inference objective of the AFHMM. As exact inference in this new model (AFHMM+SAC) is intractable, they formulate the problem as an integer quadratic program and then solve its convex relaxation as a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP). The authors also show a connection between their penalized objective and the posterior regularization framework. The method is evaluated on a real-world energy dataset (HES), demonstrating significant improvements in disaggregation accuracy over a standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP algorithm.

### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Constraint:** The core idea of using signal aggregate constraints is both simple and powerful. It introduces a form of domain knowledge that is often readily available (e.g., from national surveys or manufacturer specifications) and directly targets the identifiability problem by encouraging source signals to have realistic total energy consumption. This is a different and complementary type of constraint compared to previous work that has focused on temporal structure, such as state-transition sparsity (AFAMAP) or duration modeling (semi-Markov models). The potential impact is high, as this type of constraint could be applied to other BSS problems beyond energy disaggregation.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is a key strength of the paper. The authors show a substantial reduction in disaggregation error (a 45% decrease in NDE vs. AFHMM and 29% vs. AFAMAP on real mains data). The comparison against AFAMAP, a strong baseline from AISTATS, demonstrates a clear advance in the state of the art. The use of both synthetic aggregate data and real-world mains data (with its inherent noise and unmodeled sources) provides a robust validation of the method's effectiveness. The inclusion of an "oracle" experiment using the True Total Consumption (TTC) is also insightful, as it illustrates the upper bound on performance and highlights the potential of the proposed constraint.

3.  **Sound Methodological Approach:** The technical approach is well-founded. Formulating the constrained inference problem as a penalized MAP objective is a standard and principled technique. The relaxation to a Convex Quadratic Program is a pragmatic and computationally efficient solution to an otherwise intractable inference problem. The authors' connection of their formulation to the posterior regularization framework provides additional theoretical grounding and positions the work within a broader context of constrained probabilistic modeling.

### Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection)

1.  **Lack of Hyperparameter Tuning and Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper's most significant weakness is its handling of the regularization parameter λi. The authors state, "we simply set λi = 1 for all the experiments." This is not a principled approach. The parameter λi controls the trade-off between fitting the observed aggregate data and satisfying the prior constraint. Its optimal value is likely data-dependent and crucial for performance. By fixing it to a single arbitrary value, the authors may be under- or over-stating the performance of their model. A proper evaluation would involve tuning this hyperparameter on a validation set. This omission makes the quantitative comparison to other methods less convincing, as it is unclear if the baselines were also optimally tuned. It also undermines the claim of similar computational cost, as AFHMM+SAC would require an additional, potentially expensive, hyperparameter search in practice.

2.  **Missing Details on the CQP Relaxation:** The paper solves a relaxed version of the MAP problem where the state indicator variables `Sitk` can take values in [0, 1]. However, it is not explained how the final discrete state sequence (required for evaluation) is recovered from these fractional values. Are the results rounded? Is the most likely state chosen at each timestep? This is a critical detail for reproducibility and for understanding the quality of the relaxation. If the optimal `Sitk` values are often far from 0 or 1, the CQP solution might be a poor approximation of the original integer problem, and this should be discussed.

3.  **Limited Discussion on Acquiring Constraint Priors (µi0):** While the idea of using national averages (NTC) for µi0 is a good starting point, the paper could benefit from a deeper discussion of the practical challenges. Household consumption can vary significantly from national averages. The toy data experiment (Fig 1) clearly shows that performance degrades as the provided µi0 deviates from the truth. A discussion on the robustness of the method to this mismatch in real-world scenarios, or potential ways to adapt these priors (e.g., using household-specific metadata), would strengthen the paper's practical implications.

4.  **Insufficient Contextualization with Other Constraint Types:** The related work section mentions alternative constraints like those in semi-Markov models (for state duration) and k-segment constraints. However, the paper does not discuss the conceptual relationship or trade-offs between SACs and these other approaches. For instance, duration modeling imposes local temporal consistency, whereas SACs impose a global constraint over the entire time series. Are these complementary? When might one be more suitable than the other? A more nuanced discussion would better position the contribution within the landscape of constrained HMMs.

### Conclusion

This paper introduces a novel and highly effective method for improving energy disaggregation by incorporating signal aggregate constraints into an AFHMM. The core idea is intuitive, the methodological approach is sound, and the empirical results demonstrate a significant improvement over existing state-of-the-art methods.

However, the work is let down by a major weakness in the experimental protocol, specifically the lack of hyperparameter tuning for the constraint penalty λ. This makes the reported performance gains less reliable and the comparison to baselines potentially unfair. Additionally, the paper omits crucial details about the CQP relaxation and could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the practicalities of setting the prior µi0 and the relationship between SACs and other constraint types.

Despite these weaknesses, the strength of the core idea and the promising results suggest this is a valuable contribution. I would lean towards acceptance, but I strongly recommend that the authors address the issue of hyperparameter tuning in their final version, as it is essential for the scientific validity of their claims.
