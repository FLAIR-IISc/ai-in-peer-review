

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a novel approach to address the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS), particularly in the context of energy disaggregation. The authors propose incorporating prior knowledge about expected aggregate values of source signals (e.g., total daily energy consumption of specific appliances) into additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs). SACs are incorporated as constraints on the sum of source signals over time, transforming the problem into a convex quadratic program that can be efficiently solved.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) the novel SAC concept for BSS, (2) a convex optimization approach to handle these constraints, and (3) compelling experimental results showing significant improvements in energy disaggregation performance (45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to baseline AFHMM, and 29% improvement over state-of-the-art AFAMAP).

The motivation for SACs is strong and well-justified. The paper correctly identifies that the identifiability problem is particularly problematic in applications like energy disaggregation, where users need to interpret the resulting source signals. The authors successfully demonstrate that SACs provide a principled way to incorporate domain knowledge about expected aggregate values without making strong assumptions about the temporal structure of the signals.

The paper's main strength is its novel conceptual contribution. SACs represent a fresh perspective on how to incorporate domain knowledge into BSS models, and the authors convincingly demonstrate their practical utility in a real-world application. The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) is particularly valuable as it places the approach within a well-established framework, strengthening the theoretical foundation.

The experimental results are compelling, with the AFHMM+SAC approach showing substantial improvements over both the baseline AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP method on real-world energy disaggregation data. The authors carefully evaluate performance using both normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE), which is particularly relevant for the energy disaggregation application.

However, the paper has a few limitations. The theoretical analysis of the relaxed problem is somewhat limited - while the authors show that the relaxed problem can be efficiently solved as a convex quadratic program, they don't provide guarantees about how close the solution is to the optimal solution of the original non-relaxed problem. The paper also doesn't thoroughly explore the sensitivity of the method to errors in the specified aggregate values (µi0), which is a critical concern in practice.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution to evaluation. The related work section adequately covers relevant literature in energy disaggregation and BSS.

In terms of significance, this work represents a meaningful advance in addressing a fundamental challenge in BSS. The energy disaggregation application is timely and important, and the authors demonstrate that SACs provide a practical solution with measurable improvements over existing methods. While the approach is currently demonstrated only on energy disaggregation, the authors correctly argue that SACs could be applied to other BSS problems where aggregate knowledge is available.

As a reviewer who values novelty and bold approaches, I find the SAC concept particularly compelling. The paper successfully introduces a new way to think about incorporating domain knowledge into BSS models, which is a significant conceptual contribution. While the methodological details are not perfect (the relaxed solution is not guaranteed to be optimal), the practical improvements demonstrated in the experiments are substantial enough to justify the novelty of the approach.

The paper does not represent an incremental improvement over existing work, but rather offers a genuinely new direction for addressing the identifiability problem in BSS. The results are well-supported by experiments, and the approach is generalizable to other domains.

**Recommendation**: Accept

**Strengths**:
- Novel concept (SACs) that addresses a fundamental problem in BSS
- Strong motivation and clear application to energy disaggregation
- Significant performance improvements demonstrated in real-world experiments
- Clear connection to posterior regularization framework
- Well-written and logically organized

**Weaknesses**:
- Limited theoretical analysis of the relaxed solution's optimality
- Insufficient discussion of sensitivity to errors in specified aggregate values
- Experiments limited to energy disaggregation (though the authors argue for generalizability)

Overall, this paper makes a compelling contribution by introducing Signal Aggregate Constraints as a novel approach to a persistent challenge in blind source separation. The conceptual novelty is strong, and the experimental results demonstrate meaningful improvements over state-of-the-art methods. While the theoretical analysis could be strengthened, the practical significance and novelty of the approach outweigh these limitations for a NIPS submission.
