

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper proposes "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) for blind source separation problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation. The authors introduce SACs to address the identifiability problem in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) by constraining the total aggregate value of each source signal (e.g., total energy consumed by an appliance) to be close to expected values. The constrained optimization problem is transformed into a convex quadratic program (CQP) for efficient computation. The authors claim significant performance improvements (45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error) over the baseline AFHMM when using SACs with true aggregate values, and 33% improvement over AFAMAP (a state-of-the-art approach) when using estimated national consumption values.

The paper's strengths include a clear problem statement, well-organized technical presentation, and solid empirical results on energy disaggregation data. The connection to posterior regularization is appropriately noted, and the computational efficiency claim (similar to AFHMM and AFAMAP) is supported by experimental timing data. The application domain is well-chosen, as energy disaggregation is a well-established problem with clear practical importance.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses that undermine its contribution to the field. The most critical issue is the fundamental limitation of SACs requiring prior knowledge of aggregate values (µi0) that would typically be unavailable in practice. The paper's strongest results (45% error reduction) rely on using the "true total consumption" (TTC) as the constraint value, which is an oracle that would never be available to practitioners. This is a major flaw in the evaluation design. The more realistic results using "national total consumption" (NTC) show only a 33% improvement over AFAMAP, but the paper fails to adequately discuss how to obtain NTC values for new applications or how sensitive the results are to inaccuracies in these estimates.

The theoretical justification for the convex relaxation is weak. The paper states that the relaxed problem is a CQP, but provides no analysis of how the relaxation affects the solution quality or whether the relaxation is tight. The authors claim that the SACs "help to address the identifiability problem," but don't demonstrate how this constraint specifically resolves the underlying identifiability issue, which remains fundamentally unresolved.

The paper's evaluation is limited to a single application domain (energy disaggregation) without any broader testing or discussion of generalizability. This severely limits the paper's significance, as the authors claim SACs are "general across a wide variety of domains" but provide no evidence for this claim. The paper fails to compare SACs with other constraint-based approaches that might address similar problems more practically.

The paper's connection to prior work is somewhat superficial. While it references posterior regularization and other constraint-based methods, it doesn't clearly differentiate SACs from existing approaches. For instance, AFAMAP [12] already introduces state constraints, and the paper doesn't adequately explain how SACs differ in meaningful ways or why SACs would be preferable in practice.

The paper also lacks sufficient discussion of the sensitivity to the regularization parameter λi. The authors simply state "we set λi = 1 for all experiments" without justification or analysis of how results vary with this parameter. This is a significant omission for a method that relies on this tuning parameter.

The significance claim is overstated. The paper claims "a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM," but this result depends on an oracle constraint value that would never be available. The more realistic improvement (33% over AFAMAP) is impressive but not sufficiently contextualized against the practical limitations of the approach.

The paper would benefit from addressing these limitations. A more rigorous evaluation of SACs' performance when using approximate aggregate values (rather than oracle values) would be valuable. The authors should also explore how to obtain aggregate values in practice (e.g., through surveys, expert knowledge, or other indirect methods), and demonstrate the method's robustness to inaccuracies in these estimates.

As a reviewer who values methodological soundness and rigorous validation, I am concerned that this paper presents an approach that, while technically interesting, has significant practical limitations that aren't adequately addressed. The paper's claims of significant improvement are based on an unrealistic assumption (availability of true aggregate values), and the theoretical justification is insufficient. While the paper is well-written and the energy disaggregation results are promising, the approach appears to be a domain-specific application of constraint-based methods rather than a fundamental methodological advance with broad applicability.

The paper does not meet the high standards required for NIPS publication. It lacks sufficient theoretical justification for the proposed method, provides inadequate validation against realistic scenarios, and overstates the significance of its results. The most compelling evidence for the method's value (using true aggregate values) is not applicable to real-world scenarios, while the more realistic evidence (using estimated aggregate values) shows only modest improvements that could potentially be achieved through other means.

I recommend rejection due to insufficient evidence of general applicability and practical value, as well as the paper's failure to adequately address the limitations of the proposed approach. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to include a thorough evaluation of the method's performance with realistic aggregate value estimates, provide theoretical justification for the relaxation, and demonstrate broader applicability beyond energy disaggregation.
