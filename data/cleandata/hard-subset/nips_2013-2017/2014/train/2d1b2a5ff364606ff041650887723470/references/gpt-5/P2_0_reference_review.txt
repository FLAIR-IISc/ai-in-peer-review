Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles blind source separation in time series, focusing on energy disaggregation. It augments the additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) with signal aggregate constraints (SACs): soft penalties that encourage each latent source’s total energy over a time window to match a prior target. This directly addresses identifiability by discouraging “franken-appliance” solutions. The authors derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) for a relaxed MAP problem by introducing joint transition variables and relaxing binary constraints, and they show the equivalence to posterior regularization (PR), where the constraints correspond to expected quadratic penalties on the aggregate. Experiments on a toy dataset and on the UK HES dataset (ultra-low frequency) compare AFHMM+SAC against AFHMM and AFAMAP [Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12], reporting significant gains in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE), with similar runtimes.

The approach is related to: (i) PR/GE/measurement-based learning, which constrain posteriors via expectation penalties; (ii) AFAMAP and other NILM methods that restrict state changes or durations (semi-Markov); and (iii) aggregate constraints studied in other probabilistic settings. The main novelty is specifying aggregate constraints on sums of emissions and deriving a tractable convex relaxation for AFHMM.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is reasonable. Given emission means per state, the log posterior is quadratic in the relaxed state indicators. With Λi and Vt PSD, maximizing a sum of linear terms minus PSD quadratic forms is a concave maximization over a convex polytope, hence a convex program after relaxation. The lifted transition variables H enforce local consistency. The link to PR is correct: adding quadratic penalties to the log posterior corresponds to a KL projection onto a constraint set, yielding the same form.
- Inference/solution: The relaxation yields fractional states. For disaggregation this is acceptable because the reconstructed signal is a convex combination of per-state means; however, it weakens the interpretability as discrete states and may lose duration structure. The paper does not describe a rounding procedure or its effect; this is a limitation if discrete state sequences are desired.
- Empirical evidence: Results show consistent improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP on both synthetic and real data, with statistical tests on per-house errors. The use of an oracle target total (TTC) helps quantify an upper bound on gains from perfect aggregates; the realistic case uses national total consumption (NTC) derived from training. The toy study confirms sensitivity: performance degrades as target totals deviate from the truth. However, there is limited sensitivity analysis on real data (e.g., how performance varies as NTC is mis-specified or λ varies).
- Computational claims: While the convexity argument is solid, scalability is not thoroughly analyzed. Variable counts scale as O(T ΣKi + T ΣKi^2) due to H, which can be large; yet the paper reports similar wall-clock time to AFHMM/AFAMAP via CVX. Concrete problem sizes, solver tolerances, and runtimes are only briefly summarized in tables; more detail would strengthen the claim.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Section 5’s notation is heavy and could be streamlined; an explicit convexity statement (maximize a concave quadratic over a polytope) would help. Practical details are sparse: how λi are chosen (all set to 1), solver settings, and whether rounding is used. References have formatting issues and missing years; some equations have minor typographical artifacts. Despite this, an expert reader could reproduce the core idea.

Originality
- The idea of imposing expectations/aggregates as posterior constraints is known (PR/GE/measurement-based learning). The novelty lies in choosing aggregate-of-emissions constraints for AFHMM and showing they induce a concave quadratic objective amenable to off-the-shelf convex optimization. Compared to AFAMAP’s one-change constraint or duration models, this is a different dimension of prior knowledge and, to my knowledge, not previously instantiated in AFHMMs with a convex relaxation. Thus, originality is moderate: a specific, useful instantiation and convex formulation rather than a new learning principle.

Significance
- The constraint is intuitive, widely available in practice (e.g., appliance energy budgets), and likely useful beyond NILM (speech, biology). The reported gains on challenging low-frequency HES data are meaningful (e.g., ~29–45% reductions in error depending on baseline). The method is simple to adopt when per-state means are available. That said, reliance on good target totals and a global λ attenuates robustness; scalability for long horizons and many sources is uncertain; and comparisons are limited to AFHMM/AFAMAP (no recent NILM baselines like dictionary learning or deep models). Overall, significance is solid for the AFHMM/NILM subcommunity, with broader potential.

Pros
- Clear, principled way to inject common domain knowledge (aggregate totals) into AFHMMs.
- Convex relaxation with a straightforward implementation; link to posterior regularization well-explained.
- Consistent empirical improvements on real, low-frequency data; statistical significance reported.
- Generalizable idea; extension to multiple time-window constraints is natural.

Cons
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to mis-specified aggregates and to λ on real data; λ fixed to 1.
- No rounding strategy; interpretability as discrete sequences is reduced.
- Scalability and solver details are under-specified; reliance on CVX may limit practicality.
- Comparisons limited to AFHMM/AFAMAP; missing broader NILM baselines.
- Presentation issues (notation density, reference formatting).

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Provide a simple rounding heuristic and evaluate its effect on NDE/SAE.
- Add sensitivity analyses for λ and NTC mis-specification on real data; report per-appliance performance.
- Discuss and empirically characterize scalability (T, I, Ki), and provide solver details; consider specialized solvers beyond CVX.
- Compare against at least one non-HMM NILM baseline.
- Clarify whether any leakage occurs in computing NTC; ensure test days are excluded.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a simple, general, and empirically effective way to inject aggregate priors into AFHMMs via a convex relaxation, with clear improvements on realistic data. While the conceptual novelty is incremental relative to PR, the instantiation is valuable and likely to influence practice in NILM and related BSS settings. Addressing the noted clarity and evaluation gaps would strengthen the contribution.
