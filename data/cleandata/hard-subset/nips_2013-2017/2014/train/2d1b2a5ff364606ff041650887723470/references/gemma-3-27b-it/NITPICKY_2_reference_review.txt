This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) within an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) framework, aimed at improving blind source separation, specifically in the context of energy disaggregation. The core idea – incorporating prior knowledge about expected aggregate signal values – is intuitive and potentially valuable. The authors formulate the problem as a convex quadratic program (CQP) for efficient computation and demonstrate improvements on both simulated and real-world energy disaggregation data.

However, while the concept is sound and the results promising, the paper suffers from a surprising number of minor, yet persistent, issues that collectively detract from its overall impact. My assessment is heavily critical, leaning towards rejection, despite acknowledging the potential of the core idea.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The incorporation of SACs into the AFHMM is a novel approach to address the identifiability problem in BSS, particularly relevant to energy disaggregation.
*   **Practicality:** The formulation as a CQP is a significant strength, enabling efficient computation compared to potentially intractable MAP inference.
*   **Empirical Results:** The demonstrated 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error and 29% improvement over a state-of-the-art approach are substantial and provide evidence of the method's effectiveness.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The introduction effectively articulates the challenges of energy disaggregation and the importance of addressing identifiability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigor in Derivation:** The transition from equation (4) to (5) feels somewhat ad-hoc. While the connection to posterior regularization is mentioned, the justification for replacing the constraint with a penalized objective isn’t fully elaborated. Why is solving the penalized problem equivalent to the constrained one in this context?
*   **Overly Concise Mathematical Notation:** While generally readable, the notation in Section 5 is dense and requires careful parsing. Defining Hit and representing matrices with long-form notation (e.g., µ∗i) hinders clarity. A more streamlined presentation would improve understanding.
*   **Unclear Choice of λ:** The paper states λi are complexity parameters with a one-to-one correspondence with δi, but offers no guidance on *how* to choose these values. Setting λi=1 arbitrarily feels insufficient, and a more principled approach (e.g., cross-validation) should be explored.
*   **Inconsistent Definition of "Oracle"**: The "TTC" case in section 7.2.1 is referred to as oracle. However, in a real-world scenario, TTC would *not* be known. Using TTC as a benchmark feels misleading. The advantage gained from TTC doesn't demonstrate real-world applicability.
*   **Limited Discussion of Generalization:** The paper focuses heavily on energy disaggregation. While this provides a concrete application, a more thorough discussion of the generalizability of SACs to other BSS problems would strengthen the contribution.
*   **Minor Typographical and Grammatical Errors:** Numerous small errors throughout the paper (e.g., "eP" instead of "Ep" in equation (7), inconsistent use of subscripts and superscripts) detract from the paper’s professionalism.
*   **Redundancy:** The presentation of the CQP formulation (Section 5) feels unnecessarily detailed and repetitive. Several steps could be condensed without losing essential information.
* **Missing Citations**: The paper references prior work [4] on posterior regularization but does not adequately discuss the challenges and limitations of applying this approach to models with non-Markovian constraints.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Novel and potentially impactful approach to BSS.
*   Promising empirical results on a real-world dataset.
*   Efficient computational formulation (CQP).

**Con:**

*   Lack of rigor in derivations and justifications.
*   Ambiguous parameter setting (λ).
*   Misleading "oracle" benchmark.
*   Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies.
*   Limited discussion of generalization.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising idea, but its execution falls short of NIPS standards. The technical details require more careful treatment, the presentation needs polishing, and the justification for certain design choices is lacking. While the empirical results are encouraging, they are overshadowed by the numerous imperfections. I recommend **rejection** at this time. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly focusing on improving the mathematical rigor, clarifying the parameter selection process, and providing a more balanced and nuanced evaluation of the method's performance. A more thorough exploration of the generalizability of SACs to other BSS problems would also significantly strengthen the paper.
