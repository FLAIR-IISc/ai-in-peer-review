

# Review: Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation

## Summary
This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) to address the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS) for additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs). SACs constrain the aggregate values of source signals (e.g., total energy consumption) to be close to known values, leveraging domain knowledge. The authors incorporate SACs into AFHMMs by transforming the constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP) for efficient computation. The approach is evaluated on energy disaggregation data, showing significant improvements over baseline AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP method. The paper relates SACs to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2010), demonstrating that their objective function can be derived from this framework.

## Strengths
**Technical Soundness (Quality):** The paper presents a well-motivated technical contribution with a clear mathematical formulation. The derivation of the CQP relaxation in Section 5 is sound, and the authors carefully address the non-Markovian nature of SACs. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with both synthetic data (Section 7.1) and real-world energy disaggregation data (Section 7.2). The results show statistically significant improvements: AFHMM+SAC outperforms AFHMM by 45% in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and outperforms AFAMAP by 29% in NDE on real-world data. The computational complexity analysis shows that AFHMM+SAC scales similarly to baseline methods, which is important for practical adoption.

**Originality:** The paper identifies a novel class of constraints (SACs) that are general across domains, have practical domain knowledge available, and enable efficient computation. While related to posterior regularization, the specific application to aggregate constraints for BSS represents a distinct contribution that addresses the previously unexplored question of "what types of domain knowledge we should include" in BSS. The transformation to CQP for SACs is technically novel.

**Significance:** The energy disaggregation application is highly relevant, with the paper addressing a major practical limitation (identifiability) that prevents real-world adoption of BSS methods. The improvements shown (33% NDE reduction over AFAMAP) are substantial for a practical application. The method is generalizable to other BSS problems beyond energy disaggregation, and the paper demonstrates how domain knowledge can be systematically incorporated into probabilistic models.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical formulations are presented systematically, with appropriate notation and explanations. The experimental setup is well-described, and the results are presented effectively using tables and figures. The relation to posterior regularization (Section 6) provides a useful theoretical connection to existing work.

## Weaknesses
**Technical Limitations:** The paper acknowledges that the relaxed solution isn't guaranteed optimal due to the identifiability problem, but doesn't thoroughly discuss the potential impact of this relaxation on the final results. The choice of λi = 1 for all experiments (Section 7.1) without sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter tuning is a notable simplification that could affect reproducibility.

**Scope Limitations:** The paper primarily evaluates SACs on energy disaggregation, without demonstrating the approach on other BSS problems to establish broader applicability. While the authors mention generalizability, the lack of additional domain examples limits the evidence for this claim.

**Incomplete Exploration of Constraints:** The paper mentions that SACs could be generalized to multiple time periods (Section 4.2), but doesn't explore this extension in experiments. This represents a missed opportunity to demonstrate the full potential of the proposed constraint framework.

## Recommendation
**Pro (Accept):** The paper presents a novel and well-executed solution to a significant problem in BSS. The technical contribution (SACs and CQP transformation) is sound and well-justified. The experimental results are strong, with substantial improvements over baselines. The work advances the state of the art in energy disaggregation and provides a general framework for incorporating domain knowledge into BSS.

**Con (Reject):** The paper doesn't sufficiently explore the limitations of the relaxation approach or the sensitivity to hyperparameters. The scope is limited to energy disaggregation without broader validation.

The paper is technically sound, novel, and significant for its application domain. The weaknesses are important but do not undermine the core contribution. The work represents a meaningful advancement in the application of domain knowledge to BSS problems, with clear practical implications for energy disaggregation. The method is generalizable to other domains, and the theoretical connection to posterior regularization strengthens the contribution.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field with both theoretical and practical significance, addressing a real limitation in BSS applications. The experimental results are compelling and the method is computationally efficient. The minor weaknesses in scope and hyperparameter analysis do not substantially detract from the overall quality and novelty of the contribution.
