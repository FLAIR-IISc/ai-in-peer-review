Summary
The paper proposes Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs), motivated by applications such as energy disaggregation (NILM). SACs encode prior knowledge about total usage per source (e.g., appliance daily energy), by penalizing the squared deviation between inferred aggregate signal and a target µi0. The AFHMM+SAC MAP objective is non-Markovian; the authors introduce auxiliary pairwise variables Hit and relax integrality to obtain a convex quadratic program (CQP) that they solve with CVX. They connect the penalized objective to posterior regularization. Empirically, on synthetic data and on the HES dataset, AFHMM+SAC improves normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) over AFHMM and AFAMAP, with similar runtimes reported.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on AFHMMs [Ghahramani & Jordan 1997] and AFAMAP [Kolter & Jaakkola 2012], which impose structural constraints like “at most one chain changes.”
- Positions SACs alongside posterior regularization [Ganchev et al. 2010], measurement-based learning [Liang et al. 2009], and GE constraints [Mann & McCallum 2008]; the derivation in Section 6 is essentially a direct application of posterior regularization to quadratic aggregate features.
- Semi-Markov constraints for NILM [Johnson & Willsky 2013; Kim et al. 2011] and k-segment constraints [Titsias et al. 2013] are cited but not compared experimentally.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling motivation: domain-appropriate aggregate priors are natural and useful for NILM.
- Solid convexification: with Λi and Vt PSD, maximizing a concave quadratic plus linear terms over the relaxed local polytope is a convex problem. The use of auxiliary Hit variables is standard and appropriate.
- The posterior regularization connection is correct and helps generalize the idea.
- Practical impact suggested by results: consistent improvements in NDE/SAE over AFHMM and AFAMAP on multiple houses, with realistic mains data.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/methodological
- Hyperparameters: λi are fixed to 1 “for all experiments,” with no tuning or sensitivity analysis; this is particularly problematic since SAC strength strongly affects identifiability and performance.
- Aggregate targets: using TTC (oracle) is fine as an upper bound, but using NTC (averages across households) risks domain shift; there is no ablation on noise/mismatch in µ0 beyond a toy experiment, and no analysis per-appliance sensitivity (appliances vary widely).
- Parameter learning: µi via k-means and fixed 3-state HMMs for all appliances are simplistic; there is no justification, model selection, or sensitivity to Ki. Transitions and σt are not described in enough detail (fixed? learned? per-appliance?).
- Relaxation gap and decoding: After solving the relaxed CQP, do you round S/Hit or use fractional S to form x̂ = µ⊤S? The paper does not specify, nor analyze integrality of solutions, nor the effect on metrics.
- Complexity and scalability: The paper asserts polynomial time and “similar runtime” to AFHMM/AFAMAP, but provides no concrete complexity in terms of T and ∑Ki, no solver tolerances, and relies on a generic interior-point solver (CVX) whose worst-case scaling can be cubic in the number of variables. Memory/runtime implications for long horizons are not addressed.
- Constraints over sub-intervals are claimed “straightforward,” but no formulation is provided; this matters because interval SACs could explode the number of quadratic terms.
- Baselines: Omits comparisons to duration/semi-Markov NILM methods [Johnson & Willsky; Kim et al.] and other strong NILM baselines; given the constraint-based nature of SACs, a comparison against duration models or k-segment constraints would be informative. Also no comparison to an AFHMM with L2 prior on total energy without the auxiliary variables, to isolate the effect of convexification and relaxation.

Empirical/reproducibility
- Evaluation clarity: Tables and figures are referenced but not included here; details such as the list of appliances, number of states per appliance, training/testing splits, σt, preprocessing, solver settings, stopping criteria, and how HES sampling rates were handled are missing or too brief. Without these, reproducibility is limited.
- Statistical testing: Paired t-tests are reported, but it’s unclear what the sample size is (house-days?) and whether independence assumptions hold. Confidence intervals or per-house breakdowns would help.
- “Similar time” claims lack units, hardware specs, and problem sizes. Given the use of CVX, this needs substantiation.

Clarity and presentation
- Notational inconsistencies and typos: numerous (e.g., “equavalent,” inconsistent use of bold/arrows, 1T and eT definitions with confusing transposes, missing indices t in some sums, repeated/dangling sentence at the end of Sec. 7.1, inconsistent capitalization).
- The constraints with Hit are written in a way that is dimensionally confusing (e.g., Ki∑l Hitl. = STi,t−1 mixes row/column vectors); please clarify these as standard local polytope constraints with explicit t index ranges.
- References are often incomplete or malformed (missing years/pages, “version” without number for CVX, multiple “editors” fields, inconsistent capitalization), and some entries list only venues without years.
- The claim “AFHMM+SAC has polynomial time” is trivial but misleading; give the precise variable count and discuss solver complexity.

Originality and significance
- The core idea—aggregate (sum) constraints on latent state sequences via posterior regularization—is natural and closely related to existing frameworks. The primary novelty lies in instantiating it for AFHMM with a convex relaxation. This is interesting but incremental.
- For NILM, the gains shown are encouraging, but the experimental scope and baselines are insufficient to establish state-of-the-art advancement.

Pros
- Well-motivated constraint that matches practitioner priors.
- Sound convex relaxation; correct PR connection.
- Empirical gains on a realistic dataset.

Cons
- Many presentation and referencing issues.
- Insufficient methodological detail and sensitivity analysis (λi, Ki, µ0 robustness).
- Weak baseline coverage; unclear scalability; reliance on generic solver.
- Reproducibility concerns and unclear post-relaxation decoding.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is promising and likely useful in practice, but the paper in its current form has too many technical and presentation shortcomings for NeurIPS. I encourage the authors to (1) clean up notation and references; (2) provide precise algorithmic details, complexity, and rounding strategy; (3) conduct sensitivity analyses for λi, Ki, and µ0 mismatch; (4) compare against duration/semi-Markov and other NILM baselines; and (5) include scalability experiments with larger T and report exact runtimes. These improvements would substantially strengthen the contribution.
