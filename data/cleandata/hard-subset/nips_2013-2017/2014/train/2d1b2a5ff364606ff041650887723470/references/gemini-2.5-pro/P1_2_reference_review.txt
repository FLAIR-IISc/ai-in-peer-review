**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS) in time-series data, focusing on the common issue of source identifiability. The authors propose a novel method called Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) to incorporate prior domain knowledge into the modeling process. The core idea is that for many BSS problems, such as energy disaggregation, we have prior knowledge about the expected total aggregate value of each source over a given time period (e.g., the total daily energy consumption of a refrigerator).

The authors incorporate these SACs into a standard Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a popular model for this task. As the SACs are non-Markovian (they depend on the entire state sequence), standard inference algorithms like Viterbi are not applicable. To overcome this, the authors formulate the maximum a posteriori (MAP) inference problem as a penalized optimization problem, relax the integer constraints on the state indicator variables, and solve the resulting Convex Quadratic Program (CQP). The paper also shows a connection between their penalized objective and the posterior regularization framework. The method is evaluated on a toy dataset and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES), demonstrating significant improvements over a baseline AFHMM and a state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP.

**Strengths**

1.  **Simple, Intuitive, and Effective Idea:** The central contribution—using signal aggregate constraints—is a simple but powerful form of prior knowledge. The motivation is very clear; the "franken-appliance" problem is a well-known challenge in energy disaggregation, and SACs directly target this issue by ensuring the disaggregated signals are physically plausible in aggregate. This type of constraint seems broadly applicable to other BSS domains where similar aggregate knowledge is available.

2.  **Sound Technical Approach:** The formulation of the constrained MAP problem as a relaxed CQP is a solid and standard approach for dealing with such global, non-Markovian constraints in graphical models. It provides a principled way to find a globally optimal solution to the relaxed problem. The connection drawn to the posterior regularization framework (Section 6) provides additional theoretical grounding for the penalized objective function.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and compelling.
    *   The use of a toy dataset effectively demonstrates how the performance of AFHMM+SAC gracefully degrades as the provided prior (`µ0`) moves away from the true value, showing the method is robust.
    *   The evaluation on the real-world HES dataset is thorough. The comparison against both a standard AFHMM and a strong recent baseline (AFAMAP) is crucial for establishing the significance of the results.
    *   The use of two types of priors for `µ0`—the practical National Total Consumption (NTC) and the oracle True Total Consumption (TTC)—is excellent. It demonstrates that the method works well with realistic priors (NTC) while also showing the upper bound on performance if perfect priors were available (TTC).
    *   The reported performance gains are substantial (e.g., a 29% NDE improvement over AFAMAP on real data with mains), clearly demonstrating the practical value of the proposed constraints.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation, model, inference procedure, and results are all explained logically. The notation is consistent and the problem is formulated precisely.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Handling of the Relaxed Solution:** The paper solves a relaxed version of the problem where the state indicator variables `Sitk` lie in [0, 1]. However, it does not explicitly state how these continuous values are converted back into a discrete state sequence for evaluation (i.e., for computing NDE and SAE). Is it by rounding, or by taking the argmax at each time step? This is a critical detail for reproducibility and for understanding potential issues arising from the relaxation (e.g., if many `Sitk` values are far from 0 or 1).

2.  **Lack of Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** The complexity parameters `λi` play a crucial role in balancing the likelihood term and the constraint penalty. The authors state they "simply set λi = 1 for all the experiments." While this simplifies the experiments, it leaves open important questions about the method's sensitivity to this choice. A discussion or experiment showing how performance varies with `λ` would significantly strengthen the paper and provide practical guidance for users of the method. The paper mentions a one-to-one correspondence with the slack parameter `δi`, but offers no guidance on how to set either.

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** While the paper notes that the CQP has polynomial time complexity and that the runtime is "similar" to the baselines, the provided tables show that AFHMM+SAC is consistently the slowest method, especially on the more complex data with mains readings. A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and how it scales with the number of time steps (T), appliances (I), and states (K) would be beneficial. CQP solvers can become computationally prohibitive for very long time series, which is common in this domain.

4.  **Generalization of Priors:** The paper uses national average consumption as the prior `µi0`. This is a reasonable choice, but the performance will likely depend on how representative this average is for a specific household. A brief discussion on the impact of poor priors or strategies for adapting priors (e.g., using household metadata) would add value. The toy data experiment touches on this but could be expanded in the context of the real-world application.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a simple, novel, and effective idea for improving blind source separation. The proposed Signal Aggregate Constraints provide a well-motivated and principled way to incorporate valuable domain knowledge. The technical formulation is sound, and the experimental results are strong, demonstrating a clear and significant advance over existing methods on a challenging real-world problem.

The weaknesses, primarily concerning the handling of the relaxed solution and the lack of hyperparameter analysis, are minor and could be addressed in a final version of the paper. They do not detract from the core contribution. The work is significant as the proposed constraint type is likely to be useful for a wide range of researchers and practitioners working on BSS problems beyond energy disaggregation.

I recommend **Acceptance**. This paper is a solid contribution to the field.
