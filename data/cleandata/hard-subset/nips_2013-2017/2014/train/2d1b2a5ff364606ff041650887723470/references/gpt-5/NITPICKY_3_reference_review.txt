Summary
This paper proposes Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation, instantiated in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMM) for energy disaggregation. SACs encode prior beliefs about aggregate usage per source (e.g., per-appliance daily energy) by penalizing squared deviations between the sum of each source’s reconstructed signal and a specified target µi0. The resulting non-Markovian objective is optimized by relaxing the discrete AFHMM to a convex quadratic program (CQP) with auxiliary transition variables H, and solved with CVX. The authors also show the equivalence to posterior regularization. Experiments on a toy dataset and the UK HES low-frequency electricity data compare AFHMM+SAC to AFHMM and AFAMAP, reporting sizable gains (e.g., 45% NDE reduction over AFHMM, 29% over AFAMAP).

Relation to prior work
The idea of incorporating expectations/aggregates as constraints relates closely to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.), generalized expectation (Mann & McCallum), and measurement-based learning (Liang et al.), all cited. AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola) imposes change constraints; semi-Markov approaches model durations. Aggregate constraints have been explored in probabilistic databases but not in rich time-series models. The specific convex relaxation with H variables is similar in spirit to relaxations in AFAMAP.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: aggregate usage is meaningful for users and often available a priori; enforcing it should mitigate identifiability in BSS.
- Conceptually simple regularizer, broadly applicable beyond energy disaggregation.
- Convex relaxation is appealing; objective is concave quadratic plus linear terms with linear constraints, allowing efficient solvers.
- Useful connection to posterior regularization clarifies the probabilistic interpretation.
- Empirical results on realistic, ultra-low-frequency data (HES) show consistent gains over strong baselines.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and completeness
- Relaxation and rounding: The CQP allows fractional S and H. How are outputs converted to discrete states, if at all? The metrics (NDE/SAE) can be computed from continuous reconstructions, but then AFHMM+SAC is not directly comparable to discrete decoders (Viterbi/AFAMAP). Please specify the decoding/rounding procedure, its effect on feasibility of H, and whether baselines were also relaxed for fairness (you mention λ=0 yields a CQP but do not evaluate it).
- Objective/convexity derivation: L(S,H) includes −S^TΛS and −(1/2)S^TVS, which are concave if Λ,V are PSD (they are). This is consistent with maximizing a concave function subject to linear constraints (convex optimization). However, Section 5 initially calls it an “IQP” before relaxation to “CQP”; please explicitly state “maximize concave quadratic” and confirm CVX usage (minimization with sign flip).
- Notation/dimensions are opaque and sometimes inconsistent: 1T is a column vector (but superscript T typically denotes transpose), eT is also T×1, µ∗i = 1T ⊗ µi (dimension check?), Vt = σ−2μ→μ→T with μ→ stacking across chains—precise shapes for S, St, Si, H, and how they couple are hard to verify. Constraints on H use indices “l,” “j,” “k” inconsistently. Reproducibility suffers.
- Parameter estimation: You assume θ known in Sections 4/5 but estimate them in experiments. How are µi, π, P estimated from appliance-level data? K-means for µi is stated; how are transition/initial probabilities derived (hard assignments? smoothed counts?) and σt handled? Is σt fixed or learned?
- Hyperparameters: λi are fixed to 1 throughout; no sensitivity analysis. As the SAC is central, robustness to λ and µ0 misspecification should be demonstrated beyond a toy figure.
- Complexity/scalability: Claiming “polynomial time” is not informative; practical scaling with T, I, Ki and wall-clock times for CVX vs Viterbi/AFAMAP should be documented more fully. Interior-point solvers on dense quadratics often do not scale to long horizons; what are T and I in practice? Any problem decomposition or warm-starting?
- Posterior regularization link: The mapping from δi to λi is asserted but not derived; briefly show the dual relationship or provide a reference with the exact correspondence.

Experimental design and reporting
- Data usage and “NTC”: For the no-mains setting, “NTC” is computed as averages across training days across all households. This is not a true external prior; it leverages the same dataset and may inadvertently incorporate cross-household information. For the with-mains setting, NTC is computed “using the training data for the AFHMM+SAC” per house, which is not “national.” Please clarify and discuss deployment realism (e.g., priors from public surveys).
- Statistical tests: You mention paired t-tests at 5% but provide no p-values, sample sizes, or what is paired (days? households?). For with-mains, the number of test days per house is not stated.
- Tables/Figures: Referred tables/figures (1–3, Fig. 2) and exact numbers are missing here. Without them, it is hard to verify claims. Ensure all are present with confidence intervals.
- Baseline fairness: AFAMAP and AFHMM configurations are under-specified (e.g., number of states fixed to 3 for all appliances; was this optimal for AFAMAP? Were its hyperparameters tuned?). If AFHMM+SAC uses a strong prior µ0 and others do not, consider adding a variant of AFHMM with a simple aggregate penalty (non-factorial constraint) to isolate the effect of SAC vs convex relaxation.
- Metrics: SAE normalizes by total Y, which, for with-mains, includes unmodeled appliances; discuss potential bias across houses/models.
- Toy study: Only explores varying µ0 with λ fixed; include a baseline without constraint, and show how performance degrades with noise or incorrect K.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typos and formatting issues: “equavalent”, “distibution”, inconsistent notation for vectors/matrices, overloaded µ for both state means and aggregate targets µi0, index order in p(i)jk vs P(Zt=j|Zt−1=k), dangling/incomplete references (years/pages missing, “editors,” truncated entries, URLs unformatted). These detract significantly.
- Section 4.2: The extension to multiple windows is asserted “straightforward” without derivation or complexity implications.
- Some claims (computational efficiency “similar”) need concrete evidence.

Originality and significance
- The idea of aggregate constraints within AFHMMs and the convex relaxation are interesting and potentially useful. The reported gains on challenging low-rate data are promising. However, given the methodological ambiguities and presentation issues, it is hard to assess the true contribution and reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant, difficult problem with a simple, general idea (SACs).
- Sound use of convex relaxation; link to posterior regularization is apt.
- Promising empirical improvements on realistic datasets.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient methodological clarity (notation, rounding, parameter learning, complexity).
- Limited hyperparameter and sensitivity analysis; unclear fairness of comparisons.
- Missing details and incomplete references; numerous presentation errors.
- Ambiguity about how priors are obtained in a realistic setting.

Questions for authors
- How do you decode fractional S/H into signals/states? Do you compare against a relaxed AFHMM baseline?
- Precise estimation procedure for θ and σt?
- Sensitivity to λ and mis-specified µ0 on real data?
- Provide full details on datasets, NTC construction, number of test days, and statistical tests.
- Scalability: problem sizes, solver settings, and timings.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The core idea is appealing and likely useful, but the paper, as written, has too many methodological and presentation issues to meet NeurIPS standards. A revised version with clarified derivations, stronger experimental rigor, and polished writing could be competitive.
