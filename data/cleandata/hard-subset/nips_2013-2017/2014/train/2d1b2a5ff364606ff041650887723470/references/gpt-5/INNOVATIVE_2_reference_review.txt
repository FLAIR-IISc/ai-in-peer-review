Summary
This paper tackles identifiability in blind source separation with additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs): soft constraints that the time-summed contribution of each source matches an expected total. The authors embed these constraints as quadratic penalties in the MAP objective, show the equivalence to posterior regularization, and derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) relaxation using auxiliary transition variables H to preserve linear constraints and achieve a concave objective (sum of linear terms and negative quadratic forms with PSD matrices). They solve the relaxed problem with CVX. On a toy example, performance degrades gracefully as aggregate priors are mis-specified. On low-frequency real energy data (HES), SACs improve normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) over AFHMM and the AFAMAP one-change constraint baseline, including in the more realistic “with mains” setting.

Relation to prior work
AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12) constrains at most one chain to change state per timestep; other NILM work uses semi-Markov/duration models or other structural priors. Posterior regularization, generalized expectation, and measurement-based learning provide general frameworks for injecting expectations/measurements; aggregate constraints have been studied in simpler graphical models (probabilistic DBs). This paper’s contribution is to identify aggregate totals over time as a powerful, general class of priors for BSS and to show a tractable convex relaxation for AFHMMs that exploits these constraints.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable idea: use aggregate totals to regularize BSS solutions, which directly targets the key identifiability failure mode in disaggregation.
- Clear mathematical formulation: mapping to posterior regularization and explicit CQP with auxiliary variables; the objective is concave with linear constraints.
- Practical impact: substantial gains on a challenging, realistic low-frequency dataset; improvements over a strong AFHMM baseline (AFAMAP).
- Generalizability: natural extension to multiple intervals (daytime/nighttime) is straightforward and potentially useful beyond NILM.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Dependence on µ0 quality: The method requires expected totals per source. In the “without mains” experiments, “NTC” is computed from training data across households; this may not reflect a true external prior and could risk leakage if not carefully separated by household/day. Please clarify the exact protocol and whether household-specific information leaks into the priors.
- Limited baselines: Comparisons do not include duration/semi-Markov NILM methods or other recent NILM approaches that encode temporal structure (which could reduce identifiability similarly).
- Rounding/interpretation of relaxed solutions: It is unclear how fractional S and H are converted to discrete state sequences or used to compute appliance signals; presumably x̂it = E[xit] via S, but this should be stated explicitly. Also, how does this affect event-level accuracy?
- Hyperparameters and robustness: λi is fixed to 1; a sensitivity analysis or principled selection (e.g., via CV or Bayesian treatment) would strengthen the case. The toy study shows qualitative robustness, but a more systematic analysis on real data would help.
- Scalability/efficiency details: Solving CQPs with CVX per sequence can be heavy. The claim of similar runtime to AFAMAP is promising but needs more detail (problem sizes, K, T, I, solver settings). Complexity in terms of I, K, T and the cost of auxiliary H (O(T Σi Ki^2)) should be reported empirically.
- Clarity: Some notation is dense; equations define PSD matrices and concavity, but a brief, intuitive explanation of why the relaxation is convex (maximizing a concave QP under linear constraints) would aid readers. Tables/figures are referenced but not visible; ensure full numerical results, confidence intervals, and effect sizes are reported.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The derivation of the CQP is consistent with known relaxations for FHMM MAP inference, and the posterior-regularization connection is correct. The empirical evaluation is reasonable but would benefit from stronger baselines, more rigorous treatment of priors, and clearer reporting of runtime and rounding.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, though notation-heavy in Section 5. Clarify the handling of relaxed solutions, the computation of NTC, and add explicit complexity and runtime details.

Originality
Moderately strong. While posterior regularization and related frameworks are known, identifying aggregate totals as a cross-domain prior for BSS and providing a tractable convex relaxation for AFHMMs with these constraints is a neat, general idea. This strikes me as a meaningful conceptual step beyond existing NILM constraints such as one-change or duration models.

Significance
Promising. The approach is simple, broadly applicable (audio, biology, other BSS tasks), and shows clear gains on realistic NILM data. I expect others to adopt this idea where aggregate measurements or expert expectations exist.

Questions for authors
- How exactly are fractional S/H used to produce the final disaggregations? Any rounding? Impact on event detection?
- How are NTC priors built to avoid leakage? Are they computed excluding the test household/day?
- Can SACs be specified as inequalities or intervals (e.g., ranges) and still preserve convexity?
- How sensitive are results to λi and to mis-specified µ0 in real data?
- What are typical values of I, K, T, problem sizes, and solver configurations? Any custom solver vs. CVX speedups?

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general idea that directly tackles identifiability.
- Solid convex formulation; clear link to posterior regularization.
- Empirical improvements on realistic, challenging data.
- Potential for broad applicability beyond NILM.

Cons against acceptance
- Reliance on aggregate priors whose construction and realism need clearer justification.
- Missing stronger baselines and ablations; limited analysis of hyperparameters and scalability.
- Some clarity and reproducibility gaps (rounding, runtime, figures/tables).

Recommendation
Weak accept. The conceptual novelty and practical gains outweigh the methodological and reporting gaps. With clearer experimental protocols and additional baselines/ablations, this could be a strong contribution.
