This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) within an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) framework, motivated by the problem of energy disaggregation. The core idea is to leverage prior knowledge about expected total energy consumption of appliances to improve disaggregation accuracy. The authors formulate this as a constrained optimization problem and relax it into a convex quadratic program for efficient computation. The paper presents results on both simulated and real-world energy disaggregation data, demonstrating performance improvements over baseline methods.

While the problem addressed is relevant and the idea of incorporating SACs is conceptually sound, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet persistent, issues that collectively detract from its overall quality. These issues, while not individually fatal, raise concerns regarding thoroughness and attention to detail.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** Energy disaggregation is a practical and important problem with growing interest.
*   **Intuitive Idea:** The concept of leveraging aggregate signal constraints is intuitive and aligns with domain expertise.
*   **Performance Improvement:** The experimental results demonstrate a clear performance gain, particularly the 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error.
*   **Convex Relaxation:** Transforming the problem into a convex quadratic program is a commendable effort to ensure computational tractability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Overly Detailed Introduction:** The introduction spends excessive time establishing the broader context of blind source separation, seemingly disproportionate to its direct relevance to the specific contribution. The discussion of posterior regularization, generalized expectation criterion, and measurement-based learning feels tangential and doesn’t contribute substantially to motivating SACs.
*   **Notational Inconsistency & Clarity:** The notation is inconsistent and often needlessly complex. For instance, introducing *Hit* matrices just to represent co-occurrence of states, and then employing both *hitjk* and  *Hit.l*, *Hitl.* is confusing. The repeated use of “T” for both time steps and transpose is problematic. The mathematical formulation lacks clarity in several places, making it difficult to follow the derivations.
*   **Lack of Justification for Model Choices:** The choice of 3 states for all appliances in the HES dataset is arbitrary and lacks justification. A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of the number of states would strengthen the results.
*   **Incomplete Discussion of λ Tuning:** While the paper mentions λ as a complexity parameter, it simply states they set it to 1. A more thorough discussion of how λ affects performance and the potential for cross-validation to optimize it is missing. The lack of a discussion on the sensitivity of performance to λ is a significant omission.
*   **Limited Ablation Study:** The paper presents results for AFHMM, AFAMAP, and AFHMM+SAC. However, there is no ablation study to isolate the impact of the SACs specifically. For example, what happens if we only include the SACs *without* the AFHMM structure?
*   **Convexity Argument is Weak:** The justification for the convexity argument in Section 5 is somewhat hand-wavy and assumes the reader is already familiar with the subtleties of convex optimization. A more detailed explanation would be beneficial.
*   **Minor Typos and Grammatical Errors:** The paper contains several minor typos and grammatical errors throughout (e.g., "franken-appliance," inconsistent use of articles).
*   **Figure 1 is poorly explained:** The figure and its associated explanation do not provide much insight. What is being plotted? It simply says “as the distance increases” but gives no values.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The idea of incorporating SACs is novel and potentially impactful for energy disaggregation and other BSS problems.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a tangible performance improvement.
*   The authors have made an effort to address the computational challenges associated with SACs.

**Con:**

*   The paper is plagued by numerous minor flaws in notation, clarity, and completeness.
*   The lack of justification for key model choices and the absence of a thorough ablation study weaken the conclusions.
*   The theoretical contribution (convex relaxation) is not particularly groundbreaking.
*   The writing quality is not up to NIPS standards.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea of the paper is promising, the numerous minor flaws significantly detract from its overall quality. The lack of rigor in the presentation and the absence of a more comprehensive analysis raise concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the results. The paper reads as a somewhat rushed effort, lacking the polish and thoroughness expected of a NIPS submission.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**.

I believe that with substantial revisions addressing the points raised above, the paper could be reconsidered. However, in its current state, it does not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS. The amount of work needed to bring this up to standard is significant.
