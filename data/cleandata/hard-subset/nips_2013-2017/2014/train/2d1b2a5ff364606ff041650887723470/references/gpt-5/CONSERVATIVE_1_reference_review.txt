Summary
The paper proposes adding signal aggregate constraints (SACs) to additive factorial HMMs (AFHMM) for blind source separation, with a focus on energy disaggregation (NILM). The key idea is to encode prior knowledge about the total energy usage of each source by penalizing deviations of per-chain aggregate sums from specified values. The resulting MAP problem is non-Markovian; the authors introduce auxiliary transition variables and relax the binary constraints to obtain a convex quadratic program (CQP) that can be solved with CVX. They connect the objective to posterior regularization and evaluate on a toy example and on the HES dataset (low-frequency, real household data), reporting improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS 2012).

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable prior: aggregate (sum) constraints are intuitive in NILM and other BSS settings.
- Clear formulation: the relaxed problem is concave-quadratic with linear constraints; the link to posterior regularization is correct and helpful.
- Empirical results on realistic, low-frequency data suggest benefits over strong baselines (AFAMAP), which is nontrivial for this domain.
- The oracle (TTC) experiment is a useful upper bound to illustrate potential value of aggregate information.
- Potentially generalizable (e.g., time-windowed aggregate constraints).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practicality of constraints: In “with mains” experiments, µ0 is derived as “national total consumption (NTC)” computed from labeled training data across households. In most NILM deployments, appliance-level training labels across many households are not available; the paper should justify that NTC-quality priors can be obtained from public surveys and quantify domain shift. As presented, the method depends on information that is often inaccessible at deployment.
- Hyperparameter handling: All λi are fixed to 1 without data-driven tuning. No sensitivity analysis on real data is provided (toy only). This undermines claims of robustness and leaves unclear how to set λi in practice. Baseline hyperparameters (e.g., AFAMAP) are not discussed either, raising fairness concerns.
- Relaxation and outputs: The optimization relaxes binary indicators to [0,1]. The paper does not describe any rounding or projection back to discrete states, nor justify that using fractional Sit (x̂it = µi⊤Sit) is acceptable. This matters for interpretability (a stated goal) and comparability to AFHMM/AFAMAP which produce discrete states.
- Scalability: The formulation introduces O(T∑iKi + T∑iKi^2) variables; solving with CVX is unlikely to scale to longer horizons or many appliances. The claim that AFHMM+SAC has “similar” runtime to AFHMM/AFAMAP is surprising given interior-point costs and lacks detailed evidence (problem sizes, solver tolerances, iteration counts). Without a specialized solver or complexity analysis, I am skeptical about applicability at larger scale.
- Limited validation: Results are limited to NILM; no evidence is provided for other BSS domains (audio, biology), despite the general claim. The toy example is minimal and largely confirms the obvious (errors grow as µ0 deviates).
- Identifiability: The paper motivates SACs as addressing identifiability but provides no formal analysis. Aggregate constraints alone do not guarantee identifiability; an empirical or theoretical discussion is missing.
- Methodological details: Noise variances σt, training of θ, and number of states (fixed to 3 for all appliances) are handled simplistically; it’s unclear whether baselines could benefit similarly from better modeling (e.g., semi-Markov durations). No ablation isolating the effect of SAC from other modeling choices.
- Reporting: Statistical testing is mentioned (paired t-tests) but without p-values/effect sizes; assumptions (normality) are not discussed. Some references lack years/details; several notational inconsistencies and typos reduce clarity.

Quality
Technically, the relaxation and convexity arguments are plausible: with Vt and Λi PSD, the quadratic terms are concave under maximization; the constraints are linear. However, important practical aspects (rounding, sensitivity to λi, solver scalability) are not addressed. The experimental design leans on labeled data to obtain µ0 and uses untuned λi; comparisons could be made stronger and fairer. Overall, the empirical support is suggestive but not rigorous enough to endorse the method broadly.

Clarity
The high-level idea and optimization are reasonably presented, and the connection to posterior regularization is clear. That said, the manuscript suffers from missing details (solver settings, dataset sizes per run, σt estimation), incomplete references, and some notational clutter. Including the exact rounding/decoding procedure and a short complexity discussion would help.

Originality
Adding aggregate constraints via posterior regularization is conceptually related to prior work (posterior regularization; measurement-based learning; constrained HMMs). The specific instantiation for AFHMM with a convex relaxation is a reasonable engineering contribution, but not fundamentally novel. The novelty is moderate within the NILM context.

Significance
Within NILM, constraining aggregate energy per appliance is sensible and appears beneficial on HES data. However, the reliance on µ0 from labeled data and the scalability concerns limit impact. Broader claims for BSS are not substantiated experimentally.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- How would µ0 be obtained in a truly unlabeled deployment? Can you report performance when µ0 comes from an external survey with distributional shift?
- Provide sensitivity on λi for real data; propose a practical tuning strategy (e.g., CV over households).
- Describe and evaluate a rounding/decoding method to produce discrete states; report interpretability metrics (e.g., duration distributions) if that is a goal.
- Give concrete runtime/size numbers and compare to a specialized first-order solver; include scaling experiments (T, I, Ki).
- Compare against duration-constrained models (semi-Markov) and recent NILM baselines; include ablations varying Ki per appliance.
- Evaluate time-windowed SACs on HES (day/night) to support claims.
- Clean up references and notation.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is sensible and results are promising, but the empirical evidence and methodological rigor are not yet sufficient: dependence on hard-to-obtain priors, lack of hyperparameter tuning and rounding strategy, and unresolved scalability concerns. A strengthened version addressing these points would be much more compelling. Confidence: high.
