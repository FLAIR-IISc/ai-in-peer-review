### Paper Summary

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), specifically in the context of energy disaggregation, where an aggregate time series (total household electricity usage) must be separated into its constituent source signals (individual appliances). The authors correctly identify that a key challenge in this domain is the *identifiability* of the sources; without constraints, inference can produce nonsensical "franken-appliance" signals.

The main contribution is the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) into the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a standard model for this task. The core idea is that for many sources, we have prior knowledge about their expected aggregate value over a long time period (e.g., the total daily energy consumption of a refrigerator is expected to be within a certain range). This prior knowledge is incorporated as a quadratic penalty term in the Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) inference objective.

Because this penalty term breaks the Markov property of the model, standard inference techniques like Viterbi are not applicable. The authors' key technical contribution is to formulate a relaxed version of this MAP inference problem as a convex quadratic program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. They also demonstrate that their formulation can be viewed through the lens of posterior regularization.

The proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES). The results show a significant improvement over both the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP, reducing normalized disaggregation error by 45% and 29% respectively on the most realistic experimental setting.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a well-known and important problem (identifiability in BSS) with a simple, intuitive, and novel solution. The idea of using prior knowledge on the *aggregate* signal value is a natural and powerful way to constrain the solution space, and it appears to be novel in the context of factorial HMMs for this application. It is a valuable contribution that other researchers are likely to build upon.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical approach is sound. Recognizing that the SAC-penalized objective is non-Markovian and then deriving an equivalent CQP for a relaxed version is a solid technical contribution. This makes the proposed method computationally practical. The connection drawn to posterior regularization in Section 6 provides welcome theoretical grounding for what might otherwise seem like a heuristic penalty term.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental validation is strong. The authors compare their method against both a standard baseline (AFHMM) and a relevant state-of-the-art model (AFAMAP). The use of the HES dataset, which features challenging low-frequency data, demonstrates the method's real-world applicability. The experiments are well-designed, covering both a semi-synthetic setting (summing individual appliance readings) and a fully realistic setting (using actual mains readings), which adds to the credibility of the results. The inclusion of an "oracle" result using the true total consumption (TTC) is a nice touch, clearly illustrating the potential of this type of constraint. The reported performance gains are substantial.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is established effectively in the introduction, the model is described logically, and the experimental results are presented comprehensibly. The authors provide enough detail for an expert to understand their approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The main weakness of the evaluation is the lack of analysis regarding the complexity parameter `λi`. The authors state they "simply set λi = 1 for all the experiments". This is a significant simplification. The performance of the model will certainly depend on this parameter, which balances the data likelihood against the aggregate constraint. A sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with `λ`, or a description of how `λ` might be set in practice (e.g., via cross-validation), would make the experimental results much more robust and the method more readily usable by others.

2.  **Limited Exploration of Generalized Constraints:** The authors introduce the interesting idea of generalizing SACs to multiple, shorter time windows (e.g., day vs. night constraints) in Section 4.2. They state this is a "straightforward extension" but do not evaluate it. This feels like a missed opportunity. Demonstrating the effectiveness of these more granular constraints would have significantly strengthened the paper and showcased the full power of their framework.

3.  **Dependence on Pre-trained Parameters:** The paper focuses on the inference problem, assuming the HMM parameters (state means `µik`, transitions, etc.) are known or pre-trained. While this is a common setup, the performance of the disaggregation is highly dependent on the quality of these estimated parameters. A brief discussion of how errors in parameter estimation might interact with the SACs would be beneficial. For instance, if the k-means clustering yields poor estimates for an appliance's power states, can the SAC still guide the model to a reasonable solution?

### Summary of Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, intuitive, and highly effective method (SACs) for a significant problem (identifiability in BSS).
*   The technical contribution of formulating inference as a CQP is sound and makes the approach practical.
*   The paper is supported by a strong and convincing experimental evaluation on a challenging, real-world dataset, demonstrating a clear advance over the state of the art.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to understand.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The evaluation lacks a sensitivity analysis for the key hyperparameter `λ`, which is instead fixed to an arbitrary value. This weakens the claim of the method's general applicability.
*   The potentially powerful generalization of SACs to multiple time windows is mentioned but not explored experimentally.

### Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable and well-executed idea. The proposed Signal Aggregate Constraints offer a simple yet powerful way to improve inference in AFHMMs for energy disaggregation and related BSS problems. The technical formulation is solid, and the empirical results are impressive. The primary weakness is the lack of hyperparameter analysis, but this does not undermine the core contribution. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. The paper is a clear advancement of the state-of-the-art in an important application domain and is well-suited for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
