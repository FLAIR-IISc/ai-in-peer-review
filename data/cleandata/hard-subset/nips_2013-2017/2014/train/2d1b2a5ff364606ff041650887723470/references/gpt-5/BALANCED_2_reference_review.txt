Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses blind source separation for additive time series, with a focus on energy disaggregation. It augments the additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) with signal aggregate constraints (SACs) that encourage each latent source’s total energy over a time window to match prior expectations (e.g., daily watt-hours per appliance). This yields a non-Markovian coupling across time. The authors derive a relaxed maximum a posteriori inference procedure by introducing transition indicator variables and formulating the problem as a convex quadratic program (CQP). They also show the equivalence of their penalized objective to posterior regularization with quadratic moment constraints. Experiments on toy data and the UK HES dataset (ultra-low-frequency mains data) compare AFHMM+SAC to AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12), reporting substantial improvements in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE), with similar runtimes.

This connects well to earlier NeurIPS/ML work on factorial HMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan), energy disaggregation with AFHMM/AFAMAP, posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.), generalized expectation (Mann & McCallum), and measurement-based learning (Liang et al.). The specific form of constraints (aggregates/sums) is tailored to the application and seems widely applicable.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: aggregate usage is often known (or approximately known) and directly relevant to end users; leveraging it addresses identifiability.
- Methodologically sound formulation: turning global quadratic penalties into a CQP via relaxation and auxiliary transition variables is neat and computationally attractive; the concavity of the objective under PSD quadratics is correctly exploited.
- Connection to posterior regularization provides a principled view and relates to broader literature on constrained posteriors.
- Empirical evaluation on challenging low-frequency HES data, with both synthetic and real experiments, shows consistent gains over AFHMM and AFAMAP in NDE and SAE; the toy study demonstrates robustness to misspecified aggregates.
- Practical significance: SAE improvements directly match user-facing goals in NILM.
- Claimed runtime comparable to baselines, suggesting feasibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty is moderate: imposing expectation-based constraints on latent structures is well covered by posterior regularization/GE/measurement learning. The main novelty is the specific SACs within AFHMMs and the particular CQP relaxation; the latter resembles standard HMM LP/QP relaxations using transition indicators.
- Relaxation vs. discrete decoding: the method relaxes Sit, hit to [0,1] but does not describe how (or whether) solutions are rounded to valid discrete sequences. If outputs are fractional, comparability to Viterbi/AFAMAP (which output sequences) should be carefully justified and evaluated.
- Hyperparameter selection: λi are fixed to 1 without tuning; it is unclear whether competing methods were equally tuned. This could bias results and understate potential gains (or inflate them).
- Specification of priors: NTC aggregates are computed from training data; more detail is needed (per household vs. cross-household, per-day normalization, seasonality). Robustness analysis beyond the toy example (e.g., miscalibrated NTC in real data) would strengthen claims.
- Limited baseline coverage: semi-Markov/HSMM-based NILM methods and duration modeling have been applied (e.g., Johnson & Willsky; Parson et al.); comparisons or discussion of trade-offs are limited.
- Some modeling details are under-specified: noise variance σt, number of states choice per appliance (always 3), parameter learning for transitions, handling of “other” loads in mains experiments, and how constraints are applied over sub-intervals (though mentioned as straightforward).
- Reproducibility: key implementation details and code availability are not provided; tables/figures referenced are not fully included in the text here.

Quality (soundness)
- The optimization derivation is plausible; ensuring Λi and Vt are PSD justifies concavity. Constraints using transition matrices Hit enforce local consistency. However, the relaxation may yield fractional flows; the paper should discuss feasibility of reconstructing sequences and its impact on metrics.
- Empirical methodology is reasonable, but fairness and tuning are not fully addressed.

Clarity
- Generally clear, with intuitive motivation and transparent objective. Some notation is heavy and includes minor inconsistencies/typos; adding a short explanation of the convexity (maximizing a concave quadratic under linear constraints) would help. Provide explicit rounding/projection procedure if used.

Originality
- Moderate. The idea of SACs is simple and practically meaningful; the CQP relaxation within AFHMMs is a useful instantiation but conceptually close to prior constrained-posterior frameworks.

Significance
- Promising for NILM and potentially other BSS domains where aggregates are known (audio, biology). If robustness to mis-specified aggregates is strong in practice, the method could see adoption. Broader impact depends on generalization beyond this dataset and on integration with duration models.

Suggestions for improvement
- Detail how fractional solutions are used: do you round to sequences? If not, justify comparability and report both fractional and rounded metrics if possible.
- Provide sensitivity analyses on λi and on NTC mis-specification in real data; describe a cross-validation or empirical Bayes approach for λi.
- Clarify how NTC is computed (per-household vs. pooled), and whether house-specific priors improve results.
- Compare or discuss HSMM-based/duration models and whether SACs are complementary; an ablation with “AFAMAP + SAC” would be informative.
- Report exact runtimes, scaling with T and sum Ki, and solver settings; discuss whether specialized solvers outperform CVX.
- Consider interval-specific SACs (as you mention) with diurnal priors; a small experiment could strengthen the case.
- Tighten writing and references; fix typos and provide full citations.

Pros for acceptance
- Well-motivated, practical constraint class; principled formulation; convex relaxation with efficient inference; solid gains on realistic NILM data.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty beyond known frameworks; incomplete discussion of relaxation/rounding; tuning and baseline coverage could be stronger.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a useful, principled, and empirically effective idea with reasonable technical depth. Addressing the noted methodological and clarity issues would solidify the contribution. Reviewer confidence: medium.
