### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS) using additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs), with a specific focus on the application of energy disaggregation. The authors identify a key challenge in this domain: the inherent unidentifiability of the separated source signals, which can lead to unrealistic or uninterpretable results (e.g., "franken-appliances"). Previous work has sought to address this by adding constraints to the AFHMM, such as limiting the number of state changes at any given time (e.g., AFAMAP by Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012).

The main contribution of this paper is the introduction of a new class of constraints, termed Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The core idea is that for many BSS problems, prior knowledge exists about the expected aggregate value of each source signal over a given time period. For instance, the total daily energy consumption of a specific appliance type is often known or can be estimated. The authors incorporate SACs into the AFHMM's maximum a posteriori (MAP) inference objective by adding a penalty term that penalizes solutions where the sum of a source signal deviates from its known prior aggregate value.

Because this global, time-summed constraint breaks the Markovian structure of the model, standard dynamic programming algorithms like Viterbi are not applicable. To perform inference, the authors formulate the MAP problem as an integer quadratic program and then relax the integer constraints on the state indicator variables. This results in a convex quadratic program (CQP) that can be solved efficiently, adapting a methodology previously used for AFAMAP. The authors also provide a theoretical justification for their approach by showing its equivalence to the posterior regularization framework of Ganchev et al. (2010).

The proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on both synthetic data and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES). The experiments demonstrate that AFHMM+SAC significantly outperforms both the standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model, achieving a 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error over AFHMM and a 29% reduction over AFAMAP in a realistic setting.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is well-motivated and mathematically sound. The formulation of the MAP inference problem with the new constraints is clear, and the transformation to a CQP for efficient inference is a valid and appropriate technique. The claims of improved performance are strongly supported by a thorough experimental evaluation on a large, real-world dataset. The authors are careful to compare against a strong, relevant baseline (AFAMAP) and use appropriate evaluation metrics (NDE, SAE) and statistical tests to validate their results. The connection drawn to posterior regularization provides a solid theoretical underpinning for the penalized objective function.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a clear motivation for the problem using the intuitive "franken-appliance" example. The models, from the baseline AFHMM to the proposed AFHMM+SAC, are presented logically and with clear notation. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail to understand the procedure, and the results are presented clearly in tables and figures. The paper's contributions are explicitly stated and well-supported by the content.

**Originality:** The primary contribution—the concept of Signal Aggregate Constraints—is novel. While the general idea of constraining AFHMMs is not new, this specific form of global, sum-based constraint, derived from domain knowledge about signal totals, appears to be a new contribution to the field. The inference machinery (CQP relaxation) is adapted from prior work [12], so the novelty lies in the modeling contribution rather than a new inference algorithm. However, showing how this existing technique can be effectively leveraged for a new and powerful class of constraints is a valuable contribution in itself. The paper does an excellent job of situating its work within the existing literature on energy disaggregation and constrained HMMs.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant for several reasons. First, it addresses a difficult and important practical problem in energy disaggregation. The reported performance improvements are substantial (e.g., a 29% error reduction over a recent state-of-the-art method), demonstrating a clear advance for this application. Second, the proposed SACs are a general concept. Although demonstrated on energy data, this type of constraint could be readily applied to other BSS problems in different domains (e.g., audio processing, computational biology) where aggregate properties of the sources are known. This generality broadens the potential impact of the work. The paper provides a practical and effective method for incorporating a very intuitive form of domain knowledge into a complex probabilistic model.

A minor weakness is the limited discussion on the selection of the hyperparameter `λi`. The authors state they set `λi=1` for all experiments. While they mention that this could be tuned via cross-validation, providing a sensitivity analysis or a more principled discussion on setting this parameter would have strengthened the paper by offering more guidance to practitioners seeking to apply the method. However, the strong results obtained even with this simple choice suggest the method is reasonably robust.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Impact:** The paper introduces a novel, intuitive, and highly effective type of constraint (SACs) for addressing the critical identifiability problem in AFHMMs.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Results:** The method achieves substantial and statistically significant performance gains over strong baselines, including a recent state-of-the-art model, on a challenging real-world task.
3.  **Technical Soundness and Clarity:** The work is technically solid, well-motivated, and connected to established theoretical frameworks like posterior regularization. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, making the contributions easy to understand and appreciate.
4.  **Generality:** The proposed constraint type is general and could be impactful in other BSS application domains beyond energy disaggregation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Incremental Inference Method:** The inference algorithm is a direct adaptation of the CQP relaxation used in prior work [12], rather than a novel algorithmic contribution.
2.  **Limited Hyperparameter Analysis:** The choice of the key regularization parameter `λi` is not explored or justified in depth, which is a minor omission in an otherwise thorough evaluation.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. The novel modeling contribution and the significant empirical improvements on an important problem make it a strong candidate for acceptance.
