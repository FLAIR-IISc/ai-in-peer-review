Summary
The paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation, instantiated in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) for energy disaggregation. The key idea is to regularize the latent state sequences so that the aggregate of each source signal (e.g., total energy per appliance over a period) matches prior expectations. This is operationalized by adding quadratic penalties on the deviation between the sum over time of the source’s emitted means and specified totals. Since these constraints are non-Markovian, the authors derive a convex optimization-based relaxed MAP inference: they introduce transition-indicator variables (as in AFAMAP), then relax integrality to obtain a convex quadratic program (solved with CVX). They also relate the objective to posterior regularization. Experiments on synthetic data and on the HES energy dataset show notable gains over AFHMM and AFAMAP; the method remains computationally similar in practice.

Relation to prior work
- AFHMMs and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola) are the main baselines; prior work has constrained FHMMs via one-switch-at-a-time, semi-Markov durations, and non-homogeneous dynamics.
- The paper situates SACs within frameworks like posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.), generalized expectation (Mann & McCallum), and measurement-based learning (Liang et al.), and notes limited prior work on aggregate constraints in simple models. The novelty here is the identification of aggregate (sum) constraints as a broadly applicable prior for BSS, and a tractable convex relaxation for AFHMMs under such global constraints.

Quality
- The formulation is technically sound: the SAC penalty is convex, and the likelihood term leads to a concave quadratic (maximization over a convex set via relaxation). The use of auxiliary H variables follows established practice. The connection to posterior regularization is correct and clarifies the probabilistic footing.
- Empirically, the method consistently improves NDE and SAE on realistic low-frequency HES data, both with synthesized mains and real mains. The “oracle” TTC condition bounds potential gains, while NTC demonstrates value from realistic priors.
- Some methodological details are thin: how fractional relaxed solutions are used downstream (no rounding strategy is described), sensitivity to λ is minimally explored (λ fixed to 1), and scalability claims rely on CVX without detailed complexity accounting or wall-time breakdown vs. problem size.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with a clear problem statement, model, and optimization derivation. Notation is heavy in Section 5; a small worked example or dimensions table would help. Minor typos and formatting glitches exist (but are not fatal).

Originality
- Conceptually, using global aggregate constraints is simple but impactful; within AFHMMs, this direction appears novel. The convex relaxation is a thoughtful twist enabling efficient inference despite non-Markovian penalties. The posterior-regularization link situates the approach in a broader line while clarifying what is new (choice of constraints and application to additive FHMMs with a practical solver).

Significance
- The approach addresses a key identifiability issue in disaggregation by injecting the kind of prior users actually have (totals, not exact timings). Results suggest meaningful accuracy gains at similar computational cost, and the idea is easily portable to other BSS domains (audio, genomics).
- While shown on one domain, the constraint class is general and could influence subsequent work on constrained latent variable models.

Strengths
- Clear, domain-motivated constraint class that is broadly applicable.
- Solid variational/optimization framing with a convex relaxation; practical to implement.
- Significant empirical gains on realistic, low-frequency HES data; analysis includes both aggregate and per-time-step metrics.
- Clean connection to posterior regularization.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Practicality/scalability: Solving large CQPs with CVX may not scale to long T or many devices. Provide complexity vs. T and sum K, memory profile, and a custom solver or decomposition (e.g., block-coordinate ascent or ADMM).
- Relaxation/rounding: Clarify whether outputs are fractional; if so, discuss how this affects interpretability and whether a rounding/post-processing step is needed (and its impact on metrics).
- Sensitivity to priors: The toy study varies µ0, but real-data sensitivity (noise on NTC) is not quantified. Include robustness curves to misspecified µ0 and to different λi, as well as ablations over windowed SACs.
- Baselines: Consider comparing to duration-based HSMM/HSMM-NP or non-homogeneous FHMM baselines cited, which also address identifiability. This would strengthen claims over “state of the art.”
- Learning vs. inference: Parameters θ are fixed; exploring joint learning with SACs (posterior regularization during training) would be compelling.
- Generality: A second domain (e.g., a small audio separation task) would underscore portability beyond energy.

Questions for authors
- How large were the CQPs (variables/constraints) in the largest experiments? What were average solve times and convergence criteria for CVX?
- Are Sit fractional in practice? If yes, how do you report appliance states or on/off events, and do rounding heuristics hurt NDE/SAE?
- How were λi chosen beyond setting to 1? Do appliance-specific λi improve results?
- For mains experiments with unmodeled loads, do SACs ever bias the model to “force-fit” energy into modeled appliances? Any diagnostics to detect this?

Minor comments
- Check equation 6 for typos (“equavalent”); consider switching to a minimization form to align with standard “CQP” terminology.
- The generalization to time-window SACs is interesting; even a brief experiment would be valuable.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper proposes a simple but novel and practically meaningful constraint family for AFHMMs, with a clean convex relaxation and solid empirical gains on a challenging dataset. Despite some missing analyses (scaling, sensitivity, broader baselines), the conceptual contribution is likely to inspire follow-ups in BSS and constrained inference, which I value highly.
