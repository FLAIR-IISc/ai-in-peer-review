

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper proposes "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) for additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) to address the identifiability problem in blind source separation, particularly for energy disaggregation. The authors argue that knowing expected aggregate values (e.g., daily energy consumption of appliances) can improve disaggregation performance. They incorporate these constraints into AFHMMs by transforming the optimization problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP) and demonstrate empirical improvements over baselines on energy data.

**Strengths:** The paper addresses a meaningful problem in energy disaggregation with practical domain knowledge. The connection to posterior regularization is well-explained, and the experimental results show consistent improvements over baselines. The writing is clear, and the technical details of the CQP formulation are well-presented. The paper makes a reasonable contribution to the energy disaggregation literature, which has seen significant interest at NIPS and related venues.

**Weaknesses:** 

The core innovation (SACs) is not particularly novel. As the authors themselves note, their approach falls under the broader umbrella of posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2010) and similar constraint-based methods. The paper fails to adequately differentiate SACs from existing constraint approaches in the literature. For instance, the paper cites AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012) which uses a constraint that at most one chain changes state at a time, but doesn't compare SACs against this or other constraint-based approaches that could have been used to address identifiability.

The experimental validation is limited to energy disaggregation, without demonstrating applicability to other BSS domains where aggregate constraints might be relevant. The paper doesn't adequately address the key limitation of SACs: their performance depends entirely on the accuracy of the specified aggregate values. The authors show that using "true" aggregate values (TTC) yields the best results, but this is unrealistic in practice. The paper doesn't explore how robust SACs are to errors in the specified aggregate values, which is a critical practical consideration.

The claimed 45% improvement over AFHMM is misleading. The paper reports a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) when comparing SACs to AFHMM, but the actual NDE values are not provided in the main text (only in tables). The paper also claims a 29% improvement over AFAMAP, but the comparison is against a specific implementation (AFAMAP) that may not represent the most recent state-of-the-art. The paper doesn't adequately establish that SACs represent a significant advance over existing constraint-based approaches.

The computational approach (CQP) is presented as novel, but it's unclear how this represents a substantial improvement over standard constrained optimization techniques. The paper doesn't provide sufficient detail about the computational complexity or scalability of the CQP approach compared to existing methods. The claim that "AFHMM, AFAMAP and AFHMM+SAC scale similarly" is unsubstantiated without empirical evidence of runtimes for different problem sizes.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates technical soundness in the formulation of the constrained optimization problem and the connection to posterior regularization. However, the experimental validation is limited to a single application domain and lacks sufficient robustness analysis. The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of SACs or the sensitivity to constraint specification errors.

**Originality:** The paper's main contribution (SACs) is not novel. It represents a specific application of a well-established technique (incorporating prior knowledge into probabilistic models) to a specific problem (energy disaggregation). The connection to posterior regularization is noted, but the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from existing constraint-based methods in the literature. The novelty appears to be in the application rather than the methodological contribution.

**Significance:** While the application to energy disaggregation is relevant, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that SACs represent a significant advance over existing constraint-based approaches. The improvements claimed are modest (33% NDE improvement over AFAMAP), and the paper doesn't establish that SACs would be broadly useful across different BSS domains. The paper doesn't provide evidence that SACs would be adopted by practitioners or build upon the state of the art in a meaningful way.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The technical details are presented logically, and the connection between SACs and posterior regularization is well-explained. However, the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the limitations and sensitivity to constraint specification errors.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses a meaningful problem with a reasonable approach, it fails to establish sufficient novelty or significant advancement over existing methods. The claimed improvements are not convincingly demonstrated, and the experimental validation is limited to a single application domain without sufficient robustness analysis. The paper presents an incremental contribution to a well-studied problem rather than a significant methodological advance that would warrant publication at NIPS. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the novelty argument and provide more comprehensive experimental validation to justify acceptance.
