

# Peer Review: Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation

## Summary

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) to address the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS), specifically within the context of additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs). The authors observe that in many applications like energy disaggregation, domain knowledge about expected total consumption of sources (e.g., appliances) is available, but not typically incorporated into existing models. SACs enforce that the aggregate values (e.g., total energy consumption over time) of source signals remain close to specified values. The paper formulates SACs as constraints, transforms the resulting optimization problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP), and demonstrates significant performance improvements on both synthetic and real-world energy disaggregation data.

The work builds on related frameworks like posterior regularization and extends them with a specific, practical constraint type. While posterior regularization has been applied to various problems, this paper identifies a specific class of constraints (aggregate values) that are frequently available in real-world applications and shows how to efficiently incorporate them into AFHMMs, which are commonly used in energy disaggregation.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-justified mathematical derivations. The authors provide a clear transformation of the SAC-constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program, which is computationally efficient. The theoretical foundation is solid, with the connection to posterior regularization established in Section 6. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using both synthetic data to demonstrate robustness to constraint specification errors and real-world energy data to show significant performance gains.

The authors carefully acknowledge limitations, noting that SACs require domain knowledge about aggregate values (which might not always be available) and that the method is a relaxation of the original problem. The paper includes statistical significance testing (paired t-tests) to validate the performance improvements, which strengthens the experimental claims.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, the technical sections flow logically, and the experimental results are presented in a straightforward manner. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. The paper would be accessible to NIPS readers with a background in machine learning and probabilistic modeling, though some readers unfamiliar with AFHMMs might need to consult the references for background.

One minor suggestion: The connection between the SAC constraints and the specific application to energy disaggregation could be made even clearer earlier in the paper, perhaps in the introduction or problem setting.

## Originality

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by identifying a specific class of constraints (signal aggregates) that are both practically relevant across multiple domains and computationally feasible to incorporate. While posterior regularization has been explored previously, the authors identify a specific, meaningful constraint type that can be efficiently incorporated into AFHMMs, which is novel. The paper clearly distinguishes its contribution from related work, particularly the AFAMAP algorithm that constrains state transitions rather than aggregate values.

The technical contribution of transforming the SAC-constrained optimization into a convex quadratic program is also novel and addresses a key computational challenge that would otherwise prevent the use of such constraints.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. Energy disaggregation is a critical application with real-world impact (helping households understand energy usage), and the paper demonstrates a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over a recent state-of-the-art approach. This represents a meaningful advance in the field.

The SAC framework is general enough to be applied to other BSS problems beyond energy disaggregation, making it potentially valuable for multiple domains. The paper's demonstration that domain knowledge about aggregate values can significantly improve performance provides a compelling case for incorporating such knowledge into probabilistic models.

## Strengths

1. Strong technical contribution: The convex quadratic programming formulation for SACs is a significant advance over direct constraint handling.
2. Practical impact: The method delivers substantial performance improvements on a real-world application with clear societal benefits.
3. Clear experimental validation: Comprehensive evaluation with both synthetic data (to demonstrate robustness) and real-world energy data.
4. Well-motivated problem: The identifiability issue in BSS is well-explained, and SACs provide a natural solution.
5. Connection to posterior regularization: The theoretical foundation is well-grounded in existing literature.

## Weaknesses

1. Limited comparison with other constraint-based approaches: The paper doesn't thoroughly compare SACs with other constraint types (like the AFAMAP constraint) that have been proposed for AFHMMs. It would be valuable to see how SACs interact with or outperform these other constraints.
2. Limited exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity: The paper uses λi=1 for all experiments but doesn't explore how the method performs with different values of λi or how to select them optimally.
3. Underutilized extension: The paper mentions the possibility of multiple time-period constraints (Section 4.2) but doesn't explore this in experiments, which could be particularly relevant for energy disaggregation (e.g., daytime vs. nighttime usage).
4. Limited discussion of limitations: While the paper acknowledges the need for domain knowledge about aggregate values, it doesn't explore cases where such knowledge is unavailable or inaccurate.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling for blind source separation, with clear significance for energy disaggregation and potential applicability to other domains. The technical approach is sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the writing is clear. The minor weaknesses noted above don't detract from the overall quality of the work.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. The paper advances the state of the art in a meaningful way, provides a practical solution to a real-world problem, and offers a general framework that could be applied to other BSS problems. The method is both theoretically grounded and empirically validated, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it combines theoretical insights with practical application, addresses a significant problem with real-world impact, and demonstrates clear improvements over previous work. The work aligns well with NIPS's focus on novel machine learning approaches to challenging problems.
