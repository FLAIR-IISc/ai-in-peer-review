This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) within an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) framework, applied to the problem of energy disaggregation. The core idea – leveraging prior knowledge about expected total energy consumption of appliances – is intuitively appealing and addresses a known challenge in BSS, namely identifiability. The authors frame this as a general technique applicable across domains and present a convex quadratic program (CQP) for efficient inference under these constraints. 

While the motivation is sound and the problem well-defined, the paper presents an incremental contribution built upon existing work without demonstrating a sufficiently compelling advantage to warrant strong acceptance at NIPS. My primary concern lies with the novelty and significance. The concept of incorporating prior knowledge into probabilistic models is not new (posterior regularization, generalized expectation criterion, measurement-based learning are cited), and the specific application of aggregate constraints, while not directly present in this exact form, feels like a natural extension of existing constraint-based approaches for AFHMMs (AFAMAP, non-homogeneous HMMs, semi-Markov models). The authors acknowledge these related works but don’t convincingly position SACs as a fundamentally different or significantly superior approach. 

The technical development—formulating the problem as a CQP—is a solid engineering step, enabling efficient computation. However, the derivation feels somewhat mechanical; the connection to posterior regularization is mentioned but not deeply explored in terms of theoretical justification for *why* SACs are a particularly effective prior. The claim of a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM and a 29% improvement over a recent state-of-the-art approach [12] is promising. However, the details in Section 7.2.2 are sparse.  The tables present aggregate results across households, making it difficult to assess the consistency of the improvement.  Furthermore, the performance gain using "true" total consumption (TTC) as the constraint (an unrealistic scenario) feels like a best-case analysis that doesn't fully represent the practical challenges. The dependence of performance on the accuracy of the "national total consumption" (NTC) estimate, a practical consideration, should be discussed more thoroughly.  The statement about computing time scaling similarly across methods is helpful but doesn’t address the computational cost of estimating the required prior information (µi0).

The paper’s clarity is acceptable, but the presentation is dense. Several sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations and illustrative examples. The notation is reasonable, but the jump between equations (3) and (5) could be smoother.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a practical problem (identifiability in energy disaggregation).
*   Provides a computationally efficient solution (CQP).
*   Demonstrates performance improvements on a real-world dataset.
*   Connects to existing literature on prior incorporation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty – feels like an incremental extension of existing techniques.
*   Significance not fully established – performance gains need more robust analysis.
*   Dependence on accurate prior knowledge (µi0) not thoroughly discussed.
*   Sparse details in experimental results, making it hard to assess consistency.
*   Theoretical justification for SACs as a prior could be stronger.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a practical and potentially useful technique for energy disaggregation.
*   The CQP formulation enables efficient computation.
*   The experimental results show promising improvements over existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty is limited, and the contribution feels incremental.
*   The significance is not convincingly demonstrated – the performance gains need more rigorous evaluation.
*   The paper lacks a deeper theoretical analysis of why SACs are effective.
*   The experimental details are insufficient to fully assess the results.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While technically sound and well-motivated, it doesn't present a breakthrough contribution that warrants inclusion in NIPS. The incremental nature of the work, combined with the limitations in the experimental validation, does not justify acceptance at this highly competitive conference. I would encourage the authors to strengthen the theoretical justification, provide a more detailed analysis of the experimental results, and more clearly articulate the advantages of SACs over existing approaches. A more comprehensive comparison to other constraint-based methods, beyond AFAMAP, would also be beneficial.
