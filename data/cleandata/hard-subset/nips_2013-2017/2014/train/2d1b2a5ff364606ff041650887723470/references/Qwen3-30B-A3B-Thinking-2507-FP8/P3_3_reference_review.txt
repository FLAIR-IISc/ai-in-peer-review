

# Peer Review: "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in blind source separation (BSS) - the identifiability problem - by introducing Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs). The authors propose incorporating domain knowledge about expected total signal aggregates (e.g., daily energy consumption of appliances) into the BSS framework, transforming the optimization problem into a convex quadratic program that can be efficiently solved. The method shows significant improvements on energy disaggregation tasks, reducing normalized disaggregation error by 45% compared to baseline AFHMM.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper effectively motivates SACs by highlighting the identifiability problem in energy disaggregation, where "franken-appliance" outputs would undermine user trust. This is a compelling real-world application where domain knowledge is readily available.

2. **Theoretical Connection to Posterior Regularization:** The authors successfully connect their SAC approach to the posterior regularization framework (Section 6), providing a solid theoretical foundation for their method. This bridges their practical contribution with a well-established theoretical framework.

3. **Compelling Experimental Results:** The energy disaggregation results are impressive, showing a 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error over baseline AFHMM and 29% improvement over state-of-the-art AFAMAP. The use of both synthetic data (Section 7.1) and real-world HES data (Section 7.2) provides robust validation.

4. **Computational Efficiency:** The convex quadratic programming approach is computationally efficient (polynomial time complexity) and scales similarly to existing methods, making it practical for real-world deployment.

**Weaknesses and Critical Points:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Guarantees:** While the paper develops a convex relaxation approach, it lacks theoretical analysis of the relaxation's quality. The authors don't provide bounds on the error introduced by the relaxation or prove that the relaxed solution is close to the true optimum. This is particularly concerning given the identifiability challenges in BSS.

2. **Parameter Selection Concerns:** The paper uses λi = 1 for all experiments without tuning, despite mentioning that λi could be tuned via cross-validation. Without showing results with tuned parameters, it's unclear how sensitive the method is to this critical hyperparameter. The results with "true" total consumption (TTC) in Table 1 are misleadingly optimistic, as TTC would never be available in practice.

3. **Incomplete Comparison to Relevant Work:** The related work section (Section 2) misses important recent developments in energy disaggregation that use different constraint mechanisms. For example, the paper doesn't compare to approaches that incorporate appliance power profiles or duration constraints, which could be seen as complementary to SACs. The claim that SACs "are general across a wide variety of domains" lacks concrete examples beyond energy disaggregation.

4. **Practical Limitations of SACs:** The paper doesn't adequately address the practical limitations of SACs. Figure 1 shows that performance degrades as the specified aggregate values deviate from the true values, but the paper doesn't discuss how to obtain accurate aggregate constraints in practice or how to handle cases where domain knowledge is limited or uncertain.

5. **Overstated Significance Claims:** While the 45% improvement is impressive, the paper overstates the significance by claiming this is "a significant improvement (29%) in performance compared to a recent state-of-the-art approach [12]." The 29% improvement is actually compared to AFAMAP (not the state-of-the-art), and the paper doesn't clearly establish that AFAMAP is the current state-of-the-art for this specific energy disaggregation task.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound paper with strong practical motivation and compelling results in energy disaggregation. The SAC concept is novel and well-motivated, and the connection to posterior regularization provides theoretical grounding. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the theoretical limitations of the convex relaxation, providing results with tuned hyperparameters, and offering a more nuanced discussion of the practical constraints on using SACs.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the energy disaggregation literature and demonstrates how domain knowledge can be effectively incorporated into probabilistic models. While the method is specific to AFHMMs, the SAC concept could potentially be extended to other BSS frameworks.

**Recommendation:**

Accept. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the energy disaggregation literature with practical significance. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear improvements over strong baselines. The limitations noted above are important for the authors to address in the final version but don't undermine the core contribution. The results are sufficiently compelling to warrant publication at NIPS. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of SACs' practical limitations and better justification for the λi = 1 choice, but these are refinements rather than fundamental flaws.
