This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS) in time series data, focusing on the challenging application of energy disaggregation. The core issue in this domain is the inherent non-identifiability of the separated sources, which makes it difficult to recover meaningful component signals (e.g., individual appliances). The authors build upon the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a standard model for this task. While previous work has sought to improve identifiability by imposing local, structural constraints on the HMM state dynamics (e.g., AFAMAP, which allows only one appliance to change state at a time), this paper introduces a conceptually novel type of constraint.

The main contribution is the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). Instead of constraining local state transitions, SACs impose a global constraint on the entire time series for each source. The constraint encourages the sum (or aggregate) of each source signal over a long time window to be close to a known prior value. For energy disaggregation, this is a highly intuitive form of domain knowledge: we often have a good estimate of the total daily energy consumption of an appliance type (e.g., from national surveys), even if we don't know its precise usage pattern. Because these global constraints break the Markov property, making standard inference algorithms like Viterbi inapplicable, the authors formulate MAP inference as a penalized optimization problem. They then relax this intractable integer quadratic program into a convex quadratic program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The method is evaluated on a toy dataset and a challenging, low-frequency real-world energy dataset, showing substantial improvements over both the standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP method.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** The paper's primary strength is the introduction of a new class of constraints for BSS. The idea of using global, aggregate information as a prior is a bold and elegant departure from the existing literature, which has largely focused on local, dynamic constraints. This provides a fundamentally new and powerful way to inject domain knowledge into these models, directly targeting the identifiability problem from a different angle. This is the kind of creative thinking that can open up new research directions.

2.  **Intuitive and Practical Approach:** The proposed SACs are not just mathematically convenient; they correspond to a very natural and often available form of real-world prior knowledge. The energy disaggregation example is perfectly chosen to highlight this: knowing a refrigerator's approximate daily consumption is far more realistic than knowing its exact minute-by-minute state transitions. This makes the approach highly practical and likely to be adopted in applied settings.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of its idea with compelling experiments. The 45% error reduction over the baseline AFHMM and the 29% reduction over the strong AFAMAP baseline on real mains data are significant. The inclusion of an "oracle" experiment using the true total consumption (TTC) smartly illustrates the upper bound of the potential benefit, while the results using realistic national total consumption (NTC) priors confirm the method's practical utility.

4.  **Generality:** Although motivated by and tested on energy disaggregation, the concept of SACs is general. It could be readily applied to other BSS domains where aggregate source properties are known, such as in computational biology (e.g., total gene expression over an experiment) or audio processing (e.g., expected total duration of a speaker's voice in a recording).

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Methodological Adaptation vs. Innovation:** The technical approach for solving the inference problem—formulating a CQP relaxation—is not new and directly follows the strategy used in the AFAMAP paper [12]. While this is a pragmatic and sound choice, it means the paper's novelty lies entirely in the problem formulation and the new constraint type, rather than in the optimization machinery itself. From my perspective, this is a minor weakness, as the conceptual contribution is the most important part.

2.  **Limited Exploration of the Model:** The experiments are convincing but limited in scope. The regularization hyperparameters (`lambda_i`) are fixed rather than tuned, and the model parameters (`theta`) are assumed to be learned in a separate supervised step. An integrated, unsupervised learning approach incorporating SACs would be a more complete contribution, though the authors acknowledge this as future work. However, the current work successfully isolates and validates the core contribution, which is the value of the constraint during inference.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a conceptually novel, intuitive, and powerful class of global constraints for AFHMMs.
*   The idea represents a significant departure from prior work and opens a new direction for incorporating domain knowledge.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance with substantial improvements on a challenging real-world problem.
*   The core idea is simple, elegant, and likely to inspire follow-up research in other domains.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The optimization method used to solve the inference problem is adapted from previous work, not developed from scratch.
*   The experimental validation is not exhaustive (e.g., lacks hyperparameter tuning and an unsupervised learning formulation).

**Recommendation:** Strong Accept

This paper presents a bold and highly novel idea that effectively addresses a key limitation in a well-established model class. The concept of Signal Aggregate Constraints is a significant conceptual contribution that is both intuitive and demonstrably powerful. While the optimization method is not new and the experimental validation could be more complete, these are secondary concerns. The paper's main value lies in its fresh perspective and the new direction it charts for incorporating non-local priors into latent variable models. This is precisely the type of creative, high-impact work that NIPS should champion. It prioritizes a powerful new idea over incremental methodological refinement, and its success will likely encourage others to think more broadly about the types of domain knowledge we can embed in our models.
