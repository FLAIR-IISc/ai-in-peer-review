This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a novel form of regularization for Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs), with a compelling application to energy disaggregation. The core idea is to leverage prior domain knowledge about the expected total value of a source signal over a period of time. For instance, in energy disaggregation, we often have a good estimate of the total daily energy consumption of an appliance like a refrigerator or a toaster. This global, sequence-level constraint helps to resolve the inherent identifiability problem in blind source separation, where separated signals can be nonsensical mixtures of the true sources. Because these constraints are non-Markovian, they break standard dynamic programming inference algorithms. The authors overcome this by formulating MAP inference as a penalized objective function, which they then relax into a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP) that can be solved efficiently. This work builds upon the AFHMM framework for BSS and energy disaggregation (e.g., Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012), but its contribution is conceptually distinct from prior work that focused on local constraints, such as limiting state transitions (AFAMAP) or modeling state durations (semi-Markov models).

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its originality and the elegant simplicity of its core idea. The introduction of signal aggregate constraints is a genuinely novel approach to incorporating prior knowledge into this class of models. While much previous work has focused on the micro-structure of the time series (e.g., how states transition from one-time step to the next), this paper takes a bold step back and imposes a macro-level constraint on the entire sequence. This is a powerful conceptual shift that proves to be highly effective. It opens up a new and promising direction for regularizing latent variable sequence models, applicable to any domain where such aggregate knowledge is available.

The significance of the work is clearly demonstrated through strong empirical results on a challenging and important real-world problem. The use of low-frequency energy data from the HES dataset is particularly noteworthy, as such data is often considered more difficult for disaggregation than the high-frequency data used in other studies. The proposed AFHMM+SAC model achieves a dramatic reduction in error compared to the standard AFHMM and, more impressively, a substantial improvement (29-33% NDE reduction) over AFAMAP, a strong and recent baseline. The fact that these gains are achieved with comparable computational overhead makes the method practically compelling.

The paper is also very well-written and clear. The motivation is well-argued, the model is presented logically, and the connection to the posterior regularization framework provides a nice theoretical grounding for the penalized objective function.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper is strong, and its weaknesses are relatively minor, particularly when weighing them against the novelty of the central idea.

1.  The methodological validation could be more thorough. The regularization hyperparameter `λ` is set to 1 for all experiments without justification or a sensitivity analysis. While the authors note that it could be tuned via cross-validation, demonstrating this and showing how performance varies with `λ` would have strengthened the empirical claims. However, the fact that strong results are obtained even with this simple setting suggests the method may be reasonably robust.

2.  The paper solves a relaxed version of the MAP inference problem, resulting in fractional state assignments. It is not explicitly stated how these fractional values are converted into the discrete state sequences needed to calculate the evaluation metrics (presumably by taking the argmax at each time step). While this is a common step for such relaxation-based methods, explicitly stating it would improve clarity and reproducibility.

3.  The generalization to multiple constraints over different time periods (e.g., day vs. night) is mentioned as a straightforward extension. While this is believable, a small experiment demonstrating its utility would have been a valuable addition, showcasing the flexibility of the framework.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel, intuitive, and powerful type of constraint for AFHMMs.
*   The idea is a bold departure from conventional local constraints, opening a new direction for research.
*   Achieves state-of-the-art results on a significant real-world problem, with substantial gains over strong baselines.
*   The method is computationally efficient and practical.
*   The paper is clear, well-motivated, and technically sound.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental validation is not exhaustive (e.g., lack of hyperparameter tuning).
*   Some minor details about the implementation (e.g., handling the output of the CQP relaxation) are omitted.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It presents a genuinely new and impactful idea. The concept of signal aggregate constraints is both elegant and effective, and the authors demonstrate its value convincingly. This is precisely the kind of work—a novel approach that challenges conventional thinking and demonstrates a clear advance on an important problem—that should be highlighted at NIPS. The weaknesses are minor points of methodological polish that do not detract from the core contribution and can be easily addressed in a final version. This work is likely to be influential and inspire follow-up research in energy disaggregation and other BSS domains.
