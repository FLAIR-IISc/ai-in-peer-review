Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles blind source separation in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs), motivated by energy disaggregation (NILM). The central idea is to inject domain knowledge in the form of signal aggregate constraints (SACs): soft quadratic penalties that encourage each source’s total energy over a period to match a pre-specified value µi0. This is natural for NILM where appliance totals are often known up to a range. The authors show (i) a penalized MAP objective equivalent to a posterior-regularized AFHMM, and (ii) a convex quadratic program (CQP) for a relaxed version of the MAP problem by introducing pairwise variables H for transitions and relaxing binary constraints to [0,1]. Empirically, on the UK HES dataset (ultra-low frequency) and a toy example, AFHMM+SAC improves normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) over AFHMM and AFAMAP [Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12].

This connects well to prior frameworks for incorporating constraints into latent-variable models—Posterior Regularization (Ganchev et al., JMLR’10), Generalized Expectation (Mann & McCallum), and “learning from measurements” (Liang et al., ICML’09). It also relates to NILM-specific constraints (interleaving constraints in AFAMAP; duration constraints via semi-Markov models; k-segment constraints). The novelty here is a global, cross-time aggregate constraint aligned with end-user goals (per-appliance totals), instantiated in AFHMM via a tractable convex relaxation.

Quality:
- Methodology: The formulation is sound. The objective after relaxation is concave in (S,H) given PSD quadratic terms, with linear constraints enforcing local HMM consistency; using CVX to solve a CQP is reasonable at the scales reported. The posterior-regularization derivation is correct and clarifies the role of λ as Lagrange multipliers.
- Inference details: Some important implementation details are missing. The paper does not state how fractional solutions are post-processed into discrete states (or whether fractional S is acceptable since only totals are evaluated). It also does not specify how σt^2, transition matrices, and priors are estimated, beyond k-means for µ. Runtime claims are plausible but depend heavily on CVX settings and problem sizes; more concrete complexity and solver details would improve reproducibility.
- Empirics: Results show consistent improvements on both synthetic and real data, including with true mains where unmodeled loads are present. Statistical significance is reported via paired t-tests. However, the sensitivity analysis is limited: λ is fixed to 1 and only a toy study varies µ0. A thorough sweep over λ and robustness to mis-specified µ0 on real data are missing. Comparisons to stronger baselines (e.g., HSMM-based NILM, interleaved non-homogeneous FHMM) and to “AFAMAP + SAC” (a natural combination) would strengthen the case.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable and the vectorized CQP derivation is helpful. That said:
- Some notation is dense and could be clarified (e.g., shapes of Vt, ut, and the construction of µ∗i; make explicit that the objective is concave).
- Explain the relationship between (δi) and (λi) and how to select λi in practice.
- Describe rounding or acceptance of fractional S. If fractional, clarify fairness of comparison to discrete AFAMAP and AFHMM.
- Several references are incomplete or missing years; proofreading would help.

Originality:
The idea of constraining posterior expectations is not new; the paper is an instantiation of posterior regularization/measurement learning to AFHMMs with a specific, practically meaningful global aggregate. The CQP relaxation with transition variables H is a known trick in MAP relaxations for HMMs/factor graphs, but the particular combination with additive Gaussian emissions across chains and global aggregate penalties appears novel and useful. The extension to multiple time-window SACs is a natural, potentially impactful generalization.

Significance:
For NILM, constraining totals matches user-facing objectives and yields substantial gains on a challenging, low-frequency, population-representative dataset. The approach is general and could transfer to other BSS tasks where aggregate information is available (audio, biology). However, broader impact would be bolstered by:
- Demonstrations on additional domains, or
- Theoretical insight on how mismatch between µ0 and true totals affects recovery, and
- Scalability to longer horizons and more appliances without resorting to general-purpose convex solvers.

Specific strengths:
- Simple, principled, and well-motivated constraint class aligned with application needs.
- Convex relaxation enabling efficient inference with global non-Markovian penalties.
- Clear connection to posterior regularization.
- Empirical gains over established baselines on real, low-frequency data; significance tests provided.
- Straightforward extension to time-localized SACs.

Specific weaknesses and suggestions:
- Sensitivity/robustness: Vary λi and perturb µ0 on real data; report performance curves and show robustness regimes. Consider household-specific vs national priors.
- Baselines: Compare to HSMM-based NILM and interleaved non-homogeneous FHMM; include AFAMAP+SAC to test complementarity.
- Metrics: Report event/timing metrics (e.g., F1 for on/off transitions) to assess whether smoothing from convex relaxation harms temporal fidelity.
- Discreteness: State how fractional S is handled; if accepting fractional, consider projecting to nearest feasible discrete sequence and report impact.
- Scalability: Provide problem sizes, iterations, solver details, and time vs T, I, K; explore specialized solvers exploiting block structure.
- Theory: Even a simple bound or characterization illustrating monotonic degradation with ||µ0−true|| would increase confidence.
- Clarity and references: Clean up notation, add pseudo-code, and fix the bibliography.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a real challenge in NILM with a practical, general idea.
- Solid modeling and convex relaxation; principled PR derivation.
- Demonstrated improvements on a challenging dataset with significance tests.
- Likely to be adopted by practitioners; easy to combine with other constraints.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited sensitivity analysis and ablations; no combination with strongest structural constraints.
- Missing details on discretization and solver/scalability.
- Comparisons could be broader; theoretical guarantees are absent.
- Some presentation and reference issues.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The contribution is incremental relative to PR frameworks but is a well-executed, practically impactful instantiation with convincing empirical gains in a relevant domain. Strengthening the experimental analysis and clarifying implementation details would make it a solid accept.
