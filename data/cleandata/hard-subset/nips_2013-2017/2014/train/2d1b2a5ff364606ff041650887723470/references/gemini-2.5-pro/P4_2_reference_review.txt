### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), with a specific focus on energy disaggregation. The authors identify the core challenge in this domain as the inherent unidentifiability of the underdetermined system, where a single aggregate time series must be separated into multiple constituent source signals. To mitigate this, the paper proposes incorporating domain-specific prior knowledge into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a common model for this task.

The main contribution is the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). These constraints enforce the prior belief that the total aggregate value of a source signal over a period (e.g., the total daily energy consumption of an appliance) should be close to a known expected value. This idea is formalized by adding a quadratic penalty term to the AFHMM's maximum a posteriori (MAP) inference objective function, creating a new model dubbed AFHMM+SAC.

Because the SACs are a function of the entire state sequence for each HMM chain, they are non-Markovian and break the assumptions required for standard dynamic programming algorithms like Viterbi. To overcome this, the authors formulate MAP inference as an integer quadratic program and then relax the integrality constraints, resulting in a convex quadratic program (CQP) that can be solved efficiently. The authors also show a connection between their penalized objective and the posterior regularization framework of Ganchev et al. (2010).

The proposed method is evaluated against the standard AFHMM and a state-of-the-art constrained model, AFAMAP (Kolter and Jaakkola, 2012), on both a toy dataset and a challenging real-world energy consumption dataset (HES). The results demonstrate that AFHMM+SAC significantly outperforms both baselines in terms of normalized disaggregation error and a newly proposed signal aggregate error metric, without incurring a significant increase in computational time for inference.

### Quality and Technical Soundness

The paper is technically sound. The motivation for introducing constraints to address the identifiability problem in AFHMMs is well-established. The proposed SAC is an intuitive and sensible form of prior knowledge for many BSS applications.

The primary technical challenge is performing inference in a model with global, non-Markovian constraints. The authors' approach of reformulating the MAP problem as an integer quadratic program and then solving its convex relaxation is a valid and well-justified method. The matrices in the quadratic objective are shown to be positive semidefinite, guaranteeing that the relaxed problem is indeed a convex QP. This approach follows a similar pattern to that used in prior work like AFAMAP, demonstrating a competent application of established optimization techniques to a new problem structure.

The experimental evaluation is thorough. It includes a synthetic data experiment to demonstrate the model's robustness to misspecification of the constraint values. More importantly, the evaluation on the real-world HES dataset is convincing. The authors test their model in two scenarios: one with a synthetic aggregate signal (sum of known appliances) and another, more realistic scenario using actual mains readings, which may contain unmodeled loads. The consistent and significant performance improvements in both scenarios strongly support the paper's claims. The metrics used (NDE and SAE) are appropriate for evaluating the quality of disaggregation.

A minor weakness is the brief discussion of the CQP relaxation. The paper does not explicitly state how the continuous solutions from the CQP (where `Sitk` is in [0, 1]) are converted back to discrete state assignments. One assumes a rounding procedure, but clarity on this point would improve reproducibility. Furthermore, an analysis of the quality of the relaxation (i.e., how often the relaxed solution is integral or close to integral) would have strengthened the technical presentation.

### Clarity

The paper is very well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The introduction provides excellent motivation for the problem using the "franken-appliance" analogy, which effectively communicates the need for identifiability constraints. The problem setting and model descriptions in Sections 3 and 4 are clear, with consistent notation. The derivation of the CQP in Section 5, while dense with notation, is logically presented. The relationship to posterior regularization in Section 6 provides useful theoretical context. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly with tables and figures that are easy to interpret. The paper provides enough detail for an expert to understand the contribution and its implementation.

### Originality and Significance

The primary contribution—the concept of Signal Aggregate Constraints—is novel. While other methods have proposed constraining AFHMMs to improve identifiability, such as limiting state transitions (AFAMAP) or modeling state durations (semi-Markov models), this work introduces a new and complementary type of constraint based on a signal's global properties. This is a simple but powerful idea. The formulation of this constraint within a CQP framework, while building on techniques used by others (e.g., Kolter and Jaakkola, 2012), is a novel application to this specific constraint type.

The significance of the work is high, particularly for the application domain of energy disaggregation. The paper demonstrates substantial improvements over a recent state-of-the-art method on a challenging, low-frequency dataset. The reported 29% reduction in NDE compared to AFAMAP on real mains data is a significant advance. The idea of using aggregate constraints is general and could be readily applied to other BSS problems where similar prior knowledge is available (e.g., total word counts in topic modeling, total activity levels in biometric signal separation). The work is therefore likely to be of interest to both practitioners in energy disaggregation and researchers working on structured probabilistic models.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Novelty:** Proposes a new, intuitive, and effective class of constraints (SACs) for AFHMMs to address the critical problem of identifiability in BSS.
2.  **Significance:** Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the important and challenging problem of energy disaggregation, with significant quantitative improvements over existing methods. The proposed idea is generalizable to other domains.
3.  **Technical Soundness:** The work is technically solid, providing a well-justified and efficient inference algorithm (CQP relaxation) for the proposed model. The experimental validation is rigorous and convincing.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to understand.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
1.  **Limited Technical Detail:** The paper could provide more detail on the CQP relaxation, specifically on recovering discrete states from the continuous solution and the quality of the approximation.
2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity to the key hyperparameter `λ` is not analyzed. The paper simply sets `λ=1`, leaving open the question of how to tune this parameter in practice and how robust the model is to its setting.
3.  **Dependence on Prior Quality:** The method's performance is inherently tied to the quality of the prior aggregate values (`µi0`). The gap between results using national averages (NTC) and an oracle (TTC) suggests a strong dependency, which could be a limitation in applications with poor-quality priors. This is an inherent property, not a flaw, but warrants discussion.

Overall, the strengths of this paper—its novelty, significance, and clear presentation—far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a valuable and impactful contribution to the field.
