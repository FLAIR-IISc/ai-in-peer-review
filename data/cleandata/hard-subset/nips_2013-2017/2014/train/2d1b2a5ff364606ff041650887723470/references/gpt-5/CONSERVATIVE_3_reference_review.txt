Summary
The paper addresses blind source separation in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs): priors that the time-summed contribution of each source should be close to specified totals. The authors instantiate this in energy disaggregation, where appliance-level daily or period totals are often approximately known from domain knowledge. They formulate MAP inference with these constraints, show an equivalence to posterior regularization, and derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) by relaxing the binary HMM variables and introducing transition variables. They solve the resulting CQP with CVX. Empirically, on toy data and on the HES energy dataset, they report significant improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012) in terms of normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE), with similar computational time. They also include an oracle experiment using true totals (TTC) to show an upper bound on the potential gains.

Relation to prior work
The approach is a specific instance of incorporating side constraints into latent variable models, closely related to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2010), GE, and measurement-based learning. In NILM, several works constrain AFHMMs via one-switch constraints (AFAMAP), semi-Markov durations, and other priors; the present contribution is to penalize deviations between predicted and expected aggregate energy per appliance. The optimization relaxation is in line with previous convex relaxations for AFHMMs.

Strengths
- Simple and intuitive prior: aggregate totals are a natural and often available signal in many BSS applications, especially NILM.
- Solid optimization formulation: the concave quadratic objective plus linear constraints yields a convex optimization after relaxation; the relation to posterior regularization is clear and correct.
- Empirical gains on a realistic, challenging dataset (HES) at very low sampling rates, with consistent improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP on NDE/SAE.
- The technique seems modular and could complement other constraints.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- The relaxation yields fractional state and transition variables. The paper does not describe how these are mapped back to valid discrete state sequences, or whether evaluation uses fractional reconstructions directly. For NILM interpretability, discrete states and transition consistency matter; the current evaluation focuses on signal error only.
- Hyperparameters λi are fixed to 1 throughout. No sensitivity analysis or principled selection (e.g., cross-validation, hierarchical priors) is provided on real data. The toy example shows degradation with mis-specified µ0, but real-data robustness is not demonstrated.
- The aggregate targets µi0 are computed as “national total consumption” (NTC) from training data, but it is unclear whether this is per-household, per-appliance seasonalized priors, or a global average. The magnitude of mismatch between NTC and TTC on test days is not quantified.
- Baselines are limited. Stronger competitors (e.g., HSMM/semi-Markov models, nonparametric HSMM, appliance-type priors) are cited but not compared experimentally. Synergy with AFAMAP (one-switch) plus SAC is not explored.
- Scalability claims are light. Solving a large CQP with CVX (interior-point) per day with variables O(TΣKi + TΣKi^2) can be expensive; reporting “similar time” to AFAMAP is surprising and needs details (hardware, solver settings, problem sizes). No experiments on longer horizons (e.g., weeks/months) or many appliances.
- Experimental details are sparse: how θ (π, P, σt) are estimated; whether σt is fixed; data preprocessing; state counts per appliance fixed to 3 without justification; whether appliances absent on test days are handled; how unseen appliances in mains are modeled. Statistical testing is mentioned, but exact numbers, effect sizes, and variance are missing in the text we have.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly readable, but there are numerous formatting issues, typos, and incomplete references (years/pages missing). Some notation is heavy; guidance on dimensions and intuition around the convexity would help readers.
- Tables and figures are referenced but not visible here; ensure captions clearly specify experimental setup, data splits, and units.

Originality
- Conceptually, SACs are an application-specific instantiation of posterior regularization/measurement learning with aggregate moment constraints. The specific aggregate (sum over time of expected appliance power) is natural but not fundamentally novel. The CQP relaxation is a straightforward extension of prior convex relaxations for AFHMMs.
- The main novelty lies in identifying a practically useful class of aggregate constraints for NILM and demonstrating empirical gains.

Significance
- For NILM practitioners, the idea may be useful when reliable aggregate priors are available. However, applicability depends on the availability and accuracy of µi0 and on robustness to misspecification.
- As a general BSS contribution, the methodological advance over posterior regularization is incremental.

Questions for the authors
1. How do you obtain discrete sequences from the relaxed solution, if at all? Does rounding degrade performance? Is H consistent with S after rounding?
2. How are λi chosen in practice? Please report performance sensitivity to λi on HES.
3. How is NTC computed (per-household vs global; seasonality; per-day variation)? What is the empirical distribution of |NTC − TTC| and how does performance vary with this mismatch on real data?
4. Runtime/scalability: problem sizes (T, I, Ki), number of variables/constraints, solver tolerances, hardware, and wall-clock times vs AFAMAP/AFHMM. Any specialized solver beyond CVX?
5. Can SACs be combined with AFAMAP or duration models, and what is the joint gain?
6. How do you handle appliances absent on a given test day, or unseen loads in mains data? Do SACs spuriously force nonzero energy for absent devices?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a principled scheme to set λi (e.g., cross-validation or empirical Bayes), and a sensitivity plot on HES.
- Include stronger baselines and a combined model (AFAMAP + SAC).
- Report discrete-sequence metrics (e.g., per-appliance on/off F1) and discuss interpretability.
- Add robustness analyses to mis-specified µi0 on real data; consider hierarchical priors on µi0 rather than point targets.
- Offer a scalable specialized solver or decomposition method; demonstrate scaling to longer horizons.
- Improve references and presentation; ensure reproducibility (code or detailed pseudocode).

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper presents a reasonable and practically motivated constraint and an adequate convex relaxation, with promising gains on NILM. However, methodological novelty over established posterior regularization is modest, the evaluation is limited in baselines and robustness, and key practical details (hyperparameter selection, rounding, scalability) are underdeveloped. Strengthening these aspects could make this a solid contribution.
