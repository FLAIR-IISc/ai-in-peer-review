Summary
The paper tackles identifiability in blind source separation for additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs): priors on the total energy (or other aggregates) of each latent source over a time window. The authors (1) formalize SACs as quadratic penalties on per-chain aggregate deviations, (2) show the resulting MAP objective can be optimized via a convex quadratic program (CQP) after relaxing binary variables and introducing auxiliary transition variables, and (3) connect the formulation to posterior regularization. Empirically, on a large low-frequency energy disaggregation dataset (UK HES) and a toy problem, AFHMM+SAC improves normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) versus AFHMM and the AFAMAP baseline, with reported 29–45% reductions in NDE and similar runtime.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on AFHMMs and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12), and it is positioned among general frameworks for incorporating constraints (posterior regularization, generalized expectation, measurement-based learning). The paper’s novelty lies in identifying aggregate-sum constraints as a practically available and task-aligned prior, and in deriving an efficient convex relaxation tailored to AFHMMs with such constraints. The connection to posterior regularization is appropriate and correctly derived, framing the penalty as a log prior/regularizer.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The relaxation and convexity arguments are largely sound. After introducing transition variables Hit and relaxing integrality, the objective is linear in H and concave quadratic in S (negative quadratic forms with PSD matrices), with linear constraints; maximizing a concave quadratic under linear constraints is a convex program. This is a neat and useful observation. However, the paper should more explicitly state how the relaxed solution is converted to usable disaggregations: do the authors output x̂it = sum_k Sitk μik (which is natural and sufficient for NDE/SAE), or do they round to discrete states? If rounding is needed, what is the procedure and does it affect feasibility with SACs?
- Empirical evaluation: The HES experiments are valuable given their scale and low sampling rate. Improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP are statistically significant per the reported paired t-tests. That said, some details reduce reproducibility and leave questions about robustness:
  - Hyperparameters: λi are fixed to 1 throughout; no cross-validation or sensitivity analysis is provided. Since λ controls the strength of SACs—central to the method—this omission is important. The toy experiment shows qualitative robustness to mismatch in μ0, but a systematic sensitivity study (varying λ and SAC misspecification) on real data would strengthen claims.
  - Baselines: AFAMAP is an appropriate and strong baseline, but comparisons to duration-constrained FHMMs/HSMMs (e.g., [10,11]) or other recent NILM methods would provide a fuller picture.
  - Computational claims: The claim that runtimes are “similar” to AFHMM/AFAMAP is surprising for a generic CQP solved with CVX. Please report precise timing, problem sizes (I, T, Ki), solver tolerances, and memory usage. Also discuss scaling beyond the tested regime.
  - Data/setting: For the “with mains” case, the mains include devices not modeled; how do SACs interact with unmodeled load? Does regularizing to national averages bias modeled appliances to “absorb” unknowns?
- Theory/scope: While the posterior regularization connection is sound, there is no analysis of the relaxation gap, nor conditions under which the relaxed solution matches the integral one. Not strictly necessary for a practical paper, but even empirical integrality rates would be informative.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The notation in Section 5 is heavy but precise. The convexity intuition could be highlighted earlier to aid readers. A few practical details are missing: how μi, πi, and transitions are estimated (beyond k-means for μi), whether Sit are constrained on a per-time simplex, and the exact post-processing of relaxed solutions. Some references appear incomplete in the provided text, which should be fixed. Including a brief pseudo-code or algorithm box for the end-to-end pipeline would improve reproducibility.

Originality
Moderately novel. Aggregate constraints have been considered in broader frameworks (GE, posterior regularization, measurement-based learning), but applying aggregate-sum constraints to AFHMMs for NILM and deriving a tailored CQP with auxiliary variables is a useful and nontrivial contribution. The idea is simple but well-motivated and practically impactful.

Significance
For NILM, practitioners care deeply about per-appliance totals; SACs directly encode this target and yield substantial gains on a challenging dataset. The method is likely to influence subsequent NILM work and may generalize to other BSS domains where aggregate priors are available (e.g., audio source energy, read counts in biology). The reliance on credible aggregate priors is both a strength (easy to obtain in NILM) and a limitation (availability in other domains varies).

Pros
- Addresses a core identifiability issue with a practically available prior.
- Elegant convex relaxation with a clear connection to posterior regularization.
- Demonstrated, statistically significant gains on realistic large-scale NILM data.
- Computationally feasible for moderate I, T using off-the-shelf solvers.

Cons
- Limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and relaxation rounding.
- Comparisons could be broader (duration models, other NILM baselines).
- Sparse details on solver setup and scaling; “similar runtime” claim needs substantiation.
- Assumes reasonable μ0 priors; robustness to mis-specified or household-varying priors needs deeper evaluation.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Detail how you obtain discrete or continuous disaggregations from relaxed S and H; report integrality or rounding performance.
- Provide λ selection procedure and sensitivity plots on HES. How do results change if μ0 are biased by 20–50%?
- Expand comparisons to at least one HSMM-based or duration-constrained method.
- Report exact problem sizes, solver settings, and runtime/memory; comment on scaling beyond T≈O(10^2–10^3).
- Consider evaluating multiple interval SACs (day/night) to showcase the generalization mentioned in Section 4.2.
- Discuss how to set μ0 when “unknown” loads are substantial, and whether a slack or background chain could help.

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-motivated paper with a clear technical contribution and convincing empirical gains on an important application. With stronger analysis of hyperparameters/robustness and clearer implementation details, it would be a strong NeurIPS paper. As is, it meets the quality, clarity, originality, and significance criteria reasonably well and merits serious consideration for acceptance.
