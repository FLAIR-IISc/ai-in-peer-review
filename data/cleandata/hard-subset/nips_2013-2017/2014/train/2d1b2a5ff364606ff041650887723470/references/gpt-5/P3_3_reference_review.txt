Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses blind source separation of a single aggregated time series using additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs): soft quadratic penalties that encourage the total energy (or other aggregate) of each latent source over a time window to match a known or estimated value. The authors derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) for MAP inference in a relaxed AFHMM with SACs by augmenting with edge variables (H) to linearize transitions and leveraging the fact that the quadratic terms induced by the Gaussian likelihood and SACs are concave (negative semidefinite in the objective). They also connect the formulation to the posterior regularization framework. Experiments on a toy dataset and on the UK HES low-frequency energy dataset show improved normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) compared to a standard AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12), with similar reported runtimes.

This work sits within a rich NILM literature (Hart; reviews by Ziefman & Roth; Zoha et al.) and relates to FHMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan), AFAMAP (single-switch constraint), duration/semi-Markov constraints (Johnson & Willsky; Kim et al.), and posterior regularization/GE/measurement learning (Ganchev et al.; Mann & McCallum; Liang et al.). The proposed SACs are also thematically related to aggregate-constraint reasoning in probabilistic databases but applied here to structured time-series models.

Strengths
- Simple, general prior: SACs encode widely available prior knowledge (e.g., appliance daily energy) and can be applied across domains. The extension to multiple windows is natural.
- Convexity and computational approach: The relaxed MAP becomes a CQP with a concave quadratic objective and linear constraints. This is appealing, and, because the feasible set per chain is the HMM flow polytope (a totally unimodular network polytope), the relaxation is likely tight; the maximizer should occur at extreme points corresponding to integral node/edge marginals (a point the paper could make explicit).
- Posterior regularization connection: The equivalence between the penalized objective and PR with second-moment constraints is clean and situates the work within a principled framework.
- Empirical gains on challenging low-frequency HES data: Improvements over AFAMAP/AFHMM on both NDE and SAE, including significance testing, indicate practical value.

Weaknesses and questions
- Rounding/integrality: The paper solves a relaxed problem but does not discuss whether/how fractional S, H are mapped to discrete sequences. Given the network-flow structure of HMMs, an explicit argument that the relaxation is tight (or a rounding scheme with guarantees) would strengthen the method.
- Assumptions and robustness: The toy study shows degradation when µ0 is misspecified, but the real-data sensitivity analysis is limited. How sensitive are results to:
  - errors in µ0 (e.g., appliance heterogeneity across houses),
  - miscalibrated λi (set to 1 uniformly), and
  - the choice of using 3 states for all appliances?
  A more thorough sweep or cross-validation would increase confidence.
- Fairness of baselines and hyperparameters: AFAMAP has a key hyperparameter (switching probability/penalty). Were these tuned comparably? Fixing λi=1 without tuning may either understate or overstate SAC performance. Please clarify tuning protocols for all methods.
- Use of national totals (NTC): NTCs are computed from training days across all households. In realistic deployment, one might only have population-level priors (as suggested), but NTCs estimated from the same dataset risk optimistic bias. Report performance when µ0 is taken from external sources or from disjoint households.
- Unmodeled loads in mains data: With mains input, the FHMM covers only a subset of appliances; SACs may pull modeled appliances to meet µ0, potentially absorbing unmodeled consumption. An analysis of residuals and per-appliance bias would be helpful.
- Scalability and solver details: Relying on CVX/SDPT3/SeDuMi can be slow for long sequences; a custom first-order solver exploiting block structure (per-time Vt, per-chain Λi, and network-flow constraints) could scale better. The claim of similar runtimes to AFHMM/AFAMAP is surprising; provide concrete per-instance timings, sequence lengths, solver settings, and convergence criteria.
- Clarity and presentation: Some notation is dense; a compact derivation highlighting why the objective is concave (Λi and Vt PSD, maximizing negative quadratic) would help. A statement about the equivalence to maximizing a concave quadratic over the HMM marginal polytope would make the convexity story crisper. Minor typos and inconsistent references should be fixed.

Quality
- Technically sound overall; the convexity argument is plausible, but the lack of discussion on integrality/rounding is a gap. The PR connection is correct. Experimental methodology is mostly reasonable but could better control for hyperparameters and potential bias in µ0 estimation.

Clarity
- Generally clear, but derivations are compressed. Add a brief proof sketch on concavity and (ideally) integrality. Provide algorithmic steps (inputs, solver, decoding). Ensure tables/figures referenced are present and legible.

Originality
- The idea of aggregate constraints within FHMMs, cast as a convex quadratic program and linked to posterior regularization, is a useful and novel combination relative to AFAMAP and duration-based constraints. The notion of global-sum constraints has antecedents, but this instantiation for AFHMMs is new and practically motivated.

Significance
- Moderate to high for NILM and potentially broader BSS applications where aggregate priors exist (e.g., audio source energy, gene expression budgets). The empirical gains on low-frequency data, a difficult regime, are meaningful.

Pros
- Practical, general prior that improves identifiability.
- Convex (concave maximization) relaxation with likely tightness.
- Clear PR interpretation.
- Demonstrated gains on a realistic dataset.

Cons
- Missing discussion of integrality/rounding.
- Limited hyperparameter tuning and robustness analysis.
- Potential bias in µ0 estimation; unclear generalization to unseen households.
- Scalability claims need stronger evidence; reliance on CVX may limit adoption.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a proposition on tightness of the relaxation (integrality) leveraging the HMM flow polytope; otherwise, describe a rounding scheme and report its impact.
- Include sensitivity analyses over λi and µ0 perturbations on real data; consider inequality (interval) SACs or hierarchical priors on µ0 to model uncertainty.
- Clarify and tune baselines; report per-house/per-day timings with solver details.
- Discuss handling of unmodeled loads and provide residual/bias analyses.
- Improve exposition and fix references.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a simple, well-motivated, and effective prior for AFHMMs, a sound convex formulation, and encouraging empirical results on challenging data. Addressing the noted methodological and clarity issues would solidify the contribution. Confidence: high.
