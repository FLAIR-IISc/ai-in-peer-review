### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), with a specific focus on energy disaggregation. The authors identify a key challenge in this domain: the non-identifiability of separated sources, which makes it difficult to assign meaningful labels (e.g., specific appliances) to the inferred signals. To address this, the paper proposes incorporating prior domain knowledge in the form of "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a standard probabilistic model for this task.

The core idea is to constrain the sum of each source signal over a given time period to be close to a known or expected value. For example, one might have prior knowledge about the typical total daily energy consumption of a refrigerator. Incorporating these global, non-Markovian constraints into the AFHMM's MAP inference objective makes standard dynamic programming approaches like the Viterbi algorithm inapplicable. The authors' main technical contribution is to formulate a relaxed version of this new optimization problem as a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The paper also draws a connection between their penalized objective function and the posterior regularization framework.

The proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on a large, real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES). The results demonstrate a substantial improvement over both the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art model, AFAMAP, reducing normalized disaggregation error by 45% and 29% respectively on realistic data.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of using signal aggregate constraints is simple, intuitive, and highly effective. While incorporating prior knowledge into probabilistic models is a well-established theme, this specific form of a global, aggregate-level constraint for BSS is a novel and practical contribution. It directly targets the critical problem of source identifiability in a principled way. The application to energy disaggregation is timely and important, and the proposed method is likely to be useful for other BSS problems where similar aggregate priors exist (e.g., in audio or bioinformatics).

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper's technical execution is sound. The formulation of the constrained MAP inference problem and its subsequent relaxation into a CQP is clear and well-justified. This provides a practical and tractable inference algorithm for a problem that is otherwise difficult to solve due to the non-Markovian nature of the constraints. The connection drawn to the posterior regularization framework in Section 6 adds theoretical depth and provides an alternative, elegant motivation for the proposed objective function.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is a major strength of the paper.
    *   The authors use a large, challenging, and relevant real-world dataset (HES) with low-frequency data, which is more realistic than many prior studies.
    *   They compare their method against appropriate baselines, including the standard AFHMM and the strong, recent AFAMAP model.
    *   The experiments are well-designed, testing the models on both synthesized aggregate data (Section 7.2.1) and, more importantly, on true mains readings which include unmodeled sources (Section 7.2.2). This demonstrates the robustness of the approach.
    *   The reported performance improvements are substantial and statistically significant, clearly demonstrating the value of the proposed constraints. The use of an oracle (TTC) is a nice touch that effectively illustrates the potential of the method.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the model is described precisely, and the experimental setup and results are presented transparently.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The most significant weakness is the handling of the regularization parameter λ. The authors state, "we simply set λi = 1 for all the experiments". This choice is arbitrary and undermines the robustness of the empirical claims. The performance of any regularized model is highly dependent on the strength of the regularizer. A more convincing study would include a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with λ, or use a principled method like cross-validation on a held-out set to select λ. I strongly recommend that the authors add such an analysis, as it is crucial for understanding the practical applicability of their method.

2.  **Sensitivity to Constraint Accuracy:** The method's performance depends on the quality of the prior aggregate values (µi0). The toy data experiment provides a good initial analysis of this, showing that performance degrades as the specified aggregate deviates from the true one. However, this analysis should be extended to the real-world data. How does performance change if the "national total consumption" (NTC) values are perturbed? A discussion on the robustness to noisy or biased priors would strengthen the paper and provide practical guidance to users. For instance, how would the model handle a household whose consumption patterns differ significantly from the national average?

3.  **Scalability:** The paper reports that the CQP approach has a similar runtime to the baselines in their experiments (~30 minutes for a day of data). While acceptable, this could be a bottleneck for longer time series or a larger number of appliances. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of the computational complexity and practical scalability of the CQP solver with respect to the time series length (T) and the number of sources (I). An empirical scaling plot, even if relegated to an appendix, would be valuable.

4.  **Generalization of Constraints:** The authors insightfully mention in Section 4.2 that the SACs could be generalized to multiple constraints over different time windows (e.g., day vs. night). This is a powerful extension of their framework. While the current results are already strong, demonstrating this capability on a simple case would have made the contribution even more compelling and better showcased the flexibility of the proposed approach.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound contribution to an important problem area. The proposed method of using signal aggregate constraints is elegant and demonstrably effective, leading to significant performance gains over strong baselines on a challenging real-world task. The paper is clearly written and the experiments are thorough.

While the handling of the regularization hyperparameter λ is a notable weakness that should be addressed, it does not invalidate the core contribution. The strengths in originality, significance, and empirical validation strongly outweigh the weaknesses. The paper advances the state of the art in energy disaggregation and introduces a general technique that could be valuable in other domains. I recommend acceptance.
