

# Peer Review: Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation

## Summary
This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation (BSS) problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation. The authors address the identifiability problem in BSS by incorporating domain knowledge about expected total signal values (e.g., average daily energy consumption per appliance) into Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs). SACs constrain the sum of each source signal over the entire sequence to be close to a specified value. The authors transform the constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program that can be efficiently solved, avoiding the intractable non-Markovian constraints. The method is evaluated on both synthetic data and real-world energy disaggregation datasets, showing significant improvements over baseline methods (45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to AFHMM, 29% improvement over state-of-the-art AFAMAP).

This work relates to recent efforts in incorporating domain knowledge into probabilistic models (posterior regularization, generalized expectation criterion), but makes a specific and practical contribution by identifying a class of constraints (aggregate values) that are commonly available across applications. It extends the AFHMM framework beyond prior work that constrained state transitions (AFAMAP) or used duration constraints.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper effectively articulates the identifiability problem in BSS and the need for domain knowledge to address it. The energy disaggregation application provides a compelling motivation.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The transformation of the constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program is mathematically rigorous. The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) provides strong theoretical grounding.

3. **Significant Experimental Results**: The energy disaggregation results are impressive and well-presented with appropriate metrics (NDE and SAE). The 29% improvement over AFAMAP is a substantial contribution to the field.

4. **Practical Relevance**: The paper demonstrates how to practically incorporate domain knowledge (national appliance usage statistics) into the model, making the approach immediately applicable.

5. **Clarity**: The paper is well-structured with clear sections. The technical details in Sections 4-6 are accessible to readers familiar with HMMs and optimization.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Evaluation of Constraint Sensitivity**: While the paper shows SACs improve performance, it doesn't thoroughly explore how sensitive the method is to the choice of constraint values (µi0) or regularization parameters (λi). The toy data experiment (Section 7.1) shows some sensitivity, but more detailed analysis would strengthen the case.

2. **Narrow Application Scope**: The paper evaluates SACs only on energy disaggregation, despite claiming general applicability. A brief demonstration on a different BSS problem would better support this claim. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't address it in the evaluation.

3. **Comparison with Other Constraint Types**: The paper mentions other constraint approaches (AFAMAP, non-homogeneous HMMs) but doesn't directly compare SACs with other forms of prior knowledge incorporation (e.g., using expert knowledge about state transition patterns).

4. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the paper states the CQP has polynomial complexity, it doesn't provide specific bounds or compare computational requirements with other methods beyond a brief mention of similar inference times.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of blind source separation by introducing a novel and practical constraint mechanism. The SACs are well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and demonstrate significant empirical improvements in a challenging real-world application. The paper is clear, well-structured, and properly contextualized within the literature.

The main limitations are the narrow evaluation scope and insufficient exploration of constraint sensitivity. However, these are minor compared to the paper's substantial contributions.

## Pro and Con for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Introduces a novel and practically useful class of constraints for BSS problems
- Solves a significant technical challenge (transforming non-Markovian constraints into a convex program)
- Demonstrates substantial performance gains (29% improvement over state-of-the-art) on a real-world application
- Well-written with clear theoretical and experimental contributions
- Connects to broader frameworks (posterior regularization) showing theoretical grounding

**Con:**
- Limited to energy disaggregation application without broader validation
- Insufficient exploration of sensitivity to constraint values and regularization parameters
- No comparison with other constraint types beyond AFAMAP

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper. The contribution is significant, the technical approach is sound, and the results are compelling. While the evaluation scope could be broader, the energy disaggregation results are sufficiently strong to demonstrate the value of SACs. The paper makes a clear and valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling for source separation.

The authors should address the sensitivity analysis in the final version (e.g., by adding a brief sensitivity analysis to Section 7.1) and consider adding a sentence about broader applicability beyond energy disaggregation. These are minor points that would further strengthen an already strong paper.

This paper is well-suited for NIPS as it presents a novel algorithmic contribution with strong theoretical grounding and significant empirical results in a practically important application domain. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a principled approach to incorporating domain knowledge into probabilistic models.
