This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation, specifically within the context of Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs) applied to energy disaggregation. The core idea is to incorporate prior knowledge about the expected total energy consumption of appliances to improve the identifiability and accuracy of disaggregation. The authors formulate this as a constrained optimization problem and propose a convex quadratic program (CQP) for efficient solution. The work is well-motivated, addressing a crucial challenge in energy disaggregation – providing interpretable and reliable results to end-users.

The paper builds upon a significant body of work in energy disaggregation (Hart, 1999; Zoha et al., 2014) and probabilistic modeling for BSS (Bach & Jordan, 2002; Ghahramani & Jordan, 2000). It distinguishes itself from prior work focused on constraining the HMM states themselves (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012; Kim et al., 2013) by proposing a novel constraint directly on aggregate signal values. The connection to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2009) is also thoughtfully made, demonstrating a broader theoretical grounding. The introduction of SACs, the development of the CQP relaxation, and the empirical validation on real-world energy data represent the main contributions.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of identifiability in BSS, particularly in energy disaggregation, and persuasively argues for the incorporation of domain knowledge. The “franken-appliance” example effectively illustrates the practical need for interpretable source signals.
* **Novelty of SACs:**  The introduction of signal aggregate constraints is a novel approach to incorporating prior knowledge. This differs from standard HMM constraint techniques and leverages specific domain understanding.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the CQP relaxation appears sound, building on existing techniques (Grant & Boyd, 2008) and providing a computationally tractable solution.  The connection to posterior regularization strengthens the theoretical justification.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on both toy data and real-world energy data are compelling.  The 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM and the 29% improvement over AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012) demonstrate a significant performance gain.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The evaluation includes both simulated data and real-world energy data, using multiple metrics (NDE and SAE) and comparing against established baselines.  The ablation study with different sources for the constraint values (NTC vs. TTC) is valuable.
* **Computational Efficiency Discussion**: The authors acknowledge the computational cost and demonstrate that the proposed method maintains a similar computational complexity as existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Sensitivity to Constraint Value:**  While demonstrated in the toy data, the sensitivity of the method to the accuracy of the prior aggregate values (µi0) in real-world scenarios requires further discussion. In practice, obtaining accurate prior values for appliance energy consumption can be challenging, and the performance degradation as the estimation of µi0 deviates from reality could be more thoroughly investigated.
* **Limited Exploration of Constraint Generalization:** The paper primarily focuses on a single, global aggregate constraint per chain. The extension to multiple, time-dependent constraints (as briefly mentioned in Section 4.2) could be explored in more detail, as it aligns well with the real-world usage patterns of appliances (e.g., daytime vs. nighttime).
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The choice of λi is somewhat arbitrarily set to 1. A more systematic exploration of hyperparameter tuning strategies (e.g., cross-validation) could potentially further improve performance.
* **Assumptions of the AFHMM:**  The paper builds on the AFHMM framework, which itself has limitations. Discussing the potential benefits and drawbacks of applying SACs to other BSS models (e.g., non-negative matrix factorization) could broaden the scope of the work.
* **Lack of Statistical Significance Detail:** While paired t-tests are mentioned, more specific p-values or confidence intervals would strengthen the statistical justification for the observed performance improvements.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound approach to improve energy disaggregation.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate a significant performance gain.
* The work addresses a practically relevant problem with a clear and well-defined contribution.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The sensitivity to the accuracy of the prior aggregate values could limit the applicability of the method in some real-world scenarios.
* The limited exploration of constraint generalization and hyperparameter tuning could leave room for further improvement.


**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. The introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints is a valuable contribution to the field of energy disaggregation and BSS. The empirical results are convincing, and the theoretical justification is sound. While some minor weaknesses exist, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggested Revisions:**

*   Expand the discussion on the sensitivity of the method to inaccurate prior aggregate values and explore potential robustness strategies.
*   Further investigate the extension to multiple, time-dependent constraints.
*   Include a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on performance.
*   Provide more specific statistical details (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals) for the empirical results.
* Consider briefly discussing the applicability of SACs to other BSS models.
