**Summary**
This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a form of prior knowledge for Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs), with a primary application in energy disaggregation. The core idea is to regularize the model by encouraging the total sum of each inferred source signal over a time period to be close to a known, expected value (e.g., the average daily consumption of a specific appliance). As this constraint is non-Markovian and complicates inference, the authors propose a convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation of the MAP inference problem. The paper demonstrates empirically on both toy and real-world energy datasets that this approach, termed AFHMM+SAC, significantly improves disaggregation accuracy compared to a standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novel and Intuitive Idea:** The introduction of signal aggregate constraints is a simple, intuitive, and powerful way to incorporate domain knowledge into the challenging blind source separation problem. It directly addresses the identifiability issue by constraining the overall behavior of each source, which is a very natural form of prior for many applications like energy disaggregation.
2.  **Principled Inference Formulation:** The authors provide a clear optimization objective and derive a tractable inference algorithm by relaxing the integer program into a convex quadratic program. The connection to posterior regularization in Section 6, while brief, provides additional theoretical grounding for the proposed penalized objective function.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The use of an oracle (TTC) is a good way to demonstrate the upper bound on performance improvement. The results on real-world data with mains readings are particularly compelling, showing a 45% NDE reduction over the baseline AFHMM and a 29% reduction over AFAMAP, which is a significant advancement for this application.

**Weaknesses and Detailed Comments**
Despite the promising core contribution, the paper suffers from a significant number of minor flaws, technical omissions, and presentational issues that detract from its overall quality. A meticulous reader is left with many unanswered questions.

1.  **Lack of Methodological Detail and Justification:**
    *   In Equation (1), the observation noise variance `σ^2_t` is defined as time-dependent. This is a non-standard assumption for the basic AFHMM and is never explained or justified. Is it assumed known? Is it estimated? If so, how? This detail is critical for reproducibility.
    *   In Section 7.2, the authors state they "simply assume 3 states for all the appliances." This choice is entirely arbitrary. The performance of HMM-based models is often sensitive to the number of states. The paper would be much stronger with a sensitivity analysis or at least a justification for this choice (e.g., based on prior work or exploratory data analysis).
    *   The hyperparameter `λi` is central to the model, balancing the data likelihood with the aggregate constraint. The authors state they "simply set λi = 1 for all the experiments." This is a major weakness. It is highly unlikely that a single, un-tuned value is optimal across different appliances and datasets. The lack of any cross-validation or discussion on how to set this parameter in practice undermines the practical utility of the method. The toy data experiment (Section 7.1) explores sensitivity to `µi0` but misses the more important sensitivity to `λi`.

2.  **Clarity and Notational Issues:**
    *   The notation for transition probabilities `p(i)jk = P(Zit = j|Zi,t−1 = k)` is slightly unconventional (the indices for 'from' and 'to' states are often reversed). While consistent within the paper, it can cause initial confusion for readers familiar with the literature.
    *   The generalization of SACs to multiple time intervals is mentioned at the end of Section 4.2 but dismissed for "presentational simplicity." This feels like an oversimplification. If the extension is truly straightforward, the generalized formulation should be presented, as it would significantly strengthen the paper's claim of generality.
    *   Section 5, which contains the main technical derivation, is notationally very dense. While the steps appear correct, the presentation could be improved. For instance, the claim that there is a "one-to-one correspondence" between `δi` and `λi` is stated without reference to the underlying Lagrangian duality that establishes this relationship.

3.  **Formatting and Presentation Quality:**
    *   The paper exhibits a notable lack of care in its formatting, particularly in the bibliography. Several references are incomplete or malformed. For example:
        *   [16] ends with "(AAAI-, , ,)".
        *   [7] has extraneous commas: "version, M. Grant, S. Boyd, //cvxr.com/cvx,, , ,".
        *   [8] is missing the publication year.
        *   [1] has trailing commas: "1277–1283, ,".
    *   The caption for Figure 1 is missing from the body of the text in Section 7.1.
    *   These formatting errors, while minor individually, collectively suggest a rushed preparation and a lack of thorough proofreading, which makes one question the care with which the technical work and experiments were conducted.

4.  **Vague Claims:**
    *   The computational complexity of the CQP is described vaguely as "polynomial time." For a paper proposing a new inference algorithm, a more precise characterization of the complexity in terms of T (time steps), I (chains), and K (states) would be expected.
    *   The claim in Section 7.2.2 that "the algorithms take more time to converge for realistic data" is an interesting observation but is left without any analysis or hypothesis as to why.

**Recommendation: Reject**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The core idea of using signal aggregate constraints is novel for AFHMMs and highly effective for the target application.
*   The empirical results show a substantial and statistically significant improvement over existing methods.
*   The work addresses an important practical problem (energy disaggregation) with a method that is both principled and computationally tractable.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is undermined by a lack of methodological rigor, particularly in its arbitrary choice of key model parameters (number of states, `λi`) without justification or sensitivity analysis.
*   Numerous minor issues regarding clarity, notation, and vague claims leave critical details ambiguous.
*   The poor state of the bibliography and other formatting errors indicate a lack of care and polish that is below the standard expected for NIPS.

While the central contribution of this paper is significant and promising, the execution and presentation are not yet at the level of a top-tier publication. The sheer number of minor flaws, combined with the more serious omissions regarding hyperparameter selection and model justification, prevent a confident recommendation for acceptance. I believe a revised version that thoroughly addresses these points would be a very strong paper, but in its current state, it does not meet the high bar for quality and completeness.
