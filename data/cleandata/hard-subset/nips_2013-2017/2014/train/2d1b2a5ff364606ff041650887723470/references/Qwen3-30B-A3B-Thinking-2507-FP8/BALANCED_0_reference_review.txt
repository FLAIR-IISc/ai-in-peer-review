

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation (BSS) problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation. The authors address the identifiability problem in BSS by incorporating domain knowledge about expected total signal aggregates (e.g., "a toaster uses about 50 Wh in a day") into additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs). They formulate SACs as convex quadratic programs, enabling efficient computation, and demonstrate significant improvements over baseline AFHMM and AFAMAP approaches on energy disaggregation tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Concept**: The introduction of SACs represents a meaningful contribution to the BSS literature. While prior work has incorporated various constraints (e.g., AFAMAP's single-chain state change constraint), SACs address a different, practically important aspect of domain knowledge that hasn't been systematically explored.

2. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a clear mathematical derivation of the SACs formulation, showing its connection to posterior regularization. This theoretical grounding strengthens the contribution and places it within established frameworks.

3. **Strong Empirical Results**: The evaluation on real-world energy disaggregation data is compelling, showing a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to baseline AFHMM and 29% improvement over state-of-the-art AFAMAP. The use of both synthetic data (to demonstrate robustness to constraint specification errors) and real-world data (HES dataset) provides a comprehensive evaluation.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good mathematical notation and sufficient detail to reproduce the results. The connection between the problem, the proposed solution, and the experimental evaluation is logical and well-articulated.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Analysis of Constraint Sensitivity**: While the paper shows SACs improve performance, it doesn't thoroughly investigate how sensitive the approach is to the choice of constraint values (µi0). The paper uses fixed λi=1 and doesn't explore how errors in the constraint values (which are often estimates in practice) affect performance. A more detailed analysis of this sensitivity would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Identifiability Concerns**: The paper acknowledges the identifiability problem but doesn't adequately address how SACs specifically mitigate it beyond the constraint. The authors state that SACs "help to address this problem," but don't provide theoretical analysis of how SACs uniquely help with identifiability compared to other constraints.

3. **Limited Comparison with Other Constraint Approaches**: The paper compares SACs only with AFHMM and AFAMAP. A more comprehensive comparison with other constraint-based approaches (e.g., k-segment constraints [19] or non-homogeneous HMMs [21]) would better establish the novelty and relative advantages of SACs.

4. **Practical Implementation Details**: While the paper mentions SACs could be incorporated into unsupervised disaggregation approaches using national surveys, it doesn't provide specific guidance on how to estimate µi0 in unsupervised settings. This is a significant gap given that in most real applications, the true aggregate values (TTC) are unknown.

5. **Computational Complexity**: The paper claims the approach is computationally efficient but doesn't provide a detailed complexity analysis. The statement that "the CQP for AFHMM+SAC has polynomial time in the number of time steps times the total number of states" is vague - providing specific complexity bounds would strengthen the computational claims.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a novel and well-executed approach to incorporating domain knowledge into BSS problems through Signal Aggregate Constraints. The theoretical foundation is solid, the empirical results are strong, and the application to energy disaggregation is well-motivated. The work makes a meaningful contribution to the field of BSS and provides a useful framework for incorporating aggregate knowledge constraints.

However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the sensitivity analysis of constraint values, providing more thorough discussion of how SACs specifically address identifiability, and offering more practical guidance for implementing SACs in unsupervised settings. The comparison with other constraint approaches could also be expanded.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The contribution is novel, well-executed, and makes a meaningful advancement in the application of BSS to energy disaggregation. While there are some limitations to address, these could be resolved with minor revisions rather than fundamentally altering the paper. The work clearly advances the state of the art in the specific application domain and provides a generalizable framework for incorporating aggregate constraints into BSS models.

## Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a section analyzing the sensitivity of performance to errors in the constraint values (µi0), including experiments with varying degrees of constraint mismatch.

2. Expand the discussion of how SACs specifically help with the identifiability problem, perhaps with a theoretical analysis or comparison to other constraint approaches.

3. Include a comparison with at least one additional constraint-based method (e.g., k-segment constraints) to better establish the novelty and relative advantages of SACs.

4. Provide more concrete guidance on how to estimate µi0 in unsupervised settings, perhaps through a simple empirical study on how national survey data could be used.

5. Add a more detailed computational complexity analysis, including specific time complexity bounds for the convex quadratic program solution.
