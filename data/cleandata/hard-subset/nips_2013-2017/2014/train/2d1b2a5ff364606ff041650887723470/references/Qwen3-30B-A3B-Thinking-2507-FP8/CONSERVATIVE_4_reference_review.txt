

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper proposes "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) for Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs) to address identifiability issues in blind source separation, particularly for energy disaggregation. The authors argue that by constraining the aggregate values (sums over time) of source signals to known values (e.g., expected daily energy consumption for appliances), they can improve disaggregation performance. The method transforms the constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP) that can be efficiently solved.

The paper makes three main contributions: (a) introducing SACs for BSS, (b) providing a CQP solution for the relaxed AFHMM with SACs, and (c) demonstrating improved performance on energy disaggregation data.

**Strengths:**
- The problem statement is well-motivated, particularly regarding the identifiability challenges in energy disaggregation.
- The paper is well-written with clear mathematical formulations.
- The experimental results on energy data are presented with appropriate metrics (NDE and SAE).
- The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) is a reasonable theoretical justification.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weakness of this paper is the lack of rigorous validation of the core assumption: that aggregate constraints can be reliably specified in real-world scenarios. The authors use two approaches for constraint values: (1) "true total consumption" (TTC), which is obviously not available in practice, and (2) "national total consumption" (NTC), which is estimated from training data. However, they provide no evidence that NTC reliably approximates the true appliance consumption at the household level. This is a critical flaw because the entire value proposition of SACs depends on having accurate aggregate constraints.

The experimental results are misleading because they present the TTC as a benchmark (Table 1), which shows a 33% improvement over AFAMAP. However, TTC is an oracle value that would never be available in practice. The NTC results show more modest improvements (16% SAE improvement), but the paper fails to demonstrate how NTC could be obtained reliably without expensive and impractical household-level measurements.

The paper also lacks important comparisons to alternative constraint-based approaches. For example, AFAMAP [12] already constrains the HMM states to limit simultaneous changes across chains, which addresses identifiability in a different but practical way. The authors don't convincingly show why SACs provide a significant advantage over existing constraint methods, especially when the constraint values themselves are problematic to obtain.

The theoretical foundation is superficial. The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) is mentioned but not deeply explored. The authors claim SACs incorporate domain knowledge, but they don't provide a principled framework for determining which constraints are appropriate for different applications or how to handle cases where domain knowledge is limited or conflicting.

The paper also lacks sensitivity analysis for the constraint parameters (λi, δi). The authors simply set λi = 1 for all experiments, without demonstrating how performance varies with these hyperparameters. This is a significant omission for a method that depends on these parameters for effective operation.

While the paper claims "45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM," this result is based on the unrealistic TTC constraint. The NTC results show more modest improvements (16-33% better than AFAMAP), which are not sufficiently compelling to justify the added complexity of the SAC approach.

**Overall assessment:**
This paper presents a technically sound approach to a meaningful problem, but the core contribution is not sufficiently validated. The method's performance is heavily dependent on the availability of accurate aggregate constraints, which the authors fail to demonstrate can be obtained reliably in practice. The experimental results are misleading due to the use of oracle constraints, and the paper does not convincingly show that SACs provide a significant advantage over existing constraint-based methods.

The paper falls short of NIPS' quality standards by not providing thorough validation against realistic scenarios. It presents an interesting modification to an established framework but doesn't sufficiently demonstrate that it addresses a fundamental limitation of current methods in practice. The claimed improvements are not robust to the realistic constraints of the problem.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. While the paper is technically sound and well-written, it presents a new method without sufficient evidence that it solves a practical problem. The key assumption that aggregate constraints can be reliably specified is not validated, and the experimental results are based on unrealistic benchmarks. The improvements claimed are not substantial enough to justify the added complexity when compared to existing methods that don't require this problematic constraint specification. The paper would need to demonstrate a practical way to obtain reliable aggregate constraints and show consistent improvements across multiple realistic scenarios before it could be considered for acceptance.
