Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses identifiability in blind source separation with additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs): soft constraints that encourage each source’s total energy over a time window to match a prior target. The authors formulate MAP inference with a quadratic penalty on aggregate deviations, show that the resulting objective can be viewed as posterior regularization, and derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) by relaxing the binary state indicators and introducing pairwise transition variables. They solve the relaxed problem with CVX and evaluate on a toy dataset and the challenging, low-frequency HES energy disaggregation dataset, reporting substantial gains over AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012) in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE).

This work connects to: FHMMs and AFHMMs for energy disaggregation (Ghahramani & Jordan; Kolter & Jaakkola), duration/transition constraints via semi-Markov and interleaved constraints, and general posterior-regularization and measurement-based learning frameworks (Ganchev et al.; Liang et al.). The novelty here is a simple, interpretable global constraint class (aggregates over time) combined with a convex relaxation for AFHMM inference, and empirical validation on realistic NILM data.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: aggregate priors (e.g., per-appliance daily energy) are often known or can be estimated, and directly address identifiability.
- Sound convex formulation: by adding H variables and relaxing integrality, the objective becomes a concave quadratic (negative PSD terms plus linear), subject to linear constraints and box bounds, hence solvable efficiently with off-the-shelf solvers.
- Relation to posterior regularization is correct and insightful; the constraint penalty is the Lagrangian of a KL-projection under expected quadratic constraints.
- Empirical gains are meaningful on realistic, low-rate NILM (HES), improving both NDE and aggregate accuracy versus AFHMM and AFAMAP, with reported similar runtimes.
- Extension to multiple time-window constraints is natural and potentially powerful (e.g., day/night priors).

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality/technical soundness:
- Relaxation gap and decoding: The optimization relaxes binary states; the paper does not describe how discrete sequences are recovered (or whether fractional S/H are used directly to reconstruct signals). If outputs are fractional, interpretability and consistency with the HMM semantics are unclear. If rounding is used, the method should specify it and report any impact on metrics.
- Sensitivity to mis-specified aggregates: The toy study shows degradation as µ0 deviates from truth, but no analogous sensitivity analysis is reported on HES, where NTC priors can be household- and day-mismatched. Robustness to moderate misspecification (e.g., ±25–50%) and the role of λ should be quantified.
- Hyperparameter selection: All λi are set to 1 without tuning. Since λ controls bias-variance tradeoff, its choice is critical. Practical schemes (cross-validation on labeled houses, Bayesian treatment, or heuristics based on uncertainty in µ0) should be discussed; an ablation with varying λ would strengthen claims.
- Scalability: While the objective is convex, the variable count is O(T∑i Ki + T∑i Ki^2) due to H variables; solving general CQPs with CVX can be slow. More thorough timing vs T, number of appliances, and states would be useful, and a specialized first-order or ADMM solver could be outlined. The claim of similar runtime to AFAMAP (which is very efficient) needs concrete numbers and conditions.
- Noise/model mismatch: In mains experiments, unmodeled loads violate the additive sum of modeled appliances plus Gaussian noise. How is σt set? Is there a slack/unknown residual component? Without an explicit residual, aggregate constraints might force misattribution; yet results improve, but a brief analysis or a residual component extension would be valuable.

Clarity:
- Overall the exposition is clear, but some definitions are dense. A brief intuitive explanation of the convexity (maximize a concave quadratic) would help. The construction of Λi and Vt as rank-1 PSD matrices is neat but could be motivated more clearly.
- The paper should specify how µi, transition probabilities, and σt are estimated, and whether parameters are per-household or shared.
- Provide details on the rounding/decoding step and how evaluation metrics are computed from relaxed solutions.

Originality:
- The idea of global expectation constraints is known (posterior regularization, generalized expectation), but specializing to aggregate sums in AFHMMs and giving a tractable convex relaxation for NILM is a novel and useful combination. The link to prior frameworks is properly cited.

Significance:
- For NILM, aggregate accuracy is crucial; demonstrated improvements on a challenging dataset are practically significant. The idea is likely to transfer to other BSS tasks with known totals (e.g., metagenomics read counts, bulk RNA-seq deconvolution, audio with known energy budgets), though this is not empirically shown.

Questions for the authors
- How do you obtain discrete state sequences from the relaxed solution? If you use fractional S, how does that affect interpretability?
- How are σt and transition matrices learned? Are they tied across houses? Do you include a residual “other” appliance?
- Can you report sensitivity to λ and to errors in µ0 on HES?
- What are absolute runtimes (median, IQR) and memory footprints as a function of T and number of appliances?
- Have you tried combining AFAMAP’s change-one-at-a-time constraint with SACs?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a simple rounding/post-processing step and evaluate its effect.
- Include a sensitivity study for λ and µ0 on real data.
- Report per-appliance SAE and NDE to see which loads benefit most.
- Provide a lightweight solver or at least warm-start strategies; CVX is fine for a paper, but practitioners need guidance.
- Consider adding a residual component to absorb unmodeled loads.

Minor comments
- Several typos (“equavalent”, inconsistent spacing, missing years in references). Ensure references are complete and consistent.
- Clarify that maximizing a concave quadratic under linear constraints is a convex optimization problem.
- Specify whether µ0 comes from training data disjoint from test days to avoid leakage.

Overall assessment
This is a solid, well-motivated contribution: a simple, general constraint that meaningfully improves AFHMM-based NILM, with a clean convex formulation and promising empirical results. Addressing decoding, sensitivity, and scalability would strengthen it further.

Recommendation: Weak accept.
Confidence: High (familiar with FHMMs, posterior regularization, NILM).
