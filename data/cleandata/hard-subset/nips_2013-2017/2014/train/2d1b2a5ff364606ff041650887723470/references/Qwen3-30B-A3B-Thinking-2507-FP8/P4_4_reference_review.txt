

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a novel approach to address the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS), specifically applied to energy disaggregation. The authors observe that in many BSS applications, domain knowledge about expected aggregate values of source signals (e.g., total daily energy consumption of appliances) is available. They formalize SACs as constraints that encourage the sum of each source signal over time to be close to specified values. The paper integrates SACs into Additive Factorial HMMs (AFHMMs), transforming the non-convex constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP) that can be efficiently solved. The authors evaluate their approach on both synthetic data and real-world energy disaggregation data, demonstrating significant improvements over baseline AFHMM and AFAMAP.

The paper relates well to previous work in energy disaggregation (e.g., [8, 12, 15, 22]) and connects to posterior regularization [4] as a special case. The authors properly acknowledge related work on constraint-based approaches for HMMs (e.g., [12, 21]) and distinguish their SACs as a novel constraint type that specifically targets aggregate signal values rather than temporal state transitions.

**Quality**: The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The mathematical formulation of SACs and the transformation to a convex quadratic program is well-executed and rigorously justified. The authors clearly explain the limitations of their relaxed solution (not guaranteed optimal due to identifiability issues) while providing empirical evidence of effectiveness. The experimental design is robust, with both synthetic data (Section 7.1) and real-world energy data (Section 7.2) used to validate the approach. Statistical significance is properly assessed using paired t-tests. The computational efficiency claim (similar to AFHMM/AFAMAP) is supported by runtime data in Table 1. The authors provide a clear connection to posterior regularization [4] in Section 6, strengthening the theoretical foundation.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear from the outset, with a compelling motivation from the energy disaggregation domain. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained (e.g., in Section 4.2). The technical contributions (Section 4) are presented with appropriate mathematical precision, and the transition to the convex optimization formulation (Section 5) is logically developed. The experimental results are presented clearly with appropriate metrics (NDE and SAE) and visualizations (Figure 1, Figure 2). The paper provides sufficient detail for reproduction, including implementation specifics (e.g., using CVX [7, 6] for optimization).

**Originality**: The paper makes a significant contribution by introducing SACs as a novel constraint type for BSS. While related to posterior regularization [4], SACs represent a specific, domain-informed constraint that addresses a previously unaddressed aspect of the identifiability problem. The authors properly distinguish their approach from prior work on constraint-based HMMs (e.g., [12, 21]) that focus on temporal state transitions rather than aggregate signal values. The application to energy disaggregation with concrete examples (e.g., toaster usage) demonstrates how domain knowledge can be incorporated in a principled way. The paper appears to be substantially different from recent NIPS/ICML work on similar topics.

**Significance**: The results demonstrate substantial significance, with a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM and 29% improvement over AFAMAP on real-world energy data. The approach has clear practical value for energy disaggregation, where interpretable appliance-level energy usage is critical for consumer awareness. The method is generalizable beyond energy disaggregation to other BSS problems where aggregate signal knowledge is available. The computational efficiency (similar to AFHMM) makes it practical for real-world implementation. The paper also provides a clear path for future work (e.g., incorporating SACs into unsupervised approaches [11]).

**Strengths**:
- Novel constraint type (SACs) addressing a specific identifiability challenge in BSS
- Rigorous mathematical formulation and transformation to a convex optimization problem
- Comprehensive evaluation on both synthetic and real-world data
- Demonstrated significant performance improvements (45% over AFHMM)
- Clear connection to existing frameworks (posterior regularization)
- Well-organized, clearly written, and technically sound

**Weaknesses**:
- The relaxed solution (convex relaxation) is not guaranteed to be optimal due to identifiability issues (though the authors acknowledge this limitation)
- The choice of λi = 1 for all experiments (Section 7.1) could be better motivated (though the authors note this could be tuned via cross-validation)
- The paper doesn't explore how SACs might interact with other constraint types (e.g., combining with AFAMAP's state transition constraints)
- The analysis of computational complexity is somewhat brief (only stating it's polynomial time)

**Arguments for Acceptance**:
- Introduces a novel constraint type (SACs) addressing a well-known identifiability problem
- Provides rigorous mathematical formulation and solution via convex optimization
- Demonstrates significant performance improvements on a practical application
- Well-motivated, clearly written, and technically sound
- Has potential to influence future work in BSS and energy disaggregation

**Arguments against Acceptance**:
- The approach is somewhat narrow in scope (focused on aggregate constraints)
- The convex relaxation may not always capture the optimal solution
- Limited exploration of how SACs interact with other constraint types

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation, particularly for applications where domain knowledge about aggregate signal values is available. The introduction of SACs provides a principled way to incorporate such knowledge, addressing a significant limitation of current approaches. The technical execution is strong, with a well-justified mathematical formulation and thorough empirical evaluation. The results demonstrate meaningful improvements in a practically important domain. The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
