

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper proposes "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) to address identifiability issues in Additive Factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) for blind source separation, particularly in energy disaggregation. The authors introduce constraints on the sum of each component signal over time, transforming the constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program. They demonstrate improved performance on energy disaggregation tasks compared to baseline AFHMM and AFAMAP.

## Strengths
The problem of identifiability in blind source separation is well-motivated, and the idea of incorporating domain knowledge through aggregate constraints is novel and potentially useful. The connection to posterior regularization provides a nice theoretical foundation. The empirical results on energy disaggregation show promising improvements (33% reduction in NDE over AFAMAP), which is significant for a practical application.

## Weaknesses

**1. Notational Inconsistencies and Typos (Critical):**
The paper contains multiple notational inconsistencies that undermine technical clarity. In Equation (4), the notation "µTi Sit" is inconsistent with prior definitions where µi is a vector. This should be "µi^T Sit" for clarity. Equation (3) contains the misspelled "distibution." Equation (6) uses "equavalent" instead of "equivalent." These are basic technical writing errors that suggest a lack of careful proofreading.

**2. Incomplete Technical Derivation (Significant):**
The transformation to the convex quadratic program (Section 5) is presented as a straightforward conversion but lacks critical steps. The authors define Hit = (hitjk) such that "hitjk = 1 when Si,t-1,k = 1 and Sitj = 1" (without "and" between conditions), which is ambiguous. The claim that "the matrices Λi and Vt are positive semidefinite (PSD)" requires justification but is simply asserted without proof. The conversion from the original problem to the CQP is not sufficiently explained, making it difficult to verify the correctness of the approach.

**3. Experimental Design Issues:**
The paper claims "a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM," but Table 1 shows only a 33% improvement over AFAMAP (not the original AFHMM). This discrepancy creates confusion about the actual performance gain. The use of "true total consumption" (TTC) as a baseline is unrealistic and misleading, as it represents an oracle scenario that would never be available in practice. The paper should explicitly state that TTC is an upper bound, not a realistic comparison.

**4. Lack of Hyperparameter Analysis:**
The authors state they "simply set λi = 1 for all experiments," without justification or exploration of sensitivity to this parameter. This is a critical omission, as the performance of SACs would likely depend on the choice of λi. A proper analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would strengthen the paper significantly.

**5. Reproducibility Concerns:**
The paper lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. For example, they state "we simply assume 3 states for all the appliances," but don't explain why 3 states was chosen or whether they tested other state counts. The parameter estimation process for the HMMs is not described in sufficient detail. The computational complexity analysis of the CQP is superficial, with only a vague statement about "polynomial time."

**6. Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art:**
The comparison with the state-of-the-art is limited to AFAMAP [12], which is a specific variant of AFHMM. The paper does not compare with other recent methods in energy disaggregation (e.g., [11, 10, 16]), making the claim of "significant improvement compared to a recent state-of-the-art approach" unsubstantiated.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents a novel idea with promising empirical results, the technical flaws are substantial enough to undermine confidence in the work. The notational inconsistencies and incomplete derivations represent serious technical shortcomings. The experimental design has issues that weaken the claimed improvements, and the lack of hyperparameter analysis is a significant omission. The paper would benefit from careful proofreading, clearer technical exposition, and more thorough experimental evaluation.

The contribution is interesting but not sufficiently rigorous or complete for NIPS. The technical details are not presented with the precision expected at NIPS, and the paper would require significant revision to address the identified issues before it could be considered for acceptance.

**Recommendation: Reject**
