

# Peer Review: "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical challenge in blind source separation (BSS) - identifiability - by introducing Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs). The authors propose constraining the aggregate values of source signals (e.g., total daily energy consumption per appliance) to known expected values, transforming the problem into a convex quadratic program. The approach demonstrates significant improvements (45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error) over baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over state-of-the-art AFAMAP on energy disaggregation data.

## Strengths

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the energy disaggregation literature by addressing a practical problem that has received limited attention: the lack of domain knowledge to guide source separation. The SAC framework is well-motivated, with clear examples from energy disaggregation (e.g., "a toaster might use 50 Wh in a day"). The technical contribution of transforming the constrained optimization into a convex quadratic program is sound and computationally efficient, as evidenced by similar inference times to baseline methods.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, with both synthetic and real-world data. The comparison with AFAMAP (a recent state-of-the-art method) is well-executed, and the use of paired t-tests to establish statistical significance is appropriate. The demonstration of SACs' robustness to errors in constraint specification (Section 7.1) is particularly convincing.

The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) is a nice theoretical contribution that strengthens the paper's foundation and situates it within a broader framework for incorporating prior knowledge.

## Weaknesses

The paper's most significant weakness is its insufficient treatment of the fundamental identifiability issue in BSS. While SACs help, they don't fully resolve the exchangeability problem (Section 4.1), and the authors don't provide a theoretical analysis of how SACs mitigate identifiability or what limitations remain. The paper states SACs "help to address this problem" but doesn't quantify the degree to which they improve identifiability.

The experimental design has notable limitations. The choice of λi = 1 for all experiments without hyperparameter tuning is questionable, especially since the authors acknowledge these parameters could be tuned via cross-validation. The paper should have included ablation studies to demonstrate how performance varies with different λi values and how sensitive the results are to this choice.

The paper's claim of broad applicability beyond energy disaggregation lacks sufficient justification. The authors briefly mention other domains but provide no evidence of SACs' effectiveness in any other application. The comparison with related constraint-based approaches (e.g., k-segment constraints mentioned in Section 2) is superficial, and the paper doesn't clearly articulate how SACs differ from and improve upon these alternatives.

The theoretical analysis of the convex quadratic program is somewhat thin. While the authors state the problem is polynomial time, they don't provide complexity analysis or discuss how the computational burden scales with the number of appliances and time steps.

## Critical Assessment

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound, but the paper lacks sufficient theoretical analysis of identifiability and hyperparameter sensitivity. The experimental results are well-supported but don't fully address key methodological questions.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with precise mathematical formulations. However, the connection to related constraint-based approaches is not sufficiently developed.

**Originality:** The SAC framework is novel for BSS in the context of energy disaggregation, but the paper doesn't clearly differentiate it from other constraint-based approaches in the literature. The transformation to a convex quadratic program is technically novel but not sufficiently contrasted with alternative optimization approaches.

**Significance:** The 45% improvement in error rate is substantial and could have real-world impact for energy disaggregation. However, the paper overstates the general applicability of SACs beyond energy disaggregation without evidence. The practical challenge of obtaining accurate aggregate constraints (µi0) for new appliances or households is not adequately addressed.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a technically sound and practically useful approach to energy disaggregation that addresses a real-world challenge in BSS. While the SAC framework is novel for this application, the paper would be significantly strengthened by:

1. More thorough theoretical analysis of how SACs affect identifiability
2. Ablation studies on the choice of λi and sensitivity analysis
3. More rigorous comparison with related constraint-based approaches
4. Discussion of practical challenges in obtaining aggregate constraints

The current version demonstrates significant empirical improvements but lacks sufficient theoretical grounding and broader applicability evidence to fully justify its claim of a "general framework." The paper makes a valuable contribution to energy disaggregation, but the broader claims about the general applicability of SACs require more substantiation.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (with major revisions). The paper has strong empirical results that would be valuable to the community, but the theoretical foundation and broader claims need strengthening. With appropriate revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, this paper would make a significant contribution to the field.
