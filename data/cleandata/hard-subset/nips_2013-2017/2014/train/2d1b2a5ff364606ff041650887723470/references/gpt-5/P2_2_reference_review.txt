Summary
The paper addresses blind source separation in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs): soft constraints that encourage each source’s total energy over a horizon to match a given prior value. The approach augments the AFHMM MAP objective with a quadratic penalty on the deviation between the aggregate of each chain and a provided per-chain target (e.g., daily appliance energy). Because these constraints are non-Markovian, the authors derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) for a relaxed problem by introducing pairwise transition variables (H) and relaxing the binary state indicators (S) to [0,1]. They also connect the penalized objective to posterior regularization. Experiments on synthetic data and on the UK HES energy dataset (ultra-low-frequency mains) show notable improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP, using either national average totals (NTC) or an oracle target (TTC). Reported gains include ~45% NDE reduction vs AFHMM and ~29% vs AFAMAP in one setting, with similar runtime in their implementation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The objective is well-motivated and the relaxation is carefully set up. After relaxing S and H to [0,1], the objective becomes a sum of linear terms and negative quadratic terms with PSD matrices, hence concave; maximizing it under linear constraints is a convex program. The equivalence to posterior regularization is correct: the penalized MAP corresponds to MAP under a reweighted posterior exp(-Σλiϕi). 
- Completeness: The paper focuses on MAP under the relaxed CQP, solved by CVX. It does not discuss rounding from fractional S to discrete state sequences; however, since evaluation focuses on energy reconstruction (x̂it = μi^T Sit), using fractional S is acceptable for the chosen metrics but should be acknowledged.
- Empirics: The real-data experiments are substantive: 100 households (no mains, synthetic aggregate) and 9 houses with mains in realistic settings. Metrics (NDE, SAE) are standard for NILM, and significance tests are reported. Using NTC (cross-household average) is a reasonable, practically available prior; TTC serves as an oracle bound. One limitation is that λi are fixed to 1 without tuning; a sensitivity analysis beyond the toy example would strengthen conclusions. Runtime claims would benefit from more detailed scaling curves, given the use of a general-purpose solver.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well organized. The derivation in Section 5 is dense but adequate; still, the notational load (e.g., μ*, Λi, Vt, ut) could be streamlined. There are minor typos and reference formatting issues. It would help to:
- Explicitly state how x̂ is computed from relaxed S and whether any rounding is applied.
- Clarify the “one-to-one correspondence” between δi and λi and how λi should be set in practice.
- Provide more detail on the parameter learning for θ and how much labeled appliance data is assumed.

Originality
The core idea—global aggregate constraints across time on each chain—appears novel in the AFHMM/NILM context. Prior frameworks (posterior regularization, generalized expectation, measurement-based learning) motivate the approach, but this work instantiates them in a concrete AFHMM setting with a convex relaxation and shows a practical benefit. Related constraints (e.g., AFAMAP’s “at-most-one-change” and duration/semi-Markov constraints) are complementary rather than overlapping. Some related strands (global constraints in HMMs) are cited. Overall, originality is moderate-to-good: a principled, useful constraint class and a tractable formulation.

Significance
For NILM and other BSS applications where per-source totals are plausible to estimate, the method is attractive: simple to specify, domain-relevant, and demonstrably helpful on challenging low-frequency data. Beyond NILM, aggregate constraints could be useful in audio or biology when totals or budgets are known. The paper advances the state of the art for AFHMM-based NILM, though broader impact would be stronger with at least one non-energy application or a more general analysis (e.g., guarantees on relaxation tightness or rounding).

Strengths
- Clear, interpretable prior (aggregate totals) that matches practitioner needs.
- Solid convex relaxation with a clean link to posterior regularization.
- Substantive empirical gains on realistic, low-frequency NILM data; statistical significance reported.
- Practicality: NTC priors are obtainable; method does not require high-frequency signatures.

Weaknesses and questions
- Dependence on µ0: Results hinge on the quality of aggregate priors. The toy study shows degradation with mismatch, but more real-data sensitivity to prior bias/variance and λ would be valuable.
- Rounding/integrality: How are fractional S handled? If the goal includes interpretable state sequences, propose and evaluate a rounding/decoding step (e.g., chainwise Viterbi initialized at relaxed S).
- Scalability: CVX may not scale to long horizons or many appliances. Provide complexity constants and practical scaling (T, I, Ki), and consider specialized solvers exploiting the block structure.
- Baseline tuning: AFAMAP and AFHMM hyperparameters and model capacities (e.g., number of states) are fixed uniformly; discuss tuning fairness and whether AFAMAP benefits from duration models or combined constraints.
- Generality claims: Only NILM domain is evaluated; a second domain would bolster claims of general applicability.

Minor comments
- Clean up references (missing years, inconsistent formatting).
- Clarify training data requirements and whether the approach can be combined with unsupervised parameter learning (briefly mentioned).
- Discuss combining SACs with other constraints (e.g., at-most-one-change, durations).

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a real and important weakness of AFHMMs (identifiability) with a simple, effective, and interpretable constraint.
- Proper convex formulation and connection to established frameworks.
- Strong empirical improvements on challenging, realistic data with statistical tests.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited exploration of sensitivity to prior quality and λ, and no non-NILM task.
- Practical scalability concerns with a generic CQP solver and no rounding strategy if discrete states are needed.
- Some clarity and experimental rigor gaps (e.g., baseline tuning details).

Overall: A solid, useful contribution with clear empirical value for NILM and a principled formulation likely of interest to the NeurIPS community. With added analysis on sensitivity/scaling and minor clarity fixes, it would be a strong accept; as is, I lean toward acceptance.
