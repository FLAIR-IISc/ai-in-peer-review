This paper proposes Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework, specifically targeting the energy disaggregation problem. The core idea is to incorporate prior knowledge about expected aggregate energy usage of appliances to improve disaggregation accuracy. While the motivation is reasonable – addressing the inherent identifiability issues in blind source separation – and the application to energy disaggregation is relevant, the paper suffers from a lack of substantial novelty and thorough validation compared to established methods.

The authors correctly identify the issue of identifiability in BSS and propose a sensible approach to incorporate prior knowledge. The idea of SACs aligns with existing methods like posterior regularization and constrained optimization, as acknowledged by the authors. However, the contribution feels incremental. The application of constraints isn't new; numerous prior works (cited in the related work section) have already explored state constraints within AFHMMs, and the authors don’t convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage beyond these existing approaches *without* relying on access to the true aggregate values during testing (the “TTC” scenario). The framing of SACs as a specific type of prior, and the derivation of the CQP, are technically sound but do not represent a paradigm shift.

The technical development of the CQP is well-executed, building on established optimization techniques. The authors acknowledge the relaxation inherent in solving the CQP and its potential impact on optimality. However, the connection to posterior regularization, while interesting, doesn't fundamentally alter the contribution.

My primary concern lies with the empirical evaluation. While the results on the toy dataset are informative, the real-world energy disaggregation experiments are not entirely convincing. The comparison against AFAMAP is appropriate, but the reported improvement (45% decrease in NDE with NTC, 29% with TTC) appears substantial. Yet, the paper lacks a more comprehensive comparison against recent, state-of-the-art disaggregation techniques *beyond* the single method cited ([12]). More rigorous benchmarking against methods utilizing deep learning, for example, is crucial. Furthermore, the exclusive reliance on the HES dataset, while valuable, limits the generalizability of the findings. The authors state AFHMM+SAC has improved over both AFHMM and AFAMAP. However, the performance gap compared to AFAMAP is not overwhelmingly large and could be attributable to hyperparameter tuning or other implementation details. The paired t-tests confirm statistical significance, but do not necessarily imply practical significance.

Clarity is generally good, with clear explanations of the model and optimization procedure. However, the notation is somewhat dense, and a more intuitive presentation of the CQP derivation could enhance readability. The organization is logical, and the writing is concise. 

Originality is moderate. The SAC concept is reasonable, but the overall approach doesn’t drastically depart from existing methods. The application to energy disaggregation, while practical, is not groundbreaking.

Significance is questionable. While the results are promising, the lack of a broader comparative analysis weakens the argument for significant impact. The method’s utility hinges on accurate prior knowledge of aggregate values, which may not always be available in real-world scenarios. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound development of the CQP for solving the constrained optimization problem.
*   Demonstrated improvement over AFHMM and AFAMAP on the HES dataset, albeit with limitations.
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in energy disaggregation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution – the core idea of adding constraints is not novel.
*   Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art disaggregation techniques.
*   Reliance on access to true aggregate values (TTC) for optimal performance in experiments.
*   Limited generalizability due to reliance on a single dataset.
*   The performance gain over AFAMAP isn’t large enough to strongly justify the added complexity.



**Overall, I lean towards rejection.** While the paper presents a technically competent approach, the lack of substantial novelty and a more thorough empirical validation against a wider range of methods prevent me from recommending acceptance to NIPS. The authors should consider expanding the experimental evaluation, providing a more detailed comparison to recent state-of-the-art techniques, and addressing the limitations related to prior knowledge requirements. A more compelling demonstration of practical significance is needed.
