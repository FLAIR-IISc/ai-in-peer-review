Summary
The paper addresses blind source separation (BSS) when sources are additive and each source is modeled by a hidden Markov chain (AFHMM). The central idea is to incorporate domain knowledge about the total energy (or more generally, aggregate signal) used by each source over a time horizon. The authors introduce signal aggregate constraints (SACs), which encourage the sum over time of each source’s signal to match a specified target (e.g., prior expectation of a toaster’s daily consumption). They cast MAP inference with SACs as a penalized objective that adds quadratic penalties on aggregate deviations. Since these penalties are non-Markovian (coupling all time steps), standard dynamic programming is not applicable. The authors introduce auxiliary transition variables and relax the integer problem to obtain a concave quadratic objective subject to linear constraints (i.e., a convex quadratic program when written as maximizing a concave quadratic), solvable efficiently with CVX. They also show the formulation arises from Posterior Regularization.

Empirically, on synthetic data the method improves as the aggregate priors approach the ground truth. On a realistic UK Household Electricity Survey (ultra low frequency) dataset, they compare AFHMM+SAC to AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12). On 100 homes without mains (aggregated constructed) and 9 homes with mains (harder, because of unmodeled loads), AFHMM+SAC reduces normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE), with statistically significant improvements over AFAMAP; they cite roughly 45% NDE reduction over AFHMM and 29% over AFAMAP in some settings, with similar runtime.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on AFHMMs [Ghahramani & Jordan] and AFAMAP’s structural constraints [Kolter & Jaakkola], and connects to Posterior Regularization [Ganchev et al.], Generalized Expectation [Mann & McCallum], and measurement-based learning [Liang et al.]. It is distinct in proposing aggregate constraints over entire sequences for each chain and providing a convex relaxation for MAP with these constraints. The idea is related to aggregate constraints in probabilistic databases but applied to structured temporal models.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The relaxation is carefully constructed. By introducing transition variables H and noting the PSD structure of the quadratic terms, the authors obtain a concave quadratic objective with linear constraints; solving its relaxation is indeed a convex optimization problem. The Posterior Regularization view is correct.
- However, important practical details are missing: (1) how discrete states are recovered from the relaxed solution (rounding/decoding) and whether evaluation uses soft or discretized outputs; (2) precise parameter estimation for HMMs (initials/transitions)—only k-means for emissions is described; (3) sensitivity to λ beyond a single fixed value.
- Empirical evaluation is solidly positive but limited in breadth: comparisons are only to AFHMM and AFAMAP; no comparison to duration/semi-Markov NILM models or more recent NILM baselines. Robustness to mis-specified aggregates is shown only on a toy example.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but notation is heavy and there are typos (e.g., “equavalent”). The convexity/concavity argument could be clarified explicitly: we are maximizing a concave quadratic subject to linear constraints (convex optimization).
- The construction and dimensions of H, S, and the Kronecker products are precise, but eT is oddly defined (first element 1, others 0) and might be better denoted with a standard basis vector.
- Experimental setup needs more detail: transition estimation, train/test splits, number of time steps per day, how unknown loads are handled in the “with mains” case, and whether outputs are hard/soft.

Originality
- The specific notion of signal aggregate constraints over latent chains and its integration into AFHMM inference via convex relaxation is novel and useful. The link to Posterior Regularization is appropriate but not novel per se; here it motivates a particular regularizer.
- Prior constraints for AFHMMs (one-chain-change, duration models, k-segment constraints) differ in spirit; aggregate constraints are complementary.

Significance
- For NILM, aggregate priors are realistic (from surveys) and important for interpretability; the demonstrated gains on a challenging, low-frequency dataset are meaningful. The idea is general and could be applied in other BSS domains where aggregate priors are available (e.g., total expression counts, dose/energy budgets).
- The relaxation yields a tractable solver with similar runtime in the reported regimes; scalability to long horizons and many appliances remains to be demonstrated.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated constraint class that captures practical prior knowledge.
- Sound convex relaxation with an elegant auxiliary-variable construction and PR interpretation.
- Demonstrated, statistically significant gains over strong AFAMAP baseline on realistic data.
- Applicable beyond NILM to general additive BSS with temporal structure.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Missing details on rounding/inference: Describe how to obtain discrete state sequences (e.g., per-time argmax, projection to vertices, or solving a follow-up Viterbi with potentials from dual variables). Analyze integrality/tightness conditions or report fractionality rates.
- Hyperparameters: Only λ=1 is used. Provide sensitivity analyses, cross-validation strategy, or a principled way to set λ from uncertainty in aggregate priors.
- Baselines: Compare to duration-constrained/semi-Markov NILM (e.g., Johnson & Willsky; Kim et al.), and consider combining SACs with AFAMAP’s “one change at a time” to show complementarity.
- Robustness: Beyond the toy example, study performance when aggregate priors are biased (e.g., household-specific variations), include windowed SACs as suggested, and report results.
- Scalability: Report problem sizes, solver tolerances, and timing breakdowns; discuss specialized solvers exploiting banded/structured matrices rather than generic CVX.
- Evaluation clarity: Specify whether predictions are soft or discrete and ensure fair comparison across methods. In the “with mains” case, clarify how unmodeled loads are handled in metrics.

Questions for authors
1. How do you convert relaxed S, H to discrete sequences for evaluation? Are NDE/SAE computed on soft reconstructions?
2. How are transition probabilities estimated from training data? EM? Smoothing?
3. How sensitive are results to λ and to mis-specified µ0 on real data?
4. Can SACs be combined with AFAMAP’s transition constraint, and does that further improve accuracy?
5. What are the typical sizes (I, K, T) and CVX solver times; how does runtime scale to multi-day horizons?

Pros vs cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Practical, novel constraint with clear impact and a principled formulation.
- Sound convex optimization approach; PR connection.
- Strong empirical gains on realistic data; significance tests included.

Cons:
- Missing methodological and experimental details (rounding, parameter estimation).
- Limited baseline comparisons and robustness analyses.
- Scalability and hyperparameter selection not fully explored.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a simple, general, and effective idea with a solid formulation and promising results on an important application. Addressing the noted clarity and evaluation gaps would strengthen the final version.
