This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a method to improve blind source separation (BSS), specifically within the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework. The core idea is to leverage prior knowledge about the expected aggregate value of each source signal over a given time period. This is particularly relevant for applications like energy disaggregation, where domain knowledge exists about the typical daily energy consumption of different appliances. The authors formulate this constraint as a quadratic penalty term in the Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) objective function. Since this term breaks the Markov property of the model, making standard inference algorithms like Viterbi inapplicable, they propose relaxing the integer programming problem into a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The proposed method, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world energy disaggregation datasets, demonstrating significant performance improvements over a standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—the concept of a signal aggregate constraint—is simple, intuitive, and effective. While various constraints have been proposed for AFHMMs (e.g., temporal sparsity in AFAMAP, duration modeling in semi-Markov models), this "global" constraint on the total sum over a long period represents a novel and complementary form of prior knowledge. The problem of energy disaggregation is of significant practical interest, and the reported improvements (a 29% reduction in error over AFAMAP on real data) are substantial and advance the state of the art. The idea is also general enough to be potentially applicable to other BSS domains where similar aggregate priors exist.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The methodological development is sound. The formulation as a penalized MAP objective is a standard and principled way to incorporate soft constraints. The key technical step, relaxing the resulting non-Markovian integer quadratic program into a CQP, is a well-justified and pragmatic approach to render the problem tractable. The connection to the posterior regularization framework (Section 6) provides additional theoretical grounding and demonstrates a deeper understanding of the underlying principles.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established with the "franken-appliance" example, making the identifiability problem tangible. The mathematical notation is clear, and the derivation of the CQP is laid out logically.

4.  **Experimental Rigor:** The evaluation is thorough and convincing. The use of a toy dataset to demonstrate the model's robustness to misspecified priors is a good sanity check. The main experiments on the real-world HES dataset are well-designed. Comparing against both a standard baseline (AFHMM) and a strong competitor (AFAMAP) is crucial. Furthermore, the inclusion of an "oracle" experiment using the True Total Consumption (TTC) provides a useful upper bound on the potential benefit of the proposed constraints. The evaluation on data with real mains readings (Section 7.2.2) demonstrates the method's effectiveness in a realistic and challenging scenario.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper states that the penalty parameter `λi` was simply set to 1 for all experiments. This is a significant simplification. The performance of the model is likely sensitive to this choice, which balances the likelihood term against the aggregate constraint. The paper would be substantially strengthened by including a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with `λ`, or by employing a more principled method like cross-validation to set this parameter, as the authors themselves suggest is possible. Without this, the reported results might be optimistic or difficult to reproduce in settings where `λ=1` is not optimal.

2.  **Practicality of Priors:** The method's success hinges on the availability of accurate prior information for the aggregate values (`µi0`). In the experiments, the "National Total Consumption" (NTC) is derived by averaging over the training set. While this is a reasonable proxy for the experiment, a more detailed discussion on the practical acquisition of these priors is warranted. How robust is the method if the test household is an outlier compared to the national average? How would one obtain these priors in a truly unsupervised setting where labeled training data is unavailable? The paper mentions using national surveys, which is a good direction, but exploring the impact of the quality and source of this prior knowledge would increase the paper's practical significance.

3.  **Details of the CQP Solution:** The paper states that the relaxed CQP yields fractional state assignments (`Sitk ∈ [0, 1]`). However, it does not specify how these soft assignments are converted back into a discrete state sequence for evaluation using the NDE and SAE metrics. Are they rounded? Is the state with the maximum probability chosen at each time step? This is a critical detail for reproducibility and understanding the final output of the algorithm. A brief explanation of this post-processing step should be included.

4.  **Potential for Combination:** The SAC is presented as an alternative to constraints like those in AFAMAP. However, these constraints seem orthogonal. AFAMAP enforces sparsity in state *changes* over time, while SAC enforces a constraint on the *sum* of state emissions. It is plausible that combining these two types of constraints (e.g., in an "AFAMAP+SAC" model) could yield even better performance. A discussion of this possibility would add depth to the paper and situate the contribution more broadly within the landscape of constrained HMMs.

In summary, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel, simple, and effective idea for improving a widely used model for an important problem. The technical approach is solid and the experimental validation is convincing. The primary weaknesses relate to a lack of detail on hyperparameter tuning and the practical sourcing of priors, which could be addressed to further strengthen the contribution. The paper is a clear advancement in the field of energy disaggregation and a valuable contribution to the broader literature on constrained probabilistic models. I recommend acceptance.
