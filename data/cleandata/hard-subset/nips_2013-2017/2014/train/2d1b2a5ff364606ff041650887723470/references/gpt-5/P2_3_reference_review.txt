Summary
The paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs). The key idea is to encode prior knowledge about the total (e.g., sum over time) of each source signal via a quadratic penalty that encourages ∑t xit to match a prior target µi0. Because these constraints are non-Markovian, the authors relax the discrete MAP inference problem by introducing transition-indicator variables and derive a concave quadratic objective with affine/box constraints, yielding a convex quadratic program (CQP) that can be efficiently solved. They also show the formulation arises as posterior regularization. Empirically, on a toy example and on the HES energy dataset (including a challenging low-frequency setting and a “with mains” scenario with unmodeled loads), AFHMM+SAC improves normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) over AFHMM and AFAMAP.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on AFHMMs [Ghahramani & Jordan] and AFAMAP [Kolter & Jaakkola], but differs by adding global aggregate constraints across time.
- Conceptually fits within posterior regularization [Ganchev et al.] and related “learning from measurements” frameworks [Liang et al.]; the paper provides a concrete instantiation for additive factorial models and a tractable convex relaxation.
- Related to other constraints for HMMs (e.g., single-change, semi-Markov, k-segment) but focuses on aggregates that are inherently global and non-Markovian.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The relaxation appears correct. The quadratic terms are formed with PSD matrices (Λi, Vt) and the maximization objective is concave; combined with linear constraints and [0,1]-box constraints, this is a valid CQP. The equivalence to posterior regularization is standard and correctly derived.
- Inference details: The relaxation yields fractional S; for disaggregation this is acceptable because x̂it = µTi Sit is well-defined even if Sit is fractional. However, the paper should state explicitly how S is converted to x̂ in evaluation (likely this linear form).
- Empirical support: Results are strong across 100 households without mains and 9 with mains; reported improvements (e.g., 33–45% NDE reduction vs AFHMM, 16–29% vs AFAMAP) and significance tests are convincing. Runtime claims of similar scaling to AFHMM/AFAMAP are plausible for the evaluated settings (K=3, short horizons), though more detail on problem sizes and solver settings would help.
- Caveats: The hyperparameters λi are fixed to 1 without tuning; the toy sensitivity study is informative but a cross-validated analysis on real data would strengthen conclusions. Model parameters θ are pre-estimated (k-means for µ, simple HMM estimation), and joint learning with SACs is not explored.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The derivation is heavy but readable; notation is dense in Section 5.
- Missing details that would aid reproducibility: rounding/decoding from fractional solutions, exact T/I/K per experiment, CVX solver settings, and how unmodeled loads are handled (e.g., background “other” stream vs absorbed as noise).
- Minor typos and formatting issues in references (years missing) but not critical.

Originality
- The form of constraint (sum over time per chain) and its integration into AFHMM inference via a convex relaxation is a useful and, to my knowledge, novel instantiation. While expectation/measurement constraints are known in the PR literature, the specific AFHMM+SAC formulation and CQP derivation appear new and practically relevant.
- The extension to multiple time-window constraints is a natural generalization; not evaluated but straightforward.

Significance
- Energy disaggregation is an impactful application; the method addresses a key identifiability issue by enforcing plausible totals. The gains on realistic low-frequency data suggest practical utility.
- The approach is likely applicable to other BSS problems where aggregate priors are available (audio, biology), which increases its potential impact.
- Scalability beyond the reported settings remains an open question: variable count scales as O(IT(K+K^2)T); generic convex solvers may be a bottleneck at higher resolution or longer horizons. A specialized solver or decomposition would increase adoption.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated constraint class that encodes valuable domain knowledge.
- Sound convex relaxation with a clean connection to posterior regularization.
- Consistent empirical improvements over strong baselines on challenging real data.
- Conceptually general and likely transferable beyond energy disaggregation.

Cons
- Limited hyperparameter exploration; λ fixed to 1 on real data.
- Comparisons exclude other constrained-duration/semi-Markov baselines that are common in NILM.
- Scalability discussion is qualitative; reliance on CVX may limit larger deployments.
- Some implementation details (decoding from fractional S, handling of unmodeled loads) are underspecified.

Suggestions and questions
- Please clarify how x̂ is computed from fractional S and whether any rounding is applied.
- Provide sensitivity to λ on real data, and guidance for choosing µ0 when only coarse priors are available.
- Report problem sizes (I, K, T) and CVX runtime per instance; consider a specialized QP solver.
- Compare against at least one duration-model baseline (e.g., HSMM) or interleaved non-homogeneous HMM in the main results.
- Explore multiple windowed SACs (daytime vs nighttime) on real data, which may further improve identifiability.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a technically sound, practically motivated, and empirically validated contribution. While the conceptual tie to posterior regularization reduces novelty in the abstract, the concrete AFHMM+SAC formulation and convex relaxation, along with clear gains on realistic data, make this a worthwhile addition. Strengthening scalability discussion and experimental breadth would further solidify the paper.
