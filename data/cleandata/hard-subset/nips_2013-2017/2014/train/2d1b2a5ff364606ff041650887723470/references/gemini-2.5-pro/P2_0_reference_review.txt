### **Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"**

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), specifically focusing on the critical issue of identifiability in the context of energy disaggregation. The authors propose a novel method to incorporate domain knowledge into the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a common model for this task. The core contribution is the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), which act as priors encouraging the total sum of each separated signal over a time period to be close to a known or expected value. Since these global constraints break the Markov property and preclude efficient inference with standard algorithms like Viterbi, the authors formulate the MAP inference problem as a penalized objective function. They then relax the integer constraints on the state indicator variables and solve the problem efficiently as a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP). The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach, AFHMM+SAC, on both synthetic and real-world energy disaggregation datasets, showing significant improvements over the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP.

**Strengths and Weaknesses (Evaluation Criteria)**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Soundness:** The formulation of the problem is clear and well-motivated. The key technical step—transforming the non-Markovian MAP inference problem into a CQP by relaxing the integer variables—is a standard and sound technique for achieving a tractable approximation. The authors correctly identify that the resulting objective function is convex due to the positive semidefinite nature of the quadratic terms. The connection to the posterior regularization framework in Section 6 further strengthens the theoretical grounding of the proposed penalized objective function.
*   **Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed.
    1.  The use of a toy dataset effectively demonstrates the model's robustness to misspecification of the prior aggregate values (µi0).
    2.  The evaluation on a large, real-world dataset (HES) is a significant strength.
    3.  The comparison against not only a standard baseline (AFHMM) but also a strong, recent competitor (AFAMAP) provides a convincing benchmark of the method's performance.
    4.  The inclusion of an "oracle" experiment using the true total consumption (TTC) is an excellent methodological choice, as it bounds the potential performance gain and isolates the contribution of the SAC framework itself.
    5.  The testing on both synthetic aggregate signals and real mains readings demonstrates the method's utility in both idealized and realistic, noisy scenarios. The reported results, a 45% error reduction over AFHMM and 29% over AFAMAP on real data, are substantial.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

*   The introduction effectively motivates the problem of identifiability using the memorable "franken-appliance" example.
*   The progression from the basic AFHMM to the proposed AFHMM+SAC is logical and easy to follow.
*   The mathematical notation, while dense in Section 5, is well-defined and necessary for the CQP formulation.
*   The experimental setup and results are presented clearly in tables and figures, making the outcomes easy to interpret.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel and valuable contribution.

*   While AFHMMs and other constraint-based methods for BSS are not new, the specific idea of using *signal aggregate* constraints is, to my knowledge, novel in this context. It is a simple, intuitive, and powerful form of prior knowledge that is often available in many domains (e.g., average daily energy use, total expected rainfall in a region, typical gene expression levels over a cell cycle).
*   Previous work on constraints for AFHMMs, like AFAMAP, focused on local, temporal constraints (at most one state change at a time). This work introduces a complementary *global* constraint over the entire time series. This is a significant conceptual difference.
*   The CQP formulation for this specific problem is a direct and novel application of existing optimization techniques to solve the inference challenge posed by the SACs.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant and likely to have an impact.

*   **Practical Impact:** The method directly addresses a practical and economically important problem: energy disaggregation. The substantial performance improvements show a clear path to better real-world systems. The fact that the required prior knowledge (average appliance consumption) is often available from national surveys or manufacturer data makes the approach highly practical.
*   **Research Impact:** The idea of SACs is generalizable beyond energy disaggregation to other BSS problems in fields like audio processing or computational biology. The paper provides a clear and effective template for how to incorporate such global, non-Markovian priors into factorial models. Other researchers are likely to build on this idea, perhaps by exploring different types of aggregate statistics (e.g., variance, maximum value) or by integrating it with other types of constraints.

**Areas for Improvement / Minor Weaknesses**

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The complexity parameter λi is set to 1 for all experiments. While this simplifies the evaluation, it leaves open the question of how this parameter should be set in practice. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the results to λi and how one might tune it (e.g., using a validation set) would make the paper more complete.
2.  **Scalability:** The paper states that the CQP has polynomial time complexity and that its implementation scales similarly to the baselines. However, solving a CQP can be computationally intensive for very long time series (large T) or a large number of states. A more explicit discussion of the computational complexity in terms of T, I (number of chains), and K (number of states), and the practical limits of the approach, would be beneficial.
3.  **Unsupervised Learning:** The experiments are conducted in a supervised setting where HMM parameters are pre-trained. The paper suggests that incorporating SACs into unsupervised approaches would be "straightforward." This claim may be overly optimistic, as integrating the CQP inference step within an Expectation-Maximization loop for learning the model parameters (θ) is non-trivial. This should be framed more cautiously as an important direction for future work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Proposes a novel, intuitive, and highly effective method (SACs) for incorporating domain knowledge to solve the critical identifiability problem in AFHMMs.
*   The technical approach of using a CQP relaxation is sound, well-executed, and provides a tractable solution.
*   The experimental results on a challenging, real-world energy disaggregation task are impressive, demonstrating a significant improvement over the state-of-the-art.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear.
*   The core idea is general and likely to inspire follow-up work in other application domains.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The treatment of the hyperparameter λ is overly simplistic.
*   Potential scalability concerns for very large problems are not fully addressed.

**Recommendation:**

The strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It introduces a novel and significant idea, provides a sound technical solution, and validates it with a strong empirical study. The work represents a clear advance in the field of energy disaggregation and provides a valuable new tool for constrained inference in factorial models more broadly. I strongly recommend **acceptance**.
