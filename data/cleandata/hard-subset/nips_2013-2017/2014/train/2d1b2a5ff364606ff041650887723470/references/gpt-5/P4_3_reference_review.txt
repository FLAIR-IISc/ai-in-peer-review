Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses blind source separation (BSS) in time series by augmenting additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) with signal aggregate constraints (SACs) that encode prior knowledge about total energy (or other aggregates) of individual sources over a time horizon. The key application is energy disaggregation (non-intrusive load monitoring), where the approach aims to improve identifiability by steering inferred appliance signals to match known or expected total usage. The authors formalize SACs as quadratic penalties on the per-chain sum over time, derive a relaxed maximum a posteriori (MAP) inference objective, and show it yields a convex quadratic program (CQP) after lifting to pairwise variables Hit for transitions (similar to constructions used in AFAMAP and standard HMM relaxations). They also connect the penalized objective to the posterior regularization framework. Experiments on synthetic data and on the HES low-frequency energy dataset compare AFHMM+SAC to AFHMM and AFAMAP, reporting significant improvements in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE), with similar runtimes.

The work relates to prior NeurIPS and adjacent literature on factorial HMMs [Ghahramani & Jordan], energy disaggregation using AFHMM/AFAMAP [Kolter & Jaakkola], posterior regularization [Ganchev et al.], generalized expectation/measurement learning (as alternative ways to incorporate constraints), semi-Markov models for durations [Johnson & Willsky], and other constraint-based HMM approaches (e.g., k-segment constraints). The notion of aggregate constraints over sequences is also connected to probabilistic databases with aggregates, though those models lack temporal structure.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The derivation of the relaxed objective is coherent. Writing the observation likelihood as a quadratic in stacked state-indicator vectors produces PSD matrices; combined with subtractive signs, the overall objective is concave in the relaxed variables. Adding transition variables Hit renders the constraints linear (flow conservation across time), so maximizing the concave objective over a polytope is a convex optimization problem. The mapping to posterior regularization is correct: the penalized log posterior equals the log of the posterior-regularized distribution up to constants. However, two technical details are underspecified:
  - Rounding/projection: The relaxation allows fractional Sitk and hitjk. The paper does not state how a discrete sequence is obtained for reporting NDE/SAE. Common options include simple argmax per time/chain, decoding with reweighted potentials, or solving a final Viterbi using marginals; the chosen method and its impact on error should be specified.
  - Parameter estimation and noise: The paper assumes θ known and estimates μ via k-means and transition/initial probabilities from training data, but does not describe σt estimation, smoothing/regularization of transitions, or how appliance heterogeneity across homes is handled. The choice λi=1 for all experiments, without sensitivity analysis, is a limitation.
- Empirical evaluation: The synthetic experiment illustrates monotonic degradation in NDE/SAE as the constraint target μ0 moves away from the true aggregate, which is consistent. On HES, the method improves over AFHMM and AFAMAP in both constructed-aggregate and realistic mains scenarios; paired t-tests are mentioned. Missing details limit reproducibility and interpretation: precise dataset sizes per condition, number of appliances per home, T (2-min vs 10-min), constraint targets for mains case, variance across homes, and how unmodeled loads in mains affect SAE/NDE. Runtime claims “similar” to baselines are notable given generic CQP solving; providing absolute times and problem sizes would strengthen the claim.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized and the modeling/inference section is explicit about variables and matrices. The notation is dense but mostly consistent. Suggestions:
- Clearly state the rounding procedure from relaxed solutions to discrete sequences.
- Provide an algorithmic summary (inputs, steps, outputs) and complexity in terms of I, K, T.
- Clarify Hit.l vs Hitl. notation and index conventions.
- Expand on how σt, πi, and P(i) are estimated and whether any priors/smoothing are used.
- Summarize table/figure values in-text (some referenced tables/figures are not visible here).

Originality:
Introducing sequence-level aggregate constraints as priors for BSS/AFHMM is a novel and practically motivated idea distinct from change-time constraints (AFAMAP) and duration models (HSMM). Casting AFHMM with SACs as a CQP via lifting is a reasonable and, in this context, new application of convex relaxation. The link to posterior regularization is appropriate, though expected given the form. Related work is adequately cited.

Significance:
For energy disaggregation, aggregate priors (e.g., expected appliance totals from surveys) are realistic and valuable. The reported gains (e.g., substantial NDE reductions, statistically significant across houses) indicate practical impact. The approach is general and could transfer to other BSS domains where aggregate priors exist. However, broader significance would be enhanced by:
- Clear scaling results beyond small I/K/T and beyond general-purpose solvers.
- Robustness analyses to misspecified μ0 and to λ selection on real data.
- Evaluation against duration models or recent NILM methods beyond AFAMAP.

Pros:
- Simple, general prior that directly targets identifiability.
- Convex relaxation with a principled derivation; efficient to solve in moderate regimes.
- Demonstrated improvements on a challenging, realistic dataset with statistical tests.
- Connection to posterior regularization clarifies the modeling perspective.

Cons:
- Lack of detail on rounding from relaxed to discrete solutions.
- Limited sensitivity analysis for λ and for constraint misspecification on real data.
- Scalability and runtime claims would benefit from quantitative evidence and specialized solvers.
- Comparisons restricted to AFHMM/AFAMAP; no duration/semi-Markov or stronger contemporary baselines.
- Some training/estimation details (σt, transitions) and data specifics are missing, affecting reproducibility.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Describe and evaluate the discrete decoding from the relaxed solution; report fractionality rates.
- Provide ablations varying λi and exploring per-chain λ selection; include cross-validation or empirical Bayes approaches.
- Quantify runtime and memory vs. I, K, T; consider exploiting low-rank structure (Vt and Λi) or using first-order methods/ADMM for scalability.
- Include additional baselines (e.g., HSMM-based NILM) and report per-appliance errors.
- Explore time-windowed SACs as hinted, to encode diurnal usage, and assess gains.
- Release code and scripts for reproducibility.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound, novel, and useful enhancement to AFHMMs with clear empirical benefits in energy disaggregation. Addressing the noted methodological and reporting gaps would strengthen its contribution.
