

# Review: Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation

## Summary
This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation (BSS) problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation. SACs incorporate prior knowledge about the total energy consumption of appliances (e.g., "a toaster uses 50 Wh/day") as constraints in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs). The authors transform the constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program that can be efficiently solved. The paper demonstrates significant improvements over baseline AFHMM and state-of-the-art AFAMAP on both synthetic data and real-world energy disaggregation datasets.

The work relates to prior constraint-based approaches in HMMs (e.g., AFAMAP's "at most one chain changes state" constraint) and connects to the posterior regularization framework. While constraint-based approaches have been explored for HMMs, SACs represent a novel type of constraint that targets the aggregate signal properties rather than temporal dynamics.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-supported. The authors provide a clear theoretical justification for their approach by showing how SACs can be derived from posterior regularization. The convex quadratic programming formulation is sound and well-motivated. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with both synthetic toy data to demonstrate robustness to constraint specification errors and real-world energy disaggregation data showing substantial improvements. The 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to AFHMM and 29% improvement over AFAMAP are statistically significant (paired t-tests, p<0.05) and practically meaningful.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement, model formulation, and optimization approach are presented logically. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The connection between SACs and posterior regularization is particularly well-explained. The experimental setup for energy disaggregation is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Originality:** SACs represent a novel constraint type for BSS problems. While other constraints have been proposed for HMMs (e.g., "at most one chain changes state" in AFAMAP), SACs target the aggregate signal properties, which is a different and valuable perspective. The authors clearly distinguish SACs from prior constraint approaches and demonstrate their unique value in the energy disaggregation domain. The connection to posterior regularization provides theoretical grounding for the approach.

**Significance:** The results have clear significance for energy disaggregation, where accurate appliance-level energy monitoring can support energy conservation efforts. The improvements over state-of-the-art methods are substantial (29% in SAE), and the approach demonstrates how domain knowledge can be effectively incorporated into probabilistic models. The method is general enough to potentially apply to other BSS problems beyond energy disaggregation.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Scope of Constraints:** While the paper mentions that SACs could be generalized to multiple time periods (e.g., daytime vs. nighttime usage), the experiments only use a single aggregate constraint per appliance. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't explore the potential benefits of multiple constraints in their evaluation.

**Practical Constraint Acquisition:** The paper uses "true total consumption" as an oracle constraint value in experiments, but realistically, such precise prior knowledge would be difficult to obtain for most applications. While the paper mentions using national consumption data (NTC) as a practical alternative, the evaluation doesn't sufficiently explore how the method performs with less accurate prior knowledge. The toy data experiments show that performance degrades with constraint errors, but the real-world results don't address how robust the method is to imperfect constraint specifications.

**Comparison to Other Constraint-Based Methods:** The paper compares to AFHMM and AFAMAP, but doesn't explore how SACs might complement or integrate with other constraint approaches (e.g., the k-segment constraints mentioned in related work). It would be valuable to see if SACs could be combined with existing constraint methods for potentially greater improvements.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the problem of blind source separation by introducing a novel constraint type (SACs) that effectively incorporates domain knowledge about aggregate signal properties. The technical approach is sound, the experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing, and the results represent a clear advancement over existing methods in the energy disaggregation domain.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), introduces a novel constraint type (Originality), and demonstrates significant practical improvements (Significance). The weaknesses noted are important but don't undermine the paper's core contributions.

**Final Recommendation: Accept**

The paper is a strong contribution to the field, with clear theoretical grounding, practical significance, and compelling experimental results. It represents a valuable addition to the literature on constraint-based inference in probabilistic models and demonstrates how domain knowledge can be effectively incorporated into BSS problems. The method is general enough to inspire future work beyond energy disaggregation, making it relevant to a broad audience at NIPS.
