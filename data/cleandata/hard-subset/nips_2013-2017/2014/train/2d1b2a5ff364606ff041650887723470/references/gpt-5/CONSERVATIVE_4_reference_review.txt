Summary
The paper addresses blind source separation (BSS) with additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs), focusing on energy disaggregation (NILM). It introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs), which encourage the total energy of each latent source over a time window to match a prior target (e.g., national average appliance consumption). The authors add quadratic penalties to the AFHMM objective, derive a convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation by introducing transition variables, and solve it with CVX. They relate the penalty to posterior regularization (PR) and present results on a toy dataset and the UK HES data, comparing against AFHMM and AFAMAP [Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012]. They report substantial reductions in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE), especially when oracle totals are used.

Relation to prior work
The core idea—imposing constraints on expectations of functions of latent variables—fits squarely within established frameworks such as posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2010), generalized expectation (Mann & McCallum, 2008), and measurement-based learning (Liang et al., 2009). The use of additive FHMMs for NILM and constraints on state dynamics has notable precedents (AFAMAP’s “at-most-one-switch,” non-homogeneous FHMMs, HSMMs). The paper’s novelty lies in choosing total-signal constraints for AFHMMs, instantiating them as simple squared penalties, and solving a relaxed CQP.

Quality
- Methodological soundness: The relaxation and convexity argument hinge on Λi and Vt being PSD rank-1 terms, making the relaxed objective convex; that part is reasonable. However, the paper optimizes a fractional relaxation and does not discuss rounding or consistency of the relaxed solution with the discrete MAP objective. If outputs remain fractional, the method is not actually performing MAP inference, despite the repeated MAP framing. This gap affects interpretability, a stated motivation.
- Inference details: Solving large CQPs with CVX for long sequences and many appliances is not a scalable strategy; the claim that runtime is “similar” to AFAMAP seems implausible without detailed complexity analysis, problem sizes, and solver settings. No specialized solver/exploitation of chain structure is provided.
- Hyperparameters: λi are fixed to 1 without tuning. There is no sensitivity analysis on real data (only toy), despite the method’s performance being heavily dependent on the quality of µ0 and λi.
- Data and evaluation: The toy example is very small (two chains, T=100). On HES, evaluation is limited (100 households for a single-day synthetic aggregate; 9 houses for mains). Important baselines (e.g., HSMM duration models, non-homogeneous FHMMs) are cited but not compared. There is no per-appliance breakdown, and statistical testing is limited to t-tests that may not account for dependence. The “oracle” TTC comparison is not practical and inflates the apparent upside. The NTC prior computed across households risks mismatch due to household heterogeneity; robustness to mis-specified µ0 is not thoroughly studied beyond the toy case.
- Modeling consistency: The paper first assumes θ known (estimated via variational methods), but in practice uses k-means for µ and fixes K=3 for all appliances without justification. Noise variances σt are introduced but not estimated/used clearly.
- Claims: The method is presented as addressing identifiability, but in reality it introduces a prior that biases aggregates; identifiability is not resolved and no theoretical guarantees are offered.

Clarity
The writing is generally understandable but has numerous typographical and referencing issues (many incomplete citations, inconsistent notation), and the CQP derivation is dense with little intuition. Crucial details are missing: how fractional solutions are used, scaling behavior, solver tolerances, and how µ0 is obtained in practice. Reproducibility is limited (no code/parameters, no cross-validation strategy for λ).

Originality
The constraint idea is intuitive and useful but conceptually a straightforward application of posterior regularization/measurement constraints to AFHMMs. The CQP formulation with transition variables mirrors standard local-consistency relaxations for Markov models. As such, the contribution is incremental rather than fundamentally novel.

Significance
Energy disaggregation is important, and using aggregate priors is a sensible and potentially impactful idea. However, the reliance on accurate µ0 and the absence of stronger baselines and scalability evidence limit the broader impact. Without a rigorous treatment of the relaxation gap and robust validation, it is hard to endorse for NeurIPS.

Pros
- Clear, simple idea that leverages available domain knowledge (aggregates).
- Convex relaxation with an explicit objective; connection to posterior regularization.
- Empirical improvements over AFHMM/AFAMAP on HES data; practical relevance.

Cons
- MAP framing vs. fractional relaxation not reconciled; no rounding/optimality guarantees.
- Limited and partially unrealistic evaluation (oracle TTC; weak baselines; small sample).
- No hyperparameter tuning or robustness analysis on real data; λ fixed ad hoc.
- Scalability concerns with general-purpose CVX; runtime claims not convincing.
- Clarity/reproducibility issues (missing details, incomplete references, parameter estimation).

Questions for the authors
1. How do you obtain discrete state sequences from the relaxed solution, if at all? If you keep fractional S, how is interpretability achieved?
2. How sensitive are results to λi and to mis-specification of µ0 on real HES data? Please provide ablations.
3. Can you compare against HSMM-based NILM and non-homogeneous FHMM baselines, and report per-appliance metrics?
4. What are the typical T, I, Ki in your experiments, and what are solver times with CVX vs. AFAMAP? Any specialized solver exploiting chain structure?
5. How are σt and transition matrices estimated? Why K=3 for all appliances?
6. Can you demonstrate the proposed within-day/windowed constraints (Section 4) empirically?

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is reasonable and potentially useful, but the paper lacks rigorous validation against strong baselines, does not address the relaxation/MAP gap, and raises concerns about scalability and robustness. Strengthening the empirical study, clarifying the optimization/rounding pipeline, and providing more thorough comparisons would significantly improve the submission.
