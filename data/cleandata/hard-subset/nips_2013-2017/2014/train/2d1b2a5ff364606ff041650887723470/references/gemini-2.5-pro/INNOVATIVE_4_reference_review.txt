This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS) in time series, focusing on the critical issue of identifiability. The authors propose a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge into Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs), a common framework for BSS tasks like energy disaggregation. The main contribution is the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), which enforce priors on the aggregate value (e.g., the total sum) of each source signal over a given time period. This contrasts with previous work, such as AFAMAP, which primarily focused on local, temporal constraints like limiting the number of sources that can change state at any given time.

Because SACs are global constraints over the entire time sequence, they break the Markov property, rendering standard inference algorithms like Viterbi inapplicable. To address this, the authors formulate the MAP inference problem as a penalized optimization objective. They then present a tractable inference method by relaxing the resulting integer quadratic program into a convex quadratic program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The paper also provides a theoretical grounding for the approach by showing its equivalence to a form of posterior regularization. The method is evaluated on a large, real-world energy disaggregation dataset, where it demonstrates substantial improvements over both the standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The core idea of using signal aggregate values as a form of prior knowledge is both novel and highly intuitive. For many BSS problems, this type of global, summary information is more readily available or easier to elicit from domain experts than detailed knowledge about short-term temporal dynamics. This paper introduces a genuinely new and promising direction for constraining underdetermined source separation models. The approach is general and could inspire similar types of constraints in other domains beyond energy disaggregation, such as computational biology or audio processing.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental validation is convincing. The proposed AFHMM+SAC model achieves a very large reduction in error (45% vs. AFHMM, 29% vs. AFAMAP) on a challenging, real-world dataset. These are not marginal gains; they represent a significant advance in the state-of-the-art for this problem. The use of both synthetically aggregated data and real mains readings provides a comprehensive evaluation, and the inclusion of an oracle experiment (using the true total consumption) effectively demonstrates the potential of the proposed constraint.

3.  **Sound Technical Formulation:** The method is well-formulated. The transformation of the constrained problem into a penalized objective is standard, and the subsequent relaxation to a CQP provides a practical and principled inference algorithm. The connection drawn to the posterior regularization framework adds theoretical depth and situates the work within a broader context of constrained probabilistic modeling.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Simplistic Hyperparameter Treatment:** The regularization parameter `lambda` is set to 1 for all experiments without justification or sensitivity analysis. While the paper notes that cross-validation could be used, the lack of any exploration into the effect of this critical parameter is a methodological weakness. That said, the strong performance even with this simple choice suggests the method is somewhat robust.

2.  **Supervised Setting Assumption:** The experiments are conducted in a supervised setting where the HMM parameters for each appliance are pre-trained. This simplifies the problem by focusing solely on inference. While this is a reasonable choice to isolate and evaluate the contribution of the SACs, the full, unsupervised energy disaggregation problem is more challenging. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the challenges of integrating SACs into a full EM-style learning procedure.

3.  **Limited Analysis of the CQP Relaxation:** The paper solves a relaxed version of the original problem but does not discuss the quality of this relaxation. For instance, it is unclear how often the solutions to the CQP are integral or near-integral, or what rounding procedures are used if they are not. While this is a common technique, some brief analysis would improve the completeness of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a highly novel, simple, and powerful idea (Signal Aggregate Constraints) that represents a new direction for tackling the identifiability problem in AFHMMs.
*   The results demonstrate a clear and significant improvement over strong baselines on a real-world task, advancing the state-of-the-art.
*   The core concept is general and likely to be influential, inspiring further work on incorporating global constraints in other BSS and latent variable modeling problems.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental methodology could be more thorough, particularly regarding hyperparameter tuning and the analysis of the CQP relaxation.
*   The work is limited to the supervised inference setting, leaving the more complex unsupervised learning problem for future work.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a bold and effective new idea. The concept of using global signal aggregate constraints is a significant departure from prior work that relied on local temporal constraints. The strength of this novel contribution, backed by impressive empirical results, far outweighs the minor methodological weaknesses. This is precisely the type of work that can open up new avenues of research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
