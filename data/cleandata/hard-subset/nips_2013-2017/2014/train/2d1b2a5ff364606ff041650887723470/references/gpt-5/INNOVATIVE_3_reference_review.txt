Summary
The paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMM), motivated by applications like energy disaggregation where domain knowledge often exists about the total (aggregate) consumption of each latent source over a period. The core idea is to regularize MAP inference so that each chain’s total expected signal aligns with a prescribed aggregate µi0 (with optional time-windowed extensions), thereby improving identifiability. Because these constraints are non-Markovian, the authors derive a relaxed convex quadratic program (CQP) using auxiliary transition variables H, and also show the formulation arises from the posterior regularization framework. Empirically, on toy data and the HES energy dataset (both with synthetically aggregated and true mains data), AFHMM+SAC outperforms AFHMM and AFAMAP, with sizable reductions in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) and similar runtime.

Relation to prior work
This work sits between structured probabilistic models for disaggregation (AFHMM, AFAMAP; duration/semi-Markov constraints) and general frameworks for incorporating prior knowledge (posterior regularization, generalized expectation, measurement-based learning). The novelty here is a concrete, broadly applicable class of aggregate constraints, a tractable convex relaxation tailored to AFHMMs, and a demonstration on challenging, low-frequency HES data.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and generality: SACs are a simple, powerful way to inject domain priors on totals. The idea is broadly applicable beyond energy (audio, biology, etc.).
- Sound optimization: The relaxed problem becomes a concave quadratic maximization with linear constraints (equivalently a CQP). The auxiliary H variables make the objective separable enough for off-the-shelf solvers. The PSD argument is clear.
- Clear connection to posterior regularization: The derivation via KL-projection to a constrained posterior is elegant and positions the method in a principled framework.
- Empirical gains on realistic data: On HES mains data, improvements over AFHMM/AFAMAP are practically meaningful, and statistical significance is reported. The “oracle” TTC results quantify an upper bound on attainable gains from perfect aggregate priors.
- Practicality: Using national averages (NTC) is realistic, and robustness on toy data to mismatch in µ0 is encouraging.

Weaknesses and questions
- Rounding/integrality: The inference solves a relaxed CQP with Sitk, hitjk ∈ [0,1]. How are solutions mapped back to discrete HMM paths for evaluation? Is there a principled projection/rounding (e.g., per-chain Viterbi under linearized emissions using relaxed marginals, or solving a small flow problem) or are fractional S used directly to compute x̂? Please clarify and report the effect of rounding on NDE/SAE.
- Sensitivity to λ and µ0: All experiments use λi=1. The toy study varies µ0 but not λ. Given SACs directly bias totals, a brief sensitivity analysis over λ (and a simple heuristic for setting it from uncertainty in µ0) would strengthen the claims. How does performance degrade with biased NTC?
- Scalability: While the problem is polynomial and CVX is convenient, practical scalability to long horizons or larger K/I is unclear. Report average problem sizes (T, I, Ki), number of CVX iterations, and wall-clock times for the mains experiments; discuss using first-order or ADMM solvers to scale further.
- Baselines: AFAMAP is a strong baseline; however, duration-based/semi-Markov approaches [10,11] and other domain priors are highly relevant. Even if re-implementation is impractical, a discussion contrasting SACs with duration priors (complementarity vs redundancy) would be useful. Could SACs be combined with one-switch or duration constraints?
- Model assumptions: Fixing Ki=3 and estimating µik via k-means is pragmatic but may limit fidelity. A short ablation on Ki or on learning µik via EM would help. Also, energy usage variability across days may challenge a single µ0; the windowed SAC extension is mentioned—can you include a small experiment?
- Notational clarity: The presentation in Section 5 is dense. A compact table of symbols and a sentence clarifying that the “maximize concave quadratic” is solved as a convex QP (by sign flip) would reduce confusion.

Quality
Overall technically sound. The convexity argument is correct; the posterior regularization derivation is appropriate. The main methodological gap is the missing description of how relaxed solutions are decoded to discrete sequences. Experimental protocol is reasonable and shows clear gains; more sensitivity/scalability analysis would improve completeness.

Clarity
Generally clear, with self-contained derivations and reasonable connections to related work. Some notation is heavy and a few typos remain (e.g., inconsistent indices/spaces; “equavalent”). Adding details on decoding, solver settings, and dataset splits would improve reproducibility.

Originality
High. Using aggregate totals as priors in AFHMMs in a principled, convexly relaxable way is a fresh and useful idea distinct from common structural constraints (one-switch, durations). The posterior-regularization lens ties it to a broader methodology while maintaining novelty in the concrete instantiation and optimization.

Significance
High for energy disaggregation and likely broader BSS. The idea is simple to adopt and seems to yield large gains on realistic, low-frequency data where identifiability is especially problematic. The method’s generality makes it a promising direction for other domains with known totals or budgets.

Suggestions
- Describe the rounding/decoding from the relaxed solution and report its impact.
- Add a brief sensitivity study over λ and µ0 mismatch.
- Include runtime and scale details; discuss scalable solvers beyond CVX.
- Clarify that SACs can be combined with duration/one-switch priors and comment on expected interactions.
- Minor edits for notation/typos.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, broadly applicable idea with principled formulation
- Solid convex relaxation and posterior regularization connection
- Strong improvements on realistic data with statistical tests
- Practical and conceptually elegant; likely to influence future work

Cons against acceptance
- Missing details on decoding from relaxed solutions
- Limited sensitivity/scalability analysis
- Comparisons to duration-based baselines are only discussed, not empirically evaluated

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a compelling new constraint class with a clean optimization approach and demonstrates clear gains on an important, challenging application. Minor empirical and clarity issues are fixable.
