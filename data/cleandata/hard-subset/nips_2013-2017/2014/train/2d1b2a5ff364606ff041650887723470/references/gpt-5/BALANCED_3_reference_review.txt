Summary
The paper addresses blind source separation for time series with an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs). The core idea is that in many domains the total (aggregate) contribution of each latent source over a period is approximately known (e.g., expected daily energy per appliance). The authors incorporate these constraints as quadratic penalties on the sum of state means over time, leading to a penalized MAP objective. Since the resulting objective is non-Markovian and not directly amenable to dynamic programming, they derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) via a standard convex relaxation that introduces transition-indicator variables and relaxes binary constraints to [0,1]. They also show the equivalence to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2010). Experiments on a toy dataset and on the UK HES energy disaggregation dataset compare AFHMM+SAC to AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012), showing improved normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE), with statistically significant gains.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned within factorial HMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan) and energy disaggregation/NILM (Hart; Kolter & Jaakkola; reviews by Ziefman & Roth; Zoha et al.). It complements prior constraints such as “one chain changes at a time” (AFAMAP), non-homogeneous/semi-Markov variants, and k-segment constraints, by proposing global aggregate constraints. The connection to posterior regularization and measurement-based learning is appropriate and well-cited. The convex relaxation follows ideas used previously for making FHMM inference tractable via LP/QP relaxations.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: aggregate knowledge is often available, especially in NILM.
- Methodologically simple and general: the SAC penalty is easy to add and the CQP formulation is clean; concavity is ensured through negative quadratic terms with PSD matrices.
- Useful link to posterior regularization that clarifies the probabilistic meaning of the constraint.
- Empirical results show consistent, statistically significant improvements over strong baselines on a realistic, challenging dataset (HES; low sampling rates).
- Practicality: default λi works reasonably well; authors note windowed constraints as a natural extension.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Discrete-to-continuous gap: After convex relaxation, solutions may be fractional. The paper does not describe how solutions are projected back to valid discrete state sequences, nor the impact on interpretability (important for NILM). If signals are generated from fractional S, this departs from the intended HMM semantics.
- Sensitivity/robustness: While a toy sensitivity study varies µ0, real-data sensitivity to mis-specified µ0 (e.g., when a household deviates from national averages) is not explored; λi is fixed at 1 without tuning or robustness analysis.
- Experimental breadth: Evaluations are limited to one dataset/domain; no comparison to duration/semi-Markov models on the same data; no ablations on number of states or windowed constraints.
- Computational claims: Stating that CVX-based CQP “scales similarly” to AFHMM/AFAMAP is surprising; no scaling plots or complexity characterization beyond polynomial-time. CVX solvers can be slow for larger T, I, Ki.
- Some statistical reporting is unclear: e.g., where the paired t-tests are computed over days/houses, sample sizes, and effect sizes.

Clarity
- Notation density and some typographical issues (e.g., repeated lines, “equavalent,” undefined constants) hinder readability.
- Inconsistency in reported improvements (e.g., 29% vs 16% vs 33% against AFAMAP in different sections) needs reconciliation.
- Details missing: how σt or noise variance is set/estimated; how µi and Ki are chosen per appliance; precise solver settings; rounding or decoding from relaxed solutions.

Originality
- The specific use of aggregate constraints per chain over long horizons in AFHMMs, combined with a convex relaxation, is a neat and useful twist. However, the components—posterior regularization, convex relaxations for FHMMs, and NILM—are established. Novelty is moderate.

Significance
- For NILM, adding aggregate priors is practically meaningful and likely to be used. Beyond NILM, the idea is plausible in other BSS settings where totals are known. However, broader impact would be strengthened by demonstrations outside energy and by more thorough analysis of when constraints help vs harm.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated idea with a principled formulation and a clear empirical win over strong baselines on a realistic dataset.
- General technique potentially applicable beyond NILM.
- Clear connection to posterior regularization.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing details on rounding/discretization and interpretability of the recovered states.
- Limited analysis of robustness to mis-specified aggregate targets and of the role/tuning of λ.
- Limited scope of evaluation and some unclear or inconsistent reporting.
- Computational scalability via generic convex solvers is not convincingly demonstrated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Describe and evaluate a rounding/decoding step to obtain discrete state sequences (e.g., solve a Viterbi step with local potentials matching the relaxed marginals; or project S to the simplex vertices per t,i).
- Provide sensitivity analyses for λi and for mis-specified µ0, including adverse cases where national averages are misleading.
- Report solver details, runtime scaling with T, I, Ki, and compare against a specialized optimizer (e.g., ADMM or projected gradient exploiting structure) rather than generic CVX.
- Evaluate windowed SACs (daytime/nighttime) and report gains; include ablations per constraint type.
- Compare with duration/semi-Markov baselines on the same data; include REDD or other public NILM datasets for broader comparability.
- Clarify and unify the improvement percentages; add confidence intervals or effect sizes; specify the sample sizes for t-tests.
- Improve clarity and fix typographical issues; define σt estimation; justify the choice of Ki and µi estimation procedure.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper presents a sensible and practical idea with promising results, but key methodological details (discrete decoding), robustness analysis, and evidence for scalability/generalization are not yet at the level expected for NeurIPS. Addressing the above points would make the contribution substantially stronger.

Confidence
Medium.
