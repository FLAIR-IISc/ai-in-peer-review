This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework, aiming to improve blind source separation, specifically in the context of energy disaggregation. The core idea is to incorporate prior knowledge about the expected aggregate values of source signals (e.g., daily energy consumption of a toaster) as constraints during inference. The authors formulate this as a convex quadratic program, enabling efficient computation, and demonstrate significant performance gains on real-world energy disaggregation data.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the problem of identifiability in BSS and the practical importance of incorporating domain knowledge. It builds upon existing work in AFHMMs and related probabilistic models, referencing relevant literature including posterior regularization, the generalized expectation criterion, and measurement-based learning. The introduction of SACs feels like a natural and potentially powerful extension, addressing a key limitation of previous approaches—namely, *what* prior knowledge to include. The connection to posterior regularization, established in Section 6, further strengthens the theoretical grounding. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper effectively explains the challenges of energy disaggregation and the need for methods that address identifiability.
*   **Novelty:** The SAC approach represents a novel constraint for AFHMMs, bridging the gap between model flexibility and informed priors. The formulation as a CQP is a practical contribution.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM and the 29% improvement over AFAMAP are substantial and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   **Theoretical Justification:**  The connection to posterior regularization provides a sound theoretical basis for the proposed approach.
*   **Computational Efficiency:**  The conversion to a CQP allows for efficient inference, making the method scalable.
*   **Well-written and organized:** The paper is generally clear and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Parameter Tuning:** While the paper acknowledges the existence of tuning parameters (λi and δi), it lacks a detailed discussion of how these parameters were selected. The statement that λi were simply set to 1, while practical, feels somewhat arbitrary and may hide potential sensitivity to parameter choice.  A more thorough exploration of parameter sensitivity, perhaps through cross-validation, would be beneficial.
*   **Generalizability of SACs:**  The paper focuses primarily on energy disaggregation. While the authors claim the concept is general, a more extensive discussion of how SACs could be applied to other BSS problems (e.g., audio source separation) would strengthen the argument for broader relevance.  Are there scenarios where SACs are not applicable or might perform poorly?
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison is primarily against AFHMM and AFAMAP. Including a broader range of state-of-the-art disaggregation techniques (e.g., deep learning-based approaches) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the proposed method’s competitive advantage.
*   **Assumptions of Known µi0:** The method relies on having prior knowledge of the expected aggregate values (µi0). The paper acknowledges that these can be obtained from expert knowledge or surveys, but doesn’t address the potential impact of inaccuracies in these estimates.
*   **Relaxation and Optimality:** The paper highlights that the CQP solves a *relaxed* problem and optimality isn't guaranteed. While this is acknowledged, a more in-depth discussion of the potential gap between the relaxed solution and the true MAP estimate would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to addressing a challenging problem in blind source separation.
*   The empirical results demonstrate significant performance gains on a real-world dataset.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.
*   The CQP formulation enables efficient computation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The parameter tuning is not thoroughly investigated.
*   The generalizability beyond energy disaggregation is not fully explored.
*   The comparison to other state-of-the-art methods is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation. The introduction of SACs is a clever and effective way to incorporate prior knowledge into AFHMMs, leading to significant improvements in energy disaggregation performance. While some minor weaknesses exist, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the parameter tuning and generalizability concerns. Specifically, the authors should expand the discussion on how best to select λi and δi and provide more examples of potential applications beyond energy disaggregation.
