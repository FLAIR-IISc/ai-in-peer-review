This paper tackles the challenging problem of blind source separation, specifically within the context of energy disaggregation. The core idea – Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) – is a compelling and novel way to inject domain knowledge into Additive Factorial HMMs (AFHMMs). The authors observe that, for many BSS tasks, we often have reasonable expectations about the *total* energy consumed by each source, and leverage this via a constrained optimization problem. This is a smart move, particularly given the identifiability issues inherent in BSS. The formulation using convex quadratic programming is a practical contribution, and the reported 45% decrease in disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM is substantial. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The motivation is strong, and the connection to existing work in probabilistic modeling and energy disaggregation is well established. The authors correctly identify the limitations of previous approaches and position their work as addressing a key open question: *what* domain knowledge should be incorporated. The derivation of the CQP and the justification via posterior regularization are solid, and the inclusion of the toy data experiment is a helpful sanity check.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The SAC concept is genuinely new and offers a pragmatic solution to a long-standing problem in BSS. It’s a refreshing approach that moves beyond simply tweaking model architectures.
* **Significance:** The 45% improvement on real-world energy data is a significant result, demonstrating the practical value of the proposed method.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation and the derivation of the CQP appear to be correct. The connection to posterior regularization strengthens the theoretical grounding.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, even for readers unfamiliar with the specifics of energy disaggregation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Ablation Study:** While the comparison to AFAMAP is included, a more detailed ablation study exploring the sensitivity of the performance to the choice of λ (or δ) would be beneficial. It’s not entirely clear how robust the method is to misspecification of these hyperparameters.
* **Constraint Value Source:** The paper acknowledges that obtaining accurate prior knowledge for µi0 is crucial, but doesn’t delve deeply into practical strategies beyond referencing national surveys. Real-world application might require more sophisticated methods for estimating these values, and discussing this limitation would be worthwhile.
* **Generalizability:** While the application to energy disaggregation is convincing, the paper could benefit from briefly discussing the potential of SACs in *other* BSS domains (e.g., audio processing, computational biology) to strengthen the argument for broader applicability. A small illustrative example would be helpful.
* **Limited discussion of identifiability:** While the paper acknowledges the identifiability problem, it doesn’t fully discuss how SACs *mitigate* it. Does the constraint encourage more unique solutions, and can this be quantified?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core idea of SACs is novel and addresses a fundamental challenge in BSS.
* The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating a substantial improvement in disaggregation accuracy.
* The technical contributions (CQP formulation, posterior regularization connection) are solid.
* The paper is clearly written and well-motivated.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The ablation study is limited, and the sensitivity analysis is lacking.
* The discussion of real-world constraint value estimation is somewhat superficial.
* The generalizability of the approach could be better demonstrated.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend acceptance. While the paper isn’t perfect – and some aspects could be strengthened with further investigation – the novelty and potential impact of the SAC approach are substantial. This is a bold idea that tackles a difficult problem in a clever and effective way. I’m willing to overlook the minor flaws in validation and analysis given the significant conceptual contribution. The paper introduces a genuinely new direction for research in BSS and has the potential to inspire further work in this area. This is exactly the kind of innovative work that NIPS should be showcasing.
