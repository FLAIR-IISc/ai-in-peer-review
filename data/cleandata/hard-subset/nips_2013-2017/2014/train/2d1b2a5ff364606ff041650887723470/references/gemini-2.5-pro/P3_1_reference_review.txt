### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), with a specific focus on energy disaggregation. The authors identify the core challenge of identifiability in underdetermined BSS, where separated signals can be nonsensical mixtures of true sources (the "franken-appliance" problem). To mitigate this, they introduce Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), a novel and intuitive form of prior knowledge. The core idea is that for many sources, like home appliances, we have a reasonable prior expectation for their total consumption over a given period (e.g., a day).

The authors incorporate these constraints into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a standard model for this task. The SACs take the form of a quadratic penalty on the deviation of a source's estimated total aggregate from a known prior value. This penalty term makes the MAP inference problem non-Markovian and intractable for standard algorithms like Viterbi. The main technical contribution is to formulate a convex relaxation of this problem as a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The authors also draw a connection between their penalized objective and the posterior regularization framework.

Empirically, the proposed AFHMM+SAC model is evaluated on a challenging, low-frequency real-world energy dataset (HES). The results are strong, showing a 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to a standard AFHMM and a 29% reduction compared to the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model on a realistic subset of the data.

### Strengths (Reasons for acceptance)

1.  **Novel and Practical Constraint:** The core idea of using Signal Aggregate Constraints is simple, intuitive, and highly practical. It directly addresses a well-known weakness of BSS models in this domain. This type of prior knowledge is often available from domain expertise or external data sources (e.g., national energy surveys), making the approach widely applicable. The idea is also general enough to be potentially useful in other BSS domains beyond energy disaggregation.

2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a convincing empirical evaluation. The use of the low-frequency HES dataset is a strong point, as it represents a more challenging and realistic scenario than the high-frequency datasets often used in prior work. The performance improvements over both the baseline AFHMM and a strong state-of-the-art method (AFAMAP) are substantial and statistically significant, clearly demonstrating the value of the proposed constraints.

3.  **Sound Technical Approach:** While the individual components are not new, their combination is effective. Posing the difficult MAP inference problem as a penalized objective and then solving a convex relaxation is a principled and powerful technique. The CQP formulation provides a tractable and scalable inference algorithm. Grounding the approach in the posterior regularization framework adds theoretical justification.

### Weaknesses (Areas for improvement)

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The main novelty lies in the *type* of constraint introduced, not in the optimization machinery. The CQP relaxation for AFHMMs builds very closely on the approach presented in Kolter & Jaakkola (2012) for their AFAMAP model. The current work can be seen as adding a quadratic penalty term to that existing CQP framework. While this is a valid and effective extension, the paper could be clearer about the extent to which it leverages this prior work in its technical formulation.

2.  **Sensitivity to Prior and Hyperparameters:** The method's performance is critically dependent on the quality of the prior aggregate values (`µ_i0`) and the choice of the penalty parameters (`λ_i`). The toy experiment provides a basic sensitivity analysis for `µ_i0`, but a more thorough investigation on the real-world data would be beneficial. More importantly, the paper states that `λ_i` was simply set to 1 for all experiments. This is a strong simplification. A discussion on how this hyperparameter could be set in practice (e.g., via cross-validation) and an analysis of the model's sensitivity to `λ_i` is a notable omission.

3.  **Details of the CQP Solution:** The paper states that the relaxed problem is solved using CVX. The output of this solver is a set of fractional state indicator variables (`Sitk` in [0, 1]). It is not specified how these fractional values are converted back into a final, discrete state sequence to compute the disaggregated signals (`x̂_it`). Is a simple rounding scheme used, or are the fractional expectations used directly? This is a crucial implementation detail required for reproducibility.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   **Clarity of CQP Formulation:** In Section 5, could the authors explicitly state the relationship between their CQP formulation and the one used for AFAMAP? It appears the main difference is the addition of the `Λ_i` and `μ̃_i` terms, which arise directly from the SAC penalty. Acknowledging this explicitly would better position the contribution.

*   **Source of Priors (`µ_i0`):** The paper uses "National Total Consumption" (NTC) derived from the training data of the same dataset. This is a reasonable proxy, but in a real-world deployment, this prior might come from an external, potentially mismatched, source. It would strengthen the paper to discuss the potential impact of distribution shift between the source of the prior and the target household.

*   **Generality of Constraints:** The authors briefly mention extending the single aggregate constraint to multiple, time-windowed constraints (e.g., day vs. night). This is a compelling feature of the framework. While a full evaluation is likely beyond the scope of this paper, a short discussion in the conclusion about the potential benefits and challenges of using more fine-grained constraints would be welcome.

### Evaluation Against NIPS Criteria

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by strong experimental results on a relevant, real-world dataset. The methodology is sound. The main weakness is the lack of detail regarding hyperparameter selection and the handling of the CQP's fractional output.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the model is presented logically, and the experiments are easy to understand. The "franken-appliance" analogy is particularly effective.

*   **Originality:** The work's originality is good. It lies primarily in the conceptual contribution of Signal Aggregate Constraints as a form of domain knowledge for BSS. While the underlying optimization technique is not novel, its application to this new, effective constraint type is a valuable contribution.

*   **Significance:** The paper has high significance. Energy disaggregation is an important problem with both academic and industrial relevance. The proposed method demonstrates a marked improvement over existing state-of-the-art, and the core idea of SACs is generalizable to other domains. This work is likely to be built upon by other researchers in the field.

**Overall Recommendation:** This is a strong paper with a clear and useful contribution. The introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints is an elegant solution to a real problem in BSS, backed by impressive empirical results. The weaknesses identified are minor and can be addressed in a final version. I recommend acceptance.
