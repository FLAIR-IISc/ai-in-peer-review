Summary
This paper addresses identifiability in blind source separation for additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs) that encode prior knowledge about the aggregate (e.g., total) contribution of each latent source over a time horizon. The authors focus on energy disaggregation (NILM) as a motivating application, where appliance-level totals are often approximately known from surveys or prior usage statistics. They incorporate SACs into the AFHMM via a quadratic penalty on the deviation between the sum over time of each chain’s state-dependent mean and a target aggregate µi0. Because these constraints are non-Markovian, they propose a convex quadratic program (CQP) for a relaxed version of the MAP problem by introducing pairwise “transition” variables and relaxing integrality. They also show the equivalence to posterior regularization. On a toy example, they show robustness of performance to incorrect µ0. On the HES dataset with ultra-low frequency measurements, AFHMM+SAC outperforms AFHMM and AFAMAP [Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS-12], both when mains are synthesized and when true mains are used; reported improvements include ~45% NDE reduction over AFHMM and ~29–33% over AFAMAP in some settings.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on factorial HMMs [Ghahramani & Jordan], AFAMAP [Kolter & Jaakkola], semi-Markov NILM approaches [Johnson & Willsky; Kim et al.], and posterior regularization [Ganchev et al.]. The idea of constraining aggregate statistics is related to measurement-based learning [Liang et al.] and generalized expectations [Mann & McCallum], but prior work emphasized expectations of local features; here, global sum constraints over entire sequences are used in a structured latent-variable model and solved via a convex relaxation. The NILM literature has used other priors (e.g., at-most-one chain change, duration priors) but, to my knowledge, not convex relaxations with global aggregate constraints.

Quality
- Modeling: The SAC penalty is well-motivated and clearly integrated into the AFHMM objective. The posterior-regularization perspective is sound and helpful.
- Optimization: Introducing transition variables Hit and relaxing {0,1} to [0,1] yields a convex quadratic objective with linear constraints; Λi and Vt are PSD, so the relaxed problem is a CQP. This is technically sound.
- Inference details: One missing piece is how relaxed solutions (fractional Sit, Hit) are converted into discrete sequences or predictions. If the output is x̂it = sumk Sitk µik without rounding, please state this explicitly and discuss implications for metrics and interpretability.
- Empirics: The paper evaluates on a relevant, challenging dataset (HES) and uses both NDE and SAE; significance testing is reported. However, baselines are limited to AFHMM and AFAMAP; comparison to duration/semi-Markov NILM and more recent constrained FHMMs would strengthen the case. Hyperparameters (λi) are fixed to 1; more evidence on sensitivity and a practical tuning strategy would be useful. The toy study illustrates sensitivity to misspecified µ0, which is good.

Clarity
Overall well-written, with a clean problem statement and derivations. Section 5 is notation-heavy; a schematic of the relaxed graph (variables and constraints) and a brief algorithm box would aid readability. Some references lack years/details; please standardize. Clarify:
- How θ are learned (details on estimating µi, transitions, noise σt).
- How outputs are generated from fractional S; any rounding or decoding?
- What T, I, Ki values are typical; solver settings; and wall-clock times on representative instances.
- How µ0 is obtained in the “with mains” setting (NTC computed per household, per day, or global?).

Originality
The specific combination of global sum constraints over FHMM chains, the convex quadratic relaxation for MAP with non-Markovian penalties, and application to NILM appears novel. The posterior-regularization linkage is appropriate but not the primary technical novelty. The idea to impose aggregates over arbitrary windows (not just entire time span) is also appealing and general.

Significance
The approach addresses a core limitation (identifiability) in BSS/NILM with a pragmatic and widely applicable prior. Improvements over strong baselines on realistic data and low sampling rates suggest practical impact. The formulation is general and could transfer to other BSS problems (audio, biology) where aggregate priors exist. The main caveat is the reliance on good µ0 and the scalability of general-purpose CQP solvers.

Strengths
- Clear, principled incorporation of domain knowledge via SACs.
- Convex relaxation with a tractable CQP; connection to posterior regularization.
- Empirical gains on a challenging, real-world dataset; statistical tests reported.
- Practical insight that even coarse aggregate priors (NTC) can help a lot.

Weaknesses and questions
1) Rounding/decoding: How are fractional Sit used? If no rounding is applied, discuss interpretability and whether the solution respects HMM structure in practice. If rounding is used, describe the procedure and its effect on complexity/performance.
2) Hyperparameters: λi fixed to 1. Provide guidance for setting λi without appliance-level labels (e.g., via held-out days with only mains, or heuristic calibration to match expected aggregate errors).
3) µ0 availability and robustness: Results with NTC are promising, but how sensitive is performance to bias/variance in µ0? Consider ablations with noisy µ0 drawn from realistic distributions, and time-windowed constraints (Section 4.2 generalization).
4) Baselines: Please compare to semi-Markov NILM and non-homogeneous FHMMs, or at least discuss why such baselines were omitted. Also consider AFAMAP+SAC to show complementarity.
5) Scalability: CVX is convenient but may not scale to longer sequences/larger Ki. Report problem sizes (T, I, Ki), solver tolerance, and per-instance times; discuss specialized solvers (e.g., ADMM exploiting block structure) or warm starts across time windows.
6) Unmodeled loads: In “with mains,” mains includes appliances not in the model. How does this mismatch affect the SAC (which only constrains modeled chains)? Consider adding a slack/noise component to absorb residual energy to avoid bias.
7) Parameter learning: More detail on how µi, πi, and transitions are estimated (and updated or not) would improve reproducibility. Do you ever re-estimate θ after inferring S?

Minor comments
- Tighten Section 5 notation and check consistency of indices. Define C and make clear all constants independent of S,H.
- Standardize references (years, venues) and ensure all are complete.
- Consider releasing code or a minimal solver to aid adoption.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel and principled method; technically sound convex relaxation.
- Addresses a real identifiability pain point with a widely applicable prior.
- Strong empirical gains on realistic NILM data; statistically validated.
- Clear presentation; connection to posterior regularization.

Cons:
- Missing details on decoding from relaxed solutions.
- Limited baselines; lack of ablation on λ and robustness of µ0 in realistic settings.
- Scalability and solver practicality not fully characterized.
- Some reproducibility details (parameter learning) are sparse.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a useful, general idea with solid technical grounding and compelling empirical improvements. Addressing decoding, scalability, and baseline breadth (even in rebuttal) would further strengthen it.
