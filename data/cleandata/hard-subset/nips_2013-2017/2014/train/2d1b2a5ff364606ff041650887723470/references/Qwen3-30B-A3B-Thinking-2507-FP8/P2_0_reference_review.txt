

# Review: Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation

## Summary
This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation. SACs constrain the total aggregate values of source signals (e.g., total energy consumption of an appliance over a day) to be close to known values. The authors incorporate these constraints into Additive Factorial HMMs (AFHMMs), transforming the non-convex optimization problem into a convex quadratic program that can be efficiently solved. The approach is evaluated on both toy data and real-world energy disaggregation data, showing significant improvements over baseline methods.

## Strengths

**Quality (Strong):** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors provide a clear theoretical justification for SACs, showing how they connect to posterior regularization frameworks. The mathematical derivation of transforming the constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program is sound and well-justified. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, using both synthetic data to validate the approach and real-world energy data to demonstrate practical impact. The authors compare against relevant baselines (AFHMM, AFAMAP), use appropriate metrics (NDE, SAE), and perform statistical significance tests (paired t-tests), showing that AFHMM+SAC improves NDE by 33% and SAE by 16% over AFAMAP. The computational complexity analysis shows the method scales similarly to existing approaches.

**Clarity (Strong):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear, the motivation is compelling (addressing the "franken-appliance" problem in energy disaggregation), and the technical exposition flows logically. The authors provide sufficient mathematical detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the work. The connection between SACs and posterior regularization is particularly well-explained.

**Originality (Good):** SACs represent a novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge into BSS problems. While prior work has explored constraints on state transitions (e.g., AFAMAP), the authors' focus on aggregate constraints across the entire time series is distinctive. The paper clearly differentiates from related work (Section 2), noting that most prior approaches constrain state transitions rather than aggregate values. The connection to posterior regularization provides a theoretical foundation that elevates the approach beyond a heuristic.

**Significance (Good):** The work addresses an important real-world problem (energy disaggregation) with practical impact. The 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM is substantial, and the 29% improvement over a recent state-of-the-art approach demonstrates clear advancement. The authors' argument that SACs could be broadly applicable to other BSS domains (beyond energy disaggregation) strengthens the significance. The paper provides a general framework that could be adopted in multiple application areas.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Scope of Evaluation (Moderate):** While the authors claim SACs are "general across a wide variety of domains," the evaluation is limited to energy disaggregation. A more compelling case for general applicability would require testing on at least one other BSS domain (e.g., audio processing or computational biology). The paper mentions these applications in the introduction but doesn't demonstrate them.

**Sensitivity to Constraint Values (Moderate):** The paper assumes the aggregate values (µi0) are known, which might be difficult to obtain in practice. While the authors use national survey data as a proxy, they don't adequately explore how sensitive the results are to errors in these values. The toy data experiments show that performance degrades as the constraint values deviate from the true aggregates, but this sensitivity analysis is limited to synthetic data.

**Limited Exploration of Generalized Constraints (Moderate):** The paper mentions a generalization of SACs to multiple time periods (Section 4.2), but this extension is not evaluated in the experiments. The authors could have demonstrated the value of these more complex constraints in the energy disaggregation context (e.g., accounting for time-of-day usage patterns).

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of blind source separation by introducing a novel and practically useful constraint mechanism. The technical quality is excellent, the evaluation is rigorous, and the significance of the results is clear. While the scope of evaluation could be broader, the paper is well-positioned to make an impact in energy disaggregation and potentially other domains.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), introduces a novel constraint approach (Originality), and demonstrates significant improvements on a practical problem (Significance). The minor limitations do not detract from the overall strength of the contribution.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper provides a valuable framework for incorporating domain knowledge into BSS problems through aggregate constraints, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its practical utility. The connection to posterior regularization is particularly elegant and well-executed. This work advances the state of the art in energy disaggregation and offers a generalizable approach that could benefit multiple application areas.
