## Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of identifiability in blind source separation, specifically within the context of energy disaggregation. The authors propose incorporating “Signal Aggregate Constraints” (SACs) into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM). The core idea is to leverage prior knowledge about the total energy consumption of individual appliances to guide the inference process, improving disaggregation accuracy. They formulate the problem as a convex quadratic program (CQP) for efficient computation and demonstrate a significant performance improvement on real-world energy disaggregation data, achieving a 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over a state-of-the-art alternative.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon existing work in energy disaggregation using AFHMMs and related models (e.g., AFAMAP, semi-Markov models). It appropriately acknowledges the limitations of these approaches in addressing identifiability. The authors situate their work within the broader context of incorporating prior knowledge into probabilistic models, referencing posterior regularization, the generalized expectation criterion, and measurement-based learning.  They correctly position SACs as a novel approach to defining useful constraints within this framework. The connection to probabilistic databases with aggregate constraints is also noted, but the authors rightly point out the limitations of those approaches to complex graphical models.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Core Problem:** Identifiability is a significant hurdle in BSS and energy disaggregation. The paper tackles this head-on with a practical and theoretically sound approach.
* **Novelty of SACs:** The concept of Signal Aggregate Constraints is a valuable contribution. It provides a principled way to incorporate domain knowledge that is often readily available in applications like energy disaggregation.
* **Efficient Computation:** Formulating the problem as a CQP is a key strength.  It allows for efficient inference, making the approach scalable to real-world datasets.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The performance gains on the HES dataset are impressive, particularly the 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error. The comparison to a state-of-the-art method (AFAMAP) further strengthens the findings.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations are clear and the explanations are accessible.
* **Thorough Evaluation:**  The experiments include both a toy dataset for demonstrating robustness and a real-world dataset with a comprehensive evaluation.



**Weaknesses:**

* **Sensitivity to Constraint Values:** While the toy data experiment demonstrates robustness, the dependence on accurate prior knowledge (µi0) is a potential limitation. The NTC (National Total Consumption) results highlight this, showing less impressive gains than with the TTC (True Total Consumption). This should be more deeply discussed.
* **Limited Exploration of Constraint Generalization:**  The paper mentions the possibility of multiple constraints over time windows, but doesn’t explore this in detail. It would be beneficial to see some experiments with more complex constraint structures.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The paper states that λi = 1 for all experiments. While a justification is given (simplicity), a more thorough investigation of parameter tuning via cross-validation would strengthen the results.
* **Computational Complexity Discussion:** While the authors state the CQP scales polynomially, a more detailed analysis of the runtime complexity with respect to the number of states and chains would be helpful.
* **No discussion on model selection:** The authors assume the number of states is 3, without any justification or comparison with other values.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical problem in BSS and energy disaggregation with a novel and theoretically grounded solution.
* Demonstrates significant empirical improvements on a real-world dataset.
* Formulates the problem in a computationally efficient manner.
* Clearly written and well-organized.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Reliance on accurate prior knowledge for SACs.
* Limited exploration of more complex constraint structures.
* Lack of comprehensive parameter tuning.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation and energy disaggregation. The proposed SACs are a clever and effective way to address the identifiability problem, and the CQP formulation enables efficient computation. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them.  The empirical results are compelling, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper, contingent upon the authors addressing the following minor revisions:

1.  **Expand the discussion of the sensitivity to prior knowledge (µi0).**  Discuss strategies for obtaining reasonable estimates of these values when true values are not available.
2.  **Briefly explore the impact of different constraint structures** (e.g., multiple constraints over time windows) in an ablation study.
3.  **Include a brief discussion of parameter tuning** and potentially present results with cross-validation.
4. **Briefly explain the choice of number of states and consider model selection for it.**




**Qualitative Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** 8.5/10 - Technically sound, well-supported, and a complete piece of work.
*   **Clarity:** 9/10 - Very clearly written and organized.
*   **Originality:** 8/10 - Novel approach to addressing a well-known problem.
*   **Significance:** 8.5/10 -  The results are important and likely to be useful to researchers and practitioners in the field.
