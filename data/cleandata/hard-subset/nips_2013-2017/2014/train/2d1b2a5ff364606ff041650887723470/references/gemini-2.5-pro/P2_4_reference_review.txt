**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of identifiability in blind source separation, particularly within the context of Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs) applied to energy disaggregation. The authors propose a novel method for incorporating domain knowledge by introducing Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). These constraints encourage the total sum of each separated source signal over a time period to be close to a known prior value (e.g., the expected daily energy consumption of a specific appliance). As these constraints are non-Markovian and make standard inference intractable, the authors formulate the MAP inference problem as an integer quadratic program. They then present a tractable solution by relaxing the problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP). The paper also provides a theoretical justification for their approach by linking it to the posterior regularization framework. The method is evaluated on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, where it demonstrates a significant reduction in error compared to both the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The paper's primary strength lies in its simple, intuitive, and highly effective core idea. The problem of identifiability ("franken-appliances") is a well-known and critical barrier to the practical application of AFHMMs in energy disaggregation. The proposed solution—constraining the total energy—is a natural and powerful form of prior knowledge that has been largely overlooked in this modeling context. The experimental results strongly validate the approach, showing substantial improvements over existing methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty and Significance of the core idea:** The introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints is a novel and valuable contribution. This type of non-local, summary-statistic constraint is a new way to inject domain knowledge into factorial models, and the paper convincingly demonstrates its power. The idea is also generalizable to other BSS domains where prior knowledge about source totals might be available.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper shows marked improvements over relevant baselines, including a 29% error reduction over the state-of-the-art AFAMAP on a challenging, real-world dataset. The experimental design is sound, particularly the use of both a realistic prior (National Total Consumption) and an oracle prior (True Total Consumption) to showcase the practical utility and the upper-bound potential of the method.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed inference method is well-founded. Recognizing that the problem is a non-convex IQP and then relaxing it to a CQP is a standard and appropriate technique for finding a tractable approximate solution. The derivation is clear and the connection to the posterior regularization framework adds theoretical depth and rigor.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clear, the model is built up logically from the baseline AFHMM, and the experimental section is easy to follow.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Limited Discussion on Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper sets the regularization parameter λi = 1 for all experiments without justification or a sensitivity analysis. While the results are strong even with this simple choice, the work would be more complete with a discussion of how λ might be set in practice (e.g., via cross-validation) and how sensitive the model's performance is to this choice. This is a minor weakness, but it leaves an open question for practitioners.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** While the authors state that the CQP has polynomial time complexity and scales similarly to baselines in their experiments, CQP solvers can become slow for very long time series (large T) or a large number of states. A brief discussion of the practical limitations and potential avenues for more scalable inference (e.g., ADMM) could strengthen the paper.

**Detailed Evaluation**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by a solid experimental evaluation on a real-world dataset. The authors are honest about the difficulty of the problem (non-Markovian constraints) and propose a technically sound relaxation. The experimental methodology, including the comparison with an oracle, is rigorous and adds confidence to the results.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The problem is well-motivated with the "franken-appliance" example. The notation in Section 5, while dense, is necessary for the CQP formulation and is clearly defined. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the method and its implementation.

**Originality:** The main contribution—the concept of Signal Aggregate Constraints for AFHMMs—is novel and distinct from prior work on constraining HMMs. Previous work like AFAMAP focused on local, temporal constraints (at most one state change at a time), while this work introduces a global, non-local constraint. While the use of CQP relaxations for inference in graphical models is not new in itself, its application to this new objective function with SACs is a key part of the contribution. The authors correctly position their work relative to existing literature.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for the field of energy disaggregation and potentially for other applications of BSS. A nearly 30% improvement over a recent state-of-the-art method is a substantial advance. The proposed constraint is based on a type of domain knowledge that is often readily available (e.g., from manufacturer specifications or national surveys), making the method practical and widely applicable. The simplicity and effectiveness of the idea make it highly likely that other researchers will build upon this work.

**Final Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper that introduces a novel, simple, and effective idea for a significant problem. The technical approach is sound, the writing is clear, and the experimental results are compelling. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
