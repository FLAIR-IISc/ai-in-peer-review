### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), with a specific focus on energy disaggregation, using Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs). The key challenge in this domain is the inherent unidentifiability of the sources, which can lead to unrealistic or uninterpretable separated signals (the "franken-appliance" problem). The authors' central contribution is the introduction of "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs), a novel form of prior knowledge that regularizes the model by encouraging the total sum of each source signal over a given time period to be close to a known target value (e.g., the expected total daily energy consumption of a specific appliance).

Because these constraints are non-Markovian (i.e., they depend on the entire state sequence), standard inference algorithms like Viterbi are not applicable. To overcome this, the authors incorporate the SACs as a quadratic penalty term in the MAP inference objective. They then formulate a relaxed version of this non-convex integer programming problem as a convex quadratic program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The paper also demonstrates a connection between their penalized objective and the posterior regularization framework. The proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on a large, real-world energy dataset, where it shows significant performance improvements over both the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art model, AFAMAP.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Simple, Intuitive, and Effective Idea:** The core idea of using prior knowledge on the aggregate value of a signal is simple, well-motivated, and highly practical. In many domains, especially energy disaggregation, this type of information is readily available from historical data or national surveys but is not naturally captured by the local transition and emission structure of an HMM.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation on a challenging, low-frequency, real-world dataset (HES). The reported results are impressive: a 45% decrease in error compared to the baseline AFHMM and a 29% decrease compared to the state-of-the-art AFAMAP on the most realistic task. This demonstrates a clear and significant advance.
3.  **Sound Technical Approach and Formulation:** The method for incorporating the SACs as a penalty and solving the resulting inference problem via a CQP relaxation is technically sound. While the relaxation technique itself builds on prior work, its application to this new and useful class of constraints is well-executed. The authors also add theoretical depth by connecting their formulation to the posterior regularization framework.
4.  **Well-Written and Clear Presentation:** The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is established effectively with the "franken-appliance" example, and the progression from the problem to the solution is logical.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

1.  **Limited Novelty in the Optimization Method:** The main technical tool used to solve the inference problem—formulating a CQP by introducing auxiliary variables for state transitions—is heavily inspired by, and very similar to, the approach used in the AFAMAP paper [12]. The novelty lies almost entirely in the *type* of constraint being added, rather than the optimization machinery used to handle it.
2.  **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The penalty parameter λ, which balances the data likelihood against the SAC, is a crucial hyperparameter. The authors simply set λ=1 for all experiments. While this simplifies the evaluation, it leaves open important questions about the model's sensitivity to this parameter and how it would be set in a real-world scenario (e.g., via cross-validation). A sensitivity analysis would have strengthened the paper.
3.  **Assumes Known Model Parameters:** The paper focuses exclusively on the inference problem, assuming that the HMM parameters (transition probabilities, emission means) are learned beforehand from training data. This sidesteps the more challenging unsupervised setting where parameters and hidden states must be learned jointly. While this is a reasonable simplification to isolate the contribution, it limits the immediate applicability to fully unsupervised scenarios.

### Detailed Evaluation

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is sound, and the claims are rigorously supported by extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world data. The evaluation is careful, comparing against appropriate baselines and even an oracle setting (TTC) to demonstrate the potential of the approach. The work is complete and presented as a finished contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper's clarity is excellent. The writing is clear, the structure is logical, and the motivation is compelling. The mathematical notation, while dense in Section 5, is standard for this field and is well-defined. An expert reader should have sufficient information to understand and likely reproduce the work.

**Originality:** The originality is good, but not groundbreaking. The primary novel contribution is the conceptualization and application of Signal Aggregate Constraints for BSS. This is a genuinely new and useful idea for incorporating domain knowledge into this class of models. The connection to posterior regularization also adds a nice theoretical touch. However, as noted, the core optimization strategy is an adaptation of an existing technique [12]. Therefore, the paper represents a clever and effective combination of a new idea with a known technique, rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic breakthrough.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. It addresses an important and practical problem (energy disaggregation) and provides a method that demonstrably advances the state of the art. The core idea of SACs is generalizable and could inspire similar work in other BSS domains where aggregate statistics are known (e.g., total gene expression levels over a day, average phoneme duration in speech). Given the strong empirical results, researchers and practitioners in energy disaggregation are very likely to use and build upon these ideas. The paper successfully shows how to tackle a difficult problem in a better way than previous research.
