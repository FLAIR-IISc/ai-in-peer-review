Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses identifiability in blind source separation for additive factorial HMMs (AFHMM) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs): priors on the total (or windowed) sum of each latent source’s signal over time. The idea is that in many domains (e.g., energy disaggregation), while the exact timing is uncertain, we often have good priors on total usage per source over a day or similar horizon. The authors cast MAP inference with SACs as a convex quadratic program (CQP) via a standard relaxation with auxiliary transition variables, show an equivalence to posterior regularization, and demonstrate substantial empirical gains on challenging low-frequency NILM data (HES) compared to AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12). The connection to generalized expectation/measurement-based learning (Mann & McCallum; Liang et al.) and posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.) is discussed; the novelty is a practical, domain-driven family of aggregate constraints and a tractable convex relaxation for AFHMMs. Results show notable NDE and SAE improvements and comparable runtime to AFHMM/AFAMAP.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: SACs are a simple, widely applicable way to inject strong domain knowledge that directly tackles identifiability for additive models. This is a bold, broadly useful idea that goes beyond NILM and could benefit audio and genomics BSS.
- Sound formulation: The quadratic penalty on aggregate sums is natural and interpretable (Gaussian prior on totals). The convex relaxation using S/H variables and PSD structure mirrors prior convex MAP relaxations for factorial models, but adapted to non-Markovian aggregate penalties.
- Clear link to posterior regularization: Nicely situates the approach in a principled framework and clarifies what distribution is being optimized.
- Empirical significance: On realistic, low-frequency HES data, AFHMM+SAC materially outperforms AFHMM and AFAMAP in both NDE and SAE, with reported statistical significance, and maintains similar computational cost.
- Practical extension: The windowed constraints are a natural and potentially very impactful extension (e.g., day/night usage).

Weaknesses and questions:
- Rounding/decoding from the relaxed CQP: The method solves a continuous relaxation. The paper does not describe how discrete state sequences are recovered for evaluation (argmax per time? projection? warm-starting a discrete optimizer?). Clarify the decoding procedure and its effect on HMM temporal consistency.
- Sensitivity to mis-specified µ0: Although a toy robustness plot is shown, real-data sensitivity is not studied. Since µ0 is critical and estimated from national or training statistics (NTC), a sensitivity/ablation (varying µ0 bias and variance) on HES would increase confidence and guide practitioners.
- Hyperparameter selection: λi is fixed to 1 in all experiments. This understates the method’s potential and obscures robustness. Provide guidance (e.g., cross-validation on training days, or heuristic scaling by variance of totals) and at least a small sweep on HES.
- Comparisons: AFAMAP is a strong baseline, but related constrained models (semi-Markov/HSMM, one-change-per-time in non-homogeneous HMMs, k-segment constraints) are only cited. Given the clear significance claims, a comparison to a semi-Markov variant or a contemporaneous NILM baseline would strengthen the case.
- Scalability and solver: CVX is convenient but not representative of practical scalability. The QP has exploitable block structure (time-local quadratic terms plus low-rank global penalty) and linear transition constraints. Reporting problem sizes (I, Ki, T), number of variables/constraints, and wall-clock scaling, or a custom solver using block-tridiagonal structure, would increase confidence for larger T or I.
- Theoretical guarantees: No bounds on the relaxation’s integrality gap or conditions under which the relaxation is tight are provided. While not essential for acceptance, a brief discussion (e.g., empirical observation of near-integral solutions) would help.
- Clarity/details: Some notational density (e.g., definition of Vt, ut, Λi) could be streamlined; minor typos and incomplete references suggest a need for proofreading. More detail on parameter learning for θ (transitions/initials) would aid reproducibility.

Quality:
Technically coherent and correctly connects to posterior regularization. The convex relaxation is standard but well-adapted to the new constraints. Empirical evaluation is solid for NILM, though broader baselines and sensitivity analyses would improve rigor.

Clarity:
Generally clear, but decoding, training of θ, hyperparameter choices, and some notation could be clarified. Figures/tables referenced but not shown in submission text make it hard to fully assess.

Originality:
Moderate to good. The AFHMM and convex relaxations are known; posterior regularization is known. The novel contribution is the specific, impactful class of aggregate constraints for additive models plus an implementable convex formulation. This is a meaningful new direction rather than a minor tweak.

Significance:
High for NILM and potentially broader BSS. The idea is simple, general, and seems to deliver sizable gains on challenging data. Likely to be adopted by practitioners.

Pros for acceptance:
- Introduces a simple, general, and impactful prior class (SACs) for additive latent models.
- Effective convex formulation and connection to posterior regularization.
- Strong empirical improvements on realistic low-frequency NILM data.
- Broad applicability beyond the target domain.

Cons against acceptance:
- Missing details on decoding from the relaxation and training of θ.
- Limited comparative baselines and hyperparameter tuning.
- Scalability via CVX not convincingly addressed.
- Minor clarity and reference issues.

Recommendation:
Accept (weak accept). The conceptual contribution of signal aggregate constraints for AFHMMs and the demonstrated gains on realistic data outweigh the methodological and presentation shortcomings. Addressing decoding, sensitivity to µ0/λ, and scalability would further strengthen the camera-ready.

Confidence: Medium.
