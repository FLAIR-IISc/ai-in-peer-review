Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses blind source separation of a single aggregated time series by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs): soft constraints that the total (e.g., sum over time) of each latent source should be near a specified value. The model is an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) with known emission levels per state. Since the SACs are non-Markovian, the authors derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) by relaxing the discrete HMM variables and introducing transition-indicator variables, yielding a concave quadratic objective with linear constraints; they solve it via CVX. They also show the SAC objective is a specific instantiation of posterior regularization (PR). Empirically, on toy data and the HES low-frequency energy dataset, AFHMM+SAC improves normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) compared to AFHMM and AFAMAP.

This work sits in a line of constraining latent-variable models: AFAMAP’s “at most one chain changes at a time” constraint, duration/semi-Markov constraints, and global expectation constraints frameworks (GE, PR, measurement-based learning). The paper’s contribution is to identify totals over time (aggregates) as a general, practically available form of side information, and to show a tractable convex relaxation for AFHMM with such constraints.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The convexity argument is clear: the quadratic terms are negative semidefinite (−S^TΛS, −(1/2)S^T V S with Λ, V PSD), making the objective concave; constraints are linear after relaxing S,H∈[0,1], thus a convex optimization (maximize concave). The standard trick with transition variables H ensures Markov consistency under integrality, and becomes a linear consistency constraint in the relaxation. The PR derivation aligns with Ganchev et al. (2010).
- Correctness and support: The toy experiment sensibly probes sensitivity to mismatch in the aggregate targets µ0. On HES, the reported improvements vs AFHMM and AFAMAP are substantial and supported with paired t-tests. However, important details are missing/unclear:
  - Rounding/decoding: the relaxation yields fractional S; how are discrete states handled, if at all? If purely continuous S are used to reconstruct x̂, that should be stated, with implications for interpretability.
  - Hyperparameters: λi are fixed to 1 without tuning; this could disadvantage baselines or understate achievable gains with proper CV. Noise variances σt, K per appliance, and AFAMAP hyperparameters are not discussed; fairness of comparison could be improved.
  - Scalability: Solving a CQP via CVX at minute-level T and many appliances may be challenging; the claim of similar runtime to AFAMAP is surprising and should be quantified with more detail (problem sizes, solver tolerances, iteration counts). Nonetheless, reported times (in referenced tables) suggest it was tractable for daily windows.
- Completeness: The work focuses on MAP inference with fixed θ. Estimation of θ by k-means for emissions and simple estimates for transitions is reasonable, but stronger baselines (e.g., duration models, or AFAMAP with tuned parameters) would strengthen the case. No ablations on windowed SACs (which the paper motivates as natural) are shown.

Clarity
- The high-level idea and motivation are clear, and the mathematical construction is mostly well presented. The notation is dense with some typos/formatting issues; a short algorithmic summary (inputs, outputs, solver setup, decoding) would improve reproducibility. Explicitly stating that convexity comes from the negative of PSD forms, and clarifying boundary constraints for H at t=1, would help. Figures/tables are referenced but not shown here; assuming they are present, the narrative is coherent.

Originality
- While PR/GE/measurement-based learning are known, identifying appliance-level daily totals as a reusable, general side information type for BSS and deriving a CQP for AFHMM with these constraints is a novel and concrete contribution. The connection to PR is correctly acknowledged and situates the work conceptually. To my knowledge, a convex relaxation specifically tailored to additive FHMMs with aggregate constraints is new.

Significance
- Practical impact: Energy disaggregation at low sampling rates is an important, challenging problem. The demonstrated 29–45% improvements vs strong baselines on realistic data suggest the approach could be useful in practice, especially for applications where source totals (or reasonable priors on them) are available from surveys or domain knowledge.
- Generality: The aggregate-constraint idea is likely useful beyond energy (e.g., audio, biology) where source totals or budgets are known approximately. The relaxed convex formulation may serve as a template for incorporating other global measurements.
- Limitations: Dependence on the quality of µ0 is a key limitation; while the toy analysis shows graceful degradation, more realistic robustness tests (e.g., biased priors, per-appliance variability) would help. The relaxation may hinder interpretability of discrete usage patterns; the method optimizes reconstruction/aggregate accuracy more than event detection.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated idea: leverage aggregate priors that are often available.
- Sound convex formulation; clean link to posterior regularization.
- Significant improvements on a hard, low-frequency real dataset; statistical tests included.
- Simple to implement with off-the-shelf solvers; comparable runtime reported.

Cons
- Reliance on CVX raises scalability concerns; limited analysis of computational trade-offs.
- Fixed λi and limited hyperparameter tuning; unclear fairness of baseline tuning.
- Lack of discussion on decoding/rounding and interpretability of relaxed solutions.
- Evaluation focuses on NDE/SAE; no event-level metrics or per-appliance breakdowns.
- Minor clarity/notation issues; missing ablations on windowed SACs.

Questions and suggestions
- How are fractional S converted to predictions? If no rounding, can you comment on interpretability and whether rounding hurts performance?
- Provide runtime vs T, I, Ki scaling and solver details; consider a specialized first-order solver.
- Tune λi (e.g., via CV per household) and report sensitivity; consider uncertainty-aware priors (variance around µ0).
- Evaluate windowed/daytime SACs and robustness to biased µ0.
- Compare against duration/semi-Markov models and AFAMAP with stronger priors to solidify the empirical case.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a simple, principled, and practically effective way to inject aggregate side information into AFHMMs via a convex relaxation, with convincing gains on a challenging real dataset. Some concerns remain about scalability, baseline tuning, and clarity of the relaxed decoding, but the idea is novel and useful, and the technical treatment is sound.
