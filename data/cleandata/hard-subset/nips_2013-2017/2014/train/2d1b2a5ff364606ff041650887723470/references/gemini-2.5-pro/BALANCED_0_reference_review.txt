### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), with a specific focus on energy disaggregation. The authors correctly identify that a key challenge in this domain is the unidentifiability of the sources, which can lead to non-interpretable results. The core contribution is the introduction of "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) as a form of prior knowledge to regularize the solution. The proposed SACs enforce the constraint that the sum of each separated source signal over a given time period should be close to a known, expected value (e.g., the average daily consumption of a specific appliance).

The authors incorporate these constraints into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a standard model for this task. As SACs are non-Markovian and complicate inference, the authors formulate MAP inference as a penalized optimization problem. They then relax the integer constraints on the state variables, resulting in a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP) that can be solved efficiently. The paper also provides a theoretical justification for this approach by connecting it to the posterior regularization framework.

The proposed method, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on both toy data and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES). The experiments demonstrate that AFHMM+SAC significantly outperforms both the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP, which uses a different type of constraint. The results are strong, showing up to a 45% reduction in error compared to AFHMM and a 29% reduction compared to AFAMAP on a realistic task.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper introduces a simple, intuitive, and novel type of constraint for the BSS problem. The idea of constraining the aggregate value of a source is a natural way to incorporate domain knowledge and directly tackles the source-swapping and identifiability problems. Given the strong performance gains shown, this is a significant contribution with the potential to be useful in other BSS domains where such aggregate knowledge is available.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The technical approach is solid. Formulating the MAP inference problem with SACs as a CQP by relaxing the integer variables is an elegant and practical solution to a difficult non-Markovian inference problem. The connection drawn to the posterior regularization framework in Section 6 adds theoretical depth and situates the work within a more general, principled framework.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is a key strength of the paper.
    *   The comparison against both a standard baseline (AFHMM) and a strong, recent state-of-the-art method (AFAMAP) is convincing.
    *   The use of an oracle experiment (with "True Total Consumption") is insightful, as it effectively demonstrates the upper bound on the potential benefit of this type of constraint.
    *   The evaluation on a challenging, low-frequency, real-world dataset (HES) and under a realistic scenario (with real mains readings) demonstrates the practical applicability of the method. The reported performance improvements are substantial.
    *   The inclusion of both normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) provides a more complete picture of performance.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The "franken-appliance" example effectively illustrates the core problem, and the progression from the basic AFHMM to the proposed AFHMM+SAC is logical.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Handling of Hyperparameters:** The regularization parameter λ plays a crucial role in balancing the data likelihood and the constraint. The paper states that λi was simply set to 1 for all experiments. This is a significant weakness, as the performance of the method is likely sensitive to this choice. Without a sensitivity analysis or a more principled method for setting λ (e.g., cross-validation), it is difficult to assess the robustness of the results and how easily they could be replicated on a new dataset.

2.  **Quality of the Convex Relaxation:** The paper solves a relaxed version of the original integer quadratic program. There is no discussion about the quality of this relaxation. For instance, do the solutions to the CQP often result in fractional values for the state indicator variables `Sitk`? The paper does not specify how these fractional values are handled to produce the final disaggregated signals `x̂it` (e.g., via rounding or by computing an expected value). A brief discussion on this point would improve the technical clarity and completeness.

3.  **Limited Discussion on Generalizability:** While the energy disaggregation application is very compelling, the paper claims SACs are "general across a wide variety of domains". This claim could be strengthened by briefly suggesting a few other concrete application domains where this type of aggregate prior knowledge is readily available (e.g., total words spoken by a speaker in audio separation, or total flux from a star in astronomical time series).

### **Detailed Suggestions for Improvement**

*   To address the weakness regarding the hyperparameter λ, I strongly recommend adding a sensitivity analysis. Even a simple plot showing how NDE/SAE vary with λ on the toy dataset would significantly strengthen the paper by demonstrating the robustness of the method to this crucial parameter.
*   Please clarify in Section 5 how the final discrete state sequences or continuous power estimates (`x̂it`) are derived from the (potentially fractional) solution `S` of the CQP. A sentence or two explaining this would resolve the ambiguity.
*   The paper compares against the "one-state-change" constraint of AFAMAP. It would be interesting to briefly comment on whether these two types of constraints (aggregate vs. temporal sparsity) are complementary. Could they be combined for even better performance? This could be a nice point for the future work discussion.
*   In Section 7.2, the paper assumes model parameters (means, transition probabilities) are learned from training data. It is worth explicitly stating that the proposed contribution is focused on the inference step, and that integrating SACs into a fully unsupervised learning framework (where parameters and states are learned jointly) remains an open direction for future work.

### **Final Assessment**

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear and valuable contribution. The proposed Signal Aggregate Constraints offer a novel and effective way to address the critical identifiability issue in AFHMM-based source separation. The technical approach is sound, and the empirical results are impressive and well-documented. While there are some weaknesses related to the hyperparameter tuning and the details of the convex relaxation, these are addressable and do not undermine the core contribution. The work is of high quality, original, and significant for researchers and practitioners in energy disaggregation and potentially other BSS applications. I recommend acceptance.
