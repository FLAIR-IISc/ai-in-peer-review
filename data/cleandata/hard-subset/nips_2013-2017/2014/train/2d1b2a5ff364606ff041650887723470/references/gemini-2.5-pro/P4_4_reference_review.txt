### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), with a specific application to energy disaggregation. The central challenge in BSS is the non-identifiability of the source signals, as many different combinations of sources can produce the same aggregate signal. To address this, the authors propose incorporating domain-specific prior knowledge into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a common model for this task.

The main contribution is the introduction of "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs). These constraints enforce the prior belief that the sum of each source signal over a long time period should be close to a known or expected value. For example, in energy disaggregation, the total daily energy consumption of a specific appliance type is often known approximately from national surveys or manufacturer specifications.

Incorporating SACs into the AFHMM objective function introduces non-Markovian dependencies, making standard inference algorithms like the Viterbi algorithm inapplicable. To overcome this, the authors formulate the maximum a posteriori (MAP) inference problem as an integer quadratic program. They then relax the integer constraints, resulting in a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP) which can be solved efficiently. The paper also demonstrates that this formulation can be viewed through the lens of the posterior regularization framework.

The proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on both synthetic data and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES). The results demonstrate that AFHMM+SAC significantly outperforms both the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP [12], in terms of normalized disaggregation error and signal aggregate error, with comparable computational cost for inference.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the CQP for the relaxed inference problem is clear and appears correct. The problem formulation is a logical extension of the AFHMM, and the relaxation is a standard and appropriate technique for making an otherwise intractable problem solvable. The claims of improved performance are well-supported by empirical evidence on both synthetic and real-world data. The experiments are carefully designed, including an oracle experiment (using True Total Consumption) to show the potential upper bound of performance for this type of constraint, which effectively demonstrates the value of the proposed idea.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation for the problem, situates the work within the relevant literature, and methodically develops the proposed model and inference algorithm. The notation is consistent, and the connection drawn to posterior regularization provides additional theoretical grounding. The experimental sections clearly describe the datasets, metrics, and results, making the evaluation easy to follow.

**Originality:** The core idea of using constraints on the *aggregate value* of a time series as a form of regularization in factorial HMMs is novel. While other forms of constraints for HMMs exist (e.g., limiting state transitions as in AFAMAP [12], or modeling state durations as in semi-Markov models [10]), the proposed signal aggregate constraint is a distinct and intuitive way to incorporate a different kind of domain knowledge. The approach of formulating the inference as a CQP is similar to that used in [12], but its application to this new class of constraints is a novel contribution. The related work section is thorough and correctly positions the paper's contribution relative to prior art.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant and impactful. Energy disaggregation is a problem of substantial practical interest, and the reported performance improvements (e.g., a 29% reduction in normalized error compared to AFAMAP on a realistic dataset) represent a meaningful advance for the field. The concept of signal aggregate constraints is general and could potentially be applied to other BSS domains where aggregate statistics of the sources are known, such as in audio processing or computational biology. The work provides a practical and effective method that other researchers and practitioners can build upon.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The complexity parameter λi plays a crucial role in balancing the likelihood term against the aggregate constraint. In the experiments, this was set to a constant value (λi = 1). The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the sensitivity of the results to this parameter and guidance on how it might be set in practice (e.g., using a validation set). The toy data experiment demonstrates sensitivity to the accuracy of the constraint *target* (µi0), but not the constraint *strength* (λi).

2.  **Generalization of Constraints:** The paper mentions the possibility of more general constraints, such as applying SACs over different time windows (e.g., day vs. night), but this is not explored empirically. Demonstrating the utility of such time-varying constraints would make the contribution more powerful, as many real-world signals exhibit strong diurnal patterns that a single aggregate constraint cannot capture.

3.  **Parameter Learning:** The experiments are conducted in a setting where the HMM parameters (emission and transition probabilities) are pre-trained on labeled data from individual appliances. While this is a common evaluation setup, a key challenge in disaggregation is the fully unsupervised setting. The paper suggests that incorporating SACs into unsupervised methods would be "straightforward," but this is a non-trivial claim and a major potential direction for future work. The true power of such constraints would be most evident in a setting where they help resolve the identifiability problem during unsupervised parameter learning, not just during inference with known parameters.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, intuitive, and effective method (Signal Aggregate Constraints) for improving BSS.
*   The technical approach (formulation as a CQP) is sound and allows for efficient inference.
*   Demonstrates significant and compelling empirical improvements over strong baselines on a challenging, real-world application.
*   The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and easy to understand.
*   The idea is generalizable and could be impactful in other application domains.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The treatment of the key hyperparameter (λi) is simplistic; a sensitivity analysis is missing.
*   The full potential of the constraint framework (e.g., time-varying constraints) is discussed but not demonstrated.
*   The evaluation is limited to a supervised-learning setting for model parameters, which sidesteps the harder problem of fully unsupervised disaggregation.

Overall, the strengths of this paper—particularly its novel and effective idea, sound technical execution, and strong empirical results—outweigh its weaknesses. The limitations are primarily avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the presented contribution. The paper presents a solid advance in an important area.
