

# NIPS Review: Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs

## Summary
This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) to address the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS) for energy disaggregation. SACs constrain the total energy consumption (or other aggregate properties) of each appliance to known values, transforming the problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP). The authors demonstrate a 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over state-of-the-art AFAMAP on real-world energy data. The work connects SACs to posterior regularization and provides a theoretically grounded, computationally efficient solution.

## Strengths

The paper makes a compelling case for SACs as a practical approach to the fundamental identifiability issue in BSS. The motivation is strong: energy disaggregation requires interpretable appliance-level consumption, but standard BSS methods produce uninterpretable "franken-appliances." The authors effectively demonstrate how SACs can incorporate domain knowledge (e.g., expected daily energy consumption of appliances) to improve results.

The technical contribution is sound and well-executed. The conversion of the non-convex optimization problem into a CQP is mathematically rigorous, and the authors provide a clear derivation of the objective function. The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) strengthens the theoretical foundation and situates the work within an established framework. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using both synthetic data to validate robustness and real-world energy data to demonstrate practical impact. The results are impressive, with significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods on real-world data.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with appropriate mathematical notation and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The evaluation metrics (NDE and SAE) are well-chosen to assess both signal-level and aggregate-level performance.

## Weaknesses

The most significant limitation is the paper's insufficient treatment of how to obtain the constraint values (μi0) in practice. While the authors use "national total consumption" (NTC) as a proxy for μi0, they don't provide sufficient detail on how this would be estimated or how robust the method is to errors in these values. The toy data experiment shows sensitivity to constraint errors (Figure 1), but the real-world evaluation uses "true total consumption" (TTC) as an oracle value that would be unavailable in practice. A more thorough analysis of how the method performs when μi0 is estimated from limited data (e.g., a small training set) would strengthen the practical relevance.

The paper could also better address the limitations of SACs. For example, in energy disaggregation, appliance consumption patterns can vary significantly across households and over time (e.g., seasonal variations). The paper doesn't discuss how SACs would handle such variations or whether they could be dynamically updated. The constraint formulation assumes fixed aggregate values, which may not capture the full complexity of real-world appliance usage.

While the related work section (Section 2) correctly identifies AFAMAP and other constrained HMM approaches, it could better position SACs relative to other constraint-based methods in the BSS literature. For instance, how do SACs compare to other aggregate constraints or to methods that incorporate physical constraints (e.g., non-negativity, power conservation)?

The paper's claim that SACs are "general across a wide variety of domains" is somewhat overstated. While the concept is general, the paper only demonstrates it in energy disaggregation. More discussion of how SACs could be applied to other BSS domains (e.g., audio source separation) would strengthen the argument for general applicability.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with a novel and practically important contribution to the energy disaggregation literature. The SAC framework provides a principled way to incorporate domain knowledge into BSS, and the technical solution is sound and efficient. The results on real-world data are compelling, demonstrating significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods.

The main weakness is the limited discussion of how to obtain constraint values in practice, which is critical for real-world deployment. While the authors acknowledge this limitation by using oracle values in experiments, a more thorough analysis of the sensitivity to constraint errors would strengthen the paper. The paper also doesn't fully explore the limitations of SACs in the face of real-world variability in appliance consumption patterns.

Despite these limitations, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The SAC framework is a valuable addition to the toolkit for constrained BSS, and the demonstrated performance improvements are substantial. The connection to posterior regularization provides theoretical grounding, and the CQP approach ensures computational efficiency.

## Recommendation

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to energy disaggregation. The main revision needed is to address the practical aspects of obtaining constraint values (μi0) and to better discuss the limitations of SACs in the face of real-world variability. The authors should also more clearly position SACs relative to other constraint-based methods in the literature.

The paper is highly relevant to NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental problem in unsupervised learning (identifiability) through a novel constraint framework, with strong empirical results on a practical application. The methods are general enough to be of interest to the broader machine learning community working on structured probabilistic models and constrained inference.
