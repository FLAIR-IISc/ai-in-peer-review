Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses blind source separation in time series by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs) that regularize source-specific totals (e.g., daily energy usage of an appliance). The model augments an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) with quadratic penalties encouraging the sum over time of each source’s state-dependent means to match a target µi0. The resulting MAP inference problem is non-Markovian and non-convex; the authors relax binary indicator variables and introduce auxiliary transition variables Hit to linearize the Markov terms, yielding a convex quadratic program (CQP) that can be solved efficiently. They show equivalence to posterior regularization (PR), where the desired posterior is constrained by expectations of squared deviations from totals.

Empirically, on a toy example and real-world electricity data (Household Electricity Survey, 2–10 min sampling), AFHMM+SAC outperforms AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS 2012), reducing normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE). SACs specified using “national total consumption” (NTC) estimated from training data are effective; an oracle with true totals (TTC) gives an upper bound on gains.

This work connects to: factorial HMMs [Ghahramani & Jordan], AFAMAP’s “at most one chain changes at a time” constraint [Kolter & Jaakkola], semi-Markov constraints for durations [Johnson & Willsky; Kim et al.], and general frameworks for incorporating domain knowledge (posterior regularization [Ganchev et al.], generalized expectation [Mann & McCallum], measurement-based learning [Liang et al.]). Aggregate constraints have been studied in probabilistic databases [Yang et al.], but not in models with Markov structure. The proposed SACs are a specific PR instance tailored to additive FHMMs with an explicit convex relaxation.

Strengths:
- Clear modeling idea: penalizing deviations from source-level aggregates tackles identifiability directly for applications prioritizing totals over exact switching times.
- Sound optimization: the relaxation with auxiliary Hit converts bilinear Markov terms into linear ones; the remaining quadratic emission and SAC terms are negative semidefinite in the maximization, yielding a concave objective over a convex polytope (i.e., a CQP). The relation to PR provides an alternative derivation.
- Practical impact: consistent improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP on challenging low-frequency HES data; statistical significance reported.
- Generality: SACs can be applied over arbitrary sub-intervals, potentially encoding diurnal priors.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Dependence on µi0: Performance hinges on the quality of aggregate targets. The paper shows robustness on a toy task and compares NTC vs TTC, but does not provide a thorough sensitivity analysis across realistic prior errors or household heterogeneity. Guidance on setting λi and the impact of mis-specified µi0 would improve practicality.
- Integrality gap and decoding: The convex relaxation yields fractional S and H. The paper does not discuss rounding or whether fractional solutions are used directly to reconstruct x̂it via µ⊤Sit. For interpretability (e.g., appliance on/off sequences), the lack of discrete states may be a limitation. Empirical integrality gap statistics would be informative.
- Scalability and solver details: Solving CQPs via CVX is convenient but may not scale to long horizons or many chains/states. Complexity claims are high-level; wall-clock times are reported, but the largest problem sizes, number of variables/constraints, and solver tolerances are not detailed. A specialized first-order method or message-passing-based convex solver could be more scalable.
- Comparison breadth: The comparisons omit duration-based semi-Markov methods and recent structured regularizers that encode limited change or k-segment constraints [Titsias et al.]. Including at least one strong duration model baseline would contextualize gains relative to other identifiability-improving constraints.
- Assumptions on parameters: The model assumes known µik per appliance/state estimated via k-means from training days. The impact of the number of states and clustering quality is not analyzed. Cross-validation or BIC-style selection could be discussed.
- Statistical testing: Paired t-tests are reported, but effect sizes and per-household variance are not provided. Multiple testing corrections across houses are not mentioned.

Quality:
- The derivation of the CQP is technically sound: the auxiliary Hit variables with flow constraints linearize transitions; the quadratic terms have PSD matrices (Λi, Vt), making the maximization a concave quadratic objective over a convex feasible set after relaxing integrality. The PR derivation is correct for squared penalties.
- Experimental methodology is reasonable for the domain, but more ablations (varying λi, µi0 perturbations, interval SACs) and a stronger set of baselines would strengthen the claims. The lack of discussion on rounding/integrality is a technical gap.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized. Section 5’s notation is dense; adding a compact summary table of variables and dimensions would aid readability. A brief proof sketch explaining concavity/convexity would clarify the “CQP” claim. Minor typos and punctuation issues exist in references and equations.

Originality:
- The idea of aggregate constraints is conceptually aligned with PR/GE frameworks, but the specific instantiation for additive FHMMs, the quadratic penalty on totals, and the convex relaxation with auxiliary variables are a novel and useful combination. Novelty is moderate but meaningful for this application class.

Significance:
- For energy disaggregation on low-frequency data, the reported improvements are notable and practically relevant. The approach is potentially applicable to other BSS domains where totals are known or estimable. Broader impact depends on availability and accuracy of µi0.

Pros for acceptance:
- Introduces a simple, general, and effective constraint class that addresses identifiability.
- Provides a principled convex relaxation and links to posterior regularization.
- Demonstrates significant empirical gains on realistic datasets with statistical validation.
- Method likely transferable to other domains requiring interpretable source totals.

Cons for acceptance:
- Relies on external priors (µi0); limited analysis of robustness and parameter selection.
- No discussion of rounding/integrality gap; potential mismatch with discrete-state interpretability.
- Limited baseline coverage (no semi-Markov/duration models in comparison) and scalability analysis relies on CVX.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide sensitivity analyses over λi and systematic perturbations of µi0; include interval SACs to show added value.
- Report integrality metrics and, if needed, a rounding or projection scheme with performance impact.
- Add at least one duration-based baseline and, if possible, results on a public dataset (e.g., REDD) for broader comparability.
- Detail solver settings, problem sizes, and explore scalable solvers tailored to the structure.
- Include a variable/dimension summary and a short convexity proof sketch to improve clarity.
