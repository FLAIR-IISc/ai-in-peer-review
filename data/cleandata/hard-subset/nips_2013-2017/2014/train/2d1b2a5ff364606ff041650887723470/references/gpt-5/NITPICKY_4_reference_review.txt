Summary
This paper proposes “signal aggregate constraints” (SACs) for blind source separation when sources are modelled by an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM). The key idea is to inject prior knowledge about the total energy (or other aggregate) used by each source over a time window, by adding a quadratic penalty that encourages each chain’s aggregate sum to match a target µi0. Because these penalties destroy the Markov structure, the authors introduce auxiliary transition variables Hit to linearize the bilinear HMM transition terms and solve a convex quadratic program (CQP) relaxation over continuous indicator variables with simple flow-like constraints. They also relate the objective to posterior regularization. Experiments on synthetic data and on the HES energy dataset (2–10 min sampling) show gains in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) over a vanilla AFHMM and AFAMAP [Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS’12], with similar reported runtimes.

Relation to prior work
The paper sits at the intersection of FHMM-based disaggregation (e.g., Hart ’92; Ghahramani & Jordan ’97; Kolter & Jaakkola ’12; semi-Markov approaches) and general frameworks for structured priors/constraints (posterior regularization; generalized expectation; measurement-based learning). The novelty is largely in (i) specifying a practically useful class of aggregate constraints (L2 penalties on chain-wise totals) for disaggregation and (ii) a particular convex relaxation for AFHMM+SAC. The posterior regularization linkage is appropriate and known, but its application here is natural.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Aggregate priors (daily Wh per appliance) are realistic and widely available; using them directly targets identifiability pain points in disaggregation.
- Method: The auxiliary-transition-variable trick yields a concave quadratic objective (linear in H, quadratic in S) over a convex polytope; the optimization is standard and robust.
- Clarity of the core idea: The SAC objective and posterior-regularization equivalence are clearly stated.
- Empirics: Results suggest substantial improvements in both NDE and SAE over AFHMM and AFAMAP, on challenging low-rate HES data.
- Generalizability: Windowed SACs are discussed; the approach could port to other BSS domains.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological/technical
- Relaxation and rounding unspecified: The inference solves a relaxed CQP with Sitk, hitjk ∈ [0,1]. How are discrete state sequences recovered for evaluation? If fractional S are used to reconstruct x̂ via convex combinations of µ, this departs from the HMM and makes comparators (which output discrete states) unfair. If rounding is used, what procedure, and does it maintain transition consistency? This is a major omission.
- Indexing/constraints for H: In (6), the summations over t are ambiguous, and the constraints Ki∑l Hitl. = S⊤i,t−1, Ki∑l Hit.l = Sit require definition for t=1 (what is Si,0?). Typically H is defined for t=2..T; please fix indexing and include the initial constraints.
- Convexity claim needs precision: Initially, “logP(S|Y) is not convex,” but after introducing H, the objective becomes concave quadratic plus linear terms; maximizing concave over a convex set is a convex optimization. State this precisely; “CQP” is often used for convex minimization with PSD quadratic term.
- Scaling/runtime skepticism: Solving CQPs with CVX on day-long sequences (T≈144–720) and several chains via interior-point methods is usually far slower than chainwise Viterbi or AFAMAP’s specialized inference. The paper claims “similar time” without full details. Please report T, I, Ki, solver (e.g., SDPT3/SeDuMi), tolerances, hardware, and per-instance runtimes; include histograms or scaling plots. Otherwise, the practicality claim is not credible.
- Hyperparameters: λi are set to 1 “for all experiments,” despite varying units/variances across households. Without cross-validation, the strength of the prior relative to σt is arbitrary. At least normalize or provide sensitivity analyses.
- Noise variance and parameter learning: σt and θ are assumed known; how are they estimated? The variance matters for the curvature (Vt) and trade-offs with SACs.
- Identifiability and mis-specification: If µ0 is misassigned to the wrong appliance (label-swapping), SACs can entrench incorrect identities. The toy experiment shows degradation with distance to true aggregates but does not probe mislabelled aggregates across appliances or multiple correlated appliances.
- Comparisons: AFAMAP is included, but not semi-Markov or duration-based methods (which directly regularize segments) that could also reduce “franken-appliances.” Given citations [10,11], a comparison would be informative.
- The “multiple window” extension claims “does not complicate the algorithms”; actually it changes Λi to block-structured penalties. Please detail construction and convexity.

Clarity/presentation
- Several notation issues: t is both a time index and noise variable (εt). eT denotes a T×1 vector with first element 1; rename to e1 to avoid confusion with transpose. 1T is defined as a T×1 column vector despite the superscript T usually denoting transpose. Ensure consistent ranges for sums (e.g., over t=2..T where appropriate).
- Typos/grammar: “distibution,” “equavalent,” “equivalently,” “Those parameters can be estimated by using approximation methods,” etc. Numerous minor errors throughout.
- Missing figure/table details: Many claims rely on Tables 1–3 and Figures 1–2; without exact numbers, CIs, and axes, it’s hard to assess. Report mean±std, medians, and p-values, not just percentages.
- Reproducibility: No code, no data splits, no solver settings.

References/formatting
- Reference formatting is inconsistent and often incomplete (years missing, venues incorrect, duplicate “editors,” malformed CVX URL citation, “Neural Information Processing Systems” vs “Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems,” etc.). This needs a thorough overhaul.
- Some important related directions could be cited, e.g., convex relaxations for FHMMs and HMM MAP via LP/MIP formulations, expectation constraints beyond PR.

Questions for authors
1) How do you convert relaxed solutions (S,H) into discrete sequences for evaluation? If you use fractional S, how do you ensure fair comparison to AFAMAP?
2) Provide complete runtime details (T, I, Ki per house, solver, tolerances, hardware) and a scaling study.
3) How are σt and θ learned? How sensitive are results to λi and σt?
4) Can you evaluate robustness to misassigned µ0 across appliances?
5) Can you compare to a duration/semi-Markov baseline on HES?

Pros
- Practical, widely available prior encoded cleanly.
- Convex optimization formulation; neat PR connection.
- Promising empirical gains on realistic data.

Cons
- Missing critical details on relaxation rounding/decoding.
- Weak hyperparameter treatment; unclear runtime practicality.
- Numerous clarity, notation, and reference issues.
- Modest novelty relative to PR and AFHMM literature; limited comparisons.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is sensible and potentially useful, but the paper in its current form has several methodological gaps (especially decoding from the relaxation), insufficient experimental rigor on efficiency and sensitivity, and significant clarity/formatting problems. With a clearer decoding procedure, stronger baselines, detailed runtime evidence, and cleaned presentation, this work could be competitive. Confidence: high.
