This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework, applied to the problem of energy disaggregation. The core idea – leveraging prior knowledge about expected aggregate energy consumption of appliances – is a reasonable one, and the authors present a mathematically formulated approach to incorporate these constraints. While the problem setting and motivation are well-established and the results demonstrate a clear performance improvement, numerous shortcomings in presentation, technical detail, and justification lead me to recommend rejection.

The introduction is reasonably well-written, but suffers from repetition. The motivation for the work is clearly laid out, but the connection between the broader BSS problem and the specific application of energy disaggregation feels somewhat tenuous without further elaboration on why the challenges are similar. The positioning of SACs relative to existing approaches (posterior regularization, etc.) is weak; the authors state these approaches “leave open the question of what types of domain knowledge we should include,” which feels dismissive and doesn't acknowledge the significant body of work exploring *how* to incorporate diverse priors within these frameworks.

Section 2, "Related Work," is surprisingly brief, given the extensive literature on energy disaggregation. While key papers are cited, the discussion feels superficial. The mention of k-segment constraints [19] feels tacked on, lacking explanation of its relevance or differences from the proposed SACs. More comprehensive coverage of relevant work in constrained HMMs (beyond simply changing at most one chain at a time) would strengthen the paper.

Section 3 is mathematically straightforward, but the notation is inconsistent. The use of both Xi and xi to represent component signals is confusing. The assumption of Gaussian noise, while common, deserves some justification given the potential for non-Gaussian noise profiles in real-world electricity data.

Section 4.1 introduces the AFHMM, but relies heavily on prior knowledge of the reader. A more detailed explanation of the model, including the challenges of inference, would be beneficial. Section 4.2 introduces the SACs. The rationale for choosing a quadratic penalty is not sufficiently justified. Why not an absolute difference penalty (L1 regularization), which might encourage sparsity in deviations from the expected aggregate values? The explanation of δi as a "tuning parameter" feels hand-wavy; its connection to a Bayesian interpretation is mentioned, but not fully developed. The generalization to multiple constraints, while presented, lacks a discussion of computational complexity implications.

The derivation of the CQP in Section 5 is the most technically detailed part of the paper, but is also the most problematic. The notation is overwhelming and unnecessarily complex. The introduction of Hit and the accompanying tensor manipulations (Kronecker product) are poorly motivated and make the derivation difficult to follow. While the authors state that the problem is “relaxed”, they fail to adequately discuss the implications of this relaxation – specifically, how it affects the optimality of the solution and the potential for identifiability issues. The claim of polynomial time complexity is also suspect and requires more rigorous justification, particularly given the dimensions of the matrices involved. The use of CVX is mentioned, but no details about computational time or scalability are provided beyond the brief discussion in Section 7.

Section 6’s relation to posterior regularization feels forced and doesn't add significant value. The connection is stated but not convincingly demonstrated.

Section 7 presents the experimental results. The toy data experiment is insufficient. While it demonstrates the sensitivity of the method to the constraint value (µ0), it lacks a rigorous evaluation of the method’s ability to recover the underlying signals. The real-world experiments are more compelling, but the presentation is disorganized. The tables are difficult to interpret, and the lack of error bars raises concerns about the statistical significance of the results. The comparison to AFAMAP [12] is good, but a comparison to other state-of-the-art disaggregation techniques (beyond just AFAMAP) would be more informative. The claim of “similar” computational time across methods is unconvincing without more detailed profiling. The statement that "extensive research has been undertaken to estimate the average national consumption of different appliances" [23] is a citation to a report, not a peer-reviewed publication justifying the use of NTC as a prior.

Throughout the paper, there are numerous minor formatting issues (inconsistent spacing, missing punctuation, etc.) and grammatical errors. These, while not critical, detract from the overall polish of the manuscript.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrated performance improvement on a real-world energy disaggregation dataset.
*   Novel application of constraint-based regularization to the AFHMM framework.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Numerous technical flaws in the derivation and justification of the proposed method.
*   Overly complex notation and presentation.
*   Insufficient experimental validation and analysis.
*   Weak positioning within the related work.
*   Numerous minor formatting and grammatical errors.
*   Lack of thorough discussion regarding the implications of the relaxed optimization problem.



Overall, while the idea behind this paper is potentially valuable, the numerous technical shortcomings and lack of rigor prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper requires substantial revision and clarification before it would be suitable for NIPS.
