

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) to address the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS), specifically applied to energy disaggregation. SACs constrain the total aggregate value (e.g., daily energy consumption) of each source signal to known domain-specific values (e.g., appliance-specific national averages), incorporated into an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM). The authors derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) to efficiently solve the constrained optimization problem, avoiding the intractability of exact inference. Empirical results on energy disaggregation data show SACs reduce normalized disaggregation error (NDE) by 45% over AFHMM and 29% over AFAMAP, with minimal computational overhead.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Novel and Well-Motivated Constraint:** SACs address a critical gap in BSS literature—prior knowledge about *aggregate* signal properties (not just state transitions), which is highly relevant for energy disaggregation. This distinguishes the work from prior constraints (e.g., AFAMAP’s "at most one chain changes state" [12]) and aligns with domain expertise (e.g., appliance energy averages [23]). The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) strengthens theoretical grounding.  
2. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The method is rigorously evaluated on real-world energy data (HES dataset), demonstrating significant improvements in both NDE and signal aggregate error (SAE). The toy data experiment (Section 7.1) effectively shows robustness to constraint errors, and the use of "national total consumption" (NTC) as a realistic proxy for constraints is well-justified.  
3. **Technical Soundness:** The CQP relaxation (Section 5) is well-motivated and computationally efficient (polynomial time). The paper clearly explains the transformation from non-Markovian constraints to a convex problem, avoiding the pitfalls of naive inference.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The paper compares only to AFHMM and AFAMAP (2012), omitting recent state-of-the-art methods. For instance, [12] (AFAMAP) is from 2012, while energy disaggregation has since advanced significantly (e.g., deep learning approaches [22, 24] or semi-supervised methods [25]). The 29% improvement over AFAMAP is impressive, but without comparison to modern baselines (e.g., [25] or [26]), the significance is unclear. *Suggestion:* Add experiments against at least one recent method (e.g., [25] or [26] from the references).  
2. **Limited Discussion of Constraint Accuracy:** The paper assumes NTC (national averages) is a reliable proxy for household-specific totals but does not quantify its accuracy or variability across households (e.g., how much does NTC deviate from actual household usage?). This is critical for real-world applicability. *Suggestion:* Include an analysis of NTC’s fidelity (e.g., variance in household energy usage vs. national averages) and discuss how this impacts SAC performance.  
3. **Relaxation Justification:** The CQP relaxation is presented as a practical solution, but the paper does not address *how often* the relaxation deviates from the true MAP solution due to identifiability. While the toy data shows robustness, a formal analysis (e.g., error bounds or failure cases) would strengthen the technical contribution. *Suggestion:* Add a small experiment or discussion on the impact of relaxation on solution quality.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper makes a novel contribution to BSS via SACs, with strong empirical results in a high-impact application (energy disaggregation). The technical approach is sound, and the connection to posterior regularization is insightful. However, the omission of recent baselines undermines the significance claim, as NIPS prioritizes work that advances the *current* state-of-the-art. The method’s applicability is also limited to additive factorial HMMs, which restricts broader relevance.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Weak Accept** with major revisions. The core idea is valuable and well-executed, but the paper must address the baseline gap and constraint accuracy concerns to meet NIPS standards. If revised to include comparisons with recent methods (e.g., [25] or [26]) and analyze NTC’s real-world reliability, this would be a strong contribution. As-is, the lack of comparison to modern approaches weakens the significance argument.  

*Key revisions needed:*  
1. Compare against ≥1 recent energy disaggregation method (e.g., [25] or [26]) in Tables 1/3.  
2. Add analysis of NTC accuracy (e.g., household-level variance in appliance energy usage vs. national averages).  
3. Briefly discuss limitations of the relaxation (e.g., when it may fail).  

The paper’s focus on domain-specific constraints is timely and well-motivated, and with these revisions, it could significantly advance the field. The authors have provided a solid foundation for a compelling NIPS contribution.
