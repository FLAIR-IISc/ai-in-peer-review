This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation, specifically within the context of Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs) applied to energy disaggregation. The core idea is to incorporate prior knowledge about the expected total energy consumption of appliances to improve the accuracy of disaggregation. The authors formulate this as a constrained optimization problem and propose a convex relaxation that allows for efficient computation via a quadratic program. They demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods, including a 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error on real-world energy data.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a crucial challenge in energy disaggregation: the identifiability problem. The introduction clearly articulates the difficulty of BSS and the importance of incorporating domain knowledge. The connection to existing work on posterior regularization and other constraint-based approaches is also well-established. The introduction of SACs feels natural and intuitive, stemming from the practical understanding that we often have expectations about the total energy usage of appliances. 

The strengths of this work are significant. First, the technical contribution – formulating SACs and deriving the convex relaxation – is sound and well-explained. The conversion to a CQP is a crucial step, enabling practical application. Second, the experimental results are compelling. The 45% error reduction is substantial and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Comparing against a state-of-the-art method [12] strengthens the claims. Third, the authors acknowledge and address potential issues, such as the sensitivity to the choice of the constraint values (µi0) and the limitations of the relaxed optimization. The toy data experiment explicitly explores the robustness to errors in µi0. The clear presentation of the convex programming formulation and computational complexity is also a plus.  The connection to posterior regularization offers a nice theoretical grounding.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the experimental results on real data are strong, the details of the dataset and experimental setup could be more detailed. For instance, specifying the number of households used for training vs. testing, the length of the time series used, and any pre-processing steps would improve reproducibility.  The ablation study comparing different values of λi (or δi) is missing. While the authors mention tuning parameters, they simply state they set λi=1. Showing how performance varies with different parameter settings would provide valuable insight.  Furthermore, the discussion on the limitations of the identifiability problem feels somewhat cursory. A deeper dive into how SACs help to mitigate this issue, beyond just improving accuracy, would be beneficial. The paper relies heavily on the AFHMM as a baseline and comparison point.  Exploring the performance of SACs with other BSS models (e.g., non-negative matrix factorization) could broaden the scope and impact. The paper briefly mentions generalizing the constraints to a series of time periods, but doesn't explore this in experiments. While the authors acknowledge the advantage of TTC in the toy data, the discussion is limited. A more thorough exploration of how close the estimated µi0 needs to be to the true value for significant performance gains would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-motivated approach to a challenging problem.
*   Significant and demonstrable performance improvements on a real-world dataset.
*   Clear and concise presentation of the method and results.
*   Addresses a practical and important application (energy disaggregation).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of detailed experimental setup and ablation studies (e.g., parameter tuning).
*   Limited exploration of the identifiability problem and the mechanisms by which SACs address it.
*   Focus solely on the AFHMM model; lack of comparison with alternative BSS approaches.
*   The generalization to multiple time constraints isn’t explored.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of energy disaggregation and BSS. The proposed SACs offer a principled way to incorporate domain knowledge and improve the accuracy of source separation. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the points raised regarding experimental details, parameter sensitivity, and a more thorough discussion of the identifiability problem. A minor revision addressing these concerns would significantly strengthen the paper.
