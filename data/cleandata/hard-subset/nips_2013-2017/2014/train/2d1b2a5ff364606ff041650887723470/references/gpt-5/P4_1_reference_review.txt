Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses blind source separation (BSS) when the goal is to recover additive component signals from a single aggregate time series. Focusing on energy disaggregation (non-intrusive load monitoring), the authors argue that identifiability can be improved by incorporating prior knowledge about the total (time-aggregated) usage of each source. They introduce signal aggregate constraints (SACs) that encourage each latent source’s aggregate over time to match a preset target (e.g., expected daily energy for an appliance). They incorporate SACs into an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM), yielding an objective that augments the AFHMM MAP log-posterior with a quadratic penalty on deviations of each source’s time-sum from a target. Because the penalty is non-Markovian, dynamic programming is not directly applicable; the authors introduce transition indicator variables and relax the binary constraints to obtain a convex quadratic program (CQP) whose objective is concave quadratic and constraints are linear box/simplex constraints. They also show the same objective arises from the posterior regularization (PR) framework (Ganchev et al., JMLR 2010), with aggregate constraints expressed as expectations under the posterior.

Empirically, the method is evaluated on a toy example and on the UK HES dataset, which has ultra-low frequency measurements. The proposed AFHMM+SAC is compared to AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS 2012). Using national average appliance energy as SAC targets (NTC) and, as an oracle, true test-day totals (TTC), the method reduces normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE), with statistically significant improvements versus AFAMAP across houses, and reports similar runtime.

The work is related to: factorial HMMs for BSS (Ghahramani & Jordan), AFAMAP constraints on transition concurrency, HSMM/semi-Markov duration modeling for disaggregation, and general constraint-based learning frameworks (posterior regularization, generalized expectation, measurement-based learning). The novelty here is identifying aggregate-over-time constraints as a broadly applicable prior and deriving a convex relaxation tailored to AFHMMs that is efficient in practice.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivation of the CQP is correct under the relaxation. With Λi and Vt PSD, the quadratic terms enter with negative sign, so maximizing the objective is a convex optimization problem (concave objective, linear constraints). The introduction of transition indicators Hit to linearize transition log-probabilities is standard, and the linear constraints linking S and H are appropriate. The PR connection is accurate.
- Assumptions and limitations: The method relies on known or estimable aggregate targets µi0; the toy study explores misspecification sensitivity, but a thorough sensitivity analysis on real data (e.g., when µi0 is biased) is limited. The relaxation removes integrality; no rounding to discrete states is described. For disaggregation accuracy, soft assignments may suffice (since x̂it = Si,t^T µi), but interpretability of discrete states may be affected. The paper sets λi = 1 across experiments; a principled selection (e.g., cross-validation or Bayesian treatment) is not explored, and results could be sensitive to λi. Complexity arguments are qualitative; using CVX is convenient but may not scale to long horizons or many appliances—more detailed runtime scaling and a specialized solver would strengthen the practicality claim.
- Empirical evaluation: The comparisons to AFHMM and AFAMAP on low-frequency HES data are relevant, and the improvements in NDE/SAE with significance tests are supportive. However, baselines that also encode temporal structure (e.g., HSMM-based models, k-segment constraints) are discussed but not included in experiments. The oracle TTC setting is appropriately flagged as an upper bound. It would be useful to detail whether NTC was computed only from training data outside the test day and to clarify any potential data leakage. Reporting absolute errors and variance across days/houses and an ablation on windowed SACs (as briefly suggested) would further support the conclusions.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, with a precise mathematical formulation and a readable optimization derivation. Some notation is dense (e.g., Kronecker products, stacking), but definitions are provided. A few presentation issues could be improved: consistency in sign conventions (maximize concave), a brief explanation of how soft solutions are used to produce x̂ (and whether any rounding is applied), clearer description of σt treatment, and more polished references (several entries lack years or have formatting issues). Figures/tables referenced should be included with axis labels and units.

Originality
The idea of constraining posteriors with expectation-based functions is established (PR/GE/measurement-based learning), and AFHMMs are well-studied. The specific identification of aggregate-over-time constraints as a practically useful, broadly applicable prior for BSS, together with a task-specific convex quadratic relaxation for AFHMM MAP inference, appears novel. The contribution is incremental methodologically but concrete and well-motivated; the application to ultra-low-frequency disaggregation and the observed gains add value.

Significance
Given the prevalence of BSS tasks where aggregate totals are known or can be estimated (energy, biology, audio), SACs are potentially impactful. The reported improvements over AFAMAP on challenging low-frequency data suggest practical significance for energy analytics. Broader impact will depend on the availability and reliability of aggregate priors and on the scalability of the optimization beyond small to medium problem sizes.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated incorporation of aggregate priors into AFHMMs.
- Sound convex relaxation with a clean PR interpretation.
- Demonstrated, statistically significant performance gains on realistic, low-frequency data.
- Concept is general and potentially transferable to other BSS domains.

Cons
- Reliance on aggregate targets; limited real-data sensitivity analysis to misspecification.
- No rounding/discrete recovery discussion; interpretability of fractional states is unclear.
- Limited baselines (no HSMM/k-segment comparisons) and minimal ablation on λi, windowed constraints, or hyperparameters.
- Scalability concerns left to CVX; more detailed runtime analysis and specialized solver would strengthen the case.
- Presentation/polish issues in references and some missing experimental details.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a simple rounding/post-processing scheme or justify using soft states for interpretability.
- Include sensitivity to λi and to noise in µi0 on real data; evaluate windowed SACs as outlined.
- Add baselines with duration/segment constraints (HSMM or k-segment) to isolate the value of aggregate information versus temporal constraints.
- Clarify how NTC is computed to avoid leakage, and report per-house/day variance.
- Offer complexity/runtime scaling with T and number of appliances; consider a tailored QP solver.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper is technically sound and addresses a relevant problem with a simple, well-justified idea that leads to measurable improvements. While the methodological novelty is moderate and some experimental and scalability aspects could be strengthened, the contribution is concrete and likely of interest to the NeurIPS community working on structured models and sustainability applications.
