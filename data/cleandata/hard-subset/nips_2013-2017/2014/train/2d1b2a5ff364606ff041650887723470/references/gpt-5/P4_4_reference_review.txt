Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses blind source separation (BSS) for time series where the observation is a noisy sum of multiple latent sources. It focuses on additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs), a standard model in this setting, and proposes Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs): priors that encourage the total energy (sum over time) of each source to match specified values. The motivation is energy disaggregation, where domain knowledge often provides approximate daily/periodic totals for appliances.

Methodologically, the paper:
- Augments AFHMM inference with aggregate constraints by adding quadratic penalties of the form (∑t µi⊤Sit − µi0)2, where Sit encodes the HMM state at time t and µi are state means for source i.
- Introduces auxiliary transition variables Hit to rewrite the objective and shows that, after relaxing integrality constraints (Sit, hitjk ∈ [0,1]), the resulting problem is a concave quadratic objective with linear constraints. It is therefore solvable as a convex quadratic program (CQP) using CVX.
- Connects the penalized objective to the posterior regularization framework by showing it is the MAP solution under an exponential family posterior tilt with constraint features ϕi(S, Y) = (∑t µi⊤Sit − µi0)2.

Experiments include a toy example and a real-world evaluation on the low-frequency HES dataset. AFHMM+SAC is compared to AFHMM and AFAMAP [Kolter & Jaakkola, AISTATS 2012]. Using national average consumption (NTC) as µ0, AFHMM+SAC improves normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) over both baselines; using true total consumption (TTC) yields an oracle upper bound. Paired t-tests report significance at 5%. Runtime is reported as similar across methods.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is sound. With Λi = λi µ∗i µ∗i⊤ and Vt = σt−2 μ→ μ→⊤ positive semidefinite, the quadratic terms −Si⊤ΛiSi and −(1/2)St⊤VtSt make the objective concave; linear transition terms in Hit preserve convexity of the relaxed problem. The mapping to posterior regularization is correct and clarifies the probabilistic meaning.
- Completeness and evaluation: The empirical section demonstrates gains on a large-scale, realistic dataset at ultra-low sampling rates, which strengthens the case for usefulness in practice. Statistical testing is a plus.

  However, there are several gaps that limit reproducibility and interpretation:
  - Relaxation vs. discrete decoding: The paper does not specify how fractional Sit/Hit are handled when reconstructing time series or states. If x̂it = µi⊤Sit is used directly, the method effectively yields convex combinations of state means rather than discrete HMM states, which weakens the HMM semantics and complicates comparison to MAP decoders like AFAMAP. If a rounding scheme is used, it should be described and evaluated.
  - Hyperparameters: λi are fixed to 1 without tuning. A sensitivity analysis (or cross-validation) would strengthen claims of robustness; the toy study shows sensitivity to µ0 but not to λi.
  - Estimation details: It remains unclear how σt2, πi, and transition matrices are estimated in practice (training objectives, regularization, smoothing), and whether the same estimation pipeline is used for all methods. These details affect performance and are important for reproducibility.
  - Constraint specification: µ0 derived from NTC can be mismatched to specific households/days. A study of performance vs. mismatch magnitude (beyond the toy example) and an ablation for per-household vs. cross-household NTC would clarify robustness in real settings.
  - Complexity: While the relaxed CQP is polynomial, practical scaling depends on T and total states. Reporting the problem size (variables/constraints) and solver tolerances, and providing scaling curves, would substantiate the runtime claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear in motivation and high-level derivations. The convexification and posterior-regularization connection are presented concisely. Some notation is dense and occasionally hard to follow (e.g., Hit.l vs. Hitl., the construction of µ∗i, the exact stacking of St), and there are minor typos. The experimental section would benefit from more explicit procedural details (training/testing splits, preprocessing, variance estimation, and any rounding). References appear to have incomplete bibliographic fields; these should be fixed.

Originality
The core idea—imposing aggregate constraints on latent sequences—relates to prior frameworks for incorporating side information, including posterior regularization, generalized expectation, and measurement-based learning, all of which are cited. The novelty here lies in identifying aggregate-sum constraints as broadly available domain knowledge for BSS, instantiating them in AFHMMs, and deriving an efficiently solvable convex relaxation with auxiliary variables. While conceptually simple, this is a useful and nontrivial instantiation for time-series BSS; I am not aware of prior AFHMM work that incorporates global sum constraints via a convex program. The relation to aggregate constraints in probabilistic databases is noted, though that prior work assumes independent variables.

Significance
For energy disaggregation—an active application area—the demonstrated improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP on challenging low-frequency data are meaningful. The approach is generic and could transfer to other BSS domains where aggregate priors are available (e.g., audio source durations/energies, biology). Lack of guarantees on the relaxation gap and discrete decoding limits theoretical impact, but empirical gains and the clean connection to posterior regularization make this a potentially useful contribution.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated use of domain knowledge via aggregate constraints with a principled probabilistic interpretation.
- Sound convex relaxation and practical solver-based implementation.
- Demonstrated, statistically significant improvements over strong baselines on realistic, large-scale energy data at low sampling rates.
- Approach is general and likely transferable across BSS applications.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing details on handling fractional solutions, decoding to discrete states, and full training pipeline hinder reproducibility.
- No analysis of sensitivity to λi or to misspecification of µ0 on real data.
- Empirical comparisons are limited to AFHMM/AFAMAP; broader baselines (e.g., semi-Markov variants cited) are not included.
- Notational density and minor clarity issues; incomplete references.

Suggestions for improvement
- Specify and evaluate a rounding/decoding scheme from relaxed S,H to discrete HMM states; report impact on NDE/SAE.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for λi and for µ0 mismatch on real data; consider learning λi from data or hierarchical priors.
- Expand experimental comparisons to duration-constrained/semi-Markov models or interleaved FHMMs cited.
- Report problem sizes, solver settings, and scaling curves; consider a specialized first-order solver or ADMM to improve scalability.
- Clarify parameter estimation procedures (π, transitions, σ2) and ensure parity across baselines.
- Fix notation and references; consider evaluating windowed SACs (subsequence constraints) as suggested in Section 4.2.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound, practically useful idea with promising empirical results. Addressing the reproducibility and clarity gaps would strengthen it further.
