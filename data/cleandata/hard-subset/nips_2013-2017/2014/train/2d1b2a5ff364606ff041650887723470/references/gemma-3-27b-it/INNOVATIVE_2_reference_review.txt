This paper introduces signal aggregate constraints (SACs) within an additive factorial hidden Markov model (AFHMM) to tackle the blind source separation problem, specifically demonstrated through energy disaggregation. The core idea – incorporating prior knowledge about expected total energy consumption of appliances – is quite compelling and addresses a key practical challenge in disaggregation: ensuring interpretable and believable results. The authors appropriately position this work as a method for *identifying* what kinds of prior knowledge to include, building on existing work in posterior regularization and related areas. The technical contribution lies in formulating the problem as a convex quadratic program, allowing for efficient computation.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the motivation, model, and experimental setup. The connection to existing work on constrained AFHMMs (e.g., AFAMAP) is acknowledged, and the authors effectively highlight the novelty of their approach – focusing on aggregate constraints rather than state transition limitations. The experimental results, particularly the 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error on real-world data, are impressive and strongly suggest the practical benefits of SACs. The authors also thoughtfully consider the computational complexity and demonstrate that their approach scales similarly to existing methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The introduction of signal aggregate constraints is a fresh perspective on the BSS problem and directly addresses a key issue of identifiability and interpretability.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The energy disaggregation application is compelling and provides a strong motivating example. The constraints reflect real-world domain knowledge.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** Formulating the problem as a CQP is a significant engineering achievement, making the approach scalable.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported performance gains on real-world data are substantial and convincing.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors demonstrate empirical success, a deeper theoretical exploration of *why* SACs improve performance would be valuable. A more rigorous analysis of the identifiability gains achieved by the constraints is missing.
*   **Sensitivity to Constraint Values:** The toy data experiments (Figure 1) reveal sensitivity to the accuracy of the constraint values (µ0). While acknowledged, a more thorough investigation of this sensitivity and potential methods for robust constraint estimation is needed. Could the method be adapted to learn these constraints from the data itself?
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper mentions tuning the complexity parameter λ, but the specific methods used for tuning were not detailed.
*   **Comparison Depth:** The comparison to AFAMAP is somewhat limited. A more detailed breakdown of *where* AFHMM+SAC excels versus AFAMAP would provide more insight.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a challenging problem.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods.
*   The CQP formulation makes the approach computationally practical.
*   The work has the potential to impact the field of energy disaggregation and beyond, as the SAC concept is broadly applicable to BSS.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical justification for the approach is somewhat limited.
*   The sensitivity to constraint values requires further investigation.
*   The paper, while strong, doesn’t represent a *complete* solution – further work on constraint estimation and theoretical understanding would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty of the SAC concept, combined with the strong empirical results and computational efficiency, outweigh the limitations. The paper tackles an important problem with a bold and innovative approach. While further refinement and theoretical exploration are warranted, this work represents a significant step forward in the field of blind source separation and has the potential to drive further research in this area. I'd place it in the upper tier of submissions and believe it will be well-received by the NIPS community. The risk of rejecting a truly impactful idea is higher than the risk of accepting a paper with minor limitations.
