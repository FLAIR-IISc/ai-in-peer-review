### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of identifiability in blind source separation, specifically within the context of Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs) applied to energy disaggregation. The authors propose a novel type of constraint, termed Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), which leverages domain knowledge about the expected total value of each source signal over a given time period. For instance, in energy disaggregation, one might have a reasonable prior on the total daily energy consumption of a specific appliance. These global, non-Markovian constraints are incorporated into the MAP inference objective as a quadratic penalty term. To make inference tractable, the authors relax the resulting integer quadratic program into a convex quadratic program (CQP), similar to the approach taken by Kolter & Jaakkola (2012) for their AFAMAP model. The paper also demonstrates that this formulation can be derived from the posterior regularization framework. Experiments on a large, real-world energy dataset show that the proposed AFHMM+SAC model significantly outperforms both the standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP, reducing normalized disaggregation error by 29% compared to the latter.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the problem as a penalized MAP objective and its subsequent relaxation into a CQP is a valid and practical approach to inference. The experimental evaluation is conducted on a relevant and challenging real-world dataset (HES), and the comparisons against standard (AFHMM) and strong (AFAMAP) baselines are appropriate. The results are compelling, demonstrating a substantial performance improvement.

However, the experimental validation has minor weaknesses. The hyperparameter λ, which controls the strength of the constraint, is simply set to 1 without justification or a sensitivity analysis. While the paper notes it could be tuned, showing the model's performance under different λ values would have been more thorough. Additionally, the CQP solves a relaxed problem with continuous state indicators in [0, 1]. The paper does not specify how these continuous values are mapped back to a discrete state sequence for evaluation, which is a small but important implementation detail.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the identifiability problem, introducing their proposed solution, and detailing the mathematical formulation. The connection drawn to posterior regularization provides a nice theoretical grounding for their approach.

**Originality:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its originality. While the idea of adding constraints to AFHMMs is not new, previous work has largely focused on local constraints on state transitions (e.g., AFAMAP) or state durations (e.g., semi-Markov models). The introduction of SACs represents a fundamentally different and novel approach: applying a *global* constraint on the aggregate properties of the entire time series for each source. This is a simple, intuitive, and powerful idea that directly injects high-level domain knowledge into the model in a way that prior methods do not. It is a bold departure from the standard Markovian assumptions and opens up a new avenue for research in structured latent variable models.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. The empirical results are not merely incremental; a 29-45% error reduction on a difficult real-world task is a substantial advance. The core idea of using aggregate constraints is highly generalizable. It could easily be applied to other BSS domains, such as audio source separation (e.g., constraining the total speaking time of a person) or bioinformatics (e.g., constraining the total expression level of a gene over an experiment). By showing how to effectively incorporate such non-local priors via convex relaxation, this work provides a valuable tool and a new direction for researchers in many fields. It is the kind of paper that is likely to inspire follow-up work exploring other forms of global, semantically meaningful constraints.

### Arguments for Acceptance

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Advance:** The paper introduces a genuinely new class of constraints for AFHMMs. Moving from local, transition-based priors to global, aggregate-based priors is a significant conceptual shift and a creative way to tackle the identifiability problem.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Impact:** The method provides a large and statistically significant improvement over strong baselines on a challenging real-world problem. This is not an incremental improvement but a demonstrable leap in performance, validating the strength of the core idea.
3.  **Broad Potential for Impact:** The concept of SACs is not limited to energy disaggregation and could be influential in a wide variety of applications involving time-series decomposition and blind source separation.

### Arguments for Rejection

1.  **Incomplete Methodological Details:** The lack of discussion on rounding the CQP solution and the ad-hoc setting of the hyperparameter λ represent minor methodological gaps.
2.  **Limited Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper's performance relies on having a good prior for the aggregate values (`µi0`). While the toy experiment touches on this, a more thorough analysis of how performance degrades as this prior becomes less accurate would strengthen the paper's claims about its robustness in practice.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. Its primary contribution is a novel, elegant, and highly effective idea: using global signal aggregate constraints to resolve identifiability in AFHMMs. This represents a bold and creative step beyond the state-of-the-art, which has focused on more local types of constraints. The authors back up this novel concept with a sound inference method and truly impressive empirical results on a difficult, real-world task. While there are minor omissions in the experimental validation, such as hyperparameter tuning and handling the CQP relaxation's output, these do not detract from the importance and impact of the core contribution. This is precisely the kind of work—conceptually novel, practically significant, and likely to inspire new research directions—that NIPS should be highlighting.
