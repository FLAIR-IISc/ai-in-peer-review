## NIPS Review: Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation, specifically within the context of energy disaggregation. The authors identify a practical and often overlooked aspect of BSS – the existence of prior knowledge about aggregate signal properties – and propose a novel method (Signal Aggregate Constraints, or SACs) to incorporate this knowledge into a well-established probabilistic model (Additive Factorial HMMs). The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and demonstrates a significant performance improvement on a real-world dataset. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the inherent identifiability issues in Blind Source Separation (BSS) by leveraging domain-specific knowledge. Rather than attempting to uniquely identify source signals, the authors focus on constraining *aggregate* properties of those signals, based on the intuition that we often have a good prior belief on total energy consumption (e.g., a toaster uses roughly 50Wh/day).  They introduce SACs and integrate them into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM).  A key technical contribution is a convex quadratic program (CQP) relaxation that enables efficient inference with these constraints.

The related work section is well-structured and appropriately situates the work within the broader context of energy disaggregation and probabilistic modeling.  The authors accurately identify limitations of existing approaches, particularly the challenge of incorporating meaningful prior knowledge. They correctly highlight relevant work on posterior regularization, the generalized expectation criterion, and measurement-based learning, pointing out that the critical gap is *what* knowledge to incorporate.  The references to AFAMAP and other HMM constraint approaches in energy disaggregation are also pertinent.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Insight:** The core idea of SACs is genuinely novel and addresses a practical limitation in BSS.  It’s a smart realization that perfect source separation isn’t always necessary, and focusing on aggregate constraints is often more useful for real-world applications like energy disaggregation where interpretability and overall consumption estimates are key.
* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the CQP relaxation is well-explained and appears mathematically sound. The authors acknowledge the relaxation introduces a degree of suboptimality, but provide a justification for this trade-off given the computational benefits. The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) further strengthens the theoretical foundation.
* **Empirical Validation:** The results on both the toy dataset and the HES energy data are compelling. The 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM, and the 29% improvement over AFAMAP, are significant. The ablation study using both true and estimated aggregate consumption values (NTC) provides valuable insight into the sensitivity of the approach.
* **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise and easy to follow. The figures are informative and well-labeled.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors demonstrate that the proposed CQP approach maintains comparable computational efficiency to existing methods like AFAMAP, addressing a potential concern related to the added complexity of the constraints.



**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Sensitivity to Prior Specification (µ<sub>i0</sub>):** While the toy data experiment touches on this, the paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the sensitivity of the performance to errors in the aggregate constraint values (µ<sub>i0</sub>).  Real-world estimates of appliance energy usage are rarely perfect. A discussion on how to handle uncertainty or robustness in these values would be beneficial.
* **Generalizability Beyond Energy Disaggregation:**  The paper focuses almost exclusively on energy disaggregation. While the authors state the SAC approach is general, the examples and discussion are heavily biased towards this application.  A brief discussion of how SACs might be applied to other BSS problems (e.g., audio source separation) would strengthen the argument for broader applicability.
* **Limited Exploration of Constraint Generalization (Section 4.2):** The authors briefly mention the possibility of using multiple constraints per chain over different time periods. While acknowledging the increased complexity, a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between the granularity of constraints and computational cost could be helpful.
* **Lack of comparison to more recent disaggregation methods:** While AFAMAP is a reasonable baseline, the field has progressed since its publication. Including comparisons to other recent, state-of-the-art methods (beyond just the one mentioned) would further contextualize the improvement achieved by AFHMM+SAC.




**4. Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical, often overlooked problem in BSS: incorporating domain knowledge.
* Presents a novel and technically sound method (SACs) for achieving this.
* Demonstrates significant performance gains on a realistic dataset.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The sensitivity of the method to the accuracy of prior aggregate estimates requires further investigation.
* The focus is somewhat narrow, primarily limited to energy disaggregation.




**5.  Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Expand the discussion on prior specification:**  Include a section analyzing the impact of different levels of error in the µ<sub>i0</sub> values on the disaggregation performance.  Could strategies like robust optimization or Bayesian approaches be used to mitigate the effects of imprecise priors?
* **Discuss broader applicability:**  Provide a brief example or thought experiment illustrating how SACs could be applied to a BSS problem outside of energy disaggregation.
* **Add a comparative analysis with more recent disaggregation techniques.**
* **Elaborate on the trade-offs of generalized constraints:** Discuss the computational cost and potential benefits of using multiple constraints per chain.



**Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of blind source separation. The proposed SAC approach is a clever and effective way to incorporate domain knowledge into probabilistic models, and the empirical results are compelling. I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the minor suggestions for improvement outlined above.**
