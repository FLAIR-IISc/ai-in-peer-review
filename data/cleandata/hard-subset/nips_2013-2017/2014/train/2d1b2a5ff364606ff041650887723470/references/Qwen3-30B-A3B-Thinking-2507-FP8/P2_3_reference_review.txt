

# Review: Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation

## Summary
This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs), addressing the identifiability problem in blind source separation applications like energy disaggregation. The authors propose incorporating constraints on the aggregate values of source signals (e.g., total daily energy consumption per appliance) into the AFHMM framework. They derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) to efficiently solve the relaxed optimization problem with SACs and demonstrate significant improvements over baseline methods on both toy data and real-world energy disaggregation datasets.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound approach that addresses a well-known challenge in blind source separation. The theoretical foundation is solid, with clear mathematical derivations showing how SACs can be incorporated into the AFHMM framework. The authors properly connect their work to posterior regularization [4], providing a theoretical justification for their approach.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors demonstrate robustness on toy data (Section 7.1) and significant improvements on real-world energy data (Sections 7.2.1 and 7.2.2), showing 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to AFHMM and 29% improvement over AFAMAP. The use of paired t-tests to establish statistical significance is appropriate. The paper also appropriately acknowledges limitations, noting that SACs require prior knowledge about aggregate values (e.g., national consumption statistics), which would be unavailable in unsupervised settings.

One minor weakness is that the paper doesn't thoroughly explore the sensitivity to the constraint parameter λi (beyond the toy data experiment in Figure 1). While the authors state they used λi = 1 for all experiments, a more systematic analysis of how λi affects performance would strengthen the evaluation.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by highlighting the identifiability challenge in energy disaggregation, making the paper accessible to readers outside the specific domain. The technical sections are logically structured, with clear transitions between problem formulation, model extension, and optimization.

The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained, though the CQP derivation in Section 5 is quite dense. A brief intuitive explanation of why the problem becomes convex after the variable transformation would help readers grasp the key insight more quickly. The experimental results are presented clearly with appropriate tables and figures, and the discussion of results is well-aligned with the metrics (NDE and SAE).

## Originality

The core contribution—Signal Aggregate Constraints for AFHMMs—represents a novel approach to addressing identifiability in blind source separation. While previous work has explored constraints on HMM state transitions (e.g., AFAMAP [12]), SACs represent a different class of constraints that operate on the aggregate signal values rather than state transitions. This is a meaningful extension that is well-motivated by the energy disaggregation application.

The paper properly situates its work relative to related approaches (Section 2), noting that previous constraint-based methods focus on state transitions rather than aggregate signal properties. The connection to posterior regularization [4] provides additional theoretical grounding and demonstrates how SACs fit into a broader framework for incorporating domain knowledge.

The application to energy disaggregation is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates the practical value of the proposed constraints in a domain where domain knowledge about appliance consumption is available.

## Significance

The significance of this work is strong. The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in blind source separation (identifiability) with a practical solution that demonstrates clear improvements in a meaningful application domain. The 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to AFHMM is substantial and would be valuable for practitioners in energy monitoring.

The method is computationally efficient (similar runtime to baseline methods), making it practical for real-world deployment. The authors also demonstrate how SACs could be incorporated into unsupervised approaches by using national consumption statistics as prior knowledge, suggesting broader applicability beyond supervised settings.

While the paper focuses on energy disaggregation, the SAC concept could be applied to other BSS problems where domain knowledge about aggregate signal properties is available (e.g., audio source separation with known total volume ranges).

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Novel constraint approach (SACs) addressing a fundamental identifiability problem
- Strong theoretical foundation with connection to posterior regularization
- Comprehensive evaluation showing significant improvements (45% NDE reduction)
- Practical application with clear domain relevance
- Computationally efficient solution (similar to AFHMM runtime)

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited exploration of sensitivity to constraint parameters (λi)
- The paper doesn't discuss how SACs might be extended to unsupervised settings without prior knowledge (though they acknowledge this limitation)
- The toy data experiment is small (only two chains), which may not fully demonstrate scalability

## Recommendation

This paper makes a solid contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling for blind source separation. The SAC approach is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates significant practical improvements in a meaningful application. The technical quality is high, and the experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.

The paper meets all NIPS review criteria: it's of high quality with sound theory and experiments, clearly written, original in its approach, and significant in its application to energy disaggregation. While there are minor limitations in the exploration of constraint sensitivity, these do not undermine the overall contribution.

I recommend acceptance. This work provides a valuable methodological advance that could be broadly applied to other blind source separation problems where aggregate signal constraints are available.

## Additional Comments
The authors might consider briefly discussing how SACs could be integrated with recent advances in energy disaggregation (e.g., deep learning approaches), though this is not essential for the current contribution. The paper's focus on the theoretical and methodological contribution is appropriate for NIPS.

The connection to posterior regularization is particularly strong and well-executed, which is a significant strength of the paper. This theoretical grounding enhances the paper's contribution beyond a mere application-specific method.
