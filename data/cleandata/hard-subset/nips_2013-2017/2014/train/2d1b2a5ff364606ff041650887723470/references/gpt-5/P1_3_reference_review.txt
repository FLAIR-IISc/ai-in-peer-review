Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses blind source separation in additive, multi-chain sequential models, with a focus on energy disaggregation (NILM). The core idea is to incorporate Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs): prior knowledge about aggregates (e.g., total daily energy per appliance) to mitigate identifiability issues. Concretely, the authors add quadratic penalties enforcing that, for each source i, the sum over time of its emitted power approximates a prespecified total µi0. They instantiate this in an Additive Factorial HMM (AFHMM), yielding AFHMM+SAC, and derive a convex quadratic program (CQP) via a standard augmentation introducing transition indicator variables H and relaxing binary constraints. They also show the equivalence to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2010), where the penalized objective corresponds to KL-projection onto a constrained posterior family. Experiments on synthetic data and the UK HES dataset (ultra-low sampling rate) show improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012); on mains data across 9 houses, they report 45% lower NDE vs AFHMM and 29% vs AFAMAP, and better SAE, with similar runtimes.

Relative to prior work: AFHMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan, 1997) and AFAMAP add state-transition constraints (e.g., at most one chain changes per timestep). Semi-Markov and non-homogeneous FHMM variants (e.g., Johnson & Willsky; Zhong et al.) constrain durations or time-varying transitions. Posterior regularization and measurement-based learning allow aggregate constraints in other models, but typically not in additive FHMMs with tractable inference. This paper’s novelty is specifying sum constraints on each chain’s emissions and deriving a convex relaxation that can be solved efficiently, with an application to NILM at very low sampling rates.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The formulation is clear. The transformation to a CQP via S and H variables, PSD quadratic terms (from Gaussian likelihood and quadratic SAC penalties), and linear transition terms is correct. The posterior regularization view is appropriate.
- A major missing detail is how fractional relaxed solutions are mapped back to valid state sequences (or whether the method simply uses fractional S to reconstruct xi,t). If outputs are soft state occupancies, the link to MAP over discrete states is weakened; implications for interpretability and evaluation should be clarified.
- Hyperparameterization: λi are fixed to 1 without tuning, which can bias comparisons and hides sensitivity. Conversely, the toy study shows sensitivity of performance to SAC target accuracy.
- Empirics: The HES experiments are valuable; they include both synthetic aggregates (no mains) and realistic mains with unmodeled loads. Statistical tests are mentioned, but more detail (p-values, confidence intervals) would strengthen claims. Some strong baselines (semi-Markov NILM or more recent NILM methods) are cited but not included in the comparison.

Clarity:
- Generally readable, with clear motivation and derivations. Some sections are notation-heavy; adding a brief variable/shape table would improve readability.
- Please clarify: (a) rounding/projection from relaxed S,H to discrete states; (b) how x̂it is computed from fractional S; (c) treatment of σt and how it is estimated.
- The extension to multiple time-window constraints is well-motivated; a brief note on how it enters the CQP (sum of additional PSD terms) would help.

Originality:
- The specific use of per-chain aggregate constraints in additive FHMMs and the convex relaxation is novel to my knowledge. The connection to posterior regularization is well-known but appropriately leveraged here.
- The paper goes beyond AFAMAP’s local transition constraints and provides a different type of prior (global, per-chain totals).

Significance:
- Practical relevance is high in NILM, especially for low-frequency data where identifiability is acute. Improvements over AFAMAP are notable. The idea is general and potentially applicable in other BSS settings (e.g., audio sources with known energy budgets, genomics with known total counts).
- The approach depends on availability and accuracy of µi0. The toy results show sensitivity; in practice, household-level appliance totals may deviate substantially from national averages. This could limit robustness.

Strengths:
- Simple, interpretable prior that directly addresses identifiability.
- Convex relaxation with an off-the-shelf solver; principled link to posterior regularization.
- Demonstrated gains on realistic, challenging low-rate data.
- Straightforward extension to multi-window SACs to encode diurnal priors.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Rounding/decoding: Specify and evaluate a rounding or decoding procedure to obtain discrete sequences (e.g., per-chain Viterbi conditioned on soft marginals; projection via min-cost flow; simple argmax per time with consistency). Compare performance of soft vs rounded outputs.
- Hyperparameters: Explore λi selection (CV, held-out likelihood, or evidence maximization). Report sensitivity curves and whether per-appliance λ improves robustness.
- SAC target acquisition: Discuss practical methods beyond NTC (e.g., hierarchical priors to adapt national stats to households; slack variables with asymmetric penalties; robust losses like Huber). Evaluate robustness to mis-specified µi0 on real data.
- Baselines: Include comparisons to (a) HSMM/semi-Markov NILM; (b) interleaved/non-homogeneous FHMM; ideally one unsupervised method if feasible.
- Scalability: CVX can struggle with long sequences. Provide complexity in terms of variables/constraints, actual runtimes vs T, I, Ki, and a specialized solver (e.g., ADMM, projected quasi-Newton) or warm-started block coordinate scheme. Report hardware details.
- Reproducibility: Provide code or solver settings, and details on σt, parameter estimation, and number of training days per household. Clarify whether AFAMAP and AFHMM were tuned comparably.

Questions for the authors:
1) How do you convert relaxed S,H to a discrete sequence, if at all? Do reported metrics use fractional S? If so, how does that affect interpretability?
2) How are σt and λi chosen? Did you validate λi on a subset? How sensitive are results on HES to λi?
3) In the mains experiment, how often do µi0 from NTC substantially misestimate true totals, and what is the performance degradation in those cases?
4) Can SACs be combined with AFAMAP’s “at most one change per step” constraint within your CQP framework?

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, useful idea addressing a real weakness of AFHMMs; principled formulation and connection to posterior regularization.
- Demonstrated, statistically significant improvements on challenging real data.
- Method is general and likely to influence future work in weakly supervised/semi-supervised sequence models.

Cons for acceptance:
- Incomplete treatment of decoding/rounding; unclear whether outputs are discrete or soft.
- Limited baseline coverage and hyperparameter tuning; robustness to mis-specified SACs on real data not deeply analyzed.
- Scalability claims rely on CVX; practicality for longer horizons is uncertain.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper introduces a simple, impactful prior and a solid optimization framework with promising empirical gains. Addressing decoding, robustness, and scalability concerns would strengthen the final version.
