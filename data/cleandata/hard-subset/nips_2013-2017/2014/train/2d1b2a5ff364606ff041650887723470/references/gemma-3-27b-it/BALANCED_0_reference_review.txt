This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for improving blind source separation, specifically within the context of Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs) applied to energy disaggregation. The core idea is to incorporate prior knowledge about expected total energy usage for different appliance types as constraints during inference. The authors formulate the problem as a convex quadratic program (CQP) for efficient computation and demonstrate significant performance gains on real-world energy disaggregation data, achieving a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a crucial challenge in energy disaggregation – the identifiability problem and the need for interpretable source signals. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and the limitations of existing approaches. The connection to prior work in probabilistic modeling and BSS is thorough, citing relevant literature in posterior regularization, generalized expectation criteria, and measurement-based learning. The authors appropriately position their contribution as addressing the question of *what* types of domain knowledge to incorporate, rather than proposing a novel general framework.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The introduction of SACs as a specific class of constraints tailored to BSS problems with known aggregate characteristics is a valuable contribution. While incorporating prior knowledge isn't new, the specific formulation and theoretical justification through posterior regularization are strong points.
* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the CQP for solving the relaxed optimization problem appears rigorous and well-explained. The connection to convex optimization allows for efficient inference.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results on real-world energy disaggregation data are compelling. A 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error is a substantial improvement and demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.  The comparison to a recent state-of-the-art approach (AFAMAP) further strengthens the claims.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are accessible. The inclusion of both toy data experiments and real-world results provides a comprehensive evaluation.
* **Significance:** The improvement in energy disaggregation accuracy has practical implications for helping consumers understand and reduce their energy consumption. The generality of SACs potentially extends to other BSS applications beyond energy disaggregation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Exploration of Constraint Strength:** While the authors acknowledge the tuning parameter δ (or equivalently λ), the experimental evaluation primarily focuses on a single setting. A more systematic exploration of the impact of constraint strength on performance would be beneficial. How sensitive are the results to the choice of δ, and how can it be optimally tuned in practice?
* **Assumptions about Known Aggregate Values:** The performance relies on having accurate prior knowledge of the aggregate values (µi0). The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t delve deeply into the challenges of obtaining these values in practice.  In real-world scenarios, this information might be imperfect or unavailable. Discussing strategies for handling uncertainty or estimating µi0 would strengthen the paper.
* **Lack of Comparison to Alternative Priors:** While the paper establishes a connection to posterior regularization, it doesn’t compare SACs directly to other commonly used priors within the same framework.  How does a Gaussian prior on the total energy consumption compare to the proposed SAC?
* **Computational Complexity Discussion:** While the authors state that the CQP scales similarly to AFHMM and AFAMAP, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity, particularly in terms of the number of states and the length of the time series, would be helpful.  For very large-scale problems, scalability could become a concern.
* **Presentation of Results:** While the results are strong, the tables are a little dense and could benefit from clearer highlighting of the key improvements.  For example, directly stating the percentage improvement of AFHMM+SAC over AFAMAP in the table captions would improve readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Strong empirical results demonstrating significant performance gains on a real-world problem.
* Novel and well-motivated approach to incorporating domain knowledge into BSS.
* Technically sound derivation of the CQP formulation.
* Clear and well-organized presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Limited exploration of the sensitivity of the method to the constraint strength parameter.
* Reliance on accurately known aggregate values, which may not always be available in practice.
* Lack of comparison to alternative prior distributions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in a novel and technically sound manner. The empirical results are compelling, and the paper is well-written and organized. While there are some areas for improvement, the contributions are significant enough to warrant acceptance.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the exploration of constraint strength, discussion of aggregate value estimation, and comparison to alternative priors.  Specifically, I recommend adding a section or appendix dedicated to tuning δ/λ and analyzing the trade-off between constraint enforcement and model fit.
