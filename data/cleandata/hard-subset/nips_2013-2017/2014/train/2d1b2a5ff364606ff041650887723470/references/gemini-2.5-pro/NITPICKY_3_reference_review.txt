**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), with a specific application to energy disaggregation. The authors identify the key challenge of non-identifiability in underdetermined BSS settings. To mitigate this, they propose incorporating domain knowledge through "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM). The core idea is to penalize solutions where the total sum of a source signal over a time period deviates from a known prior value (e.g., the expected daily energy consumption of an appliance). As this constraint breaks the Markov property, making standard inference intractable, the authors formulate MAP inference as an integer quadratic program and then solve a convex relaxation (a Convex Quadratic Program - CQP). The authors demonstrate a connection between their penalized objective and the posterior regularization framework. Experiments on a toy dataset and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES) show that their method, AFHMM+SAC, significantly outperforms a standard AFHMM and a state-of-the-art competitor, AFAMAP.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Intuitive Constraint:** The central contribution—the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints—is a simple, intuitive, and novel way to inject valuable domain knowledge into the BSS problem. It directly addresses the identifiability issue by encouraging sources to conform to realistic, long-term aggregate behaviors, which is a sensible approach for applications like energy disaggregation.
2.  **Principled Formulation:** The method is well-grounded, framed as a MAP inference problem with a penalty term that can be interpreted as a log-prior. The authors further strengthen this by showing its equivalence to a posterior regularization framework, lending it additional theoretical weight.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental evidence on a challenging, low-frequency real-world dataset. The reported 45% reduction in NDE compared to the baseline AFHMM and 29% reduction compared to the state-of-the-art AFAMAP are substantial improvements. The use of both oracle (TTC) and realistic (NTC) constraints provides a good sense of the potential and practical utility of the method.

**Weaknesses**

While the core idea is promising, the paper is marred by a significant number of flaws in its technical exposition, experimental methodology, and overall presentation.

1.  **Critical Methodological Omission:** The entire inference procedure hinges on solving a relaxed CQP where the state indicator variables `Sitk` are in the continuous interval [0, 1]. However, the paper completely fails to describe how these fractional values are converted back into a discrete sequence of states required for calculating the NDE and SAE metrics. Is a rounding scheme used? Are the fractional values used to compute an expected signal? This is a critical detail for reproducibility and understanding the method's behavior, and its omission is a major flaw.

2.  **Lack of Experimental Rigor:**
    *   The key hyperparameter λ, which balances the data likelihood against the constraint, is arbitrarily set to 1 for all experiments. The paper notes that it could be tuned via cross-validation but provides no justification for this choice, nor any sensitivity analysis. The performance of the model is likely highly dependent on λ, and this lack of analysis severely weakens the empirical claims.
    *   The number of HMM states is fixed to 3 for all appliances. This is an oversimplification, and no analysis is provided on how this choice affects performance.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation Issues:** The manuscript lacks the polish expected of a top-tier conference submission.
    *   **Notation:** The notation is frequently confusing and inconsistent. In Eq. (1), `t` is used as both the time index and the noise term. The use of `P` vs. `p` for probabilities is inconsistent (Eq. 2). The notation introduced in Section 5 is extremely dense and non-standard (e.g., `1T`, `eT`, `Hit.l`), making the CQP formulation difficult to parse.
    *   **Typos and Grammatical Errors:** There are numerous typos (e.g., "distibution", "equavalent") and awkward phrasings (e.g., "the objective can be viewed as a log posterior distribution").
    *   **Incomplete Figures and References:** Figure 1 is referenced but not included, and its caption in the text is truncated. Several bibliographic entries are malformed (e.g., [7], [16], [23]), suggesting a lack of care in preparation.
    *   **Expository Gaps:** The explanation for why chainwise Viterbi fails in Section 4.1 is misleading; the issue is its nature as a local search, not the model's identifiability. The claim in Section 4.2 that extending to multiple constraints "does not complicate the algorithms" is a strong and likely inaccurate statement, as it would increase the problem size and computational cost.

4.  **Limited Scope of Comparison:** While the comparison to AFAMAP is relevant, the authors miss the opportunity to compare against or combine with other orthogonal constraint types. For instance, why not evaluate an AFAMAP+SAC model to see if the constraints are complementary? Other related methods for constraining HMMs, such as semi-Markov models mentioned in the related work, are also not included as baselines.

**Recommendation**

Reject.

The core idea of signal aggregate constraints is valuable and the empirical results appear strong. However, the paper in its current form falls well below the standard for NIPS. The omission of the discretization step after solving the CQP is a critical flaw that makes the work irreproducible. This, combined with the unprincipled handling of hyperparameters and the multitude of clarity, notational, and formatting issues, makes the paper feel rushed and incomplete. While the contribution has potential, it requires a major revision to address these fundamental problems before it can be considered for publication.
