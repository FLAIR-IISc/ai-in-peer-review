**Summary**
This paper proposes incorporating "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) into the Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework to improve performance on blind source separation tasks. The core idea is to regularize the model by encouraging the total sum of each latent source signal over a given time horizon to be close to a pre-specified, known value. This constraint is non-Markovian, making standard MAP inference with algorithms like Viterbi intractable. The authors address this by formulating a relaxed version of the MAP inference problem as a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The method, termed AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on the task of energy disaggregation using both semi-synthetic and real-world household electricity data, demonstrating improved performance over a standard AFHMM and the AFAMAP algorithm, a previously published state-of-the-art method.

**Strengths**
1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses the critical issue of identifiability in blind source separation, particularly in the context of AFHMMs. The proposed solution of using prior knowledge on signal aggregates is intuitive and well-suited for applications like energy disaggregation, where such priors (e.g., average daily consumption of an appliance) are often available.
2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The approach of relaxing the intractable integer quadratic program into a CQP is a standard and sound technique in machine learning. This provides a principled and computationally feasible inference algorithm. The authors also correctly show the connection of their penalized objective function to the posterior regularization framework [4], which lends theoretical justification to the approach.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The model, the proposed constraints, and the derivation of the CQP are explained clearly.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Novelty:** The core contribution, the "Signal Aggregate Constraint," is essentially a quadratic regularizer on the sum of state emissions over time. While its application to AFHMMs for this task is new, the idea of using global summary statistics as a form of regularization or prior is not fundamentally novel. The paper presents this as a new class of constraints, but the generalization to other types of aggregates or time periods is only briefly mentioned and not explored, making the contribution feel narrower than claimed. It is more of an effective application of domain knowledge via a standard regularization technique than a new methodological advance.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The experimental evaluation, while showing promising results, suffers from several weaknesses that undermine the strength of the paper's claims:
    *   **Baselines:** The primary comparison is against the AFAMAP algorithm from 2012. While AFAMAP is a relevant baseline, the field of energy disaggregation has seen significant progress since then. The evaluation would be far more convincing if compared against more recent and diverse state-of-the-art methods, including those based on different model classes (e.g., deep neural networks, other non-parametric approaches) that have been shown to be effective for this task. The related work mentions semi-Markov models [10, 11] but does not include them in the comparison.
    *   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** A significant flaw is the lack of proper hyperparameter tuning. The authors state that the complexity parameter `λi` was simply set to 1 for all experiments. This parameter is crucial as it balances the likelihood against the new constraint. Without a principled method for setting this parameter (e.g., via cross-validation on a held-out set), it is impossible to know if the reported gains are robust or simply an artifact of a fortuitous choice of `λ`. A proper comparison would require tuning the key hyperparameters for *all* competing methods. The reported improvements over AFAMAP could be less significant if both models were properly tuned.
    *   **Handling of Relaxed Solution:** The paper formulates inference as a CQP, which yields a relaxed solution where the state indicator variables `Sitk` lie in [0, 1]. However, the paper fails to describe how this continuous solution is projected back into a discrete state sequence for evaluation (i.e., for calculating the NDE and SAE metrics). This is a critical missing detail. The choice of rounding or projection scheme can significantly impact the final performance, and its omission makes the results difficult to interpret and reproduce.

3.  **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The paper demonstrates that the method works well when the prior on the aggregate is accurate (the TTC "oracle" case) and reasonably well with a plausible proxy (the NTC case). However, a more thorough sensitivity analysis is warranted. How gracefully does performance degrade as the provided aggregate prior `µi0` deviates from the true value? The toy data experiment provides a glimpse, but a more systematic analysis on the real-world data would be necessary to understand the practical robustness of the method.

**Summary of pros and cons for acceptance:**

**Pros:**
*   Addresses a well-motivated and important problem (identifiability in BSS).
*   The proposed method is intuitive and built upon an established model (AFHMM).
*   The use of a CQP relaxation for inference is a sound and computationally efficient approach.
*   The empirical results on the energy disaggregation task appear promising.

**Cons:**
*   The novelty of the core contribution (the SAC itself) is limited. It is a straightforward application of a quadratic regularizer.
*   The experimental evaluation is not rigorous enough for a top-tier venue. The baselines are somewhat dated, and the complete lack of hyperparameter tuning for the proposed method's key parameter (`λ`) is a major concern.
*   The paper omits a critical methodological detail: how the continuous solution from the CQP is converted to a discrete sequence for evaluation.
*   The analysis lacks a thorough investigation of the method's sensitivity to the accuracy of the provided priors.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper presents an interesting application of a constrained AFHMM to an important real-world problem, its contributions are undermined by a lack of methodological novelty and, more critically, an insufficiently rigorous experimental evaluation. The claims of significant improvement are not fully supported due to the absence of hyperparameter tuning and comparison to a wider set of contemporary baselines. The paper feels more suitable for an applied or workshop venue. In its current form, it does not meet the high standards of originality and rigor expected for NIPS. I therefore recommend rejection.
