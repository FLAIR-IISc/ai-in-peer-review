### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS) in the context of an underdetermined system, with a specific application to energy disaggregation. The authors propose incorporating "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM). The core idea is that for many BSS problems, one has prior knowledge about the expected aggregate value of each source signal over a given time period (e.g., the average daily energy consumption of a specific appliance). These constraints are non-Markovian, making standard inference techniques like the Viterbi algorithm inapplicable. To overcome this, the authors formulate the MAP inference problem as a penalized objective function, relax the integer constraints on the hidden states, and solve the resulting convex quadratic program (CQP). The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach, termed AFHMM+SAC, on both toy data and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset, reporting significant improvements over a standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated Problem and Approach:** The paper tackles the well-known identifiability issue in BSS. The proposed solution of using aggregate constraints is intuitive and directly leverages a type of domain knowledge that is often available in practical applications like energy disaggregation. This is a sensible and pragmatic approach to regularizing an otherwise ill-posed problem.

2.  **Sound Methodological Formulation:** The authors provide a clear and technically sound method for incorporating the proposed constraints. While the SACs themselves break the Markov property, the transformation of the MAP problem into a CQP via relaxation is a standard and valid technique. The connection drawn to the posterior regularization framework of Ganchev et al. (2010) adds theoretical justification to the penalized objective function, framing it as more than just an ad-hoc penalty term.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results on a Relevant Task:** The experimental evaluation shows substantial performance gains on the energy disaggregation task. The reported 29% improvement in normalized disaggregation error over a strong and recent baseline (AFAMAP) on real-world data is significant and suggests that the proposed constraints are highly effective for this application.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Evaluation of the Core Contribution:** The central contribution is the signal aggregate constraint, which relies on a known prior value `µi0`. The paper's evaluation of this component is not sufficiently rigorous. The experiments rely on two settings for this prior: an oracle "True Total Consumption" (TTC) and a "National Total Consumption" (NTC) derived from the training data of the *same dataset*. The TTC is an unrealistic upper bound. The NTC setting demonstrates that the method works when the train and test distributions are very similar, but it fails to address the more realistic scenario where the prior is derived from an external, potentially mismatched source (e.g., a national survey from a different year or region). The robustness of the method to errors in the prior is only explored on a simple toy dataset and needs more thorough investigation on the real-world data.

2.  **Lack of Hyperparameter Analysis:** The proposed method introduces a crucial complexity parameter, `λ`, for each constraint. The authors state that this parameter was simply set to 1 for all experiments. This is a significant weakness in the experimental methodology. There is no justification for this choice, no sensitivity analysis, and no discussion of how this critical parameter would be tuned in practice (e.g., using a validation set). The strong reported results might be highly dependent on this specific choice, and without a proper analysis, the generalizability and robustness of the findings are questionable. A rigorous evaluation would present results across a range of `λ` values or employ a principled method for its selection.

3.  **Novelty is Somewhat Incremental:** The underlying model (AFHMM) and the optimization technique (relaxing an integer program to a CQP) are established methods. The AFAMAP paper, used as a baseline, also employs a CQP relaxation for inference in a constrained AFHMM. The main novelty of this work lies in the specific *type* of constraint being proposed. While effective, this can be viewed as the introduction of a strong, well-chosen prior rather than a fundamental advance in inference or modeling methodology. The claim of introducing a "new model" (AFHMM+SAC) is perhaps an overstatement; it is more accurately an AFHMM with a novel regularization term.

4.  **Insufficient Comparison to Other Constraining Methods:** The paper's related work section mentions several alternative approaches for constraining HMMs, such as semi-Markov models that model state durations. While AFAMAP is a good baseline, the paper would be stronger if it compared its performance against other distinct classes of constrained models to better situate the contribution within the existing literature.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   The paper introduces a simple, intuitive, and highly effective idea for improving a standard model on a challenging real-world problem.
*   The reported performance gains over a strong baseline are substantial and practically significant.
*   The methodological formulation is sound, and the connection to posterior regularization is a nice theoretical contribution.

### Arguments against Acceptance:

*   The experimental validation is not sufficiently rigorous for a top-tier conference. The lack of hyperparameter tuning for `λ` and the limited exploration of the prior's quality (`µi0`) are major methodological flaws that cast doubt on the reliability and generality of the results.
*   The novelty is limited to the specific form of the constraint, while the core model and inference strategy are largely borrowed from prior work.

### Conclusion

Overall, this paper presents a sensible and effective idea. However, the experimental evaluation lacks the rigor expected for a NIPS publication. The arbitrary setting of the key hyperparameter `λ` and the limited testing of the method's sensitivity to the quality of the prior information are significant shortcomings. While the results are promising, they are not yet convincingly validated. The paper feels more like a strong workshop paper or a good application paper than a fundamental methodological contribution for a general machine learning audience. I am therefore leaning towards rejection, but I would be open to changing my mind if the authors could address the weaknesses in their experimental validation, particularly regarding hyperparameter selection and sensitivity to the prior.
