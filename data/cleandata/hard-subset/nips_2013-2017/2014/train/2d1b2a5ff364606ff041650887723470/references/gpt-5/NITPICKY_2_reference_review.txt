Summary
The paper proposes adding signal aggregate constraints (SACs) to additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) for blind source separation, with energy disaggregation as the main application. The key idea is to incorporate prior knowledge about the total energy consumed by each source over a horizon (or over sub-intervals) and penalize deviation from these aggregate targets. The MAP inference with these global, non-Markov constraints is relaxed and reformulated as a convex quadratic program (CQP) by introducing auxiliary transition variables, enabling efficient optimization with CVX. The authors also connect the approach to posterior regularization, showing the constrained objective corresponds to a KL projection with quadratic moment constraints. Empirically, the method improves normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) over AFHMM and AFAMAP on HES data, with a toy experiment exploring sensitivity to mis-specified aggregates.

Relation to prior work
The contribution sits between prior structure-imposing AFHMM variants (e.g., AFAMAP’s “at most one chain changes” constraint) and framework-level approaches like posterior regularization and measurement-based learning. The novelty is the specific, global-in-time aggregate constraints over latent chains (distinct from duration constraints or interleaving constraints). The formal connection to posterior regularization is appropriate and known, but helpful here.

Strengths
- Clear, broadly applicable idea: domain aggregates are common in BSS (e.g., energy budgets).
- Solid optimization formulation: linearization via joint transition variables yields a concave quadratic objective with linear constraints; the relaxation is convex and tractable.
- The posterior-regularization view is correct and clarifies the role of λ as Lagrange multipliers.
- Empirical gains on realistic, low-frequency HES data are promising; sensitivity on toy data is informative.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Relaxation vs. discrete decoding: The paper never states how the fractional S,H from the CQP are converted for evaluation. Are x̂ computed from fractional states (soft) while baselines produce hard states? If so, the comparison may be biased. Please specify the rounding/decoding procedure and evaluate both soft and hard outputs for fairness.
- Hyperparameters: λi are fixed to 1 for all experiments; no tuning or principled setting is provided. Since performance (and convexity scaling) depends on λi, an empirical sensitivity analysis on real data is needed. How would one set λi without access to TTC? Are λi shared or per-appliance?
- Computational claims: The use of CVX is convenient but not scalable beyond small T, I, Ki due to O(∑i TKi^2) auxiliary variables (H). The paper asserts similar runtime to AFAMAP but does not report concrete wall-clock times, hardware, solver back-end (SDPT3/SeDuMi), or problem sizes (T, I, Ki). Provide detailed runtime/memory scaling and limits; otherwise claims of comparable efficiency are not credible.
- Model assumptions: xit ≈ μik with Gaussian noise only at the observation Y level implies deterministic per-state emissions for appliances. This is strong and likely violated. How robust are SACs when per-state appliance draws have variance? Is σt learned or fixed?
- Estimation of θ: The paper says θ are “assumed known,” then later that they are estimated from 15–30 days via k-means (μi) but does not specify how initial/transition probabilities and σt are estimated. Please provide full training details and any regularization.
- Incomplete baselines: Comparisons are limited to AFHMM and AFAMAP. Missing are strong baselines used in NILM (e.g., NMF variants, sparse coding, semi-Markov/HsMM approaches, interleaved non-homogeneous FHMMs). This makes significance claims less convincing.
- Identifiability: SACs partially address identifiability but do not resolve it. Under what conditions (e.g., distinct μi, accurate μi0) is identifiability improved? Some analysis or counterexamples would strengthen the paper.
- Statistical reporting: Paired t-tests are mentioned, but effect sizes, p-values, and variance/CI are not reported. Tables/Figures referenced (Tables 1–3, Figure 2) are required to assess claims.

Clarity
- Several definitions are terse or missing: exact form of constraints for windowed SACs, proof sketch for convexity after introducing H, and the linearization details of transition terms.
- Notation is heavy and error-prone; multiple typos (“equavalent,” “distibution”), undefined symbols appear (e.g., Hit.l notation introduced but used sparsely). Some lines are truncated/repeated in Section 7.1 (duplicated sentence fragment).
- Units and data: It is unclear whether Yt is power or energy; HES measures at 2/10 minutes—are features rescaled? The observation model uses Gaussian noise on a nonnegative quantity; negative predictions possible—any truncation?
- Figures/Tables are not present; text references them for key results.

Originality
- SACs as quadratic moment constraints within posterior regularization are conceptually straightforward and anticipated by prior frameworks (posterior regularization, measurement-based learning). The novelty lies in the particular aggregate choice and an AFHMM-specific convex relaxation. This feels incremental unless broader applicability or stronger theory is provided.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for NILM and other BSS settings where aggregate budgets are known. However, the lack of broader baselines, weak hyperparameter handling, and unclear scalability limit practical adoption. The claimed improvements (29–45%) are compelling but insufficiently substantiated without full experimental detail.

Minor formatting and reference issues
- Many references lack years, venues, or are malformed (e.g., “editors,” repeated names; broken URL for CVX; “and C, J.-P. Zimmermann…”). Please fix all citations.
- Inconsistent citation numbering/text (e.g., [6] listing editors twice).
- Several grammatical errors and missing spaces; equation numbers sometimes not referenced; cross-references to tables/figures without content.

Questions for the authors
- How are fractional S,H mapped to discrete states for evaluation? Does using soft states advantage your method?
- How are λi selected in practice without oracle TTC? Any unsupervised/empirical Bayes approach?
- What solver and settings were used in CVX? Report runtime/memory vs T,I,Ki. What are the largest problems you solved?
- How are πi, P(i), σt estimated? Are they shared across households?
- Results for windowed SACs (day/night) would be informative; can you include an ablation?
- Compare to at least one NMF/sparse baseline and an HsMM baseline.

Pros
- Simple, intuitive way to inject domain knowledge; principled posterior-regularization link.
- Convex relaxation with linearization is well motivated.
- Promising improvements on realistic data.

Cons
- Insufficient experimental detail, missing baselines, unclear fairness in evaluation.
- Hyperparameter and solver issues unaddressed; scalability claims unsubstantiated.
- Numerous presentation and referencing problems.
- Incremental originality relative to posterior regularization/measurement-based learning.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The idea is interesting and likely useful, but the cumulative technical, experimental, and presentation shortcomings prevent confident acceptance. With stronger baselines, clear decoding procedures, robust hyperparameter treatment, detailed runtime scaling, and substantial editing, this could become a solid contribution.
