

# Peer Review: "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

## Summary
This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs), addressing the critical identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS) applications like energy disaggregation. The authors observe that in many BSS domains, domain knowledge about total signal aggregates (e.g., "a toaster uses approximately 50 Wh per day") is available but underutilized. SACs enforce constraints that the sum of each source signal over time should be close to specified values, transforming the problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP) for efficient optimization. The approach is theoretically grounded in posterior regularization and evaluated on both synthetic data and real-world energy disaggregation data from the Household Electricity Survey (HES), demonstrating significant improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP baselines (45% and 29% reduction in normalized disaggregation error, respectively).

The work builds on prior BSS and energy disaggregation research, particularly the AFHMM framework [5, 17] and constraint-based approaches like AFAMAP [12], while making a novel contribution by formalizing and efficiently implementing aggregate constraints that leverage domain knowledge about total signal values rather than state transitions.

## Strengths

**Quality & Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a technically rigorous formulation of SACs and their incorporation into AFHMMs. The transformation to a convex quadratic program is well-justified and supported by theoretical connections to posterior regularization [4]. The experimental evaluation is thorough, including both synthetic data (to demonstrate robustness to constraint specification errors) and real-world energy data with statistical significance testing (paired t-tests). The authors carefully address limitations, such as the need for constraint tuning and the oracle-like nature of the "true total consumption" evaluation.

**Clarity & Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear motivation, precise mathematical formulations, and effective use of figures/tables to present results. The problem statement, model description, and algorithmic contributions are logically presented, making the technical content accessible to the NIPS audience. The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) is particularly helpful for contextualizing the method.

**Originality:** The SAC concept represents a novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge in BSS, distinct from prior work that focused on state transition constraints (e.g., AFAMAP [12] constraining at most one chain to change state at a time). While posterior regularization [4] provides a theoretical framework, the specific implementation of aggregate constraints for time series BSS is original. The application to energy disaggregation is well-motivated, and the paper clearly differentiates its contribution from related work (Section 2).

**Significance:** The energy disaggregation problem is of significant practical importance for energy efficiency and consumer awareness. The demonstrated 45% improvement over AFHMM (a standard baseline) and 29% improvement over the state-of-the-art AFAMAP is substantial in this domain. The approach is generalizable to other BSS applications where aggregate constraints are available, making it potentially impactful beyond energy disaggregation.

## Weaknesses

**Computational Complexity Justification:** While the paper claims computational efficiency, the justification for the CQP approach over alternatives (like iterative constraint satisfaction) could be strengthened. The paper mentions "polynomial time in the number of time steps times the total number of states," but doesn't provide concrete complexity comparisons with the chainwise Viterbi algorithm used in AFAMAP.

**Constraint Specification in Practice:** The paper acknowledges that the "true total consumption" (TTC) is unattainable in practice, but the National Total Consumption (NTC) approach used for real data is somewhat limited—it's based on training data averages across households, which may not reflect individual household appliance usage patterns. The paper doesn't explore how to obtain better constraint values in real-world scenarios beyond national surveys.

**Lack of Ablation on Constraint Types:** The paper focuses on a single type of constraint (total daily energy), but energy disaggregation might benefit from more complex constraints (e.g., time-specific constraints for appliances used only during certain hours, as mentioned in Section 4.2). While the paper acknowledges this as a possible extension, it doesn't explore the impact of different constraint types in the experiments.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the energy disaggregation literature and BSS more broadly. It addresses a critical practical challenge (identifiability) with a novel, theoretically grounded, and practically effective approach. The experimental results are compelling, and the connection to posterior regularization provides a solid theoretical foundation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel approach to incorporating domain knowledge via aggregate constraints
- Strong theoretical grounding connecting to posterior regularization
- Significant empirical improvements over state-of-the-art baselines
- Clear, well-organized presentation of technical content
- Practical relevance to energy disaggregation, a growing application area

**Arguments for Rejection:**
- Limited discussion of constraint specification in real-world scenarios
- Slightly underdeveloped justification for computational efficiency
- No exploration of more complex constraint types (e.g., time-specific)

## Final Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field with well-executed experiments and strong theoretical grounding. The minor weaknesses noted above are not substantial enough to outweigh the paper's substantial strengths and contribution. The work advances the state of the art in energy disaggregation and provides a general framework for incorporating domain knowledge in BSS problems, which aligns well with NIPS' focus on novel machine learning approaches to practical problems. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field.

The authors should consider addressing the computational complexity justification and constraint specification challenges in the camera-ready version, but these are not sufficient to warrant rejection.
