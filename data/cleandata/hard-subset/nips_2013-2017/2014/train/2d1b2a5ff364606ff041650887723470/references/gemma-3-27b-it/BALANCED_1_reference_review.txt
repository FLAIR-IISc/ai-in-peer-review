This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation, specifically within the context of Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs), and applies this to the problem of energy disaggregation. The core idea is to leverage prior knowledge about the expected total energy consumption of individual appliances to improve the accuracy of disaggregation. The authors formulate this as a constrained optimization problem and propose a convex quadratic program (CQP) for solving the relaxed version. Experimental results on both simulated and real-world energy data demonstrate significant performance gains compared to the baseline AFHMM and a state-of-the-art alternative.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a critical challenge in energy disaggregation – the identifiability problem. The problem statement is clear, and the connection to broader BSS literature is appropriately established. The introduction effectively highlights the practical relevance of the work and the limitations of existing approaches. The use of SACs is a sensible and intuitive way to incorporate domain knowledge, and the mathematical formulation appears sound. The derivation of the CQP is a significant technical contribution, offering a computationally efficient solution to a challenging inference problem.  The experimental validation on the HES dataset, a realistic and challenging benchmark, is a strong point.  The results showing a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error are impressive. The inclusion of comparisons to AFAMAP and discussion of computational complexity further strengthens the evaluation. The connection to posterior regularization is a nice theoretical addition, demonstrating the broader applicability of the proposed approach.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the CQP formulation is a key contribution, the explanation of the derivation could be more detailed. The jump from equation (5) to the introduction of *Hit* feels abrupt and would benefit from more elaboration.  Specifically, a clear explanation of *why* introducing *Hit* leads to a convex relaxation is necessary.  The notation is dense and could be made more readable, particularly in Section 5. While the authors mention that the relaxed problem is not guaranteed optimal, a deeper discussion of the impact of the relaxation on the solution quality would be beneficial. The exploration of different values for the tuning parameter *δi* (or equivalently *λi*) could be more extensive. A more systematic parameter tuning strategy, possibly using cross-validation, would strengthen the experimental results.  The analysis could benefit from examining specific examples of disaggregated appliance profiles to visually demonstrate the improvements achieved by incorporating SACs.  While the toy data provides a useful proof-of-concept, it’s relatively simple and doesn’t fully demonstrate the method’s capabilities. The writing, although generally clear, contains several instances of unnecessarily complex phrasing. For instance, “This is exactly the distribution in equation (5)” is redundant. The discussion around the choice of 3 states for the appliances seems somewhat arbitrary. A justification for this choice, or an exploration of the sensitivity of the results to the number of states, would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The introduction of SACs as a method for incorporating domain knowledge into BSS, and their application to AFHMMs, is a novel contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The CQP formulation provides a computationally tractable solution to a challenging inference problem.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The significant performance improvements on real-world energy data demonstrate the practical value of the proposed approach.
*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper effectively articulates the problem of identifiability in energy disaggregation and motivates the use of SACs.
*   **Connection to Established Frameworks:** Relating the work to posterior regularization provides a broader theoretical context.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Derivation Clarity:** The derivation of the CQP lacks sufficient detail and could be more clearly explained.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The parameter tuning strategy is limited, and a more systematic approach could yield further improvements.
*   **Relaxation Impact:** The impact of the relaxation on the solution quality is not fully discussed.
*   **Notation:** The dense notation in Section 5 hinders readability.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of energy disaggregation and BSS. The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and empirically effective. While the paper has some weaknesses related to clarity and parameter tuning, the strengths outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly improving the explanation of the CQP derivation and expanding the discussion of parameter sensitivity. Specifically, the authors should consider adding a detailed walkthrough of the CQP derivation in the appendix, and expanding the experimental section to show parameter sensitivity analysis.
