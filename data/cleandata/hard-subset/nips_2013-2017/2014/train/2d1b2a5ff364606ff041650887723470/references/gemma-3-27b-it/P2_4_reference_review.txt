## Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), specifically within the context of energy disaggregation. The authors identify the challenge of identifiability in BSS and propose incorporating “signal aggregate constraints” (SACs) – prior knowledge about the total energy consumption of individual appliances – into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM).  They formulate this as a convex quadratic program (CQP) for efficient inference and demonstrate significant performance improvements on real-world energy disaggregation data, achieving a 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over a state-of-the-art method.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature on energy disaggregation and BSS. It acknowledges related work using HMM constraints (e.g., AFAMAP, semi-Markov models) and broader approaches to incorporating prior knowledge (posterior regularization, generalized expectation criterion). The connection to posterior regularization, explicitly drawn in Section 6, is a nice addition, providing a theoretical grounding for the proposed constraints. The authors appropriately reference and contextualize their work against existing methods in both the energy disaggregation and machine learning domains.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a practical problem with a clear motivation:** Energy disaggregation is a relevant and challenging problem with real-world impact. The identifiability issue is well-explained and the motivation for incorporating domain knowledge is compelling.
* **Novelty of SACs:**  The explicit formulation and incorporation of signal aggregate constraints as a regularizer within the AFHMM framework appears novel. While prior knowledge is often used implicitly, this provides a structured and principled way to encode it.
* **Theoretical grounding:**  The derivation of the CQP for efficient inference is a significant contribution.  Transforming the optimization problem into a computationally tractable form is crucial for practical applicability.  The connection to posterior regularization strengthens the theoretical basis.
* **Strong empirical results:** The 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error is substantial and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  Comparing against a state-of-the-art method and showing improvement adds further weight to the findings.  The ablation study (using TTC vs NTC) is valuable.
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The equations are clearly presented, and the explanations are concise and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Sensitivity to constraint values:** While the toy experiment demonstrates robustness, the reliance on accurate prior knowledge (µi0) is a potential limitation. In real-world scenarios, obtaining precise values for total energy consumption can be difficult. The paper acknowledges this but a more in-depth discussion of methods to estimate these values from limited data would be beneficial.
* **Limited exploration of constraint generalization:** The authors mention the possibility of generalizing the constraints to multiple time periods (J constraints). While acknowledging this extension, they do not explore its impact empirically.  This is a potentially valuable direction for future work.
* **Computational complexity discussion is superficial:**  While the authors state the CQP has polynomial time complexity, a more detailed analysis of scaling with the number of states and chains would be helpful, particularly for large-scale problems. The statement that implementations “scale similarly” feels insufficient.
* **Limited comparison to other regularization techniques:** The paper focuses on SACs. A brief comparison to other common regularization techniques (e.g., L1/L2 regularization) within the AFHMM framework would provide a more complete picture of the benefits of the proposed method.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to a practical problem.
* The theoretical contributions (CQP formulation, connection to posterior regularization) are significant.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate a substantial improvement over existing methods.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The method relies on accurate prior knowledge, which may not always be available.
* The exploration of constraint generalization and computational complexity is limited.
* A more comprehensive comparison to other regularization techniques would strengthen the analysis.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses a relevant problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach. The empirical results are convincing, and the paper is well-written and organized. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept**.  I believe this paper is a valuable contribution to the field and is well-suited for presentation at NIPS.  I recommend the authors consider addressing the minor weaknesses in a future revision to further strengthen the work.
