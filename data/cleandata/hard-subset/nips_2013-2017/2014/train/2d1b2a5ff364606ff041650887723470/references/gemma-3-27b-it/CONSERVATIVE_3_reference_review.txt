This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework, aimed at improving blind source separation, specifically demonstrated in the context of energy disaggregation. The core idea – incorporating prior knowledge about expected total energy consumption for appliances – is intuitive and potentially valuable. The authors formulate the problem as a convex quadratic program, enabling efficient computation. While the application to energy disaggregation is well-motivated, and the performance gains reported are substantial (45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the base AFHMM, and 29% improvement over a recent state-of-the-art method), I have significant reservations about the novelty and overall rigor presented.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in constrained HMMs and probabilistic modeling. The introduction acknowledges this, but doesn’t sufficiently distinguish SACs from existing approaches like posterior regularization or the generalized expectation criterion, beyond stating they focus on a specific *type* of constraint. While the authors claim to address the question of “what types of domain knowledge we should include,” the SACs proposed feel rather straightforward and not particularly groundbreaking in their conception. The core contribution seems to be the efficient implementation of these constraints within the AFHMM, rather than the constraints themselves.

My primary concern lies with the validation. While the results on the HES dataset are promising, the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail regarding parameter tuning (beyond noting λi = 1) and sensitivity analysis. The comparison to AFAMAP is useful, but a more comprehensive benchmark against other established energy disaggregation techniques, beyond the single cited method, would be expected for a NIPS submission.  The presentation of results using both National Total Consumption (NTC) and True Total Consumption (TTC) is somewhat misleading; TTC represents an idealized scenario unattainable in practice. The performance boost using TTC, while demonstrating the potential of the approach, doesn’t translate directly to real-world applicability.

The theoretical justification is adequate but somewhat repetitive. The connection to posterior regularization, while correctly established, doesn’t add significant value to the paper. The derivation of the convex quadratic program is clearly presented, but the reliance on existing convex optimization packages (CVX) doesn't represent a substantial methodological innovation. The computational complexity analysis is brief but reasonable.

Clarity is generally good, although the mathematical notation could be streamlined in places. The organization is logical, and the paper is relatively easy to follow. However, the discussion surrounding identifiability feels superficial. The authors acknowledge the problem but don’t deeply engage with how SACs specifically address it, beyond hoping to guide the inference towards more interpretable solutions.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Significant performance improvements on a real-world dataset.
*   Efficient computational implementation via convex optimization.
*   Well-motivated application to a practical problem (energy disaggregation).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty of the proposed constraints.
*   Insufficiently rigorous experimental evaluation (limited baselines, lack of sensitivity analysis).
*   Overreliance on an idealized “oracle” constraint (TTC).
*   Superficial treatment of identifiability issues.
*   Doesn’t significantly advance the state-of-the-art in probabilistic modeling or constrained HMMs beyond a practical implementation.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the results are encouraging, the lack of substantial novelty and the insufficiently rigorous validation prevent me from recommending acceptance to NIPS. The work represents a solid engineering effort, but falls short of the originality and theoretical depth typically expected at this conference. I suggest the authors perform a more thorough comparison against established methods, conduct a detailed sensitivity analysis, and more clearly articulate the theoretical contributions of SACs beyond a straightforward application of constrained optimization.
