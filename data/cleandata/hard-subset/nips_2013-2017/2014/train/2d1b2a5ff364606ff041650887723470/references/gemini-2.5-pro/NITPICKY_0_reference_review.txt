This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a method for incorporating domain knowledge into Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs) for blind source separation tasks. The primary application and evaluation domain is energy disaggregation. The core idea is to penalize solutions where the total aggregate value of a source signal over a given time window deviates from a known prior value (e.g., the expected daily energy consumption of an appliance). The authors formulate this as a MAP inference problem with a non-Markovian regularizer. To make inference tractable, they relax the resulting integer quadratic program into a convex quadratic program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The paper demonstrates that this approach significantly reduces disaggregation error on a real-world energy dataset compared to a standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Intuitive Idea:** The central contribution, the SAC, is an elegant and intuitive way to inject powerful domain knowledge into the BSS problem. It directly tackles the identifiability issue (the "franken-appliance" problem) in a principled manner by constraining the global behavior of a source, which is often easier to specify a priori than local dynamics.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation shows substantial improvements over existing methods. On real-world data with mains readings, the reported 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to AFHMM and 29% improvement over AFAMAP are significant and demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed constraints.

3.  **Principled Formulation:** The authors provide a clear probabilistic interpretation of their objective function as a MAP problem with a prior over the aggregate statistics. The connection to the posterior regularization framework in Section 6 further solidifies the theoretical grounding of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

While the core idea is promising, the paper suffers from a number of methodological omissions, notational inconsistencies, and a lack of rigor in its presentation and evaluation that undermine its overall quality.

1.  **Critical Methodological Omission:** The central technical step involves relaxing the integer program (6) into a CQP where the state indicator variables `Sitk` lie in the continuous interval [0, 1]. However, the paper completely fails to mention how a final discrete sequence of states is recovered from this continuous solution. Is the result simply rounded? Is an argmax taken at each time step? This is a fundamental detail required for reproducibility and for understanding the properties of the final solution. Without this information, the method is not fully described.

2.  **Clarity and Notation:** The mathematical exposition in Section 5 is exceptionally dense and contains confusing notation. For instance, `eT` is defined as a `T x 1` vector (a column vector), which contradicts the conventional use of the transpose superscript `T` to denote a row vector. The proliferation of new variables (`µ∗i`, `Λi`, `µ̃i`, `π̃i`) without sufficient intermediate explanation makes the CQP objective function nearly inscrutable. Clarity is sacrificed for brevity.

3.  **Unjustified Assumptions and Simplifications:**
    *   The entire paper operates under the assumption that the HMM parameters (`θ`) are known and fixed. This is a significant simplification, as in most real-world scenarios these parameters must be learned from data. The paper frames itself as a solution for energy disaggregation but sidesteps the learning problem, which is a key part of most practical systems.
    *   The statement in the introduction that "the time at which the appliance is used is of less importance" is a broad oversimplification. While total consumption is important, temporal usage patterns are critical for many applications, such as demand-response programs.
    *   The model for `Yt` in Equation (1) assumes additive Gaussian noise, yet `Yt` is defined in `R+`. The model does not enforce this positivity constraint, which could be an issue if the signal is close to zero.

4.  **Insufficient Experimental Rigor:**
    *   The regularization hyperparameters `λi` are set to 1 for all experiments without any justification or sensitivity analysis. These parameters directly control the trade-off between the data likelihood and the aggregate constraint, and their choice is critical. A proper evaluation would involve a discussion of how these would be set in practice (e.g., cross-validation) and an analysis of how the model's performance varies with their values.
    *   The paper mentions a generalization to multiple constraints over different time windows but dismisses it "for presentational simplicity." While understandable, this makes the contribution feel less complete and avoids tackling the potentially interesting implications of using more fine-grained temporal priors.

5.  **Formatting and Minor Errors:**
    *   The caption for Figure 1 is incomplete, ending abruptly. This is a notable formatting error.
    *   The formatting of references is inconsistent. For example, [7] is just a URL with no other details, while [23] is missing standard publisher information.
    *   The complexity of the CQP is described vaguely as "polynomial time" without specifying the dependence on the number of time steps (T), sources (I), and states (K). While they report that it "scales similarly" to baselines, a formal analysis is missing.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is not technically sound in its current form due to the critical omission of how discrete states are recovered from the relaxed CQP solution. The experimental evaluation lacks rigor regarding hyperparameter selection.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is poorly written in key sections. The mathematical formulation of the CQP is difficult to follow due to dense and confusing notation.
*   **Originality:** The core idea of using signal aggregate constraints is novel and well-motivated. The connection to posterior regularization is also a nice contribution.
*   **Significance:** The problem is important, and the reported results are impressive. If the methodological and clarity issues were resolved, the work would be significant.

This paper presents a compelling and promising idea for improving BSS. However, the execution is flawed. The omission of a crucial step in the methodology makes the work non-reproducible, and the lack of rigor in the experimental setup casts some doubt on the practical deployment of the method. The paper is further marred by poor clarity in its technical sections and minor formatting errors. While the core contribution has merit, the number and severity of these flaws are too substantial for a NIPS paper. I recommend **rejection**, with the strong encouragement that the authors revise the manuscript to address these critical issues for a future submission.
