Summary
The paper addresses blind source separation in additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs): soft constraints that encourage each latent source’s total activity over a time horizon to match a specified target (e.g., per-appliance daily energy). The authors cast MAP inference with SACs as maximizing a concave quadratic objective subject to linear constraints by introducing auxiliary transition variables H, yielding a convex quadratic program (CQP) after relaxing integrality. They also show the objective arises from the posterior regularization framework. Experiments on a toy example and on the UK HES energy dataset demonstrate sizable improvements in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) over AFHMM and the AFAMAP baseline.

Relation to prior work
The approach is well-situated within: (i) NILM/energy disaggregation using factorial models (AFHMM, AFAMAP, semi-Markov variants), (ii) general sources of weak supervision/constraints such as posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.), generalized expectation (Mann & McCallum), and learning from measurements (Liang et al.), and (iii) aggregate constraints in probabilistic databases. The novelty is to identify aggregate (sum) constraints as a reusable prior in additive BSS and to derive a tractable convex relaxation for AFHMMs that incorporates them.

Quality
- Modeling and optimization: The relaxation is sound. With Λi = λi μ* μ*^T and Vt = σt^-2 μ μ^T both PSD, the quadratic terms enter with negative signs, so the objective is concave, and maximizing it over a polytope is a convex optimization problem. This is a nice, clean formulation. The posterior-regularization derivation is correct for fixed θ.
- However, two important algorithmic aspects are underspecified:
  - Rounding/interpretation: The relaxed solution yields fractional state assignments S and transitions H. The paper evaluates NDE/SAE on reconstructed signals, which is acceptable, but the initial motivation (interpretable appliance trajectories) is not fully addressed without a discrete rounding step. No procedure is given to recover a discrete trajectory or to measure integrality gaps.
  - Scalability: Solving general CQPs in CVX introduces O(T Σi Ki + T Σi Ki^2) variables; memory/time can grow quickly with T and number of chains. While the authors report similar wall-clock to AFHMM/AFAMAP on their tasks, a specialized solver or an analysis of scaling and sparsity exploitation would strengthen the claim of computational efficiency.
- Experimental design: Results on HES (ultra-low frequency) are valuable, and improvements over strong baselines (AFHMM/AFAMAP) are consistent, with significance tests. The “oracle” TTC setting is informative as an upper bound. That said:
  - Sensitivity to µ0 and λ: The toy example shows monotonic degradation with misspecified µ0, but no real-data sensitivity analysis is provided. In practice, per-appliance daily totals vary substantially across households/days; using national averages (NTC) may be badly mismatched. The paper fixes λ=1 across all experiments; adaptive tuning (CV or dual variables) or a robustness analysis is needed.
  - Parameter learning: θ is learned via k-means (fixed 3 states for all appliances). This is simplistic, and the consequences are not analyzed. Some baselines (e.g., semi-Markov, duration models) are mentioned but not compared; combining SAC with such constraints is not explored.
  - Mains-with-unmodeled-loads: For the mains scenario, unmodeled devices violate the generative assumptions. A slack chain or heavy-tailed noise could help; this is not discussed.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear, and the notation-heavy derivation is careful. Suggestions:
- Clearly state that maximizing a concave quadratic is a convex program; briefly justify PSD and concavity to avoid confusion.
- Provide a short algorithmic summary (inputs: θ, µ0, λ; outputs: fractional S; how to compute x̂ and, if desired, any rounding).
- Explain how σt^2 and θ are estimated and whether they are shared across methods.
- Minor typos/inconsistencies in notation (e.g., p(i)jk indexing) could be cleaned.

Originality
The core idea—aggregate-sum constraints as posterior regularization for AFHMMs—and the convex relaxation are novel combinations of known ingredients. Prior weak-supervision frameworks exist, but to my knowledge, applying aggregate constraints in AFHMMs via a tractable convex relaxation is new and relevant.

Significance
The constraints are broadly applicable to additive BSS beyond NILM (e.g., biology, audio) whenever plausible aggregate targets are available. Empirical gains on realistic NILM data are non-trivial. The approach is simple to adopt and combine with other priors. Its impact will grow if the authors address sensitivity to misspecification and provide scalable solvers.

Pros
- Simple, general idea that injects valuable domain knowledge.
- Solid optimization formulation; connects cleanly to posterior regularization.
- Consistent, significant empirical improvements on a challenging real dataset.
- Extensible to multiple time-window constraints and potentially combinable with other structural priors.

Cons
- Reliance on accurate aggregate targets; limited analysis of robustness and λ selection.
- No discrete rounding strategy despite interpretability motivation.
- Scalability concerns with general-purpose CVX; no specialized solver or complexity analysis beyond asymptotics.
- Limited comparisons (no semi-Markov/duration baselines with/without SAC; no ablations on θ estimation).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a principled strategy to set λ (e.g., CV, dual learning in PR, or hierarchical Bayesian treatment of µ0 with uncertainty).
- Provide a rounding/post-processing step (e.g., per-chain Viterbi with adjusted unaries from the convex solution; randomized rounding) and report integrality gap statistics.
- Present sensitivity analyses for µ0 mismatch and λ on real data; consider interval/soft bounds instead of point µ0.
- Explore combining SAC with AFAMAP’s one-change constraint or duration models.
- Discuss handling of unmodeled loads (slack chain/heavy-tailed noise).
- Include scaling experiments (vary T, I, Ki) and, if possible, a first-order or ADMM-based solver that exploits problem structure.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a compelling, broadly useful constraint class for AFHMMs with a solid convex formulation and promising empirical gains. Addressing robustness, rounding, and scalability would make it stronger.
