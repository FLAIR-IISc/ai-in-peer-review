## Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of blind source separation, specifically within the context of energy disaggregation. The authors identify a key issue – the identifiability of source signals – and propose a novel approach using “Signal Aggregate Constraints” (SACs) within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM). The core idea is to incorporate prior knowledge about the expected total energy consumption of appliances, guiding the inference process towards more interpretable results. They develop a convex quadratic program (CQP) to efficiently solve the resulting optimization problem and demonstrate significant performance improvements on real-world energy disaggregation data, achieving a 45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to the base AFHMM and a 29% improvement over a state-of-the-art method.

**Related Work:**

The paper appropriately situates itself within the growing field of energy disaggregation, referencing key works like Hart (1992) and recent survey papers. The discussion of related work on constraining AFHMMs (AFAMAP, non-homogeneous HMMs, semi-Markov models) is comprehensive.  The connection to posterior regularization and aggregate constraints in probabilistic databases is well-placed, highlighting the novelty of applying these concepts specifically within the AFHMM framework and efficiently solving the resulting optimization.  

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The introduction of SACs as a principled way to incorporate domain knowledge into AFHMMs is a significant contribution. It addresses a core challenge in BSS - identifiability - in a practical and potentially broadly applicable manner.
* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the CQP for the relaxed optimization problem appears mathematically sound and well-explained.  The use of CVX provides a robust and efficient implementation strategy.
* **Empirical Results:**  The reported performance gains on the HES dataset are compelling, particularly the 45% reduction in NDE. The comparison to AFAMAP, a strong baseline, further strengthens the validity of the results.  Demonstrating improvement on real data, *especially* the challenging low-frequency data from HES, is a significant strength.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results. The mathematical notation is consistent and manageable.
* **Connection to Posterior Regularization:** Establishing the link to posterior regularization provides a strong theoretical grounding for the proposed approach, demonstrating its connection to established Bayesian principles.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Sensitivity Analysis:** While the toy experiment demonstrates the impact of constraint error, a more thorough sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameter λ (controlling the strength of the SAC) on the real-world data would be valuable. How robust is the performance to different λ values?
* **Constraint Specification:**  The paper relies on knowing *a priori* the total energy consumption (µi0). While the authors acknowledge obtaining this from national surveys, the accuracy of these estimates can vary.  The impact of errors in µi0 on performance needs further investigation.  How does performance degrade with increasing error in these values?
* **Generalizability Beyond Energy Disaggregation:** While the authors claim the approach is general, the evaluation is entirely focused on energy disaggregation.  Exploring applications to other BSS problems (e.g., audio source separation) would strengthen the claim of broad applicability.  A discussion of the types of problems where SACs are *most* likely to be effective would be helpful.
* **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper mentions that the CQP scales polynomially and the implementations have similar timing, but a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity – perhaps in terms of the number of states or appliances – would be beneficial.
* **Relaxation Impact:** The paper acknowledges the relaxation introduces a potential suboptimality. While a full characterization of the optimality gap is likely intractable, a brief discussion of the potential impact of this relaxation on the results would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*  Addresses a critical challenge (identifiability) in BSS with a novel and theoretically grounded approach.
*  Demonstrates compelling empirical results on a challenging real-world dataset.
*  The CQP formulation allows for efficient computation.
*  The connection to posterior regularization strengthens the theoretical foundation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  The evaluation is limited to a single application domain.
*  The sensitivity to prior knowledge (µi0) and the relaxation’s impact could be more thoroughly investigated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation and energy disaggregation. The proposed SACs offer a practical and effective way to incorporate domain knowledge, leading to significant performance improvements. While some further investigation into the sensitivity and generalizability of the approach would be beneficial, the paper’s strengths outweigh its weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions addressing the points raised in the weaknesses section. Specifically, the authors should include a sensitivity analysis of the λ parameter and a discussion of the potential impact of errors in the prior knowledge (µi0) on the performance.
