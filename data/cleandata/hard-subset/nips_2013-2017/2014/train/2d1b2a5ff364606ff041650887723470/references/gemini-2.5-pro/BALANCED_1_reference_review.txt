### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS) from a single aggregated time series, focusing on the application of energy disaggregation. The authors identify the core challenge as the inherent unidentifiability of the sources, which hinders the interpretability of the separated signals. To address this, they propose incorporating domain knowledge through "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a standard model for this task. The key idea is that for many sources, such as home appliances, we often have prior knowledge about their expected total signal value over a given period (e.g., the average daily energy consumption of a refrigerator).

The authors formulate the maximum a posteriori (MAP) inference problem in this new model, AFHMM+SAC, as a penalized optimization problem. Since the SACs are non-Markovian (as they depend on the entire state sequence for each source), standard dynamic programming methods like the Viterbi algorithm are inapplicable. To overcome this, the authors relax the integer problem into a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP), following a similar approach to prior work like AFAMAP [12]. They also provide a theoretical justification for their objective function by linking it to the posterior regularization framework [4]. The proposed method is evaluated on both synthetic data and a real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES). The results demonstrate that AFHMM+SAC significantly outperforms both the standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP method in terms of disaggregation accuracy.

### Strengths

*   **Novelty and Significance of the Constraint:** The primary contribution—the introduction of signal aggregate constraints—is simple, highly intuitive, and effective. It represents a practical and powerful method for injecting domain knowledge to mitigate the critical identifiability problem in BSS. For applications like energy disaggregation, where such aggregate priors are often available from surveys or historical data, this is a very significant contribution that is likely to be adopted by other researchers and practitioners in the field.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation. The use of a toy dataset effectively illustrates the mechanism and robustness of the SACs. The main experiments on the real-world HES dataset are compelling, showing a substantial improvement over both a standard baseline (AFHMM) and a strong, recent competitor (AFAMAP). The reported 29% reduction in disaggregation error compared to AFAMAP on a challenging, realistic task is a strong result. The inclusion of an "oracle" experiment using the true total consumption (TTC) is a nice touch that clearly delineates the maximum potential benefit of this type of constraint.
*   **Technical Soundness and Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The mathematical formulation is sound. Transforming the non-Markovian MAP problem into a CQP is a valid and well-justified approach to find a tractable approximate solution. Furthermore, connecting the proposed penalized objective to the principled framework of posterior regularization adds theoretical depth to the work.

### Weaknesses

*   **Limited Novelty in the Inference Machinery:** While the proposed *constraint* is novel, the technical machinery for performing inference—relaxing the problem into a CQP—is heavily based on the approach used in AFAMAP [12]. The paper is transparent about this similarity, but it means the novelty lies more in the modeling aspect (the constraint itself) than in the inference algorithm.
*   **Lack of Hyperparameter Analysis:** The performance of the model depends on the regularization parameters `λi`, which balance the likelihood term against the aggregate constraints. The authors simply state that `λi = 1` was used for all experiments. This is a potential weakness, as the performance may be sensitive to this choice. A discussion of how one might set these parameters in a real-world scenario (e.g., via cross-validation) and an analysis of the model's sensitivity to `λi` would significantly strengthen the paper's practical utility.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The paper notes that the computational time is similar to baselines in the conducted experiments. However, CQP solvers can face scalability challenges with very long time series (large `T`) or a large number of sources and states. The complexity of the CQP grows with `T`, the number of appliances `I`, and the number of states `K`. A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and potential limitations for larger-scale problems would be beneficial.
*   **Generalizability Claim:** The paper claims that SACs are "general across a wide variety of domains," but this is not substantiated beyond the energy disaggregation application. While plausible, the paper would be more convincing if it briefly discussed other potential application areas (e.g., separating audio streams based on expected total speaking time for each person) to support this claim.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** I strongly recommend adding a brief experiment or a paragraph discussing the model's sensitivity to the `λi` parameters. For instance, on the toy data, showing a plot of the NDE/SAE as a function of `λ` (assuming `λi` are equal) would be very informative for a practitioner looking to apply this method.
2.  **Generalization of SACs:** In Section 4.2, the paper introduces a more general form of SACs over arbitrary time windows but does not explore it further. While this is acceptable for a conference paper, it would be a valuable addition to briefly comment on the potential of this extension in the conclusion or future work section. This could be used, for example, to enforce that certain appliances (like an oven) are used more during the day than at night, making the model even more powerful.
3.  **Computational Complexity:** To address the scalability point, it would be helpful to explicitly state the number of variables and constraints in the CQP as a function of `T`, `I`, and the number of states `Ki`. This would give readers a clearer picture of how the problem size scales.
4.  **Relation to AFAMAP:** In the CQP formulation, it might be beneficial to more explicitly highlight the terms that are unique to this work (i.e., the `Λi` and `μ̃i` terms) versus the parts that are common with the CQP formulation for the standard AFHMM or AFAMAP. This would further clarify the paper's technical contribution.

### Recommendation: Accept

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a valuable and practical contribution to the field of blind source separation and its applications. The core idea of using signal aggregate constraints is novel, well-motivated, and shown to be highly effective through strong empirical results on a challenging real-world problem. While the inference technique itself builds heavily on prior work and the analysis could be strengthened with a discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity, the strengths of the paper—particularly its significance and clear, positive results—outweigh these minor weaknesses. The work advances the state of the art in energy disaggregation and introduces a constraint type that could inspire further research in related BSS problems.
