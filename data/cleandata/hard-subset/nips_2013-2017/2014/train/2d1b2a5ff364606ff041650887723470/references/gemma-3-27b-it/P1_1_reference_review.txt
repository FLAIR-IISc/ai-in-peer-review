## Peer Review: Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of blind source separation, specifically within the context of energy disaggregation. The introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) to an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) is a well-motivated and technically sound approach to address the identifiability issues inherent in this problem. The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements on a real-world dataset, and the theoretical justification and algorithmic details are clearly presented. While the work builds upon existing methods (AFHMM, posterior regularization), the specific application of SACs and the resulting convex program formulation are novel and impactful. I recommend **acceptance** of this paper.

**1. Summary of Main Ideas & Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper tackles the challenging problem of blind source separation, motivated by applications like energy disaggregation where the goal is to decompose a total signal into its constituent sources (e.g., appliance energy consumption). The inherent underdetermined nature of the problem leads to identifiability issues, making source interpretation difficult.  The authors propose incorporating domain knowledge via Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) – enforcing expectations on the total energy consumption of each source over a given period.  They integrate these constraints into an AFHMM framework, a common probabilistic model for BSS.  The resulting optimization problem is addressed by a relaxation technique leading to a computationally efficient convex quadratic program.

This work builds upon existing research in several areas. The authors rightly position their contribution within the broader context of BSS and energy disaggregation (citing foundational works like Hart’s initial proposal and reviews by Ziefman and others).  They acknowledge and differentiate their approach from existing methods for improving AFHMMs, such as AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012) and semi-Markov models (Johnson & Willsky, 2009), which primarily focus on constraining the *state transitions* within the HMMs. The SACs, instead, directly constrain the *aggregate signal values*.  The paper also acknowledges and relates to general frameworks for incorporating prior knowledge like posterior regularization (Ganchev et al., 2009) and measurement-based learning (Liang et al., 2011), demonstrating awareness of the wider landscape of probabilistic modeling techniques.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Insight:** The core idea of SACs is a practical and intuitive way to inject domain knowledge into the BSS problem, addressing a critical limitation of traditional methods.  The recognition that aggregate signal values are often well-defined (e.g., a toaster will likely use a specific amount of energy per day) is insightful.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation of the SACs, the derivation of the relaxed optimization problem, and the resulting convex quadratic program are all presented with clarity and rigor. The use of CVX for solving the CQP is a practical choice.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on the HES dataset are compelling.  The 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the standard AFHMM and the 29% improvement over AFAMAP demonstrate a significant performance gain.  The results using both national total consumption and true total consumption are well presented and analyzed.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized. The authors clearly explain the problem, their proposed solution, and the experimental setup.  The notation is consistent and the mathematical derivations are easy to follow.
* **Connection to Posterior Regularization:** The link between SACs and posterior regularization provides a deeper theoretical understanding of the approach and reinforces its grounding in probabilistic modeling principles.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors explicitly address computational complexity and demonstrate that their method scales similarly to existing approaches like AFAMAP.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Sensitivity to Constraint Values:** While the toy data experiment demonstrates robustness to errors in specifying the constraint value, the real-world results rely on accurate estimation of the aggregate values (µi0).  The paper briefly discusses obtaining these values from expert knowledge or surveys, but a more detailed discussion of practical methods for estimating these values in real-world scenarios would be beneficial.
* **Limited Exploration of Constraint Generalizations:** The authors acknowledge the possibility of using multiple constraints over different time periods (Section 4.2), but this extension is not fully explored experimentally. Evaluating the impact of using such constraints could lead to further performance improvements.
* **Assumptions about State Count:** The choice of 3 states per appliance seems somewhat arbitrary and could potentially impact performance.  A more thorough investigation of the influence of the number of states on the results would be valuable.
* **Lack of Comparison to More Recent Methods:** While the comparison to AFAMAP is relevant, the field of energy disaggregation has advanced since its publication. Including a comparison to more recent state-of-the-art methods (if applicable to the same dataset and constraints) would strengthen the evaluation.



**4. Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Significant improvement in disaggregation accuracy on a real-world dataset.
* Novel and well-motivated approach to addressing identifiability issues.
* Technically sound and clearly presented.
* Strong connection to existing theoretical frameworks (posterior regularization).
* Demonstrates practical computational feasibility.

**Against:**

* Sensitivity to the accuracy of aggregate value estimates.
* Limited exploration of more complex constraint structures.
* Could benefit from comparison to more recent state-of-the-art methods.



**5. Final Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend **accepting** this paper. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field of energy disaggregation by introducing a novel and effective approach to incorporating domain knowledge into the BSS process. While there are minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper far outweigh them. The work is technically sound, well-written, and demonstrates clear practical benefits. The ideas presented are likely to be influential and will stimulate further research in this important area.
