

# Review: Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation

This paper introduces a novel concept—"Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs)—for addressing the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS) applications, particularly in energy disaggregation. The authors observe that in many BSS scenarios, we have prior knowledge about the aggregate values of source signals (e.g., expected daily energy consumption of appliances), and they propose incorporating these as constraints into Additive Factorial HMMs (AFHMMs). This is a significant departure from prior work on posterior regularization, which has not addressed the specific question of "what types of domain knowledge we should include."

The paper's core contribution is the SAC framework, which transforms the BSS problem with aggregate constraints into a convex quadratic program. The authors demonstrate that this approach significantly improves energy disaggregation performance (45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error compared to baseline AFHMM, and 29% improvement over state-of-the-art AFAMAP). The theoretical grounding in posterior regularization (Section 6) provides a strong connection to established methods while offering a new direction for constraint design.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Concept:** SACs represent a genuinely new approach to incorporating domain knowledge in BSS problems. While prior work has focused on temporal constraints (e.g., AFAMAP's "at most one chain changes state at a time"), SACs address a different dimension of domain knowledge—aggregate signal properties. This is a bold, conceptually distinct contribution that addresses a fundamental gap in the literature.

2. **Significant Impact:** The empirical results are impressive, with consistent improvements across multiple datasets. The 45% error reduction is not incremental but represents a substantial advancement in a well-studied problem. The paper demonstrates that SACs are not just theoretically sound but practically impactful.

3. **General Applicability:** While the paper focuses on energy disaggregation, the SAC concept is general enough to apply to other BSS problems where aggregate constraints are meaningful (e.g., audio source separation with expected total volume per source). The authors acknowledge this in Section 4.2, noting that SACs could be applied across domains.

4. **Theoretical Clarity:** The derivation of the convex quadratic program (Section 5) is well-executed, and the connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) provides a strong theoretical foundation. The paper clearly articulates the problem's difficulty (non-Markovian constraints) and the solution's elegance.

**Weaknesses (Minor):**

1. The paper doesn't fully explore how SACs interact with other constraint types (e.g., AFAMAP's temporal constraints). While the authors note SACs could be combined with other constraints, they don't provide empirical evidence of such combinations.

2. The hyperparameter selection (λi) is simplistic (fixed to 1 for all experiments), though the authors acknowledge this could be improved with cross-validation. This is a minor methodological limitation given the overall significance of the contribution.

3. The toy data experiment (Section 7.1) is somewhat basic, but the energy disaggregation results (Sections 7.2.1 and 7.2.2) are robust and comprehensive.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of unsupervised learning and BSS. The SAC concept is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates substantial empirical gains. While the methodological details are sound, the true value lies in the conceptual breakthrough—identifying and formalizing a new class of constraints that address a fundamental limitation in BSS applications. This is the kind of bold, novel idea that NIPS thrives on.

I am particularly impressed by how the authors identify a gap in prior work ("all of these approaches leave open the question of what types of domain knowledge we should include") and directly address it with a new, generalizable concept. This isn't an incremental improvement but a new direction for the field.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria:
- **Quality:** Strong theoretical foundation, well-executed experiments, honest about limitations.
- **Clarity:** Well-structured, clear exposition of the novel concept.
- **Originality:** SACs represent a genuinely new contribution to the literature.
- **Significance:** The 45% error reduction in a real-world application is highly significant and demonstrates clear value.

While the paper has minor methodological limitations, they are completely overshadowed by the novelty and impact of the SAC concept. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS, providing a new direction for researchers working on BSS problems across multiple domains. The authors have successfully identified a meaningful gap in the literature and provided a compelling solution.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Introduces a genuinely novel constraint class (SACs) addressing a fundamental gap in BSS
- Demonstrates substantial empirical improvements (45% error reduction)
- Theoretical grounding in posterior regularization provides strong foundation
- General applicability beyond energy disaggregation
- Clear distinction from prior work (AFAMAP, posterior regularization)

**Con-acceptance arguments (minor):**
- Hyperparameter selection could be more sophisticated
- Limited exploration of combining SACs with other constraint types
- Toy experiment is somewhat basic (though not critical to the main contribution)

Given the high novelty and significant empirical impact, I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper represents the kind of bold, conceptually rich contribution that advances the field rather than incrementally improving existing methods.
