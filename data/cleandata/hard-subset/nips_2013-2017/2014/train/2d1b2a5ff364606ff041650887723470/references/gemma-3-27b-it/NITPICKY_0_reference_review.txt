This paper proposes Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM) framework, aiming to improve blind source separation, specifically in the context of energy disaggregation. The core idea – incorporating prior knowledge about expected total energy consumption for individual appliances – is intuitively appealing and addresses the inherent identifiability issues in BSS. The authors formulate the problem as a convex quadratic program (CQP) for efficient inference.

While the problem tackled is relevant, and the proposed approach offers a potentially valuable addition to the toolkit for energy disaggregation, the paper suffers from a concerning number of shortcomings, ranging from superficial theoretical justification to meticulous presentation issues.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** Energy disaggregation is a practically important problem, and improving its accuracy is highly valuable.
*   **Intuitive Approach:** SACs represent a sensible way to incorporate domain knowledge and address identifiability.
*   **CQP Formulation:** Converting the problem into a CQP allows for tractable computation, a crucial aspect for real-world applications.
*   **Empirical Results:** The reported 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over AFAMAP are encouraging.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Justification:** The connection to posterior regularization, while mentioned, feels tacked on and doesn’t fundamentally deepen understanding. The derivation lacks thoroughness. The claim that the constraint terms can be viewed as a logarithm of prior distributions is stated without detailed justification.
*   **Relaxation Concerns:** The relaxation to a CQP is presented as straightforward, but the impact of this relaxation on the optimality of the solution isn’t sufficiently discussed. The authors acknowledge non-optimality due to identifiability, but don’t quantify the expected error introduced by the relaxation itself.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of λ<sub>i</sub> (or δ<sub>i</sub>) is crucial, yet the paper only mentions setting λ<sub>i</sub> = 1. A sensitivity analysis demonstrating the impact of different values and a proper cross-validation strategy are missing.
*   **Implementation Details:** The description of the CQP solving process is brief. Specifying the solver used and computational resources would enhance reproducibility.
*   **Presentation & Notation:** The paper is littered with minor inconsistencies and notational ambiguities. The definition of µ∗i and the use of the Kronecker product are unnecessarily convoluted and make the equation difficult to follow. Similarly, the introduction of numerous notations (e.g., Hit.l, Hitl.) without clear explanation is confusing. The jump between equations (3) and (4) lacks smooth transition and justification.
*   **Redundancy and verbosity**: The conclusion repeats the contributions list, and some sections have excessive wording.
*   **Related work omissions**: While citing key papers, the authors omit recent advances in sparse decomposition and deep learning methods for energy disaggregation, which provide alternative and competitive approaches.
* **Figure 1**: The figure lacks proper axis labels, making it difficult to interpret the results.

**Specific Points Requiring Attention:**

*   Equation (5): The optimization problem includes a regularization term but does not mention how the parameters λ<sub>i</sub> are chosen.
*   Section 5: The explanation of how the problem is transformed into a CQP is overly concise. More details are required.
*   Table 1 & 3: The standard deviation of the results are missing.
*   "Note that the constraints in equation (4) could be generalised..." – This extended constraint discussion feels tacked on and disrupts the flow.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

*   **For:** The idea is sound, and the experimental results demonstrate a significant improvement over baseline methods. The CQP formulation is a practical contribution.
*   **Against:** The theoretical justification is weak, the potential impact of the relaxation isn't adequately addressed, and the paper suffers from numerous presentation flaws. The lack of a thorough hyperparameter study and omission of relevant related work are concerning.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially valuable contribution, the paper, in its current form, falls short of NIPS standards. The multitude of minor (yet significant) flaws and the lack of rigor in the theoretical analysis outweigh the positive empirical results. While the core idea holds promise, the paper requires substantial revision to address the identified weaknesses. I recommend **rejection** at this time, but encourage the authors to resubmit after thoroughly addressing the issues raised in this review. The paper needs more careful attention to detail, a more rigorous theoretical treatment, and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation.
