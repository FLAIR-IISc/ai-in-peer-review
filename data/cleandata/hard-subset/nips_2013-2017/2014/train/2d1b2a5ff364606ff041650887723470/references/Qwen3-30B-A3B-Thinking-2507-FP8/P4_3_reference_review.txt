

# Review

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs) to address identifiability issues in blind source separation (BSS), particularly for energy disaggregation. The authors observe that in many BSS applications, the total signal across time for each source is known or can be estimated (e.g., expected daily energy consumption for appliances), and propose incorporating these aggregate values as constraints into the AFHMM framework. The paper shows that SACs transform the inference problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP) that can be efficiently solved, and demonstrates significant performance improvements on real-world energy disaggregation data.

The paper builds on previous work in energy disaggregation (e.g., [8, 12, 15, 22]) and BSS methods, while positioning SACs as a novel constraint type that generalizes beyond specific domain knowledge. It connects SACs to the posterior regularization framework [4], providing theoretical grounding for the approach. The authors compare their method against AFHMM and AFAMAP [12], which are state-of-the-art for energy disaggregation, and demonstrate improvements using both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-structured mathematical formulation of SACs and their incorporation into AFHMMs. The derivation of the convex quadratic program from the constrained optimization problem is technically sound, and the paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors appropriately validate results with statistical tests (paired t-tests) and compare against relevant baselines.

2. **Significant Results**: The experimental evaluation demonstrates substantial improvements (45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to AFHMM, 29% improvement over AFAMAP [12]) on real-world energy data. The paper's use of both synthetic and real-world data provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of SACs.

3. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical progression from problem statement to mathematical formulation to experimental evaluation. The technical content is presented accessibly, with sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the work.

4. **Theoretical Connection**: The paper effectively connects SACs to the posterior regularization framework [4], providing theoretical justification for the approach and positioning it within a broader machine learning context.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Constraint Selection**: The paper does not thoroughly address how to select appropriate constraint values (μi0) in practical settings. While it uses national averages as a proxy for μi0, it doesn't provide guidance on how to obtain or estimate these values when they're not available from national surveys. This is a significant practical limitation for real-world deployment.

2. **Insufficient Theoretical Justification**: While the paper demonstrates empirically that SACs improve performance, it lacks a deeper theoretical analysis explaining *why* SACs are effective. A more detailed discussion of the relationship between SACs and the identifiability problem would strengthen the paper.

3. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address potential limitations of SACs, such as performance degradation when constraint values are poorly specified. The paper notes that SACs are robust to errors in constraint specification (Section 7.1), but doesn't provide a comprehensive analysis of this robustness.

4. **Brief Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the paper states that the CQP approach scales similarly to other methods, it provides only minimal discussion of computational complexity, which would be valuable for practitioners considering implementation.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper makes a technically sound contribution by introducing Signal Aggregate Constraints as a novel method for addressing identifiability in BSS problems. The approach is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and demonstrates significant empirical improvements over state-of-the-art methods in energy disaggregation. The paper is clearly written and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The main limitation is the lack of practical guidance for selecting constraint values (μi0) in real-world applications, which is a significant barrier to adoption. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more thorough theoretical analysis of why SACs improve performance and a more comprehensive discussion of the approach's limitations.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Introduces a novel constraint type (SACs) that significantly improves performance in a challenging real-world problem
- Provides strong empirical evidence through both synthetic and real-world experiments
- Well-motivated and well-structured technical contribution
- Connects to broader machine learning frameworks (posterior regularization)

*Con:*
- Limited practical guidance for selecting constraint values (μi0)
- Insufficient theoretical justification for why SACs improve performance
- Inadequate discussion of limitations and failure cases
- Brief computational complexity analysis

The paper addresses a significant problem in energy disaggregation with a technically sound approach that demonstrates clear improvements over existing methods. While the practical guidance for constraint selection could be improved, the core contribution is novel, well-executed, and has meaningful impact. The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, clarity, and significance, with only minor weaknesses that do not undermine the overall contribution. I recommend acceptance.
