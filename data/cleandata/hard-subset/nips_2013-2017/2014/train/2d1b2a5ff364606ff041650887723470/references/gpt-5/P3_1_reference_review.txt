Summary
The paper tackles identifiability in blind source separation with additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) by introducing signal aggregate constraints (SACs): quadratic penalties that encourage each chain’s total energy over time to match a target µi0 (e.g., prior knowledge of daily appliance consumption). The MAP objective becomes the AFHMM log posterior augmented with these penalties. Because the penalty couples all time steps for each chain, standard dynamic programming is not applicable. The authors introduce transition variables Hit (local edge marginals), relax integrality of state and transition indicators, and show the resulting objective is a concave quadratic function over a polytope, i.e., a convex quadratic program (QP) solvable with CVX. They also show the objective follows from the posterior regularization (PR) framework. Experiments on synthetic data and the HES low-frequency energy dataset show improvements in normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) over AFHMM and AFAMAP, with similar runtimes on these datasets.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-situated among NILM methods that constrain FACTORIAL HMMs (AFAMAP’s “one chain changes state at a time,” non-homogeneous HMMs, and HSMMs with durations), and connects to general frameworks for priors and constraints (posterior regularization, generalized expectation, measurement-based learning). The proposed constraints differ by operating on global aggregates per chain (total energy), which, to my knowledge, have not been integrated into AFHMM inference for NILM. The link to PR is correct and helpful. Additional relevant lines of work to mention: learning from label proportions/aggregate supervision and constrained conditional models (e.g., Chang et al., ACL 2008), which are conceptually close to “bag-level totals.”

Technical quality
- Formulation and convexity: The reparameterization with local pairwise variables Hit and relaxation yields a concave quadratic objective (negative semidefinite quadratic forms from both the likelihood and SAC) over a linear feasible set; calling this a convex QP (maximization of a concave function) is appropriate. The PSD properties of Vt and Λi are correct.
- Soundness: The approach is a standard local-polytope relaxation of the MAP problem, now with extra quadratic penalties. However, the paper does not discuss integrality gaps or rounding. Since evaluation uses NDE/SAE (which can be computed from fractional states), this is acceptable, but if interpretability of state sequences is a goal, some rounding or decoding strategy should be described and evaluated.
- Parameter estimation: θ is assumed known. In practice, µi are estimated by k-means on per-appliance training data, and transitions from data, but the paper does not quantify sensitivity to these choices. λi are fixed to 1 without tuning; a limited robustness analysis is provided on toy data by varying µ0 but not λ. The PR dual view suggests a principled way to learn λ via dual optimization; this is not explored.
- Complexity and scalability: The QP has O(T∑i Ki + T∑i Ki^2) variables. Empirically, runtimes are “similar” to AFAMAP on low-frequency, single-day windows. Nonetheless, reliance on CVX/generic solvers raises questions about scaling to higher sampling rates or longer horizons; there is likely structure (block-sparse, nearly banded) that could be exploited by custom solvers or first-order methods (e.g., ADMM).
- Experimental validation: Results indicate consistent gains over AFHMM and AFAMAP on HES data with and without mains. The “oracle” SAC (true totals) shows a clear upper bound. The “national total consumption” prior is a realistic though coarse prior. Missing details: per-appliance breakdowns, variability across households, ablations on mis-specified µ0 and varying λ, and comparisons to duration models (HSMM) would strengthen claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a thorough derivation. A few suggestions:
- Clarify notation for vectors 1T vs eT earlier; current reuse of T as a superscript and time horizon is slightly confusing.
- Be explicit that you maximize a concave quadratic (hence a convex optimization problem). Avoid the ambiguous “CQP” phrasing without that qualifier.
- Describe how fractional solutions are used in practice (e.g., x̂it = µTi Sit); if any rounding is used, specify and assess impact.
- Provide more implementation details (solver, tolerances, warm-starting, convergence criteria) for reproducibility.

Originality
Introducing global per-chain aggregate constraints into AFHMMs and solving the relaxed MAP as a convex QP is a neat and, to my knowledge, novel combination in the NILM context. The PR connection is known but appropriate. The idea of using aggregate priors is broadly useful and generalizes to other BSS settings. The technical novelty is moderate (standard relaxation and QP), but the application-motivated constraint class and its practical benefits are the main contributions.

Significance
NILM is a challenging, impactful application; improving identifiability by leveraging realistic priors on appliance totals is valuable. The demonstrated improvements on a difficult, low-frequency dataset are practically significant. The approach is generic and could be reused in other factorial models with aggregate supervision. The reliance on aggregate priors means availability/quality of µ0 determines gains; still, national or building-type priors are often available.

Pros
- Simple, principled way to incorporate domain knowledge (totals) that directly aligns with end-user metrics (energy per appliance).
- Clean convex formulation with a clear PR interpretation.
- Consistent empirical gains over a strong baseline (AFAMAP) on realistic data.
- Extension to multiple time-window constraints is natural.

Cons and questions
- No discussion of integrality gap or rounding; interpretability of per-appliance on/off trajectories may be limited by fractional solutions.
- Sensitivity to λ and mis-specified µ0 is only lightly explored; can the method degrade badly when µ0 is wrong or when mains includes significant unknown loads?
- Comparisons lack duration-aware baselines (HSMMs) and other constrained inference approaches; would SACs complement or outperform duration constraints?
- Scalability beyond the tested regime is unclear with CVX; can the method handle week-long traces at 1-minute resolution?
- How are µ0 values obtained in the “with mains” setting when significant unmodeled load exists? Are priors renormalized to account for unknown/other?
- Minor: references have missing years and some typos.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments varying λ and deliberately mis-specifying µ0 to quantify robustness.
- Include a per-appliance breakdown of improvements and error bars; report variance across houses.
- Compare against HSMM-based methods or combine SAC with duration constraints.
- Provide a simple rounding heuristic and report its effect on NDE/SAE and interpretability.
- Discuss or provide an efficient specialized solver (e.g., projected gradient/ADMM exploiting structure), and report scaling with T.

Overall assessment
A solid, well-motivated paper with a practical and general idea that yields measurable gains on a challenging NILM dataset. While technically incremental in optimization, the contribution is novel in this context and likely of interest to NIPS attendees working on structured prediction with weak/aggregate supervision and energy analytics. I recommend acceptance after addressing clarity and experimental breadth concerns.
