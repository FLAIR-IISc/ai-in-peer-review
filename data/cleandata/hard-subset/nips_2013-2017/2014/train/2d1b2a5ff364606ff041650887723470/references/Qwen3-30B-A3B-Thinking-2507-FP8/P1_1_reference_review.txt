

# Peer Review: "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in blind source separation (BSS) - identifiability - by introducing Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs). The authors observe that in many BSS applications (particularly energy disaggregation), the total energy consumption of individual appliances is known or can be estimated from domain knowledge. SACs enforce that the aggregate values of source signals match these prior expectations. The paper formulates SACs as constraints, transforms the problem into a convex quadratic program, and demonstrates significant performance improvements (45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error) over baseline AFHMM and a state-of-the-art AFAMAP approach on real energy disaggregation data.

The work connects to prior research on incorporating domain knowledge into probabilistic models (posterior regularization, generalized expectation criterion) while making a concrete contribution to the energy disaggregation problem domain. The paper builds upon the AFHMM framework, which has been used for energy disaggregation since the seminal work of Hart [8].

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a theoretically sound approach to a well-known problem. The derivation of SACs from posterior regularization (Section 6) provides a strong theoretical foundation. The convex quadratic program formulation (Section 5) is well-motivated and the optimization problem is correctly transformed. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using both synthetic data (Section 7.1) and real-world energy data (Section 7.2) with appropriate metrics (NDE and SAE). The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the need for accurate prior knowledge about appliance consumption.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement, motivation, and mathematical formulation are presented logically. The connection between SACs and the broader framework of posterior regularization is particularly well-explained. The experimental setup and results are described with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Originality:** SACs represent a novel approach to addressing the identifiability problem in BSS. While prior work has constrained state transitions (AFAMAP [12]) or used duration constraints (semi-Markov models [11]), SACs directly target the aggregate values of source signals - a different and complementary approach. The paper clearly distinguishes SACs from related work (Section 2).

**Significance:** The 45% improvement over AFHMM and 29% over AFAMAP on real energy disaggregation data represents a meaningful advance in the state of the art. Energy disaggregation has significant practical implications for energy conservation and smart grid applications. The approach is generalizable to other BSS problems beyond energy disaggregation, as the authors note.

## Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper could better address the practical challenges of implementing SACs. Specifically, it's unclear how to obtain accurate aggregate values (µi0) for appliances when domain knowledge isn't available. While the authors use "national total consumption" as a proxy, they don't discuss how this might be estimated when such data isn't available or is inaccurate. The sensitivity analysis of the regularization parameter λi is limited to a single value (λi=1), without exploring how performance varies with different λi values.

**Originality:** The paper could more clearly articulate how SACs differ from other constraint approaches. For example, the "k-segment constraints" mentioned in Section 2 might have some conceptual overlap with SACs, but the paper doesn't thoroughly compare them. A more detailed discussion of how SACs complement rather than replace existing constraint approaches would strengthen the originality claim.

**Significance:** The evaluation is primarily focused on energy disaggregation, which, while important, limits the perceived generalizability of the approach. The paper would benefit from at least one brief demonstration of SACs on a different BSS application to better establish broader significance.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the energy disaggregation literature and the broader field of blind source separation. The SACs represent a novel and practical approach to addressing the identifiability problem, with compelling empirical results. The theoretical foundation is solid, the writing is clear, and the experiments are well-designed.

The main weaknesses relate to the practical implementation details of SACs and the limited demonstration of generalizability beyond energy disaggregation. However, these do not undermine the core contribution, which is both theoretically sound and empirically validated.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
1. Novel and practical solution to a well-known problem (identifiability in BSS)
2. Strong theoretical foundation (connection to posterior regularization)
3. Significant empirical improvements (45% reduction in error over baseline)
4. Well-written and well-organized paper
5. Clear connection to real-world application with practical impact

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
1. Limited exploration of parameter sensitivity (λi) and practical estimation of µi0
2. Focus primarily on energy disaggregation without broader application demonstration
3. Some overlap with related constraint approaches (k-segment constraints) could be more thoroughly addressed

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to the energy disaggregation literature and the broader field of blind source separation. The SACs are a novel and practical approach to addressing the identifiability problem, with compelling empirical results that significantly outperform previous methods. The paper is well-written, theoretically sound, and addresses an important problem with meaningful practical implications. While the focus on energy disaggregation limits the perceived generalizability, the approach is clearly applicable to other BSS domains. The minor weaknesses in parameter sensitivity analysis and broader application demonstration do not detract from the core contribution.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a valuable new tool for addressing a fundamental challenge in blind source separation.
