Summary
This paper proposes Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation under an additive factorial HMM (AFHMM). The key idea is to exploit prior knowledge about aggregate quantities (e.g., total daily energy for each appliance) and encode them as soft quadratic penalties on the aggregate of each latent chain. The authors show how to convert MAP inference with these non-Markovian penalties into a convex quadratic program (CQP) by introducing transition-indicator variables, resulting in a tractable relaxation. They further connect the objective to posterior regularization, clarifying the conceptual grounding. Experiments on toy data and a large, realistic energy disaggregation dataset (HES) demonstrate substantial improvements over AFHMM and the AFAMAP method of Kolter & Jaakkola (2012), with reported reductions in normalized disaggregation error and signal-aggregate error, while maintaining similar runtime.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on AFHMMs for disaggregation, the AFAMAP constraint (single chain change per time step), semi-Markov approaches for duration modeling, and frameworks like posterior regularization, generalized expectation, and measurement-based learning. The specific novelty here is the definition and integration of global aggregate constraints over entire chains (and potentially time windows), solved via a convex relaxation. Prior database work on aggregate constraints covers independent variables, not coupled Markovian models. The connection to posterior regularization is appropriate, and the paper positions itself well against AFAMAP and semi-Markov approaches.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: SACs are simple, broadly applicable, and encode a type of prior that practitioners often have (totals) but that is rarely leveraged in BSS/HMM settings.
- Technical contribution: A clean CQP relaxation using additional variables for transitions, leveraging PSD structure for convexity; the posterior-regularization derivation strengthens the framing.
- Empirical impact: Consistent and sizable gains on realistic low-frequency energy data, including mains-only scenarios, with practical runtime.
- Generality: The constraints naturally extend to multiple time windows, offering a path to incorporate diurnal patterns or usage priors.
- Robustness: Toy experiments illustrate sensitivity to misspecified aggregates; improvements persist with imperfect “national total consumption” priors.

Weaknesses and questions
- Dependence on aggregate priors: Performance hinges on having reasonable µi0. The paper shows NTC works, but it remains unclear how robust this is under significant distribution shift across households/days. A sensitivity analysis on real data would strengthen claims (the toy results are indicative but limited).
- Relaxation and decoding: The relaxed solution yields fractional Sit. How are discrete sequences recovered (if at all), and do fractional states undermine interpretability? A rounding or projection scheme and its effect on performance should be described.
- Scalability and solver details: The approach relies on generic CVX. While runtimes are reported as “similar,” more detail on problem sizes, solver settings, and asymptotics would help assess scalability to longer sequences and more appliances. Specialized QP solvers or problem structure (block-diagonal, banded) could be exploited.
- Hyperparameters: λi are fixed to 1 in all experiments. This is convenient but begs the question of tuning strategy and sensitivity. Showing a modest sweep or automatic calibration (e.g., via dual variables, cross-validation, or Bayesian treatment) would increase confidence.
- Comparisons: The method is compared to AFHMM and AFAMAP. Given the paper’s aim to address identifiability, additional baselines leveraging duration (semi-Markov) or other global constraints would contextualize the gains. Even if not exhaustive, a brief ablation versus simple priors (e.g., L2 penalties toward per-appliance means without temporal structure) would clarify where SACs help most.
- Minor clarity: Some notation is dense; a short algorithmic summary (inputs, outputs, key steps) would help. A more explicit statement of convexity (signs and PSD arguments) would aid readers.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound overall, with an appropriate convex relaxation and solid empirical results. Some missing details (decoding from the relaxation, hyperparameter tuning, solver specifics) limit reproducibility but are addressable.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized, though notation is heavy in Section 5. The posterior regularization connection is a plus.
- Originality: The SAC idea—global aggregate constraints over latent chains in AFHMMs—and the CQP formulation are novel and nontrivial. The work distinguishes itself from AFAMAP (local change constraints) and duration models.
- Significance: Likely to be useful beyond energy disaggregation (audio, biology), as aggregates are a common form of domain knowledge. The demonstrated improvements on realistic data suggest practical impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a simple, broadly applicable new constraint class that addresses identifiability in BSS.
- Provides a principled and efficient convex formulation with a clear connection to posterior regularization.
- Demonstrates strong empirical gains on a challenging, realistic dataset with practical runtimes.
- Opens a path to incorporate multiple-window aggregates and other structured priors.

Cons against acceptance
- Reliance on external aggregate priors with limited analysis of robustness on real data.
- Incomplete discussion of how to obtain discrete, interpretable sequences from the relaxed solution.
- Limited exploration of hyperparameters and scalability details; baselines could be broader.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a short section on decoding/rounding strategies and their empirical impact.
- Provide sensitivity analyses to λi and to errors in µi0 on the real dataset.
- Clarify solver choices, problem sizes, and time/space complexity; consider exploiting problem structure for scalability.
- Include one more baseline that uses duration modeling or simple global priors to better isolate the SAC effect.
- Consider an extension that learns µi0 from a prior (e.g., hierarchical Bayesian) when ground-truth aggregates are unavailable.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a compelling and general idea—signal aggregate constraints—for a long-standing identifiability issue in additive factorial models and delivers a practical convex formulation with meaningful gains on realistic data. The novelty and potential for broader impact outweigh the relatively minor gaps in experimental detail and analysis.
