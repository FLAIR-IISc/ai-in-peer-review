**Summary**

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) as a form of regularization for Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Models (AFHMMs), with a primary application in energy disaggregation. The core idea is to leverage prior knowledge about the expected total value of each source signal over a given time period (e.g., the expected daily energy consumption of an appliance). The authors incorporate this constraint into the MAP inference objective for the AFHMM. As this new objective function is non-Markovian and difficult to optimize directly, they propose a convex quadratic programming (CQP) relaxation of the problem. The paper demonstrates through experiments on a toy dataset and real-world energy data that their proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, significantly outperforms both the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Intuitive Idea:** The central contribution, the Signal Aggregate Constraint, is a simple, intuitive, and powerful way to incorporate domain knowledge into a complex blind source separation problem. It directly addresses the identifiability problem by encouraging solutions that are physically plausible in aggregate, a type of constraint that is often available in many domains.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation shows a marked improvement over existing methods. The 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over AFAMAP on real-world data with mains readings is a significant and compelling result.
3.  **Sound Formulation:** Framing the problem as a penalized MAP objective and then solving a CQP relaxation is a reasonable and practical approach to a computationally hard problem. The connection drawn to the posterior regularization framework in Section 6 provides a nice theoretical grounding for the objective function.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising core idea and results, the paper is marred by a substantial number of minor flaws, technical omissions, and a general lack of precision that, in aggregate, significantly detract the quality of the work.

1.  **Lack of Technical Precision and Clarity:**
    *   In Equation (1), the noise variance is denoted as $\sigma^2_t$, implying a time-varying variance. This is never justified, explained, or mentioned again. The Gaussian likelihood in Equation (2) also uses $\sigma^2_t$, but the subsequent experiments seem to implicitly assume a constant variance. This is a critical and unexplained inconsistency in the model definition.
    *   The notation in Section 5 is exceptionally dense and presented without sufficient explanation. While using Kronecker products can be compact, it severely hampers readability. A more pedagogical description would greatly benefit the paper.
    *   The term "franken-appliance" in the introduction is colloquial and inappropriate for a top-tier scientific publication.
    *   In Section 4, the statement `xit ≈ µit` is imprecise. An HMM generates observations from a distribution conditioned on the state, it doesn't state that the signal *is* the state's mean value. While the meaning can be inferred from the model likelihood, the initial description is confusing.
    *   The generalization of SACs to multiple time windows in Section 4.2 is mentioned and then immediately dismissed "for presentational simplicity." This makes the idea feel underdeveloped and leaves the reader questioning its feasibility and impact.

2.  **Critical Methodological Omissions:**
    *   The most significant omission is the failure to describe how the solution from the relaxed CQP (where state indicators `Sitk` are in `[0, 1]`) is converted back into a discrete sequence of states. Is the result rounded? Is a Viterbi-like path found on these soft assignments? This is a fundamental step required for reproducibility and for interpreting the results, and its absence is a major flaw.
    *   The experimental methodology lacks rigor. The crucial hyperparameter $\lambda_i$ is simply set to 1 for all experiments with the comment that it "could be tuned using cross validation." A sensitivity analysis or a validation procedure for this parameter is essential for a convincing evaluation. Without it, the strong results could be due to a fortuitous choice of $\lambda$.
    *   The choice of 3 states for all appliances is arbitrary and unjustified. The paper mentions using k-means to estimate the state means (`µi`), but provides no further detail on this critical pre-processing step.

3.  **Superficial Experimental Analysis:**
    *   The toy data experiment (Section 7.1) demonstrates only that the regularizer works as intended (performance degrades as the constraint value moves away from the truth). This is an expected outcome and provides little new insight.
    *   While the aggregate results are strong, there is no detailed breakdown of performance. For which appliances does SAC help the most? Are there cases where it hurts performance? A more nuanced analysis is missing.
    *   The paper notes that the algorithms "take more time to converge for realistic data" but offers no explanation for this phenomenon.

4.  **Sloppy Presentation and Formatting:**
    *   There is a typo in Section 5: "equavalent" should be "equivalent."
    *   The bibliography is riddled with errors, which suggests a lack of care in the paper's preparation. For example, reference [7] is just a URL followed by commas, reference [8] is incomplete, and reference [16] contains "AAAI-". These are not minor issues; a polished submission to NIPS is expected to have a properly formatted bibliography.

**Final Assessment**

*   **Quality:** The technical quality is compromised by the accumulation of inconsistencies (e.g., noise variance), methodological gaps (handling the CQP relaxation), and a lack of experimental rigor (hyperparameter selection).
*   **Clarity:** The paper is poorly written in places. The dense notation in Section 5 and the imprecise model definitions make it difficult to fully understand and reproduce the work.
*   **Originality:** The core idea of Signal Aggregate Constraints is novel and a valuable contribution to the field of constrained probabilistic modeling for time-series.
*   **Significance:** The work is potentially significant. The problem of energy disaggregation is important, and the demonstrated performance improvements are substantial. If presented more carefully, this work could be highly influential.

**Recommendation: Reject**

This paper presents a very promising direction with a strong central idea and impressive empirical results. However, the manuscript in its current form is not ready for publication at NIPS. The sheer number of minor issues—ranging from notational inconsistencies and critical methodological omissions to a superficial experimental analysis and sloppy formatting—collectively undermine the reader's confidence in the work's technical soundness and reproducibility. While no single flaw is fatal, their high density suggests a rushed preparation. I would be willing to reconsider a future submission that thoroughly addresses these numerous points, as the core contribution is valuable.
