### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), specifically in the context of energy disaggregation, where an aggregate time series signal (e.g., total household electricity usage) must be separated into its constituent source signals (e.g., individual appliances). The authors correctly identify the core challenge of this task: the problem is ill-posed and unidentifiable, often leading to unrealistic "franken-appliance" solutions. To combat this, the paper introduces a novel type of constraint called Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs). The key idea is to leverage prior knowledge about the expected *total* value of each source signal over a given time period (e.g., the average daily energy consumption of a refrigerator).

These constraints are incorporated into a standard Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a common model for this task. A major technical challenge is that SACs are non-Markovian, as they depend on the entire state sequence of an HMM chain, rendering standard inference algorithms like Viterbi inapplicable. The authors overcome this by formulating the MAP inference problem as a penalized objective function and then relaxing it into a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The paper also provides a theoretical justification for this approach by linking it to the posterior regularization framework.

The proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on a challenging, low-frequency real-world energy disaggregation dataset (HES). The results demonstrate a significant improvement over both the baseline AFHMM and a state-of-the-art constrained model, AFAMAP, reducing normalized disaggregation error by 45% and 29% respectively on a key task.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Intuitive Constraint:** The central contribution, the Signal Aggregate Constraint, is simple, intuitive, and novel for this problem space. While other works have constrained AFHMMs (e.g., AFAMAP's one-state-change constraint), they typically focus on local, temporal properties. SACs provide a global constraint on the behavior of a source, which is a fundamentally different and complementary type of domain knowledge.

2.  **Practicality and Applicability:** The prior knowledge required for SACs (i.e., the expected aggregate value `µi0`) is often readily available from external sources, such as manufacturer specifications or national energy surveys, as the authors point out. This makes the method highly practical and applicable without needing perfectly labeled training data from the specific deployment environment.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are convincing. The authors use a challenging, low-frequency dataset (HES), which is more realistic for many smart meter deployments than the high-frequency data used in some prior work. The performance gains over both the baseline AFHMM and the strong AFAMAP baseline are substantial and statistically significant. The inclusion of an "oracle" experiment using the true total consumption (TTC) effectively demonstrates the upper bound of the method's potential.

4.  **Sound Technical Approach:** The formulation of the inference problem as a CQP is a well-founded and computationally tractable approach. The authors clearly lay out the transformation and correctly identify it as a relaxation of the original integer program. The connection drawn to posterior regularization in Section 6 adds theoretical depth and rigor to the proposed penalized objective function.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Sensitivity to Prior Quality:** The method's performance inherently depends on the accuracy of the aggregate prior `µi0`. The toy data experiment confirms this sensitivity. The paper could be strengthened by discussing the robustness of the method to poor priors. What happens if the prior is significantly wrong for one or more appliances? A more detailed sensitivity analysis on the real-world data would be valuable.

2.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The complexity parameter `λi` is set to 1 for all experiments. While the authors mention it could be tuned via cross-validation, the paper would benefit from a discussion on its role and sensitivity. Does a single `λ` for all appliances suffice? Or would appliance-specific values, perhaps related to the variance of their daily consumption, be more effective? An ablation study showing the effect of varying `λ` would improve the paper's completeness.

3.  **Clarification on CQP Solution:** The CQP solves a relaxed problem where the state indicator variables `Sitk` are in the continuous interval [0, 1]. However, the evaluation metrics (NDE, SAE) require discrete state assignments to calculate the estimated signal `x̂it`. The paper does not specify how this conversion from a fractional solution to a discrete one is performed (e.g., by rounding, or taking the argmax at each timestep). This is a crucial detail for reproducibility and should be clarified.

4.  **Scope of Comparison:** While the comparison to AFAMAP is strong and relevant, the related work section mentions other constrained HMMs, such as semi-Markov models that constrain state durations. A brief discussion on how SACs are conceptually different from or could potentially be combined with such duration-based constraints would further situate the contribution within the field.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, simple, and highly effective type of constraint for a difficult and important problem (energy disaggregation).
*   The approach is well-motivated and technically sound, using a standard CQP relaxation for efficient inference.
*   Provides very strong empirical results on a challenging, realistic dataset, demonstrating a clear advance over the state of the art.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The paper lacks a sensitivity analysis for its key hyperparameters (`µi0` and `λi`), making it harder to gauge its robustness.
*   A key implementation detail (converting the CQP's fractional solution to a discrete sequence for evaluation) is omitted.

### Final Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a clear and significant contribution. The proposed Signal Aggregate Constraint is an excellent idea that elegantly incorporates practical domain knowledge to solve the key identifiability problem in energy disaggregation. The technical execution is solid, and the experimental results are compelling, demonstrating a substantial improvement over existing methods. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and can be addressed in a final version. The work is original, technically sound, and likely to be influential for researchers and practitioners in energy disaggregation and other BSS applications.

**Recommendation: Accept**
