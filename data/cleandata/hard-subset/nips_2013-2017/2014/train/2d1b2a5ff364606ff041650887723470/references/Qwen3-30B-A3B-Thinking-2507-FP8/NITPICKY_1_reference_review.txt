

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper proposes Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs) to address identifiability issues in blind source separation, particularly for energy disaggregation. The authors introduce SACs that constrain the aggregate values of source signals to be close to specified values, and transform the constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program. The paper claims significant improvements over baseline methods, with a 45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error compared to the original AFHMM.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important practical problem in energy disaggregation, where identifiability is a key challenge. The SAC approach is theoretically grounded in posterior regularization, and the convex optimization formulation provides a computationally efficient solution. The experimental results on real-world energy data demonstrate promising improvements over baseline methods, which is significant for the domain.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Inconsistencies
The paper contains several mathematical inconsistencies that undermine its technical soundness. In equation (5), the penalty term is written as -Σλi(Σµi^T Sit - µi0)^2, but the constraint in equation (4) is (Σµi^T Sit - µi0)^2 ≤ δi. The penalty term should be λi(Σµi^T Sit - µi0)^2 without the outer square, which is a critical error. This mistake is not just a typo but affects the entire formulation of the optimization problem.

The paper claims the problem can be transformed into a convex quadratic program, but fails to demonstrate convexity. While the authors state that Λi and Vt are positive semidefinite, they don't show that the entire objective function is convex, which is essential for their CQP claim. The relaxation to Sitk ∈ [0, 1] is not justified as a convex relaxation of the original problem.

### Experimental Design Issues
The toy data experiment (section 7.1) uses state values (0, 24, 280) and (0, 300, 500) where the means are very different (24 vs 300, 280 vs 500). This makes the disaggregation problem artificially easy, potentially overestimating the method's performance. The paper doesn't explain why these specific values were chosen or how they relate to real-world scenarios.

In section 7.2.1, the paper claims AFHMM+SAC using NTC improved 33% and 16% over AFAMAP in terms of NDE and SAE, respectively. However, the paper doesn't provide the exact values for comparison, making it impossible to verify these claims. The results in Table 1 are presented without standard deviations, which is essential for statistical significance.

### Clarity and Presentation
The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies. In equation (1), the summation notation is written as "I∑ i=1 xit" instead of the standard "∑_{i=1}^I x_{it}". Equation (3) contains a typo: "distibution" instead of "distribution." The description of how to obtain constraint values µi0 is vague and lacks practical guidance, which is crucial for the method's applicability.

The relationship between SACs and existing constraint-based approaches (e.g., [12]) isn't clearly articulated. The paper claims to generalize [12] but doesn't explain how SACs differ from the constraints in [12] or why their approach is superior.

### Significance Limitations
While the energy disaggregation application is valuable, the paper doesn't sufficiently explore how SACs could be applied to other domains beyond energy disaggregation. The experimental evaluation is limited to energy data, and the paper doesn't discuss whether the method would generalize to other blind source separation problems.

## Overall Assessment
The paper addresses a meaningful problem with a theoretically interesting approach, but the technical inconsistencies in the mathematical formulation are severe. The experimental design overestimates the method's performance, and the paper fails to provide sufficient detail for replication. The claimed improvements (45% and 29%) appear exaggerated due to the experimental setup and lack of statistical reporting.

While the paper has potential, the technical errors and presentation issues are too significant to overlook. The mathematical inconsistencies alone would prevent the method from being reliably implemented or extended by other researchers. The paper would benefit from a complete reworking of the mathematical formulation, more rigorous experimental design, and clearer articulation of the method's novelty relative to existing work.

I recommend rejection due to the critical mathematical errors and insufficient experimental validation. The authors would need to thoroughly revise the paper to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.
