- Overall assessment: Introducing Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) into AFHMM is a well-motivated, technically sound contribution that merits acceptance.  
- Main idea: Use domain knowledge as expectations on per-source aggregate energy (SACs) within AFHMM and solve a relaxed convex quadratic program for efficient inference.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on AFHMM and posterior regularization; differs from AFAMAP and semi-Markov models by constraining aggregate signal values rather than state transitions.  
- Novelty & insight: SACs provide a practical, intuitive way to address identifiability in blind source separation using realistic aggregate priors.  
- Technical soundness: Mathematical formulation, relaxation, and convex program derivation are clear and rigorous.  
- Empirical validation: Strong results on the HES dataset—~45% reduction in normalized error vs standard AFHMM and ~29% vs AFAMAP.  
- Clarity & presentation: Paper is well-written, organized, and easy to follow, with consistent notation and derivations.  
- Theoretical grounding: Clear connection to posterior regularization and measurement-based learning frameworks.  
- Computational efficiency: Method scales comparably to AFAMAP and is practically solvable (e.g., via CVX).  
- Weakness — sensitivity: Performance depends on accurate estimates of aggregate values (µi0); practical estimation methods need more discussion.  
- Weakness — limited constraint exploration: Multiple/time-varying constraints are mentioned but not experimentally explored.  
- Weakness — modeling choices: The arbitrary selection of three states per appliance needs further investigation for impact on performance.  
- Weakness — comparisons: Lacks evaluation against more recent state-of-the-art energy disaggregation methods.  
- For acceptance: Significant empirical gains, novel and practical approach, strong theoretical and computational grounding.  
- Against acceptance: Reliance on accurate aggregate estimates, limited exploration of richer constraint structures, and missing recent-method comparisons.  
- Final recommendation: Strong accept.
