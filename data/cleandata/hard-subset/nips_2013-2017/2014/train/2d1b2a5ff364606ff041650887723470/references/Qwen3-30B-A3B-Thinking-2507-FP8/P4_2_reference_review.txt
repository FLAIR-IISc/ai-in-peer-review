

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for blind source separation (BSS) problems, specifically applied to energy disaggregation. The authors address the identifiability problem in BSS by incorporating prior knowledge about expected total signal values (e.g., expected daily energy consumption of appliances) as constraints within an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM). The key contribution is a method to transform the constrained optimization problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP) that can be efficiently solved. The paper relates SACs to posterior regularization [4], showing that the proposed approach is a specific instantiation of this general framework.

Previous work in energy disaggregation has primarily focused on constraining state transitions (e.g., AFAMAP [12] which limits state changes to at most one chain per time step), or using semi-Markov models [11, 10]. The paper appropriately references these approaches while demonstrating that SACs represent a fundamentally different class of constraints that can be incorporated without modifying the model structure. The authors also acknowledge the general problem of incorporating domain knowledge into probabilistic models [4, 14, 13], but specifically address the question of "what types of domain knowledge" should be incorporated.

**Strengths:**
1. Technical soundness: The transformation of the constrained optimization problem into a CQP is mathematically rigorous, with clear derivation in Section 5. The authors properly acknowledge the relaxation of the integer constraints and its implications for optimality. The computational complexity analysis shows polynomial time scaling, which is crucial for practical applications.

2. Empirical evaluation: The paper includes thorough experiments on both toy data (Section 7.1) and real energy disaggregation data (Section 7.2). The results demonstrate significant improvements over both the baseline AFHMM (45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error) and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP [12] (29% improvement). The use of both NDE (normalized disaggregation error) and SAE (signal aggregate error) provides a comprehensive evaluation of performance.

3. Clarity: The paper is well-organized with clear mathematical notation. The problem statement, model definitions, and algorithmic details are presented in a logical flow. The experimental setup is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility, and the results are presented with appropriate statistical validation (paired t-tests).

4. Significance: The approach addresses a fundamental limitation of BSS (identifiability) by incorporating practical domain knowledge. The method is generalizable beyond energy disaggregation to other BSS applications where aggregate signal values are known. The computational efficiency (similar to AFHMM and AFAMAP) makes it practical for real-world deployment.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper assumes that the aggregate constraint values (µi0) are known in advance, which may not always be feasible in practice. While the toy data experiment (Section 7.1) demonstrates robustness to errors in these values, the paper does not discuss practical methods for estimating these values when they are not directly available.

2. The comparison with related work is somewhat limited. While AFAMAP [12] is compared, more recent approaches to energy disaggregation (e.g., [15, 16]) could be included for context. The paper mentions that SACs could be incorporated into unsupervised approaches [11], but this is not demonstrated empirically.

3. The theoretical analysis of the convex relaxation's optimality gap is limited. The paper states that the solution is not guaranteed optimal due to identifiability, but does not provide bounds on the suboptimality or discuss how the relaxation affects the quality of the solution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel class of constraints (SACs) addressing a fundamental limitation in BSS
- Rigorous mathematical derivation of the CQP solution
- Significant empirical improvements over strong baselines on real-world data
- Clear connection to posterior regularization framework
- Practical computational efficiency (similar to AFHMM/AFAMAP)
- Well-structured and clearly written paper

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited discussion of practical challenges in obtaining the aggregate constraint values
- Comparison with only two baselines (AFHMM and AFAMAP)
- Theoretical analysis of the relaxation's optimality gap is minimal

This paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of blind source separation with clear practical applications. The proposed SACs represent a novel class of constraints that address a key limitation of BSS models. The transformation to a CQP is well-executed, and the empirical results demonstrate significant improvements in a meaningful application domain. While the paper could be strengthened by addressing the practical challenges of obtaining constraint values and expanding the comparison with recent work, the core contribution is substantial and well-supported.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (quality), clearly written (clarity), introduces a new class of constraints (originality), and demonstrates significant improvements in a practical application (significance). The work is a valuable addition to the literature on constrained probabilistic modeling and energy disaggregation.
