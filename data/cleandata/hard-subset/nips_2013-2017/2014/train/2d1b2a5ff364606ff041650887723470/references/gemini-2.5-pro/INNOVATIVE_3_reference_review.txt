This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), specifically in the context of additive factorial hidden Markov models (AFHMMs), with a primary application in energy disaggregation. The core contribution is the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), a novel form of prior knowledge that regularizes the solution to the BSS problem. The authors observe that in many domains, one has a reasonable prior on the total aggregate value of a source signal over a long time period (e.g., the total daily energy consumption of a refrigerator). They incorporate this idea into the AFHMM framework by adding a penalty term to the MAP inference objective, encouraging the aggregate sum of each inferred source to be close to a predefined value. As this constraint is non-Markovian and makes direct inference via dynamic programming intractable, the authors cleverly formulate a relaxed version of the problem as a convex quadratic program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. The method is evaluated on a challenging, low-frequency real-world energy consumption dataset, where it is shown to yield substantial improvements over both the standard AFHMM and a recent state-of-the-art method, AFAMAP.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The central idea of using Signal Aggregate Constraints is simple, intuitive, and highly novel in this context. It directly tackles a fundamental and challenging issue in AFHMM-based BSS: the identifiability problem. While other works have proposed constraints (e.g., AFAMAP's constraint on state changes), SACs represent a conceptually different and powerful form of domain knowledge. The idea is bold and provides a new direction for incorporating priors into this class of models. This is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates not just a minor improvement, but a substantial leap in performance on a difficult and practical problem. A 45% decrease in error compared to the baseline AFHMM and a 29% improvement over the state-of-the-art AFAMAP on real mains data are very impressive. These results strongly validate the utility of the proposed approach and suggest that SACs capture crucial information missed by previous models.

3.  **Elegant Formulation:** The translation of the non-Markovian constrained optimization problem into a tractable convex quadratic program is a key technical contribution. This makes the approach practical and scalable, with reported computation times similar to existing methods. The connection drawn to the posterior regularization framework also provides a principled theoretical grounding for the penalized objective function.

4.  **Generality:** While the paper focuses on energy disaggregation, the concept of SACs is broadly applicable to other BSS domains where aggregate statistics of the sources can be estimated or are known a priori (e.g., computational biology, audio processing). This broadens the potential impact of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Relaxation Details:** The paper solves a relaxed version of the integer program where the state indicators `Sitk` are in [0, 1]. However, it is not explicitly stated how a final discrete state sequence is recovered from these continuous values for evaluation. One assumes a rounding or argmax operation is performed, but clarifying this would strengthen the paper's methodological description. While the relaxation is a necessary step for tractability, a brief discussion of its potential impact would be welcome.

2.  **Sensitivity to Priors:** The toy data experiment shows that performance degrades as the aggregate prior `µi0` moves away from the true value. In the real-world experiments, the "National Total Consumption" is used as a proxy. While this is a reasonable choice, a more thorough analysis of the model's sensitivity to the quality of this prior in the real-data setting would be beneficial. However, the strong performance achieved using this general prior already suggests a good degree of robustness.

3.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The complexity parameters `λi` are set to 1 for all experiments. While the authors note that they could be tuned via cross-validation, the paper does not explore this. The fact that the model performs so well even with this simple, untuned setting is arguably a strength, but showing the effect of varying `λi` could provide further insight.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This is an excellent paper that introduces a creative, powerful, and conceptually novel idea. The core contribution—the Signal Aggregate Constraint—is a significant step forward in addressing the critical identifiability problem in AFHMMs. The authors support this bold idea with an elegant CQP formulation and compelling empirical results on a challenging real-world problem, demonstrating substantial gains over existing state-of-the-art methods.

The weaknesses identified are minor and relate more to incomplete validation details rather than fundamental flaws in the approach. Given the high novelty of the core concept and the significance of the results, these are easily overlooked. This work opens up a promising new direction for research in structured latent variable models and their applications. It is precisely the kind of innovative, high-impact paper that NIPS should showcase.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Introduces a highly novel and intuitive type of constraint (SACs) for AFHMMs.
*   Provides a simple yet powerful solution to the long-standing identifiability problem.
*   Demonstrates a very large, non-incremental performance improvement over a recent SOTA model on a challenging real-world task.
*   The approach is generalizable and likely to inspire follow-up work in other domains.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The CQP solves a relaxation, and the paper is not fully explicit about the final discretization step.
*   Sensitivity to the quality of the aggregate priors and the `λ` hyperparameters is not deeply explored.

**Decision:** I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty and significance of the core idea, backed by strong empirical evidence, far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which can be addressed in a final version.
