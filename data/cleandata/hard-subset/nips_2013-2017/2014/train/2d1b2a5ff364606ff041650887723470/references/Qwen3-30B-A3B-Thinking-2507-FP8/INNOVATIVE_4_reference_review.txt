

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper introduces a novel concept called Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) for addressing the identifiability problem in blind source separation (BSS), with a specific focus on energy disaggregation. The core insight is that in many BSS applications, we often have domain knowledge about the total expected values of source signals (e.g., a toaster's daily energy consumption), and SACs enforce these aggregate values as constraints. This approach differs fundamentally from previous work that constrained local state transitions (like AFAMAP) or used other forms of prior information.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) the SAC concept itself, (2) a convex quadratic program formulation for efficiently solving the constrained optimization problem, and (3) a thorough evaluation on real-world energy disaggregation data showing significant performance improvements (45% reduction in normalized disaggregation error over baseline AFHMM and 29% over state-of-the-art).

The novelty of SACs is particularly compelling. While prior work has explored various constraints for HMMs (like state change limits in AFAMAP), SACs represent a fundamentally different type of constraint that addresses the core identifiability problem by incorporating global aggregate knowledge rather than local temporal structure. The paper effectively positions SACs as filling a critical gap in the literature regarding "what types of domain knowledge we should include" for BSS.

The technical contribution of transforming the non-convex SAC optimization problem into a convex quadratic program (CQP) is well-executed. The authors correctly acknowledge that the constraints are non-Markovian (depending on the entire sequence), making standard Viterbi algorithms inapplicable. The relaxed CQP formulation is elegant and computationally efficient, with polynomial complexity that scales similarly to standard AFHMM approaches.

The evaluation is strong and well-motivated. The toy data experiments demonstrate SACs' robustness to constraint specification errors, while the energy disaggregation results on real-world data show clear, statistically significant improvements over both baseline AFHMM and AFAMAP. The use of both national average consumption (NTC) and true consumption (TTC) as constraint values provides a useful theoretical upper bound on performance. The paper's focus on both normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE) is particularly insightful for the energy disaggregation domain.

This work stands out from typical incremental BSS papers by introducing a fundamentally new constraint type that directly addresses a core problem in the field. The approach is generalizable beyond energy disaggregation to other BSS applications where aggregate knowledge is available. The paper demonstrates that incorporating this specific type of domain knowledge leads to meaningful performance gains, which is a significant contribution.

The paper's main limitations are minor and do not detract from the core contribution: (1) SACs require prior knowledge of aggregate values, which may not always be available (though the authors acknowledge this); (2) the evaluation is focused on energy disaggregation, though the authors note the approach's general applicability. Neither limitation undermines the paper's core novelty.

Compared to other NIPS papers on constrained BSS, this work is significantly more innovative than incremental extensions of existing constraint approaches. It's a bold step that moves beyond the usual "constrain state transitions" paradigm to consider global aggregate constraints. The paper successfully demonstrates how this novel approach leads to better results in a practical application domain.

The paper's strength lies in its clear identification of a previously unaddressed problem (what types of constraints to use for BSS) and the introduction of a novel solution that delivers tangible improvements. This is exactly the kind of bold, novel contribution NIPS should prioritize.

**Strengths:**
- Novel concept (SACs) that directly addresses identifiability in BSS
- Clear theoretical foundation with elegant convex optimization solution
- Strong empirical results with significant performance gains
- Well-motivated application domain with meaningful evaluation metrics
- Clear distinction from prior work (AFAMAP, etc.)

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited exploration of how to obtain aggregate constraints when not directly available (though acknowledged)
- Application focused primarily on energy disaggregation (though generalizability is noted)

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to a fundamental problem in BSS, with clear theoretical and empirical justification. The performance improvements are substantial and statistically significant, and the methodological contribution (convex optimization formulation for SACs) is clean and efficient. While the application domain is specific, the conceptual contribution is generalizable and could inspire similar approaches in other BSS contexts. This is the kind of bold, novel contribution that advances the field beyond incremental improvements.
