Summary
The paper tackles blind source separation for additive time series, with a focus on energy disaggregation. It augments the additive factorial HMM (AFHMM) with signal aggregate constraints (SACs) that encode prior knowledge of the total energy consumed by each source over a time horizon. These constraints are implemented as quadratic penalties on the difference between the aggregate of each chain’s emissions and a target µi0. Because this penalty breaks Markov structure, the authors introduce auxiliary transition variables and relax integrality to obtain a convex quadratic program (maximize a concave quadratic subject to linear constraints). They show that their objective can be derived from posterior regularization, and that the relaxed problem can be solved efficiently with CVX. On toy data they show performance degrades gracefully as the aggregate priors deviate from the truth. On the UK HES dataset (low sampling rate, realistic, challenging), they report substantial improvements over AFHMM and AFAMAP in both normalized disaggregation error (NDE) and signal aggregate error (SAE), with similar runtimes.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on AFHMMs (Ghahramani & Jordan) and AFAMAP (Kolter & Jaakkola), as well as related constraints for HMMs (e.g., duration/semi-Markov models, k-segment, at-most-one-change constraints). It is closely connected to posterior regularization (Ganchev et al.), generalized expectation (Mann & McCallum), and measurement-based learning (Liang et al.), but differs in the specific form of aggregate constraints over entire sequences and the convex relaxation tailored to AFHMM inference. The paper cites related aggregate constraints in databases and non-homogeneous HMM variants for NILM. Overall, the relationship to PR/GE is appropriate and the computational contribution is more specific than prior general frameworks.

Strengths
- Clear, application-motivated constraint: enforcing expected aggregate usage is natural in many BSS domains and particularly compelling for energy disaggregation where totals are interpretable and often known approximately.
- Sound optimization: with auxiliary variables and relaxation, the objective is concave in the decision variables subject to linear constraints. The derivation is careful about PSD structure; maximizing a concave quadratic under linear constraints is a standard convex program.
- Link to posterior regularization: frames the penalty as a prior over posteriors, clarifying the statistical meaning.
- Empirical value: consistent improvements on realistic, low-frequency HES data over AFHMM and AFAMAP; toy experiments illustrate sensitivity to prior quality.
- Practical considerations: discusses extending SACs to multiple windows (e.g., day/night), which is natural in NILM.

Weaknesses and questions
- Rounding/discretization: The relaxed variables S,H are fractional. The paper does not clearly specify how discrete sequences are recovered for evaluation (per-time argmax per chain? projection consistency with H? post-processing Viterbi under soft costs?). This affects reproducibility and may impact results.
- Hyperparameter tuning: λi are fixed to 1 in all experiments. While simplicity is appreciated, a sensitivity analysis or cross-validation (even on a subset) would strengthen claims and clarify robustness to mis-specified regularization strength.
- Where µ0 comes from: For “with mains” experiments, NTC is computed from training data; for “without mains,” NTC is averaged across households. This is reasonable, but AFAMAP/AFHMM baselines do not leverage this side information. A variant where baselines are given equivalent aggregate priors (e.g., as a post-hoc calibration or a soft constraint) would better isolate the benefit of the inference formulation rather than the extra information.
- Scalability: CVX is convenient but may not scale to longer sequences or many appliances. The paper claims similar runtimes to AFHMM/AFAMAP and shows summary times, but more detail is needed: number of variables, solver tolerances, typical T, I, K, and how runtime scales. A bespoke first-order method or ADMM could be discussed.
- Identifiability and constraint conflicts: While SACs help, identifiability is still an issue. What happens when multiple appliances have similar µ and overlapping usage? Could SACs push the solution to pathological allocations when priors are inaccurate? The toy plot helps, but further stress tests (e.g., biased µ0, mismatched appliance dictionary) would be useful.
- Missing ablations: It would help to include results for the relaxed AFHMM without SAC (λ=0 via CQP) to separate gains from the relaxation itself vs the constraint.
- Clarity and presentation: The notation in Section 5 is dense. Some references are incomplete or poorly formatted; adding a brief algorithmic description (variables, constraints, objective) and the rounding step would improve readability.

Quality
The technical formulation appears sound, and the convexity argument is correct under the stated PSD matrices. The empirical methodology is mostly solid, with statistical tests and clear metrics. However, the lack of details on rounding and hyperparameter selection limits reproducibility. The reliance on CVX without complexity analysis raises questions about scalability.

Clarity
Generally clear, but Section 5 is not easy to follow; a compact algorithm box and an explicit post-processing step would help. Some citation formatting needs attention.

Originality
Moderate. SACs as global aggregate constraints are a natural but novel addition to AFHMMs, and the specific convex relaxation for MAP inference is a useful contribution beyond generic PR/GE frameworks. The posterior-regularization connection is known but well integrated.

Significance
Moderate to high for NILM and similar BSS domains where aggregate priors exist. The reported gains over AFAMAP on challenging HES data suggest practical impact. The idea is general enough to apply beyond energy.

Suggestions for improvement
- Specify and evaluate the discretization/rounding method; report its impact.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for λ and for µ0 accuracy; include multiple-window SAC results.
- Compare to AFHMM with duration/semi-Markov constraints and to AFAMAP augmented with aggregate priors if possible.
- Add complexity analysis and discuss scalable solvers beyond CVX.
- Clean up references and notation.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, well-motivated constraint with clear statistical and optimization framing.
- Convex relaxation enabling efficient inference for a hard non-Markovian objective.
- Strong empirical improvements on realistic datasets with statistical significance.
- General idea applicable to other BSS problems.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing details on rounding and hyperparameter selection; limited ablations.
- Potential unfairness in comparisons due to extra side information not provided to baselines.
- Scalability concerns with CVX not fully addressed.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a useful and well-executed idea that advances NILM and potentially other BSS tasks. Clarifying the rounding, adding sensitivity/ablation studies, and discussing scalability would strengthen it further.
