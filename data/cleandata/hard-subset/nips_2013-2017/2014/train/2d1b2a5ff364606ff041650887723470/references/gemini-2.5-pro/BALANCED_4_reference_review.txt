This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), focusing on the application of energy disaggregation. The core challenge in this domain is the inherent non-identifiability of the sources, which makes it difficult to recover meaningful component signals (e.g., individual appliance consumption) from an aggregate signal (e.g., total household electricity usage). The authors propose to address this by incorporating prior domain knowledge into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM), a common model for this task.

The main contribution is the introduction of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs), which enforce a prior belief that the total energy consumed by a specific appliance over a given time period should be close to a known value. This type of global constraint on the entire time series is novel in this context and contrasts with prior work that has focused on local constraints, such as limiting the number of state changes at each time step (e.g., Kolter & Jaakkola, 2012) or modeling state durations (e.g., Johnson & Willsky, 2013).

Because SACs are non-Markovian, they make standard MAP inference algorithms like the Viterbi algorithm inapplicable. To overcome this, the authors formulate MAP inference as an Integer Quadratic Program and then relax it into a Convex Quadratic Program (CQP), which can be solved efficiently. They provide a theoretical justification for their approach by showing its equivalence to the posterior regularization framework (Ganchev et al., 2010). The proposed method, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on a large, real-world energy disaggregation dataset, where it is shown to significantly outperform both the standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP algorithm.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of using signal aggregate constraints is simple, intuitive, and highly effective. It represents a novel way to inject powerful domain knowledge into the BSS problem. The problem of energy disaggregation is of significant practical importance, and the paper demonstrates a substantial improvement (a 29-45% reduction in error compared to strong baselines) on this task. The idea is also general and could likely be applied to other BSS domains where similar aggregate priors are available (e.g., total expression level of a gene pathway over time).

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of MAP inference with SACs as a CQP is elegant and provides a practical path to a solution. The mathematical derivation is clear and correct. Furthermore, grounding the penalized objective function in the posterior regularization framework adds theoretical depth and connects the work to a broader literature on constrained probabilistic modeling.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The motivation for the problem is well-established, the proposed method is described logically, and the experimental setup and results are presented transparently. The structure guides the reader effectively from the problem definition to the solution and its evaluation.

4.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The use of a toy dataset effectively illustrates the mechanism and robustness of the SACs. The evaluation on a large, challenging, and realistic low-frequency energy dataset (HES) is a major strength. The authors compare against appropriate and strong baselines, including AFAMAP. The inclusion of an oracle experiment (using True Total Consumption) provides a valuable upper bound on the potential of this type of constraint. Finally, the experiments on data with and without actual mains readings cover both idealized and realistic scenarios.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The most significant weakness is the handling of the regularization parameter λ. The authors state that they "simply set λi = 1 for all the experiments." The performance of any regularization-based method is typically sensitive to the choice of this hyperparameter, which balances the data likelihood against the constraint. Without a sensitivity analysis or a principled method for setting λ (e.g., cross-validation), it is difficult to assess the robustness of the results. The authors should, at a minimum, include a discussion of how this parameter would be set in a real application and ideally provide an experiment showing how performance varies with λ.

2.  **Scalability of the CQP:** While the paper notes that the CQP runs in polynomial time and shows empirically similar runtimes to baselines for one-day-long sequences, a more formal discussion of computational complexity would be beneficial. The number of variables and constraints in the CQP scales with the number of chains (I), time steps (T), and states (K). For very long time series (e.g., a month of data at a 2-minute resolution), the CQP could become prohibitively large. A brief analysis of the complexity and a discussion of potential limitations or scaling strategies would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Generalization of Constraints:** The paper briefly mentions the possibility of applying SACs over smaller time windows (e.g., enforcing that a TV is not used between 2 am and 5 am) but does not explore this. While understandable for "presentational simplicity," demonstrating this capability, even with a small example, would have showcased the flexibility of the framework more powerfully and made the contribution even stronger.

4.  **Interaction with Other Constraints:** The proposed SACs are presented as an alternative to the constraints in AFAMAP (at most one state change at a time). An interesting question is whether these different types of constraints are complementary. It is plausible that combining a global aggregate constraint (SAC) with a local transition constraint (AFAMAP) could yield even better performance. A brief discussion of this as a direction for future work would be welcome.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel, significant, and well-executed idea. The proposed Signal Aggregate Constraints are intuitive and shown to be highly effective, leading to state-of-the-art results on a challenging real-world problem. The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. The paper is a model of clarity.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The lack of analysis regarding the key hyperparameter λ is a notable omission that raises questions about the robustness and practical application of the method.

**Decision:**

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The core contribution is a strong one that advances the field of energy disaggregation and offers a valuable tool for constrained probabilistic modeling more broadly. The identified weaknesses, particularly concerning hyperparameter selection, are addressable and do not undermine the validity of the core results. This is a high-quality paper suitable for publication at NIPS.
