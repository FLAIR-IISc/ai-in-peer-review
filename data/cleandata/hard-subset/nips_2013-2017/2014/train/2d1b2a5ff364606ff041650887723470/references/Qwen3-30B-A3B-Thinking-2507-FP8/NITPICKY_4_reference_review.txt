

# Review of "Signal Aggregate Constraints in Additive Factorial HMMs, with Application to Energy Disaggregation"

This paper proposes "Signal Aggregate Constraints" (SACs) for additive factorial HMMs (AFHMMs), aiming to improve blind source separation in energy disaggregation. The authors argue that domain knowledge about expected total energy consumption for appliances can be incorporated as constraints to address the identifiability problem in BSS. They formulate SACs as convex quadratic programs and demonstrate 33% improvement in NDE over AFAMAP on real energy data.

## Strengths
The paper identifies a meaningful problem in energy disaggregation where identifiability hinders practical application. The SAC framework is conceptually sound and connects to posterior regularization, providing a theoretically grounded approach. The experimental results on real-world data are promising, showing consistent improvement over AFHMM and AFAMAP.

## Weaknesses

### Technical and Methodological Issues
1. **Critical mathematical inconsistency**: The paper claims "the matrices Λi and Vt are positive semidefinite (PSD)" (Section 5), but provides no proof or justification. This is a fundamental requirement for the convexity claim, and its absence undermines the entire optimization approach.

2. **Notational inconsistencies**: 
   - Equation (4) uses "T∑t=1" instead of standard summation notation
   - Confusing notation in "maximize S" (should be "maximize over S" or "maximize L(S)")
   - Inconsistent vector/matrix dimensions (e.g., Si defined as "size TKi × 1" but should be TKi × T)

3. **Reproducibility issues**:
   - Claims "45% decrease in normalized disaggregation error" (Abstract) but Table 1 shows only 33% improvement (0.186 vs 0.124 NDE)
   - States "29% improvement" over [12] but doesn't specify the metric or provide supporting numbers
   - Fails to explain why λi = 1 was chosen for all experiments without sensitivity analysis
   - Does not clarify how model parameters were estimated for the SAC method

4. **Experimental details**:
   - The paper claims "AFHMM+SAC using TTC performs the best" but TTC is an oracle (not available in practice), which the paper fails to adequately qualify
   - The paper states "the mean errors are different at the 5% significance level" for Figure 2 but doesn't provide statistical significance for Table 3 results
   - Missing Figure 1 (referenced in Section 7.1) severely hinders reproducibility

### Presentation and Clarity
1. **Terminology**: SAC is never spelled out in the abstract or introduction, only defined in Section 4.
2. **Reference formatting**: Multiple references have inconsistent punctuation (e.g., extra commas at the end of entries).
3. **Ambiguous claims**: "AFHMM+SAC using NTC had improved 33%..." - "improved" should be "reduced" since lower NDE is better.
4. **Inconsistent reporting**: The paper refers to "SAE" in Section 7.1 but doesn't define it until later, and the formula (9) appears after the first mention.

### Originality and Significance
The SAC concept is novel for BSS, but the paper fails to sufficiently differentiate from related work. For instance, the constraint formulation bears resemblance to posterior regularization (Section 6) but doesn't clearly articulate how this differs from existing regularization approaches. The claimed improvements (33-45%) are modest compared to the state-of-the-art, and the paper doesn't convincingly argue why this method should replace existing approaches.

## Recommendation

While the paper addresses a meaningful problem with a conceptually sound approach, it contains numerous technical, methodological, and presentation flaws that significantly undermine its credibility and reproducibility. The critical mathematical claim about PSD matrices without justification is particularly concerning for a technical conference like NeurIPS. The reported performance improvements don't match the paper's claims, and the lack of details about parameter estimation and hyperparameter selection makes the results difficult to verify.

The paper's significance is also limited by its narrow application domain (energy disaggregation) without clear evidence that SACs generalize to other BSS problems. The connection to posterior regularization (Section 6) is interesting but not sufficiently developed to be a major contribution.

Although the paper presents a novel constraint framework, the combination of mathematical inconsistencies, unverified claims, and unclear experimental details prevents it from meeting NeurIPS's standards for technical soundness. The minor flaws are substantial enough to warrant rejection.

I recommend rejection. The authors should address the mathematical justification for PSD matrices, clarify all notational inconsistencies, provide complete experimental details, and verify all performance claims before resubmission.
