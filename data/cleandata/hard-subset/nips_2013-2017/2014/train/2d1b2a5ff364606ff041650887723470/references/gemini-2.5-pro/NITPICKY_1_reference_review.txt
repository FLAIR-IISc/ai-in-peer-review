**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of blind source separation (BSS), specifically in the context of energy disaggregation, where an aggregated time series signal (e.g., total household electricity usage) needs to be separated into its constituent source signals (e.g., individual appliances). The authors correctly identify that this problem is ill-posed due to identifiability issues. To address this, they propose incorporating domain knowledge in the form of Signal Aggregate Constraints (SACs) into an Additive Factorial Hidden Markov Model (AFHMM). The core idea is to constrain the sum of each source signal over a given time period to be close to a known or expected value. As these constraints are non-Markovian and complicate inference, the authors formulate MAP inference as a penalized optimization problem, which they relax into a convex quadratic program (CQP) that can be solved efficiently. The proposed model, AFHMM+SAC, is evaluated on a toy dataset and a real-world low-frequency energy consumption dataset (HES), demonstrating significant improvements over a standard AFHMM and the state-of-the-art AFAMAP model.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The introduction of signal aggregate constraints is an intuitive and practical way to incorporate prior knowledge into the BSS problem. This type of constraint is particularly well-suited for energy disaggregation, where average consumption data for appliances is often available. The approach is general and could likely be applied to other BSS domains.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling. On a real-world dataset, the proposed method achieves a substantial reduction in error (e.g., a 45% decrease in NDE over AFHMM and 29% over AFAMAP in the most realistic setting). The inclusion of an "oracle" experiment using the true total consumption provides a useful upper bound on the potential benefit of the proposed constraints.

3.  **Sound Technical Approach:** The formulation of the problem as a penalized objective function and its subsequent relaxation into a CQP is a solid and well-established approach for tackling otherwise intractable inference problems. The connection drawn to the posterior regularization framework in Section 6 adds theoretical grounding to the proposed objective function.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising core idea, the paper suffers from a considerable number of minor flaws, ambiguities, and methodological omissions that detract from its overall quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The paper contains several typographical errors (e.g., "distibution" in Sec 4.1, "equavalent" in Sec 5).
    *   The notation in Section 5 is introduced in a single, dense paragraph, making it difficult to follow. Symbols like `1T`, `⊗`, `µ∗i`, `Λi`, `µ̃i`, `eT`, and others are defined in rapid succession without sufficient spacing or explanation.
    *   The reference list is formatted inconsistently. For example, [1] includes page numbers while [2] does not; [7] is just a URL; punctuation and author formatting vary across entries.
    *   The caption for the (unseen) Figure 1 is incomplete and appears to merge with the main body text, which is confusing. It should be self-contained.
    *   In Equation (3), the use of `logP(S|Y) ∝ ...` is slightly imprecise. The objective function being optimized is `logP(S,Y)`, as the `logP(Y)` term is constant with respect to S. While this does not affect the optimization, it reflects a lack of precision.

2.  **Methodological Details:**
    *   **Hyperparameter Selection:** The choice of the regularization parameter `λi = 1` for all experiments is a significant weakness. This parameter is crucial as it balances the data likelihood term against the aggregate constraint. The authors provide no justification for this choice, nor do they perform any sensitivity analysis. A proper evaluation would require tuning this hyperparameter, for instance via cross-validation. Without this, the reported improvements may be misleading or specific to this arbitrary setting.
    *   **Recovery from Relaxation:** The core of the proposed algorithm solves a relaxed CQP where the state indicator variables `Sitk` can take fractional values in [0, 1]. The paper completely omits the crucial step of how a valid discrete state sequence is recovered from this continuous solution. Is it by simple rounding, or by taking the argmax at each time step? This is a critical detail required for reproducibility and for understanding potential issues arising from the relaxation gap.
    *   **Model Parameter Estimation:** The paper states that the HMM parameters `θ` were estimated from training data, and the state means `µi` were found using k-means. However, the choice of K=3 states for all appliances seems ad-hoc. Is there a justification for this specific number? The quality of inference is highly dependent on the accuracy of these estimated parameters, yet the estimation process is described only superficially.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   **Computational Cost:** The paper claims in Section 5 and 7.2.2 that the proposed method has a similar computational cost to the baselines. However, Table 3 shows AFHMM+SAC taking 580s compared to 451s for AFAMAP, a ~28% increase. This is not insignificant and the claim of similar time consumption should be qualified.
    *   **Qualitative Results:** The evaluation relies solely on aggregate error metrics (NDE and SAE). The inclusion of qualitative results, such as plots of the disaggregated signals versus the ground truth for a few examples, would provide much deeper insight into the model's behavior and where it succeeds or fails.
    *   **Inconsistent Reporting:** The abstract and introduction claim a "significant improvement (29%)" over AFAMAP, while the results section (7.2.1) claims a 33% improvement. These figures appear to come from different experimental settings (with and without mains readings). This inconsistency should be clarified.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper presents a promising and well-motivated idea, the execution and presentation are not yet at the level required for publication at NIPS. The large number of minor presentation flaws, coupled with significant methodological omissions—most notably the lack of hyperparameter tuning for `λ` and the failure to describe how discrete states are recovered from the CQP relaxation—prevent a clear and reproducible understanding of the work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The core idea of using signal aggregate constraints is novel, intuitive, and significant for the energy disaggregation domain.
*   The reported quantitative improvements over state-of-the-art methods are substantial.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   Crucial methodological details are missing (hyperparameter tuning, recovery from relaxation), hindering reproducibility and casting doubt on the robustness of the results.
*   The paper suffers from numerous minor errors in formatting, notation, and consistency, indicating a lack of careful preparation.
*   Claims regarding computational cost are not fully supported by the paper's own results.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks qualitative results that would provide deeper insight.

Given the perfectionist standards of the conference, the accumulation of these flaws outweighs the strengths of the core contribution. I recommend **rejection**, but I would encourage the authors to thoroughly revise the manuscript to address these issues for a future submission, as the underlying research direction is valuable.
